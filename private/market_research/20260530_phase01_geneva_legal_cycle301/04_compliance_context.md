# Phase 01 — Compliance Context: Geneva Legal
## Cycle 301 | 2026-05-30

**ICP:** Managing partner or senior associate at a 2–5 attorney Geneva law firm. Personal accountability for AI decisions. June 19 SBA Day of Lawyers ~2 weeks away.

---

## Switzerland / Geneva Regulatory Framework

### 1. Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) — Active Enforcement

**Status:** nFADP in force. FDPIC enforcement posture has shifted from guidance letters to operational consequences.

**Relevance for the ICP:**

- Article 9: Right to human review of AI automated decisions — relevant when AI systems used in legal research or document drafting produce outputs that affect client-facing processes
- Article 21: Client data subject rights — the ICP cannot currently respond to Article 21 requests for AI-processed client data because processing registers do not exist
- Article 22: Automated decision-making disclosure obligations — engagement letter AI clauses must address this; existing clauses may not
- Processing register obligations: FDPIC is auditing for documentation completeness; small firms operating with pre-nFADP compliance infrastructure are at immediate risk

**Key enforcement signal:** Multi-firm audit patterns in Geneva suggest FDPIC focus on documentation completeness and processing register accuracy. The enforcement posture is not theoretical. Firms are being reviewed against standards that did not exist 18 months ago.

### 2. Swiss Bar Association (SBA / SAV) — AI Practice Guidance

**Status:** SBA AI practice rules finalized Q2 2026. First compliance audits expected Q3 2026.

**Relevance for the ICP:**

- Mandatory AI tool disclosure in engagement letters
- Client consent frameworks for AI-assisted case handling
- AI audit trail maintenance obligations
- Competence verification requirements before deploying AI tools in client matters

**The gap:** SBA published principle without methodology. The rules say AI governance is the attorney's responsibility. They do not say what responsible looks like for a 2–5 attorney firm. The ICP is required to demonstrate compliance with a standard that has not been defined.

**June 19 SBA Day of Lawyers:** This is the first major SBA occasion where AI governance will be discussed in formal session since the practice rules went into effect. The ICP will be in the room. The managing partner who signed carrier attestations will face peer discussion of what those attestations commit them to.

### 3. Geneva Cantonal Bar (SVA-Geneva / Ordre des Avocats Genevois)

**Status:** SVA-Geneva implementing SBA AI practice rules. Label de Bonne Gouvernance IA under development with TÜV Switzerland third-party audit. Q3 2026 launch. 12 Geneva firms committed.

**Relevance for the ICP:**

- The label creates a market signal that will be visible in Geneva legal community
- Firms with the label will be distinguishable from firms without it
- Getting the label requires documentation infrastructure the ICP does not currently have
- The June 19 event is likely to surface questions about label readiness

### 4. Attorney-Client Privilege and Professional Secrecy

**Framework:** Federal Act on the Free Movement of Lawyers, Swiss Criminal Code Art. 321.

**Relevance for the ICP:**

- AI tools handling client data must be contractually and technically secured to prevent unauthorized disclosure
- Attorney-client privilege creates specific obligations around AI system data processing that generic nFADP compliance does not address
- The privilege-secrecy framework is not automatically preserved when client data is processed in AI systems outside the firm's direct control
- The FDPIC has confirmed nFADP applies directly to AI-supported data processing, including in legal services contexts

**The structural problem:** The ICP's clients' files are in AI platforms the ICP did not choose and whose data processing terms the ICP has not evaluated. The privilege framework was designed for a world where the attorney controlled the infrastructure. That world is gone.

### 5. Professional Liability Insurance — Carrier Attestation

**Status:** Third-generation carrier questionnaires now in circulation. Signing managing partner commits personally to AI review methodology adequacy.

**Relevance for the ICP:**

- The attestation commits the signatory personally to a methodology standard that has not been defined
- No Geneva authority has published what "adequate" means for a 2–5 attorney firm
- No service exists to review the attestation from the signatory's personal liability perspective before signing
- Retroactive documentation of AI methodology is structurally impossible — the attestation was signed without documentation infrastructure in place

**The gap:** The carrier form is written by carrier lawyers. The ICP signed it without understanding what the signature commits them to. If a claim arises, the carrier will read the attestation against the firm's documentation. The firm has no documentation that addresses what the attestation actually says.

### 6. Council of Europe AI Convention (CETS No. 225)

**Status:** Federal Council deliberation underway. Switzerland considering ratification. Consultation draft for domestic implementation expected end of 2026. Geneva cantonal representatives supporting ratification.

**Relevance for the ICP:**

- If Switzerland ratifies, domestic implementation will create additional obligations for professional services firms
- The Convention's human-rights-centric approach aligns with the accountability framework the ICP is struggling to demonstrate
- Geneva's positioning as AI governance standard-setter is directly affected by whether Switzerland ratifies or maintains observer-only status
- First-mover advantage in establishing AI governance documentation infrastructure will be significant as implementation timeline crystallizes

### 7. Geneva Civil Procedure (CPTG) — AI Disclosure Requirements

**Status:** CPTG amendments under discussion. OCEP enforcement has begun — first penalties issued under AI disclosure provisions.

**Relevance for the ICP:**

- Mandatory AI system disclosure in written submissions
- AI-drafted document identification requirements
- Stylometric detection triggering enforcement in documented cases

**The structural problem:** Opposing counsel are requesting formal disclosure of AI use in case preparation. No Geneva court has published what adequate disclosure looks like. The first responses in specific TAC chambers are creating operative precedent before any standard exists. The ICP is forming precedent by default — and may be forming it incorrectly.

### 8. EU AI Act — Extraterritorial Reach

**Status:** In force. Annex I high-risk classification creates conformity assessment obligations for providers.

**Relevance for the ICP:**

- Geneva-based law firms serving EU clients may qualify as EU AI Act "providers" or "deployers" depending on where AI systems are operated and who controls outputs
- EU AI Act does not directly apply to Swiss domestic legal services, but cross-border AI-enabled services to EU clients create exposure
- Swiss companies placing AI systems on EU market or whose AI outputs are used in EU are subject to EU AI Act
- ISO/IEC 42001 (AI management systems) is emerging as voluntary attestation mechanism that demonstrates responsible AI governance in a way that is internationally recognized

## The Compliance Gap: What "Adequate" Means at Boutique Scale

The central compliance problem for the ICP is this: the SBA says AI governance is the attorney's personal responsibility. The nFADP says processing registers and documentation must exist. The carriers say AI review methodology must be adequate. The Geneva label requires documentation infrastructure.

None of these authorities have defined what "adequate" means for a 2–5 attorney firm.

The ICP is required to demonstrate compliance with standards that have not been published. This is not a compliance gap in the technical sense — it is a regulatory vacuum at the boutique scale. The standards exist for large firms with compliance departments. They do not exist for the managing partner who is personally accountable and has no methodology to point to.

The June 19 SBA Day of Lawyers is likely to surface this vacuum explicitly. The question "what does adequate AI governance look like for a small firm" will be asked, and the answer from the SBA may be: "that's your responsibility to determine."

This creates the specific advisory opportunity: helping the ICP understand what they committed to, what documentation they need, and how to build it before the next accountability occasion arrives.
