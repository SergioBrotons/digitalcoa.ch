## English Version

**The managing partner who signed something they cannot explain.**

I signed the carrier AI attestation form in early 2025. The platform we were using came with our practice management software — I didn't evaluate it separately, I didn't read the terms update when it came in March, and I didn't build a methodology before I put my signature on a form that committed me personally to AI review adequacy.

I didn't know what I was committing to. That's the honest answer.

What I know now: the form I signed commits me to a methodology I cannot describe. My junior associate runs the AI tools with fluency I don't have. I am signing work product I cannot independently evaluate. Client files from the past twelve months are in systems I did not consciously choose. Processing registers do not exist. And in approximately two weeks, I will be in a room at the SBA Day of Lawyers where AI governance will be discussed by peers — peers who also signed something, and who are also not sure what they signed.

The problem is not that I made a decision. The problem is that the decision was made before I understood what it committed me to, and the window for doing it correctly has closed.

Here's what I have learned since:

**The attestation you signed is a personal commitment, not a compliance checkbox.** The carrier form was written by carrier lawyers to protect carrier interests. When you signed it, you committed to a standard that has not been defined. If a claim arises, the carrier will read your attestation against your documentation. Your documentation — if it exists — will be evaluated against what you promised the system would do. If you can't describe the methodology, the attestation doesn't protect you. It exposes you.

**Retroactive documentation is not possible.** The window for building AI governance infrastructure was before you deployed the tools. That window is closed. What you can do now is document what the systems are currently doing, establish the baseline, and build the framework that would allow you to explain your AI use to a client, a carrier, or a court — if asked.

**The June 19 SBA event is an accountability occasion, not an advisory session.** The managing partners who will be in that room are not there to learn about AI generally. They are there because they signed something, and they need to understand what they signed before they walk into a room where it may come up.

**The Geneva label (Label de Bonne Gouvernance IA) is a market signal that is coming.** Twelve Geneva firms are already committed. If you are not working toward the same destination, you will be identifiable as a firm that has not engaged. Q3 2026 is the launch. If you want to be ready, the work needs to start now — not because of the label itself, but because the label represents what clients, carriers, and courts will begin to expect as a baseline.

I built DigitalCoa specifically for this problem: the managing partner who needs to understand their current exposure, make the documentation exist that should have existed, and be able to stand behind their AI decisions when accountability arrives.

The first conversation is a pre-briefing call. I ask specific questions about your current situation — carrier forms, AI tool use, documentation state, team structure. The purpose is not to sell you something. The purpose is to understand whether what I do is actually what you need. If it is, we talk about next steps. If it isn't, I tell you that too.

If you are a managing partner at a small Geneva law firm, you have probably already made more AI commitments than you realize. The question is not whether to engage with this. The question is whether to do it before the next accountability occasion arrives — or after.

— Sergio
