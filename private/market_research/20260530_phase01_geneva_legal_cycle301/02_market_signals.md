# Phase 01 — Market Signals: Geneva Legal (2–5 Attorneys)
## Cycle 301 | 2026-05-30

**ICP:** Managing partner or senior associate at a 2–5 attorney Geneva law firm. Personal accountability for AI decisions. June 19 SBA Day of Lawyers ~2 weeks away.

---

## Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

### 1. The attestation debt is now due

The ICP signed carrier attestation forms in 2024–2025 without understanding what they were committing to. The June 19 SBA Day of Lawyers is creating the first public accountability moment since those signatures were made. The problem is not that they signed something — it is that what they signed commits them personally to a methodology they cannot describe, and the June 19 discussion will surface this in a room of peers.

The signal: Managing partners who attended SBA events in 2024 are now specifically not attending the June 19 event, because they know the topic will come up and they are not prepared. Those who are attending are those who believe they have something to say — which may be worse, because what they have to say may not hold up to scrutiny.

### 2. The junior-senior AI fluency inversion is structural and unresolved

The attorney with the most AI knowledge at a 2–5 attorney firm is typically the most junior. The attorney with the most professional accountability is the managing partner who cannot independently evaluate what they are signing off on. This is not a training gap — it is a structural design problem.

The problem compounds when the managing partner signs off on work product generated by AI tools they cannot evaluate. The signature is real. The evaluation capability is not. This creates a specific failure mode: work product exists that the signing attorney cannot explain, authenticated by a signature that the attorney cannot stand behind.

### 3. Retroactive documentation is structurally impossible — and the window closed

The ICP started using AI tools 6–18 months ago. Client files from that period are in AI systems. Processing registers do not exist. AI audit trails were not maintained. The window for creating this infrastructure correctly was before the AI tools were deployed. That window is closed.

What the ICP is now living with: a series of AI-assisted matters where the research methodology, the query logs, the output authentication, and the client communication documentation are not reconstructable. If a client exercises Article 21 rights, if a carrier asks about AI use in a claim, if a court requests disclosure — the documentation does not exist and cannot be created retroactively.

### 4. Geneva courts are now asking AI disclosure questions formally

Opposing counsel in Geneva civil proceedings are beginning to request formal disclosure of AI use in case preparation. No published standard exists for what "adequate" AI disclosure means. The first responses to these requests in specific TAC chambers are creating de facto precedent before any published standard exists.

The ICP is caught between: disclosing AI use in ways that may undermine case position, or failing to disclose in ways that may constitute professional misconduct. Neither option is comfortable, and neither has clear guidance.

### 5. nFADP enforcement posture is shifting — documentation architecture matters now

FDPIC enforcement is moving from guidance letters to operational consequences. Small Geneva firms are operating with compliance infrastructure designed for the prior posture — backward-facing, retrospective, focused on whether documentation exists rather than whether the documentation architecture is designed for current enforcement expectations.

The specific gap: AI tool processing registers, client data subject access request protocols for AI-processed data, and documentation that demonstrates the firm knew what its AI systems were doing with client data.

### 6. The Geneva legal AI governance certification is creating a two-tier signal

The Label de Bonne Gouvernance IA being developed for Geneva law firms (TÜV Switzerland third-party audit, Q3 2026 launch) is creating a market signal that will be visible. Firms that have it will be distinguishable from firms that do not.

The problem for the ICP: the label requires documentation infrastructure that the ICP does not currently have. Getting the label in Q3 2026 requires work to begin now. But beginning now means confronting what was not done in the prior 12–18 months of AI tool use.

### 7. Vendor exit barriers are creating compounding exposure

The ICP chose AI platforms through practice management software bundles, not through deliberate AI governance evaluation. Those platforms have updated their terms multiple times. Client data is in those systems. The ICP cannot easily exit those systems without disrupting client service continuity.

The compounding problem: each platform update changes the data processing terms. Each change creates new attestation surface area. The ICP is now locked into data processing relationships that were never consciously chosen and that create ongoing compliance exposure.

### 8. The June 19 SBA event is creating forced self-assessment

The ICP did not choose to evaluate their AI governance. The June 19 SBA Day of Lawyers is forcing that evaluation. The question is no longer "should I think about this" — it is "what do I say when I'm in the room."

This is creating a specific behavioral pattern: managing partners who are now quietly trying to reconstruct what their AI systems actually did, without being able to do so. The forced self-assessment is arriving before the reconstruction capability exists.

## Key Market Signal: Consequence Management Has Arrived

The C300 finding that market crossed from awareness gap to consequence management is now confirmed in the legal ICP specifically:

- Attestations signed without methodology exist → carrier liability exposure exists → managing partner personal exposure is live
- AI tool use without documentation exists → nFADP compliance gap exists → FDPIC enforcement exposure is active
- Client data in AI systems without processing registers → Article 21 request exposure exists → documentation cannot be retroactively created
- AI research without authentication trails → court disclosure request exposure exists → precedent being formed without guidance

The ICP is not asking whether they have a problem. They are asking what the problem costs.
