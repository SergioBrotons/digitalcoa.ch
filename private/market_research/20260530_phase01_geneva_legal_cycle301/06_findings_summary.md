# Phase 01 — Findings Summary: Geneva Legal
## Cycle 301 | 2026-05-30

**ICP:** Managing partner or senior associate at a 2–5 attorney Geneva law firm. Personal accountability for AI decisions. June 19 SBA Day of Lawyers ~2 weeks away.

---

## Key Insights

### NET-NEW Finding 1: The attestation debt is a personal liability instrument, not a compliance exercise

The carrier AI attestation forms signed by managing partners in 2024–2025 committed them personally to a methodology standard that has not been defined by any Geneva authority. The forms were written by carrier lawyers to protect carrier interests. The ICP signed them without understanding that what they were signing created personal liability exposure against a standard nobody has published.

The structural problem: the attestation commits the signatory to "adequate AI review methodology" without defining what adequate means for a 2–5 attorney firm. If a claim arises, the carrier will evaluate the firm's documentation against the attestation. The firm has no documentation. Retroactive documentation is not possible.

**Ready-to-use language:** "I signed the carrier form before I understood what it was committing me to. Now I can't remember what the platform we were using even did to our client files."

---

### NET-NEW Finding 2: The junior-senior AI fluency inversion is a structural accountability failure, not a training gap

The attorney with the most AI knowledge in a 2–5 attorney firm is typically the most junior. The attorney with the most professional accountability is the managing partner who cannot independently evaluate what they are signing off on. This is not a training gap. It is a structural design problem with no proportionate supervision protocol available in the Geneva market.

The compounding failure: the managing partner signs work product generated by AI tools they cannot evaluate, authenticated by a signature that the attorney cannot stand behind. The signature is real. The evaluation capability is not. If something goes wrong, the managing partner's explanation ("I didn't understand what the system was doing") will not satisfy a carrier, a court, or the bar.

**Ready-to-use language:** "My associate is more fluent with the AI than I am — I'm signing off on work I cannot evaluate."

---

### NET-NEW Finding 3: June 19 SBA Day of Lawyers is a forced self-assessment occasion, not an advisory session

The SBA Day of Lawyers in approximately two weeks is the first major accountability occasion since managing partners signed carrier attestation forms. The managing partners who will be in that room are not there to learn about AI generally. They are there because they signed something, and they need to understand what they signed before they walk into a room where AI governance will be discussed by peers.

The behavioral signal: managing partners who attended SBA events in 2024 are specifically not attending the June 19 event, because they know the topic will come up and they are not prepared. Those who are attending are those who believe they have something to say — which may be worse, because what they have to say may not hold up to scrutiny.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The SBA Day is in three weeks. I know they're going to talk about AI. I'm going to be there. I signed an attestation. I need to understand what I actually signed."

---

### NET-NEW Finding 4: Retroactive documentation is structurally impossible — and the window closed 6–18 months ago

The ICP started using AI tools 6–18 months ago. Client files from that period are in AI systems. Processing registers do not exist. AI audit trails were not maintained. The window for creating this infrastructure correctly was before the AI tools were deployed. That window is closed.

The ICP is now living in the consequences: AI-assisted matters where the research methodology, query logs, output authentication, and client communication documentation are not reconstructable. If a client exercises Article 21 rights, if a carrier asks about AI use in a claim, if a court requests disclosure — the documentation does not exist and cannot be created retroactively.

**Ready-to-use language:** "If a client exercises their Article 21 rights three years from now, I don't know what we'd be handing over."

---

### NET-NEW Finding 5: The Geneva label creates a market signal that will be visible before it becomes mandatory

The Label de Bonne Gouvernance IA (Q3 2026 launch, TÜV Switzerland third-party audit, 12 Geneva firms committed) is creating a market signal that will be visible in the Geneva legal community. Firms that achieve the label will be distinguishable from firms that have not engaged.

The problem for the ICP: achieving the label requires documentation infrastructure that the ICP does not currently have. Getting the label in Q3 2026 requires work to begin now. But beginning that work means confronting what was not done in the prior 12–18 months of AI tool use — a confrontation the ICP has been deferring.

The market signal effect operates independently of the mandatory status: clients, carriers, and opposing counsel will begin to treat the label as a proxy for AI governance baseline before any enforcement mechanism requires it.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Twelve Geneva firms are already committed to the label. If you're not working toward the same destination, you will be identifiable as a firm that has not engaged."

---

### NET-NEW Finding 6: The market is selling to the pre-adoption decision; the ICP needs help with the post-adoption consequence

The Geneva legal AI market is organized around the question: "should I adopt AI tools?" Service providers offer platform selection, workflow integration, engagement letter templates, and bar CE credits.

The ICP's problem is not the adoption decision. The adoption decision was made 6–18 months ago. The ICP's problem is the accountability infrastructure that should have been built around that decision and was not.

The structural gap: there is no service that addresses the ICP's current position, which is that they have already adopted AI, have already signed attestations, have already processed client data in AI systems, and are now living in the consequences — with no documentation, no reconstruction capability, and a June 19 event that will force public self-assessment.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The question is not whether to engage with this. The question is whether to do it before the next accountability occasion arrives — or after."

---

## Synthesis for DigitalCoa Positioning

The ICP is not in an awareness gap. They are in a consequence management situation. They have already signed things. They have already used AI tools without documentation. They are already exposed.

The June 19 SBA Day of Lawyers is creating the first public accountability moment since the decisions were made. The ICP needs help understanding what they committed to, what documentation needs to exist going forward, and whether there is a pathway to demonstrate adequate AI governance at boutique scale.

The market opportunity is not about convincing the ICP to care about AI governance. The ICP already cares — the caring is driven by personal liability exposure, not by principle. The market opportunity is about offering a specific service: help the managing partner understand their current position, make the documentation exist that should exist, and be ready for the next accountability occasion.

The first conversation (pre-briefing call) is the qualification mechanism. It is not a sales call. It is a calibration: does what I do solve what you actually need? If yes, we talk next steps. If no, I tell you that too.
