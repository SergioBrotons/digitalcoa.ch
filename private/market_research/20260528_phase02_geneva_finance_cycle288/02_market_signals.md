# 02 — Market Signals: Geneva Finance — Boutique Wealth Management

## Phase 02 | Cycle 288 | 2026-05-28

---

## Current Problems, Complaints, Failures

---

### Problem Pattern 1: Institutional Client AI Questionnaire First-Shock

Boutique wealth managers in Geneva are receiving institutional client AI governance questionnaires for the first time — often 30–50 pages, detailed in structure, asking for practices the firm has never documented.

**Signal**: The institutional client is operating from a global due diligence framework developed in London or New York, applied without Geneva calibration. The boutique has no template for response. The first response is either silence (damaging for the mandate) or a reconstructed narrative assembled after the question arrives (inconsistent, exposing).

**Acute pain window**: Once the first questionnaire arrives, the MD realizes the portfolio-wide exposure. The institutional client sets a benchmark the boutique didn't know was forming. Future questionnaires from other institutional clients will use the first response as a reference point.

**What the market is not providing**: Pre-arrival response architecture. No Geneva service offers a simulation of what an institutional client would ask given the firm's actual AI tool stack and portfolio construction process.

---

### Problem Pattern 2: Carrier Third-Generation Attestation Personal Signature Without Pre-Signing Review

PI insurance renewals now include AI attestation sections requiring personal MD signature. The third-generation format asks for documentation the boutique does not have, before a personal liability commitment the MD has not evaluated.

**Signal**: The carrier's form is written by the carrier's legal team. It commits the signatory to representations about practices the signatory did not design and may not accurately describe. The MD is asked to warrant adequacy against a carrier-defined standard that is not published as a guidance document.

**Acute pain window**: Pre-renewal period. The form arrives 60–90 days before renewal. The MD must sign to maintain coverage. There is no Geneva market service offering pre-signing personal liability review of carrier AI attestation forms for wealth management boutiques.

**What the market is not providing**: Pre-signing review methodology. A proportionate analysis of what the attestation commits the signatory to personally, what evidence the form actually requires, what gap exists between current practice and attestation requirements, and what the MD's actual personal exposure is under Swiss professional liability doctrine.

---

### Problem Pattern 3: Portfolio Construction AI Accountability Void

AI-assisted portfolio construction is the highest-consequence AI use case in the boutique wealth management firm. It is also the least governed, least documented, and least named as a discrete accountability category.

**Signal**: The MD is personally accountable for portfolio construction decisions. AI tools are now contributing to allocation recommendations. The MD does not have a documented review process for AI-assisted allocation decisions. The engagement letter does not define the attribution obligations for AI-assisted recommendations. The carrier questionnaire asks about AI governance in terms that do not distinguish between use cases with different accountability weights.

**Acute pain window**: When an institutional client asks specifically about portfolio construction AI methodology. When the carrier asks for three examples of AI-assisted decisions and the human review that preceded each. When a client portfolio underperforms and the AI contribution to the decision is not documented.

**What the market is not providing**: Portfolio construction AI as a named discrete governance category with its own documentation requirements, review frequency, and attribution architecture proportionate to boutique wealth management scale.

---

### Problem Pattern 4: nFADP Art.63 + Art.21 + Carrier Attestation Convergence on MD

Three accountability streams are converging on the Geneva boutique wealth management MD simultaneously, each creating personal liability exposure, none coordinated.

**Signal**: nFADP Art.63 criminal liability for serious AI failures. nFADP Art.21 automated decision obligations for AI-influenced portfolio recommendations. Carrier attestation committing the MD personally to representations about AI practices. The MD has not received any guidance that maps how these three instruments interact, where they overlap, and what the cumulative personal exposure is.

**Acute pain window**: When an institutional client asks about nFADP compliance. When a client requests explanation of an AI-influenced decision under Art.21. When the carrier asks for evidence of human review of AI-assisted decisions.

**What the market is not providing**: Multi-obligation coherence mapping at boutique scale. A document showing which obligations interact, what satisfying one does to position on another, and what the cumulative personal MD exposure is across all three instruments.

---

### Problem Pattern 5: AI Model Update Silent Propagation

AI model updates propagate silently through active client mandates without firm awareness. Different clients in the same portfolio receive outputs from different AI model versions during the same period. The firm has no detection mechanism.

**Signal**: The AI vendor issues an update. The boutique receives no notification unless the vendor's changelog is monitored. Client reports for the same mandate, produced three weeks apart, may reflect different analytical conditions. The MD cannot confirm whether recommendations in active client use are based on the model version the original analysis assumed.

**Acute pain window**: When a client asks how a recommendation made six months ago holds under current conditions. When the carrier asks what model version was active when a specific decision was made. When an institutional client probes reproducibility of the firm's analytical framework.

**What the market is not providing**: Silent propagation detection architecture. A methodology for monitoring AI model versions across active mandates, detecting intra-portfolio inconsistency, and documenting the conditions under which each active recommendation was produced.

---

### Problem Pattern 6: SRO Guidance Vacuum as Operative Uncertainty

Geneva wealth management firms are supervised through SROs (Self-Regulatory Organizations). The SROs have not published AI guidance. The MD interprets this as either reassuring or ominous, depending on risk tolerance.

**Signal**: The MD calls the SRO. The SRO acknowledges the question, provides no written guidance, suggests waiting for FINMA. FINMA guidance 08/2024 exists but is calibrated for larger institutions. No Geneva cantonal SRO has published supplementary guidance for boutique wealth managers. The MD operates in operative uncertainty resolved by risk tolerance, not information.

**Acute pain window**: When a carrier renewal asks for documentation of SRO guidance followed. When an institutional client asks what SRO guidance the firm applies to AI governance. When the MD must explain to a board member why the firm's AI governance follows no published cantonal standard.

**What the market is not providing**: Geneva-specific SRO AI guidance gap mapped to actual boutique obligations. A document showing what the SRO silence means operationally, what it does not cover, and what the MD's actual discretion is in the absence of published guidance.

### Pattern Confirmation Against Prior Research

Patterns 1–3 above confirm findings from C280–C287: institutional questionnaire first-shock, carrier attestation without pre-signing review, portfolio construction AI as unnamed primary exposure. Patterns 4–6 focus on the convergence mechanism (three streams meeting on MD), silent propagation (distinct from general versioning), and SRO vacuum (distinct from formal doctrine absence).

- Pattern 5 isolates silent AI model update propagation as intra-portfolio consistency exposure distinct from general version staleness
