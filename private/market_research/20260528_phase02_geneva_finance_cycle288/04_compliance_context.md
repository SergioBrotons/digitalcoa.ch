## Geneva/Switzerland-Specific Regulatory Landscape

---

### 1. FINMA Supervisory Framework

**FINMA Guidance 08/2024 — AI in Financial Services**

FINMA published Guidance 08/2024 establishing expectations for AI use in supervised institutions. The guidance addresses:
- AI inventory and model risk management for licensed institutions
- AI governance framework expectations at institutional scale
- Supervisory expectations for AI risk management, including model validation, monitoring, and human oversight
- Documentation obligations for AI-assisted decision processes

**Geneva boutique calibration gap**: FINMA Guidance 08/2024 is calibrated for licensed institutions (banks, securities dealers, fund managers) with dedicated compliance and risk management functions. A 50–200 employee Geneva wealth management boutique operating under SRO supervision has no published guidance on how FINMA's expectations translate to its organizational scale. SROs have not published supplementary guidance addressing the boutique wealth management segment specifically.

**Key operative issue**: FINMA does not directly supervise most Geneva boutique wealth managers. SROs do. The operative question is whether FINMA's AI expectations flow through SRO supervisory frameworks, and whether SRO surveillance includes AI-specific review. The answer varies by SRO and is not published as an integrated framework.

**Practical consequence for the boutique MD**: The absence of cantonal supplementary guidance means the MD applies FINMA expectations without institutional translation. The MD is personally accountable for a governance standard that exists at institutional scale but has no methodology for proportionate implementation at boutique scale.

### 2. SRO (Self-Regulatory Organization) AI Guidance Landscape

**SRO-FINMA relationship for AI obligations**: SROs are recognized by FINMA as supervisory bodies for specific categories of financial intermediaries (particularly asset managers not requiring direct FINMA licensing). The SRO framework creates a two-level supervisory structure: SRO supervisees operate under SRO rules, which must meet FINMA minimum standards.

**Current SRO posture**: No Geneva SRO has published specific AI guidance for wealth management boutiques in 2025–2026. SRO webpages and circulars acknowledge digital transformation and AI as emerging risks without operationalizing specific obligations. Market participants interpret SRO silence as either absence of obligation or administrative gap — the distinction matters, but cannot be resolved without published SRO clarification.

**SRO as operative standard-setter under absence conditions**: In the absence of published SRO AI guidance, the carrier's AI attestation questionnaire operates as the de facto operative standard for the Geneva wealth management sector. This displacement mechanism — carrier questionnaire filling the SRO guidance vacuum — was identified in prior Phase 02 cycles (C287/C286) and is confirmed as structural in the current compliance landscape. The MD signs a carrier attestation form written by the carrier's legal team; this form functionally determines what adequate AI governance means for the sector, without Geneva consulting, SRO, or legal community input.

**Practical consequence**: The absence of SRO guidance is not neutral. It creates conditions where the carrier's attestation form sets the operative standard for the entire Geneva wealth management sector, regardless of SRO, FINMA, or nFADP doctrine. The MD seeking to understand their obligations faces a three-layered void: no published SRO guidance, no Geneva-specific FINMA translation, and a carrier form designed by parties with no Geneva wealth management input.

### 3. nFADP Automated Decision Obligations

**Art.21 — Right to Explanation for Automated Decisions**

nFADP Art.21 grants data subjects the right to request explanation of automated decisions, including AI-influenced decisions. In the wealth management context, this applies to:
- AI-influenced investment recommendations in client reports
- AI-generated allocation suggestions embedded in mandate management
- Risk profiling and suitability assessments produced with AI assistance
- Performance attribution analysis using AI inference

**Boutique wealth manager exposure**: Most Geneva boutiques have not established a response protocol for Art.21 requests related to AI-influenced portfolio decisions. The obligation is present-tense; the infrastructure to respond is not. Fine of up to CHF 50,000 for violation of Art.21 requirements applies.

**Art.63 — Personal Criminal Liability**

nFADP Art.63 introduces personal criminal liability for serious violations of the act. For wealth management AI governance, this creates liability for the MD personally when AI-assisted processes produce serious data protection failures. The liability is personal, not corporate. The exposure is unnamed in Geneva wealth management professional discourse; no Geneva market participant has published a mapping of where Art.63 personal criminal liability attaches in the context of AI-assisted wealth management.

**Art.8 — Profiling Notification**

AI-assisted portfolio construction constitutes profiling under nFADP Art.8. Data subjects have the right to notification when profiling is used in relation to them, and the right to human review of profiling-based decisions. Geneva wealth management boutiques have not issued profiling notifications as a documented practice category. The obligation is present-tense for any boutique using AI in portfolio construction, regardless of firm awareness.

**Art.15 — AI Inference Accuracy**

nFADP Art.15 requires correction and erasure of inaccurate personal data. AI-generated inferences about client financial behavior may be inaccurate. Inaccurate AI inferences embedded in client-facing financial documents activate Art.15 obligations when a client relies on those inferences. The distinct question from model accuracy: Art.15 applies to inferred content about the specific client embedded in client-facing documents, independently of whether the AI model functions correctly in the abstract.

### 4. AI Convention (CoE Treaty — London, May 2025)

**Treaty status and Switzerland ratification process**: The Council of Europe AI Convention (Treaty of London, May 2025) was signed by Switzerland. The treaty creates binding obligations for signatory states on AI governance in private sector contexts. Implementation requires Swiss legislation; the ratification process is underway as of 2026.

**Deployer obligations flowing through existing mandate contracts**: The AI Convention creates obligations for "deployers" of AI systems — entities that use AI tools in their operations. For Geneva wealth management firms, AI tool deployment creates treaty obligations that flow through existing client mandate contracts and carrier underwriting terms without published implementation guidance. The obligations are present-tense in substance, awaiting legislative expression.

**Key implication for wealth management**: The MD who signed client mandate engagement letters before AI adoption, and who renewed PI insurance before carrier third-generation attestation arrived, may have treaty obligations operating through those documents that were not disclosed or anticipated. The documentation window that was open during adoption is not described anywhere as a treaty obligation window; yet the AI Convention deployer obligations may be measured from adoption date for ongoing mandates.

**Portfolio-level implications**: AI Convention obligations apply to each AI deployment. A boutique wealth manager with 40 active client mandates, each involving ongoing AI-assisted portfolio management, faces multiple concurrent treaty obligation streams. No Geneva service has produced an AI Convention compliance mapping for the Geneva wealth management boutique portfolio.

### 5. Carrier PI Insurance AI Attestation Evolution

**Third-generation attestation format**: Carrier AI attestation forms have evolved through three formats:
- First generation: Basic checkbox confirming AI tool use exists
- Second generation: Positive attestation of AI governance practices
- Third generation (current): Specific evidence requirements for AI-assisted decisions, including human review documentation, model performance evidence, and adherence to defined methodology

**Fourth-generation emergence signals**: Evidence from prior cycles (C287, C286, C285) indicates carriers are shifting from decision-logic documentation to reasoning-chain architecture — asking not just what the firm decided, but how the decision was constructed and what evidence the MD reviewed for each AI-assisted decision.

**ISAE 3000 limitation at boutique scale**: Carriers have introduced AWS 183-service ISAE 3000 reports as evidence of vendor AI controls. This documentation proves vendor controls, not the professional's own governance practice. The MD cannot present an ISAE 3000 as evidence of the firm's own AI methodology to an institutional client or a board member.

**Personal signature, personal liability**: The carrier attestation requires a personal MD signature. The form commits the signatory to representations about practices the signatory may not have designed, documented, or evaluated. Swiss professional liability doctrine does not provide a defined standard for what the MD's personal oversight of AI-assisted decisions must evidence. The gap between carrier questionnaire requirements and Swiss professional liability doctrine creates personal exposure that is not visible until a claim or inquiry arrives.

### 6. Documentation Window as Burden-of-Proof Positioning

**Structural timing property**: The documentation window has a specific timing property distinct from a compliance deadline. Before it closes, firms building documentation are evaluated against standards forming through informal circuit mechanisms. After it closes, firms explaining the absence of documentation are evaluated against standards that have arrived. New documentation created after the window closes cannot retroactively reconstruct the reasoning that would have existed during the window period.

**Burden-of-proof reversal**: The window creates a burden-of-proof reversal. The MD with documented AI governance architecture built before formal standards arrive has a fundamentally different legal and regulatory position than an MD who built the same documentation after standards arrived — even if the documentation content is identical. The timing of documentation is evidence of governance reasoning, not merely compliance completion.

**Practical implication**: The Geneva boutique wealth management MD must view AI governance documentation as a burden-of-proof positioning exercise, not a compliance checklist. The window is open now. Its closing conditions are determined by informal circuit standard formation mechanisms, carrier iteration velocity, and institutional client expectation crystallization — none of which publish explicit closing dates. The MD operates without knowing when the window closes.
