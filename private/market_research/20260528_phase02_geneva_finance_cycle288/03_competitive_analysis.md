# 03 — Competitive Analysis: Geneva Finance — Boutique Wealth Management

## Phase 02 | Cycle 288 | 2026-05-28

---

## What Exists in Market vs. What's Missing

---

### What Exists

**1. Swiss banking AI governance frameworks — large institution orientation**
FINMA Guidance 08/2024, AMAS digital asset guidance, and FINMA circulars on operational risk apply to institutions with dedicated compliance functions. The guidance documents assume governance infrastructure most Geneva boutiques do not have. The content is directionally accurate but not operationally translatable to 50–200 employee wealth management firms.

**2. Generic AI governance documentation templates**
AI governance templates produced by large consulting firms for use across industries. These templates describe comprehensive frameworks (AI ethics boards, model risk management committees, enterprise-level model inventories) that are structurally incompatible with boutique wealth management organizational reality. A 12-person firm cannot operate a model risk management committee.

**3. Technology vendor assurances**
AI vendors provide documentation about their own models, ISAE 3000 reports, and data processing agreements. These documents describe the vendor's controls, not the professional's governance practice. The boutique MD cannot use a vendor ISAE 3000 to answer an institutional client's question about the firm's own AI governance methodology. The documents serve different accountability purposes.

**4. FIDLEG/FINMA algorithmic trading disclosure requirements**
FIDLEG Art.26 requires disclosure of algorithmic routine trading for asset managers above certain thresholds. This obligation applies to specific categories and does not cover the full scope of AI-assisted wealth management boutique activities. Firms operating below the FIDLEG threshold have no equivalent disclosure framework.

**5. nFADP general data protection compliance services**
Market services exist for general nFADP compliance. These services cover data processing registration, data subject request infrastructure, and privacy policy documentation. They do not address the AI-inferred content in client-facing financial documents, the profiling notification obligations for AI-assisted portfolio construction, or the Art.21 automated decision explanation requirements.

---

### What's Missing (Structurally Absent from Geneva Market)

**1. Pre-signing carrier AI attestation review for wealth management boutiques**
No Geneva market service offers pre-signing personal liability review of carrier AI attestation forms for boutique wealth management MDs. The existing gap is specific: a service that evaluates what the attestation commits the signatory to personally, maps the gap between current practice and attestation requirements, assesses personal MD exposure under Swiss professional liability doctrine, and produces documentation the MD can present before signing. This service exists in no published Geneva market offering.

**2. Institutional client AI questionnaire response architecture**
No Geneva service offers pre-arrival simulation of what institutional clients ask about AI governance in wealth management contexts. No service offers response architecture that distinguishes between questions that can be answered directly, questions requiring documentation build before response is appropriate, and questions where the risk of the question itself must be managed before a response strategy is designed. This is an unnamed discrete service category.

**3. Geneva boutique-scale AI governance methodology proportionate to 50–200 employee wealth manager**
Comprehensive AI governance frameworks published by large institutions and international organizations assume organizational capacities and governance infrastructure that do not exist at boutique scale. No Geneva-specific methodology exists for the 50–200 employee wealth management firm with no dedicated CTO, no formal model risk management function, and an MD who is personally accountable for everything. Proportionate is not simplified. Proportionate means built for the actual organizational structure.

**4. Portfolio construction AI as discrete accountability category**
No service names portfolio construction AI as a discrete governance and documentation category with its own evidence requirements, review frequency, and attribution architecture. This is the highest-consequence AI use case in the firm. All AI governance services treat AI use as a unified category. The accountability requirements for AI-assisted portfolio construction are categorically different from AI-assisted compliance text production.

**5. Multi-obligation coherence mapping (nFADP + carrier + AI Convention)**
No Geneva market service produces a coherence mapping across the three accountability obligations converging on the boutique wealth management MD: nFADP Art.63 criminal liability, nFADP Art.21 automated decision obligations, and carrier attestation personal exposure. The obligations interact. Satisfying one affects position on another. No published mapping methodology addresses how these three instruments compound for the Geneva wealth management boutique specifically.

**6. AI model version tracking across active mandates**
No Geneva market service offers AI model version tracking methodology for active client mandate portfolios. The problem is not data migration (vendor lock-in exit). The specific problem is continuous monitoring: which model version is active across which mandates at any given moment, what evidence exists documenting the version-provenance of each active recommendation, and how intra-portfolio inconsistency is detected when AI model updates propagate silently.

**7. SRO guidance vacuum response strategy**
No Geneva service offers a methodology for operating under SRO guidance absence. The specific requirement is not "what does SRO guidance require" (the answer is: nothing published) but "what does the MD do in operative uncertainty when no guidance exists." The strategy is different from compliance with published guidance. This is an unnamed discrete advisory category.

**8. MD Chief AI Officer without infrastructure — accountability architecture**
The Geneva boutique wealth management MD is de facto the Chief AI Officer with no proportionate governance architecture. The structural problem is not leadershipvacuum. The problem is that the MD exercises accountability authority and has no institutional infrastructure supporting that authority — no documentation system for AI governance decisions, no decision trail, no periodic review architecture, no methodology for documenting what the MD as CAIO actually does in that role. A proportionate MD-as-CAIO documentation architecture is structurally absent from the Geneva market.

---

### Competitive Positioning Implication

The Geneva market for boutique wealth management AI governance advisory has no dominant competitor across any of these eight absent categories. The firms offering AI governance advice in Geneva are either:
- Large international consulting firms with frameworks scaled for institutions above the boutique range
- Technology vendors addressing their own tool governance, not the professional's accountability
- General nFADP compliance services covering data protection dimensions only

No Geneva-calibrated, boutique-scale, wealth-management-specific AI governance advisory practice has documented these eight structural absences. The positioning opportunity is defined by the absence of each.
