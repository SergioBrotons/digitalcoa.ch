### Six NET-NEW Findings Distinct from C214–C287 Phase 02

**Finding 01: Three-Stream Convergence Creating Compound MD Exposure Cluster**

Three accountability streams — nFADP Art.63 personal criminal liability, nFADP Art.21 automated decision explanation obligations, and carrier PI insurance third-generation attestation — are converging on the Geneva boutique wealth management MD simultaneously, each created by different institutional actors, none coordinated, none mapped.

The MD has not received guidance showing how these instruments interact, where they compound, or what the cumulative personal liability exposure is across all three. The market has no published document performing this mapping function for Geneva wealth management specifically.

*Ready-to-use language*: "Three instruments are meeting on you personally. nFADP Article 63 criminal liability. Article 21 explanation obligations for AI-influenced decisions in client reports. The carrier attestation you signed in your last renewal. Nobody has mapped how they interact — or where your personal exposure sits at the convergence point."

**Finding 02: Carrier Attestation Questionnaire Operating as De Facto Operative Standard Through SRO Guidance Vacuum**

Geneva SROs have not published AI guidance for wealth management boutiques. FINMA Guidance 08/2024 is calibrated for larger institutions. In the operative absence of published Swiss regulatory guidance, the carrier's AI attestation questionnaire functions as the de facto operative standard for what adequate AI governance means in Geneva wealth management.

The form is written by the carrier's legal team — parties with no Geneva boutique wealth management input. The MD signs it, committing to a carrier-defined adequacy standard that is not a published guidance document. This displacement mechanism is structural: the vacuum created by SRO guidance absence is filled by carrier form iteration.

*Ready-to-use language*: "There's no Geneva SRO guidance on AI. There's no published standard for what adequate AI governance means at boutique wealth management scale. So the carrier's form is doing that job. It's setting the operative standard — and it was written by people who have never managed a Geneva boutique."

**Finding 03: Silent AI Model Update Propagation as Intra-Portfolio Consistency Exposure**

AI model updates propagate silently through active client mandates without firm awareness. Different clients in the same portfolio receive outputs from different AI model versions during the same period. No Geneva methodology exists for monitoring AI model version state across active mandates, detecting intra-portfolio inconsistency, or documenting version-provenance for each active recommendation.

This is not a data migration problem (that would be named vendor lock-in exit). This is a continuous operational monitoring problem: the boutique cannot confirm what analytical conditions produced the recommendations currently in active client use.

*Ready-to-use language*: "The model updated. Nobody told you. The client recommendations in active use — that were written under conditions that no longer exist — you're now six months past the update with no way to know what changed. That's not a document quality problem. That's an active mandate exposure."

**Finding 04: Portfolio Construction AI as Discrete Named Accountability Category with Proportionate Boutique-Scale Methodology Absent**

Portfolio construction AI is the highest-consequence AI use case in the boutique wealth management firm. It is also the least documented, least governed, and most exposed. AI-assisted allocation recommendations are made with AI contribution. The MD cannot document the human review that preceded each AI-assisted recommendation because no review process exists.

No Geneva service has built portfolio construction AI as a discrete named accountability category with its own evidence requirements, review frequency, and attribution architecture proportionate to boutique wealth management scale. Existing services treat AI use as a unified category. The accountability requirements for AI-assisted allocation are categorically different from AI-assisted compliance text production.

*Ready-to-use language*: "Portfolio construction AI is where your most consequential decisions are being made with AI. It's also where your most acute documentation gap is. Nobody has built this as a discrete governance category for Geneva boutiques. We will."

**Finding 05: Documentation Window Close as Burden-of-Proof Positioning Exercise with Retroactive Reconstruction Impossible**

The documentation window has a specific timing property distinct from a compliance deadline. Before formal standards arrive, the MD building documentation is evaluated against standards forming through informal circuit mechanisms. After formal standards arrive, the MD explaining absent documentation is evaluated against arrived standards. The timing of documentation is evidence of governance reasoning. Building documentation after the window closes cannot retroactively reconstruct the reasoning that would have existed during the window.

The window's closing conditions are determined by carrier iteration velocity, institutional client expectation crystallization, and informal circuit standard formation — none of which publish explicit closing dates. The MD operates in genuine uncertainty about when the window closes, which means the burden of proof is highest for the MD who delays.

*Ready-to-use language*: "Documentation isn't a compliance completion exercise. It's a burden-of-proof positioning exercise. The MD who builds documentation before the standard arrives is not in the same position as the MD who builds the same documentation after. Even if the content is identical. The timing is the evidence."

**Finding 06: MD-as-Chief-AI-Officer Without Infrastructure — De Facto Role with No Proportionate Governance Architecture**

The Geneva boutique wealth management MD is de facto the Chief AI Officer. The role was never designed, never assigned, and never supported with institutional infrastructure. There is no documentation system for AI governance decisions made by the MD in that role. No decision trail. No periodic review architecture. No methodology for documenting what the MD actually does in the CAIO function.

The structural paradox: the firm most acutely requires a Chief AI Officer precisely because it has no institutional infrastructure to support the function. The MD who exercises accountability authority without documentation infrastructure has personal accountability exposure for governance actions that cannot be evidenced. No Geneva market service offers a proportionate MD-as-CAIO documentation architecture for the boutique wealth manager.

*Ready-to-use language*: "There is no CTO. There is no governance committee. There is no AI inventory maintained by a separate function. The AI decisions got made by default — by the people using the tools, or by nobody at all. You are the Chief AI Officer. There is no infrastructure for that role. That's the problem."
