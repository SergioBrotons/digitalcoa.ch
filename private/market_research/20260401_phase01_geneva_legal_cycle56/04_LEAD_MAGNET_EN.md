## The Three-Layer Problem

### Layer 1: FADP + Federal AI Ordinance (You're Already Subject To)

The revised Federal Act on Data Protection (FADP) requires human review for automated decisions that produce legal effects. The Federal Council's AI Ordinance — in force since January 1, 2025 — adds risk classification requirements for AI systems used in legal practice.

Together, these create a dual-track compliance obligation. If you're using AI in case assessment, client communication, or contract review without a documented framework, you're likely non-compliant. You just don't know it yet.

**What "automated decision" means in practice:** Any AI system that makes or materially influences a legal determination — including AI-assisted billing recommendations, case outcome probability tools, or contract clause analysis — triggers FADP Art. 21 human review requirements.

**What the AI Ordinance adds:** AI tools used in your practice are now classified by risk level. High-risk AI uses require documented human oversight. "Limited risk" uses require transparency disclosures. If you haven't assessed your current AI tools against this classification, you're operating without a required compliance record.

### Layer 2: The Swiss Bar Association Standard (June 2024)

The SBA Guidelines on AI Use (adopted June 2024) established a professional standard of care for Swiss lawyers using AI. The guidelines are non-binding, but they set the benchmark that a reasonable lawyer would observe.

In practice: if a client challenges your AI-assisted work, or if a court reviews your processes, the SBA Guidelines define what "adequate AI governance" looks like. No written policy means you're relying on unwritten, unvalidated practices to defend yourself.

### Layer 3: Your Malpractice Insurer Is Watching

Swiss legal malpractice insurers have begun flagging AI-assisted work in liability assessments. The key principle: **your firm is always liable for AI-assisted decisions, whether you reviewed them adequately or not.**

"It wasn't me, it was my AI" is not a valid defense.

The relevant question isn't "did the AI make an error?" — it's "did you have adequate oversight of the AI's output?" Without a documented governance framework, there's no answer to that question that protects you.

## The Envelope vs. Letter Framework

Here's a practical way to think about AI governance in your firm.

**Automating the envelope (safe to do now):**

- New matter intake: conflict check, ID document collection, mandate letter drafting
- Time recording: narrative support, entry reminders, billing preparation
- Invoice sequences: 30/60/90 day chasing automation
- Document formatting:毓, formatting, exhibit preparation
- Executed document tracking: signature follow-up triggers

These workflows don't touch privileged content. They don't make or influence legal determinations. They are safe to automate — and they're consuming 8–14 hours a week of lawyer and paralegal time.

**Automating the letter (needs governance first):**

- AI interpreting case facts for substantive legal advice
- AI drafting legal positions, memos, or court filings
- AI analyzing contracts for substantive terms and risk allocation
- AI generating client recommendations based on case assessment

These uses require a governance framework before automation. The governance framework itself — the human review checkpoint, the tool assessment record, the documentation — is what keeps you protected.

**Quantified opportunity:** One four-lawyer Geneva firm recovered CHF 34,440 per year from intake automation alone. The typical time-to-bill gap in small firms is 10–15%. These numbers are real, and they're achievable.

## Your AI Governance Checklist

Use this to assess your current position:

**1. AI Tool Inventory**
Do you have a documented list of every AI tool used in your practice, with the data flows and third-party processing agreements for each?

**2. Risk Classification**
Have you assessed each tool against the Federal AI Ordinance risk categories?

**3. Human Review Protocol**
For each AI use that touches client data or influences legal determinations, is there a documented human review checkpoint?

**4. FADP Transparency**
Are your AI-assisted client communications compliant with FADP Art. 26 transparency requirements — meaning clients know when they're interacting with AI?

**5. SBA Governance Documentation**
Do you have a written AI governance policy aligned with the SBA Guidelines (June 2024)?

**6. DPA Coverage**
Do you have executed Data Processing Agreements with every AI tool vendor that handles client data?

**7. Intake Automation Audit**
Have you assessed your new matter intake workflow for automation opportunity?

**8. Malpractice Review**
Has your firm discussed AI-assisted work with your malpractice insurer?

**9. Staff Training Record**
Do you have documented training for associates and staff on AI tool use and governance?

**10. Annual Review Cycle**
Is your AI governance documentation reviewed at least annually or when new tools are adopted?

**Score:**
- 0–3: Immediate attention required. AI use is creating unmanaged exposure.
- 4–6: Governance is developing. Significant gaps remain.
- 7–9: You're ahead of most Geneva firms. Focus on documentation and annual review.
- 10: Governance is in place. Maintain and monitor regulatory updates.
