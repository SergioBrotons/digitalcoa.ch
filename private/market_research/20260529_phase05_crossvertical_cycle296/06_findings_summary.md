# Findings Summary: Cross-Vertical Synthesis — C296 Phase 05

## 6 NET-NEW Findings: Distinct from C214–C295

---

### Finding 01 — Three-Party Delegation Chain as Universal Structural Exposure

**The finding:** Across all four verticals, the accountable professional has accountability for AI decisions made two steps down the delegation chain — with no agency over those decisions. The structural pattern is universal: partner/associate/consumer AI; MD/portfolio manager/AI engine; partner/sub-consultant/AI tool; physician/staff/AI system.

**Mechanism:** AI was adopted for productivity reasons before any governance evaluation. The adoption created distributed accountability that the accountable professional did not recognize when signing the attestation, the engagement letter, or the vendor agreement.

**Implication:** This is not a firm-specific failure. It is a structural feature of how AI was adopted across all four verticals simultaneously. The fix requires a delegation governance protocol that names where accountability sits across the chain — not a better policy template.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The person who signed the attestation doesn't control the AI decisions. That's the exposure no one's naming."

---

### Finding 02 — Attestation Stampede Effect Producing Self-Incriminating Evidence

**The finding:** Carrier attestation deadlines (2–4 weeks) create urgency that produces retroactive documentation using vendor templates. The attempt to comply creates evidence that will be used against the signing professional when the next incident occurs. The documentation produced under pressure satisfies the carrier's current form without satisfying the underlying accountability standard.

**Mechanism:** The carrier's form asks for evidence of human authorization. The boutique has 3 weeks to produce documentation. They use vendor-provided templates, sign them, and submit. The documentation satisfies the form. The underlying accountability exposure — contemporaneous evidence of human authorization for each AI-assisted decision — is not addressed. The documentation is evidence of an attempt to comply, not evidence of adequate governance.

**Implication:** Pre-signing review is distinct from post-signing documentation. The professional needs to understand what they are signing before they sign — not build documentation after the fact.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The carrier's form asks you to prove adequate governance. Three weeks of retroactive documentation proves you tried to comply. It doesn't prove you were compliant."

### Finding 03 — Documentation Architecture Impossibility as Structural Problem

**The finding:** The evidence standard implied by attestation forms requires contemporaneous documented human authorization for each AI-assisted decision. Boutique internal processes were not designed to produce this documentation. The gap is not procedural — it is architectural. Adding more forms or templates does not close the gap. The fix requires a different documentation architecture that is designed to produce this evidence from the moment AI is used.

**Mechanism:** The boutique's current process: AI makes a recommendation, the professional considers it, implements it, and documents the implementation. The documentation exists. The attestation requires: evidence that a human authorized the AI recommendation before implementation. The current process does not produce that evidence because the authorization decision is implicit, not documented.

**Implication:** The solution is not a better form. It is a different architecture — one where the authorization decision is documented at the moment it is made, not reconstructed retrospectively.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You're being asked to prove something that can't be proven with your current process. The problem isn't your process — it's that your process wasn't designed for this standard."

### Finding 04 — Cross-Mandate Coherence Paradox

**The finding:** Four independent documentation systems (FINMA Guidance, institutional questionnaire responses, nFADP ROPA/DPIA, carrier attestation evidence) describe the same AI governance reality with different evidence standards, update cycles, and disclosure scopes. No Geneva methodology exists for cross-referencing them. They are likely inconsistent without any systematic check. The boutique is managing four documentation systems simultaneously, each operating independently.

**Mechanism:** The FINMA guidance requires evidence across seven dimensions. The institutional questionnaire probes AI governance methodology. The nFADP ROPA documents data processing activities. The carrier attestation requires evidence of human authorization. All four describe the same AI tool usage, the same human decisions, the same documentation. None of them reference each other. A firm may be fully compliant with one system while appearing non-compliant with another.

**Implication:** A cross-mandate coherence methodology — one framework that satisfies multiple documentation requirements simultaneously — is the missing tool. This is distinct from any single-mandate documentation solution.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You're maintaining four separate documentation systems that describe the same reality. They're probably inconsistent. One coherent framework could satisfy all four simultaneously."

### Finding 05 — Formation Participation as Unclaimed Structural Moat

**The finding:** The standards that govern Geneva professional accountability are forming in rooms the ICP cannot access — Federal Council AI deliberation, FINMA/SRO frameworks, SAV/FMH guidance development. The ICP is subject to standards formed without their participation. No competitor has claimed participation in these formation rooms as a positioning statement.

**Mechanism:** Formation participation generates intelligence about what standards are actually being set, why, and what they will require. This intelligence cannot be obtained from published regulations. The intelligence compounds into positioning advantage: the practitioner who knows what the standards will require before they are published can advise clients with prescience, not just compliance.

**Implication:** Formation participation is the primary competitive moat. It cannot be replicated by content investment, methodology publication, or price competition. The first advisor to publicly claim this position with documented participation will own it.

**Ready-to-use language:** "I participate in the Bern deliberations where these standards are being set. I know what the regulators are actually trying to accomplish — and what they'll accept."

### Finding 06 — Insurance Market as Present-Tense Commercial Distortion

**The finding:** Insurance brokers across all four verticals are already pricing AI governance risk into renewals. This is happening before any standardized SMB framework exists. The non-standardized underwriting creates current commercial distortion: firms with documentation are treated differently from firms without. This is live and immediate, not future risk.

**Mechanism:** The broker asks: what AI tools do you use, do you have documented AI governance, have you had AI-related incidents. The boutique cannot answer most of these questions. The broker notes the absence. The premium increases or the firm is declined. The distortion is present-tense: firms without documentation are paying a premium right now.

**Implication:** Insurance underwriting documentation is an immediate entry point. The broker's questions create urgency. The professional needs to answer the broker's questions with documentation before the renewal — not after the premium is quoted.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your insurance broker is pricing your AI risk right now. The question is whether you want to be the firm with documentation or without it when the quote comes in."
