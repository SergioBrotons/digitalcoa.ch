## What Exists in the Market vs. What's Missing

### Existing Market Offerings

#### 1. Large International Consultancies (Big Four)
**What they offer:**
- Generic AI governance frameworks designed for financial institutions
- Global compliance templates calibrated for large banks
- AI governance documentation bundles covering general regulatory requirements

**Gaps for Geneva boutique WM:**
- Designed for institutions that don't exist at 50–200 employee boutique scale
- Pricing structure assumes global firm budget
- No Geneva calibration, no SRO contextualization
- Framework documents not proportionate to boutique operational reality
- Personal MD liability not addressed as discrete service category

**Why it fails the ICP:** Big Four frameworks are enterprise tools for enterprise problems. The boutique WM MD sitting alone with a carrier attestation form needs proportionate methodology, not enterprise framework adaptation.

---

#### 2. Swiss Domestic Banks' Internal Guidance
**What they offer:**
- Internal AI governance standards for internal use
- Some published thought leadership on AI in wealth management

**Gaps for Geneva boutique WM:**
- Designed for internal bank operations, not external boutique distribution
- No competitive differentiation concern (not competing for institutional mandates)
- Institutional client questionnaire response framework not addressed
- Carrier attestation pre-signing review not offered as service

**Why it fails the ICP:** Internal bank guidance is internal documentation architecture. Doesn't translate to boutique external accountability environment where institutional clients and carriers are the accountability counterparties.

---

#### 3. Technology Vendors' Compliance Guides
**What they offer:**
- Vendor-published AI governance compliance guides
- Platform-specific documentation templates
- "Getting started with AI compliance" materials

**Gaps for Geneva boutique WM:**
- Written to sell platform, not to serve professional liability
- "Swiss hosted" marketing doesn't resolve processing location questions
- Satisfies vendor's commercial interest, not boutique's liability exposure
- No carrier attestation pre-signing review, no personal MD liability analysis
- Processor chain disclosure obligations not addressed from boutique perspective

**Why it fails the ICP:** Vendor guides serve vendor commercial objectives. The MD's question "what does this mean for my personal liability?" is not answered by documentation designed to sell platform access.

---

#### 4. Regulatory Publications (FINMA, SRO)
**What they offer:**
- FINMA Guidance 08/2024 on AI in financial services
- SRO regulatory frameworks for WM sector

**Gaps for Geneva boutique WM:**
- Guidance written for financial institution scale, not boutique scale
- No proportionate methodology for 50–200 employee WM boutique
- SRO has been completely silent on AI — no Geneva-specific interpretation
- No carrier attestation pre-signing review calibration
- nFADP obligations not integrated with FINMA guidance
- Institutional client questionnaire context not addressed

**Why it fails the ICP:** Regulatory publications set the accountability landscape but don't map it for boutique operations. MDs read FINMA guidance and don't know what "adequate AI governance" means for their specific situation.

---

#### 5. nFADP/FINMA Compliance Documentation Templates
**What they offer:**
- Data protection impact assessments
- ROPA templates
- Art.26 processor agreement templates

**Gaps for Geneva boutique WM:**
- General data protection compliance, not AI-specific accountability
- No integration with carrier attestation requirements
- Institutional client questionnaire not addressed
- Personal MD liability under Art.63 not mapped to documentation requirements
- Cross-mandate coherence paradox not addressed (four independent systems)

**Why it fails the ICP:** Data protection templates address data protection obligations. AI accountability in portfolio management context involves different evidence standards, different accountability counterparties, different timing pressures.

### What's Actually Missing — Market White Space

#### 1. Pre-Signing Carrier Attestation Review — Personal Liability Analysis
**The gap:** No Geneva market service offers pre-signing review of carrier attestation instruments for boutique WM MDs. The form is designed by carrier underwriting teams, commits MD to undefined adequacy standard, creates personal liability. No independent analysis available before signature.

**Why it exists:** Service requires Geneva-specific calibration (SRO context, FINMA expectations, nFADP obligations, carrier instrument variation). Generic compliance consultants don't have this calibration. Legal counsel typically addresses contract terms, not adequacy standard analysis.

**Market opportunity:** Pre-signing review as discrete service category defined by structural timing property (before personal signature on undefined adequacy standard) with Geneva-calibrated analysis unavailable elsewhere.

---

#### 2. Cross-Mandate Documentation Coherence Architecture
**The gap:** Four independent documentation systems (FINMA Guidance, institutional client questionnaires, nFADP ROPA/DPIA, carrier attestation evidence) describing same AI governance reality with no cross-reference methodology.

**Why it exists:** Each documentation system developed independently. No service category addresses multi-obligation documentation coherence for boutique WM. Each consultant addresses their domain (data protection, regulatory compliance, legal).

**Market opportunity:** Cross-reference architecture as discrete service category. Boutique managing four parallel systems needs integration methodology, not four separate documentation programs.

---

#### 3. Portfolio Construction AI Authorization Chain Documentation
**The gap:** Sequential chaining documentation from AI output to human authorization moment with own evidence standard. None of current AI governance frameworks address chaining documentation as discrete requirement.

**Why it exists:** Chaining documentation requires operational record architecture most boutiques never built. Institutional clients beginning to ask specifically about authorization moments, not policy documents or tool inventory.

**Market opportunity:** Authorization chain documentation methodology proportionate to boutique WM operations. Not enterprise-level compliance program, boutique-scale sequential integrity documentation for portfolio decision AI.

---

#### 4. Agentic AI Authorization Architecture for Continuous Operations
**The gap:** Authorization architecture designed for discrete decisions incompatible with continuous AI operation. No Geneva methodology exists for proportionate continuous authorization documentation.

**Why it exists:** Agentic AI is new operational reality. Authorization frameworks designed for human decisions made at discrete moments. Agentic AI makes decisions continuously — documentation model built for discrete events cannot serve continuous operation.

**Market opportunity:** Proportionate methodology for continuous AI authorization documentation at boutique WM scale. Architectural solution (not documentation improvement) required.

---

#### 5. Multi-Carrier Attestation Coherence Analysis
**The gap:** Boutiques with multiple carrier relationships receiving non-standardized attestation instruments with compound non-standardized exposure across simultaneous carrier relationships. No service maps cross-carrier documentation coherence.

**Why it exists:** Multi-carrier relationship complexity is boutique-specific problem. Large consultancies don't target boutique WM segment. No Geneva-specific service addresses multi-carrier attestation coherence.

**Market opportunity:** Cross-carrier coherence analysis as discrete service. Mapping documentation obligations across carriers, identifying conflicts, building coherent position that satisfies all simultaneously.

---

#### 6. Institutional Client Questionnaire Response Framework
**The gap:** Institutional clients sending second-generation questionnaires with sophisticated questions about portfolio construction AI, authorization chains, model version tracking. Boutiques responding without triage taxonomy or response strategy.

**Why it exists:** Questionnaire response treated as compliance exercise, not strategic communication. No distinction between questions that can be answered immediately, questions requiring pre-documentation build, and questions where disclosure risk exceeds response value.

**Market opportunity:** Questionnaire triage methodology with response strategy per question category. Boutique protecting competitive intelligence while satisfying legitimate client due diligence.

---

#### 7. Vendor Termination Compliance Continuity Planning
**The gap:** Compliance infrastructure (nFADP authorizations, purpose limitation records, profiling notifications, access logs, retention schedules) maintained through vendor relationship, not independently firm-owned. Gap revealed at termination notice.

**Why it exists:** Vendor terms define compliance infrastructure. Boutique assuming vendor maintains compliance artifacts without independent verification. Termination scenario forces discovery of vendor-dependence.

**Market opportunity:** Pre-termination compliance continuity assessment. Mapping which compliance artifacts are vendor-dependent vs. firm-owned, building transition architecture before termination forces action.

---

#### 8. nFADP Art.8 Profiling Notification Implementation
**The gap:** AI-assisted portfolio construction profiling notification obligation present-tense but unnamed. No Geneva methodology for implementing Art.8 notification at boutique WM scale.

**Why it exists:** Art.8 profiling notification is new accountability category for boutique WM. AI systems inferring client characteristics from transaction data creating profiling that triggers notification rights. Obligation exists regardless of awareness.

**Market opportunity:** Art.8 profiling notification implementation methodology proportionate to boutique WM. Client notification for AI-influenced profiling as discrete compliance category with own documentation requirements.

## Competitive Moat Analysis

### DigitalCoa.ch Position

**Moat components:**
1. Geneva accountability ecosystem participation — pattern knowledge from sustained informal circuit participation, non-replicable through methodology documents or expertise claims
2. Cross-vertical synthesis intelligence — accountability attribution problem structurally similar across legal/finance/consulting/NGO, pattern reveals same solution architecture across verticals
3. Pre-signing review as discrete service category — named and positioned, highest-conversion entry point
4. Bilingual EN/FR market specificity — trust-building mechanism with no comparable offering

**Vulnerabilities:**
- Own AI governance completeness as primary credibility instrument — practice advising on carrier attestation has not navigated one
- Documentation architecture for criminal defense vs. administrative compliance incompatibility not yet built into positioning
- Documentation window close urgency not yet operationalized as specific intervention trigger

### Competitive Positioning Requirements

1. **Naming precision** — ICP recognizes language before content evaluation. Problem-naming precision creates recognition-loop trust.
2. **Geneva calibration** — More accurate Geneva-calibrated methodology = more defensible positioning. Non-portable outside Geneva is a feature, not a limitation.
3. **Personal liability framing** — Liability language activates MD personal accountability reflex before intellectual interest. Efficiency framing misses the point.
4. **Structural timing property** — Pre-signing review is categorically different from post-signing compliance building because it prevents self-injury vs. treating existing wound.
