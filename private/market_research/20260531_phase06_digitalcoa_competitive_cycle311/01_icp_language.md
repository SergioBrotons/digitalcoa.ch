## How the Market Describes AI Accountability (Competitive Landscape)

This file captures how competitors in the Geneva AI accountability space describe what they offer — and how clients describe what they actually need. The gap between competitor language and client language is where DigitalCoa operates.

---

## Competitor Language Patterns

### Big Four / Global Consultancies
- "AI governance framework"
- "Enterprise AI risk management"
- "AI compliance program design"
- "Regulatory readiness assessment"
- "AI ethics board setup"
- "Responsible AI principles"
- "AI audit and assurance"

**What they actually deliver:** Policy documents, governance principles, organizational design, readiness assessments. Designed for large organizations with compliance teams. Leave the personal accountability question unanswered. Leave the evidence architecture non-existent.

**The gap they don't name:** They build frameworks. They don't produce evidence. The framework is not the documentation the carrier wants.

### Swiss Law Firms (AI Task Forces)
- "AI governance policy"
- "Ethical AI use guidelines"
- "Associates AI usage framework"
- "Client matter AI disclosure protocol"
- "Internal AI standards committee"

**What they actually deliver:** Internal policy documents. Firm-level guidelines. Training programs. The managing partner is still personally named on the carrier form. The documentation doesn't address the individual exposure.

**The gap they don't name:** Firm-level policy doesn't answer the per-matter, per-signature, per-professional question that carrier renewal actually asks.

### Compliance Consultancies (nFADP Specialists)
- "nFADP compliance program"
- "Article 21 AI transparency documentation"
- "AI system registration"
- "Data protection impact assessment for AI"
- "Privacy-by-design for AI systems"

**What they actually deliver:** Privacy-compliant documentation. Article 21 transparency statements. DPIAs for AI. Designed for data protection officers, not for professionals whose personal license is on the line.

**The gap they don't name:** nFADP compliance is a data protection exercise. It doesn't produce the evidence a carrier form wants. The personal liability question under Article 63 is not a data protection question.

### LegalTech Vendors
- "AI-powered legal research"
- "Intelligent document review"
- "Automated compliance checking"
- "AI case management"
- "Contract analysis platform"

**What they actually deliver:** Tool functionality. Feature documentation. Vendor certifications. Leave the accountability architecture entirely to the client firm.

**The gap they don't name:** The vendor holds the records. The client is accountable for them. Vendor "compliance" is not client accountability evidence.

### Training Programs / Workshops
- "AI governance training for professionals"
- "Understanding AI risks for advisors"
- "AI compliance for wealth managers"
- "Responsible AI use in legal practice"
- "AI literacy for compliance teams"

**What they actually deliver:** Knowledge transfer. Awareness sessions. Slide decks. Leave the documentation gap exactly as it was.

**The gap they don't name:** Training is not documentation. Learning about AI governance is not producing evidence of AI governance.

## What ICPs Actually Say (vs. What Competitors Offer)

### The Language Gap
ICP (legal managing partner): "Everyone offers training. Nobody offers to close the gap. I know what AI tools I'm using. I need to prove it to my carrier. Training doesn't do that."

ICP (wealth management MD): "The big firm sent a framework. Forty pages. I need to answer three questions on a carrier form. That framework doesn't answer those questions."

ICP (physician): "I've been to the seminar. I understand AI governance. I still don't know what to put in the carrier form. The form asks about human oversight. I don't know what that means in practice."

**Pattern:** Competitors offer general frameworks. ICPs need specific evidence. The gap between "understanding AI governance" and "producing carrier-ready evidence" is the market gap no competitor has closed.

### The French-Language Problem
ICP (Geneva, French-speaking): "The carrier form is in English. The FINMA guidance is in English. My regulatory proceeding will be in French. My client communication is in French. I need documentation that works in both environments. Every consultant offers English-language frameworks. Nobody offers bilingual evidence architecture."

**Pattern:** English frameworks + French regulatory proceedings = documentation mismatch. No competitor offers EN+FR evidence architecture designed for the carrier form in English and the regulatory proceeding in French.

### The Personal Liability Language
ICP (all verticals, post-form signing): "I signed it thinking it was a formality. Now I understand what I certified to. I can't undo the signature. I need to build evidence for something I already signed."

**Pattern:** The market has moved from "do I need AI governance?" to "I already signed. Now what?" No competitor offers retroactive reconstruction as a distinct service offering.

## The Language That Creates Clarity (vs. The Language That Creates Confusion)

### What Creates Clarity
- "The form has your name on it. Not the firm's."
- "Your vendor holds records your carrier will ask you to produce."
- "Your staff is using AI you didn't authorize — and you're accountable for all of it."
- "You signed it. Now you need evidence for what you certified."
- "Training doesn't close the gap. Documentation does."

### What Creates Confusion
- "AI governance framework"
- "Enterprise AI risk management"
- "Responsible AI principles"
- "Regulatory readiness assessment"
- "Compliance program design"

**Pattern:** Clarity language is personal, specific, and addresses what the carrier actually asks. Confusion language is organizational, abstract, and describes what the competitor wants to sell.

## The Competitive Positioning Question

**The question no competitor is asking:**
"The carrier form has your name on it. What evidence can you produce for what you certified?"

**The question every competitor is asking:**
"Do you need an AI governance framework?"

**The market has moved past the competitor's question. The ICP's real question is:**
"I already signed. I already certified. Now what evidence do I need to produce — and who helps me produce it?"

**That's the question Sergio answers. That's where the market gap is. That's the competitive position.**
