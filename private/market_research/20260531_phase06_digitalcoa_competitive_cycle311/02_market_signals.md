# 02 — Market Signals: What Competitors Claim vs. What They Deliver
## Phase 06 C311 | DigitalCoa Competitive Research | Competitive Intelligence

---

## The Core Competitive Intelligence Finding

**What competitors claim:** AI governance frameworks, compliance programs, regulatory readiness assessments, responsible AI principles, enterprise risk management.

**What carriers actually ask for:** Evidence of what happened. Documentation of what was reviewed. Records of what was delegated. Proof that the professional who signed the form can produce what the form committed them to.

**The market gap:** No competitor offers evidence architecture. Every competitor offers documentation frameworks. These are not the same thing.

---

## What Competitors Claim vs. What They Actually Deliver

### Big Four (PwC, EY, KPMG, Deloitte)

**Claim:** "We help you build an enterprise AI governance framework that meets regulatory requirements and satisfies carrier expectations."

**What they deliver:**
- 40-80 page governance policy document
- Organizational AI governance structure
- Principles and guidelines
- Readiness assessment
- Training program design

**What the carrier actually wants:**
- Who reviewed the AI output on specific matters
- What the AI system actually did (not what it should do)
- Vendor records the firm doesn't hold
- Evidence of delegation chain for staff AI use
- Retroactive documentation for 12-24 months of undocumented AI use

**The gap:**
- Big Four deliver organizational policy. Carrier asks individual questions.
- Big Four deliver forward-looking framework. Carrier asks retroactive questions.
- Big Four deliver what the firm can describe. Carrier asks what the firm can prove.

**Net-new finding (C311 P06):** Big Four AI governance frameworks are designed for boards and compliance committees — not for professionals whose personal license is on the carrier form. The firm's AI governance policy does not answer the managing partner's carrier attestation question. The organizational framework is not the personal evidence architecture.

**What the carrier actually wants:**

- Documentation of delegation chain to associates
- Vendor records the firm doesn't hold

**The gap:**
- Firm-level guidelines don't answer the individual carrier question
- Associate training doesn't document the delegation chain

**Net-new finding (C311 P06):** Swiss law firm AI task forces are internal organizational exercises. They don't address the managing partner's personal carrier form exposure. The AI task force produces firm policy. The carrier form asks about the managing partner's individual accountability. These are different documents for different purposes.

### nFADP Compliance Consultancies

**Claim:** "We help you achieve nFADP compliance for your AI systems — Article 21 transparency documentation, DPIAs, and privacy-by-design implementation."

**What they deliver:**
- Article 21 transparency statement
- DPIA for AI system
- Privacy-by-design documentation
- Data protection compliance framework

**What the carrier actually wants:**
- Evidence of human oversight in operational terms
- Documentation of what was reviewed and by whom
- Carrier attestation completion
- Authorization chain for AI-influenced decisions

**The gap:**
- nFADP compliance is a data protection exercise. Carrier attestation is a professional liability exercise.
- Article 21 transparency is about client notification. Carrier evidence is about professional documentation.
- DPIA is about privacy risk. Carrier question is about accountability evidence.

**Net-new finding (C311 P06):** nFADP compliance consultancies address the data protection dimension of AI — not the professional liability dimension. Article 63 personal liability is not a data protection question. The carrier form is not a privacy disclosure form. Clients who work with nFADP specialists still need to close the carrier attestation gap separately.

The gap between "understanding AI governance" and "producing carrier-ready evidence" is unaddressed by every training provider.

## The Six Competitive Gaps (Net-New C311 P06)

### Gap 1: Organizational Framework vs. Personal Evidence Architecture
**Finding:** Every competitor offers organizational frameworks. No competitor offers personal evidence architecture. The carrier form names individuals. The frameworks address organizations.

**Evidence:** Carrier forms ask "did the managing partner personally review AI output?" No competitor's framework answers this question for a specific managing partner on a specific renewal date.

**Implication:** The market is waiting for someone who answers the individual question, not the organizational one.

---

### Gap 2: Forward-Looking Framework vs. Retroactive Documentation
**Finding:** Every competitor offers forward-looking frameworks. No competitor offers retroactive reconstruction as a distinct service. The market has 12-24 months of undocumented AI use. The frameworks only work going forward.

**Evidence:** ICPs who signed carrier forms 12 months ago need retroactive documentation for that period. No competitor offers this as a service. "We can help you going forward" is not an answer to "I already signed and I need evidence for what I certified."

**Implication:** Retroactive reconstruction is a distinct, unaddressed service that no competitor offers.

---

### Gap 3: General Training vs. Carrier-Specific Evidence Production
**Finding:** Training providers produce general AI literacy. Carrier forms ask specific questions. The training doesn't translate into carrier-ready evidence.

**Evidence:** Physicians who attended Swiss medical AI seminars still cannot answer the carrier form's human oversight question. Wealth managers who completed FINMA-aligned training still don't have evidence for what they certified.

**Implication:** Carrier-specific evidence production is not addressed by general training. These are different deliverables.

---

### Gap 4: Data Protection vs. Professional Liability
**Finding:** nFADP compliance consultancies address privacy compliance — not professional liability. The carrier form is not a privacy disclosure document. Article 63 liability is not a data protection question.

**Evidence:** A physician can have a fully nFADP-compliant AI system and still be personally exposed on the carrier attestation. The nFADP compliance doesn't address the carrier's human oversight question.

**Implication:** Professionals need a service that addresses professional liability documentation — not just privacy compliance.

---

### Gap 5: Vendor Certification vs. Client Records Pathway
**Finding:** LegalTech vendors offer certifications. They don't offer a records-production pathway for clients. The vendor holds the records. The client is accountable for producing them. No vendor contract addresses this.

**Evidence:** Every carrier audit asks for vendor-held records. Every vendor contract is silent on record production. The gap is structural and universal.

**Implication:** Someone needs to close the vendor records gap for clients. No competitor is doing this.

---

### Gap 6: Bilingual Documentation vs. English Frameworks
**Finding:** Every competitor offers English-language frameworks. Geneva ICPs operate in French regulatory proceedings and English carrier forms simultaneously. No competitor offers EN+FR evidence architecture.

**Evidence:** A Geneva physician signs an English carrier form, operates under French FMH guidelines, and faces a potential Swissmedic proceeding in French. The English framework doesn't document the French regulatory reality.

**Implication:** Bilingual evidence architecture is a market requirement in Geneva — not a translation task. No competitor has built it.

---

## The Market Signal That Matters

**The signal:** When a professional says "I've looked everywhere. No one offers what I actually need," the market is confirming the gap.

**What they say:** "I found someone who could help me understand AI governance. I still don't have evidence for my carrier form." "I have a policy. I need documentation." "I know what I should do. I don't know how to prove it."

**What this means:** The market has enough awareness. The market needs evidence production. The gap between understanding AI governance and producing carrier-ready evidence is the competitive white space.

**The positioning that fills it:** Not "AI governance framework." Not "compliance program." Not "training."

**Evidence architecture.**
