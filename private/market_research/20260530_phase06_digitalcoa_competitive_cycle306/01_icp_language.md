## Purpose

Document how the competitive market — other advisors, carriers, institutions, and the ICP's own peer network — talks about AI accountability for Geneva SMBs. The language patterns reveal competitive positioning failures, category gaps, and the specific vocabulary that converts ICP recognition into engagement.

## The Competitive Language Map

### How Competitors Talk (The Framework Language Trap)

**All named competitors in the Geneva SMB AI accountability space use the same vocabulary:**

| Competitor Type | Language Used | Positioning Claim | Language Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| International consultancies | "AI governance framework," "risk management strategy," "digital transformation roadmap" | We design your AI governance system | No evidence language |
| Geneva law firms | "Professional obligations under Art.321 CC," "regulatory compliance," "data protection adequacy" | We advise on your legal obligations | No documentation delivery |
| IT security firms | "AI tool security assessment," "vendor due diligence," "technical controls" | We assess whether your AI is safe | Wrong layer (tool security vs. human authorization) |
| Data protection consultancies | "nFADP compliance," "DPIA," "privacy by design" | We make you FADP-compliant | FADP layer ≠ carrier attestation layer |
| Online platforms | "AI compliance toolkit," "step-by-step governance," "automated workflows" | We make governance simple | Self-service without execution capacity |
| Swiss Cyber Institute | "AI governance best practices," "enterprise AI risk," "governance frameworks" | We train your team on AI governance | Training completion ≠ evidence production |

**The pattern:** Every competitor uses framework language. No competitor uses evidence language. The market is saturated with framework-talk and empty of evidence-talk.

**The ICP translation:** The Geneva SMB principal has heard "AI governance" from every advisor they've ever contacted. None of them connected the language to the specific document the principal signed, the specific form language they didn't fully read, or the specific evidence gap they don't know how to close.

**EN ready-to-use:** "Every advisor you've talked to uses the word 'governance.' None of them tell you what evidence your carrier actually asks for."

**FR ready-to-use:** « Chaque conseiller utilise le mot 'gouvernance'. Aucun ne vous dit quelles preuves votre assureur demande réellement. »

### How Carriers Talk (The Language the ICP Doesn't See)

**Carrier attestation language is evolving faster than any competitor's positioning:**

- **1st generation (2019–2022):** "Do you have an AI policy?" → Policy existence as adequacy proxy
- **2nd generation (2022–2024):** "Describe your AI governance framework" → Framework description as adequacy evidence
- **3rd generation (2024–2025):** "What AI tools do you use and how do you oversee them?" → Tool inventory + oversight mechanism as evidence
- **4th generation (2025–present):** "Provide evidence of human authorization for specific AI-assisted decisions; confirm documentation exists independent of vendor attestations" → Behavioral evidence, vendor-independent documentation required

**The ICP rarely sees fourth-generation form language until they're sitting with the renewal in front of them.** The carrier writes to a standard the ICP didn't know existed. The ICP signs what they understand, not what the form actually says.

**The competitive gap:** No competitor's positioning language reflects fourth-generation carrier form language. The ICP searching for "AI compliance help" encounters competitors using first- and second-generation framework language. The mismatch between what competitors promise and what carriers ask creates a conversion failure at the engagement stage.

**EN ready-to-use:** "The form your carrier sent you asks for things your current documentation doesn't contain. That's not a compliance problem — that's an evidence gap. It's closable."

**FR ready-to-use:** « Le formulaire que votre assureur a envoyé demande des choses que votre documentation actuelle ne contient pas. Ce n'est pas un problème de conformité — c'est un manque de preuves. C'est comblable. »

### How Brokers Talk (The Channel Without a Product)

**The Geneva insurance broker is the primary referral channel for AI accountability services — and has no named product to refer to:**

**Broker vocabulary when describing AI governance to clients:**
- "You need to have something in place"
- "Make sure you're documented"
- "The forms are getting more detailed"
- "Get some help on this"
- "I can't advise you specifically, but you should look into it"

**Broker vocabulary when referring to an advisor:**
- "There's a consultant in Geneva who does this"
- "I don't have a specific name, but you should find someone"
- "Call your bar association for a recommendation"
- "Some firms specialize in AI compliance"

**The gap:** The broker sees the problem clearly and has no specific name to attach to the solution. The warm referral that would convert the ICP's concern into an engagement becomes a generic instruction to "find someone." The ICP who can't find a specific someone delays engaging.

**The naming opportunity:** The advisor who gives brokers a specific name, a specific number, and a specific first-engagement format becomes the named solution to the broker's referral problem. The broker who can say "Call Sergio Brotons, he does this specifically" has solved the broker's problem. The named referral with a specific engagement format converts.

**EN ready-to-use:** "When your broker can't give you a name for what you need — that's the gap. Give them my number."

**FR ready-to-use:** « Quand votre courtier ne peut pas vous donner un nom pour ce dont vous avez besoin — c'est la lacune. Donnez-leur mon numéro. »

### How Institutional Guidance Talks (The Principle Without Tuesday)

**Geneva institutional guidance (SAV, SVA, FMH, SRO/ROMAN) uses consistent language structure:**

| Element | Language Used | What It Actually Provides |
|---|---|---|
| Obligation statement | "Members should ensure adequate AI governance" | Principles, no definition of adequacy |
| Implementation guidance | "Appropriate measures should be in place" | Abstraction, no Tuesday pathway |
| Recommended action | "Members are encouraged to review their AI arrangements" | Suggestion, no specific review format |
| External resource | "Members should consult qualified advisors" | Demand generation, no advisor recommendation |
| Enforcement signal | "Non-compliance may have professional consequences" | Anxiety creation, no specific consequence definition |

**The ICP experience:** The principal reads the guidance, recognizes they have an obligation, has no idea what "adequate" means for their specific practice, and is told to find a qualified advisor without a name or framework to evaluate them.

**The competitive opportunity:** The advisor who is named in institutional context (known by the institution's staff, referenced in informal guidance, present at institutional events) becomes the natural destination for the member who reads the guidance and needs help. The institutional referral is the warmest possible introduction.

**The circuit participation mechanism:** The advisor who shows up at bar events, FMH sessions, and broker briefings is building institutional recognition. The guidance writer at the SAV receives questions from members and says "there's someone you should talk to." The person who gets named is the person who was there.

### How ICPs Talk to Each Other (The Peer Network Effect)

**Geneva SMB professional firms operate within tight peer networks where AI governance conversations travel through informal channels:**

**Pre-crisis peer language:**
- "Did you see they're asking about AI now on the carrier forms?"
- "We just got one of those questionnaires from our big client"
- "I think we need to do something but I don't know what"
- "I asked our IT guy and he said we were fine"
- "Our broker mentioned something at the last meeting"

**Attestation moment peer language:**
- "I just signed it, I didn't really know what to put"
- "The form was confusing so I just checked the boxes"
- "I figured we'd deal with it if anything came up"
- "I assume our AI vendor is handling their part"

**Post-event peer language (when something goes wrong):**
- "I wish I'd known that was what they meant"
- "I didn't realize I was signing something personally"
- "I thought the vendor coverage covered us"
- "Where do you even start?"

**The network effect:** The Geneva SMB professional community is small and well-connected. A bad experience with a carrier claim, a client questionnaire response that created exposure, or an attestation that produced complications spreads through the peer network within weeks. The advisor who serves one principal badly becomes known in the peer network — and the peer network is the primary demand generation engine.

**The competitive implication:** The advisor who produces evidence that survives carrier scrutiny earns peer network referrals. The advisor who produces framework documents that don't satisfy carrier requirements earns peer network warning signs. The peer network is a credibility multiplier in both directions.

## The Language Gap That Creates Engagement Failure

**The gap:** Competitors use framework language. ICPs use problem language. Carriers use evidence language. No one is translating between the three.

| Speaker | Language Used | Translation Gap |
|---|---|---|
| Competitor | "AI governance framework" | Not connecting to the form the ICP signed |
| ICP | "What do I put on this form?" | Not connecting to the evidence standard behind the question |
| Carrier | "Provide evidence of human authorization" | Not explaining what that means in practice |
| Institution | "Adequate AI governance" | Not translating to Tuesday morning |
| Broker | "Get some help on this" | Not naming who or what format |
| Vendor | "We're fully compliant" | Not explaining what the client's separate obligations are |

**The engagement failure:** The ICP who recognizes they have a problem searches for help in problem language ("AI compliance Geneva," "carrier attestation help," "AI governance support"). They encounter competitors using framework language ("AI governance framework," "risk management strategy"). The mismatch causes the ICP to disengage — they don't see their specific problem in the competitor's language.

**The conversion moment:** The ICP who hears evidence language in the context of their specific problem ("your carrier is asking for evidence of human authorization for your AI-assisted decisions — do you have that?") converts. The language connects directly to the document they signed and the fear they have.

**EN ready-to-use:** "The form asks for evidence. Do you have evidence? That's the question."

**FR ready-to-use:** « Le formulaire demande des preuves. Avez-vous des preuves ? C'est la question. »

## Cross-Competitive Language Patterns — What the Market is Saying Without Saying It

### Pattern 01: The "I Didn't Know I Was Signing Something Personal" Pattern

**How the ICP phrases it:** "I thought I was signing for the firm."
**What they mean:** The attestation signature created personal representations they didn't recognize as personal.
**Market signal:** The principal signed believing corporate liability coverage protects them personally. The carrier's form language and the broker's coverage discussion didn't correct this belief before signing.

### Pattern 02: The "Vendor Said We're Covered" Pattern

**How the ICP phrases it:** "The vendor's DPA covers our obligations."
**What they mean:** The vendor's compliance posture is being confused with the firm's accountability architecture.
**Market signal:** The vendor's marketing and legal language create a false coverage belief that no one corrects until the carrier asks the question the vendor's language didn't answer.

### Pattern 03: The "Form Came With the Renewal" Pattern

**How the ICP phrases it:** "It was just part of the renewal package."
**What they mean:** The attestation arrived without context, warning, or explanation of what the signature committed them to.
**Market signal:** Carriers are embedding attestation forms in renewal packages to normalize the signature. The ICP who signs without reading the specific language has created documented representations they may not be able to support.

### Pattern 04: The "I Have a Policy" Pattern

**How the ICP phrases it:** "We're fine, we have an AI policy."
**What they mean:** Policy existence equals compliance adequacy.
**Market signal:** The policy document is being used as evidence by principals who don't know what the carrier's form actually asks for. The policy may satisfy the first-generation form question (do you have a policy?) while failing the fourth-generation question (can you show evidence of human authorization for specific decisions?).

### Pattern 05: The "Client Questionnaire" Pattern

**How the ICP phrases it:** "One of our big clients sent us a questionnaire about AI governance."
**What they mean:** The institutional client is creating a de facto accountability standard through the questionnaire.
**Market signal:** The DDQ response sets a peer benchmark. The first firms to respond without architecture are defining what "adequate" means for their peer group — at a level below genuine accountability.
