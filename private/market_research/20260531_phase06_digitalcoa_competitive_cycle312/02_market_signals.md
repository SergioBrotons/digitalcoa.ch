## Competitive Claims vs. Delivery Analysis

### Competitor Type 1: International AI Governance Platforms

**Claims:**
- "Enterprise AI governance at scale"
- "Automated AI compliance monitoring"
- "Complete AI risk management platform"
- "AI policy automation"
- "Real-time AI oversight dashboard"
- "Comprehensive AI compliance for regulated industries"

**What They Actually Deliver:**
- Organization-level policy documents
- Process flowcharts and governance charts
- Training content libraries
- One-size-fits-all compliance templates
- SaaS dashboards showing tool inventory (not evidence of human authorization)
- Forward-only documentation systems
- English-only content
- Enterprise engagement models (6-12 month implementations, 6-figure fees)

**The Gap:**
Carriers ask for evidence of human authorization for specific AI-assisted decisions made during a specific period, calibrated to the specific renewal questions. These platforms deliver organizational charts, policy documents, and tool inventories. They cannot answer carrier form questions because they were not designed to answer carrier form questions. The engagement model requires 6-12 months and 6 figures — structurally incompatible with the ICP who needs evidence reconstruction in weeks before renewal.

## The Universal Delivery Failures Across All Competitor Types

### Failure 1: Forward-Only Documentation
Every competitor offers forward-looking documentation. No competitor has a retroactive reconstruction methodology. The market has 12-24 months of undocumented AI use. "Going forward" is not an answer to "I already signed the form."

### Failure 2: Organization vs. Individual
Every competitor addresses organizational liability or privacy compliance. No competitor addresses the managing partner's, managing director's, or physician's personal accountability under carrier attestation. The form names the individual. The competitor addresses the firm.

### Failure 3: English vs. EN+FR
Every competitor offers English-only or German-only content. No competitor offers English carrier forms + French regulatory proceedings documentation architecture. Geneva's bilingual accountability environment is unaddressed.

### Failure 4: Carrier Form Blindness
Every competitor offers what they can build and sell. No competitor reads the carrier form as a design specification and builds evidence architecture to answer it. The carrier form question types are: specific, retrospective, individual, calibrated to the professional's actual situation.

### Failure 5: Framework vs. Evidence
Every competitor offers frameworks, policies, assessments, or training. No competitor produces evidence of human authorization for specific AI-assisted decisions. The market has been trained to ask for frameworks. The carrier asks for evidence. The gap between these two things is the competitive moat.

### Failure 6: Vendor Records Gap
No competitor addresses the gap between what the vendor holds and what the professional is accountable for producing. The vendor's records are the professional's liability. No competitor has a vendor contract review and record production methodology.

### Failure 7: Authorization Chain Blindness
No competitor documents the authorization chain — who authorized which tools, for which purposes, with what oversight, during which period. This is the primary liability gap carriers are now asking about. No competitor has a documentation methodology for it.

### Failure 8: "Adequate" Undefined
Every competitor uses "adequate human oversight" language. No competitor defines "adequate" operationally for the ICP's specific situation. "Adequate" is the universal floating signifier that paralyzes action. The competitor who defines it for the specific vertical wins the engagement.

## The Structural Reason the Gap Exists

Competitors are selling what they can build:
- Framework vendors sell SaaS platforms
- Big Four sells regulatory expertise
- Boutique firms sell policies and training
- Legal consultants sell legal opinions
- Vendors sell tool documentation

None of these products match what carriers are asking for. The market is offering supply that doesn't match demand. The demand is specific: evidence of human authorization for specific AI-assisted decisions, retrospectively documented, calibrated to carrier form questions, in the professional's name, with personal liability implications.

The reason the gap exists is not that competitors are unaware of the market. It is that the gap requires a different product — evidence architecture, not compliance documentation — and evidence architecture requires a different delivery model: one senior professional, specific document review, retroactive reconstruction methodology, EN+FR capability, carrier form reading as primary intelligence, broker referral as primary channel.

This is not a consulting firm product. This is not a SaaS product. This is an operator product — one professional, specific deliverables, personal accountability for the output.

## Competitive Signals: What Is Changing

**Signal 1: Carrier forms are escalating faster than competitor offerings**
Carrier forms are becoming more specific, more personal, and more retrospective. The market's offerings are not escalating at the same rate. The gap is widening.

**Signal 2: Institutional DDQ is creating secondary commercial pressure**
The institutional client DDQ is now asking questions carriers don't yet ask — pushing the standard above the regulatory floor. Competitors are not reading DDQ as competitive intelligence.

**Signal 3: Broker referral channel is becoming more active**
Brokers are receiving questions about AI accountability coverage. They have no one to refer to. The channel is activating but empty.

**Signal 4: The "I already signed" ICP is the new market**
The dominant inquiry type is shifting from discovery ("do I have a problem?") to action ("I signed, now what?"). No competitor has a product for this specific moment.

## Summary: What Phase 06 Confirms About Market Signals

1. **Every competitor offers what they can build, not what carriers ask for** — the gap between supply and demand is structural, not temporary.

2. **The gap is widening** — carrier forms are escalating faster than competitor offerings. The competitive moat deepens over time.

3. **The broker channel is activating and empty** — brokers identify the problem but have no one to refer to. This is the primary first-mover opportunity.

4. **Retroactive reconstruction is the primary market need and zero competitors offer it** — every competitor offers forward-only documentation. The market has 12-24 months of undocumented AI use.

5. **"Adequate" operationalization is the single highest-value competitive differentiator** — no competitor defines it. The one who does wins the engagement.

6. **Personal liability framing is独占** — "For physicians, evidence is personal. And your license is on the line." No competitor uses this framing. It applies across all four verticals.
