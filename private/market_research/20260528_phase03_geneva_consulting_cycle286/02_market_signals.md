## Primary Market Problems

### 1. Carrier AI Attestation as Personal Liability Trap

**What's happening:** Swiss professional liability carriers have begun including AI-specific attestation questions in renewal forms. These require the managing director's personal signature. The questions ask about AI review methodology, but define no standard for what adequate means.

**The specific problem:** 
- MD signs attestation personally committing to adequacy against undefined carrier standard
- No Geneva market service offers pre-signing review of what MD commits to
- Carriers are not disclosing how attestation responses affect coverage scope for AI-assisted errors
- MDs are signing because "everyone signs" without understanding personal exposure

**What ICPs are saying:**
> "I signed the AI attestation section because the renewal was due and I didn't know what else to do. The form was written by the carrier's lawyers. I had no independent review of what I was committing to."
> — Geneva managing director, 6-person boutique

> (fr) "Je ne savais pas que je m'engageais personnellementsi quelque chose ne va pas. Ce n'est pas indiqué clairement. C'est ma signature, donc c'est ma responsabilité, c'est clair."

### 2. Engagement Letter Architecture Collapse

**What's happening:** Active client relationships governed by engagement letters written before AI adoption. These contracts contain no AI authorization language, no methodology disclosure obligations, no version-locking provisions. Actual AI-assisted delivery practice has already superseded the governing documents for every active engagement.

**The specific problem:**
- No mechanism for updating engagement letters mid-relationship when AI practice evolves
- Client may be entitled to know what methodology produced their deliverables; engagement letter silent
- If client requests methodology documentation, boutique has no contractually-backed position on what to disclose
- Engagement letter obsolescence invisible until client asks specific question

**What ICPs are saying:**
> "Our engagement letters say nothing about AI because they were written before we adopted AI tools. We've been delivering AI-assisted work under contracts that predate our AI use. That's not a comfortable position when I think about it."
> — Managing partner, boutique consulting firm

### 3. Proposal Defensibility Failure

**What's happening:** AI-assisted proposals with accumulated analytical framework influence from 2-3 years of AI use. Boutique's own analytical positions have been shaped by AI pattern synthesis in ways no longer attributable to deliberate firm judgment. When clients probe methodology, boutique cannot explain what analytical positions it genuinely holds independently of AI influence.

**The specific problem:**
- AI tool has shaped firm's analytical voice without explicit authorization
- Different boutiques produce structurally similar proposals; sophisticated clients may notice
- Client-side stylometric analysis could identify AI-assisted language patterns
- MD cannot defend AI-assisted proposal methodology because reconstruction path doesn't exist

**What ICPs are saying:**
> "I've looked at three AI-assisted proposals from different boutiques side by side. They sound more similar than they should. If clients are doing the same comparison, we have a problem we don't know about."
> — Senior partner, 15-person firm

### 4. Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Chain Without Framework

**What's happening:** Prime boutique contracts sub-consultant who uses their own AI tools. Prime boutique is contractually accountable to client. Sub-consultant's AI tool operates under terms prime boutique has not evaluated. nFADP Art.9 third-party processing authorization, accountability chain documentation, engagement letter standard — all absent.

**The specific problem:**
- Prime boutique cannot explain what AI the sub-consultant used or how
- No framework defines what sub-consultant AI delegation requires in consulting context
- nFADP processor obligations may flow to prime boutique for sub-consultant AI processing
- Client may hold prime boutique accountable for sub-consultant AI methodology

### 5. Analytical Framework Ownership Void

**What's happening:** Boutique has accumulated institutional capital in prompt libraries, workflow configurations, client-specific model adaptations, and analytical frameworks built across years of AI use. This capital lives in vendor infrastructure. Vendor uses accumulated improvements to enhance product for all users. Boutique bears production costs, retains liability, receives none of the value contributed.

**The specific problem:**
- AI platform commercial terms transfer boutique institutional capital to vendor
- Boutique cannot document what AI does in firm's analytical process
- When vendor updates model, accumulated framework behavior may change
- MD cannot explain why current analytical framework produces different outputs than 18 months ago

### 6. Geneva Peer Accountability Vacuum Structuring

**What's happening:** Geneva consulting has no SVA equivalent with AI governance guidance, no ORDRE des Avocats equivalent for professional standards, no SFAMA/SRO equivalent for advisory firms. Carrier AI questionnaires are functioning as de facto operative standards for the sector, designed without Geneva consulting input.

**The specific problem:**
- No feedback loop to guidance development — vacuum is structural
- MDs operating without reference standard that defines adequate AI governance
- Carrier questionnaire becomes the standard precisely because no other standard exists
- No informal circuit providing calibration at elevated stakes

## Secondary Problems Surfacing

### 7. Internal AI Governance Surface as Ungoverned Domain

Internal AI use (internal research, analysis, document preparation, client data processing through AI tools) operates as unmanaged accountability surface. Client data in internal workflows unreviewed for nFADP obligations. Internal AI outputs reach clients through deliverables without internal review architecture. Invisible to clients but full professional accountability flows through it.

### 8. Client AI Question Inventory as Liability Asset

Clients are asking recurring categories of AI questions: model version, cross-client data segregation, human review protocols, firm AI configuration knowledge. Different MDs answering same questions differently, creating client-formed disclosure records boutique cannot reconstruct. What clients ask defines what firm is accountable for having answered.

### 9. Vendor Terms Structural Incompatibility With MD Accountability Architecture

Vendor terms designed for vendor commercial model, not Geneva consulting MD professional liability structure. Satisfying vendor terms and Art.398 CO proper performance and MD personal liability cannot all be achieved simultaneously with standard platform terms. No Geneva compatibility analysis mapping specific incompatibility points.

### 10. Multi-Obligation Documentation Interaction Mapping Absent

Carrier questionnaires, client AI governance requests, engagement letter standards, AI Convention obligations, nFADP requirements operating simultaneously. Documents built at different times against different requirements without coherence applied to system. No Geneva service mapping how obligations interact, what satisfying one does to position on another, compounding effects across active client portfolio.
