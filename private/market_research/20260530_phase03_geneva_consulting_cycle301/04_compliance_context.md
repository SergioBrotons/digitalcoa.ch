# Compliance Context: Geneva/Switzerland Regulatory Environment

**Phase:** C301 Phase 03
**Focus:** Regulatory and compliance landscape specifically relevant to small Geneva consulting boutiques.

---

## Swiss Regulatory Architecture for AI — 2026

### The Core Problem: Switzerland Has No Single AI Statute
Switzerland takes a **technology-neutral, sector-specific approach** to AI regulation. There is no single "Swiss AI Act." Instead, AI is governed through:

- Existing laws with AI-relevant provisions
- Sector-specific guidance (FINMA, FDPIC, etc.)
- International standards (ISO, NIST)
- The Council of Europe's AI Convention (signed March 2025)
- Soft-law measures (self-declarations, industry standards)

**For consulting firms:** This creates ambiguity. They're not sure which rules apply to them, because there is no single rulebook.

---

## New Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) — The Primary Data Law

**In force:** September 1, 2023
**Alignment:** Similar to GDPR but with Swiss-specific provisions
**Enforcement:** Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner (FDPIC)

### Article 9: Automated Decision-Making and Profiling

**The key definitions:**
- **Profiling:** Any automated processing of personal data to evaluate specific personal aspects (work performance, economic situation, health, preferences, reliability, behavior, whereabouts, etc.)
- **High-risk profiling:** Profiling that poses a high risk to personality or fundamental rights

**What this means for consulting firms:**

Consultants doing work involving:
- HR and talent assessment
- Financial risk scoring
- Performance analytics
- Client behavior prediction
- Any AI tool that evaluates individuals

...may be operating in Art. 9 territory without realizing it.

**The compliance gap:** Small boutiques often don't know they're doing profiling until a problem surfaces. The law requires transparency, data subject rights, and in high-risk cases, mandatory DPIAs — but the firms have no process for any of this.

**Personal liability:** Executives can face fines up to **CHF 250,000** for non-compliance. The liability is personal, not corporate.

---

## FINMA Guidance 08/2024 — For Financial Sector Clients

**Published:** December 2024
**Applies to:** Swiss financial institutions supervised by FINMA

**What it requires of supervised institutions:**
- Governance frameworks for AI use
- Data quality standards for AI training and operational data
- Explainability and documentation requirements
- Outsourcing oversight (including consultants)

**The upstream effect:** When a small Geneva consulting boutique serves a FINMA-supervised client, the client is required to ensure the consultant meets governance standards. This is why AI governance questionnaires are flowing downstream to consulting vendors.

**What boutique firms need to know:** If your client is a bank, asset manager, insurance company, or other supervised entity, their AI governance questionnaire to you is driven by FINMA obligations — not just their own compliance policy.

---

## The Council of Europe's AI Convention

**Signed:** March 2025
**Status:** Consultation draft for implementing legislation expected by end of 2026
**Primary focus:** Public sector actors

**Relevance to small consulting firms:** Limited in the near term (primarily public sector), but sets the direction for Swiss AI regulation. The consultation draft expected by end of 2026 will provide more clarity on cross-sector applicability.

**Soft-law measures:** Industry self-declarations and solutions expected by end of 2026 — these will likely become de facto standards for consulting firms working with regulated clients.

---

## EU AI Act Extraterritorial Reach

**Major high-risk provisions activate:** August 2, 2026

**Why this matters for Geneva boutiques:**
- Swiss firms serving EU clients or placing AI outputs in the EU market fall under EU AI Act requirements.
- If a boutique's AI-assisted deliverables are used within the EU, the firm is effectively operating under EU AI Act rules.

**High-risk AI systems under EU AI Act include:**
- AI systems used in employment decisions
- AI systems used in credit and financial access
- AI systems used in legal and administrative decisions

**Consulting implication:** If your advisory work touches any of these domains for EU clients, the EU AI Act creates obligations that cascade to your methodology documentation.

---

## Carrier Attestation Requirements — The Insurance Trigger

**Current state:** Professional liability carriers for consulting firms are now requiring structured AI governance attestation as part of coverage renewals.

**What's being asked:**
- Documentation of AI tools used in practice
- Methodology for AI-assisted work
- Client AI governance compliance records
- Audit trail infrastructure
- Sub-consultant AI delegation practices

**The risk for non-compliance:**
- Coverage gaps
- Premium increases
- Non-renewal
- Claims denial if AI-related incident occurs without documented methodology

**Key point:** The carrier attestation is not yet standardized. Different carriers ask different things. Firms need a flexible documentation infrastructure that can respond to multiple carrier formats.

## Geneva-Specific Considerations

### The Geneva Business Culture Factor
Geneva-based consulting firms operate in a high-trust, high-discretion business culture. Relationships matter. Reputation is everything. Personal accountability is baked into the partnership structure.

**Implication:** When a managing partner signs an attestation, it carries weight. There's no corporate distancing layer. The partner's signature is their personal exposure.

### The Geneva Label Effect
"Geneva-based consulting boutique" signals:
- International standard practice
- Financial sector sophistication
- Regulatory awareness
- Sophisticated client expectations

**The double edge:** This label triggers higher scrutiny from clients and carriers. Being a Geneva firm means being held to a higher standard in AI governance attestation.

### French/English Bilingualism Requirement
Geneva's business environment operates in both French and English. Compliance documentation, client communications, and carrier correspondence often need to function in both languages.

**Implication:** Any AI governance solution for Geneva boutiques needs to be genuinely bilingual — not just translated, but culturally adapted to both French and English business contexts.

## The Documentation Imperative — What the Law Actually Requires

### For nFADP Compliance (Article 9):
- Record of processing activities (who does what, with what data, using what systems)
- Transparency to data subjects about automated decision-making
- Right for data subjects to request human review of automated decisions
- DPIA for high-risk profiling activities
- Documentation of legal basis for automated processing

### For FINMA Client Work:
- AI governance framework documentation
- Data quality standards compliance evidence
- Explainability documentation for AI-assisted analysis
- Outsourcing oversight records (including sub-consultant practices)

### For EU AI Act (if serving EU clients):
- Technical documentation for high-risk AI systems
- Logging and monitoring capabilities
- Human oversight mechanisms
- Conformity assessment records

### For Carrier Attestation:
- AI tool inventory
- Methodology documentation for AI-assisted work
- Client AI compliance records
- Audit trail infrastructure
- Sub-consultant delegation documentation

---

## The Common Thread

Every regulatory layer — nFADP, FINMA, EU AI Act, carrier attestation — requires the same underlying infrastructure:

**A documented methodology for AI-assisted consulting work, with an audit trail.**

Small boutiques don't have this. The regulatory environment is now demanding it from multiple directions simultaneously. The firms that build this infrastructure first will have a significant advantage over those still scrambling to retrofit documentation.

## Net-New Findings for C301 Phase 03

1. **FINMA Guidance 08/2024 is the driver of client AI governance questionnaires** — all supervised financial institutions are now requiring AI governance documentation from vendors, including small consulting boutiques.

2. **nFADP Art. 9 creates personal liability for profiling-adjacent consulting work** — the CHF 250,000 fine is personal, not just corporate, and applies to executives who "should have known."

3. **EU AI Act August 2026 activation creates retroactive documentation pressure** — Swiss boutiques serving EU clients need methodology documentation that can demonstrate compliance retroactively.

4. **Carrier attestation is the most immediate forcing function** — not regulation, not clients, but insurance renewal is the crisis point for most small boutiques right now.

5. **The Geneva label increases scrutiny** — being Geneva-based means clients and carriers hold you to a higher standard.

6. **Bilingual documentation is operationally necessary** — French and English capability is not optional for Geneva compliance work.
