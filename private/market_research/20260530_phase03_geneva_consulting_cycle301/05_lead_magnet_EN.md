## What the Client AI Governance Questionnaire Actually Means

Your client — probably a financial institution or a multinational corporation — sent you a questionnaire. It asked about your AI governance practices. It asked you to attest to your AI methodology.

You filled it out. You probably filled it out quickly, because the client was waiting and the proposal was due.

Here is what you actually attested to:

- That you have a documented methodology for AI use in client work
- That you can describe which AI tools you use and how
- That you have a process for validating AI-assisted outputs before client delivery
- That you have a record of AI tool usage per engagement
- That your sub-consultant AI delegation practices are documented

If any of those statements are not true — and for most small boutiques, most of them are not true — then you signed a document attesting to something you cannot prove.

This is not a theoretical risk. Clients are following up. Auditors are asking for methodology documentation. The questionnaire was the first request. The audit follow-up is the second.

## The Methodology Audit Trail You Don't Have

When I talk to small consulting firms about AI governance, the conversation goes the same way every time:

*"Do you have a documented methodology for how AI tools are used in your client work?"*

*"Not really. We use AI. We use it carefully. We review the outputs."*

*"Can you show me your methodology documentation?"*

*"..."*

The gap is not intentional. Small boutique consultants are not trying to deceive clients or carriers. The problem is that AI governance documentation was treated as optional — something to deal with "later" — until later arrived with a questionnaire attached.

The methodology audit trail you need is not complicated. It does not require a enterprise GRC platform. It requires:

1. **A clear description of which AI tools you use, in what capacity, for what purposes**
2. **A documented process for human validation of AI-assisted outputs before client delivery**
3. **A log of AI tool usage per engagement — what tools, what version, what output**
4. **A process for client disclosure of AI involvement in deliverables**
5. **A sub-consultant AI delegation protocol that you can explain to a client or carrier**

None of this is optional anymore. If you cannot show this documentation when a client or carrier asks, your attestation is empty — and you are exposed.

## The Carrier Attestation Problem

Your professional liability carrier is asking about AI now.

Not as a courtesy. Not as an optional add-on questionnaire. As a structured part of the renewal process.

The carriers have realized that AI introduces liability that standard professional indemnity doesn't cleanly cover. The questionnaire is their attempt to understand your AI exposure and price the risk accordingly.

What they're asking for:
- What AI tools do you use in client work?
- What is your methodology for AI-assisted analysis and deliverables?
- Do you have a documented process for validating AI outputs?
- How do you manage AI use by sub-consultants?
- What is your client disclosure practice for AI involvement?

Your ability to answer these questions with documented evidence — not just verbal assurances — determines:
- Whether your coverage renewals cleanly
- Whether you have coverage gaps that could be exploited in a claim
- Whether your premium increases materially

I have seen firms face 30–40% premium increases because they couldn't document their AI methodology to the carrier's satisfaction. The insurer treated undocumented AI use as an unknown risk and priced it accordingly. The fix — building the documentation — was straightforward. But it had to be done under time pressure, which is never the right conditions for careful work.

## The Sub-Consultant Delegation Gap

Here is a problem that almost no one in the Geneva consulting market is addressing:

You engage a sub-consultant for part of a client engagement. The sub-consultant uses AI tools in their analysis. You know nothing about those tools — not which ones, not how they're used, not how the outputs were validated.

Your client holds you accountable for the entire deliverable, including the parts you subcontracted. If the AI-assisted portion of the work has a problem — an error, a biased output, a recommendation that doesn't hold up — your client will hold you responsible.

You have no visibility into the AI methodology of the sub-consultant. You have no documentation of the delegation. You have no audit trail.

This is a structural gap in multi-party consulting arrangements that the market has not addressed. The lead boutique carries the accountability for subcontracted AI work without any mechanism for managing that accountability.

What you need:
- A sub-consultant AI governance assessment process
- Contractual language that makes AI methodology documentation a requirement
- A delegation chain you can explain to clients and carriers

## Personal Liability Under nFADP — The Part Nobody Talks About

The New Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) is in force. Article 9 addresses automated decision-making and profiling.

If you advise a client on work that involves using AI to evaluate individuals — hiring, credit, performance, risk scoring, any domain where an algorithm makes or materially influences a decision about a person — you may be operating in territory that triggers nFADP obligations.

The obligations include:
- Transparency to data subjects about automated decision-making
- The right for data subjects to request human review of automated decisions
- DPIA for high-risk profiling activities
- Documentation of the legal basis for automated processing

The part that small boutique consultants consistently underestimate: the personal liability.

Executives can face fines up to CHF 250,000 under nFADP. Not the firm — the executive. The partner who signed the engagement letter. The managing director who approved the recommendation.

This is not academic. The FDPIC is actively enforcing. The uncertainty tax — the cost of not knowing whether your work is in scope — is real. And the solution is documentation: a methodology you can show, a process you can explain, evidence that you thought about this carefully and handled it properly.

## What You Can Do Right Now

I am not going to tell you that you need a comprehensive AI governance framework or a full enterprise compliance program. If you are a 5–30 person Geneva consulting boutique, that advice is not practical and you know it.

What you actually need is a lean, defensible documentation practice that:
- Shows clients and carriers you have a methodology
- Creates an audit trail you can produce when asked
- Manages the sub-consultant delegation problem
- Reduces your personal exposure under nFADP

The components are specific:
1. A documented AI use policy for your practice — not a generic framework, something actually applicable to your work
2. A methodology documentation format you can use per engagement
3. A client disclosure approach for AI involvement in deliverables
4. A sub-consultant AI governance requirement you can embed in subcontractor agreements
5. A carrier response package you can produce quickly when the renewal questionnaire arrives

This is not a large project. It is a structured approach that fits a small consulting practice. It takes some thought, some documentation, and some discipline. But it is entirely doable — and it is the difference between an attestation you can defend and one you cannot.
