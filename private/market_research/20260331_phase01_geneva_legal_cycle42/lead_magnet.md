# Lead Magnet: "The Small Law Firm AI Governance Question"
## Phase 01 — Geneva Legal — Cycle 42

---

## Positioning
For Geneva small/mid-market law firm managing partners who are using (or considering) AI tools without a formal governance framework — who need clarity on obligations, protection from enforcement risk, and a path to compliance that respects their constraints.

**Primary urgency lever (Cycle 42):** 70% projected AI adoption increase in 2026 + 90%+ already using AI tools. The governance gap is widening. The question is not whether to govern AI — it is whether to govern it before or after an incident.

**Secondary urgency lever:** FADP directly applicable to AI since FDPIC May 2025 confirmation — no new legislation required. Obligations are already live.

---

## ICP Entry Points

### Decision 0: Shadow AI Audit
The single lowest-commitment entry point: identify every AI tool currently in use by lawyers and staff in the firm. No cost. No external involvement required. Done internally in one meeting.

**Why this works:** 43% of firms have no formal AI policy and no plans to create one. The Shadow AI audit creates awareness without demanding a policy decision. It surfaces the gap without prescribing a solution.

### Decision 1: FADP Compliance Mapping
Once the Shadow AI audit is complete, the firm maps which AI tools process personal data (likely: most of them) and whether the firm has a FADP-compliant basis for that processing (Art. 21 right to human review; Art. 5(c) transparency; Art. 16 data source disclosure).

**Why this works:** FADP is already operative. The FDPIC confirmed May 2025 that existing FADP obligations apply to AI-supported data processing. No new law required. This is actionable now.

### Decision 2: Data Residency Check
Every AI tool used by the firm must have data residency in Switzerland or the EU. US-based AI tools create CLOUD Act exposure (US authorities can demand data from US providers regardless of where data is stored). Swiss-hosted alternatives (CASUS, Swiss-Noxtua) eliminate this exposure.

**Why this works:** Professional confidentiality obligations under Swiss law require data control. Using US-based AI tools without understanding CLOUD Act implications is a known risk that small firms are currently absorbing unknowingly.

### Decision 3: DPIA for High-Risk AI Processing
If the firm uses AI for contract analysis, due diligence, or legal opinion drafting — these likely constitute high-risk AI processing under FADP Art. 22 requiring a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA).

**Why this works:** Most small firms do not know a DPIA is required. Identifying this obligation creates urgency without being prescriptive about solutions.

### Decision 4: Privacy by Design AI Procurement Policy
Any new AI tool adopted by the firm must go through a procurement assessment covering: (1) data residency, (2) FADP compliance basis, (3) transparency obligations, (4) human review availability.

**Why this works:** Creates a simple, repeatable process that the managing partner can implement without external help initially. Reduces future compliance drift.

### Decision 5: Documentation and Staff Briefing
Document the firm's AI governance decisions. Brief all lawyers and staff on the policy. Assign responsibility for ongoing compliance.

**Why this works:** FDPIC expects purpose + functionality + data sources disclosure for AI deployments. Documentation demonstrates good faith. The FDPIC Feb 2026 joint statement on AI-generated images reinforced that transparency obligations are active.

5. Privacy by Design — AI procurement assessment required before adoption
