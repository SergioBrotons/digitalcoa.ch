# Findings Summary: Geneva Healthcare — C299 Phase 04

## 6 NET-NEW Findings: Distinct from C214–C298

---

### Finding 01 — Carrier Attestation for Medical Practices as Personal Liability Activation

**The finding:** Medical liability insurers are beginning to ask about AI tool usage at renewal. Small practices have no framework for answering these questions. The attestation asks for evidence of "adequate AI governance" — undefined for solo and small group practices. The personal signature activates personal exposure under professional liability standards before the practice knows what adequate means.

**Mechanism:** Carrier attestation forms for medical practices are asking AI governance questions designed for larger healthcare organizations. The small practice signs without documentation to support the representations made. If a claim occurs, the attestation becomes evidence of inadequate governance — signed by the physician personally.

**Implication:** Pre-signing review is the service that intercepts this activation. The physician needs to know what they are signing before they sign — and what documentation they need to have in place.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your carrier is asking about AI governance. Before you sign, you need to know what you're committing to — and have the documentation to support it."

### Finding 02 — FMH Human Oversight Without Operational Definition

**The finding:** FMH states human oversight requirement without providing operational definition. The requirement exists; the Tuesday translation does not. Small practices cannot determine what documented oversight looks like, what evidence satisfies the requirement, or how to handle AI tools where the underlying decision logic is not interpretable.

**Mechanism:** "Human oversight" is interpreted differently by different practices. Some document AI input as notes. Others document the final decision. None have a consistent protocol that would survive challenge. The FMH standard is intent-based; the evidence standard implied by attestation is output-based.

**Implication:** A documented human oversight protocol specific to small practice workflows is the missing tool. This is not about whether AI can be used — it is about how to document that human oversight occurred.

**Ready-to-use language:** "FMH says human oversight. It doesn't say what that looks like in your practice. I can show you what documented oversight looks like for a 3-person practice."

### Finding 03 — Patient-Generated AI Content as Discrete Documentation Category

**The finding:** Patients are beginning to arrive with consumer AI-generated health analyses. The physician must evaluate and document AI input they did not request, did not use, and may have disagreed with. No documentation category exists for this scenario. The highest-accountability moment — where the physician deliberately overrides or rejects patient-provided AI — is the least-documented.

**Mechanism:** When a patient brings in AI-generated analysis, the physician evaluates it, may disagree with it, and makes an independent clinical decision. The documentation normally records only the final decision. The AI's contribution to the patient's thinking, and the physician's evaluation of it, are invisible in the record.

**Implication:** A documentation category for patient-generated AI content is a discrete named service component. The protocol handles: documenting that AI input was evaluated, documenting the physician's independent judgment, and recording the final clinical decision.

**Ready-to-use language:** "When a patient brings in AI analysis, you have documentation obligations you probably haven't thought about. This is the highest-risk moment and the least-documented."

### Finding 04 — Swiss Hosting Confused with Swiss Data Protection Compliance

**The finding:** Medical practices selecting AI tools look for "Swiss hosting" as a compliance proxy. Swiss data residency and Swiss data protection compliance are different things. Most AI tools marketed to Swiss practices have US-based API processing, model training, or personnel access regardless of where data is stored. The Swiss hosting label creates false confidence.

**Mechanism:** The practice selects a Swiss-hosted tool, believes data protection compliance is addressed, and signs vendor terms without reviewing them. The vendor terms may include data retention, model training, or personnel access provisions that create nFADP compliance gaps. The practice has no monitoring process for vendor term changes.

**Implication:** A Swiss AI tool verification methodology specific to medical practice obligations is the missing service. The verification distinguishes storage location from processing location, reviews vendor terms for nFADP compliance, and identifies model training provisions.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Swiss hosting covers where data is stored. It doesn't cover how your AI tool processes that data or who can access it."

### Finding 05 — No Small-Practice AI Governance Framework Exists

**The finding:** All existing AI governance frameworks are designed for hospitals or large group practices. No framework is calibrated for 2–10 professional independent practices with no compliance infrastructure, no legal department, and no IT staff. The frameworks exist; the proportionate version does not.

**Mechanism:** The frameworks designed for hospitals (ISO 27001, NIST, etc.) require documentation infrastructure, formal policies, and regular audits that a 3-person practice cannot produce. The practice either ignores the framework or produces documentation that looks compliant but isn't. No middle ground exists.

**Implication:** A proportionate AI governance framework for small independent practices — 2–10 professionals, no compliance infrastructure — is a discrete named product. This is not a scaled-down hospital framework. It is a framework designed for how independent practices actually operate.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Everything that exists for AI governance was designed for a hospital. You're a 3-person practice. What you need is designed for your size."

---

### Finding 06 — The Productivity Trap in Medical Practices

**The finding:** Independent practices that want to use AI for productivity are constrained by the same trust deficit that affects other verticals. AI is used only for administrative tasks where errors have low consequence. The potential productivity gain in clinical workflows is not captured because the liability risk is too high and the trust is too low. The practice absorbs the manual overhead without capturing the productivity benefit.

**Mechanism:** The practice would use AI for clinical note documentation, referral letters, and patient communication — higher-value applications — but cannot trust the output sufficiently to accept liability for errors. AI is limited to lower-stakes administrative tasks where physician review is robust. The productivity trap is structural: the tasks where AI would help most are the tasks where liability risk is highest.

**Implication:** The governance framework that enables appropriate AI use at the right trust level — not overtrusted, not undertrusted — is the tool that unlocks productivity without unacceptable liability exposure.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You're using AI for the wrong tasks because you don't trust it enough for the right ones. A governance framework changes the trust calculus."
