# The Boutique Wealth Manager's AI Governance Readiness Checklist
**A strategic assessment for Geneva and Swiss private banks facing a new regulatory reality**

---

## Why This Checklist Now

Three things happened in the past 90 days that changed the calculus for Swiss wealth managers:

**1. FINMA's expectations became operational.** Guidance 08/2024 (December 2024) is not a future consultation — it's the current supervisory standard. Institutions using AI are already subject to on-site inspections that began in Q4 2023.

**2. The AI you already use became your liability.** The shift to agentic AI — autonomous systems that monitor communications, flag risks, and initiate portfolio actions — means governance gaps are no longer abstract compliance concerns. They are operational risk events waiting to happen.

**3. FDPIC enforcement is accelerating.** After targeting social media and retail, the regulator opened an investigation into a transport company's bodycam AI in February 2026. Private banking holds far more sensitive client data. The sector is not immune — it is next.

This checklist gives you a precise read on where you stand, what the gaps are, and what to do about them before either a regulator or a client incident forces the issue.

## Part 1: Shadow AI Audit — What Are You Actually Running?

Before you can govern AI, you need to know what exists. For most boutique wealth managers, this is the most uncomfortable exercise in the document.

### Decision 0: Inventory What You Already Use

- [ ] Do you have a documented inventory of every AI tool currently used by advisors, assistants, or operations staff?
- [ ] Do you know which of these tools handle client data — names, portfolios, communications, financial profiles?
- [ ] Do you know which tools make or influence decisions autonomously? (This is a new category created by agentic AI in 2026)
- [ ] Are any of these tools running without formal approval or oversight — shadow AI?

**The 2026 reality:** 40% of Swiss financial institutions are still in AI ideation or pilot phases (FINMA survey, November 2024–January 2025). Many are running AI tools informally while formal programs stall. Every informal tool is an ungoverned risk.

## Part 2: FINMA 08/2024 — Where Do You Stand?

FINMA's Guidance 08/2024 (December 18, 2024) establishes six concrete supervisory expectations. Assess yourself honestly.

### 2.1 AI Inventory and Risk Classification
- [ ] Is every AI tool — approved and unapproved — documented in a central inventory?
- [ ] Have you classified AI tools by risk level (low / medium / high)?
- [ ] Do high-risk AI tools (client decisioning, compliance monitoring) have documented governance?

### 2.2 Responsibility Assignment
- [ ] Is there a named accountable owner for every AI tool or system?
- [ ] Is it clear who is responsible for monitoring AI output quality?
- [ ] Is there an escalation path when an AI system produces unexpected results?

### 2.3 Data Quality and Integrity
- [ ] Are data inputs to AI systems validated for completeness and accuracy?
- [ ] Is client data used in AI applications protected against manipulation or corruption?
- [ ] Is your AI data lineage documented?

### 2.4 Testing, Monitoring, and Explainability
- [ ] Is AI system performance tested regularly, not just at deployment?
- [ ] Is there continuous monitoring of AI output quality and reliability?
- [ ] Can you explain AI-generated results to clients and regulators on demand?
- [ ] Is there independent verification of AI model development processes?

**The supervisory reality:** FINMA has identified persistent weaknesses in independent reviews of AI model development across Swiss financial institutions. This is a specific focus area for 2026 inspections.

## Part 3: FADP — Your Data Protection Obligations

The Federal Act on Data Protection has been operative since September 1, 2023. There is no grace period. For wealth managers, client financial data is among the most sensitive personal data regulated under the FADP.

### 3.1 Disclosures and Transparency
- [ ] Do your client communications disclose when AI is used in analysis, drafting, or communications?
- [ ] Do clients have a clear right to request human review of AI-assisted decisions?
- [ ] Can you demonstrate what data was processed by AI and for what purpose?

### 3.2 Training Data Rights
- [ ] Do you know whether client data submitted to AI tools was used to train or improve the underlying models?
- [ ] Are your AI vendors contractually obligated to disclose training data use?
- [ ] Do client agreements include FADP-compliant disclosures on AI processing?

### 3.3 Privacy by Design
- [ ] Is data minimization applied to AI tool deployments?
- [ ] Are AI tools configured to avoid retaining client data beyond what's necessary?
- [ ] Is there a process to respond to client requests for data access or deletion in the AI context?

## Part 4: CARF and CRS 2.0 — AML Obligations Already Running

Both CARF design constraints and CRS 2.0 became operative on January 1, 2026. If you are using AI for compliance monitoring, transaction surveillance, or client due diligence, these obligations already apply.

### 4.1 AI Monitoring Documentation
- [ ] Is AI-driven transaction monitoring documented as part of your AML framework?
- [ ] Are AI-generated alerts reviewed by qualified personnel before action?
- [ ] Is your AI AML tooling validated against current regulatory specifications?

### 4.2 CRS/CARF Data Integrity
- [ ] Is AI-assisted CRS/CARF reporting using validated data sources?
- [ ] Is TIN (Tax Identification Number) validation integrated into your AI-driven due diligence?

---

## Part 5: Agentic AI — The 2026 Governance Gap

Agentic AI represents a qualitative shift in how wealth managers use artificial intelligence. Unlike chatbots or document assistants, agentic systems execute tasks autonomously: monitoring inboxes, flagging compliance risks, initiating rebalancing, generating client reports.

This creates new governance categories that did not exist 18 months ago.

### 5.1 Autonomous Decision Documentation
- [ ] Do you have systems where AI initiates actions without human review in the loop?
- [ ] Is there a documented decision log for autonomous AI actions?
- [ ] Are there human override procedures for AI-initiated transactions or communications?

### 5.2 Agentic AI Risk Assessment
- [ ] Have you conducted a specific risk assessment for agentic AI deployments?
- [ ] Do your AI vendors provide audit trails for autonomous actions?
- [ ] Is your professional liability coverage adequate for autonomous AI decisions?

## Part 6: Your Competitive Position

The market is shifting. Clients — particularly UHNW individuals and family offices — are asking more sophisticated questions about data governance, AI transparency, and operational integrity. Firms that can demonstrate mature AI governance have a trust advantage in client acquisition and retention.

### 6.1 Trust as a Differentiator
- [ ] Can you demonstrate to a prospective client how their data is protected in your AI systems?
- [ ] Do you have a documented AI governance policy you can share with clients or prospects?
- [ ] Is your AI infrastructure Swiss-hosted or US-cloud? (CLOUD Act implications for non-Swiss infrastructure)

### 6.2 Sovereign AI Positioning
- [ ] Are you leveraging Swiss-hosted AI infrastructure as a data sovereignty selling point?
- [ ] Do your AI vendors offer Swiss data residency commitments in writing?
