# 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

*Phase 01 · Cycle 289 · 2026-05-28*

---

## Current Problems Geneva Small-Law Attorneys Are Experiencing

Market signals are observable events: what attorneys are doing, what is being demanded of them, where the system is visibly breaking down. This file captures what is happening now, as reported through Geneva professional service channels, carrier exchanges, bar communications, and institutional signals.

---

## Signal 1: Third-Generation Carrier Attestation Arriving Without Pre-Signing Review Infrastructure

The most acute current market signal is the third-generation carrier questionnaire arriving at small Geneva law firms. These questionnaires arrived with:
- Personal attestation language
- No pre-signing review service available in Geneva market
- No minimum viable protocol for a 2–5 attorney firm
- MD signature required, personally committing to an undefined adequacy standard

Small firms report receiving these questionnaires during routine PI policy renewals with no warning, no methodology guidance, and a signature deadline that does not allow time for proper review. Market signals indicate firms are signing under time pressure without understanding what they are committing to personally.

**The structural market failure:** No Geneva service offers independent pre-signing review of carrier AI attestation from the signatory's personal liability perspective. The instrument exists. The review function is absent.

---

## Signal 2: Client AI Governance Questionnaires as Unprepared Accountability Moments

Sophisticated clients — particularly international clients with multi-jurisdictional operations — are now sending law firms AI governance questionnaires. Geneva small-law firms report:
- Receiving 20–40 page questionnaires with no prior buildup
- No policy document to reference as basis for answers
- Inconsistent answers being given across different client conversations within the same firm
- No Geneva-specific template or methodology for response preparation

Market signals indicate that responses are being produced reactively by whoever receives the questionnaire, with no coherence check across the firm's overall AI governance position.

---

## Signal 3: Associate AI Use Operating Outside Supervisory Visibility

Small law firm partners report a specific operational failure mode: associates are producing AI-assisted legal documents at a quality level that the supervising partner cannot evaluate through normal review processes. The document reads correctly. The partner cannot determine:
- Whether the reasoning represents independent legal analysis
- What role the AI played in producing the conclusion
- Whether the AI produced the reasoning or merely the language of correct reasoning

The market signal is partners beginning to feel they must review AI-assisted work differently from traditionally produced work but having no defined review methodology for doing so. Staff are effectively operating AI systems with professional accountability remaining at partner level.

---

## Signal 4: Engagement Letter Architecture Obsolescence

The market signal: engagement letters written before firm's own AI adoption contain no AI authorization language, no disclosure to client, no methodology documentation obligation. Attorneys report:
- Discovering that active client matters are governed by contracts that predate the firm's AI adoption
- No protocol for determining what disclosure is appropriate for AI-assisted legal research
- Clients beginning to ask about AI methodology in the context of specific matters

A significant portion of small Geneva law firms are operating under engagement letter architectures that do not address AI at all. The gap is present-tense for active matters, not merely a future architecture question.

---

## Signal 5: Bar Guidance Produces Compliance Appearance Without Operational Substance

The SBA published AI principles. The Ordre des Avocats has communicated on AI. Market signals from small firm attorneys indicate:
- Having read available guidance without understanding what it requires of a 2–5 attorney firm
- Guidance written at a principle level requiring operational translation for small firm implementation
- No guidance specifically calibrated to what the Geneva small-law attorney actually does

The signal is that available guidance has confirmed that the guidance vacuum exists rather than resolving it. Attorneys report reading bar communications and concluding that available guidance confirms the absence of guidance calibrated to their scale.

---

## Signal 6: AI Tool Vendor Terms Remain Unaudited at Small-Firm Level

Geneva small-law attorneys report using AI research tools and document drafting tools under vendor terms they have not reviewed against their professional obligations. Specific failure modes:
- No attorney in a 2–5 person firm has evaluated what data the AI vendor retains
- No assessment of whether query metadata constitutes attorney-client privileged analytical material
- No evaluation of whether AI outputs qualify as attorney work product under Art.321 CP
- Platform selection based on cost and convenience, not professional obligation analysis

Market signals indicate that AI tool adoption has outpaced any systematic professional obligation review of those tools at the small-firm scale. Vendor terms are accepted by click-through. The professional obligation implications of those terms have not been evaluated.

---

## Signal 7: nFADP Article 21 as Emerging Adversarial Instrument

Market signals from litigation attorneys indicate that opposing counsel is beginning to probe how AI was used in case preparation. The specific mechanism:
- Requests for AI reasoning chains bypassing traditional privilege analysis
- Questions about what AI tools were used on specific matters
- No Geneva court established procedure for how these requests are handled

This signal indicates the privilege/professional obligation collision identified in prior research cycles is now beginning to surface in actual adversarial practice, not as theoretical risk but as present-tense procedural question.

---

## Signal 8: Geneva Sui Generis Accountability Formation Operating Without Small-Firm Knowledge

Sustained participation in Geneva accountability formation mechanisms is revealing that:
- Standards are forming through institutional interactions that small firms do not observe
- Carrier iterations responding to Geneva institutional signals create operative standards without small-firm participation
- The Geneva accountability formation process is operating faster than information circulates to the attorney level

Small-firm attorneys are arriving at carrier renewals and client questionnaires having been governed by standards they had no mechanism to observe forming. Market signals indicate widespread unawareness of how Geneva AI governance obligations are actually being set at the institutional level.

---

## Signal 9: AI Model Deprecation Creating Silent Research Continuity Gaps

Market signals from research-active practices indicate that AI tool model deprecations are creating undisclosed gaps in analytical continuity:
- Briefs and memoranda filed in 2024–2025 were produced under AI model versions no longer available
- Analytical frameworks underlying filed documents may have changed since production
- No advance notice protocol from Swiss-hosted platforms for model deprecation
- No retrieval protocol for accessing prior model versions

The specific legal professional exposure: research reproducibility questions arising when AI-assisted analytical work product is later challenged or examined. The market signal is attorneys discovering this gap only when a brief's underlying analysis is questioned — with no archival methodology in place at the time.

---

## Cross-Cutting Pattern

The 9 market signals converge on a single structural observation: **Geneva small-law attorneys are being governed by accountability mechanisms they did not design, cannot evaluate against published standards, and have not participated in forming.** The carrier attestation, client questionnaires, bar guidance, and Geneva institutional formation are all producing obligations that arrive at small firms as external events requiring retrospective response.

The market is generating accountability outcomes without generating the governance infrastructure that would allow small firms to produce those outcomes intentionally.
