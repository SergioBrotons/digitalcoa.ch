## 6 NET-NEW Findings (Phase 02 C313 — Distinct from C214–C312 and C313 Phase 01)

### Finding 01: DDQ Form-Completion Vector — The Institutional Response Loop

**The finding:** The institutional client is now offering (directly or through third-party DDQ-completion vendors) AI-assisted DDQ response tools. The Geneva boutique WM is using the tool to draft responses to the institutional DDQ by entering portfolio construction methodology, mandate-specific authorization chain logic, vendor processor chain details, model drift monitoring summaries — exactly the categories of information the DDQ is asking about. The DDQ-completion tool processes this through its own AI vendor's servers. The DDQ response is now being produced through a vendor the institutional client's own due diligence has not reviewed, with data flows the boutique's nFADP Art.9 review has not addressed. The exposure loop is closed at the institutional DDQ level, parallel to the C313 Phase 01 carrier form-completion finding. The response is generic — every boutique using the same tool produces indistinguishable language. The institutional client's investment committee reads past the generic response to find the boutique's specific authorization chain evidence. The boutique that submitted the form-completion response has no specific evidence behind the language.

**Mechanism:** Institutional client offers AI DDQ-completion tool → boutique enters portfolio construction methodology → DDQ-completion vendor processes mandate data through its AI → response generated → response submitted → DDQ response now incorporates content processed through a separately-disclosed vendor → nFADP Art.9 vector doubled → response is generic because every boutique using the same tool produces similar language → institutional client investment committee reads past generic language → boutique's specific authorization chain is invisible → mandate at risk.

**Implication:** DDQ form-completion vendor review is a discrete, named C313 service. It audits which DDQ-completion tool the boutique uses, what data that tool processes, whether its terms create nFADP Art.9 exposure, and what the alternative is (answering the DDQ directly with the boutique's own evidence architecture). The service also addresses the generic-response trap — the form-completion tool produces a response that looks like every other boutique's response. The boutique needs a specific authorization chain documentation architecture to anchor the response. The first service to publish the DDQ form-completion vendor review owns the C313 institutional DDQ category.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The AI tool you used to complete the institutional DDQ creates the same nFADP Art.9 exposure the DDQ is asking about. And your response now looks like everyone else's. I'm showing you how to break the loop and produce a response that anchors to your specific authorization chain."

### Finding 02: Mid-Year Institutional Questionnaire Cadence — Documentation Truncation Between Annual DDQ Cycles

Institutional clients that sent annual DDQs in Q4 2025 are now sending mid-year questionnaires in June/July 2026 — asking specifically "what changed in your AI governance in the last 90 days" and requesting updated authorization chain documentation, model drift monitoring evidence, and processor chain updates. The boutique's documentation is dated within days of the prior annual DDQ response. The mid-year questionnaire asks about freshness the boutique's documentation cycle isn't built for. Documentation aged within 90 days is the new freshness standard. Boutiques treating DDQ documentation as annual are now visibly behind at mid-year questionnaire moments. The gap widens with every institutional mid-year cycle.

**Mechanism:** Boutique submits annual DDQ response (e.g., November 2025) → institutional client sends mid-year questionnaire (June/July 2026) → boutique's documentation is 6-7 months old → mid-year asks "what changed in last 90 days" → boutique has no documentation of changes in last 90 days → institutional client investment committee checks documentation cadence as part of manager risk assessment → annual DDQ documentation cycle structurally behind mid-year questionnaire cadence → mandate at risk.

**Implication:** Mid-year documentation refresh is a discrete service. It produces DDQ documentation aged within days, refreshed quarterly, updated at new mandate start and vendor change moments, designed for the mid-year questionnaire request. This is the documentation cycle the C313 boutique WM MD needs and no current service delivers. The first service to publish a mid-year DDQ documentation refresh component owns the C313 mid-year category. The service is structurally parallel to the C313 Phase 01 mid-year carrier check-in documentation refresh, applied to the institutional client context.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your institutional client is now asking twice a year. Your DDQ documentation is annual. You're behind at every mid-year questionnaire. I'm refreshing the authorization chain documentation at the cadence your institutional client actually asks."

### Finding 03: New Institutional Mandate Authorization Discontinuity — The Mandate Handover Gap

**The finding:** The Geneva boutique WM that wins a new institutional mandate in 2026 creates the most concrete, time-stamped authorization chain gap in the wealth management market. New mandates begin with portfolio construction AI active within weeks of mandate start. The authorization chain documentation reflects pre-mandate state — the boutique's authorization register lists the AI tools, authorization levels, and supervision architecture for the prior mandate book, not the new mandate. The boutique cannot produce, on demand, the authorization chain documentation for the new mandate's portfolio construction AI. The handover window also includes mandate losses — when the boutique loses a mandate, the AI tools and authorization levels are deactivated (sometimes), but the AI-generated portfolio construction work product (historical recommendations, model tuning parameters, AI conversation history with the prior mandate's portfolio managers) remains in the boutique's systems. nFADP Art.9 obligations begin when data flows, not when contracts are reviewed — and data flows the moment a new mandate activates the AI tool with mandate data.

**Mechanism:** Boutique wins new institutional mandate (e.g., May 2026) → mandate start date triggers portfolio construction AI activation → authorization chain documentation reflects pre-mandate state → institutional client mid-year questionnaire (June/July) asks "what is your current authorization chain documentation for all mandates including new arrivals in last 90 days" → boutique cannot produce → mandate at risk → boutique loses mandate in Q4 → portfolio construction AI for that mandate is deactivated but AI-generated work product remains → boutique's Art.9 obligations for that work product continue.

**Implication:** Authorization chain documentation tied to mandate-change cadence — not annual DDQ cadence. The service produces authorization chain documentation refreshed at the rate of mandate book change, with documented handover for both mandate wins and mandate losses. This is the discrete, named service the C313 boutique WM MD needs and no competitor has built. The service is the WM-specific parallel to the C313 Phase 01 summer associate authorization handover documentation, applied to the institutional mandate context where "new arrival" is "new mandate."

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your authorization chain documentation was written before your new institutional mandate started. That mandate's portfolio construction AI is your liability. The documentation cycle is annual; the mandate cycle is faster. I'm refreshing the authorization chain at the cadence your mandate book actually changes."

### Finding 04: Portfolio Construction Vendor Data Residency as False Reassurance — The Sub-Processor Lens

**The finding:** The Geneva boutique WM that selected a "Swiss-hosted" portfolio construction AI vendor on nFADP Art.9 residency grounds has a documented misconception. Article 9 is not satisfied by data residency alone. The Swiss-hosted portfolio construction vendor's sub-processors (logging, telemetry, authentication, monitoring) operate extraterritorially. The vendor's parent company's cloud infrastructure (when non-Swiss) inherits CLOUD Act extraterritorial access. The metadata — query classifications, mandate identifiers, model parameters, AI conversation history with portfolio managers — routes through infrastructure that doesn't honor Swiss borders. The residency claim is a marketing claim. The boutique's nFADP Art.9 obligation reads through the marketing.

**Mechanism:** Boutique selects Swiss-hosted portfolio construction vendor on Art.9 residency grounds → vendor's terms disclose sub-processors (extraterritorially) → metadata routes through parent-company infrastructure → boutique believes Art.9 satisfied → exposure persists → institutional client DDQ asks vendor contract review → vendor disclosure reveals sub-processor chain → Art.9 exposure visible → boutique's documented misconception is documented → institutional client mid-year questionnaire flags it → mandate at risk.

**Implication:** Swiss-hosted portfolio construction vendor sub-processor audit is a discrete service. It maps the vendor's sub-processor chain (logging, telemetry, auth, monitoring, parent-company cloud) against nFADP Art.9 requirements, identifies which sub-processors create extraterritorial exposure, and produces a sub-processor audit memo the boutique can attach to its DDQ response. The service is bounded, named, and triggerable at any vendor review or new vendor selection moment. The first service to publish a portfolio construction vendor sub-processor audit owns the C313 vendor review category for boutique WM.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your Swiss-hosted portfolio construction vendor still routes mandate metadata through U.S. cloud infrastructure. Article 9 reads through the marketing. I'm auditing the sub-processors your vendor didn't disclose at the front of the contract."

### Finding 05: Peer DDQ Response Visibility — The Geneva WM Conference Benchmark

**The finding:** The June 2026 Geneva WM conference season (Geneva Private Banking Week, Geneva Family Office Forum, Swiss WM Association summer sessions) produced public disclosures of named-firm DDQ response patterns (with firm consent). The boutique WM MD now has visibility into what specific peer firms declared to their institutional clients — not inference from peer-group averages, but direct reading of named-firm DDQ response language. They are discovering their DDQ response was less specific, less defensible, less well-formed than what named peers produced. The comparison trap has moved from peer inference to direct comparison. The MD is also discovering that several named peers' responses look generic — they used the same DDQ form-completion vendor tool. The boutique that submitted a generic response is now visibly indistinguishable from the form-completion vendor user group. The gap is visible in writing. The boutique carries the visibility whether they want it or not.

**Mechanism:** Geneva WM conference (June 2026) → named firms consent to disclosure of DDQ response patterns → public summary published → institutional clients, peers, and consultants read each other's responses → boutique WM MD reads peer A's DDQ response (specific, defensible, anchored to authorization chain evidence) → boutique MD reads peer B's DDQ response (generic, indistinguishable, likely form-completion vendor output) → boutique MD reads their own DDQ response (generic because they used the form-completion vendor) → MD realizes they are in peer B's category, not peer A's → gap visible in writing → institutional client investment committee reads the same peer benchmarks → mandate at risk.

**Implication:** Peer-DDQ gap analysis is a discrete service. It maps the boutique's current DDQ response against the public benchmark, identifies specific gaps in specificity, defensibility, and formation, and produces a documented comparison the boutique can use to update their DDQ response position before the next institutional cycle. The service also addresses the form-completion vendor trap — it identifies whether the boutique's response is anchored to specific authorization chain evidence or generated through a form-completion tool. The service is peer-benchmarked, documented, and bounded — a discrete, named C313 offering for boutique WM. The service is the WM-specific parallel to the C313 Phase 01 peer-attestation gap analysis, applied to the institutional DDQ context.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You saw what your peer boutiques declared at the Geneva WM conference. The benchmark is now public. I'm mapping the gap between your DDQ response and theirs — and showing you whether your response is anchored to your specific authorization chain or generated by the same form-completion vendor the other generic responses came from."

### Finding 06: AI Vendor Succession / Data Portability — Portfolio Construction Mandate Data Continuity Gap

**The finding:** The portfolio construction AI vendor may go bankrupt, get acquired by a larger player, terminate the service, or pivot the offering. When this happens, the boutique's institutional client mandate data — historical allocation recommendations, model tuning parameters, mandate-specific AI conversation history, portfolio manager workflow patterns — transfers with the vendor's asset sale to a new controller who did not negotiate the boutique's nFADP Art.9 obligations or the boutique's contractual obligations to the institutional client. The boutique is now in privity with a new data controller without their consent. The institutional client did not consent to the new controller either. nFADP Art.9 doesn't terminate on vendor insolvency — the obligation transfers to the legal entity that holds it (the boutique), and the boutique becomes accountable for the mandate data's protection downstream of the asset transfer. The boutique's contractual obligations to the institutional client (data processing agreements, confidentiality commitments, professional secrecy) survive vendor exit. The institutional client's mandate data is being processed by a successor who didn't inherit any of these obligations. This exposure is unnamed in vendor contracts, unspecified in institutional client DDQ responses, and unaddressed by any current service.

**Mechanism:** Portfolio construction vendor files for bankruptcy / signs asset purchase / terminates service → institutional client mandate data transfers with asset sale to new controller → new controller did not inherit boutique's Art.9 obligations or contractual obligations to institutional client → institutional client's mandate portfolio construction methodology, tuning data, AI conversation history is now being processed by a successor without consent → Art.9 obligation transfers to boutique (still legal entity) → boutique is professionally responsible for mandate data protection the successor isn't providing → institutional client learns of vendor exit → institutional client mid-year questionnaire asks about vendor succession → boutique's DDQ response now includes vendor-exit exposure the boutique cannot satisfy → mandate at risk.

**Implication:** Portfolio construction vendor succession / data portability planning is a discrete service. It includes: pre-vendor-selection succession terms in vendor contracts (what happens on insolvency, acquisition, termination — including mandate data export obligations, transition period, controller-change disclosure to institutional clients); mid-vendor-relationship succession monitoring (vendor financial signals, acquisition signals, service-termination signals); post-vendor-exit response (data portability execution, controller-change documentation, successor-controller audit, institutional client notification architecture). The service is novel, discrete, and triggerable at any vendor exit signal. The first service to publish a portfolio construction vendor succession methodology owns the category. The service is the WM-specific parallel to the C313 Phase 01 vendor succession planning finding, applied to portfolio construction mandate data with its specific institutional client contractual complexity.

**Ready-to-use language:** "When your portfolio construction AI vendor goes bankrupt or gets acquired, your institutional client mandate data transfers to the buyer. nFADP Art.9 doesn't transfer with it. Your contractual obligations to the institutional client don't transfer with it. Your obligations stay. I'm building the succession terms into your vendor contracts now, before the exit happens."
