# Compliance Context: Geneva Finance — C313 Phase 02

## Boutique Wealth Management Firms, Post-31-Day Pause, July 2026

---

## Regulatory Framework Summary

Geneva boutique WM firms at 50–200 employees face a post-pause C313 dual-accountability structure: institutional client DDQ now operating on a twice-yearly cadence (annual DDQ + mid-year questionnaire) with the institutional client or third-party vendor offering AI-assisted DDQ form-completion tools that create nFADP Art.9 exposure loops, while carrier attestation renewal continues to operate on its own twice-yearly cadence (per C313 Phase 01 legal). New institutional mandate wins create authorization chain handover gaps within weeks of mandate start. The "Swiss-hosted" portfolio construction vendor selection is a documented nFADP Art.9 misconception. Geneva WM conference disclosures have moved the peer DDQ benchmark from inference to direct comparison. Portfolio construction vendor succession creates mandate data continuity exposure the vendor contract does not address.

The C313 compliance context is the C312 compliance context (FINMA Guidance 08/2024, nFADP, institutional DDQ as competitive gate, SRO silence, carrier attestation renewal) plus six new C313 conditions specific to the post-pause institutional DDQ environment. The six NET-NEW compliance findings in Phase 02 surface specific structural intersections between these regulatory frameworks in the post-pause boutique WM context.

## 6 NET-NEW Compliance Findings (Distinct from C312 and C313 Phase 01)

### Finding 01: nFADP Art.9 DDQ Form-Completion Vector — The Institutional Response Loop Exposure

**Mechanism:** The institutional client is now offering (directly or through third-party DDQ-completion vendors) AI-assisted DDQ response tools. The Geneva boutique WM is using the tool to draft DDQ responses by entering portfolio construction methodology, mandate-specific authorization chain logic, vendor processor chain details — the very categories the DDQ asks about. The DDQ-completion tool processes this through its own AI vendor's servers, creating a parallel nFADP Art.9 processor chain the institutional client's own due diligence has not reviewed. The boutique's Art.9 obligations to the institutional client (data processing agreement, processor terms, sub-processor disclosure) are now being satisfied — or not — through a vendor the boutique didn't separately contract with. The exposure is doubled: the DDQ form-completion vendor processes the same institutional client mandate data the original portfolio construction vendor processes, but through different terms, different sub-processors, and different controller relationships. The boutique's documented Art.9 compliance is now structurally dependent on a vendor it didn't evaluate.

**C313 Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 named the carrier form-completion loop for legal (Article 321); Phase 02 names the DDQ form-completion loop for WM (Article 9). Same mechanism (form-completion vendor processes form data through undisclosed AI vendor), different document (carrier attestation vs. institutional DDQ), different obligation framework.

**Regulatory intersection:** nFADP Art.9 processor obligations + institutional client DDQ data flow + DDQ form-completion vendor terms = boutiques cannot document Art.9 compliance for the very DDQ response they submitted because the response was processed through a vendor whose terms create Art.9 exposure the boutique didn't evaluate.

**ICP language:** "I used the AI tool my institutional client offered to fill in their DDQ. I didn't realize the tool processes my portfolio construction methodology through its own vendor servers. Now I have two vendors processing institutional client mandate data — and I only reviewed one." / "The DDQ response I submitted looks the same as everyone else's. The institutional client read past my response to peer A's response because peer A's was anchored to their actual authorization chain. Mine was generic."

**FR:** "J'ai utilisé l'outil IA offert par le client institutionnel pour remplir son DDQ. Je n'ai pas réalisé que l'outil traite ma méthodologie de construction de portefeuille via ses propres serveurs fournisseurs. Maintenant j'ai deux fournisseurs qui traitent les données de mandat — et je n'en ai révisé qu'un."

**IMO compliance angle:** DDQ form-completion vendor review as discrete service — audits which form-completion tool the boutique uses, what data that tool processes, whether its terms create Art.9 exposure, and what the alternative direct-response methodology is.

### Finding 02: Mid-Year Institutional Questionnaire Cadence — Documentation Adequacy Window Compressing at Twice-Yearly Rate

**Mechanism:** Institutional clients sending mid-year questionnaires (June/July 2026) to Geneva boutique WM firms are operating on a twice-yearly cadence that did not exist 12 months ago. The boutique's authorization chain documentation cycle is annual, dated within days of the prior annual DDQ response (typically Q4 2025). The mid-year questionnaire asks "what changed in your AI governance in the last 90 days" — and the boutique's documentation cannot answer the question because the cycle is annual. The boutique's documented AI governance is now structurally behind the institutional client's cadence. The FINMA Guidance 08/2024 proportionate framework principle remains the operative regulatory expectation, but the principle does not address cadence — only "proportionality" of the framework to firm size and risk. Twice-yearly cadence is the new freshness standard, defined by institutional client practice rather than regulatory guidance. Boutiques treating documentation as annual are now visibly behind at mid-year questionnaire moments. The gap widens with every institutional mid-year cycle.

**C313 Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 named the mid-year carrier check-in cadence for legal; Phase 02 names the mid-year institutional questionnaire cadence for WM. Same mechanism (twice-yearly institutional cadence vs. annual documentation cycle), different forcing function (carrier vs. institutional client).

**Regulatory intersection:** FINMA Guidance 08/2024 proportionate framework (principle without cadence) + institutional client mid-year questionnaire cadence (operational practice) + carrier attestation renewal cycle (parallel cadence) = boutique documented AI governance now structurally behind institutional client cadence on two parallel tracks simultaneously.

**ICP language:** "My institutional client sent a mid-year questionnaire. They want to know what changed in 90 days. My documentation is from the annual DDQ response six months ago. The cycle doesn't match." / "I thought I needed annual documentation. Now I need quarterly documentation. Every mid-year questionnaire is a documentation freshness check I can't pass."

**FR:** "Mon client institutionnel a envoyé un questionnaire à mi-année. La documentation est datée de six mois. Le cycle ne correspond pas."

**IMO compliance angle:** Mid-year institutional questionnaire documentation refresh as discrete service — produces DDQ documentation aged within days, refreshed quarterly, updated at mandate change and vendor change moments, designed for the mid-year questionnaire request.

### Finding 03: New Institutional Mandate Authorization Discontinuity — Mandate Handover Creates Documented nFADP Art.9 Gap

**Mechanism:** Geneva boutique WM firms winning new institutional mandates in 2026 create documented authorization chain handover gaps within weeks of mandate start. The new mandate's portfolio construction AI is active. The boutique's authorization chain documentation — what AI tools are authorized for the mandate, what authorization levels apply, what supervision architecture governs portfolio managers using the AI for the new mandate, what sub-processors are documented for the new mandate's data — reflects pre-mandate state. The nFADP Art.9 obligation to maintain documented processor chains for institutional client mandate data begins when data flows, not when contracts are reviewed. The mandate's portfolio data flows the moment the AI tool is activated with the mandate. The boutique cannot produce, on demand, the authorization chain documentation for the new mandate because the documentation cycle hasn't caught up.

The handover window also includes mandate losses. When the boutique loses an institutional mandate, the AI tools and authorization levels are deactivated (sometimes), but the AI-generated portfolio construction work product (historical recommendations, model tuning parameters, AI conversation history with portfolio managers for the prior mandate) remains in the boutique's systems. nFADP Art.9 obligations continue for retained work product. The boutique's authorization documentation does not reflect the deactivation or the retained work product.

**C313 Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 named the summer associate authorization discontinuity for legal; Phase 02 names the new institutional mandate authorization discontinuity for WM. Same mechanism (new "arrival" creates authorization gap), different "arrival" type (associate vs. mandate), different obligation framework.

**Regulatory intersection:** nFADP Art.9 documented processor obligations + nFADP Art.5 lawful basis for mandate data processing + FINMA Guidance 08/2024 authorization documentation expectation + institutional client contractual data processing terms = four-track authorization documentation requirement at mandate change moments that no current documentation cycle addresses.

**ICP language:** "We won a new institutional mandate in May. The AI portfolio construction tool is active. Our authorization chain documentation is from last year. The institutional client will ask for current authorization at the mid-year questionnaire." / "When we lost the Q1 mandate, we deactivated the AI tool for that mandate. But the AI-generated recommendations are still in our systems. The Art.9 obligation didn't end with the mandate."

**IMO compliance angle:** Mandate-handover authorization documentation as discrete service — produces authorization chain documentation refreshed at the rate of mandate book change, with documented handover for both mandate wins and mandate losses. The service addresses both the activation gap (new mandate AI use without authorization documentation) and the deactivation gap (lost mandate AI-generated work product retention).

### Finding 04: nFADP Art.9 Portfolio Construction Vendor Residency Fallacy — The Sub-Processor Lens Applied to Portfolio Construction

**Mechanism:** The Geneva boutique WM that selected a "Swiss-hosted" portfolio construction AI vendor on nFADP Art.9 residency grounds has a documented misconception. Article 9 is not satisfied by data residency alone. The Swiss-hosted portfolio construction vendor's sub-processors (logging, telemetry, authentication, monitoring, parent-company cloud infrastructure) operate extraterritorially. The metadata — query classifications, mandate identifiers, model parameters, AI conversation history with portfolio managers — routes through infrastructure that doesn't honor Swiss borders. The vendor's parent-company cloud infrastructure (when non-Swiss, which is the common case for portfolio construction AI vendors marketed as "Swiss-hosted") inherits CLOUD Act extraterritorial access. The boutique's nFADP Art.9 obligation reads through the marketing claim.

The institutional client's DDQ asks about vendor processor chain. The boutique's DDQ response refers to the "Swiss-hosted" vendor. The institutional client's investment committee reads the response. The boutique cannot produce the sub-processor chain documentation because the vendor's terms create undisclosed processing the boutique accepted without evaluation. The institutional client identifies the gap at the next DDQ cycle. The boutique's documented Art.9 compliance is exposed as a documented misconception.

**C313 Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 named the Swiss-hosted vendor residency fallacy for legal (Article 321); Phase 02 names the portfolio construction vendor residency fallacy for WM (Article 9). Same mechanism (Swiss residency doesn't satisfy data protection / professional secrecy obligation), different obligation framework (legal professional secrecy vs. financial data protection).

**Regulatory intersection:** nFADP Art.9 processor obligations + nFADP Art.5 lawful basis + institutional client DDQ vendor processor questions + FINMA Guidance 08/2024 vendor due diligence expectations = four-track vendor review requirement that the "Swiss-hosted" marketing claim doesn't satisfy.

**ICP language:** "We selected our portfolio construction vendor because they said Swiss-hosted. Our institutional client is asking about the vendor's sub-processors. I just realized I don't know who the sub-processors are." / "The vendor's parent company is US-based. Their cloud infrastructure routes through US servers. We thought Swiss-hosted meant Swiss-resident. It didn't."

**IMO compliance angle:** Portfolio construction vendor sub-processor audit as discrete service — maps vendor sub-processor chain against nFADP Art.9 requirements, identifies extraterritorial exposure, and produces sub-processor audit memo the boutique can attach to DDQ response.

### Finding 05: Geneva WM Conference Peer DDQ Benchmark — Public Benchmark Activates Cross-Mandate Response Coherence Standard

**Mechanism:** June 2026 Geneva WM conference season (Geneva Private Banking Week, Geneva Family Office Forum, Swiss WM Association summer sessions) produced public disclosures of named-firm DDQ response patterns (with firm consent). The institutional clients attending these conferences are now reading peer DDQ responses. The boutique WM MD is now reading peer DDQ responses. The institutional client's investment committee is now reading peer DDQ responses. The peer benchmark has moved from inference to direct comparison. The FINMA Guidance 08/2024 proportionate framework principle remains the regulatory expectation, but the operative standard for "adequate" is now being defined by what named peers have committed to in their DDQ responses. Boutique WM firms submitting DDQ responses that are less specific, less defensible, or more generic than named peers are now visibly behind — and the visibility is in writing, distributed through the conference materials, and discussed in the institutional client investment committees.

The form-completion vendor trap is now publicly visible at Geneva WM conferences. The peer DDQ responses that look generic are identifiable as form-completion vendor output. The boutique WM MD who used the form-completion vendor tool is now visibly grouped with other form-completion vendor users. The institutional client's investment committee reads the form-completion vendor output as one category and the specific authorization chain anchored output as another category. The form-completion vendor category is structurally disadvantaged.

**C313 Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 named the peer signature visibility at bar conference for legal; Phase 02 names the peer DDQ response visibility at Geneva WM conference for WM. Same mechanism (peer benchmark moved from inference to direct comparison), different document (carrier attestation vs. institutional DDQ response), different conference type.

**Regulatory intersection:** FINMA Guidance 08/2024 proportionate framework + institutional client DDQ practice + Geneva WM conference peer disclosure practice = three-track activation of peer DDQ benchmark as operative standard for "adequate" without any regulatory codification.

**ICP language:** "I attended the Geneva WM conference. I saw what three named boutiques declared in their DDQ responses. Their language was specific, anchored to their authorization chain. Mine was generic because I used the form-completion tool. I'm in the wrong category." / "The institutional client's investment committee is reading peer DDQ responses at the conference. They know my response was form-completion vendor output. They know peer A's was anchored to their authorization chain."

**IMO compliance angle:** Peer DDQ response gap analysis + form-completion vendor identification as discrete service — maps boutique DDQ response against Geneva WM conference peer benchmarks; identifies whether response is anchored to specific authorization chain evidence or generated through form-completion vendor tool; produces documented comparison for next DDQ cycle.

### Finding 06: Portfolio Construction Vendor Succession — Mandate Data Continuity Gap with Dual-Obligation Architecture

**Mechanism:** The Geneva boutique WM portfolio construction AI vendor may go bankrupt, get acquired by a larger player, terminate the service, or pivot the offering. When this happens, the institutional client mandate data transfers with the asset sale to a new controller. The new controller inherits the data without inheriting the boutique's nFADP Art.9 obligations or the boutique's contractual obligations to the institutional client (data processing agreements, confidentiality commitments, professional secrecy terms). The boutique is now in privity with a new data controller without their consent. The institutional client did not consent to the new controller either. The boutique's Art.9 obligations don't terminate on vendor insolvency — the obligation transfers to the legal entity that holds it (the boutique), and the boutique becomes accountable for the mandate data's protection downstream of the asset transfer. The boutique's contractual obligations to the institutional client survive vendor exit. The institutional client's mandate data is being processed by a successor who didn't inherit any of these obligations.

The exposure is dual: nFADP Art.9 obligations the boutique is personally accountable for + contractual obligations to the institutional client the boutique is contractually bound by. The vendor's exit triggers both. The boutique's documented AI governance does not address either. The institutional client's mid-year questionnaire asks about vendor continuity. The boutique cannot produce evidence of vendor succession planning because no such planning exists.

**C313 Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 named the vendor succession / data portability gap for legal (Article 321 obligations); Phase 02 names the portfolio construction vendor succession gap for WM (Article 9 obligations + institutional client contractual obligations). Same mechanism (vendor exit creates data exposure the boutique is accountable for), different obligation framework.

**Regulatory intersection:** nFADP Art.9 processor obligations (survive vendor exit) + nFADP Art.5 lawful basis (must be reassessed on controller change) + institutional client contractual data processing obligations (survive vendor exit) + FINMA Guidance 08/2024 vendor risk management expectations = four-track vendor succession obligation architecture with no current vendor succession methodology.

**ICP language:** "My portfolio construction AI vendor was acquired. The acquirer now holds my institutional client mandate data. They didn't inherit my Art.9 obligations or my contractual obligations to the institutional client. I'm still accountable. What do I do?" / "When I selected my portfolio construction vendor, I asked about data residency. I didn't ask about what happens on acquisition. Now the question is live and I don't have an answer."

**IMO compliance angle:** Portfolio construction vendor succession / data portability planning as discrete service — pre-vendor-selection succession terms in vendor contracts, mid-vendor-relationship succession monitoring, post-vendor-exit response architecture including institutional client notification.
