## English

### The Post-Pause ICP: "I Thought Things Had Calmed Down"

They hadn't.

For the boutique WM managing director, the four-week pause between late May and July 1 didn't feel like a pause. The institutional client stopped sending DDQs. The carrier stopped asking questions. The FINMA Newsletter went quiet. The Geneva WM summer circuit — usually starting mid-June — looked like it might be a lighter cycle. The MD assumed the regulatory and client pressure had settled.

Then July arrived. And the world looked different.

**The pause betrayed them. The market kept moving.**

### What Changed During the Pause (Boutique WM Lens)

Three things happened during what was not a pause at all:

*First:* **the institutional client now sends mid-year questionnaires between annual DDQs.** The boutique's authorization chain documentation is dated within days of the prior annual DDQ response. The mid-year questionnaire asks "what changed in last 90 days" — and the boutique's documentation cycle is annual. The MD didn't expect to answer institutional AI governance questions twice in the same year. They treat it as anomalous. It isn't. It's a new cycle.

*Second:* **the institutional client is now offering AI-assisted DDQ completion.** Same as carrier form-completion (C313 Phase 01 Finding 01), but applied to the institutional DDQ. The boutique enters portfolio construction methodology, mandate-specific authorization chain logic, vendor processor disclosures — into the DDQ vendor's form-completion tool. The tool processes the response through its own AI vendor. The boutique's institutional DDQ answer now passes through a vendor the institutional client's own due diligence has not reviewed. The response is generic — every boutique using the same tool produces indistinguishable language.

*Third:* **June 2026 Geneva WM conference disclosures produced public peer DDQ benchmarks.** Geneva Private Banking Week, the Geneva Family Office Forum, and the Swiss WM Association summer sessions all produced public disclosures of named-firm DDQ response patterns (with firm consent). The MD now has visibility into what specific peer boutiques committed to in their institutional client responses — not inference from peer-group averages, but direct reading of named-firm DDQ language. They are discovering their DDQ response was less specific, less defensible, less well-formed than what named peers produced. The comparison trap has moved from inference to direct comparison.

And the new mandate the boutique won in May? The portfolio construction AI is now active. The authorization chain documentation reflects pre-mandate state. The mandate arrived and the documentation didn't catch up.

The institutional client's DDQ answer now passes through a vendor the institutional client has not separately evaluated. The exposure loop is closed at the DDQ level.

**"We just won a new institutional mandate in May. The AI portfolio construction tool is live. The authorization chain documentation is from last year."**
New mandate authorization discontinuity. The boutique WM that won a new institutional mandate creates a documented authorization chain gap. The mandate's portfolio construction AI is active. The authorization chain documentation reflects pre-mandate state. The mandate arrived and the documentation didn't catch up — same mechanism as the C313 Phase 01 legal summer associate handover, applied to the boutique WM context where "new arrival" is "new institutional mandate."

**"I switched to a Swiss-hosted portfolio construction AI vendor. I thought that resolved the nFADP Art.9 issue."**
The portfolio construction residency fallacy. The boutique that moved from a US-hosted AI vendor to a Swiss-hosted one on residency grounds believes they have resolved their nFADP Art.9 processor chain exposure. They haven't. The portfolio construction vendor's sub-processors (logging, telemetry, authentication, monitoring) operate extraterritorially. The vendor's parent company's cloud infrastructure inherits CLOUD Act exposure. The metadata — query classifications, mandate identifiers, model parameters — routes through infrastructure that doesn't honor Swiss borders. The residency claim is a marketing claim. nFADP Art.9 looks through it.

**"I saw what three other Geneva boutiques declared at the WM conference. I didn't submit anything like that."**
Public DDQ response visibility. Geneva WM conference season has produced public disclosures of named-firm DDQ response patterns. The MD now has visibility into what peers actually submitted. They are discovering their DDQ response was less specific, less defensible, less well-formed than what named peers produced. The comparison trap (C310 Finding 02) has moved from inference to direct comparison. The gap is now visible in writing.

**"What happens to my clients' portfolio construction data if my AI vendor disappears?"**
Vendor succession exposure for portfolio construction data. The AI vendor question now includes the worst-case scenario: what happens when the portfolio construction AI vendor goes bankrupt, gets acquired, or terminates the service? The data the boutique has been processing — institutional client mandate parameters, historical allocation recommendations, model tuning data, AI conversation history with portfolio managers — transfers with the vendor's asset sale. nFADP Art.9 obligations don't terminate on vendor insolvency. The new controller inherits mandate data without inheriting the boutique's contractual obligations to the institutional client. The succession gap is unnamed in vendor contracts.

## The Institutional DDQ Form-Completion Loop (C313 Specific)

The most distinctive C313 Phase 02 ICP language pattern is the discovery that the institutional DDQ is now being answered with AI assistance — and that the AI tool is being offered by the institutional client or by a third-party DDQ-completion vendor.

**"I used the client's AI form tool. They offered it. Why wouldn't I?"**

The institutional client offered the DDQ-completion tool as a "convenience" — "let our AI help you draft your response." The MD didn't realize the tool would process portfolio construction methodology, mandate-specific authorization chain logic, and vendor processor chain details through a vendor the institutional client's own due diligence has not reviewed. The MD didn't realize the tool produces a generic response — every boutique using the same tool produces indistinguishable language. The MD didn't realize the form-completion tool creates the same nFADP Art.9 exposure the DDQ is asking about.

**"The response looks the same as everyone else's. I thought that was good."**

The MD is discovering that the DDQ response generated by the form-completion tool is structurally similar to other boutiques' responses. They thought this was reassuring — "we're all saying the same thing." It isn't. It's the marker of a form-completion vendor response, not a firm-specific evidence-based response. The institutional client's investment committee will read past generic responses to the boutique's specific authorization chain documentation. The boutique that submitted the form-completion response has no specific evidence behind the language.

This is the C313 Phase 02 ICP language pattern: "I thought I was being efficient. I was being generic. Now my DDQ response looks like everyone else's — and my mandate is at risk because my specific authorization chain isn't visible."
