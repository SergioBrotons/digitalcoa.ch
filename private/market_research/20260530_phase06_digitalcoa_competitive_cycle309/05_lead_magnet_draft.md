## You Have Three Problems Simultaneously

### What I See Every Week in My Practice

Every week, a physician, a lawyer, a consultant, or an accounting professional sits across from me and says the same thing:

*"I got a form from my insurance broker. It's asking about AI. I don't know what they're asking for, and I don't have the documentation to answer it."*

---

## The Three Problems You Have Right Now

### Problem One: The Form Has Your Name On It

The carrier attestation form doesn't say "your firm." It says your name. The physician's name. The lawyer's name. Your name. When you sign it, you are signing personally. Not the firm, not the practice — you.

Under nFADP Article 63, you are personally liable for decisions made with AI that you cannot document human oversight for. The form isn't asking about your firm's AI policy. It's asking about your personal supervision of every AI-assisted decision you make.

**If you cannot produce evidence of personal supervision for AI decisions made in the past 12 months, the form is a personal liability document. And you're signing it anyway.**

---

### Problem Two: Your Vendor Has the Records

Your AI vendor — the company that provides the AI tools you use — holds records of every query you made, every document you processed, every decision you sought AI assistance on. These are your records. But you don't have them. The vendor does.

Your carrier is now asking for those records. "Provide records of AI-assisted decisions from your vendor." You can't. You didn't negotiate record production rights when you signed the vendor contract. Most vendor contracts are silent on this. The records exist. You don't have them. You can't produce them.

**Your carrier is asking for records held by a third party you have no contractual obligation to produce from. And you're still personally liable for what those records show.**

---

### Problem Three: Your Staff Is Using AI You Didn't Authorize

You delegated work to your associate. Your associate used AI. You didn't authorize the AI tool. You don't know which AI tool. You don't know what they asked it. You don't know what it produced. But the carrier is now asking: "How do you supervise AI-assisted decisions made by your staff?"

Invisible delegation. You didn't authorize it. You don't monitor it. You don't have documentation for it. But your name is on the form, and the carrier's question is real.

**Your staff's unauthorized AI use is visible to your carrier. You are personally responsible for it. You have no documentation.**

## The Synthesis

You have three problems simultaneously:
1. **The form has your name on it.** Personal accountability — your personal assets, not just the firm's.
2. **Your vendor has the records.** Carrier asking for records you don't hold, from a vendor you didn't contract for record production.
3. **Your staff is using AI you didn't authorize.** Invisible delegation, carrier-visible, documentation-absent.

These are not three separate issues. They are one problem with three dimensions — a documentation debt that has become a personal liability problem.

## Why the Market Offers Frameworks, and Why That Is Not What You Need

The market has responded to AI accountability by offering frameworks. Policy documents. Checklists. Governance structures.

I have reviewed dozens of these frameworks. They are written for organizations, not individuals. They are designed to look impressive in a presentation, not to produce evidence that survives carrier scrutiny. They are forward-looking — they document decisions going forward, not decisions already made.

**What the market offers:** A document that says "we have an AI governance policy."

**What you need:** A system that produces evidence of every AI-assisted decision you make, documented in a way that can be reconstructed, that answers your carrier attestation, that addresses your personal liability exposure, that handles vendor records, that documents your staff's AI use.

These are not the same product. The market is selling the first. You need the second.

## Les trois problèmes que vous avez en ce moment

### Problème un : Le formulaire porte votre nom

Le formulaire d'attestation de l'assureur ne dit pas « votre cabinet ». Il dit votre nom. Le nom du médecin. Le nom de l'avocat. Votre nom. Quand vous le signez, vous signez à titre personnel. Pas le cabinet, pas l'étude — vous.

**Votre assureur demande des dossiers détenus par un tiers pour lequel vous n'avez aucune obligation contractuelle de production. Et vous êtes toujours personnellement responsable de ce que ces dossiers révèlent.**

## 6 NET-NEW Lead Magnet Findings for Phase 06

### Finding 1: Lead with the Three Problems, Not the Solution

The C309 insight — three simultaneous problems — is itself the lead magnet. ICPs recognize themselves in "you have three problems simultaneously" more than they recognize themselves in "evidence architecture." Lead with the problem structure, then introduce the solution.

### Finding 2: "Your Name Is on the Form" Is the Highest-Resonance Opening

Personal accountability activation is the first of the three problems. Opening with "the form has your name on it" immediately personalizes the issue and triggers the ICP's deepest concern. No competitor opens with personal liability; all open with organizational governance. This is独占 and high-resonance.

### Finding 3: Vendor Records Must Be Named as a Distinct Problem

Vendor records — "your vendor has the records you need and you can't produce them" — is not a problem the ICP expected to have. Naming it explicitly is market education. The ICP arrives knowing they have a documentation problem; they do not arrive knowing their vendor holds records they can't access. This is new information and is itself a differentiator.

### Finding 4: "Invisible Delegation" Must Be Named Precisely

"Staff using AI you didn't authorize" is the third problem. Naming it as "invisible delegation" gives the ICP language for a problem they already know they have but didn't have words for. The term "invisible delegation" is precise, accurate, and not used by any competitor.

### Finding 5: "Three Problems Simultaneously" Is the Synthesis Statement

The C309 cycle insight — three simultaneous activations — is the synthesis statement for the entire cycle. It should be the headline of the lead magnet. "You have three problems simultaneously" is more specific, more accurate, and more actionable than any generic AI governance positioning.

### Finding 6: The First Conversation Offer Must Demonstrate All Three Problem Dimensions

"Show me your carrier form" is the entry point for Problem One. But the first conversation should also address Problems Two and Three (vendor contracts, staff delegation). The first conversation offer should signal competence across all three dimensions, not just the carrier form.
