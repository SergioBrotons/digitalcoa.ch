## 1. The Claim-Delivery Gap

The competitive landscape for Geneva SMB AI accountability is characterized by a systematic gap between what competitors claim to offer and what they actually deliver to SMBs. This gap is the primary reason the market remains underserved.

### The Core Claim-Delivery Mismatch

| Competitor Type | Claims | Delivers | Gap |
|----------------|--------|----------|-----|
| Generalist consultancies | "AI governance framework" | Policy document, 30 pages | Documentation that doesn't survive carrier scrutiny |
| Law firms | "Regulatory compliance" | Legal opinion, risk register | Legal advice ≠ operational evidence system |
| Big 4 | "Enterprise-grade AI governance" | Framework methodology, audit methodology | Wrong price point (CHF 50k–200k+) for SMBs |
| Mid-tier accounting | "nFADP compliance package" | Template documents, checklist | Template ≠ evidence architecture |
| Boutique AI gov | "Specialized AI accountability" | Sophisticated framework, no SMB delivery | Enterprise price (CHF 20k–60k+), no retroactive |
| Tech vendors | "AI governance built into the platform" | Platform audit logs only | Vendor-siloed, not carrier-portable |

## 2. Specific Claim-Delivery Failures

### Failure 1: The Policy Document Problem

**What competitors claim:** "We will develop your AI governance policy" / "Comprehensive AI policy documentation"

**What they actually deliver:** A 15–40 page document titled "AI Governance Policy" with sections on acceptable use, data handling, human oversight principles. Formatted for board presentation, not carrier review.

**The gap:** Carrier attestation forms don't ask for a policy document. They ask for evidence of supervision, decision documentation, reconstruction capability. A policy document answers none of these questions. SMBs receive the document, then discover it doesn't satisfy the carrier form when it arrives.

**Why it persists:** Policy documents are easy to produce, easy to price, and easy to present. Evidence systems require deeper engagement, carrier-specific design, and ongoing maintenance. The policy document is the consultant's product, not the SMB's need.

---

### Failure 2: The Compliance Checklist Problem

**What competitors claim:** "Complete AI compliance checklist" / "nFADP compliance gap analysis"

**What they actually deliver:** A checklist of controls with "compliant" / "non-compliant" / "in progress" status for each item.

**The gap:** Compliance checklists measure activity, not outcomes. A checklist can show "human oversight: in progress" for 18 months without producing a single piece of evidence of actual human oversight in operation. Carriers want records, not status indicators.

**Why it persists:** Checklists are easy to audit and easy to present as evidence of systematic attention. They create the appearance of rigor without the substance of documentation.

---

### Failure 3: The Retroactive Blind Spot

**What competitors claim:** "Comprehensive AI governance implementation" / "Future-ready accountability systems"

**What they actually deliver:** Systems designed to document decisions going forward from the implementation date. Zero capability to reconstruct evidence for decisions made before the system was in place.

**The gap:** The market's documentation debt is almost entirely retrospective — decisions made in the past 18 months without documentation. Competitors offer no solution for retroactive reconstruction. The SMB is left with documentation debt that no competitor will acknowledge as addressable.

**Why it persists:** Retroactive reconstruction requires a specific methodology (interview-based reconstruction, document triage, carrier form mapping) that is not part of any standard consulting methodology. It is also harder to scope, price, and deliver than forward-looking systems.

**Sergio's differentiation:** Retroactive reconstruction is the unique capability no competitor offers. It addresses the primary market need (documentation debt accumulated over 18 months) that no other provider will acknowledge.

---

### Failure 4: The Bilingual Gap

**What competitors claim:** "Swiss market expertise" / "Local Geneva presence" / "Bilingual service delivery"

**What they actually deliver:** English-only documentation, or French documentation with no English equivalent, or bilingual documents where one language is clearly the original and the other is a translation.

**The gap:** Geneva SMBs operate in both official languages. Carrier attestation forms arrive in French. Client communications happen in both languages. Professional body guidance is issued in both languages. A bilingual product means two complete, equivalent, parallel documents — not a document translated into another language.

**Why it persists:** True bilingual documentation (two complete, equivalent documents, not translation) doubles production time. Competitors price based on single-language delivery and then "localize" the output. The market has accepted this as normal. It is not normal for Geneva SMBs who need carrier-ready evidence in both languages.

**Sergio's differentiation:** Bilingual EN/FR is the default delivery mode, not an add-on. Two complete, equivalent evidence packages, one in French, one in English, both carrier-ready.

---

### Failure 5: The Vertical Specificity Gap

**What competitors claim:** "Sector-specific AI governance" / "Industry expertise" / "Professional practice compliance"

**What they actually deliver:** Generic frameworks with a cover page that says "for [Professional Sector]" — same methodology, same templates, different branding.

**The gap:** The documentation requirements for a physician's FMH attestation, a lawyer's bar association renewal, an accountant's fiduciary compliance, and a consultant's client RFP AI scoring are structurally different. Each requires different evidence types, different carrier forms, different professional body guidance. "Sector-specific" in competitor language means sector-branded, not sector-designed.

**Why it persists:** Developing four genuinely different vertical overlays requires deep vertical expertise, carrier form database, and evidence architecture that can be configured per vertical. This is a product architecture investment, not a marketing adjustment.

**Sergio's differentiation:** One evidence architecture with four genuine vertical overlays — not one document rebranded per vertical.

---

### Failure 6: The Carrier Form Gap

**What competitors claim:** "Compliance with insurance requirements" / "Professional liability protection through AI governance"

**What they actually deliver:** General AI governance documentation with no specific mapping to actual carrier attestation forms in the Geneva SMB market.

**The gap:** The carrier attestation form is the de facto standard for AI accountability documentation in the Geneva SMB market. The form defines what evidence is needed. No competitor designs evidence architecture to carrier form specifications. They design to regulatory frameworks and then hope the carrier form is satisfied. This is a structural misalignment.

**Why it persists:** Carrier forms are specific to carriers and change regularly. Mapping evidence architecture to specific carrier forms requires ongoing carrier form monitoring that competitors do not invest in. It is easier to design to abstract regulatory frameworks than to specific carrier document requirements.

**Sergio's differentiation:** Carrier form as design specification. Evidence architecture built to answer the specific questions in the specific carrier form the ICP will receive.

## 3. The Market Signal: What the Gap Reveals

### Signal 1: The Market Is Selling to the Wrong Buyer

Competitors sell to the SMB principal who is worried about regulatory risk. The actual buyer — the SMB principal who received a carrier attestation form and doesn't know how to answer it — is not being addressed by any competitor's marketing.

**What this means:** The primary trigger for AI accountability purchase is the carrier attestation form, not regulatory risk. Competitors are selling the risk, not the trigger. The trigger is the entry point.

### Signal 2: Retroactive Reconstruction Is a Category That Doesn't Exist

No competitor offers retroactive reconstruction as a service category. SMBs with documentation debt assume they are stuck with it. The market has not educated SMBs that retroactive reconstruction is possible.

**What this means:** The market is unaware of its own solution. Market education is part of the value proposition. "You can reconstruct documentation debt" is itself a differentiating message, not just a feature.

### Signal 3: Bilingual Is Undervalued by Competitors

Competitors treat bilingual as a nice-to-have feature or a localization task. Geneva SMBs treat bilingual as a baseline requirement. This systematic undervaluation creates a persistent market gap.

**What this means:** Shipping bilingual by default (two complete, equivalent documents) is not a differentiator in the market — it is the minimum viable product for the Geneva market. Competitors who don't do this are systematically incomplete.

### Signal 4: Broker Channel Is Unaddressed

No competitor has built their business around broker referral as the primary acquisition channel. Insurance brokers are the primary trigger point for AI accountability urgency (carrier form arrival), but no competitor has built a broker referral program.

**What this means:** The broker channel is open and uncontested. First-mover advantage in broker relationships is the most defensible competitive moat available.

## 4. Six NET-NEW Market Signal Findings for Phase 06

### Finding 1: Competitors Claim "Carrier Compliance" But Have No Carrier Form Database

Every competitor claims their product satisfies carrier requirements. None have built a database of actual carrier attestation forms across professional liability carriers in the Swiss market. This means their "carrier compliance" claim is marketing language, not operational fact.

**Implication:** Build the carrier form database. This is the foundation for evidence architecture that is genuinely carrier-ready, not just carrier-adjacent.

### Finding 2: Competitors Who Claim "Retroactive Capability" Are Lying

Some competitors claim their frameworks "document decisions going forward and can be used to demonstrate past practices." This is not retroactive reconstruction — it is documentation built after the fact, which is not the same as reconstruction of actual decision processes from the period.

**Implication:** "Retroactive reconstruction" must be defined precisely: interview-based reconstruction of actual decision processes, document triage from the period in question, carrier form mapping for the period in question, evidence package assembled from reconstructed materials. Competitors who claim this without this methodology are making false claims.

### Finding 3: Boutique AI Governance Firms Have the Language But Not the Product

Boutique AI governance firms (EUR 20k–60k+ engagements) use language very similar to Sergio's — "evidence systems," "accountability architecture," "operational documentation." This is the closest competitive proximity. However, they have no SMB pricing tier, no retroactive reconstruction, no broker channel, and no bilingual EN/FR delivery.

**Implication:** Monitor boutique AI governance firms for SMB market expansion. Their language is close to Sergio's; their product design is not. If they develop SMB pricing and retroactive capability, they become a more direct competitor.

### Finding 4: Mid-Tier Accounting Firms Are the Most Likely Near-Term Competitors

Mid-tier Swiss accounting firms have existing SMB relationships, regulatory positioning, and trust. They currently lack AI accountability expertise and evidence architecture methodology. If they hire one experienced AI accountability practitioner and develop an evidence architecture product, they could leverage existing client relationships to compete directly.

**Implication:** The competitive threat from accounting firms is not product today — it is product + channel tomorrow. Monitor for hiring signals and product announcements from mid-tier accounting firms.

### Finding 5: The "For Physicians, Evidence Is Personal" Framing Has No Competitor Equivalent

No competitor frames AI accountability through the personal liability lens for any vertical. They frame it as corporate compliance, organizational governance, regulatory risk. The personal framing — "your name is on the form, your personal assets are exposed" — is absent from all competitor messaging.

**Implication:** The personal liability framing is独占. It speaks to the deepest ICP concern and is not addressed by any competitor. This is the highest-resonance positioning available.

### Finding 6: Evidence Architecture as a Category Has No Named Competitor

When SMB principals describe their need to competitors, they describe it as "something to show my broker." When they describe it to Sergio, they describe it as "evidence architecture." The category doesn't have a name in the market yet. This is category creation, not differentiation.

**Implication:** Own the category before it gets named. "Evidence architecture" is the right category name — specific, accurate, differentiated from "governance" and "compliance." The first mover to name and own the category wins the category.
