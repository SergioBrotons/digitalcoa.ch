# Phase 02 ICP Language — Geneva Finance / Boutique Wealth Management
## Cycle 298 (2026-05-29)

## Addressable Profile
Boutique wealth management firms and multi-family offices in Geneva, 50–200 employees, managing CHF 500M–5B AUM. Managing director or partner personally accountable. Serving UHNW and institutional clients internationally. CIO or Managing Director owns compliance decisions and bears personal liability.

## The ICP's Language: How They Talk About AI Problems

### Framing 1: "Institutional Client AI Governance Questionnaires"
When institutional clients — family offices, pension funds, sovereign wealth — send AI governance questionnaires, the managing director feels the pressure directly. The language they use:

- "Our biggest client sent us a 40-point AI governance questionnaire. I had no idea how to answer half of it."
- "The questionnaire asks about our AI systems, data lineage, model oversight, and human accountability. We don't have documentation for any of that."
- "Institutional clients are now requiring AI governance attestations as part of due diligence. It's becoming a gate to keeping their business."
- "I signed off on using that AI portfolio tool two years ago. If something goes wrong, am I personally liable?"

### Framing 2: "Carrier Attestation Requirements"
Insurance carriers and custodians are increasingly demanding AI attestations before renewing institutional coverage or providing custody services. The managing director hears:

- "Our D&O insurer wants us to attest to our AI governance framework. We don't have one."
- "The carrier's questionnaire asks: 'Describe your AI risk management framework.' We had to leave that blank."
- "I heard a peer got a lower premium because his firm had an AI governance certification. We're paying the unclassified rate."
- "The attestation asks about algorithmic bias testing, model documentation, human override procedures. I don't know what half of that means for our firm."

### Framing 3: "FINMA Expectations and the Guidance Gap"
FINMA published AI guidance in December 2024 — and then went silent. The managing director feels the ambiguity:

- "FINMA published something about AI last year, but there's no clear checklist. We're guessing."
- "The SRO hasn't said anything about AI governance. No guidance, no templates, no expectations. It's like they don't know it exists."
- "If FINMA audits us on AI, what exactly do they want to see? Nobody knows."
- "We're a boutique. We don't have a compliance team. We have me."

### Framing 4: "Personal Liability Under nFADP Article 63"
The nFADP introduced Art. 63 with personal liability for compliance failures. Managing directors feel this acutely:

- "Article 63 means I'm personally on the hook if we have a data breach involving AI."
- "The FDPIC can come after me directly, not just the firm. That's new."
- "I need to document that I'm accountable for every AI decision made in my firm. I don't have a system for that."
- "If an AI system makes a bad recommendation and we can't explain it, that's a breach. And I'm responsible."

### Framing 5: "AI Deployment Reality"
Despite the uncertainty, AI tools are already in use:

- "We deployed three AI tools this year — portfolio analytics, client reporting, CRM scoring. Each one processes client data."
- "The tools are already in production. We can't easily undo them."
- "My team set up these tools without a governance framework. I'm now realizing that's a problem."
- "The vendor says we're responsible for how we use their AI. That doesn't help me sleep at night."

### Emotional State
The managing director of a Geneva boutique WM firm in 2026 is:
- Aware that AI is now mission-critical for competitiveness
- Under pressure from institutional clients to prove AI governance
- Under pressure from carriers to attest to AI risk management
- Operating with no clear guidance from FINMA or SRO
- Personally exposed under nFADP Art. 63
- Without a compliance team to absorb this work
- Looking for someone who understands boutique scale and Swiss regulatory specificity

## NET-NEW Finding C298-P2-F1
**Institutional client AI governance questionnaires as a forcing function:** The specific trigger for action is not abstract regulatory fear — it is receiving a specific questionnaire from an institutional client that they cannot answer. The managing director feels personal embarrassment ("I had to leave half of it blank") and client retention fear simultaneously. This is a more actionable trigger than regulatory ambiguity. The advisory positioning should lead with "when your institutional client sends you that questionnaire" rather than "FINMA guidance."
