# Phase 02 Compliance Context — Geneva Finance / Boutique WM
## Cycle 298 (2026-05-29)

## Regulatory Framework: What Applies to the ICP

### Swiss Domestic Layer

#### FINMA AI Guidance (December 2024)
- Published December 2024; approximately 17 months old as of May 2026
- First and only Swiss-specific AI governance framework for supervised entities
- Four pillars: Governance, Risk Management, Transparency, Non-Discrimination
- Governance: board-level accountability, documented AI inventory required
- Risk Management: nFADP DPIA for AI systems processing personal data; ongoing monitoring
- Transparency: client disclosure of AI-assisted decisions; explainability where feasible
- Non-Discrimination: algorithmic bias testing; fairness audit procedures
- **Status:** Non-legal framework; no formal compliance checklist; no updated guidance published since December 2024
- **ICP experience:** The guidance exists but provides no actionable checklist for a boutique WM managing director. The 17-month silence from FINMA has created anxiety — they interpret it as "we still don't know what FINMA wants."

#### nFADP (Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection) — Operative since September 1, 2023
- Article 5: Lawfulness of data processing; AI-based data processing falls under Art. 5(c) legitimate interest or consent
- Article 6: Proportionality principle
- **Article 63: Personal liability** — managing director can be pursued directly by FDPIC for compliance failures involving AI systems
- DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment) required for high-risk AI processing
- FDPIC enforcement trajectory: May 8, 2025 guidance confirmed AI-based data processing triggers nFADP obligations
- FDPIC has issued fines and enforcement actions against organizations with inadequate AI governance; WM sector not yet specifically targeted but pattern established

#### AMLA Revision (H2 2026)
- Beneficial ownership verification expanded
- Advisor scope expansion: lawyers, notaries, trustees, CSPs — wealth managers with advisory component may face dual SRO obligations
- New 30-day discrepancy reporting to the Transparency Register (once LETA enters force)
- SRO membership required for advisors under expanded scope
- Consultation on implementing ordinances concluded January 30, 2026; referendum period ongoing; implementation machinery active
- Timeline: H2 2026 (~1-6 months from May 2026)

#### LETA (Transparency Register) — H2 2026 same wave
- Central beneficial ownership register
- UBO (Ultimate Beneficial Owner) filing requirements
- 30-day discrepancy reporting: if firm's KYC records differ from Transparency Register, must report within 30 days
- Managing director accountability for KYC accuracy

#### CARF/CRS 2.0 — Operative since January 1, 2026
- Already 5 months in effect
- New data fields required (financial account balances, UUIDs)
- DAC2/CARF automated exchanges expanding
- Due date for 2025 reporting: 2026

### Swiss International Layer
- FATF standards: AMLA alignment with international FATF standards
- OECD CRS 2.0: already live since January 1, 2026
- SIF (State Secretariat for International Finance) oversight

### EU Extraterritorial Layer (If EU Market Nexus)
- **EU AI Act:** August 2, 2026 (~9 weeks from late May 2026); extraterritorial for Geneva WM firms with EU market nexus
- GPAI (General Purpose AI) provisions already operative since August 2, 2025
- Full EU AI Act February 2027 for GPAI systemic risk obligations
- GDPR legacy obligations (still applicable for EU client data)
- DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act): applicable since January 2025

### SRO Layer
- FINMA-recognized SROs: currently silent on AI governance
- No SRO has published AI governance guidance, templates, or expectations
- SRO silence is a specific pain point for the ICP — they want guidance and nothing is coming
- Expanded advisor scope under AMLA may create new SRO membership pressure

## NET-NEW Finding C298-P2-F4
**SRO silence as a distinct pain point from FINMA silence:** The ICP distinguishes between FINMA silence (which they expected — FINMA rarely publishes detailed guidance) and SRO silence (which they did not expect — SROs are supposed to provide practical operational guidance to their members). When an SRO provides no AI governance guidance, the managing director feels that even their own industry body has abandoned them. This makes the advisory offer's SRO alignment credentials more valuable — positioning as "we translate SRO expectations into practical boutique WM governance frameworks" directly addresses this pain point.
