## Market Signals: What the ICP Is Experiencing

### Signal 1: Institutional Clients Are Sending AI Governance Questionnaires
Family offices, pension funds, and sovereign wealth funds — Geneva WM firms' largest clients — are now sending formal AI governance questionnaires as part of due diligence. This is a new forcing function that bypasses regulatory abstract anxiety and creates immediate business risk:

- Client retention risk: cannot answer the questionnaire = risk losing the client
- Competitive disadvantage: peers with documentation are winning renewals
- Liability exposure: managing director feels personally accountable for the attestation

The questionnaire typically covers:
- AI system inventory (all AI tools processing client data)
- Data lineage and provenance
- Model oversight and human accountability
- Algorithmic bias testing and fairness procedures
- Incident response and escalation procedures
- Vendor risk management and DPA compliance

### Signal 2: Carrier Attestation Requirements Are Tightening
D&O (Directors & Officers) insurers and custodians are now requiring AI governance attestations as part of coverage and custody renewals. Boutique WM firms without a documented AI governance framework face:

- Higher premiums (unclassified rate vs. structured rate)
- Conditional coverage (attestation required before binding)
- Coverage gaps (AI-related incidents may not be covered without governance documentation)
- Custodian requirements: custodians are asking about AI risk management before providing services

The carrier questionnaire asks about:
- Documented AI risk management framework
- AI decision audit trail capabilities
- Human override procedures for AI-influenced decisions
- Algorithmic bias testing documentation
- Incident response and breach notification procedures

### Signal 3: FINMA Has Gone Silent on AI Guidance Since December 2024
FINMA published AI guidance in December 2024 — and then published nothing further. The 17-month gap is creating anxiety:

- No updated guidance despite AI adoption accelerating
- No clarification on what a "compliant AI governance framework" looks like for a boutique WM
- No sector-specific FAQ or template
- SROs (FINMA-recognized SROs) have published nothing on AI governance
- The guidance exists but provides no actionable checklist

Managing directors interpret this silence as: "We don't know what FINMA wants, and FINMA isn't telling us."

### Signal 4: The nFADP Art. 63 Personal Liability Shadow
Article 63 of the nFADP introduces personal liability for compliance failures. For managing directors:

- FDPIC can pursue the individual, not just the firm
- Personal liability for AI-related data breaches
- Accountability for AI decisions made without adequate governance
- The need to document personal accountability for AI oversight

This creates a specific anxiety: "I'm personally on the hook for AI decisions I don't fully understand."

### Signal 5: AI Tools Are Already Deployed Without Governance
Despite the lack of governance framework, AI tools are already in production:

- Portfolio management AI (algorithmic rebalancing, risk analysis)
- Client communication AI (automated reporting, proposal generation)
- CRM AI (contact scoring, next-best-action, client segmentation)
- Compliance AI (transaction surveillance, KYC automation)
- Document processing AI (contract analysis, meeting notes)

Each deployment represents a governance gap. Each gap represents nFADP exposure. The managing director deployed these tools for competitiveness — not understanding the compliance implications.

### Signal 6: Agentic AI Acceleration in 2026
The 2026 shift toward agentic AI — autonomous systems that take action without human review — is amplifying governance concerns:

- Agentic AI systems make decisions in real-time
- Human accountability principle becomes harder to satisfy
- Portfolio management with autonomous rebalancing raises specific questions
- The question is no longer "can AI help?" but "who is accountable when AI acts?"

## NET-NEW Finding C298-P2-F2
**Carrier attestation as a distinct forcing function from client questionnaires:** Client questionnaires create retention fear; carrier attestations create coverage and cost fear. These are two separate forcing functions. A managing director may act on a client questionnaire (direct revenue threat) before acting on a carrier attestation (cost threat). Both should be addressed in positioning — client questionnaire first (revenue), carrier attestation second (cost). This matters because the advisory entry point should match the most acute forcing function first.
