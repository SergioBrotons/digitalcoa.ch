# ICP Language: Geneva Boutique Wealth Management — AI Accountability

**Phase:** C289 Phase 02
**ICP:** Boutique wealth management firms, 50–200 employees, Geneva-based
**Role:** Managing director or partner, personally accountable
**Date:** 2026-05-28

---

## How the ICP Talks About AI Problems

### Primary Concern Framing

The ICP does not say "AI governance." They say things like:

- **"I need to be able to explain every recommendation my system makes"** — liability framing
- **"My clients are starting to ask me questions I can't answer about how the software works"** — client pressure
- **"If something goes wrong with a portfolio because of an AI tool, who is responsible? Me. Always me."** — personal accountability
- **"We're being asked to fill out these questionnaires from institutional clients — they want to know our AI policy, our data handling, our model validation"** — institutional pressure
- **"FINMA hasn't told us what they expect, but you feel it coming"** — regulatory anxiety
- **"The SROs are silent. They haven't issued anything useful on AI."** — vacuum of guidance
- **"Article 63 of the nFADP — if I deploy a tool that causes a data breach, it's my personal liability. Not the vendor's. Mine."** — nFADP fear

### Language Patterns

**Avoiding, not adopting:**
- "I'm not touching AI tools until someone tells me what's expected"
- "I don't want to be the first mover on this — too much liability"
- "Every vendor promises compliance. None of them take the hit when it goes wrong."

**Adoption hesitation triggers:**
- Carrier attestation requirements ("my insurer is asking me to attest to AI governance standards I can't even define")
- Client questionnaires ("we've received AI governance questionnaires from 3 institutional clients this quarter")
- Audit preparation ("I need to be ready for when FINMA asks questions")

**Operational language:**
- "Portfolio management AI tools" — not "generative AI" or "LLMs"
- "Client data handling" — data residency, confidentiality, consent
- "Model validation" — can we explain, audit, and justify algorithmic decisions
- "Audit trail" — need to show what the system did and why

### What They Sound Like in Conversation

> "We have 140 clients. About 40 of them are institutions — family offices, pension funds, corporates. Every single one of them has sent us some kind of AI governance questionnaire in the last 12 months. They want to know: where does our data go, who has access, how are models trained, can we demonstrate oversight. We don't have good answers. And when I say 'we,' I mean me — because at the end of the day, I'm the one who signs."

> "The carrier sent us an attestation form. 'Confirm that you have adequate AI governance controls.' Adequate for what? There's no standard. So I just checked the box and moved on. That's not a strategy."

> "Everyone talks about efficiency gains from AI. I'm interested. But not at the cost of my license. If I deploy something that blows up a client portfolio and I can't explain the model's logic to FINMA, I'm done."

### Emotional State

- **Confused** — no clear regulatory path
- **Pressured** — clients and carriers are asking questions
- **Personally exposed** — personal liability, not just firm liability
- **Waiting** — for something to become clearer before committing
- **Skeptical of vendors** — "they all say they're compliant, none of them take liability"

### How They Find Information

- Peer conversations at Geneva financial events
- Industry associations (ASG, SBA, SFAMA)
- Carrier communications
- Legal counsel (data protection, financial regulation)
- Google, but cautiously — "you find a lot of vendor noise and very little regulatory clarity"

## French-Language ICP Language (Parallel)

> « Je dois pouvoir justifier chaque recommandation que mon système produit. Si quelque chose tourne mal avec un portefeuille à cause d'un outil IA, la responsabilité est pour qui ? Pour moi. Toujours pour moi. »

> « Mes clients institutionnels nous envoient des questionnaires de gouvernance IA. Ils veulent savoir : où vont nos données, qui y accède, comment les modèles sont entraînés, pouvons-nous démontrer une supervision. Nous n'avons pas de bonnes réponses. Et quand je dis 'nous,' je veux dire moi — parce qu'à la fin, c'est moi qui signe. »

> « L'assureur nous a envoyé un formulaire d'attestation. 'Confirmez que vous disposez de contrôles de gouvernance IA adéquats.' Adéquats pour quoi ? Il n'existe aucun standard. Donc je coche la case et je passe à autre chose. Ce n'est pas une stratégie. »

> « L'art. 63 nFADP — si je déploie un outil qui cause une violation de données, c'est ma responsabilité personnelle. Pas celle du fournisseur. La mienne. »

## Key Terminology They Use vs. Technical Terms

| What they say | Technical term |
|---|---|
| "Explain every recommendation" | Model explainability, interpretability |
| "Where does data go" | Data residency, sovereignty, third-party processing |
| "Who can see client data" | Access controls, least privilege |
| "How do we validate a model" | Model validation, backtesting, performance monitoring |
| "Audit trail" | Logging, traceability, decision records |
| "Client consent" | nFADP legal basis, Art. 6, Art. 31 |
| "Attestation" | Carrier AI governance requirements |
| "Governance questionnaire" | Institutional client AI DDQ (due diligence questionnaire) |
