# [EN] The MD's AI Accountability Problem

## A Framework for Geneva Wealth Managers Who Need to Respond to Institutional Clients, Carrier Attestations, and FINMA Without Losing Sleep

---

### Opening

I run a boutique wealth management firm in Geneva. We have 80 employees. I manage the firm, I manage client relationships, and I carry the license.

In the last 18 months, something has changed that I didn't plan for.

My institutional clients — the family offices, the pension funds, the corporate treasury accounts — have started sending me questionnaires about how we use AI. Not once. Every quarter now, at least two or three arrive. They want to know about our data governance, our model oversight, our explainability standards. They want to know who is accountable.

And my carrier — the PI insurer — has added a clause to the renewal asking me to confirm that we have "adequate AI governance controls." Adequate for what? Nobody has defined that. So I signed it and moved on.

FINMA hasn't issued specific guidance for wealth managers on AI. I wait for it. I also dread it, because when it arrives, I need to be able to show that I understood what my systems were doing. Right now, I can't say that with confidence.

This paper is the framework I built for my own firm to handle all of this. I'm sharing it because I think every Geneva boutique WM MD is facing the same problem and nobody is talking about it honestly.

---

### The Core Problem: Three Simultaneous Pressures

**Pressure 1: Institutional clients are asking questions I can't answer**

My clients' AI governance questionnaires arrive with questions I recognize as legitimate but can't respond to with any structure. They ask things like:
- "How do you ensure oversight of AI-generated recommendations?"
- "Can you demonstrate that your AI systems do not produce discriminatory outcomes?"
- "What is your vendor due diligence process for AI tools?"
- "Do you have a written AI governance policy?"

I don't have written answers to most of these. I write responses in Word, run them past legal, and send them out. It's not a policy. It's improvisation.

**Pressure 2: Carrier attestation requires something I can't define**

My PI insurer added an AI governance attestation clause to the renewal. The language is vague: "Confirm adequate controls for AI-assisted decision-making." I asked the broker what "adequate" means. He said there's no industry standard.

I signed it. But the moment I signed, I understood: if something goes wrong and my carrier can show that my attestation was empty, they may not cover the claim. I'm not protected. I'm just compliant on paper.

**Pressure 3: FINMA is coming, and I don't know what they'll ask**

FINMA issued general AI guidance in 2024. They haven't issued specific guidance for wealth managers. The absence of guidance is not the same as absence of expectation. When FINMA comes to ask questions, I need to be able to demonstrate:
- That I understand what my AI systems are doing
- That I can explain algorithmic decisions to regulators
- That I have proper oversight of vendor-deployed models
- That I can show documentation of how AI recommendations were made

I can't demonstrate that today. That's the problem I'm solving.

---

### The Deeper Problem: Personal Liability

Here's what nobody tells you in plain language.

Article 63 of Switzerland's revised Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) creates administrative sanctions up to CHF 1M for serious violations. These sanctions apply to natural persons — specifically managing directors.

When I deploy an AI tool in portfolio management, I'm deploying a system that processes client personal data: financial situations, risk profiles, asset allocations, family structures. That's all nFADP scope.

If that tool causes a data breach, or processes data unlawfully, or produces a discriminatory outcome that violates FinSA suitability requirements — the liability is mine. Not the vendor's. Mine.

Every AI vendor contract I've signed says the client is responsible for ensuring regulatory compliance. That means me. The vendor disclaims everything. I carry everything.

I need to be able to demonstrate that I made a reasonable, informed decision to deploy each AI tool. That's what Article 63 demands. And right now, I can't show that with confidence.

### What I Built: The MD Accountability Framework

I built a practical framework for my own firm. It doesn't require Big 4 consulting budgets or legal teams working for months. It's designed for a boutique WM operation — 50 to 200 people — in Geneva.

The framework has four components:

**Component 1: The AI Governance Policy Document**

A written policy that:
- Defines which AI tools we use and why
- Documents our oversight structure (who reviews AI outputs, how often, what triggers review)
- Establishes our model validation approach (how we test whether an AI system is performing as expected)
- Sets out our incident response process (what happens when an AI system produces an unexpected or problematic recommendation)
- Addresses data governance (where client data goes, who processes it, what consent frameworks apply)

This document is what I present when institutional clients send their questionnaires. It shows that I have a structured approach — not improvisation.

**Component 2: The Institutional AI DDQ Response Template**

For each AI governance questionnaire that arrives, I now have a template that maps questions to our policy document. I can respond to any question by referencing specific sections. Each response is consistent, defensible, and grounded in an actual policy.

This has saved me approximately 3 hours per questionnaire and produces much stronger responses than the Word-doc improvisation I was doing before.

**Component 3: The Carrier Attestation Package**

I built a documentation package that:
- Shows the AI governance policy
- Documents our model validation process
- Includes our vendor due diligence records
- Shows our incident response capability

When my carrier asks me to attest, I now have something to point to that actually supports the attestation. It's not just a checked box. It's a defensible position.

**Component 4: The FINMA Preparation File**

A structured file that, if FINMA asks questions, I can present immediately. It shows:
- Our AI systems and their purposes
- Our oversight structure
- Our documentation practices
- Our model validation evidence
- Our data governance arrangements

The goal is not to show perfection. It's to show that I understood what I was deploying, that I maintained oversight, and that I can explain it. That's what FINMA will want to see.

### Why This Is Urgent

Three things are converging.

First, institutional clients are making AI governance part of their manager selection criteria. Firms that can't respond to their questionnaires are losing mandates. It's happening now, not as a future risk.

Second, carriers are updating their attestation requirements with each renewal cycle. The "adequate controls" language will get more specific. When it does, firms that have nothing to point to will have a coverage problem.

Third, FINMA will issue guidance. When they do, the firms that have already built structured governance will be in a defensible position. The firms that are still improvising will be exposed.

I built this framework because I needed it. I share it because I think every Geneva boutique WM MD needs it, and nobody is offering it.
