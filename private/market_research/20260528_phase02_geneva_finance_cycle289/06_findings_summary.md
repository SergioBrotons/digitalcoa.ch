# Findings Summary: Geneva Boutique Wealth Management — AI Accountability

**Phase:** C289 Phase 02
**ICP:** Boutique wealth management firms, 50–200 employees, Geneva-based
**Role:** Managing director or partner, personally accountable
**Date:** 2026-05-28

---

## Key Insights

### Insight 1: Three Pressures Converge on the MD Simultaneously

The Geneva boutique WM managing director is caught in a convergence of three distinct pressures:

1. **Institutional client AI governance questionnaires** — coming quarterly, from family offices, pension funds, corporate treasury clients. These clients have made AI governance part of manager selection and due diligence.

2. **Carrier attestation requirements** — PI insurers adding vague AI governance attestation clauses to renewals. No reference standard exists. MDs sign attestations they can't defend.

3. **FINMA anticipation** — No specific WM AI guidance exists, but MDs sense it coming. The firms that adopted AI early feel exposed; those who avoided it feel unprepared. Both need to be ready.

The MD is personally accountable for all three. The liability is theirs, not the vendor's.

---

### Insight 2: The nFADP Article 63 Personal Liability Problem Is Not Well Understood

Article 63 of the revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection creates administrative sanctions up to CHF 1M that apply to managing directors personally. AI tools in portfolio management process extensive client personal data — financial situations, risk profiles, family structures, asset allocations.

MDs are deploying AI tools via vendor contracts that disclaim all regulatory liability back to the client. This is standard. But it means every AI deployment decision is a personal liability decision for the MD under Article 63.

**What nobody has told them clearly:** Article 63 asks whether you made a reasonable, informed decision to deploy each tool. You need to be able to demonstrate that you understood what you were deploying, maintained oversight, and can explain it. That's the liability exposure.

---

### Insight 3: Institutional AI DDQs Are Becoming the De Facto Standard

With SROs silent and FINMA guidance absent, institutional client AI governance questionnaires are functioning as the de facto standard for what "adequate AI governance" looks like. Firms that can respond well are winning mandates. Firms that can't are losing them.

This is a competitive signal: strong institutional AI DDQ responses = retention and acquisition of institutional mandates. Weak responses = client flight.

---

### Insight 4: The Market Has a Boutique-Specific Gap

Available solutions:

- **Big 4**: Designed for institutions. Wrong scale, wrong price, wrong format.
- **Legal firms**: Issue legal opinions, not operational governance systems.
- **AI vendors**: Sell tools with liability disclaimers. No liability sharing.
- **SROs**: Generic principles, no operational guidance.
- **nFADP/FINMA-focused consultants**: Address data protection law, not MD accountability across the full regulatory stack.

**Nobody owns the "MD accountability" positioning for Swiss boutique wealth management AI governance.** This is an open market position.

---

### Insight 5: "Attestation theater" is a coverage risk that MDs don't recognize

MDs are signing carrier attestations for "adequate AI governance controls" without any underlying standard or documentation. They don't understand that if a claim arises from an AI-assisted decision and the carrier can demonstrate the attestation was hollow, coverage may be denied.

This is a hidden risk embedded in standard PI renewal cycles. It will become more acute as attestation language gets more specific with each renewal cycle.

---

### Insight 6: The ICP Wants a Practical System, Not a Theoretical Framework

The Geneva boutique WM MD is not looking for:
- Academic frameworks
- Big 4 strategy documents
- Legal opinions
- Vendor product pitches

The MD is looking for:
- A written AI governance policy they can show to clients and carriers
- A structured response process for institutional AI questionnaires
- Documentation that supports carrier attestations defensibly
- A file they can present to FINMA when asked

The successful engagement is operational and document-based. It solves the problem of "I can't answer the questions and I don't know what ready looks like."
