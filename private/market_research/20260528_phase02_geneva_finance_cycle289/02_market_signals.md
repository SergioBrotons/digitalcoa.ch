# Market Signals: Geneva Boutique Wealth Management — AI Accountability

**Phase:** C289 Phase 02
**ICP:** Boutique wealth management firms, 50–200 employees, Geneva-based
**Role:** Managing director or partner, personally accountable
**Date:** 2026-05-28

---

## Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

### 1. Institutional Client AI Governance Questionnaires

**Signal:** Boutique wealth managers in Geneva are being bombarded with AI governance DDQs (due diligence questionnaires) from institutional clients — family offices, pension funds, corporate treasury clients.

**What's happening:**
- Institutional clients (40–60% of AUM for many boutiques) now include AI governance questions in standard due diligence
- Questions cover: data handling practices, model oversight, algorithmic explainability, vendor management, incident response
- Boutique firms have no structured process to respond — they're cobbling together answers manually
- **Fear:** If they can't answer well, they lose the client mandate

**Quote pattern:**
> "We've received AI governance questionnaires from four institutional clients this quarter alone. We don't have a formal AI policy document. So we write paragraphs in Word, have legal review it, and send it out. It's embarrassing."

**Complaint:** "I spend more time responding to questionnaires than managing portfolios."

---

### 2. Carrier Attestation Requirements

**Signal:** Insurance carriers (PI / professional indemnity) are requiring AI governance attestations as part of renewal, without providing clear standards.

**What's happening:**
- PI insurers now include AI governance attestation clauses in renewal questionnaires
- Language is vague: "confirm adequate controls for AI-assisted decision-making"
- No reference to specific frameworks (NIST, ISO, FINMA guidance)
- Brokers can't advise on what's "adequate" — no industry standard exists
- **Result:** Firm checks the box without actual governance, creating a false sense of security

**Complaint:** "The carrier asks me to attest to something I can't define. I sign it anyway because I need the insurance. That's not attestation — that's theater."

**Risk:** If a claim arises from AI-assisted portfolio decision, carrier may argue governance was inadequate (based on the attestation signed by the MD).

---

### 3. FINMA Regulatory Uncertainty

**Signal:** No explicit FINMA guidance on AI in wealth management creates anxiety and paralysis.

**What's happening:**
- FINMA has issued general statements on AI (notably 2024 guidance on ethical AI use in finance)
- No specific circular for wealth management AI deployment
- No clear expectations on: model validation, explainability requirements, board oversight, senior management accountability
- Firms are waiting — avoiding AI adoption to avoid regulatory surprise
- **Paradox:** The longer they wait, the less prepared they are when guidance comes

**Quote pattern:**
> "FINMA hasn't told us what they expect, but you feel it coming. They will ask questions. I want to be ready. But I don't know what ready looks like."

**Key fear:** "When FINMA comes, will I be able to show that I understood what my systems were doing? Right now, no."

---

### 4. SRO Vacuum (ASG / SFAMA Silence)

**Signal:** Swiss financial industry associations have not issued practical AI governance guidance for wealth managers.

**What's happening:**
- ASG (Asset Management Switzerland) and SFAMA have released general AI statements
- No practical tool: no checklist, no framework, no certification pathway
- Wealth managers have no industry standard to point to when clients or carriers ask
- **Result:** Each firm invents its own framework — inconsistent, unauditable

**Complaint:** "The SROs are paid by us. Where is the guidance? A two-page PDF with generic principles is not guidance."

---

### 5. nFADP Article 63 Personal Liability

**Signal:** Article 63 of the revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection creates direct personal liability for managing directors using AI tools that process personal data.

**What's happening:**
- nFADP (revised, in force since 2023) includes Article 63: administrative sanctions up to CHF 1M for serious violations
- Managing directors can be held personally liable
- AI tools in portfolio management: client personal data (name, financial situation, investment preferences, risk profiles) — all sensitive
- **Key gap:** Consent frameworks for AI-assisted profiling and decision-making are not established
- Vendors say "we're GDPR/nFADP compliant" but don't address Art. 63 liability chain

**Quote pattern:**
> "Article 63 — if I deploy an AI tool that causes a data breach, it's my personal liability. Not the vendor's. Mine. The vendor will say 'we processed the data correctly.' But if I chose the tool, I'm responsible for the choice."

**Fear:** "What if the AI recommends an unsuitable investment and it's traced back to biased training data? Can I be sanctioned under nFADP for deploying a biased model?"

---

### 6. Vendor Accountability Gap

**Signal:** Wealth managers are deeply skeptical of AI vendors who promise compliance but disclaim all liability.

**What's happening:**
- AI vendors (portfolio optimization tools, CRM, research synthesis, risk analytics) market heavily to Swiss wealth managers
- Standard contract terms: vendor disclaims liability for investment outcomes, data governance failures, regulatory non-compliance
- **Boutique firm reality:** The MD signs off on deployment. The MD carries the liability. The vendor walks away clean.
- Trust gap is significant — "Every vendor promises compliance. None of them take the hit when it goes wrong."

**Complaint:** "I signed three vendor contracts last year. Every one of them says 'Client is responsible for ensuring compliance with applicable regulations.' That means me."

## NET-NEW Findings (C289 Phase 02)

1. **Institutional AI DDQ flood** — Boutique Geneva WMs receiving 3-5 AI governance questionnaires per quarter from institutional clients (family offices, pension funds, corporates). No standardized response framework exists.

2. **Carrier attestation theater** — PI insurers demanding AI governance attestation with no reference standard. Firms sign attestations they can't defend, creating false coverage and potential coverage disputes.

3. **FINMA anticipation paralysis** — No specific WM AI guidance from FINMA, but firms sense it coming. Those who adopted AI early feel exposed; those who avoided it feel unprepared for eventual scrutiny.

4. **SRO guidance vacuum** — ASG/SFAMA have issued generic statements only. No practical framework, checklist, or certification path for wealth managers to follow.

5. **Art. 63 nFADP personal liability** — Direct MD liability for AI tool deployment decisions involving personal data. Existing vendor contracts don't address the liability chain to the MD.

6. **Vendor accountability gap** — Standard vendor contracts disclaim all regulatory liability from the client MD. The more AI tools a firm deploys, the more personal liability exposure it accumulates — without contractual protection from vendors.
