## Draft Article 01
**Target:** Brotons.net | **Cluster:** Geneva Digital Sovereignty | **TIER:** TIER 1

### EN Version
**Headline:** Geneva's Hospital Systems Made Opposite Bets on Healthcare IT. The Consulting Implications Are Significant.

In the same canton, two major hospital systems are making opposite choices about clinical software. The Geneva University Hospitals (HUG) and Hôpital du Valais are co-developing their own integrated patient file system (DPI+) — a 30-year build project that now spans two cantons. Meanwhile, the CHUV in Lausanne is acquiring Epic Systems, the American hospital software giant.

The Vaud cantonal parliament is now examining the CHUV/Epic decision, with data protection and digital sovereignty concerns voiced across multiple parties. HUG, for its part, is open to expanding its DPI+ collaboration to other hospitals.

What does this mean for Geneva consulting firms?

The build option (HUG/DPI+) creates demand for program management, change management, clinical workflow redesign, and IT governance consulting. The buy option (CHUV/Epic) creates demand for implementation consulting, training, data migration, and regulatory compliance advisory. Both are large, multi-year mandates.

More broadly, the divergence illustrates a Swiss healthcare IT governance question that consulting firms should be ready to answer: when does it make sense to build, and when does it make sense to buy? The answer is not uniform — it depends on scale, canton-specific regulations, data sovereignty requirements, and existing IT infrastructure.

Geneva consulting firms with healthcare and IT practices should watch the DPI+ expansion discussions. If HUG opens the platform to additional cantons, the implementation and change management opportunity grows substantially.

## Draft Article 03
**Target:** DigitalCoa.ch | **Cluster:** Healthcare IT / Digital Sovereignty | **TIER:** TIER 1

### EN Version
**Headline:** Build or Buy? What the HUG/CHUV IT Divergence Tells Swiss Healthcare Boards

**Subhead:** Two major Swiss hospital systems made opposite decisions on clinical software. The decision framework that got them there deserves board attention.

The Geneva University Hospitals (HUG) chose to co-develop DPI+ with the Hôpital du Valais — a 30-year in-house development project now expanded to two cantons. The CHUV chose to buy Epic Systems. Both decisions have rationale. Neither is obviously wrong.

The build decision reflects: data sovereignty requirements (patient data stays in Swiss control), long-term cost optimisation at scale, and integration with existing HUG clinical workflows built over decades. The buy decision reflects: speed to implementation, Epic's scale advantages in large hospital settings, and reduced internal IT burden.

For Swiss healthcare institution boards evaluating IT strategy, the HUG/CHUV divergence is a natural case study. Key decision variables:

**Scale matters.** HUG/DPI+ makes economic sense at multi-cantonal scale; CHUV/Epic makes sense for a single large hospital seeking fast implementation.

**Data sovereignty is a board-level variable.** Swiss healthcare data residency requirements are not hypothetical — they are increasingly enforced, and they favor build decisions for large systems with sufficient IT capacity.

**Integration complexity determines buy attractiveness.** If existing clinical workflows are already digitised, Epic's standardisation is an advantage. If workflows are highly customised, the build path preserves flexibility.

Geneva consulting firms should be able to walk clients through this framework. The DPI+ expansion discussions — HUG is open to future collaborations — suggest this remains an active strategic question.

## Draft Article 04
**Target:** DigitalCoa.ch | **Cluster:** AI Governance / Compliance | **TIER:** TIER 2

### EN Version
**Headline:** The Deepfake Regulation Gap Is Creating an AI Compliance Consulting Opportunity

**Subhead:** The Swiss Green Party is demanding a ban on non-consensual AI-generated imagery. The regulatory response will define the AI Act compliance mandate for Swiss firms.

Les Vert·e·s (Swiss Green Party) demanded a ban on services that create non-consensual AI-generated nude images on April 1, 2026. The demand is not isolated — it reflects a broader recognition that AI-generated imagery represents a specific regulatory gap in both Swiss and EU law.

For Swiss companies with EU market exposure, the AI Act's provisions on AI-generated content are already creating compliance obligations. The deepfake demand raises the urgency: companies will need to demonstrate that their AI systems do not produce non-consensual imagery, and that they have adequate safeguards.

The consulting opportunity: AI governance framework development, AI Act compliance assessments, deepfake detection implementation, and training. Swiss consulting firms with AI practices are positioned to capture this demand — particularly for Geneva-based tech companies and financial services firms subject to both Swiss and EU AI governance requirements.

The timeline matters: AI Act implementation is underway in the EU. Swiss firms that delay AI governance work will face compliance gaps when AI Act obligations become enforceable.

**Titre:** Le vide réglementaire autour des deepfakes crée une opportunité de conseil en conformité IA

Pour les entreprises suisses exposées au marché européen, les dispositions de l'AI Act sur le contenu généré par l'IA créent déjà des obligations de conformité.
