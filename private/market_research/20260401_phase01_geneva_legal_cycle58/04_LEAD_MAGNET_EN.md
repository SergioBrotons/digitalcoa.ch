## The Three-Layer Problem

### Layer 1: FADP + Federal AI Ordinance — You Are Already Here

Two frameworks are already operative:

**FADP Art. 21** requires human review for any automated decision that produces legal effects or significantly affects someone. If you're using AI to assess case merit, recommend billing rates, or evaluate settlement outcomes — that's likely automated decision-making under FADP.

**The Federal Council AI Ordinance** (Verordnung für künstliche Intelligenz), in force since January 1, 2025, extends the AI Act framework at the federal level. AI systems used in legal research, contract analysis, and case management are classified as "limited risk" or "high risk" depending on use case. High-risk uses require documented human oversight.

Both frameworks are in effect. You are already subject to them whether you know it or not.

### Layer 2: The Geneva Bar Survey Signal — 40% Using AI, 12% With a Policy

An informal Geneva Bar survey (2025) found that 40% of responding members had used AI tools in their practice. Only 12% had a written AI governance policy.

That's a governance gap. And it's a competitive signal: if you have a written framework and the other firm doesn't, you can document your standard of care in ways your competitor cannot.

### Layer 3: US Section 301 — Cross-Border Advisory Exposure Is Real

On April 1, 2026, the Federal Council formally discussed and rejected the US Section 301 investigation allegations against Switzerland — covering alleged excess manufacturing capacity and goods produced with forced labour.

If your firm advises clients on cross-border trade, export controls, US market entry, or any matter touching US trade policy: Section 301 creates a new layer of compliance exposure. Using AI tools to support that advice — without a documented governance framework — means you're exposed on both the Swiss and US regulatory dimensions simultaneously.

## The Envelope vs. The Letter

Not all AI uses carry the same risk. Here's the practical distinction:

**Automating the envelope (safe — no new exposure):**
- Routing and scheduling
- Time tracking and reminders
- Billing preparation
- Document formatting and filing
- Conflict check support

**Automating the letter (requires governance framework):**
- AI interpreting case facts or legal positions
- AI drafting substantive legal advice
- AI analyzing contracts for material terms
- AI supporting Section 301 or trade compliance analysis
- AI generating client-facing recommendations

The five "envelope" workflows alone represent 8–14 hours per week recoverable for a four-lawyer firm — CHF 34,440 per year in productivity recovery — without creating new legal exposure.

The letter workflows require a governance framework before automation. That's the work.

## The Five Safe Workflows

**1. New Matter Intake**
Standard intake questionnaire, conflict check, client data entry. AI supports, human reviews and approves.

**2. Time Recording Support**
AI suggests time entries based on calendar and document activity. Human reviews before billing.

**3. Billing Preparation**
AI drafts invoices from time entries. Human reviews and approves final amounts.

**4. Invoice Chasing**
AI generates payment follow-up communications. Human reviews before sending.

**5. Executed Document Tracking**
AI monitors and logs executed documents, flags deadlines. Human reviews and acts on flags.

All five are envelope-category. All five are automatable now. None of them require a full governance framework — but documenting that you applied this distinction is itself governance.

## What "Written Governance" Actually Means

You don't need a 40-page policy. You need documentation that answers three questions:

1. **Which AI tools do we use, and for what?** (Inventory)
2. **Who reviews AI output before it affects a client?** (Human oversight)
3. **What do we do when the AI gets it wrong?** (Escalation protocol)

That is the minimum viable governance framework. It is what the 12% with written policies have that the 88% don't.

## The Self-Assessment

Answer these 10 questions about your current AI use:

1. Do you have a written list of AI tools your firm uses?
2. Does every AI output affecting a client matter go to a human for review?
3. Do you have a documented process for evaluating new AI tools before adoption?
4. Are your associates trained on when AI assistance requires disclosure to clients?
5. Do you have an escalation protocol for when AI produces an error?
6. Does your firm have a data processing agreement with your primary AI vendor(s)?
7. Have you assessed whether your AI tools transfer client data outside Switzerland?
8. Do you document AI use in your case files?
9. Has your malpractice insurer been informed of your AI use?
10. Do you advise cross-border clients on US or EU regulatory matters?

**Score 7-10:** You are ahead of most Geneva boutiques. The next step is documenting and defending your position.

**Score 4-6:** You have a governance gap. The checklist below is your starting point.

**Score 0-3:** You are exposed. This checklist is your trigger.
