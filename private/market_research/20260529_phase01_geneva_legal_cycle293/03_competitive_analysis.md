## What's Missing in the Market

### Gap 1: The "Boutique AI Governance Starter Kit"

**What exists**: Big Law AI governance frameworks (extensive, complex, consultancies to implement)
**What's missing**: A **simple, Swiss-specific, legally-sound starting point** for a 2–5 attorney firm that tells them:
- What AI policy to have
- What questions to ask vendors
- How to document AI use
- What "human review" means in their workflow
- How to respond to carrier attestation questions

**Why it matters**: Firms are stuck at zero because the available frameworks assume Big Law infrastructure. A lightweight, legally accurate starting framework does not exist commercially.

### Gap 2: The Swiss Attorney AI Documentation System

**What exists**: General legal tech tools that track document versions and edits
**What's missing**: A **Swiss-contextualized system for documenting AI use** on client matters that:
- Creates an audit trail of what AI was used for which client task
- Documents human review and sign-off
- Produces a defensible record if bar or carrier asks
- Is simple enough for a 3-person firm to actually use daily

**Why it matters**: The documentation requirement is real (SBA Guidelines, carrier attestation, professional liability) but no tool fills this specific niche for Swiss attorneys.

### Gap 3: The AI Matter-Level Control System

**What exists**: General practice management tools; AI tools with broad access controls
**What's missing**: A way for a boutique firm to **mark specific client matters as "no AI"** and have that restriction propagate across whatever tools the firm uses. As corporate clients begin instructing firms not to use AI on their matters, there is **no practical system** for honoring this at small-firm scale.

**Why it matters**: Client-directed AI restrictions are emerging as a new relationship management problem. Firms have no infrastructure to manage this operationally.

### Gap 4: The Carrier Attestation Answer Template

**What exists**: General AI governance questionnaires
**What's missing**: A **Swiss law firm-specific AI attestation response template** that:
- Anticipates what PI carriers are actually asking in 2025–2026
- Provides defensible answers in the language of Swiss professional responsibility
- Maps to SBA Guidelines (June 2024)
- Creates a document the managing partner can sign with confidence

**Why it matters**: Carriers are asking questions that have no standard answers. Firms are either avoiding the questions or making up answers.

### Gap 5: The "AI Use Inventory" Tool

**What exists**: General SaaS spend tracking; IT asset management
**What's missing**: A **simple registry** where a small firm can:
- List what AI tools are used in the firm
- By whom
- For what types of tasks
- With what vendor agreements in place
- Version-controlled and dated

**Why it matters**: When a carrier asks "what AI tools do you use?" the correct answer requires knowing this. Most small firms cannot answer accurately.

### Gap 6: The Swiss Attorney AI Training (Practical, Not Theoretical)

**What exists**: Online CLE courses on AI ethics; Chambers/Lexology analysis
**What's missing**: **Practical, in-Geneva, in-French-and-English training** that:
- Tells a junior associate what they can and cannot put into AI tools
- Gives a managing partner a framework for oversight
- Focuses on real workflows (research, contract review, brief drafting)
- Addresses the FADP and professional secrecy specifics

**Why it matters**: Most training available to Swiss attorneys is either too general or too US-focused. Practical Swiss-context training is scarce.

## Competitive Positioning Observations

### Swiss-Tool Advantage is Real but Under-Marketed
Swiss-hosted tools (CASUS, Silex, Swiss-Noxtua) have genuine compliance advantages — but their marketing does not directly address the specific anxiety points of small Geneva firms (attestation, personal liability, oversight documentation). They speak to enterprise and mid-market. Boutique firms feel like an afterthought.

### No "Geneva Boutique" Positioning in the Market
There is no AI tool or service currently positioned as: "designed for 2–5 attorney Geneva law firms navigating the gap between SBA Guidelines and carrier attestation." This is a **clear market positioning opportunity**.

### The Big Law Shadow
Consultancies (McKinsey, BCG, Big 4) offer AI governance for law firms — but at Big Law prices. Boutique firms know they need something but cannot afford to buy what Big Law buys. The **affordable professional-grade alternative is missing**.

### Legal Insurance Carriers as Unexpected Gatekeepers
Carriers asking AI questions at renewal are effectively creating demand for AI governance solutions — but no product or service has yet positioned itself as the answer to "my PI carrier asked me about AI." This is a **direct lead-generation opportunity**.

## NET-NEW Findings for C293 Phase 01

1. **The "attestation answer" is the unmet product**: No commercial solution exists to help Swiss boutique firms answer carrier AI questions defensibly. This is a pure product-market gap.

2. **Swiss tools lack boutique positioning**: CASUS, Silex, and Swiss-Noxtua are genuinely compliant solutions — but their marketing targets mid-market and enterprise. Small Geneva firms don't see themselves in their messaging.

3. **Documentation as the hardest problem**: Every tool assumes documentation will happen; no tool makes documentation effortless for a 3-person firm. This is the actual operational barrier.

4. **Matter-level AI restrictions are a coming problem with no current solution**: As client-directed "no AI" instructions increase, boutique firms will need infrastructure they don't have and can't buy off the shelf.

5. **Training market is US-centric and theoretical**: Swiss attorneys seeking practical guidance on AI use in their actual workflows have very few options that are both Swiss-contextualized and operationally useful.

6. **The pricing gap is structural**: No credible solution exists in the CHF 500–3,000/month range that addresses the specific bundle of needs (policy + inventory + documentation + training + attestation support) that a Geneva boutique firm has. Either too cheap (generic) or too expensive (Big Law tool).
