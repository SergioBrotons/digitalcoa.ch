**The Situation**

The form has your name on it.

You signed the carrier attestation. Like everyone does. You answered the questions honestly — yes, you use AI tools. Yes, you review the output. Yes, you supervise how they're used in your practice.

Six months later, you realized something: the form wasn't asking you to acknowledge something. It was asking you to commit to a standard. And now the carrier wants to see if you can prove you meet it.

The problem isn't that you did anything wrong. The problem is that the form created a documented standard, and you don't have the evidence to back it up.

---

**You have three problems simultaneously:**

**1. The form has your name on it.**
The carrier form asks for evidence of what you actually documented — not what you intended to document, not what your policy says you do. The form creates a record under your personal signature. If the carrier asks for evidence and you can't produce it, the attestation becomes evidence of inadequate governance.

**2. Your vendor has the records.**
Your AI vendor processes data on their servers. Their terms of service — which you accepted — define what they can do with that data. The carrier is now asking questions about vendor-held records that your vendor contracts never addressed. You are personally accountable for records you don't hold.

**3. Your staff is using AI you didn't authorize.**
Your associates and employees are using AI tools. You didn't authorize most of it. You don't have a system for tracking it. You are personally accountable for supervising AI use in your practice — including AI use you don't know about.

---

**The Documentation Gap No One Talks About**

The conversation about AI governance is full of frameworks, guidance, and principles. "Follow best practices." "Use AI responsibly." "Maintain professional standards."

What no one talks about is the evidence.

When the carrier asks whether you supervise AI tools adequately — they don't want to see your policy. They want to see what you actually documented about how you supervised those tools, for which matters, and what you found when you reviewed the output.

That's the gap. Not the policy gap. The evidence gap.
