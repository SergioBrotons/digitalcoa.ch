# Compliance Context: Geneva Consulting — Small Boutique AI Accountability
## Phase 03 · Cycle 295 · 2026-05-29

## Switzerland & Geneva-Specific Regulatory Landscape

### 1. Revised Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP / nLPD)
**Full name:** Loi fédérale sur la protection des données — révisée (applicable since 1er septembre 2023)

**Status:** In force. Switzerland updated its data protection law to approximate GDPR-level standards. Enforcement is active as of 2026.

**For small consulting firms operating in Geneva:**
- The firm is likely a "data controller" when it processes client data for consulting engagements
- If the firm uses AI tools that process client data (e.g., Claude, ChatGPT, Copilot), those AI vendors are "data processors"
- Under nFADP, the controller has obligations even when using processors: selecting capable processors, signing data processing agreements (DPAs), ensuring legal basis for processing
- **Personal liability is real** — the managing director or partner who owns the client relationship can face individual liability, not just the firm

**Key obligations for Geneva boutiques:**
- Establish legal basis for processing client data (consent, contract, legitimate interest)
- Ensure DPAs exist with AI tool vendors used in client work
- Respond to client data subject requests (access, deletion, correction)
- Notify the FFADO (Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner) in case of breaches within 72 hours
- Maintain records of processing activities (RoPA) — even small firms benefit from organized records

**Specific Geneva/ROMANDIE consideration:** French-language contract defaults matter. If engagement letters are in French, privacy disclosures and AI clauses must be in French too. The FFADO operates in all official languages.

### 2. FINMA AI Guidance (Banken and Insurance Sectors)
**Status:** FINMA has issued guidance on AI governance for supervised institutions (banks, insurers). Not directly applicable to small consulting firms, but increasingly relevant for Geneva boutiques working with financial sector clients.

**For boutiques with financial sector clients:**
- If consulting for a FINMA-supervised institution, the client's own AI governance requirements will filter down into questionnaires sent to consultants
- FINMA's focus on model risk management and AI explainability means clients will increasingly ask consultants about methodology documentation
- Boutique advisors supporting financial sector clients should anticipate stricter AI governance questionnaire expectations

**Direct applicability to small firms:** Low. Indirect applicability: HIGH via client questionnaires and expectations.

### 3. Swiss AI Regulatory Developments (2025–2026)
**Context:** Switzerland is developing its own AI regulatory framework. The Federal Council has issued AI principles and is moving toward a cantonal and federal AI strategy. Unlike the EU AI Act, Switzerland has not yet enacted comprehensive AI-specific legislation.

**Current state:** No binding AI-specific regulation for private sector beyond data protection. However:
- Sector-specific guidance (FINMA, health, education) is developing
- Public procurement AI requirements are becoming more specific
- Geneva cantonal government has AI usage guidelines for public administration

**What this means for small consulting firms:**
- No mandatory AI-specific compliance obligations exist yet at federal level
- De facto compliance pressure comes from CLIENTS, not regulators — AI questionnaires are the actual enforcement mechanism
- The absence of regulation does NOT mean no risk — client expectations ARE the enforcement
- As EU AI Act influences Swiss policy, the regulatory gap may narrow; firms preparing now have first-mover advantage in accountability infrastructure

---

### 4. Professional Liability Insurance (RC Pro / Berufshaftpflicht) — Evolving Standards
**Context:** Swiss professional liability insurers are rapidly updating policy conditions to include AI-related exclusions, attestation requirements, and coverage condition clauses.

**Current trends in Swiss RC Pro for professional services:**
- AI use attestation clauses are being added to policy renewal forms — this is NEW and many brokers don't understand them
- Policy conditions increasingly reference "AI governance" as a coverage condition
- If a firm certifies AI controls that don't exist, and an incident follows, carriers may dispute coverage based on misrepresentation
- The attestation question: "Do you maintain adequate controls over AI tools used in client engagements?" — what does "adequate" mean for a 10-person firm?

**What boutiques need:**
- Clear understanding of what they're certifying when they sign AI attestation clauses
- A practical AI control inventory that makes certification honest and defensible
- A documented AI governance posture that can be shared with brokers if questioned

**Swiss insurance market considerations:**
- Major Swiss RC Pro carriers (Zurich, Helvetia, Generali Suisse, AXA, Bâloise) are at different stages of AI policy evolution
- Boutique brokers (small insurance brokers serving SME market) often lack AI expertise — they can't help interpret attestation clauses
- This creates an advice vacuum: insurers ask questions but don't help answer them

### 5. Swiss Contract Law — General Terms and AI Liability
**Framework:** Swiss Code of Obligations (CO) governs commercial contracts and general terms and conditions.

**AI-specific considerations:**
- If a consulting engagement uses AI-assisted analysis, the engagement letter / general terms should ideally address:
  - Disclosure of AI assistance in deliverables
  - Limitations of AI-assisted analysis
  - Allocation of risk for AI-generated errors or omissions
  - Sub-consultant AI use notification requirements
- Current general terms in most small boutique firms do NOT contain any AI clauses — significant legal exposure gap
- Swiss courts have not yet established precedent on AI-generated advice liability — the legal framework is unsettled over who bears risk when AI-assisted consulting advice proves incorrect

**Practical risk:** If a client is harmed by AI-assisted advice, and the engagement letter has no AI disclosure clause, the firm's liability exposure is unclear and potentially broader — Swiss courts would apply general negligence standards with no contractual framework guiding AI-specific risk allocation.

### 6. Geneva Cantonal and Business Landscape
**Context:** Geneva has a specific professional services ecosystem: numerous small consulting boutiques, many working with international clients (corporate, NGO, diplomatic, institutional), often in French-English bilingual settings.

**Geneva-specific triggers:**
- International organizations (UN, WHO, WMO, ICC) increasingly send AI due diligence questionnaires to local vendors — Geneva boutiques are in the firing line
- Private banking and wealth management clients (Geneva's major sector) are among the most rigorous in AI governance expectations
- Geneva cantonal S委 (economic development) has limited AI advisory support for small professional services firms — the support programs are mostly aimed at tech startups, not professional services
- The Geneva Bar Association and Romandie professional associations have not yet published AI accountability guidance for consulting members — gap remains

**Implication:** No institutional support from Geneva ecosystem on AI accountability for professional services. This creates market opportunity but also means boutiques must self-source guidance.

## Key Practical Takeaways

1. **The nFADP is the primary regulatory exposure** — active, enforceable, personal liability attached. This is the most urgent compliance concern for small Geneva boutiques.

2. **Carrier attestation is a new and immediate risk** — insurers are asking questions in renewal forms that boutiques don't know how to answer honestly. This is a live coverage exposure, not theoretical.

3. **Client enforcement is ahead of regulatory enforcement** — boutiques will face actual commercial consequences (lost mandates, contracts challenged) from client AI questionnaires BEFORE they face regulatory penalties.

4. **Swiss contract law creates unaddressed liability exposure** — most engagement letters have zero AI clauses. In the absence of AI-specific case law, general negligence standards apply broadly.

5. **No Geneva institutional support exists** — the local ecosystem is not providing guidance. The market opportunity for trusted, practical, boutique-scale AI accountability advisory is wide open.
