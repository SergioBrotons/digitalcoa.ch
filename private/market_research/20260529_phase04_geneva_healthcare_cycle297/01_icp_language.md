# 01 — ICP Language: Geneva Independent Practice AI Accountability
## Phase 04 C297 | Geneva Healthcare | Independent Physician / Small Group Practice (2–10 physicians)

---

## How the ICP Speaks About AI Problems

### Primary Language Register

The Geneva independent physician does not describe their situation as an AI governance challenge. They describe it as a **personal accountability problem**:

- *"J'ai signé quelque chose pour l'assurance — je ne sais pas ce que j'ai signé"* (I signed something for the insurance — I don't know what I signed)
- *"On me demande de démontrer que je contrôle quelque chose dont je ne connais même pas l'existence"* (They're asking me to demonstrate I control something I don't even know exists)
- *"Je ne sais pas répondre à ces questions sur l'IA — et je ne voulais pas le dire"* (I don't know how to answer these AI questions — and I didn't want to say it)
- *"Mon EHR a intégré des fonctions d'IA — je ne les ai pas demandées"* (My EHR integrated AI functions — I didn't ask for them)
- *"Si quelque chose ne va pas, c'est mon nom sur le dossier, pas celui de l'outil"* (If something goes wrong, my name is on the record, not the tool's)

### Emotional Topology

The Geneva independent physician experiences AI accountability in this order of intensity:

1. **Personal liability fear** — direct, immediate, name-on-chart accountability with no delegation
2. **Carrier attestation anxiety** — document has arrived requiring signature on "adequate AI governance" they cannot define
3. **Patient confidentiality** — Art.321 CP professional secrecy obligations extended to AI-processed data they don't control
4. **Professional isolation** — peer circuit has no Geneva-calibrated exchange on this subject, each practice building in isolation
5. **Regulatory incomprehension** — FMH guidance tells them to apply human oversight without defining what that looks like Wednesday at 8:30 AM

### Specific Vocabulary: Where What They Say Diverges from What They Mean

| What They Say | What They Actually Mean |
|---|---|
| *"On utilise l'IA depuis des années"* | "We have AI features active we never evaluated" |
| *"Notre EHR est conforme"* | "We assume Swiss-hosted = compliant" |
| *"Le FMH va publier des lignes directrices"* | "Someone else will define what we need to do" |
| *"Notre approche est proportionnée"* | "We don't have a documented approach at all" |
| *"On a todo ce qu'il faut pour l'attestation"* | "The vendor provided templates we signed without review" |
| *"L'IA nous aide à mieux servir les patients"* | "AI output goes into records under our professional authority with no attribution architecture" |

### French-Language Specific Expressions (Geneva register)

- *"Ça m'est tombé dessus"* — AI things happened to the practice without decision event
- *"Je ne sais pas où ça se trouve dans mon dossier"* — AI-generated content unlocatable in medical record
- *"C'est ma tête qui est responsable, pas la machine"* — Confession of accountability without formal attribution
- *"On a signé pour la continuité"* — Carrier attestation signed under time pressure for coverage continuity
- *"On n'a pas de problème, mais…"* — Pre-concerned positioning before naming the actual problem
- *"LeFMH ne peut pas nous aider sur ça"* — Explicit frustration that professional body offers no proportionate guidance
- *"Personne ne m'explique concrètement ce que je dois faire"* — Request for operational methodology not principles

### Key Distinction: License-Centric vs. Fine-Centric Framing

Geneva healthcare physicians absorb AI accountability through **license-centric** framing (what threatens my ability to practice) not the fine-centric framing used in finance or legal. This is not a contractual financial exposure — it is a professional existence exposure.

The physician asks: *"Est-ce que je risque mon cabinet?"* not *"Est-ce que je risque une sanction?"*

This produces:
- Higher urgency driven by existential stakes
- Different information-seeking behavior (starts from "what does this do to my practice?" not "what does this cost?")
- Resistance to abstract compliance frameworks that don't map to their specific situation
- Willingness to act when problem is named in recognizable personal-professional terms

### What Triggers Action vs. What Produces Paralysis

**Produces action:**
- Carrier attestation arrives — concrete, unavoidable, has a deadline
- Colleague mentions a challenge case — peer circuit is primary information source
- Patient brings in AI-generated analysis — direct, present-tense, record-visible problem
- Swissdamed registration deadline approaches — 1 July 2026 creates finite pressure point

**Produces paralysis:**
- FMH principles without operational endpoint — guidance generates no actionable path
- "Proportionate" anything — term meaningless without scale reference for 2-10 physician practice
- General AI governance frameworks — enterprise-designed tools create intimidation rather than clarity
- Multi-year regulatory timeline — "2026" or "2027" sounds distant enough to defer

### How They Talk About What They Cannot Discuss

The Geneva independent physician holds a specific problem they cannot name in peer settings: **their EHR and AI tools are making decisions about their own professional accountability in ways they did not architect and cannot enumerate.**

French verbal marker for this unspeakable problem: *"Je ne suis pas maître de—"* (I am not master of—)

Completion varies: the AI system, the documentation architecture, the data flows, the vendor's model updates.

This is the ICP's most acute unformulated anxiety. It is not "AI is good or bad." It is "I am accountable for something I did not design, cannot audit, and did not choose."
