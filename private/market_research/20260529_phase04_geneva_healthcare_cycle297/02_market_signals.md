# 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Complaints, Failures
## Phase 04 C297 | Geneva Healthcare | Independent Physician / Small Group Practice (2–10 physicians)

---

## Current Problems Surfacing in Geneva Healthcare Right Now

### Signal 1: Carrier Attestation Stampede with Post-Signature Realization

**What's happening:** Swiss medical liability carriers are sending AI attestation questionnaires to independent practices. Physicians are signing under time pressure (coverage continuity required) using vendor-downloaded templates and internet searches. Post-signature, during a patient incident or carrier review, they realize the documentation they signed does not correspond to their actual practice.

**Why it's different from finance/legal:** Geneva medical practices have no pre-signing review service calibrated to medical professional liability. The finance world developed pre-signing carrier review as a service category. Medical practice market has no equivalent — the same personal liability exposure with no discharge pathway.

**Market signal heard from:** Independent practice MDs expressing confusion about what they certified, combined with evidence that attestation documents refer to AI governance adequacy no one has operationally defined at small-practice scale.

**Failure mode:** Urgency-driven documentation creation produces self-incriminating evidence. The physician's signature certifies to a standard they cannot meet, for AI tools they cannot enumerate, using evidence they did not produce at the time.

---

### Signal 2: Swissdamed Registration Deadline (1 July 2026) — Proportionate Path Unknown

**What's happening:** MedDO requires medical device registration in Swissdamed (based on EUDAMED) from 1 July 2026. Independent physicians using AI-assisted documentation tools do not know whether their tools qualify as medical devices under the classification criteria. The Tier 1/Tier 2 distinction is written for manufacturers, not outpatient practices. The registration obligation exists; the proportionate compliance path for a 2-10 physician practice does not.

**Market signal heard from:** Practice managers and MDs asking whether specific EHR AI features, diagnostic support tools, or documentation assistance software require registration — and receiving vendor silence or generic "contact us for more information" responses.

**Failure mode:** Practices either over-abstract (assume nothing requires registration) or over-comply (treat everything as requiring registration). Neither is proportionate. Neither addresses the actual classification question.

---

### Signal 3: Multi-Physician Practice Internal Accountability Diffusion

**What's happening:** In a 3-6 physician practice, each physician independently activates different AI features, uses different tools for documentation, and implements different AI-assisted practices. The practice receives a carrier attestation. The signing physician certifies to an AI surface they cannot enumerate because they don't fully know what their partners are using. No Geneva methodology exists for practice-level AI governance coordination.

**Market signal heard from:** Group practice directors acknowledging they don't know what their colleagues use, combined with carrier attestation form asking about "all AI tools used in your practice."

**Failure mode:** One physician signs for the entire practice's AI governance. That physician cannot document what they do not know. The signature creates personal liability for AI usage that occurred through other physicians' independent decisions the signing physician cannot audit.

---

### Signal 4: Patient-Generated AI Content Entering Medical Records

**What's happening:** Patients are arriving at independent practices with consumer AI-generated health analyses (symptom checkers, AI diagnostic tools, health data from wearables). Physicians incorporate this content into medical records under their professional authority. The physician is now accountable for AI output they did not deploy, did not configure, and did not evaluate.

**Market signal heard from:** Physicians reporting this is now frequent and increasing, with no documentation standard for how patient-generated AI content should enter the record, be attributed, and be evaluated.

**Failure mode:** Medical record documents patient AI input and physician decision but not the deliberation moment where AI input was evaluated and either accepted or rejected. The high-accountability moment (clinical decision influenced by patient-provided AI) is undocumented.

---

### Signal 5: AI Tool Mid-Record Switching Without Transition Architecture

**What's happening:** When a practice switches EHR AI vendors or updates its AI documentation features mid-patient-record, the record becomes a continuous stream from multiple systems with no transition documentation. The physician cannot identify which AI generated which clinical content. The record is authoritative under the physician's name with no AI attribution architecture.

**Market signal heard from:** Practices that have changed EHR systems or upgraded AI features mid-practice, acknowledging the AI contribution is not traceable in their historical records.

**Failure mode:** Each tool switch compounds the attribution problem — a cascade of AI-to-AI influence the physician is accountable for without a reconstructable chain. When a challenge occurs, the physician's name is on the record with no AI attribution.

---

### Signal 6: AI-Associated Adverse Outcome Documentation Architecture Absence

**What's happening:** When an adverse clinical outcome occurs, existing incident documentation infrastructure (designed for human clinical decision-making) has no category for: AI system state, AI tool version at time of event, AI recommendation made, physician's AI-influenced vs. AI-independent decisions. The physician's name is on the record; the AI's contribution is invisible to the review process.

**Market signal heard from:** Risk management discussions at the practice level acknowledging post-incident AI documentation is an unnamed category with no defined requirement from any Geneva medical body or carrier.

**Failure mode:** Adverse events in AI-assisted practices are reviewed as if they occurred under fully human decision-making. The AI contribution to the outcome cannot be isolated because it was never documented.

## Specific Complaint Vignettes (Composite from Market Intelligence)

**Complaint A: "Mon carrier m'a envoyé un questionnaire — j'ai两周 pour répondre"**
(My carrier sent me a questionnaire — I have two weeks to respond)
Context: The attestation format assumes the physician has documented AI governance in advance. No physician has. The two-week deadline produces emergency documentation retrofitting.

**Complaint B: "Je ne sais pas combien d'outils d'IA sont actifs dans mon cabinet"**
(I don't know how many AI tools are active in my practice)
Context: EHR has integrated AI features through updates. Physicians using different tools for different functions can't enumerate aggregate AI surface. The question "what AI do you use?" cannot be answered by the person signing for the practice.

**Complaint C: "Le FMH dit qu'il faut une surveillance humaine — mais ça veut dire quoi concrètement?"**
(FMH says human oversight is required — but what does that actually mean?)
Context: FMH principle without operational endpoint. 8:30 AM Wednesday with ten patients, at a two-physician practice, with one person holding user/compliance/billing roles simultaneously. The principle cannot be operationally fulfilled.

**Complaint D: "Un patient m'apporte une analyse IA — qu'est-ce que je dois noter?"**
(A patient brings me an AI analysis — what should I document?)
Context: No Geneva protocol for this scenario. Patient-generated AI content entering the medical record under physician authority. The physician evaluation moment is the highest-accountability moment in this scenario — and the most undocumented.

**Complaint E: "Si quelque chose se passe mal, est-ce que mon dossier montre ce que l'IA a vraiment fait?"**
(If something goes wrong, does my record show what the AI actually did?)
Context: Present-tense concern about retroactive reconstruction. The concern is accurate: records show decision and outcome, not AI system state or recommendation at time of decision. The physician suspects their records would not survive this question.

## Distinction from Prior Cycles

These signals are distinct from C214–C296 Phase 04 in that they name specific present-tense market failures (carrier attestation stampede, Swissdamed deadline proximity, multi-physician diffusion, patient-generated AI evaluation void, mid-record tool switching, adverse outcome documentation architecture) rather than naming governance gaps without isolating specific market failure mechanisms.

The market is not failing to understand AI governance abstractly. The market is experiencing specific consequences of AI governance absence and cannot find proportionate Geneva-calibrated methodology to address them.
