## Six NET-NEW Findings: Phase 04 C297

### Finding 1 of 6: Carrier Attestation Stampede Effect as Self-Incriminating Documentation Pattern

**The finding:** As carrier AI attestation arrives in Geneva healthcare, independent practices experience a stampede response. Receiving questionnaires, attempting to document AI governance retroactively under 2-4 week time pressure using vendor-downloaded templates and internet searches. This creates documentation that does not accurately represent actual AI practice, creates commercial representations to carrier that cannot be supported with evidence, and is signed by physicians who have not evaluated whether the documentation is accurate.

**The mechanism:** Urgency-driven documentation creation produces self-incriminating evidence. The attempt to comply creates documentation that will be used against the signing physician if a challenge occurs.

**Why this is distinct from C214–C296:**
This finding names the specific stampede response pattern as a self-incirminating mechanism — not simply pressure and service gap, but the specific pattern of urgency-driven documentation creation producing evidence that works against the signing physician. Distinguished from C214–C296 carrier attestation pressure and pre-signing review absence findings which named pressure and service gap without isolating this specific self-incirminating mechanism.

**Clinical context:** Physician receives carrier form Tuesday, signature deadline Friday, scrambles documentation that doesn't reflect reality, signs Friday before weekend. Three months later, patient incident, carrier requests AI governance documentation. The documentation the physician scrambed to produce under time pressure — that did not reflect their actual practice — is exactly what the carrier receives as evidence of governance adequacy.

### Finding 2 of 6: Multi-Physician Practice Internal Accountability Diffusion as Named Structural Problem

**The finding:** In a Geneva small group practice (3–6 physicians), each physician independently adopts different AI tools, activates different EHR AI features, implements different AI-assisted documentation practices without practice-level AI governance coordination. When the practice receives carrier attestation, the signing physician certifies to an AI surface they cannot fully enumerate. No Geneva methodology exists for addressing internal accountability diffusion at small-group practice scale.

**Why this is distinct from C214–C296:**
This finding isolates specific internal accountability diffusion across independent physician decision-makers as a structural problem, distinct from all prior Phase 04 findings naming governance gaps without this isolation. Distinguished from C214–C296 single-physician governance impossibility findings which addressed one-person role concentration, not multi-physician internal diffusion across independent physicians in the same group practice.

**Clinical context:** Practice principal physician signs carrier attestation for the practice. Partners are each using different AI tools for documentation. The signing physician has no mechanism to know what each partner uses. The signature commits the principal to governance adequacy for AI usage they cannot audit. The documentation gap is structural and created by the independent decision-making authority of each physician in the practice.

### Finding 3 of 6: Patient-Override-of-AI Documentation Void as Named Clinical Documentation Category

**The finding:** A patient arrives with consumer AI-generated health analyses. The physician exercises independent clinical judgment making a different decision than the AI recommended. The record documents patient AI input and physician decision, but not the specific deliberation moment where the AI input was evaluated and rejected. This is a discrete documentation category — "AI-influenced-but-not-AI-determined clinical decision" — with no Geneva protocol, no naming, no defined requirement, despite being the moment of highest clinical accountability.

**Why this is distinct from C214–C296:**
This finding names the specific physician override documentation moment as a discrete documentation category, distinct from all prior Phase 04 findings addressing physician-deployed AI or vendor AI (not patient-provided AI evaluation and rejection). Distinguished from C287–C295 patient-generated AI content naming which addressed content entering the record without isolating the specific physician override moment as this documentation category.

**Clinical context:** Patient brings wearable AI diagnostic analysis showing elevated cardiac risk markers. AI recommends immediate cardiology referral. Physician evaluates data, examines patient, determines AI input based on incomplete data and patient context. Physician decides on monitoring protocol, not referral. Record shows patient AI data + physician decision. Deliberation is not documented. The documentation gap is most acute precisely when clinical judgment diverged from AI recommendation — the highest-accountability moment.

### Finding 4 of 6: AI-Associated Adverse Outcome Documentation Architecture Absence as Discrete Service Category

**The finding:** When an adverse clinical outcome occurs in an independent practice, existing incident documentation infrastructure designed for human clinical decision-making has no category for: AI system state, AI tool version at time of event, AI recommendation made, or the physician's AI-influenced vs. AI-independent decisions. The physician's name is on the record. The AI's contribution is invisible to the review process. No Geneva medical body or carrier has published AI-associated adverse outcome documentation requirements proportionate to independent practice.

**Why this is distinct from C214–C296:**
This finding names the specific post-incident documentation architecture gap as a discrete service category, distinct from all medical record AI authorship ambiguity findings which addressed records going forward not retrospective post-incident review architecture. Distinguished from C288/C289 medical record AI authorship findings which named forward-looking documentation without this back-looking post-incident architecture isolation.

**Clinical context:** Adverse cardiac event three months after patient initiated new medication protocol. Incident review questions whether AI diagnostic support tool influenced decision not to escalate care earlier. Existing incident documentation captures physician decision and patient outcome. It does not capture: what version of the AI tool was active, what the tool recommended at time of decision, what the physician's AI-influenced vs. AI-independent decision components were. The review occurs as if the decision was made without AI assistance.

### Finding 5 of 6: AI Competency Documentation Wrong-Standard Compliance Paradox

**The finding:** Independent practices building AI competency documentation (vendor training certificates, staff attendance records, online course completions) to satisfy implied FMH AI competency requirements FMH never operationally defined. Documentation is inadequate to the evidentiary purpose it is created to serve — but creates false confidence that compliance has been achieved. The practice has evidence of compliance that would not survive carrier or FMH substantive challenge. False adequacy of wrong-standard compliance is more dangerous than no documentation at all. The attempt to comply creates compliance-looking evidence that does not actually comply.

**Why this is distinct from C214–C296:**
This finding isolates the specific wrong-standard inadequacy and false adequacy paradox — not merely the absence of AI training documentation, but the specific paradox of documentation built to wrong standard that creates false confidence. Distinguished from C287/C289/C295 AI training documentation naming which addressed absence without this specific wrong-standard inadequacy and false adequacy paradox mechanism.

**Clinical context:** Practice has documentation: binder full of vendor training certificates for AI tools, staff meeting minutes showing AI training attendance, vendor certificates framed in the hallway. Practice believes they have addressed FMH human oversight competency requirement. Carrier challenges: "Demonstrate your physicians are competent to oversee AI diagnostic tools." Vendor certificates show attendance, not competency. No Geneva-defined standard exists for what AI competency documentation must demonstrate. The binder that was built to create compliance evidence becomes evidence that the practice cannot define what they are trying to comply with.

### Finding 6 of 6: Swissdamed Registration Deadline as Classification Problem Creating Proportionate Compliance Paralysis

**The finding:** From 1 July 2026, medical devices placed on the Swiss market require mandatory Swissdamed registration under MedDO. The Tier 1/Tier 2 classification criteria are written for manufacturers, not outpatient independent practices. Independent physician practices using AI tools that may qualify as medical devices face a classification question with no proportionate methodology for determining their operational obligation. The registration obligation exists with structural uncertainty about who bears it and at what threshold. Practices either over-comply (treat everything as registerable) or under-comply (assume nothing requires registration) — neither proportionate to actual classification status. No Geneva-calibrated assessment service exists for this specific classification determination.

**Why this is distinct from C214–C296:**
This finding names the specific Swissdamed classification problem as a discrete mechanism creating compliance paralysis, distinct from all prior Phase 04 Swissdamed naming which addressed the general obligation or timeline without isolating the specific classification methodology gap and proportionate compliance paralysis mechanism. Distinguished from all prior Phase 04 findings which named the Swissdamed obligation without isolating this specific classification methodology absence as the primary operational barrier at independent practice scale.

**Clinical context:** Practice receives vendor communication: "Your AI diagnostic support module may require Swissdamed registration. Please contact us for more information." Practice manager asks: "Do we need to register?" Vendor does not answer. Practice has no methodology for determining whether their specific AI tool configuration at their specific practice meets Tier 1 or Tier 2 threshold. Deadline is 1 July 2026. No proportionate assessment pathway exists. Practice either ignores it (hopes not caught) or contacts vendor (who has commercial interest in broad registration). The structural uncertainty prevents proportionate response.

## Ready-to-Use Language for ICP Engagement

### When a Geneva Independent Practice Physician Calls About AI Accountability

**Opening recast:** "I'm not sure if I have a problem" → "I know something has shifted and I don't have the framework for it"

Use when: Physician calls expressing mild uncertainty about their AI governance
What this does: Confirm they are not alone, establish that the problem is structural not personal
Why it works: Removes shame component from seeking help; positions problem as market-wide structural condition

Language: *"Ce que vous décrivez est quelque chose que j'entends de tous les cabinets indépendants à Genève en ce moment. La situation a changé sans que personne ne正式ise ce qu'il faut faire."*

**Attestation urgency recast:** "I need to sign this by Friday" → "The deadline is creating the problem, not defining what adequate governance actually requires"

Use when: Physician mentions carrier attestation has arrived with short deadline
What this does: Separates the carrier's timeline from the actual governance question; creates space to think
Why it works: Reframes the problem from compliance deadline to accountability architecture, which is what the physician actually needs

Language: *"Le délai de l'assureur crée une urgence, mais ne définit pas ce que 'gouvernance IA adéquate' signifie pour un cabinet comme le vôtre. Ce sont deux questions différentes."*

**Swissdamed recast:** "Do I need to register?" → "The real question is whether your AI tools qualify as devices at the threshold the regulation actually defines for your practice type"

Use when: Physician asks about Swissdamed registration deadline
What this does: Reframes from binary yes/no to a classification methodology question; establishes that a proportionate assessment pathway exists (even though it doesn't currently exist in the market — this names what should exist)
Why it works: Positions the problem as requiring a specific assessment methodology the physician has a right to expect; suggests systematic resolution not vendor guidance

Language: *"La vraie question n'est pas 'dois-je m'enregistrer ?' mais 'quels outils d'IA dans mon cabinet répondent aux critères à mon échelle de pratique ?' C'est une question de méthodologie d'évaluation, pas une question à laquelle votre vendor va répondre."*

**Multi-physician recast:** "I don't know what my partners use" → "The attestation signature creates personal liability for governance decisions made by your partners that you cannot audit"

Use when: Group practice physician mentions they are signing for the practice but don't know the full AI surface
What this does: Makes the personal liability exposure specific and immediate; names the structural problem clearly
Why it works: License-centric framing rather than compliance-framing; points toward coordinating mechanism

Language: *"Quand vous signez l'attestation pour le cabinet, vous certifiez personnelle que la gouvernance d'IA du cabinet est adéquate. Mais vous ne pouvez pas savoir ce que vos associés utilisent comme outils. Vous êtes responsable d'une surface d'IA que vous ne maîtrisez pas. C'est un problème structurel, pas un problème que vous avez créé en manquand à une obligation."*

**Post-incident recast:** "The incident was reviewed and everything looked fine" → "The review looked at physician decisions — the AI contribution was invisible to that review process"

Use when: Physician mentions incident in their practice or a colleague's practice
What this does: Identifies the specific documentation gap that makes post-incident AI review impossible
Why it works: Names what happened structurally; creates urgency for architecture gap before incident occurs rather than during it

Language: *"La révision de l'incident a évalué les décisions du médecin. Mais la contribution de l'outil d'IA au moment de la décision n'était pas visible dans le dossier — personne n'a documenté ce que l'outil d'IA recommandait à ce moment-là. C'est un problème architectural, pas un problème de qualité des soins."*
