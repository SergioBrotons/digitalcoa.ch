# 03 — Competitive Analysis: What Exists vs. What's Missing
## Phase 04 C297 | Geneva Healthcare | Independent Physician / Small Group Practice (2–10 physicians)

---

## What Exists in the Geneva Healthcare AI Governance Market

### What Exists

**1. Vendor-Provided AI Governance Documentation**
- AI tool vendors (EHR providers, diagnostic AI companies) provide "compliance guides" that are designed to support vendor's market positioning, not to define physician professional accountability
- These documents certify vendor's AI system meets regulatory requirements — they do not address how the physician should document their own governance decisions
- Physician is told they are "covered" by vendor documentation — but carrier attestation asks physician to certify their own governance adequacy, not vendor's

**2. General AI Governance Frameworks**
- FMH publications on AI are principle-level (human oversight, physician responsibility) without operational translation for independent practice scale
- MedTech and health AI associations publish guidelines designed for institutional or manufacturer context
- No framework exists that is written for 2-10 physician Geneva outpatient practice from the physician's personal accountability starting point

**3. Carrier Attestation Forms**
- Swiss medical liability carriers are sending attestation questionnaires
- Forms are designed for underwriting risk assessment, not professional liability protection
- No pre-signing review service exists to help physicians understand what they are certifying before signature

**4. Legal Guidance on Data Protection**
- FADP/nFADP guidance exists — but it addresses data protection obligations, not medical professional accountability specific to AI-assisted clinical decisions
- Data protection lawyer and medical liability lawyer are two different professional communities with no integrated offering for AI-assisted practice

**5. Swissdamed Registration Support**
- Swissdamed database exists (EUDAMED-based)
- Vendor community offering "help with registration" — but for manufacturers, not for practices trying to determine if their tools require registration
- Registration deadline of 1 July 2026 creating anxiety without proportionate methodology

### What's Missing (The White Space)

**1. Medical Practice Pre-Signing Carrier Review Service**
What it is: A service that analyzes the carrier AI attestation from the physician's professional liability perspective before signature. What it addresses: Whether the attestation standard is achievable at the practice's current AI governance state, what gaps exist, what the physician is actually certifying to, and what actions reduce liability before signing.

Why it doesn't exist: The finance world developed pre-signing carrier review because carrier attestation has been active longer in that sector. Medical practice carrier attestation wave is 6-12 months behind. The service gap is specifically temporal — medical practice is arriving at the same problem finance discovered 12 months earlier, without having developed the response infrastructure.

**2. Small-Practice Proportionate AI Governance Methodology**
What it is: A documented, repeatable governance system designed for 2-10 physician scale. What it addresses: Single-person accountability structures, practice-level AI surface documentation, multi-physician coordination mechanisms, evidence standards proportionate to carrier attestation.

Why it doesn't exist: All existing AI governance methodology is designed for organizations with separate compliance functions, IT departments, legal counsel, and delegated accountability. Independent physician practices are structurally incompatible with these frameworks. No methodology exists for this segment because the governance design problem is genuinely different at small practice scale.

**3. Practice-Level AI Attribution Architecture**
What it is: A documentation system that traces which AI tool contributed which content to which clinical record, with version tracking and update awareness. What it addresses: The physician's ability to answer "what did the AI do in this record?" — a question that becomes acute when challenged.

Why it doesn't exist: This requires a documentation infrastructure different from anything used in human clinical decision-making. No Geneva medical body has defined the standard. No carrier has published the requirement. The gap persists because no authoritative body has named it as an obligation.

**4. Multi-Physician Practice AI Coordination Protocol**
What it is: A practice-level mechanism for ensuring the signing physician can certify to the practice's AI surface because they have actually enumerated it. What it addresses: Internal accountability diffusion — the structural problem where separate physicians independently adopt different AI practices without coordination.
Why it doesn't exist: Group practice AI governance has no precedent framework. Each practice is solving this informally, in isolation, without knowing what adequate coordination looks like.

**5. Patient-Generated AI Content Documentation Protocol**
What it is: A clinical documentation practice for evaluating and recording patient-provided AI health analyses. What it addresses: The specific documentation moment where the physician evaluates and either accepts or rejects AI-generated patient content.

Why it doesn't exist: No Geneva protocol, no naming, no defined requirement. This category did not exist before consumer AI tools made this scenario common. It exists as a market problem right now without any service addressing it.

**6. Independent Practice Adverse Incident AI Documentation System**
What it is: A post-incident documentation architecture that captures AI system state, tool version, recommendation made, and the physician's decision-making process in a format reviewable under medical liability standards. What it addresses: After an adverse outcome in an AI-assisted practice, the ability to demonstrate what the AI contributed to the outcome.

Why it doesn't exist: Medical incident documentation infrastructure is designed for human clinical decisions. No Geneva medical body or carrier has published AI-associated adverse outcome documentation requirements proportionate to independent practice. Risk management infrastructure has not caught up to AI-assisted practice reality.

**7. Practice AI Governance Budget Architecture**
What it is: A category for AI governance maintenance costs within independent practice financials. What it addresses: The structural under-governance caused by governance costs being invisible in practice budgets.

Why it doesn't exist: Independent practices budget clinical operations, staff, premises, insurance. No budget category exists for "governance of AI tools we didn't choose, can't enumerate, and can't stop using without losing core functionality." This gap is structural, not a priority failure.

## Competitive Positioning Assessment

### Who Could Fill These Gaps

**Medical device manufacturers:** Will not provide small-practice methodology because doing so would limit their market positioning advantage
**EHR vendors:** Will not provide independent practice governance architecture because it would create liability for their own products
**General AI governance consultancies:** Not Geneva-calibrated, not medical-practice-calibrated, too expensive and too enterprise-designed
**Swiss medical associations:** Producing principle-level guidance without operational implementation path
**Legal counsel:** Data protection focus, not medical professional liability focus at this specific intersection
**Carriers:** Will not provide pre-signing review as it creates conflict with their underwriting function

### Structural Barrier to Entry

The white space is not unfilled because no one noticed. It is unfilled because:

1. The segment (2-10 physician independent practice) is structurally underserved by design — enterprise tools do not serve this scale
2. The problem requires Geneva-specific regulatory intelligence that most service providers cannot access or interpret
3. The service must be priced at a point proportionate to independent practice budgets — too expensive and the market doesn't materialize
4. The service requires sustained participation in Geneva circuit to understand the accountability formation mechanisms

### Moat Assessment

The Geneva clinical AI accountability service space has a structural moat created by the information economy architecture: most operationally relevant intelligence cannot be published — it is formed in informal circuit, accessible only through sustained participation.

This moat is more durable than analytical framework replication because pattern knowledge from circuit participation cannot be produced from methodology documents or general expertise claims.
