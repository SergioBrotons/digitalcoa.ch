# 02 — Market Signals: Geneva Boutique Wealth Management

## Phase 02: Geneva Finance — Cycle 292

---

## Market Context

Geneva's boutique wealth management sector operates in a specific accountability ecosystem: Swiss private banking heritage, institutional counterparty expectations, FINMA supervision, SRO frameworks, and nFADP obligations simultaneously. AI tool deployment is accelerating across the sector, but the governance architecture is not keeping pace.

The result: accumulation of accountability exposure that boutique WM MDs are only beginning to name.

---

## Current Market Problems

### Problem Cluster 1: The Institutional Client Questionnaire Is the First Accountability Moment

**What's happening:** Institutional clients (pension funds, family offices, institutional investors) are sending increasingly detailed AI governance questionnaires to their Geneva boutique WM managers. These questionnaires arrive at the manager's desk as 20–40 page documents with short response deadlines.

**Why it's a problem:**
- The MD has no pre-built response framework
- The questionnaire is a competitive intelligence instrument for the client — they are comparing responses across their manager roster
- The MD's response becomes a commercial representation about the firm's AI governance
- A poorly constructed response creates competitive disadvantage
- A completed response with no underlying architecture creates liability exposure

**Signal intensity:** High and accelerating. Institutional clients are treating AI governance as a due diligence priority.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Institutional clients with Geneva presence are calibrated to Geneva's regulatory ecosystem. They ask questions that map to Swiss-specific obligations (nFADP Art.21, FINMA Circular 2016/01, SRO frameworks). A response written for a UK or US institutional client may not satisfy Geneva institutional expectations.

---

### Problem Cluster 2: Carrier Attestation Is Personal Liability, Not Compliance Documentation

**What's happening:** At policy renewal, carriers are including AI governance attestation sections. The MD is asked to personally sign attesting to "adequate AI governance controls." The standard is undefined. The MD's signature creates personal liability.

**Why it's a problem:**
- The attestation form is written by carrier legal teams, designed for underwriting risk evaluation
- No Geneva service exists to provide pre-signing review of what the MD is committing to personally
- The MD does not know what "adequate" means in the context of boutique WM AI governance
- Carrier attestation forms are evolving — third-generation forms now ask for specific evidence of AI governance decisions made, not just policy documents
- The MD's signature does not have the same protection as a lawyer's signature on a brief

**Signal intensity:** Acute. Carrier renewals are predictable calendar events. The attestation question arrives on a known schedule.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Swiss carrier iteration patterns reflect Swiss regulatory evolution. Third-generation attestation questions in Switzerland are different from US or UK carrier forms because they reflect Swiss regulatory concepts (FINMA supervision, SRO frameworks, nFADP obligations) that non-Swiss forms do not address.

---

### Problem Cluster 3: Agentic AI Is Arriving Before Governance Architecture Exists

**What's happening:** AI tool vendors are moving from generative AI (producing text, analysis) to agentic AI (executing tasks autonomously: rebalancing, reporting, compliance monitoring). Boutique WM firms are beginning to deploy or evaluate agentic tools.

**Why it's a problem:**
- Agentic AI operates continuously without discrete human authorization moments
- Traditional AI governance documentation (what tools, what data, what policies) does not address continuous execution authority
- The MD cannot document what they did not authorize through discrete decisions because agentic AI self-initiates
- Existing FINMA and SRO guidance was written for generative AI, not agentic AI
- The accountability question is different: not "did we use AI?" but "did we authorize what AI did autonomously?"

**Signal intensity:** Emerging, accelerating toward acute within 12–18 months. Agentic AI is the next wave in WM technology.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva boutique WMs are smaller than institutional banks but face the same agentic AI exposure. The proportionality question — what does adequate agentic AI governance look like at boutique scale — has not been answered by any Swiss authority.

---

### Problem Cluster 4: nFADP Art.21 Automated Decision Obligations Are Present-Tense and Unnamed

**What's happening:** nFADP Article 21 gives data subjects the right to:
1. Be informed when a significant decision is made solely by automated processing
2. Request human review of the automated decision
3. Express their point of view

For a boutique WM firm, every AI-assisted portfolio recommendation could constitute an automated decision triggering Art.21 obligations.

**Why it's a problem:**
- Boutique WMs have not issued Art.21 notices to clients
- No Art.21 request response protocol exists at boutique scale
- Client-facing AI-assisted reports do not contain Art.21 disclosure language
- The obligation is present-tense — it exists regardless of firm awareness
- FDPIC enforcement posture has shifted from guidance-first to enforcement-live (May 2025)

**Signal intensity:** Present-tense exposure, structurally invisible. No Geneva boutique is proactively addressing this.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Art.21 obligations are triggered by AI-assisted decisions in client portfolios. This is not a data protection abstraction — it is a specific obligation arising from specific client-facing AI use.

---

### Problem Cluster 5: FINMA Circular 2016/01 AI-Adjacent Disclosure Requirements Are Not Mapped to AI Usage

**What's happening:** FINMA Circular 2016/01 on information obligations for client portfolio management requires disclosure of portfolio management approach, including any significant changes. AI-assisted portfolio construction may constitute a significant change requiring disclosure.

**Why it's a problem:**
- No Geneva boutique has a documented methodology for determining when AI-assisted portfolio construction triggers Circular 2016/01 disclosure
- The question of what disclosure is required — to whom, in what form, at what moment — has not been answered at boutique scale
- Circular 2016/01 disclosure obligations and nFADP Art.21 obligations may be simultaneously triggered, creating compound disclosure requirements
- The MD cannot determine the disclosure scope because the AI usage history across the mandate book is undocumented

**Signal intensity:** Present-tense and unnamed. No SRO or FINMA guidance addresses this specific question for boutique WMs.

---

### Problem Cluster 6: The Documentation Window Is Not a Future Problem — It Is Closing Now

**What's happening:** Documentation built during the current window period (before standards crystallize) has different evidentiary value than documentation built after standards arrive. The documentation window is the period when the MD can build evidence of governance reasoning that predates the accountability question.

**Why it's a problem:**
- Documentation built after the carrier asks, after the institutional client asks, after a regulatory question arrives, is retrospectively constructed
- Retrospective documentation does not have the same evidentiary weight as contemporaneous documentation
- The window is closing through the passage of time — each month that passes without documentation reduces the firm's retrospective evidence position
- The MD does not know this is a present-tense problem: every month of undocumented AI usage is a month of unrecoverable evidence loss

**Signal intensity:** Present-tense and accelerating. The window closes with every day the firm operates without AI governance documentation architecture.
