### Finding 01: Institutional Client AI Questionnaire as Competitive Intelligence Instrument Creates Documentation Paradox Distinct from Compliance Response

**What it is:** Institutional clients send AI governance questionnaires to Geneva boutique WMs as competitive intelligence instruments — they are comparing responses across their manager roster to assess which firms have thought carefully about AI governance and which have not. The questionnaire is not primarily a compliance exercise from the client's side; it is a selection and comparison tool.

**Why it's NET-NEW vs. C214–C291:**
- Prior findings addressed institutional questionnaires as accountability triggers or as de facto standard-setting mechanisms
- This finding identifies the specific competitive intelligence function as creating a documentation paradox: a response written without underlying architecture is worse than no response because it creates commercial representations the boutique cannot support with evidence
- The paradox: disclosure is required for compliance, but disclosure without architecture creates competitive exposure
- **Distinct mechanism:** competitive intelligence instrument vs. accountability trigger vs. standard-setting displacement

**The specific problem:** The MD must produce a response that simultaneously satisfies compliance obligations, reflects accurately what the firm has, and positions the firm competitively against peers — with no framework for managing these three requirements simultaneously.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your institutional client is using the questionnaire to compare you against your peers. A response without architecture is worse than no response."

### Finding 02: Agentic AI Portfolio Execution Creates Continuous Authorization Exposure Structurally Different from Generative AI Documentation

**What it is:** Agentic AI — systems that execute autonomously without discrete human authorization moments — is arriving in wealth management before any governance framework addresses it. Portfolio rebalancing, compliance monitoring, reporting, client communication: AI that acts, not just advises. Traditional AI governance documentation covers tools selected. Agentic AI selects its own actions continuously.

**Why it's NET-NEW vs. C214–C291:**
- Prior findings addressed AI tool documentation, AI usage documentation, and AI model version staleness
- Agentic AI creates a fundamentally different accountability question: not "did we use AI?" but "did we authorize what AI did autonomously, continuously, and can we demonstrate that authorization?"
- The Geneva-specific dimension: FINMA Guidance 08/2024 was written for generative AI, not agentic AI. No proportionate Geneva boutique methodology exists for agentic AI governance.
- **Distinct mechanism:** continuous autonomous execution vs. discrete AI event documentation

**The specific problem:** Every moment an agentic system operates without documented authorization is an undocumented decision. The documentation requirements for agentic AI are architecturally different from generative AI governance documentation. Boutique WMs have no Geneva-calibrated methodology for determining what continuous authorization documentation looks like.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Agentic AI is arriving before your governance architecture exists to receive it. The question is not did you use AI. The question is did you authorize what AI did autonomously."

### Finding 03: nFADP Art.21 Automated Decision Obligations Are Present-Tense and Operate Independently of Firm Awareness, Creating Compound Disclosure Requirements with FINMA Circular 2016/01

**What it is:** nFADP Article 21 gives clients the right to be informed when a significant decision is made by automated processing and to request human review. AI-assisted portfolio recommendations may constitute significant automated decisions under Art.21. Simultaneously, FINMA Circular 2016/01 requires disclosure of significant changes in portfolio management approach. The same AI-assisted decision may trigger both obligations simultaneously, creating compound disclosure requirements that no Geneva boutique has a response methodology for.

**Why it's NET-NEW vs. C214–C291:**
- Prior findings addressed nFADP Art.63 criminal liability, Art.8 profiling notifications, and Art.15 AI inference accuracy
- Art.21 automated decision obligations have not been named as a specific present-tense compound disclosure problem for Geneva boutique WMs
- The compound problem with Circular 2016/01 is structurally different from individual framework obligations because it requires integrated disclosure methodology satisfying two authorities with different frames
- **Distinct mechanism:** Art.21 + Circular 2016/01 compound disclosure requirement vs. individual framework obligations

**The specific problem:** Boutique WMs have not issued Art.21 notices to clients, have no Art.21 request response protocol, and have no methodology for determining when AI-assisted decisions trigger Circular 2016/01 disclosure. The obligations exist now. The discharge mechanism does not exist at boutique scale.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Article 21 is a present-tense obligation that no Geneva boutique is addressing. It exists regardless of whether you have thought about it. And the disclosure you may need to make under FINMA rules may be the same disclosure required under nFADP — with no integrated methodology for doing both."

### Finding 04: Swiss Banking Ombudsman Jurisdiction Ambiguity for AI-Adjacent Disputes Creates Structural Uncertainty About Where Accountability Lands

**What it is:** When a dispute arises involving AI-assisted wealth management decisions — a client claims an AI-driven recommendation caused harm, or an institutional client challenges an AI methodology response — the jurisdictional path is unclear. Does the dispute go to the Banking Ombudsman (FINMA-supervised), to courts, to SRO arbitration, or to some combination? No Geneva authority has published guidance on which venue has jurisdiction for AI-adjacent WM disputes.

**Why it's NET-NEW vs. C214–C291:**
- Prior findings addressed carrier attestation, institutional questionnaires, FINMA guidance, SRO silence, and nFADP obligations as separate accountability tracks
- The jurisdictional ambiguity for AI-adjacent disputes is a structurally distinct problem: it determines which accountability venue applies, what evidence standard governs, and what the MD's procedural position is
- This finding identifies the specific mechanism: AI-adjacent disputes fall into a jurisdictional gap where no authority has claimed explicit jurisdiction or published guidance on applicable standards
- **Distinct mechanism:** jurisdictional ambiguity vs. framework-specific obligations

**The specific problem:** The MD cannot know, in advance of a dispute, which venue will govern. The evidence standards differ by venue. The MD's documentation must be defensible across multiple possible venues simultaneously — without knowing which venue will apply.

**Ready-to-use language:** "If something goes wrong with an AI-assisted decision and the client disputes it, nobody has told you which door to walk through. The Banking Ombudsman? Court? SRO? The evidence standard is different in each. Your documentation has to work across all of them."

### Finding 05: Carrier Third-Generation Attestation Creates Evidence Standard That Existing AI Governance Documentation Does Not Address, Specifically at the Intersection of Portfolio Construction AI and Client-Facing Output

**What it is:** Third-generation carrier forms ask for evidence of specific AI governance decisions made — not just policy documents. Carriers probe: what three specific AI-assisted decisions did the firm make, what review preceded each, what evidence did the MD personally review. The evidence standard is specifically about portfolio construction AI and client-facing output, not about general AI tool inventory.

**Why it's NET-NEW vs. C214–C291:**
- Prior findings addressed carrier attestation as personal liability instrument, pre-signing review absence, and evidence requirements broadly
- This finding isolates the specific evidence gap: portfolio construction AI is the most consequential AI decision a WM firm makes, but existing AI governance documentation covers tool inventory, not the specific evidence of human review and authorization preceding AI-assisted portfolio decisions
- The evidence standard requires documented human authorization moments that agentic AI and continuous AI systems specifically do not create
- **Distinct mechanism:** evidence of specific portfolio construction decisions vs. general AI tool documentation

**The specific problem:** The boutique WM that has documented its AI tools does not have the evidence the third-generation form requires. The evidence required is: documented human review of specific AI-assisted portfolio construction decisions, not tool inventory.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your carrier isn't asking if you have an AI policy anymore. They're asking what you did, specifically, when AI made a decision about someone's portfolio. And they want evidence you reviewed it. Tool documentation doesn't answer that question."

### Finding 06: Geneva Boutique WM MD Personal Liability Exposure Under nFADP Art.63 Operates on a Different Evidentiary Standard Than Administrative Fines, Creating Structurally Different Defense Requirements

**What it is:** nFADP Article 63 creates personal criminal liability for data protection violations. This is structurally different from administrative fines in three ways: (1) different evidentiary standard — criminal beyond reasonable doubt vs. administrative preponderance; (2) different enforcement logic — prosecutor's discretion vs. regulatory agency action; (3) different consequences — personal criminal record vs. financial penalty. The boutique WM MD is exposed to criminal liability under Art.63 for AI tool deployment decisions, but existing AI governance documentation and carrier pre-signing review are designed for administrative/regulatory exposure, not criminal defense.

**Why it's NET-NEW vs. C214–C291:**
- Prior findings named Art.63 criminal liability as a personal exposure for the MD
- This finding identifies that the defense requirements for criminal exposure are architecturally different from those for administrative/regulatory exposure — existing pre-signing review and AI governance documentation are insufficient for criminal defense because they were not designed for criminal evidentiary standards
- The boutique WM MD may have documentation that satisfies carrier attestation and FINMA expectations while being inadequate for Art.63 criminal defense
- **Distinct mechanism:** criminal defense documentation requirements vs. administrative compliance documentation

**The specific problem:** The MD is building documentation against administrative/regulatory standards while being personally exposed under criminal standards. The documentation architecture required to defend an Art.63 criminal charge is different from the documentation architecture required to satisfy carrier attestation.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The carrier asks for evidence of adequate controls. The criminal standard is different. What protects you from a regulatory question may not protect you from a criminal question. And your name is on the door for both."
