## Switzerland Regulatory Architecture for AI in Wealth Management

The Geneva boutique wealth management firm operates in a multi-layered regulatory environment where AI governance obligations arise from at least five distinct frameworks simultaneously, none of which provides a unified or proportionate implementation pathway for boutique-scale operations.

---

## Layer 1: FINMA Supervision

### FINMA Guidance 08/2024 (Operative Since 2024)
- **What it says:** Financial institutions using AI systems must ensure adequate governance, risk management, and transparency. AI systems must not produce discriminatory outcomes. Human oversight must be maintained.
- **What it does not say:** Proportionate implementation methodology for boutique WMs. Specific documentation requirements. What "adequate" means at 50–200 employee scale.
- **Status:** Operative. FINMA has not issued boutique-specific implementation guidance.
- **Enforcement posture:** Principles-based. Supervision through existing prudential channels.

### FINMA Circular 2016/01 (Information Obligations)
- **AI-adjacent relevance:** Disclosure of significant changes to portfolio management approach. AI-assisted portfolio construction may constitute a significant change requiring client disclosure.
- **Implementation gap:** No methodology exists for determining when AI-assisted decisions trigger Circular 2016/01 disclosure obligations at boutique WM scale.
- **The specific problem:** The obligation to disclose "significant changes" in approach is triggered by AI adoption. But no Geneva boutique has a documented methodology for determining disclosure scope, timing, or form for AI-assisted portfolio decisions.

### Supervisory Expectations
- FINMA's supervisory approach to AI is evolving through examination practice, not published doctrine.
- Third-generation carrier attestation questions are informed by FINMA supervisory expectations — carrier forms reflect what FINMA is asking carriers to probe.
- Boutique WMs cannot observe FINMA supervisory expectations directly. They must infer them through carrier form evolution and institutional client questionnaire patterns.

---

## Layer 2: SRO Frameworks

### SRO-Specific Obligations
- Asset management firms in Switzerland are supervised through SROs (Self-Regulatory Organizations) under FINMA oversight.
- SROs have not issued AI-specific guidance for wealth management members.
- The SRO guidance vacuum is structural: SRO guidance formation requires consensus among members, and the AI governance question has not yet crystallized sufficient member consensus for published guidance.

### The SRO Silence as Operative Condition
- SRO silence does not mean AI obligations do not apply.
- Boutique WMs are subject to general fiduciary and suitability obligations that AI governance affects.
- The MD cannot use SRO silence as a safe harbor. SRO silence means the MD must make their own proportionality determinations without institutional guidance.
- This shifts accountability to the individual MD more than in any prior regulatory period.

---

## Layer 3: nFADP (Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection)

### Article 63 — Criminal Liability (Personal)
- **What it says:** Criminal penalties for violations of data protection obligations, including obligations relating to personal data processing.
- **Why it matters for boutique WM:** The MD is personally accountable for AI tool deployment decisions involving client personal data. Vendor contract liability disclaimers do not eliminate the MD's personal exposure.
- **Key distinction:** Art.63 creates personal criminal liability that is structurally separate from administrative fines. Different evidentiary standard. Different enforcement logic. Different consequences.
- **Status:** Operative. Unnamed in most boutique WM AI governance documentation.

### Article 21 — Automated Decision Rights
- **What it says:** Data subjects (clients) have the right to:
  1. Be informed when a significant decision is made solely by automated processing
  2. Request human review of the automated decision
  3. Express their point of view
- **Why it matters for boutique WM:** AI-assisted portfolio recommendations may constitute "significant decisions" under Art.21. Every client-facing AI-assisted report potentially triggers disclosure obligations.
- **Key implementation gap:** No Geneva boutique has issued Art.21 notices to clients. No Art.21 request response protocol exists at boutique scale.
- **The compound problem:** Art.21 obligations and FINMA Circular 2016/01 disclosure obligations may be simultaneously triggered by the same AI-assisted decision, creating compound disclosure requirements with no integrated response methodology.

### Article 8 — Profiling Notifications
- **What it says:** Data subjects must be informed when profiling is conducted.
- **Why it matters for boutique WM:** AI-assisted portfolio construction constitutes profiling — analyzing client financial data to generate investment recommendations.
- **Key implementation gap:** Boutique WMs have not issued profiling notifications. The legal basis for AI-inferred client categorizations in portfolio construction is unestablished.

### Article 6 — Lawful Basis for Processing
- **What it says:** Processing must have a valid lawful basis (consent, legitimate interest, contract performance, legal obligation).
- **Why it matters for boutique WM:** Client financial data processed through AI tools requires documented lawful basis. Vendor terms may have mutated the original authorization scope without the firm's awareness.

### FDPIC Enforcement Posture (May 2025)
- The Federal Data Protection Inspectorate shifted from guidance-first to enforcement-live in May 2025.
- Existing nFADP obligations apply directly to AI processing. No regulatory gap required.
- Enforcement is possible now, not only prospectively.

## Layer 4: Carrier Underwriting Standards

### Carrier Attestation Evolution
- **First-generation:** Simple yes/no on whether AI is used.
- **Second-generation:** Policy acknowledgment requiring documentation of AI governance policy.
- **Third-generation (current):** Specific evidence of AI governance decisions made. Carriers ask what three specific AI-assisted decisions the firm made, what review preceded each, what evidence the MD reviewed.
- **What this means for boutique WM:** The attestation is no longer a policy acknowledgment. It is a performance evidence request. The MD must produce evidence of actual governance decisions, not just a policy document.

### The Geneva-Specific Dimension
- Swiss carrier forms reflect Swiss regulatory concepts (FINMA supervision, SRO frameworks, nFADP obligations).
- Non-Swiss carrier forms do not address this specific regulatory compound.
- Geneva boutique WMs with international institutional clients may face both Swiss and international carrier/institutional expectations simultaneously.

### The Pre-Signing Review Gap
- Carrier forms are written by carrier legal teams for underwriting risk evaluation.
- No Geneva service offers pre-signing review of what the MD's personal signature commits them to.
- The MD signs without independent analysis of the personal liability implications.

---

## Layer 5: Institutional Client AI Governance Standards

### Institutional DDQ as Operative Standard-Setter
- Institutional clients (pension funds, family offices, institutional investors) are setting AI governance expectations through their questionnaires.
- Through the SRO guidance vacuum, institutional client questionnaires function as de facto operative standards.
- A boutique WM's institutional clients are forming the market's expectations of what "adequate AI governance" means for Geneva boutiques.

### The Competitive Intelligence Function
- Institutional clients use questionnaire responses for competitive comparison across their manager roster.
- A boutique WM's response is compared against responses from other boutiques and against institutional expectations calibrated through institutional circuit participation.
- The MD's response creates commercial representations that clients may rely on.

---

## The Compound Compliance Problem

### What No Swiss Authority Has Done

No Swiss authority has published:
1. A proportionate AI governance methodology for Geneva boutique WMs (50–200 employees)
2. A specific interpretation of "adequate AI governance controls" for boutique WM carrier attestation
3. An Art.21 automated decision implementation protocol for AI-assisted portfolio management
4. A documentation standard for AI usage in active client mandates that satisfies FINMA, SRO, nFADP, and institutional client expectations simultaneously
5. A pre-signing review framework for carrier attestation at boutique WM scale

### The Boutique WM Reality

The MD must navigate all five framework layers simultaneously, with no Geneva-calibrated integration methodology, at personal liability risk, while institutional clients and carriers are asking concrete questions on known calendar schedules.
