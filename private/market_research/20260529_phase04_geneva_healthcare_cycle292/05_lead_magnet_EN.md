### What You Will Learn in 90 Minutes

**1. The carrier attestation is your immediate problem — not your future problem.**

Your medical malpractice carrier has sent you an AI governance questionnaire. You signed it because renewal was due. What you signed is a personal attestation committing you to "adequate AI governance controls" with no Geneva-calibrated definition of what adequate means for your practice.

I will show you what the attestation actually commits you to, what your existing coverage does and doesn't address, and what the gap looks like before something goes wrong.

**2. Patient-generated AI content is entering your medical records — and you have no documentation protocol for it.**

Your patient arrives with consumer AI-generated health analysis. They show you what their symptom checker said about their scan. You note it in the record. That note is now under your professional authority. If the AI was wrong and your patient suffered, that note is your liability — not the consumer app's.

I will show you what the documentation architecture looks like for this category, what it protects, and why the absence of a protocol is a present-tense exposure.

**3. Your colleague's AI second opinion creates accountability in your record.**

A consulting colleague uses AI for a second opinion on one of your patients. Their AI-generated analysis goes into your patient's record — your record. You are accountable for content generated by a system you did not deploy, did not configure, and cannot independently verify.

No Geneva protocol exists for this. I will show you what it should look like and why the absence matters more than most physicians realize.

**4. Vendor terms are creating liability you did not negotiate.**

Your billing software updated. You clicked through terms to access a feature you needed. The AI features that activated cannot be technically disabled without losing core functionality. The data usage provisions for model training were buried in settings you never reviewed.

I will show you what your vendor terms are actually committing you to, which provisions create exposure, and what proportionate evaluation looks like at practice scale.

**5. The AI authorship question will determine your evidentiary position when something goes wrong.**

Every medical record you have written for the past two years may contain AI-generated content. You do not know which ones. When a record is challenged — in liability proceedings, in a carrier dispute, in a peer review — you cannot explain what AI contributed to your clinical judgment.

I will show you why retroactive reconstruction is structurally impossible, what the present-tense documentation requirement looks like, and why the window is closing faster than most physicians understand.

**6. The training documentation void is a specific and nameable exposure.**

Your vendor provided training on AI features. You completed it. This training does not document what FMH human oversight requires — it documents feature familiarity. The gap is unnamed, unaddressed, and creating an exposure the physician cannot see.

I will show you what AI competency documentation actually needs to contain for a Geneva independent practice, and why vendor training certificates do not close the requirement.
