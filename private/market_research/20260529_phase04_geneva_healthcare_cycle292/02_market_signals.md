# Market Signals: Geneva Healthcare AI Accountability
## Phase 04 C292 — Geneva Independent Practice

---

## Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

### 1. Carrier Attestation Wave Arriving with Healthcare Delay

**Signal:** Medical malpractice carriers are beginning to deploy AI attestation questionnaires in renewal processes, 6–12 months after the finance and legal sectors received similar instruments.

**What's happening:** Independent practices receiving carrier AI questionnaires for the first time. The questionnaires are designed by underwriting teams, not by medical professional liability specialists. The MD signs personally, committing to "adequate AI governance controls" with no Geneva-calibrated definition of what adequate means for a 2–10 physician practice.

**The failure:** No pre-signing review service exists in the Geneva market for medical practitioners. The attorney market has the same gap (pre-signing review absent) but the medical market has zero coverage for this specific product. Physicians are signing personal attestation instruments without understanding what they are committing to or whether their existing coverage responds.

**Complaint as stated:** "My carrier sent me a form asking about AI. I don't know what they're actually asking for. I signed it because renewal was due."

### 2. Patient-Generated AI Content Entering Medical Records

**Signal:** Growing occurrence of patients arriving at appointments with consumer AI-generated health analyses — symptom checkers, diagnostic support tools, imaging interpretations — incorporated into clinical conversations and eventually into formal medical records.

**What's happening:** The physician receives output from an AI system they did not deploy, cannot evaluate for accuracy, and must incorporate into a medical record under their professional authority. No documentation standard exists for this category. The record shows physician analysis with no marker distinguishing patient-arrived AI content from physician-generated clinical judgment.

**The failure:** Evidentiary gap — physician accountable for content in their record they did not generate. Retroactive reconstruction impossible: physician cannot explain, when challenged, what AI contributed to the record because no documentation of AI's role was made at the time.

**Complaint as stated:** "My patient showed me what their AI app said about their scan. I noted it in the record. If that AI was wrong and my patient suffered — whose fault is that? Mine, because it's in my record."

### 3. AI-Assisted Second Opinion Accountability Gap

**Signal:** Consulting colleague using AI for second opinion creates output that enters the original physician's patient record. The original physician is accountable for AI-generated content from a system they did not deploy, did not configure, and may not have documentation about.

**What's happening:** Informal second opinion consultation between independent physicians has always existed. Now this consultation happens with AI tools. The AI-assisted second opinion is documented in the original patient's record. The original physician's record contains output from an AI system selected and configured by a colleague.

**The failure:** No Geneva protocol exists for documenting AI-assisted second opinion consultation. The accountability chain — original physician → consulting colleague → colleague's AI tool → output in original record — is entirely ungoverned. The original physician bears responsibility for AI content they did not author and cannot independently verify.

**Complaint as stated:** "A colleague showed me what their AI suggested for a second opinion on my patient. I used it. It's now in the record. If the AI was wrong — that was my colleague's AI, not mine."

### 4. Vendor Contract Signature as Unseen Liability Acceptance

**Signal:** Independent practices accepting vendor terms through click-through as standard operational behavior, unaware that these terms may create obligations incompatible with FMH physician accountability requirements.

**What's happening:** Software updates activate AI features that cannot be technically disabled without losing core functionality. Data usage provisions for model training are buried in settings the practice never reviewed. By accepting terms to access functionality the practice needs, the physician is accepting conditions that may conflict with professional accountability architecture.

**The failure:** No Geneva-calibrated method exists for evaluating vendor AI terms against FMH physician accountability obligations. The physician lacks the contractual sophistication and the AI governance context to know which provisions create exposure. The technical opt-out impossibility means the choice is functionality or compliance — and most choose functionality.

**Complaint as stated:** "I clicked through terms for my billing software to get a feature I needed. I have no idea what I agreed to. Now my billing software trains models on my patient data and I can't turn that off."

### 5. Medical Record AI Authorship Ambiguity

**Signal:** Medical records authored under physician's name with zero documentation of AI contribution. When records are challenged — in liability proceedings, peer review, carrier disputes — the physician cannot explain what AI did in the record creation process.

**What's happening:** Two years of AI-assisted record keeping has produced authoritative records that are mixtures of physician judgment and AI generation. The physician signed each record. The records are authoritative. But the physician cannot reconstruct which portions came from AI, what the AI's reasoning was, or whether AI influenced a clinical decision they believed was entirely their own.

**The failure:** Evidentiary architecture does not exist for documenting AI's role in medical record creation. The physician's signature says "this is my clinical judgment" when in fact portions of the record are AI-generated output that the physician reviewed and adopted without independent verification. The gap only becomes visible when an incident triggers a challenge.

**Complaint as stated:** "Every record I've written for the past two years may have AI content in it. I don't know which ones. If something goes wrong and someone asks how I made a specific decision — I can't tell them whether the AI affected that decision."

### 6. AI Training Documentation Void

**Signal:** No Geneva market standard for what AI competency documentation a physician must maintain. Most independent practices maintain zero AI training documentation. FMH has issued no guidance defining competency documentation requirements for AI-assisted clinical practice.

**What's happening:** Vendor training documentation (platform tutorials, feature walkthroughs) does not map to FMH oversight requirements. Physicians have completed vendor training programs that teach them to use AI features but do not document clinical oversight competency or AI limitations awareness. The training exists but does not satisfy any documented accountability requirement.

**The failure:** The gap between vendor training completion and FMH human oversight accountability is unnamed and unaddressed. No Geneva service has produced AI competency documentation proportionate to independent practice scale. Physicians do not know what they should be documenting about their own AI competency.

**Complaint as stated:** "I did the training for my AI scribe. That doesn't mean I know what I'm supposed to document about my AI competency. There's no standard that tells me."

### 7. Swiss-Hosted Data Confusion

**Signal:** "Swiss-hosted" used as compliance signal by independent physicians selecting AI tools, while the actual data processing chain involves US-based API endpoints that are not covered by Swiss hosting declarations.

**What's happening:** A tool with Swiss data storage but US-based API processing creates FMH accountability exposure regardless of storage location. "Swiss-hosted" label does not resolve the processing-location compliance question. Physicians selecting tools on the basis of Swiss hosting may be making compliance decisions based on incomplete information.

**The failure:** Data residency (storage location) and data processing location (where computational work happens) are structurally different questions being answered as the same question. No Geneva market service offers proportionate analysis of AI tool data flows for independent practices. Physicians are making storage-location decisions that do not address processing-location obligations.

**Complaint as stated:** "My software is Swiss-hosted so I thought patient data stayed in Switzerland. But the AI processing happens in the US. Does that matter? Nobody's told me."

### 8. GHF 2026 Awareness Amplification Without Operational Output

**Signal:** Geneva Health Forum 2026 generated awareness of AI accountability questions in the Geneva healthcare community without producing operational guidance, methodology, or proportionate tools for independent practices.

**What's happening:** GHF 2026 elevated AI governance as a topic in Geneva healthcare discourse. Institutional participants (HUG, CHUV, cantonal health authorities) engaged substantively. Independent practitioners attended sessions, recognized the problems, and found no follow-on resources calibrated to their scale. The awareness arrived; the operational response infrastructure did not.

**The failure:** Awareness without methodology creates a specific psychological condition — the physician knows there is a problem but has no path to addressing it. The gap between awareness and action produces compliance paralysis rather than compliance resolution. No Geneva service has used the GHF 2026 moment to build practice-calibrated AI governance tools.

**Complaint as stated:** "I went to a GHF session on AI governance. I understood the problem. I have no idea what to do about it for my practice. Nothing calibrated for us came out of it."

## Market Failure Observations

**The three-consent-architecture gap:** Informed consent for treatment, informed consent for data processing under nFADP, and AI-specific consent disclosure are treated as a single category when they require separate architecture. No Geneva service has named this gap or built a proportionate solution for independent practices.

**FMH human oversight — no Tuesday translation:** The FMH principle of human oversight over AI in clinical practice is stated but has no Tuesday translation — no operational guidance on what Wednesday looks like for an independent physician. The principle provides no diagnostic criteria, no documentation requirement, no review frequency recommendation.

**Compliance theater at small-practice scale:** The same person holds user, compliance officer, and billing roles simultaneously. Genuine oversight architecture is structurally impossible with current guidance. The result is appearance of compliance without substance.
