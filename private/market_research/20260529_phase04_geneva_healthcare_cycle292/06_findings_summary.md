### Finding 01: FMH Human Oversight — No Tuesday Translation (Structural)

**The finding:** FMH human oversight principle has no operational Tuesday translation for independent practices. The principle exists as professional accountability obligation without defining what Wednesday looks like.

**Distinct from prior findings:** All prior Phase 04 findings addressed specific AI governance gaps (tool inventory, Swissdamed classification, carrier attestation, medical record authorship). This finding isolates the specific mechanism — principle without operational endpoint — as structurally different from the documented gaps. Prior findings named what was missing; this finding names why the principle cannot function as governance infrastructure at small-practice scale.

**The mechanism:** FMH stated human oversight as principle. No Geneva authority has published what human oversight documentation consists of, what review frequency is adequate, what competency the physician must demonstrate. The same person holds user/compliance/billing roles simultaneously — genuine oversight architecture is structurally impossible with current guidance. The result is compliance theater: appearance of oversight without substance.

**Market implication:** Any service that produces the Tuesday translation for FMH human oversight — specific documentation requirements, review frequency, competency evidence — captures the primary structural gap in Geneva medical AI governance. This is the foundational product requirement no one has built.

### Finding 02: Carrier Attestation as Medical Practice Personal Liability Instrument (Unnamed Exposure)

**The finding:** Medical malpractice carrier AI attestation questionnaires are designed by underwriting teams to evaluate risk, not by medical liability specialists to document professional accountability. The MD signs personally committing to undefined "adequate AI governance controls." No Geneva market service offers pre-signing review for medical practitioners. The attestation creates personal liability exposure without a discharge pathway.

**Distinct from prior findings:** C290 Phase 04 named carrier attestation as de facto operative standard. C291 Phase 04 named license-centric framing distinct from fine-centric. This finding isolates the specific mechanism: the attestation is not just a market standard, it is a personal liability instrument with no pre-signing review pathway in the Geneva medical market — structurally identical to the legal market gap but with zero coverage for the medical vertical. The finding names the specific exposure creation mechanism (underwriting-design vs medical-liability-design) not just the market absence.

**The mechanism:** Carrier questionnaire → MD personal signature → undefined adequacy standard → no Geneva-calibrated definition available → MD personally committed to a standard they cannot evaluate. Coverage question (what does policy respond to) and attestation adequacy question (what satisfies the form) are answered separately — most physicians answer both as one.

**Market implication:** Pre-signing carrier attestation review is the highest-need service for Geneva independent practices. The conversion trigger is immediate — physicians are receiving these instruments now. The service is unnamed in the medical market, identical in structure to the legal market gap that DigitalCoa.ch has already identified.

### Finding 03: Patient-Generated AI Content as Discrete Medical Record Accountability Category (Named Exposure)

**The finding:** Patient-initiated consumer AI-generated health content entering medical records is a named, present-tense accountability category that no Geneva protocol has addressed. The physician is accountable for AI content in their record they did not generate, deploy, or configure. The documentation standard for this category does not exist.

**Distinct from prior findings:** C290 Phase 04 named patient-initiated AI content as structural problem. C287 Phase 04 named incremental AI adoption accumulation. C279 Phase 04 named Swissdamed classification inaccessibility. This finding isolates the specific category: patient-generated AI content (not physician-deployed AI, not vendor-deployed AI) entering the medical record through the patient as vector, creating a three-party accountability chain (patient → physician record → physician liability) that is not governed by any existing documentation standard.

**The mechanism:** Patient brings consumer AI output → physician reviews and incorporates into clinical conversation → note enters medical record under physician's professional authority → if AI was wrong, physician bears liability for AI content they did not generate or evaluate. The record shows physician judgment. The physician cannot demonstrate what AI contributed because no documentation of AI's role was made.

**Market implication:** Medical record AI authorship architecture — the documentation system that allows the physician to distinguish what they independently judged from what AI contributed — is the primary ongoing service need. This is not a one-time gap; every patient interaction potentially generates new content in this category.

### Finding 04: AI-Assisted Second Opinion Derivative Accountability Chain (Unnamed Product Category)

**The finding:** The accountability chain for AI-assisted second opinion consultation between independent physicians is entirely ungoverned. A consulting colleague uses AI for second opinion review; AI-generated output enters the original physician's patient record; the original physician is accountable for content from a system they did not deploy. No Geneva protocol exists for this configuration.

**Distinct from prior findings:** All prior Phase 04 findings addressed the primary physician's own AI use. This finding addresses derivative accountability — physician A accountable for AI output from physician B's independent AI deployment. The finding isolates a specific three-party chain: original physician → consulting colleague → colleague's AI tool → output in original record. Each party's documentation obligation is undefined. The original physician bears responsibility for work product they cannot evaluate.

**The mechanism:** Informal second opinion consultation is standard medical practice. AI tools are now involved in this consultation without any modification to the accountability architecture. The consulting colleague's AI system is not the original physician's AI system — but the output is in the original physician's record under their authority. The chain creates accountability without corresponding verification rights.

**Market implication:** This is a standalone product category. A Geneva-specific protocol for AI-assisted second opinion documentation — defining what the consulting physician must document about their AI use, what the original physician must verify, and how the chain is reflected in records — is a named service offering that does not exist anywhere in the market.

### Finding 05: Three-Consent Architecture Structural Gap as Integrated Compliance Problem (Unnamed Market Gap)

**The finding:** Informed consent for treatment, informed consent for data processing under nFADP, and AI-specific consent disclosure are structurally different obligations being treated as a single consent category. No Geneva consent form integrates all three. The structural gap means the physician cannot demonstrate that the patient understood AI's role in their care when an AI-assisted decision is challenged.

**Distinct from prior findings:** C291 Phase 04 named three-consent-architecture as market gap. C279 Phase 04 named consent form obsolescence for AI-assisted documentation. This finding isolates the specific structural mechanism: the three consents are not just missing individually — they are structurally incompatible in a single form architecture. Treatment consent requires disclosure of clinical procedure and risk. nFADP consent requires disclosure of data processing rights and purposes. AI-specific consent requires disclosure of AI's role, limitations, and what happens when AI is wrong. A single consent form cannot satisfy all three simultaneously — they require separate architecture with coherent integration.

**The mechanism:** The physician uses one consent form for all patient interactions. The form covers treatment consent. It does not cover AI's role in clinical decisions. When a patient challenges an AI-assisted decision, the physician cannot produce evidence that the patient understood AI's involvement — because AI-specific disclosure was never included in the consent architecture.

**Market implication:** Three-consent integration service — designing consent architecture that handles all three obligations as separate systems with documented coherence — is a named service category for Geneva healthcare practices. This is distinct from general consent form templates and from nFADP consent frameworks that treat all consent as single category.

### Finding 06: Geneva Institutional Intelligence Structural Exclusion of Independent Practices (Formation Mechanism)

**The finding:** Institutional decisions at HUG/CHUV are forming effective standards for AI governance in Geneva healthcare without methodology transmission to independent practices. The standards form in governed rooms independent physicians do not access. The operative standard for AI governance is being set by institutional participants who have resources independent practices do not have — and the outcome of that standard-setting process applies to independent practices without their participation.

**Distinct from prior findings:** C290 Phase 04 named institutional intelligence inaccessibility as structural positioning disadvantage. C286 Phase 04 named Geneva cantonal institutional caution signal. C279 Phase 04 named institutional ecosystem formation operating faster than formal regulatory process. This finding isolates the specific mechanism: institutional decisions at HUG/CHUV create precedent that carriers, patients, and legal challengers can reference as operative standard — without the precedent being published, transmitted, or accessible to independent practices building their own governance architecture. The formation mechanism is structural exclusion, not merely information gap.

**The mechanism:** HUG/CHUV have governance committees, legal resources, compliance infrastructure that independent practices do not. When these institutions make AI governance decisions, those decisions shape the operative standard. The independent practice, building governance in isolation, may be building against standards set by institutional decisions they never observed.

**Market implication:** The advisory practice sustained participation in Geneva institutional ecosystem is the structural moat. Independent practices have no access to the standard-formation process. Advisory intelligence derived from institutional participation is not replicable through public sources or methodology documents — it is produced by sustained presence in the rooms where standards form.
