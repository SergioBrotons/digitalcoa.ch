# ICP Language: Geneva Healthcare AI Accountability
## Phase 04 C292 — Geneva Independent Practice

---

## How the ICP Talks About AI Problems

### Primary Language Register: Accountability Before Clinical Utility

Geneva independent practitioners do not frame AI as an efficiency opportunity. They frame it as a liability question they did not choose to answer. The language reflects this:

**"I didn't decide to use this — it came with the software."**
The AI arrived through an update, a staff activation, an embedded feature in the billing system they already owned. No governance decision preceded the AI's presence in the practice. The physician now carries accountability for a tool they did not consciously deploy. This language appears consistently across independent practice conversations and distinguishes the ICP from institutional healthcare actors who made deliberate procurement decisions.

**"If something goes wrong, I'm the one whose name is on the record."**
Professional accountability framing dominates. The physician understands they sign the record, own the decision, carry the liability. They do not have a clear mental model of how AI changes this accountability structure, only a strong intuition that it does change it in ways that are not resolved by standard medical malpractice frameworks.

**"My patient brought AI-generated analysis from home. It's now in their file."**
Patient-initiated AI content entering medical records is a new category the ICP is actively navigating. The patient arrives with consumer AI health analysis. The physician must decide whether to incorporate it, how to document it, and who bears accountability for AI content they did not generate or deploy.

**"I've been signing things I haven't read."**
Vendor terms, software updates, platform agreements — accepted through click-through without evaluation. The ICP recognizes this as a pattern and feels exposed by it, but lacks an alternative that preserves the functionality they need. The language reflects pragmatic resignation rather than active consent.

**"What would 'adequate' even mean for a two-person practice?"**
Proportionality question. The ICP understands that frameworks exist (FMH guidance, Swissdamed thresholds, carrier questionnaires) but cannot translate them into operational decisions at their scale. They suspect the frameworks were not designed for their context.

**"I don't know what my carrier actually covers if an AI-assisted decision goes wrong."**
Insurance clarity question. The ICP has carrier AI attestation questionnaires but cannot connect the attestation to actual coverage scope. Different instruments, different timelines, different answers.

**"There's no one to ask. The FMH doesn't have an answer for this. My cantonal authority hasn't said anything."**
Guidance vacuum recognition. The ICP has looked for Geneva-specific or canton-specific guidance and found none. The FMH principle exists but has no operational endpoint. This absence is experienced as a structural condition, not a temporary gap.

**"My colleague's AI second opinion went into my patient's record. That AI wasn't my AI."**
Derivative accountability problem. A consulting colleague uses AI for a second opinion. The AI-generated input enters the original physician's patient record. The original physician is accountable for content in their record generated by a system they did not deploy or oversee.

### Secondary Language Patterns

**"The software updated and I didn't notice."**
Silent update problem. The ICP understands at some level that AI models update silently but has no system for tracking which version produced which output. Medical records carry the output, not the version metadata.

**"Swiss-hosted sounds safe but I don't actually know what that means for my patient data."**
Data flow confusion. "Swiss-hosted" is used as a compliance proxy but the ICP cannot explain what happens to data when a Swiss-hosted AI platform processes it through US-based API endpoints.

**"I want to do the right thing but I don't know what the right thing is."**
Compliance uncertainty without malice. The ICP is not trying to avoid accountability — they are trying to find it. This distinction matters for framing.

## French Register (Registers Parallèles)

**"Je n'ai pas décidé d'utiliser ça — c'est venu avec le logiciel."**
L'IA est arrivée via une mise à jour, une activation par le personnel, une fonction intégrée dans le système de facturation qu'ils possédaient déjà. Aucune décision de gouvernance n'a précédé la présence de l'IA. Le médecin porte désormais la responsabilité d'un outil qu'il n'a pas déployé consciemment.

**"Si quelque chose tourne mal, c'est mon nom sur le dossier."**
Cadre de responsabilité professionnelle dominates. Le médecin comprend qu'il signe le dossier, ownership the decision, porte la responsabilité. Ils n'ont pas de modèle mental clair de comment l'IA change cette structure de responsabilité.

**"Je signe des choses que je n'ai pas lues."**
Terms du fournisseur, mises à jour logicielles, accords de plateforme — acceptés via click-through sans évaluation. Le médecin reconnaît ce pattern et se sent exposé, mais n'a pas d'alternative qui préserve la fonctionnalité dont ils ont besoin.

**"Je ne sais pas ce que mon assureur couvre réellement si une décision assistée par IA tourne mal."**
Question de clarté d'assurance. Le médecin a des questionnaires d'attestation IA mais ne peut pas relier l'attestation à la portée réelle de la couverture.

## Language Notes

- The ICP does not say "AI governance." They say "who's responsible" and "what happens if."
- The word "adequate" appears frequently but without definitional content — it's a placeholder for an undefined standard.
- "Should" language indicates normative expectation the ICP cannot operationalize: "I should have something documented."
- Fear language is personal, not institutional: "my license," "my name," "my record," "my patient."
- The ICP uses "we" (nous) when describing the practice but "je" when describing accountability: collective operation, singular liability.
