# Cross-Vertical Compliance Context — Phase 05 C288
# Regulatory patterns across legal, finance, consulting, and healthcare

## Cross-Vertical Compliance Pattern 1: Multi-Framework Simultaneous Non-Reconciliation

**Pattern:** All four verticals face multiple AI governance frameworks that apply simultaneously and are not designed to be mutually coherent.

**Legal vertical:**
- nFADP Art. 21: obligation to explain AI-processed data to data subjects
- Art. 321 CP: professional secrecy protecting client communications
- Bar association AI guidance: SVA/ORDRE obligations on attorney AI use
- Carrier AI questionnaire: PI insurance attestation requirements
- Geneva court procedural expectations: localized, inconsistent, unpublished

**Finance vertical:**
- nFADP Art. 21: AI-generated investment recommendation subject to data subject explanation
- FINMA supervisory requirements: AI model governance and documentation
- FINMA Circular 2016/1: investment advice documentation standards
- ISSB sustainability AI: AI-generated ESG claims subject to ISSB documentation
- Carrier D&O questionnaire: personal liability attestation requirements

**Consulting vertical:**
- nFADP Art. 21: client data processed with AI tools, explanation obligation
- Professional liability framework: AI-assisted deliverables and warranty obligations
- Client AI governance questionnaires: EU AI Act service provider obligations for EU-connected clients
- Carrier professional liability: AI methodology documentation requirements

**Healthcare vertical:**
- nFADP Art. 21: patient data AI-processed, explanation obligation
- MedDO/GesBG: medical device AI governance requirements
- Art. 40 ZPO: medical liability and documentation standards
- FADP Art. 13: accessible explanation for vulnerable populations
- Carrier malpractice: AI-assisted clinical decision documentation

**Cross-vertical synthesis:** The multi-framework collision is not unique to any vertical — every professional in Geneva faces it. The collision is structurally guaranteed because each framework was designed independently without mutual coherence obligation. Professionals cannot resolve the collision themselves because no Geneva authority has published resolution guidance. The first provider to name the specific collision points and provide operational resolution protocol will capture trust from all four verticals simultaneously.

## Cross-Vertical Compliance Pattern 2: Regulatory Timing Asymmetry

**Pattern:** Regulatory guidance arrives after the accountability exposure has already materialized — the standard arrives after the decision it would have governed.

**Legal:** Bar association AI guidance being drafted now for AI tools deployed 18–36 months ago. The guidance will apply retroactively to decisions already made.

**Finance:** FINMA AI governance expectations forming from examination questions, not published guidance. Compliance officers discovering requirements through examination, not through regulatory publication.

**Consulting:** Client AI questionnaires referencing EU AI Act obligations that Swiss professional bodies have not yet translated into domestic guidance. Professionals implementing obligations before Swiss interpretation exists.

**Healthcare:** Swissmedic AI SaMD guidance in development for clinical AI tools already deployed. Practitioners held to standards their regulators haven't published.

**Cross-vertical synthesis:** The timing asymmetry creates a specific structural problem: professionals are making decisions in a regulatory vacuum that will close around them — and the closure will be retrospective. The only rational response is building evidence architecture that can survive evaluation against standards that don't exist yet — because by the time they exist, the evidence architecture window will have closed. This is the strongest possible argument for immediate action: the window for building contemporaneous records for past decisions is closed, but the window for building evidence architecture for current decisions is open now and closing.

## Cross-Vertical Compliance Pattern 3: Carrier Iteration as Primary Standard-Setting Mechanism

**Pattern:** Carrier AI questionnaires are iterating faster than regulatory guidance and setting the de facto standard that Geneva professionals are being measured against.

**Legal:** Third-generation PI carrier questionnaires now asking for:
- Tool-specific AI configuration documentation
- Model version tracking for AI-assisted matters
- Reassessment protocol for AI governance decisions
- Client communication records for AI disclosure
- Evidence of substantive review for AI-generated content

**Finance:** Fourth-generation carrier questionnaires probing:
- What happens to firm liability position if documented practices fail
- Risk distribution architecture across client contracts, PI coverage, carrier conditions
- Evidence of board-level AI governance oversight
- Personal liability discharge mechanism for compliance officers

**Consulting:** Professional liability carriers beginning to ask:
- AI methodology documentation for deliverables
- Client AI content provenance records
- Configuration knowledge succession protocol

**Healthcare:** Malpractice carriers adding AI-specific questions:
- AI-assisted diagnostic decision documentation
- Clinical AI tool configuration records
- Patient consent architecture for AI-assisted decisions

**Cross-vertical synthesis:** The carrier iteration pattern is producing the fastest-moving standard in Geneva AI governance. Each generation sharpens the questions, narrows the scope, and increases the personal liability implications. Professionals who treat carrier questionnaires as compliance exercises are missing the strategic reality: carrier iteration is standard-setting, and responding to it strategically — not just completing the form — is the only way to influence the standard before it crystallizes around them.

## Cross-Vertical Compliance Pattern 4: Personal Liability Without Discharge Mechanism

**Pattern:** All four verticals have senior professionals with personal AI governance liability that is structurally defined but operationally unresolved — liability exists without a path to discharge it.

**Legal:** Managing partner personal liability for AI governance adequacy under cantonal bar obligations. No published discharge mechanism. No Geneva bar guidance on what "adequate" requires. Signature on carrier attestation personally warrants something undefined.

**Finance:** Compliance officer personal liability for AI approval under FINMA supervisory framework. No published discharge methodology. Approval is personal attestation to something not defined in any external standard.

**Consulting:** Managing director personal liability for AI methodology in client deliverables. No professional body has defined the liability parameters. Engagement letter AI clauses committing to methodology without defined content.

**Healthcare:** Practitioner personal liability for AI-assisted clinical decisions. Medical professional obligations predate AI tools. No published protocol for discharging AI-specific liability. Malpractice carrier attestation personally warrants something undefined.

**Cross-vertical synthesis:** The liability-without-discharge pattern is the highest-stakes shared structural problem across all four verticals. Every senior professional is personally exposed, and none have any operational path to reduce that exposure through documented discharge. This is the primary demand driver that no provider has fully addressed — the professional needs liability discharge architecture, not just compliance documentation.

## Cross-Vertical Compliance Pattern 5: Cross-Border Regulatory Pressure Arriving Through Domestic Channels

**Pattern:** EU AI Act obligations are arriving in Geneva through client questionnaires, counterparty requirements, and carrier iterations — not through Swiss regulatory enforcement.

**Legal:** EU-connected corporate clients sending AI governance questionnaires referencing EU AI Act high-risk system requirements. Swiss law firms receiving these questionnaires with no Swiss domestic interpretation of what EU AI Act compliance requires.

**Finance:** EU institutional investors requiring AI governance documentation that references EU AI Act standards. Swiss asset managers being measured against framework that doesn't formally apply to them.

**Consulting:** EU-based clients requiring EU AI Act service provider compliance confirmation as part of RFP process. Swiss consulting boutiques being asked to warrant EU AI Act compliance without clear jurisdictional guidance.

**Healthcare:** EU healthcare institutions requiring AI documentation from Swiss clinical AI vendors referencing EU AI Act MDR requirements. Swiss practitioners uncertain which requirements apply to their EU-connected practice.

**Cross-vertical synthesis:** The EU AI Act is creating a de facto compliance requirement for Swiss professionals through market mechanisms (client questionnaires, counterparty requirements) before it creates a formal compliance requirement through regulatory channels. The pressure is real and immediate, but the resolution pathway is unclear. Professionals need a specific framework for understanding which EU AI Act obligations apply to their Swiss practice through client/counterparty pressure — and what documentation architecture satisfies those obligations without triggering additional exposure.

## Cross-Vertical Compliance Pattern 6: Regulatory Enforcement Preceding Published Doctrine

**Pattern:** FDPIC and other authorities are conducting enforcement actions and investigations before publishing the doctrine that would govern those actions — creating retrospective standard formation.

**Legal:** FDPIC investigations of AI systems in law firms producing findings without published guidance on what compliant AI governance requires. Law firms under investigation before doctrine exists.

**Finance:** FINMA examination questions probing AI governance adequacy before published standards define what adequate means. Compliance officers being measured against standards they couldn't have known.

**Consulting:** Client AI governance questionnaires creating enforcement-like pressure before any regulatory body has published methodology for compliance. Boutiques being asked to demonstrate compliance with frameworks no one has defined.

**Healthcare:** FDPIC investigations of clinical AI systems proceeding with no published compliance pathway. Practitioners under scrutiny for AI governance practices that authorities haven't defined.

**Cross-vertical synthesis:** The pattern of enforcement preceding doctrine means that professionals who wait for published guidance before building AI governance will be building it in response to enforcement actions — not to prevent them. The strategic opportunity is using enforcement patterns (observable through investigation activity, examination questions, carrier iterations) to anticipate doctrine before it arrives — and building evidence architecture that positions the professional as compliant with the standard before it's published. This is only possible with sustained multi-vertical pattern observation — which is exactly what the synthesis moat provides.
