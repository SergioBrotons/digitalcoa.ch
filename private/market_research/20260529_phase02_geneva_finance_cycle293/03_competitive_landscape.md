# 03 — Competitive Landscape: Geneva Boutique Wealth Management

## Phase 02: Geneva Finance — Cycle 293

---

## Competitive Landscape Overview

The Geneva boutique WM AI governance market is not yet a mature competitive market. The boutique WMs are not being pitched by many credible providers. The competitive landscape is defined by non-market forces: regulatory pressure, institutional client questionnaires, and carrier attestation requirements — none of which offer a choice.

The competitive question for DigitalCoa.ch is not "who else is selling AI governance to Geneva boutiques?" The competitive question is "what are the boutique WMs doing instead of buying a structured solution, and what does that cost them?"

---

## The Five Alternatives the Boutique WM Is Currently Using

### Alternative 1: The Big Law Firm
The Geneva boutique WM calls their existing big law firm relationship for AI governance advice.

**What they get:** Excellent legal analysis written for large financial institutions, at large financial institution billing rates, with no methodology proportionate to boutique scale. The law firm tells them what they need to do under FINMA Guidance 08/2024 and nFADP. The boutique leaves with a list of requirements and no implementation path.

**Why it fails:** The big law firm cannot build the boutique-scale governance architecture. They can describe what FINMA expects. They cannot sit in the room and help the MD decide what the attestation actually means for their specific tool stack.

**The gap DigitalCoa.ch fills:** Proportionate methodology for boutique-scale AI governance architecture, not legal analysis of what the regulation requires.

---

### Alternative 2: The IT Vendor
The boutique WM asks their existing IT or platform vendor whether their AI tools are compliant.

**What they get:** The vendor confirms their tools are compliant. The vendor's confirmation is based on the vendor's interests, not the boutique's obligations. The boutique WM MD now believes they have a compliance response because their vendor told them they are compliant.

**Why it fails:** FINMA Guidance 08/2024 and nFADP obligations attach to the boutique WM as the AI user — not to the vendor as the AI provider. The vendor's attestation of compliance is irrelevant to the boutique's obligations as the deploying firm. The boutique may be signing carrier attestations based on vendor reassurance that doesn't cover their actual exposure.

**The gap DigitalCoa.ch fills:** Independent assessment of the boutique's obligations as an AI user, independent of vendor interests.

---

### Alternative 3: The Industry Association Template
The boutique WM downloads the AMAS or SFAMA AI governance template or guidance.

**What they get:** A generic AI governance policy template that describes what a financial institution's AI governance should look like. The boutique fills in the blanks. The boutique now has a document called an AI governance policy.

**Why it fails:** A template AI governance policy without underlying operational architecture creates the worst possible outcome: a written representation of obligations the boutique cannot evidence. The template produces documentation that may make the boutique look more sophisticated in an institutional comparison while simultaneously creating more legal exposure through the representations in the document itself.

**The gap DigitalCoa.ch fills:** Architecture before documentation. The boutique needs to build the thing, then document it — not document a thing they don't have.

---

### Alternative 4: Doing Nothing and Hoping
The boutique WM has not responded to the institutional questionnaire. They have signed the carrier attestation. They have not built AI governance architecture. They are hoping the issue goes away or that their size makes them unlikely to be inspected.

**Why it persists:** FINMA has been clear that supervisory focus on AI includes smaller supervised institutions. The institutional client comparison effect means that doing nothing is not neutral — it is a competitive signal in the comparison. The boutique that does not respond to an institutional AI questionnaire is being evaluated as less sophisticated than the boutique that responds with even an imperfect response.

**The gap DigitalCoa.ch fills:** A clear-eyed assessment of the actual risk of inaction, in Geneva-specific terms, specific enough for the MD to make a rational authorization decision.

---

### Alternative 5: The External CISO / Virtual CISO
The boutique WM brings in an external CISO or virtual CISO to address AI governance as part of their broader technology risk program.

**What they get:** A technology risk framework that covers AI governance as a subset of IT risk. The CISO produces an AI inventory. The inventory may be technically accurate but is not calibrated to the specific regulatory obligations of a Geneva boutique WM under FINMA Guidance 08/2024 and nFADP.

**Why it fails:** The external CISO approach produces a technically-oriented AI governance response that satisfies the IT risk frame but may not address the specific FINMA supervisory expectations, the nFADP documentation obligations, the institutional client comparison effect, or the personal liability exposure of the MD signing carrier attestations.

**The gap DigitalCoa.ch fills:** A DigitalCoa.ch engagement is calibrated to the Geneva boutique WM MD's specific accountability frame — personal liability, institutional questionnaire, carrier attestation, FINMA supervision — not a generic technology risk program.
