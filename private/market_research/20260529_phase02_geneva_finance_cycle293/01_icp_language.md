## ICP Profile
- **Firm type:** Boutique wealth management, Geneva
- **Size:** 50–200 employees
- **Role:** Managing Director or Partner, personally accountable
- **Core problem:** AI tool deployment for client portfolios and data
- **Key pressures:** Institutional AI governance questionnaires, carrier attestation, FINMA Guidance 08/2024, SRO vacuum, nFADP enforcement, cross-firm institutional comparison, proprietary methodology exposure risk

---

## How the ICP Talks About AI Problems

### The Language of Structural Paralysis

The Geneva boutique WM MD is not ignoring AI governance. The MD is trying to act on it and finding that every action creates a new problem. The MD does not say "AI isn't our problem." The MD says:

> *"We know we need to do something. We've had three conversations about it this quarter. Every time we start building a response, we realize the response itself creates a new exposure. We're spending money we can't measure, on documentation we don't know how to use, for a questionnaire we don't understand the purpose of. And the guy sitting next to us — we don't know if he's in the same boat or if he figured it out."*

The structural paralysis frame is newer than the ignorance frame. More MDs have moved from "what is AI governance?" to "we know we need it, we can't get to yes."

---

### Primary Language Patterns

#### Pattern 1: "We've got something — I think"
The MD has some AI tools running. The MD is not sure what all of them are doing.

> *"We signed up for this platform last year. It helps with reporting. I think it's AI. My team uses it. I'm not sure what it does with our client data or whether it's making decisions about anything. And now I'm being asked to attest to our AI governance."*

**What this reveals:** The AI inventory problem is not "we have no AI." It is "we have AI, we don't know what it does with our clients' data, and we are being asked to account for it." The gap is between active deployment and governance awareness.

**Language markers:** "I think," "my team uses it," "not sure," "signed up last year," "it helps with reporting."

---

#### Pattern 2: "The client is asking questions I can't answer — and I think they know it"
The institutional questionnaire arrives. The MD reads it. The MD recognizes that the institutional client knows more about AI governance than the MD does — and the MD does not know if this is a test, a genuine comparison, or a signal.

> *"This questionnaire has questions about our AI model validation process. We don't have an AI model. Or do we? If the platform we're using has AI models in it, does that count? The client is asking as if they know the answer to these questions. I don't know if I'm being evaluated or educated."*

**What this reveals:** The institutional client sophistication gap. The boutique doesn't know whether the institutional client is asking from a position of operational knowledge or theoretical knowledge. The response is being written in uncertainty about what standard is being applied and by whom.

**Language markers:** "I can't answer," "do I count," "being evaluated or educated," "they know more than I do."

---

#### Pattern 3: "Every time I try to fix it, I create another problem"
The MD tries to respond to the carrier attestation or the institutional questionnaire. The act of responding creates new legal exposure.

> *"I tried to describe our AI governance in the attestation. I wrote something that sounded reasonable. Then I read it back and realized I had just described obligations I can't prove we meet. My broker said just sign it. My lawyer said don't sign it until we've built something. I signed it. I don't know what I signed."*

**What this reveals:** The documentation paradox. A written AI governance response without underlying operational architecture creates representations the boutique cannot support with evidence. The act of documenting is now risk-generating, not risk-mitigating.

**Language markers:** "sounded reasonable," "obligations I can't prove," "don't know what I signed."

---

#### Pattern 4: "The budget conversation"
The board or the partners ask: what are we buying with this AI governance investment?

> *"I can't show ROI on AI governance. I can show risk. But the partners want to know what we're getting. We're spending on legal, on external advisors, on documentation. It doesn't generate revenue. It doesn't get clients. It just reduces exposure — exposure we didn't quantify. How do I put that in a budget presentation?"*

**What this reveals:** The Geneva boutique AI governance budget paradox. The investment is real, the ROI is invisible, and the authorization logic is circular: spend to reduce exposure you can't prove exists, to avoid consequences you can't quantify.

**Language markers:** "can't show ROI," "partners want to know," "doesn't generate revenue," "budget presentation."

---

#### Pattern 5: "Our proprietary methodology"
The MD is aware that AI explanation requirements create a structural conflict.

> *"If a client asks me to explain why the AI recommended this portfolio, I either give them our methodology — which is our competitive advantage — or I give them something vague that doesn't satisfy the requirement. I've been hoping no one asks."*

**What this reveals:** The nFADP Art.20 right to explanation creates a structural conflict with proprietary portfolio construction. The MD knows this is live. The MD is hoping it doesn't become acute.

**Language markers:** "our methodology," "competitive advantage," "doesn't satisfy the requirement," "hoping no one asks."

---

## Where the ICP Is Right Now

The Geneva boutique WM MD is in the paralysis-to-action transition. The MD knows AI governance is required. The MD has tried to build a response. The MD has discovered that the response itself is a risk. The MD is now caught between:

- Signing documents without underlying architecture (creating representations)
- Building architecture without clear methodology (spending without ROI signal)
- Responding to institutional clients without knowing the comparison standard (flying blind)
- Protecting proprietary methodology while facing explanation requirements (structural conflict)

The market is at the point where the paralysis is costing something — in time, in external advisory spend, in signed documents the MD doesn't fully understand — but the value of acting is still not visible.

The MD is not apathetic. The MD is stuck.
