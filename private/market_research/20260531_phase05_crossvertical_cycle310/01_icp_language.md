# ICP Language — Cross-Vertical | C310 Phase 05

## How All Four Verticals Talk About the Same Problem

The ICP in legal, finance, consulting, and healthcare has the same core problem: they started using AI, they didn't document it, and now a form is asking specific questions about a period when no documentation existed.

The language pattern is identical across all four verticals — not similar, identical.

---

## The Universal ICP Language Pattern

### Pattern 1: "I didn't know I needed to document this."

**Legal (Geneva):**
> "I started using AI on client matters. I didn't think I needed a system. Now the carrier's asking about specific matters — and I have nothing from that period."

**Finance (Geneva WM):**
> "I've been running AI-assisted portfolio construction for 18 months. I didn't document the authorization or the monitoring. The institutional DDQ is asking about exactly that period."

**Consulting (Geneva boutique):**
> "I put AI tools into my proposals. I didn't think about what it meant for documentation. Now the client's questionnaire is asking about methodology — and the form I signed covers AI use I can't evidence."

**Healthcare (Geneva physician):**
> "I started using AI diagnostic tools 18 months ago. I didn't document anything at the time. Now carriers are asking about 2023-2024 AI use — and I have no records."

**The language they use:**
- "I didn't know I needed to document this"
- "I didn't think it required a system"
- "I assumed the policy was enough"
- "I didn't realize it was personal"
- "I thought the vendor handled it"

**The trigger phrase:**
> "The form your carrier sent you asks about AI use starting in 2023. You have no documentation from that period."

---

### Pattern 2: "The questions changed — I didn't notice."

**Legal:**
> "The carrier's form used to ask: do you have a policy? Now it asks: what AI was used in this specific matter, by whom, with what authorization? That's a different question."

**Finance:**
> "The institutional DDQ used to ask about our AI governance framework. Now it asks for evidence of human authorization for specific portfolio decisions. That's a different question."

**Consulting:**
> "The client questionnaire used to ask about our general approach to technology. Now it asks specifically about AI methodology in our proposals. That's a different question."

**Healthcare:**
> "The carrier form used to ask if we used AI. Now it asks about specific AI tools, when we started using them, and who authorized each one. That's a different question."

**The language they use:**
- "The questions changed"
- "It escalated"
- "It got more specific"
- "I didn't notice the change"
- "The form is asking for something different now"

**The trigger phrase:**
> "The carrier's questions changed from 'do you have a system?' to 'what happened in this specific matter?' These are different questions. Your documentation was built to answer the first one."

---

### Pattern 3: "My standard is higher than the regulatory requirement — I didn't know that."

**Legal:**
> "I'm told I need to be 'adequate.' Adequate relative to what? I'm not sure what the carrier actually requires versus what FINMA or the bar association says."

**Finance:**
> "FINMA says I need 'adequate' human oversight. But the institutional DDQ is asking for specific authorization evidence — that's a higher standard than what FINMA specified."

**Consulting:**
> "The client questionnaire is asking me to commit to AI methodology disclosure that exceeds what any Swiss regulation requires. I didn't realize the client's standard was higher than the regulator's."

**Healthcare:**
> "FMH said I need human oversight. They didn't say what that means in practice. Swissmedic is asking specific questions about operator classification. I'm being held to a standard no one defined."

**The language they use:**
- "I thought I was aligned to the requirement"
- "The regulatory floor is lower than what they're asking"
- "I didn't realize the client sets a higher standard"
- "I was told 'adequate' but no one defined it"
- "The comparison group isn't the right comparison"

**The trigger phrase:**
> "The standard is not what the regulator specified. It is what the carrier's form actually asks. And the form has changed."

---

### Pattern 4: "I thought the policy was the documentation."

**Legal:**
> "I wrote an AI governance policy. I thought that was what the carrier wanted. The renewal form is asking about matter-level authorization — the policy doesn't answer that."

**Finance:**
> "We have a comprehensive AI governance policy. But the institutional DDQ wants evidence of authorization for specific AI systems in specific portfolio decisions. The policy doesn't provide that."

**Consulting:**
> "We described our AI methodology in the proposal. But the client questionnaire asks for evidence of how we actually use AI on engagements. The proposal description doesn't evidence the practice."

**Healthcare:**
> "We have an AI governance framework in the practice. But the carrier is asking for physician-specific documentation of AI use on patient matters. The framework doesn't cover that."

**The language they use:**
- "I thought the policy was the documentation"
- "I produced what was asked for originally"
- "The policy is there, but it's not answering the new questions"
- "It's comprehensive but it's not specific"
- "The policy doesn't help me answer the form"

**The trigger phrase:**
> "The policy answered the question the carrier asked two years ago. The carrier is asking different questions now. The policy can't answer matter-level questions."

---

### Pattern 5: "The authorization chain is my problem — I didn't know it was invisible."

**Legal:**
> "My associate used AI on client matters. I didn't authorize it. The carrier's form asks who authorized the AI use — and the answer is: no one did."

**Finance:**
> "My associate activated the AI module. My name is on the recommendations that went to clients. The authorization for that activation is undocumented — and it's my liability."

**Consulting:**
> "My sub-consultant used AI on the client engagement. I signed the carrier form covering third-party AI use. I have no evidence of what the sub-consultant actually used."

**Healthcare:**
> "My medical assistant interacts with the AI system using patient data. I delegated the interaction. I have no documentation of what the AI actually processed or why."

**The language they use:**
- "I didn't know it was my liability"
- "I didn't know the authorization was required"
- "I assumed it was handled"
- "I didn't realize my name was on it"
- "The chain from me to the AI is invisible"

**The trigger phrase:**
> "You never authorized it. Your name is on the output. That is your authorization chain — and it has steps you didn't document."

---

### Pattern 6: "The broker told me — nobody else translated it."

**Legal:**
> "My broker mentioned that carrier forms are asking more specific questions this year. He said the attestation is getting more detailed. That was the first time anyone told me to look at the form."

**Finance:**
> "My broker said: 'Your institutional clients are asking AI governance questions now — it's in the DDQ. You need to take this seriously.' That was the translation."

**Consulting:**
> "The broker mentioned that a consulting client asked for AI governance documentation in a procurement review. He said it's coming for all consulting boutiques. That was the alert."

**Healthcare:**
> "The broker said: 'Carriers are now asking physicians directly about AI use, naming individual doctors on the forms.' That's when I understood — it's personal."

**The language they use:**
- "The broker mentioned it"
- "Nobody told me except the broker"
- "My peer mentioned it first"
- "I heard it through the network"
- "That's when it became real"

**The trigger phrase:**
> "The broker is the translation mechanism. Nobody else is telling you what the form actually requires."

## Cross-Vertical ICP Profile

**Who they are:** Managing professional — attorney, managing director, principal, physician — personally accountable for AI use in their practice. 2–20 person organization. Geneva-based. Revenue-generating work is their primary focus. AI adoption happened without a documentation system.

**What they know:** They have a problem. They've received a form. The form is asking questions their documentation can't answer.

**What they don't know:** The pre-signing review exists. The authorization chain is their liability. Documentation debt is non-linear. The comparison group is wrong. The policy doesn't answer the matter-level question.

**What activates them:** Personal liability. Not regulatory penalty — personal liability. Attorney license. Managing director Article 63. Responsible person Article 52. Physician license. The commercial gate (client questionnaire, institutional DDQ, mandate competition) creates urgency before the regulatory deadline does.

**What they respond to:** Evidence language. Not framework language. Not "governance" — "documentation." Not "policy" — "what the form actually asks for." Not "training" — "what you need to answer the specific question on the form."
