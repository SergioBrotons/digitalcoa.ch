# Market Signals — Cross-Vertical | C310 Phase 05

## Problems That Appear Across All Four Verticals

The cross-vertical synthesis identifies problems that are structurally identical across legal, finance, consulting, and healthcare — despite different regulatory frameworks, different professional contexts, and different carrier forms. These are not similar problems. They are the same problem with vertical-specific surfaces.

---

## Pattern 1: The Form Changed the Question Type — And Nobody Noticed

**Cross-vertical signal:** The carrier form (legal, finance) or client procurement document (consulting, healthcare) has shifted from asking firm-level questions to asking matter-level questions. The firm has been producing firm-level documentation (policy, framework, statement) that cannot answer matter-level questions.

**Legal:** Carrier attestation shifted from "do you have an AI policy?" to "what AI was used in this specific matter, by whom, with what authorization?" — matter-level question requiring contemporaneous record.

**Finance:** Institutional DDQ shifted from "describe your AI governance framework" to "provide evidence of human authorization for the AI systems used in managing our assets — who authorized each system, when, on what basis?" — matter-level question requiring authorization chain documentation.

**Consulting:** Client questionnaire shifted from "what is your general technology approach?" to "describe your AI methodology for this specific engagement — what tools, what disclosure, what oversight?" — matter-level question requiring proposal-level evidence.

**Healthcare:** Carrier form shifted from "do you use AI in your practice?" to "which AI tools did you use for patient care, when did you start, who authorized each tool, and can you show the clinical reasoning when AI was involved in a diagnosis?" — physician-personal matter-level question.

**The market signal:** The documentation the market produced was built to answer the old question. The forms are asking the new question. The gap is structural — a policy document cannot answer a matter-level question.

## Pattern 2: The Authorization Chain Is Undocumented — And It's the Managing Professional's Liability

**Cross-vertical signal:** The managing professional (managing partner, managing director, principal, physician) is personally accountable for AI use in their organization that they did not authorize. The authorization chain from the managing professional to the AI tool is invisible — no documentation exists at any step.

**Legal:** Associate uses AI on client matter → managing partner never authorized specific tool → carrier attributes associate's AI use to managing partner → managing partner personally signs attestation covering unauthenticated AI use.

**Finance:** Managing director selects portfolio AI system → vendor implements AI module → associate activates module → MD's name is on recommendations that went to clients → authorization for steps 2 and 3 undocumented → MD personally accountable under Article 63.

**Consulting:** Principal signs carrier form covering third-party AI use → sub-consultant uses AI on client engagement → principal has no visibility into sub-consultant tool stack → carrier form attestation covers unauthenticated third-party AI use.

**Healthcare:** Physician delegates patient interaction to AI system → medical assistant operates AI interface with patient data → physician has no documentation of what AI processed, why, or with what oversight → physician personally accountable under FMH human oversight requirement and Swissmedic operator classification.

**The market signal:** The authorization chain is the managing professional's liability. The chain runs through vendor implementation, associate activation, and staff interaction — none of which the managing professional documented. Retroactive authorization chain documentation is the highest-urgency need across all four verticals.

## Pattern 3: Documentation Debt Is Non-Linear — Linear Solutions Are Structurally Insufficient

**Cross-vertical signal:** The documentation debt accumulated by firms using AI without a documentation system is non-linear — it compounds faster than the firm's ability to address it with linear solutions. A forward-looking policy document cannot reconstruct a retrospective non-linear debt.

**Legal:** Started with one associate, one AI tool → now three associates, multiple tools, multiple matter types → documentation debt is not 3x what it was, it is 10x → policy cannot address the compounded debt → retroactive reconstruction required for 12–24 months of non-linear debt.

**Finance:** Started with one AI-assisted portfolio model → now multiple AI modules across multiple asset classes → model has drifted without documented review → 18 months of undocumented drift → institutional DDQ asking for model monitoring evidence → retroactive reconstruction of undocumented period required.

**Consulting:** Started using AI in proposals → now AI in methodology, sub-consultant AI in delivery, client AI governance questionnaire asking for evidence → proposal-level documentation debt compounds across mandates → retroactive reconstruction of AI methodology use required.

**Healthcare:** Started with one AI diagnostic tool → now two to four AI tools across administrative, documentation, and diagnostic use → documentation debt compounds per tool per physician per patient interaction → 12–24 months of undocumented AI use visible to carriers → retroactive reconstruction required.

**The market signal:** The majority market problem is retrospective, not forward-looking. Most firms have 12–24 months of undocumented AI use that cannot be addressed with a new policy. The solution is retroactive reconstruction — a specific methodology for documenting undocumented AI use in defensible form, not another policy document.

## Pattern 4: The Comparison Trap Is Creating False Adequacy Signals

**Cross-vertical signal:** The managing professional believes their documentation is "adequate" because it is similar to what their peers have produced. The comparison group is other small Geneva firms (legal), other boutique WM firms (finance), other consulting boutiques (consulting), other small medical practices (healthcare). The carrier's standard is not the peer group standard — it is escalating faster than the peer group norm improves.

**Legal:** "I wrote an AI governance policy. I compared it to what other Geneva firms produced. I believe I'm adequate." → Carrier's standard has escalated from firm-level policy question to matter-level authorization question → peer group norm has not moved → gap widens → false adequacy persists until carrier renewal.

**Finance:** "I completed the carrier attestation. I reviewed what other boutique WM firms did. I believe I'm prepared." → Institutional DDQ has shifted to authorization evidence and model monitoring → carrier attestation doesn't answer institutional DDQ → two-document false security → renewal reveals gap.

**Consulting:** "I answered the client questionnaire. I looked at what other boutiques submitted. I think my response was adequate." → Client procurement AI governance standards escalating → proposal methodology exposure active → questionnaire becomes audit standard → false adequacy until client audit.

**Healthcare:** "I have an AI governance framework in my practice. My colleagues have similar approaches. I'm probably fine." → Carrier forms now ask physician-personal questions about specific AI tools → FMH oversight principle with no defined protocol → Swissmedic operator classification unresolved → false adequacy until carrier renewal or Swissmedic inquiry.

**The market signal:** The comparison trap is structurally invisible to the ICP. They cannot see that their peer group norm is not the carrier standard. The service that resolves this requires showing the ICP what the carrier's form actually asks — not what the peer group produced. Reading the carrier form as market intelligence is the primary value proposition.

## Pattern 5: The Fee Agreement / Engagement Structure Has No AI Clause — And Client Questions Are Activating It

**Cross-vertical signal:** The governing commercial document (fee agreement in legal, engagement letter in consulting, client mandate in finance, patient consent in healthcare) predates AI. The document has no clause addressing: AI tool use, AI-generated content, client data processing by AI vendors, human oversight of AI, or AI's role in the deliverable. Clients are now asking directly — and the managing professional has no calibrated response or documented basis.

**Legal:** Client asks directly: "Did you use AI on my case?" → Managing partner has no response → fee agreement has no AI clause → client trust erodes or deceived feeling activates → relationship risk → possible bar complaint.

**Finance:** Institutional client asks: "What AI systems are managing our assets, and how do we know human oversight was applied?" → Managing director has no documented response → mandate document has no AI clause → institutional client initiates governance review.

**Consulting:** Client procurement asks: "What AI methodology was used in the deliverable?" → Principal has no calibrated response → engagement letter has no AI clause → client uses questionnaire response as audit standard → methodology exposure confirmed.

**Healthcare:** Patient asks: "Did you use AI to diagnose me?" → Physician has no documented response → patient consent form has no AI clause → patient trust issue → potential complaint.

**The market signal:** The AI clause in the governing commercial document is the document that makes the client conversation straightforward. It is not a compliance requirement — it is a client trust document. The managing professional needs it before the client asks, not after.

## Pattern 6: The Documentation System Precedes the Regulatory Deadline — The Commercial Gate Activates First

**Cross-vertical signal:** The regulatory deadline (nFADP enforcement, Swissmedic July 1 2026, FINMA guidance evolution, bar association AI standards) creates urgency — but the commercial gate activates before the regulatory deadline creates pain. The institutional DDQ (finance), client questionnaire (consulting), carrier renewal (legal), and broker conversation (healthcare) create urgency before the regulatory calendar does.

**Legal:** Carrier attestation renewal arrives → questions more specific than prior year → managing partner discovers documentation gap before bar association issues AI standards → commercial consequence precedes regulatory standard.

**Finance:** Institutional DDQ arrives before FINMA examination cycle → mandate retention becomes the urgency trigger → Article 63 personal liability activates through commercial gate, not regulatory enforcement.

**Consulting:** Client procurement AI governance questionnaire arrives → proposal methodology exposure active → mandate competition creates urgency → broker translates regulatory language into commercial consequence → commercial gate activates first.

**Healthcare:** Carrier form arrives with physician-personal AI questions → Swissmedic July 1, 2026 operator classification deadline → broker translates Swissmedic requirement into carrier consequence language → commercial urgency precedes regulatory enforcement.

**The market signal:** The commercial forcing function (carrier form, institutional DDQ, client questionnaire, broker conversation) is the primary activation mechanism — not regulatory enforcement. Services that address commercial vulnerability activate ICPs before regulatory compliance services do.
