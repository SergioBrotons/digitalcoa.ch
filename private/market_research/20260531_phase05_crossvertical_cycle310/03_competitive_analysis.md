# Competitive Analysis — Cross-Vertical | C310 Phase 05

## Cross-Vertical Competitive White Space

The cross-vertical competitive analysis identifies structural gaps in how the market is addressing AI accountability — gaps that exist across all four verticals simultaneously. These are not vertical-specific gaps. They are universal gaps that no competitor has addressed in any vertical.

---

## Finding 1: No Competitor Offers Pre-Signing Carrier Form Review — The Universal Intervention Point

**The gap:** The market has no service that reviews the carrier's form before the managing professional signs it. Every vertical (legal, finance, consulting, healthcare) experiences the same pattern: carrier sends form → managing professional signs without review → carrier renewal asks questions the form didn't prepare for → gaps become coverage problems → post-signing review is too late.

The pre-signing review is the highest-value intervention point in the accountability chain — and it is unoccupied. No competitor has built it.

**Why it hasn't been built:** The service requires reading carrier forms as a market intelligence function. It requires understanding how carrier form questions have evolved over time. It requires mapping the specific questions to the firm's documentation architecture. It requires producing a gap analysis before the form is signed, not after. The market has no reference point for this service — the concept of a "pre-signing form review" doesn't exist in the ICP's mental model.

**The competitive white space:** First-mover opportunity. The broker referral product for the pre-signing review is the ideal acquisition channel: bounded (90-minute session), specific (the carrier form in question), directly tied to the renewal trigger. The broker who serves law firms, WM firms, consulting boutiques, and medical practices is looking for a service to refer. No one has built it.

**Implication:** The pre-signing carrier form review is the universal first product — the entry point for all four verticals. The same methodology applies regardless of vertical: read the form, map the questions to the firm's documentation, identify the gaps, produce a prioritized action list.

## Finding 2: No Competitor Addresses the Authorization Chain Documentation — The Universal Liability

**The gap:** Every vertical has the same authorization chain problem: the managing professional is personally accountable for AI use they did not authorize. The chain runs through vendor implementation, associate activation, and staff interaction — and no documentation exists at any step. No competitor offers a service that specifically documents the authorization chain.

**Legal:** Managing partner's associate used AI without authorization → no documentation of tool authorization, supervision protocol, or output review → managing partner personally liable.

**Finance:** Managing director selected a system, vendor implemented it, associate activated it → MD never authorized steps 2 and 3 → MD's name is on the recommendations → no authorization chain documentation exists.

**Consulting:** Principal signed carrier form covering third-party AI use → sub-consultant used AI without disclosure → no authorization chain for delegation.

**Healthcare:** Physician delegated patient interaction to AI → medical assistant operated AI interface → no documentation of what was processed, why, or with what oversight.

**Why it hasn't been addressed:** The authorization chain documentation is not a "policy" problem — it is a "multi-step evidence" problem. It requires documenting the chain at each step: tool selection authorization, vendor implementation oversight, associate/staff activation authorization, output review documentation. No framework in the market addresses this as a chain.

**The competitive white space:** Authorization chain documentation is the second universal product. It is distinct from AI governance policy — it is the evidence that the managing professional authorized specific AI use, at specific times, with specific oversight. The retroactive documentation of undocumented authorization chains is part of the service.

**Implication:** The authorization chain audit + documentation is the second universal product, applicable across all four verticals with vertical-specific overlays.

## Finding 3: No Competitor Offers Retroactive Reconstruction Methodology — The Universal Documentation Debt Problem

**The gap:** Every vertical has the same retrospective documentation problem: 12–24 months of undocumented AI use that needs to be reconstructed in defensible form. No competitor offers a retroactive reconstruction methodology.

**The problem:** Forward-looking solutions (write a policy, create a framework, conduct training) cannot address retrospective non-linear documentation debt. The documentation debt that exists now — from the period when no system was in place — is the majority market problem. Linear solutions are structurally insufficient regardless of quality.

**Why it hasn't been addressed:** Retroactive reconstruction requires understanding what makes retroactive documentation defensible (not the same as creating contemporaneous records), what the carrier form specifically asks about the undocumented period, and what the firm's specific AI use timeline looks like. It requires a methodology, not a template.

**The competitive white space:** The retroactive reconstruction service is the third universal product. It is bounded (defined scope per firm), specific (maps to carrier form questions), and high-urgency (the undocumented period is now visible to carriers). No competitor offers this as a designed product.

**Implication:** Retroactive reconstruction is the primary activation product for ICPs who have already tried a policy and discovered it doesn't address the retrospective debt. It is the entry point for the C310 ICP who is past discovery mode.

## Finding 4: No Competitor Reads the Carrier Form as Competitive Intelligence — The Universal Positioning Gap

**The gap:** The carrier form (legal, finance, consulting, healthcare carrier forms) is not being read as a competitive intelligence document. The market reads carrier forms as compliance documents — something to complete and file. No one is reading the form as the specification that determines what documentation architecture is actually required.

**The pattern across verticals:**
- Legal: Carrier form sophistication escalating faster than firm documentation improves. Form question type has shifted (firm-level → matter-level). No competitor is reading the form as a design specification.
- Finance: Institutional DDQ has shifted from firm-level to matter-level. Carrier attestation and institutional DDQ are different documents. No competitor is reading both as competitive intelligence.
- Consulting: Client procurement questionnaires are setting standards above regulatory requirements. The questionnaire is the design specification for mandate-winning documentation. No competitor is reading it as such.
- Healthcare: Carrier forms now name individual physicians with physician-personal questions. Swissmedic operator classification questions are specific. No competitor is reading the carrier form as physician-personal intelligence.

**Why it hasn't been addressed:** Reading carrier forms as competitive intelligence requires a market intelligence function that doesn't exist in the typical consulting or legal service practice. The form reading is not a one-time exercise — it requires tracking how forms evolve across renewal cycles and how different carriers' forms compare.

**The competitive white space:** The DDQ/form reading service is the fourth universal product. It is the design specification function — the service that tells the ICP what the carrier's form actually requires, and therefore what the documentation architecture needs to produce. This is not "compliance review." It is "market intelligence applied to documentation design."

**Implication:** The carrier form reading service is the foundation of the evidence architecture. It determines what evidence is required before the ICP produces any documentation.

## Finding 5: No Competitor Offers Bilingual EN/FR AI Accountability Documentation — The Geneva Market Gap

**The gap:** Geneva-based firms operate in two documentation environments simultaneously: English (carrier forms, client questionnaires, EU-based client contracts, institutional DDQs) and French (nFADP compliance, professional association guidance, Swiss client communications, FMH guidance). No competitor offers bilingual EN/FR AI accountability documentation as a designed product.

**The problem:** The documentation system must produce evidence that satisfies English-language carrier form questions AND French-language nFADP obligations — as one integrated documentation architecture, not as translated versions of separate documents. The bilingual requirement is a design constraint, not a translation exercise.

**Why it hasn't been addressed:** Bilingual documentation requires two parallel evidence systems that are functionally identical but culturally and linguistically distinct. The French version must satisfy nFADP-specific language requirements (Article 63, Article 52, Article 28 basis language). The English version must satisfy carrier form question structure. Producing these as separate translated documents fails — they must be designed as one system.

**The competitive white space:** The bilingual EN/FR evidence architecture is the fifth universal product. It is specific to the Geneva market — and no global competitor has built a localized Geneva product. The Swiss domestic competitors (Big Four, domestic consultancies) do not offer bilingual EN/FR AI accountability documentation as an evidence architecture.

**Implication:** Bilingual EN/FR documentation is a differentiator in the Geneva market. The ICP who operates in both languages needs documentation that works in both regulatory environments simultaneously.

## Finding 6: No Competitor Offers Cross-Vertical Evidence Architecture — The Universal Solution Structure

**The gap:** No competitor offers an evidence architecture that serves legal, finance, consulting, and healthcare simultaneously. The evidence architecture for legal (matter-level contemporaneous records), finance (authorization chain + model drift + institutional DDQ evidence), consulting (proposal methodology documentation + sub-consultant delegation protocols + engagement letter AI clause), and healthcare (retroactive reconstruction + clinical reasoning documentation + FMH protocol + Swissmedic classification) share a common structure — but no competitor has identified the universal structure.

**The universal evidence architecture:**
1. **Pre-signing carrier form review** — read the form as design specification, map to documentation, identify gaps
2. **Authorization chain documentation** — document the chain from managing professional to AI tool at each step
3. **Retroactive reconstruction** — reconstruct the undocumented period in defensible form
4. **Ongoing evidence production system** — matter-level contemporaneous records, not firm-level policy documents

**Why it hasn't been addressed:** The market has responded to AI accountability with vertical-specific "AI governance frameworks" — generic documents that don't answer the specific questions the carrier forms ask. No competitor has identified that the carrier form question structure is the common element across verticals, and that the evidence architecture is universal despite vertical-specific surfaces.

**The competitive white space:** The cross-vertical evidence architecture is the sixth universal product. It is not four separate products — it is one system with vertical-specific overlays. The methodology is universal; the documentation outputs are vertical-specific.

**Implication:** The cross-vertical evidence architecture positions against all competitors simultaneously — it is the only product that serves legal, finance, consulting, and healthcare with the same underlying methodology. The positioning statement: "The market offers frameworks. I produce evidence. The carrier form is the design specification."
