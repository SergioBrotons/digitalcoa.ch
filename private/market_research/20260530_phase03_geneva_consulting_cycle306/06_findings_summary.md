# Phase 03 Findings Summary — Geneva Consulting Boutique
## C306 Phase 03 (2026-05-30)

## Key Insights

### NET-NEW Findings (Distinct from C214–C305 Consulting Phases)

**Finding 1: Client AI Governance Questionnaires as Commercial Gate**
Large corporate clients and institutional buyers are using AI governance questionnaires as supply-chain compliance mechanisms. This is not regulatory pressure — it's commercial pressure. The client's compliance team is managing their own EU AI Act and nFADP obligations and extending them to their vendor list. Geneva consulting boutiques are receiving these questionnaires without warning and without a response framework. The commercial consequence is immediate: inability to respond may mean losing the next engagement.

**Finding 2: Carrier AI Attestation as Insurance Renewal Condition**
Swiss insurance carriers have introduced AI governance attestation as a condition of professional liability policy renewal. This is distinct from regulatory attestation — it's a commercial gate controlled by the insurance carrier. The accuracy risk is the critical issue: signing an attestation that doesn't reflect actual practice may constitute misrepresentation to an insurer, potentially voiding coverage if a claim arises. Most managing partners signed without understanding what they attested to.

**Finding 3: Proposal Methodology Defensibility — Two-Dimensional Challenge**
Proposal methodology defensibility has two distinct dimensions: (1) disclosure — did you tell the client you used AI, and (2) traceability — can you walk through the logic of your AI-assisted analysis and correct errors or identify bias. Most boutique consulting firms have neither dimension covered. The professional liability risk is active regardless of disclosure: if AI-assisted methodology produces a flawed recommendation, the liability exposure is live.

**Finding 4: Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Chain — Structural Accountability Gap**
Geneva consulting boutiques typically operate with1–3 sub-consultants. The AI delegation chain creates a structural accountability gap: the managing partner is accountable for deliverables that incorporate AI they didn't directly control. Client questionnaires and carrier attestations ask about sub-consultant AI use — but the managing partner has no systematic way to know what the sub-consultant used. The accountability gap is structural, not procedural.

**Finding 5: nFADP Article 52 Personal Liability — Managing Partner Direct Exposure**
nFADP Article 52 establishes individual administrative liability for natural persons, including managing directors and partners. Fine up to CHF 250,000 for intentional violations. Unlike corporate compliance officers, who may have institutional protections, the managing partner in a small consulting boutique has direct personal exposure — including personal assets at risk. Professional liability insurance may not cover nFADP administrative fines. This is the trigger that accelerates decision-making.

**Finding 6: EU AI Act GPAI Obligations Mediated Through AI Tool Providers**
The EU AI Act GPAI obligations (operative since August 2, 2025) apply to AI providers, not directly to consulting firms. But the compliance burden cascades to consulting firms through their AI tool providers: transparency requirements, copyright compliance, technical documentation. Most boutique firms don't know which of their AI providers are GPAI-compliant, what documentation they maintain, or what information the consulting firm is entitled to receive. The documentation gap is invisible to the consulting firm.

## Service Model Implications

### Entry Sequence for Consulting Boutique ICP
1. **Client AI Questionnaire Response** (bounded, specific, high commercial urgency) — immediate need, creates trust
2. **Carrier Attestation Accuracy Review** (high-intent, time-sensitive) — addresses signed document risk
3. **Proposal Methodology Defensibility Review** (medium-term, relationship-deepening) — professional liability protection
4. **Sub-Consultant AI Governance Framework** (structural, ongoing) — accountability chain documentation
5. **Personal nFADP Article 52 Exposure Assessment** (trigger-based, high-intent) — managing partner personal liability
6. **AI Governance Advisory Retainer** (ongoing, EN + FR) — durable revenue model

### Service Model Distinction from Phase 01 (Legal) and Phase 02 (Finance)
- Phase 01 (legal): regulatory accountability pressure from broker/dealer context
- Phase 02 (finance): institutional investor DDQ pressure on WM firms
- Phase 03 (consulting): commercial accountability pressure from clients and carriers — distinct from regulatory pressure

### Key Differentiation
The Phase 03 consulting boutique ICP is distinguished by **personal accountability at the managing partner level** combined with **commercial gate pressure** (client questionnaires, carrier attestation) that is more immediately actionable than regulatory deadlines. The entry trigger is not "the regulator is asking" — it's "my client is asking" or "my carrier is asking."

## Ready-to-Use Language

### For Client Questionnaire Response Service
- "Your client sent you a 40-question AI governance questionnaire. You have two weeks. We help you answer it systematically — documenting your actual practice, not inventing compliance theater."
- "The questionnaire is a supply-chain compliance gate. Your client is managing their own regulatory exposure. They're asking you to demonstrate that you're a responsible part of their chain."

### For Carrier Attestation Service
- "You signed an AI attestation to your carrier. Did you know what you were attesting to? If you attested to procedures you don't have, and something goes wrong, your carrier may deny coverage."
- "Carrier attestation accuracy review: we identify the gaps between what you signed and what you actually do — before a claim arises."

### For Methodology Defensibility Service
- "If a client challenges your proposal and you've used AI to build it, can you defend it? Not just 'did you disclose the AI use' — but 'can you walk through the logic and correct errors?'"
- "Methodology defensibility review: documentation that is both disclosure-ready and defensible under professional liability standards."

### For Sub-Consultant AI Governance Service
- "You're accountable for deliverables that incorporate AI you didn't directly control. Your sub-consultant uses AI for research and presentation. You sign the carrier attestation confirming you monitor AI use — but you don't have a monitoring system."
- "Sub-consultant AI governance framework: structure the accountability chain, document the AI use, answer client questions before they arise."

### For Personal Liability Advisory
- "nFADP Article 52 makes you personally liable as a managing partner. Fine up to CHF 250,000. Your professional liability insurance may not cover it. The documentation you keep today is your evidence of due diligence tomorrow."
- "Personal exposure assessment: what are you personally accountable for, what does your insurance actually cover, and what documentation do you need to demonstrate due diligence?"

- Carrier attestation: already live (insurance sector, commercial gate)
- nFADP Article 52: already operative (individual liability active)
- EU AI Act GPAI: operative since August 2, 2025 (documentation cascade to consulting firms)
- Swiss Federal Council AI consultation: end of 2026 (~7 months)

## First-Mover Window
~9–12 months before the market floods with compliance-driven demand and larger advisory firms enter the boutique tier. The consulting boutique AI governance advisory gap is structurally distinct from the legal and finance gaps — it is defined by commercial gates (client questionnaires, carrier attestation) rather than regulatory deadlines. The window is defined by the speed at which client-side AI governance questionnaires are proliferating — and that speed is accelerating.
