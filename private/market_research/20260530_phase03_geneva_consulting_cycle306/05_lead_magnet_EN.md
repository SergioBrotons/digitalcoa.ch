## The Consulting Boutique's AI Governance Survival Guide
### What Your Clients Are Asking For — And What You're Not Ready to Answer

---

### Section 1: The Three Questions That Just Became Your Job

Three years ago, your clients never asked about AI. Today, they send you40-question governance questionnaires. Your insurance carrier wants an AI attestation. A client just challenged your proposal methodology — because you used AI to build it.

These are not regulatory questions. They are commercial ones. Your clients are managing their own regulatory exposure — and they're asking you to demonstrate that you're a responsible part of their supply chain.

**The three questions you now need to answer:**

1. **What AI tools are you using in our engagement?** — Client AI governance questionnaire
2. **Can you defend your methodology if we challenge it?** — Proposal methodology defensibility
3. **What happens if your AI use creates a problem for us?** — Carrier attestation and personal liability

If you can't answer these three questions, you're one client conversation away from a commercial problem.

---

### Section 2: The Client Questionnaire — You're Already Behind

Your biggest client just sent you a 40-question AI governance questionnaire. You have two weeks to respond. Here's what's actually happening:

**The questionnaire is a supply-chain compliance gate.** Your client has EU AI Act obligations. They're asking their entire vendor list about AI governance maturity. If you can't respond, you don't get the next engagement.

**The questions are specific:**
- Which AI tools do you use in client engagements?
- How do you ensure human oversight of AI-assisted work?
- What data processing agreements do you have with your AI vendors?
- How do you manage AI use by sub-consultants in your team?
- What is your incident response procedure if an AI tool produces an error?

**The problem:** Most boutique consulting firms can't answer these questions systematically. They either wing it, copy answers from the AI vendor's website, or delay responding — which signals immaturity to the client.

**What you need:** A structured response process that documents your AI governance posture, demonstrates accountability, and doesn't require you to build an enterprise compliance infrastructure.

---

### Section 3: The Carrier Attestation — You Signed Something

Your professional liability carrier sent you an AI attestation form. You signed it. But did you know what you were attesting to?

**What the attestation actually says:**
- You confirm you have documented AI governance procedures
- You confirm you have data processing agreements with AI vendors
- You confirm you monitor AI tool use by your team and sub-consultants
- You confirm you have an incident response procedure

**The problem:** If you signed an attestation that isn't accurate, and something goes wrong, your carrier may deny coverage. You attested to procedures you don't have. That's a misrepresentation to an insurer — and it can void your policy.

**What you need:** An attestation accuracy review that identifies gaps between what you signed and what you actually do — before a claim arises.

---

### Section 4: The Methodology Defensibility Problem

A client challenges your proposal. They want to know how you arrived at your recommendations. You used AI to assist the analysis. Now what?

**The two dimensions of methodology defensibility:**

1. **Disclosure:** Did you tell the client you used AI? Most consulting firms haven't established a disclosure practice.

2. **Traceability:** Can you walk through the logic of your AI-assisted analysis? If the AI tool produced an error or a biased output, can you identify it and correct it?

**The professional liability risk:** If your AI-assisted methodology produces a flawed recommendation and you can't demonstrate quality control, your professional liability exposure is active — regardless of whether you disclosed the AI use.

**What you need:** A methodology review process that documents AI-assisted work in a way that is both disclosure-ready and defensible under professional liability standards.

---

### Section 5: The Sub-Consultant AI Chain — Your Accountability Gap

You delegate to one or two sub-consultants. One uses AI for research. One uses AI for presentation design. You're responsible for their work. What does that mean for your AI governance obligations?

**The structural problem:**
- Your client asks about AI governance in your engagement
- You don't know what AI your sub-consultant used
- You sign the carrier attestation confirming you monitor AI use — but you don't have a monitoring system
- Your sub-consultant's AI use is not documented in your engagement record

**The accountability gap is structural.** Managing partners are responsible for deliverables that incorporate AI they didn't directly control.

**What you need:** A sub-consultant AI governance framework that establishes:
- Which AI tools sub-consultants may use in your engagements
- What disclosure or documentation is required
- How AI use by sub-consultants gets reported to you
- How to answer client questions about sub-consultant AI use

---

### Section 6: Your Personal Liability — It's Not Corporate

You are a managing partner. You signed the carrier attestation. You approved the proposal. You're personally accountable under nFADP Article 52.

**Article 52 — Individual Administrative Liability:**
- Natural persons are explicitly in scope
- Fine up to CHF 250,000 for intentional violations
- Managing directors and partners are directly exposed
- Professional liability insurance may not cover nFADP administrative fines

**The trigger:** Your personal assets. If an AI-assisted engagement produces a client problem and FDPIC enforcement follows, the fine is personal — not corporate.

**What you need:** A personal liability exposure assessment and a documented AI governance practice that demonstrates due diligence — reducing personal exposure if enforcement arises.

---

### Section 7: The EU AI Act — It's Already Your Problem

If you use AI tools to serve clients — and you do — the EU AI Act already applies to your AI providers. GPAI obligations have been operative since August 2, 2025.

**What this means for you:**
- Your AI providers (OpenAI, Microsoft, Google) are required to maintain technical documentation
- You are entitled to receive certain transparency information from your AI providers
- If you serve EU-based clients, you may have EU AI Act deployer obligations

**The problem:** Most boutique consulting firms don't know which of their AI providers are GPAI-compliant, what documentation they maintain, or what information they're entitled to receive.

**What you need:** An AI provider compliance review that maps your AI tool stack to GPAI obligations and identifies documentation gaps.

### Section 8: Your90-Day AI Governance Sequence

**Immediate (Next 30 Days):**
- [ ] Identify every AI tool used in your client engagements (including sub-consultant use)
- [ ] Review your carrier attestation for accuracy — identify gaps between what you signed and what you do
- [ ] Establish a client AI questionnaire response process (documentation, review, response)
- [ ] Brief your sub-consultants on AI disclosure requirements

**Near-term (30–60 Days):**
- [ ] Conduct a methodology defensibility review for your core proposal formats
- [ ] Establish a sub-consultant AI governance framework (which tools, documentation, reporting)
- [ ] Assess personal nFADP Article 52 exposure as managing partner
- [ ] Map your AI provider stack to GPAI obligations

**Strategic (60–90 Days):**
- [ ] Implement an ongoing AI governance documentation practice (not one-time compliance)
- [ ] Establish carrier attestation accuracy monitoring (annual review)
- [ ] Develop client-ready AI governance disclosure documentation
- [ ] Consider professional liability coverage review for AI-related exposure
