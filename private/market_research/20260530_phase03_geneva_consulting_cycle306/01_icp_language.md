# Phase 03 ICP Language — Geneva Consulting Boutique
## C306 Phase 03 (2026-05-30)

## Addressable Profile
Small consulting boutiques,2–50 employees, Geneva-based. Managing director or partner personally accountable. Struggling with AI tool deployment in client engagements. Concerned about client AI governance questionnaires, carrier attestation, proposal methodology defensibility, sub-consultant AI delegation, and personal liability under nFADP.

## How They Talk About AI Problems

### The Client Questionnaire Trap
"I received a 40-question AI governance questionnaire from my biggest client. I didn't know how to answer half of it. I had to tell them I'd get back to them — and I still haven't."

They are not asking "should we adopt AI." They are being asked BY their clients to demonstrate AI governance maturity. The questionnaire is a new commercial gate — not a regulatory one.

### The Carrier Attestation Gap
"My insurance carrier sent me an AI attestation form. They're asking about our AI tools, our data processing, our sub-consultant AI use. I signed it but I wasn't confident the answers were accurate."

Carrier attestation has become a commercial condition, not just a regulatory one. Consulting firms are being asked to attest to carriers about AI governance — and many are signing without understanding what they've attested to.

### The Methodology Defensibility Problem
"If a client challenges my proposal methodology and I've used AI to generate it, can I defend it? Do I have to disclose the AI use? What does 'methodology defensibility' even mean in 2026?"

Proposal methodology is being challenged in two ways: (1) clients asking how the methodology was produced, (2) potential liability if the AI-assisted methodology produces a flawed recommendation.

### The Sub-Consultant AI Chain
"I delegate to two sub-consultants. One uses AI for research. One uses AI for presentation design. I'm responsible for their work. What does that mean for my AI governance obligations?"

The sub-consultant AI delegation chain creates a cascading responsibility problem. The managing director is accountable for deliverables that incorporate AI they didn't directly control.

### The Personal Liability Trigger
"I'm a managing partner. If our AI use creates a client problem, what's my personal exposure? The nFADP has individual liability provisions. Do I need personal coverage?"

Personal accountability is the trigger. Unlike corporate compliance officers, managing partners and directors face direct personal liability under nFADP Article 52 — fines up to CHF 250,000 for intentional violations.

### The Proposal Defense Language They Use
- "I didn't know the client would ask about AI governance"
- "The attestation form was generic — I just signed it"
- "My sub-consultant handles that side of things"
- "I use AI like everyone else — I didn't think it needed documentation"
- "What does this mean for my professional license?"
- "This is going to get more complicated before it gets simpler"

## Regulatory Urgency Stack (Phase 03 — Consulting Boutique)

1. **Client AI governance questionnaires** — already arriving (commercial gate, not regulatory)
2. **Carrier AI attestation** — already arriving (insurance/finance sector carriers)
3. **nFADP enforcement trajectory** — FDPIC enforcement active; individual liability under Article 52
4. **EU AI Act** — August 2, 2026 (~14 months; extraterritorial if consulting firm has EU clients)
5. **Swiss Federal Council AI consultation** — end of 2026 (~7 months)

## Timing Signal
- Client questionnaires: already live — no transitional period, immediate commercial consequence
- Carrier attestation: already live — insurance/finance sector carriers leading
- nFADP Article 52 personal liability: already operative — individual fines up to CHF 250,000
- EU AI Act: ~14 months — if consulting firm advises EU-based clients or uses EU-based AI providers
- Swiss Federal Council AI consultation: ~7 months — policy framework incoming

## The ICP Decision Trigger
The managing director or partner in a Geneva consulting boutique doesn't wake up concerned about AI governance. They wake up when:
- A major client sends an AI governance questionnaire
- A carrier sends an attestation form
- A sub-consultant delivers AI-assisted work that gets challenged
- A peer mentions a client asking about AI methodology
- A regulatory deadline approaches and they realize their proposal documentation is thin

## Emotional State at Point of Contact
- Confused: "I didn't know I needed to think about this"
- Defensive: "Everyone uses AI — why am I being singled out?"
- Overwhelmed: "There are too many frameworks, too many forms, too many questions"
- Anxious: "What does this mean for my license/personal exposure?"
- Urgent: "I need to respond to this client questionnaire in two weeks"

## Entry Language (What Gets Them to Engage)
- "I need to respond to a client AI questionnaire — can you help me answer it?"
- "I signed a carrier attestation and now I'm worried it was inaccurate"
- "A client challenged my methodology — I used AI to build it"
- "I need to understand my sub-consultant AI delegation obligations"
- "Do I need personal nFADP coverage as a managing partner?"

## Unique Phase 03 Insight
Unlike Phase 01 (legal) and Phase 02 (finance), the Phase 03 consulting boutique faces AI governance pressure from **commercial counterparts** (clients, carriers) — not primarily from regulators. The questionnaire and attestation are commercial gates, not regulatory ones. This shifts the accountability frame from "regulatory compliance" to "commercial defensibility."
