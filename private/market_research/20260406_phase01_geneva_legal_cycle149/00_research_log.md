## NET-NEW Findings This Session (C149 Phase 01)

### Finding 01: nFADP Art. 6 Cross-Border Data Transfer Compliance — Swiss Adequacy Gap Advisory

**Topic:** Revised nFADP (effective 2023-2024) requires adequacy decisions or Safeguards+ notifications for personal data transfers to countries without equivalent protection. Switzerland has no adequacy decision with the US (post-Schrems II, post-Data Privacy Framework), limited adequacy for UK, and specific gaps for certain Asia-Pacific jurisdictions. Geneva's position as a global data hub — financial institutions, commodity trading firms, commodity traders, international organizations, and professional services — creates a disproportionate cross-border data transfer exposure compared to other Swiss cantons.

**Geneva-specific angle:** Geneva's data economy includes: Swiss-headquartered multinationals with global data flows (commodity trading houses, private banks, pharmaceutical companies, watch industry); Geneva-based international organizations and NGOs processing sensitive personal data globally; Geneva law firms and advisory practices handling cross-border client data; Geneva-headquartered tech companies with international operations. The nFADP cross-border transfer framework — adequacy decisions, Safeguards+ (SCCs + TIA), or derogations — is technically complex and frequently mishandled. Geneva-specific risk: transfers to US cloud vendors (AWS Ireland → US parent; Microsoft EU → US; Google Cloud EU → US) are a latent exposure that FDPIC has not explicitly addressed in Geneva-specific guidance.

**Why NET-NEW vs. C96-C148:** None of the prior cycles addressed nFADP cross-border transfer adequacy gaps. C113 mentioned nFADP Art. 9 AI training data consent (distinct from Art. 6 transfer rules). C121/C148 mentioned IO immunity + biometric data (distinct from general cross-border transfer adequacy). The adequacy gap is a distinct, unresolved legal question with significant exposure for Geneva data controllers.

**Advisory opportunity:** nFADP adequacy audit for Geneva firms; Safeguards+ documentation (SCCs + Transfer Impact Assessment); US data transfer remediation for Geneva FIs using US-based cloud/AI vendors; UK/Asia-Pacific adequacy mapping for Geneva multinationals.

### Finding 02: Basel IV AI Model Risk — FINMA Model Documentation Gaps in Geneva Banks' Algorithmic Lending/Credit Assessment

**Topic:** Basel IV (finalized Basel III reforms, effective January 2025 for Swiss banks under FINMA scope) introduces more stringent internal models requirements for credit risk (IRB approach), market risk (FRTB), and operational risk (SMA). Geneva-based banks and financial institutions deploying AI/ML for credit scoring, algorithmic lending, and automated credit decisions face a specific compliance gap: AI/ML credit models used under IRB approaches must meet FINMA's model risk management documentation standards — and most Geneva banks' existing AI credit model governance frameworks do not yet satisfy these requirements.

**Geneva-specific angle:** Geneva's banking sector concentration (UBS, Lombard Odier, Pictet, Mirabaud, Baumann & Cie, Rahn & Bodmer, and the Swiss operations of international banks) includes institutions with significant private banking, asset management, and corporate lending exposures. AI-driven credit assessment tools — both for internal credit decisions and for fintech/corporate lending clients — are increasingly common. FINMA's supervisory expectations for AI model validation (consistent with BCBS239 principles for model risk governance) create a documentation and governance burden that is novel for most Geneva institutions. The specific Basel IV IRB validation requirements: conceptual soundness documentation, performance monitoring, backtesting, and independent validation.

**Why NET-NEW vs. C96-C148:** None of the prior cycles addressed Basel IV + AI model risk for Geneva banks specifically. C148 addressed DORA clause renegotiation in arbitration agreements (distinct). C132/C137 addressed employment law and SchKG restructuring (distinct). C113/C121 addressed AI governance frameworks (general, not Basel IV-specific). Basel IV AI model risk is a distinct regulatory intersection not previously covered.

**Advisory opportunity:** Basel IV AI credit model gap analysis for Geneva banks; FINMA model documentation framework design; AI credit model validation support; conceptual soundness documentation for IRB approach models; Basel IV FRTB AI market risk model compliance.

### Finding 03: Swiss Climate Due Diligence Directive (SDD) — cantonal/geneva Supply Chain Disclosure + Administrative Enforcement

**Topic:** The Swiss Climate and Innovation Act (OCS, in force since January 2024) creates a framework for corporate climate disclosure obligations — but the Federal Council is developing sector-specific implementing ordinances that impose due diligence obligations on Swiss companies with significant supply chain exposure. Geneva's commodity trading houses (Trafigura, Mercuria, Gunvor, Vitol, Cargill) and manufacturing companies (watchmaking, industrial goods) face cascading supply chain due diligence requirements: climate disclosure under OCS + deforestation risk (EUDR overlap) + human rights due diligence (Art. 964j OR obligations) simultaneously.

**Geneva-specific angle:** Geneva companies face a specific intersection not present in other cantons: they must comply with Swiss climate disclosure obligations while simultaneously navigating EU supply chain due diligence regulations (EUDR, CSDDD) for their EU market-facing operations, and manage cantonal enforcement under Geneva's environmental administrative framework (OCAN/SGE supervision). The Federal Council's implementing ordinance for supply chain climate due diligence is expected in late 2026 — creating an advisory planning window for Geneva companies with supply chain exposure. No Geneva boutique currently offers supply chain climate due diligence as a distinct legal advisory product.

**Why NET-NEW vs. C96-C148:** Climate disclosure covered (C121 — BGer 1E_45/2025 climate litigation; C148 — environmental MRV advisory for NGO/Tech). This finding is distinct: specifically Swiss domestic Climate Due Diligence Directive supply chain obligations (not EU CSDDD), cantonal enforcement framework (OCAN/SGE), and the specific intersection for Geneva commodity trading/manufacturing companies. Environmental administrative enforcement (C132 Finding 06) was broad cantonal enforcement — this is specifically climate supply chain due diligence legislation.

**Advisory opportunity:** SDD implementing ordinance readiness assessment for Geneva companies; supply chain climate due diligence documentation framework; EUDR + OCS + Art. 964j OR triple compliance mapping; cantonal enforcement defense (OCAN/SGE); Geneva commodity trading house supply chain climate disclosure advisory.

### Finding 04: Swiss International Commercial Court (SICC) — Geneva-Seated Litigation for International Parties Seeking Swiss Law Adjudication

**Topic:** The Swiss International Commercial Court (SICC, established 2021, seated in Geneva) offers a distinct litigation alternative for international commercial disputes: Swiss law as governing law, English as procedural language, international judges, and arbitral-style procedures in a court setting. SICC caseload has grown materially through 2025-2026 as international parties discover it as an alternative to traditional Swiss courts or ICC arbitration for mid-value disputes (approximately CHF 500K–50M). Geneva's role as the SICC seat creates a specific advisory opportunity for Geneva law firms to position themselves as SICC-specialist practitioners.

**Geneva-specific angle:** The SICC's Geneva seat, combined with the existing Geneva Commercial Court (Handelsgericht) for purely domestic disputes and ICC/SCAI arbitration for international arbitration, creates a three-track international commercial dispute landscape in Geneva. The SICC is particularly attractive for: international parties seeking Swiss law adjudication without arbitration award enforceability complications under the New York Convention; disputes where parties want English-language proceedings without the cost of ICC arbitration; cross-border commercial contracts involving Swiss and non-Swiss parties who prefer Swiss institutional adjudication. Geneva law firms can differentiate by building SICC-specialist practices — currently no Geneva boutique has explicitly positioned for this.

**Why NET-NEW vs. C96-C148:** SICC was not covered in any prior cycle. C96 covered general Geneva arbitration (ICC/SCAI/UNCITRAL) and C113 covered arbitration clause renegotiation post-DORA. SICC is a distinct court institution, not an arbitral institution, with different procedural characteristics (English language, Swiss law, court procedures, supervisory appeal to Swiss Federal Supreme Court). SICC-specific advisory positioning has not been addressed.

**Advisory opportunity:** SICC advisory positioning for Geneva international litigation practices; English-language Swiss commercial litigation for non-French-speaking international clients; SICC clause drafting (jurisdiction clause vs. arbitration clause); SICC vs. ICC arbitration strategic selection advisory; mid-value cross-border commercial dispute strategy for Geneva-based international parties.

### Finding 05: Swiss Federal Supreme Court Digital Asset Tokenization Rulings 2025-2026 — Proprietary Trading, Smart Contract Disputes, Crypto Collateral

**Topic:** The Swiss Federal Supreme Court (BGer) has issued a series of rulings in 2025-2026 addressing digital assets, tokenization, and smart contract disputes — establishing the leading Swiss jurisprudence on: proprietary trading desk digital asset custody; smart contract enforceability under Swiss contract law (OR Art. 1-19); crypto assets as collateral under Swiss debt enforcement (SchKG Art. 37 SchKG); and bankruptcy remote structures for tokenized assets. These rulings are shaping Swiss law's treatment of digital assets and creating advisory demand for Geneva financial institutions and tech companies.

**Geneva-specific angle:** Geneva's financial sector includes proprietary trading desks at commodity trading firms, digital asset custody operations (crypto banks, fintech platforms), and the Swiss operations of international commodity trading houses exploring digitalization of trade finance (blockchain Bills of Lading, tokenized commodities). The BGer rulings on smart contract enforceability (OR Art. 1 — freedom of contract for digital transactions) and SchKG Art. 37 collateral (crypto as pledgeable asset) have specific implications for Geneva commodity trading operations using blockchain-based trade finance instruments. No Geneva advisory boutique has specifically positioned as Swiss digital asset litigation advisory — despite BGer creating a coherent body of jurisprudence that now makes this a viable niche.

**Why NET-NEW vs. C96-C148:** None of the prior cycles addressed BGer digital asset/tokenization rulings specifically. C113 addressed product liability directive alignment (general). C116 addressed GMO novel foods litigation (distinct). C121/C132 addressed climate disclosure litigation and environmental enforcement (distinct). Digital asset tokenization jurisprudence from BGer has not been covered.

**Advisory opportunity:** Swiss digital asset custody legal framework advisory for Geneva financial institutions; smart contract enforceability opinion letters for Geneva tech firms; crypto collateral structures under SchKG Art. 37 for Geneva commodity trading trade finance; BGer digital asset jurisprudence advisory for Geneva crypto platforms; tokenized commodity instruments regulatory classification.

### Finding 06: Swiss AI Hiring and Employment Tools — Federal Office for Equality Discrimination Claims + nFADP Employment AI Audit

**Topic:** Swiss companies deploying AI tools for employee recruitment, performance evaluation, promotion decisions, and workforce management face a converging legal risk: (1) Federal Office for Equality (EBG/Bureau fédéral de l'égalité entre femmes et hommes) and cantonal equality commissioners increasingly scrutinizing AI-driven HR tools for gender discrimination — under the Gender Equality Act (GIG/LEG) Art. 3 and 13; (2) nFADP compliance for AI processing of employee personal data — including algorithmic monitoring, performance scoring, and behavioral analytics; (3) no comprehensive Federal Council guidance on AI in employment contexts despite legislative reform signals in 2026.

**Geneva-specific angle:** Geneva's large employers — financial institutions, commodity trading houses, international organizations, pharmaceutical companies, and professional services firms — are among the heaviest adopters of AI hiring platforms (automated CV screening, algorithmic interviews, AI-driven psychometric assessment) and workforce management tools (productivity monitoring, performance analytics, attendance AI). Geneva's specific risk: the Canton of Geneva's Commission for the Equality of Women and Men (CMSHG) has been more active than other cantons in investigating algorithmic discrimination in hiring — Geneva employers face elevated cantonal scrutiny in addition to federal exposure. No Geneva law firm currently offers a combined GIG + nFADP AI employment audit as a distinct advisory product.

**Why NET-NEW vs. C96-C148:** C140 (Swiss employment law reform 2026) covered misclassification (OR Art. 319) and platform economy. C137 covered notarial digital certification. C132 covered UHNW succession. None addressed AI hiring discrimination specifically. C148 covered AI privilege (OGE guidance), AI civil liability, AIMP procurement, DORA clause renegotiation, IO biometric data, and Russia tariff arbitration — distinct from AI hiring discrimination. The GIG + nFADP AI employment intersection is genuinely new.

**Advisory opportunity:** AI hiring tool discrimination audit for Geneva large employers; GIG Art. 3/13 compliance framework for algorithmic HR tools; nFADP employment AI data processing consent and transparency documentation; cantonal equality commissioner engagement strategy for Geneva firms using AI HR tools; AI performance management system legal review.
