# Compliance Context: Geneva/Switzerland Specific — AI Accountability

## Regulatory Landscape for Boutique Wealth Management

---

### 1. FINMA Guidance 08/2024 (December 18, 2024)

**What it is:** FINMA's supervisory guidance on AI governance and risk management for Swiss financial institutions.

**Key obligations relevant to Geneva boutiques:**

- **AI Inventory Requirement:** Supervised institutions must maintain a centralized inventory of all AI applications, classified by materiality and risk likelihood. This is the most immediate operational requirement.

- **Governance Framework:** Clear responsibilities for AI development, implementation, monitoring, and use must be defined and integrated into existing governance structures.

- **Model Risk Management:** AI systems must be assessed for lack of robustness, correctness, bias, and explainability. This applies to any AI application — purchased or in-house.

- **Data Quality:** Data used in AI systems must be of sufficient quality to prevent inaccuracies and biases. Client portfolio data governance falls directly here.

- **Testing and Continuous Monitoring:** AI systems require ongoing testing and monitoring. Users ignoring or modifying AI outputs must be analyzed as a potential indicator of system weakness.

- **Third-Party Risk:** Institutions using external AI providers (cloud, SaaS AI tools, vendor-provided AI features) must conduct due diligence and manage outsourcing risk.

- **Human Oversight:** The responsibility for AI-driven decisions cannot be delegated to AI or third parties. A human must remain accountable.

- **Documentation:** AI applications and their risk classifications must be documented. Documentation must be sufficiently detailed and recipient-oriented.

- **Independent Review:** Development and independent review of AI applications should be separated; few institutions currently do this properly.

**Proportionality note:** FINMA explicitly states its approach is risk-proportionate — obligations scale with the materiality and risk profile of the AI application. A boutique wealth manager using one AI-powered CRM tool is not held to the same standard as a bank running dozens of AI models. But the inventory and governance requirements still apply.

**Supervisory activity:** FINMA began on-site inspections and supervisory exchanges on AI from 2023. A survey of ~400 Swiss financial institutions ran from November 2024 to January 2025. Results published April 2025.

### 2. Self-Regulatory Organization (SRO) Framework

**Key SROs for Geneva wealth management:**
- **Asset Management Association Switzerland (AMAS)** — Swiss Fund and Asset Management Association
- **Swiss Banking Ombudsman** — dispute resolution
- **Various SROs recognized by FINMA** for anti-money laundering and organizational oversight

**Current SRO status on AI:**
- No SRO has issued specific AI governance guidance for wealth management firms as of early 2025
- Existing SRO frameworks (organizational requirements, AML, client suitability) are being applied by default to AI contexts
- The vacuum creates ambiguity: boutiques don't know whether existing SRO standards cover AI or whether they need something new
- FINMA expects SRO-supervised institutions to follow FINMA guidance regardless of SRO silence

**Practical implication:** Waiting for SRO guidance is not a safe compliance strategy. FINMA guidance applies now.

### 3. nFADP (revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection)

**Revised nFADP in force since September 1, 2023.**

**Article 63 — Personal Liability:**
- Article 63 introduces the possibility of personal liability for bodies corporate that fail to comply with data protection obligations
- The revised law expands supervisory authority and increases fines
- For managing directors: the question is whether personal liability can attach directly — the law creates liability for "responsible bodies" which include corporate organs
- In practice: if an AI system processes client data improperly and no adequate governance framework exists, the question of who is personally responsible becomes live
- The DPO role becomes more critical; managing directors need to ensure DPO oversight covers AI-specific data risks

**AI-specific data protection considerations:**
- Client portfolio data processed by AI systems requires clear legal basis
- Third-party AI vendors processing client data require data processing agreements (DPAs)
- Cross-border data transfers (e.g., US-based AI vendors) require appropriate safeguards
- Right to explanation for automated decisions (limited in Swiss law but relevant for client-facing AI)

**Key gap:** Most boutiques do not have DPAs with their AI tool vendors, do not have AI-specific data governance policies, and do not have clarity on which AI tools process client personal data.

### 4. EU AI Act — Extraterritorial Reach

**For Geneva boutiques with EU clients or EU operations:**
- The EU AI Act applies to institutions using AI outputs in connection with EU persons, regardless of where the institution is located
- High-risk AI systems (including AI used in credit decisions, insurance risk assessment) face strict requirements under the Act
- Obligations began February 2025 (prohibited AI practices, AI literacy); high-risk system obligations apply August 2026
- FINMA itself recommends institutions consider EU AI Act alignment for operational resilience and regulatory preparedness
- Geneva boutiques with any EU institutional clients are within scope

**Practical implication:** If your AI tool vendor's platform is subject to EU AI Act (which large SaaS AI providers are working toward), your downstream usage is indirectly affected. The attestation requirements will flow upstream to you.

---

### 5. Institutional Client AI Governance Questionnaires

**Not a formal regulation, but a practical compliance pressure:**

- Family offices, pension funds, and foundations are sending AI governance questionnaires as part of vendor due diligence
- These questionnaires are not harmonized — each institution has its own format and questions
- Common topics: AI inventory, data governance, model risk management, third-party oversight, explainability, incident response
- The implied consequence of poor responses: loss of client mandates
- This is effectively a market discipline mechanism that complements formal regulation

---

### 6. Insurance Carrier / Prime Broker Attestations

**Banking and insurance relationships creating attestation pressure:**

- Carriers are adding AI governance attestations to coverage renewals
- Prime brokers may request similar attestations
- The attestation asks for representations about governance that most boutiques cannot currently make
- The legal effect: signing creates representations that could be material if a dispute later arises
- This is a contractual, not regulatory, obligation — but the liability consequences are real

### Key Compliance Gaps for Geneva Boutiques

1. **No AI inventory** — cannot answer the most basic FINMA requirement or institutional client question
2. **No formal AI governance framework** — responsibilities undefined, no risk classification
3. **No data processing agreements** with AI tool vendors — nFADP exposure
4. **No model risk management** — cannot address FINMA's specific expectations on testing and monitoring
5. **No DPO review of AI systems** — AI-specific data protection risks unassessed
6. **No documentation** — cannot demonstrate compliance readiness to FINMA or clients
7. **No human oversight mechanism** — cannot demonstrate accountability chain for AI-driven decisions

The compliance gap is not theoretical. Each of these gaps creates immediate exposure to institutional client questions, carrier attestations, and potential FINMA supervisory engagement.
