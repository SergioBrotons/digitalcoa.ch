# ICP Language: Geneva Boutique Wealth Management — AI Accountability

## How They Talk About AI Problems

### The Framing They Use

When Geneva managing directors and partners talk about AI, they don't use the language of technologists. They use the language of risk, liability, and institutional obligation.

**Their dominant frame:** *"AI is something my clients and regulators are asking me to account for — not something I chose to adopt."*

They experience AI as a pressure coming from two directions:
- **Upward:** Institutional clients sending governance questionnaires; carriers demanding attestation; FINMA's December 2024 guidance raising supervisory expectations
- **Downward:** Team members wanting to use AI tools on client data; vendors pitching AI-powered portfolio tools; competitive pressure to appear modern

The result is a specific kind of anxiety: not resistance to technology, but uncertainty about where personal liability attaches when AI is involved in decisions about someone else's wealth.

---

### Core Language Patterns

**Pattern 1: Accountability-First Language**
They don't ask "how do we implement AI?" They ask "who is responsible when AI gets it wrong?"

Typical phrasing:
- *"If I'm the signatory, am I liable for what the AI recommended?"*
- *"My client sent us a 40-point AI governance questionnaire. I don't know what half of it means."*
- *"The carrier is asking us to attest to AI oversight. What exactly do they want us to say?"*
- *"If FINMA comes in and looks at our AI use, what do I show them?"*

**Pattern 2: Institutional Questionnaire Dread**
Receiving AI governance questionnaires from institutional clients is a specific trigger. They describe it as:
- *"Being put on the spot by people who know more about this than I do"*
- *"A test I didn't study for"*
- *"They want to know about our AI inventory. We don't even have a list."*

**Pattern 3: The Attestation Problem**
Carrier attestation requirements create a specific linguistic pattern. The word "attestation" itself carries weight — it implies personal signature, personal accountability, personal exposure. Phrases like:
- *"I have to sign something saying we have AI governance. We don't."*
- *"What happens if I attest and something goes wrong?"*
- *"The attestation asks if we have model risk management. We don't."*

**Pattern 4: SRO Silence as Anxiety**
The absence of clear SRO guidance is described as:
- *"We keep waiting for the SRO to tell us what to do, and nothing comes."*
- *"The big banks have teams for this. We have me."*
- *"I've asked my SRO twice. They say they're working on it."*

**Pattern 5: Personal Liability Focus**
nFADP Article 63 and personal liability surface in specific ways:
- *"If client data gets misused by an AI system, is it my fault?"*
- *"I keep reading about Art. 63. It sounds like it's aimed at companies, but it could attach to me personally."*
- *"I don't want to be the managing director who signed off on something that created a data breach."*

**Pattern 6: The Portfolio Tool Problem**
Deploying AI on client portfolio data creates a specific vocabulary:
- *"We want to use better tools for our clients, but we don't know how to validate them."*
- *"The vendor says it's 'AI-powered.' What does that actually mean for my data?"*
- *"If I feed client portfolios into an AI system, where does that data go?"*
- *"Our clients are sophisticated. They ask about our AI. What do I tell them?"*

---

### Words They Avoid

They tend not to say:
- "AI strategy" (feels too corporate, too aspirational)
- "AI adoption" (implies they chose it)
- "machine learning" (sounds technical, not relevant to their risk)
- "innovation" (not their frame)

### Words That Land

- "accountability"
- "attestation"
- "governance questionnaire"
- "personal liability"
- "oversight"
- "responsibility"
- "FINMA"
- "institutional client"
- "client data"
- "what do I show them"

---

### Emotional State Summary

Underlying emotional state: **proactive worry** — they know AI governance matters, they know they're behind, they know they're personally exposed, and they're looking for a way to get defensible without having to become technologists.

They want someone to tell them: "Here's what you actually need to have in place, at a level appropriate for a boutique firm, so you can answer the questionnaire, sign the attestation, and sleep at night."
