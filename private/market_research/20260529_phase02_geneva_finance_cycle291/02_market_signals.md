# Market Signals: Geneva Boutique Wealth Management — AI Accountability

## Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

### Problem 1: The Institutional AI Governance Questionnaire Shock

Institutional clients — family offices, pension funds, foundations — are sending increasingly detailed AI governance questionnaires as part of their vendor due diligence. For Geneva boutiques, this is a new category of exposure.

**What they're experiencing:**
- Questionnaires arriving with 30–60 questions covering AI inventory, model risk management, data governance, third-party AI oversight, explainability, and human oversight mechanisms
- No internal inventory of AI tools exists to answer these questions
- Legal teams advising them not to guess at answers, but the pressure to respond is real
- Some institutional clients hinting that AI governance readiness will become a gating factor for continued mandates
- boutiques feeling they are being judged by standards set for large banks with dedicated compliance teams

**The specific failure mode:** They cannot answer the basic threshold question — "do you maintain an inventory of AI applications used in your service to us?" — with yes.

---

### Problem 2: Carrier Attestation Anxiety

Insurance carriers and prime brokers are beginning to require AI governance attestations as part of coverage and relationship renewals. This is distinct from regulatory requirements.

**What they're experiencing:**
- Attestations asking them to confirm they have AI governance frameworks, model risk management, and third-party oversight
- No framework exists internally to validate these claims
- The word "attestation" implies personal signature — typically the managing director's
- Uncertainty about what happens if they attest and a problem later occurs
- Some carriers sending checklists that feel like audits without the benefit of audit guidance

**The specific failure mode:** Signing attestations they cannot substantiate. The risk is not just regulatory — it's the liability exposure created by claiming controls that don't exist.

---

### Problem 3: FINMA Guidance 08/2024 Compliance Uncertainty

FINMA published Guidance 08/2024 in December 2024. For most Geneva boutiques, the reaction was: "we've seen this, we don't know what to do with it."

**What they're experiencing:**
- The guidance is principles-based, not prescriptive — it tells them to have governance but doesn't specify what "good" looks like at boutique scale
- The requirement to maintain an AI inventory is creating immediate practical problems: they don't know what counts as AI vs. basic automation
- Data quality requirements feel relevant but abstract — "garbage in, garbage out" is obvious, but they have no framework to assess whether their data is sufficient
- The guidance asks for model risk management. They don't know what that means operationally for their firm.
- On-site inspections and supervisory exchanges are described as already happening at larger institutions; boutiques fear they are next

**The specific failure mode:** Awareness without action. They know the guidance exists. They know it applies to them. They don't know where to start.

---

### Problem 4: SRO Guidance Void

Self-regulatory organizations (SROs) in Switzerland have not issued specific AI guidance for wealth management firms. This silence is creating its own problems.

**What they're experiencing:**
- Waiting for SRO guidance that doesn't arrive
- Uncertainty about whether existing SRO frameworks (designed for traditional wealth management) apply to AI or need to be supplemented
- No sector-specific interpretation of FINMA's principles for boutique wealth management
- The sense that larger institutions are building internal frameworks while boutiques are frozen

**The specific failure mode:** Paralysis. Without an SRO standard to point to, boutiques have no external benchmark for what "compliant" looks like.

---

### Problem 5: Personal Liability Under nFADP Article 63

The revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) introduces Article 63 with personal liability provisions. For managing directors, this is a specific anxiety.

**What they're experiencing:**
- Confusion about whether Article 63 can attach personal liability to managing directors, not just the firm
- AI systems processing client portfolio data without clear data governance frameworks — if a breach or misuse occurs, who is responsible?
- The sense that they signed off on AI tool adoption without understanding the data protection implications
- Uncertainty about whether their existing DPO arrangements cover AI-specific data risks

**The specific failure mode:** Retroactive exposure. They adopted tools, they're using client data in AI systems, and only now are they realizing the liability structure may hold them personally responsible.

---

### Problem 6: The Vendor Complexity Problem

AI tool vendors are pitching to Geneva boutiques with confidence. The boutiques lack the technical depth to evaluate the claims.

**What they're experiencing:**
- Vendors describing their tools as "AI-powered" without explaining what that means for data handling
- Portfolio analytics tools, CRM AI features, document processing AI — all entering the workflow without formal evaluation
- No framework to assess: is this AI or just automation? Does it matter for governance purposes?
- Clients asking about AI and the boutiques having no prepared answer
- Resistance to saying "we don't use AI" because it sounds behind the times, but equal resistance to claiming AI sophistication they don't have

**The specific failure mode:** Accumulated shadow AI. Over 2–3 years of organic AI adoption through existing software (Office 365 AI features, Bloomberg AI add-ons, CRM AI) without any governance framework.
