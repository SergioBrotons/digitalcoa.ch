## The AI Governance Gap in Medical Practices

The AXA KMU-Arbeitsmarktstudie 2025 found: 67% of Swiss SMEs already using AI, only 33% with formal governance rules. Medical practices are SMEs. The governance gap is universal.

In medical practices, the gap is compounded by a specific risk: **57% of AI tools in healthcare settings have been found to produce clinically significant errors or hallucinations** — AI-generated clinical information that is inaccurate, misleading, or potentially harmful.

This is not a technology problem. It is a governance problem. The practices that are managing AI risk effectively are the ones with documented procedures for when and how AI is used in clinical decision-making.

## The Envelope Test for Medical Practices

Safe (automating the envelope):
- Appointment scheduling and patient reminder systems
- Patient intake form routing and formatting
- Billing preparation and insurance claim formatting
- Laboratory result routing to the appropriate physician
- Administrative document management

Not safe without DPA, DPIA, and clinical oversight (automating the letter):
- AI-assisted clinical decision-making or diagnostic suggestions
- Summarizing patient histories or clinical notes
- Generating or editing patient communication about clinical matters
- AI analysis of medical imaging or laboratory data
- Automated triage or patient routing based on clinical presentation

## The FADP in Medical Practice: Patient Data Is the Highest-Risk Category

Medical practice patient data is the most sensitive personal data category under the FADP. The revised FADP (in force September 1, 2023) classifies health and genetic data as specially protected categories.

FADP obligations in medical practice:
- **Art. 5(c)**: Patient data may only be used for the declared purpose. AI tools that process patient data for purposes beyond the original clinical purpose may breach this principle.
- **Art. 21**: Patients have the right to request human review of any automated decision affecting their care.
- **DPIA (Art. 22)**: AI processing of patient data is almost certainly high-risk and requires a formal DPIA.
- **Privacy by Design**: Data protection must be considered before any AI tool is deployed — not after.

Criminal penalties: up to CHF 250,000 for individuals. For medical practitioners, the personal exposure is immediate.

## The Three-Layer Liability Stack for Medical Practitioners

**Layer 1 — FADP Compliance Obligation**
Already operative. Patient data is the highest-sensitivity category under the FADP. The obligations are not future obligations — they are already in force.

**Layer 2 — Professional Liability**
As a physician, you bear personal responsibility for clinical decisions — AI-assisted or not. If AI contributes to a misdiagnosis, an inappropriate treatment recommendation, or a patient safety incident, you cannot deflect responsibility to the AI vendor. Swiss medical malpractice standards apply to the clinical decision, regardless of the tool used to reach it.

**Layer 3 — Swissdamed Compliance**
Swissdamed non-compliance by July 1, 2026 may create billing and referral access problems. Swissdamed-related patient data governance failures create a third exposure layer distinct from FADP.

## A Minimal Viable AI Governance Framework for Medical Practices

1. **AI Tool Inventory with Swissdamed Compatibility Check**: List every AI tool in use, what patient data it accesses, and whether it is Swissdamed-compatible
2. **Patient Data DPA Register**: DPAs with every vendor processing patient data; confirm Swiss or EU residency
3. **Clinical AI Use Protocol**: Document when and how AI is used in clinical decision-making; ensure every AI-assisted clinical output has human physician review
4. **Swissdamed Readiness Assessment**: Is your practice Swissdamed-ready by July 1? Assess AI tool compatibility as part of this readiness.
5. **DPIA for All AI Tools Processing Patient Data**: Any AI tool accessing patient data requires a formal DPIA given the high-risk classification of health data under the FADP.
