## Primary Market Signals

**1. Carrier Attestation Dematerialization — AI-Assisted Form Completion Creates New Exposure Loop**
Carriers and broker platforms are introducing AI-assisted form completion tools. The Geneva firm is using AI to answer the carrier's AI attestation. The form completion tool processes matter descriptions, associate tool lists, supervision protocols — exactly the categories of information the form is asking about. New exposure vector: the AI tool used to complete the form creates the same Article 321 / privilege / supervision exposures the form is asking about. The loop is closed. The managing partner doesn't see it because the form-completion tool feels like the carrier's tool, not their own AI exposure.

**2. Summer Associate Authorization Discontinuity — New Hires Create Temporal Authorization Gap**
July 1 associate arrival creates the most concrete, time-stamped documentation gap in the Geneva boutique market. New associates begin using AI tools on their first week. The firm's authorization documentation reflects pre-arrival authorization state. The handover window (the weeks when a leaver's tools are still active and the new arrival's tools are not yet tracked) creates an authorization chain that is structurally out of date within days of the new associate's start date. Every annual cycle produces this gap. The C313 ICP is encountering it now.

**3. Vendor Data Residency as False Reassurance — Swiss-Hosting Doesn't Resolve Article 321**
The Geneva firm that moved to a Swiss-hosted AI vendor believes they have resolved their Article 321 exposure. They have not. The vendor's sub-processors (logging, telemetry, auth) still operate extraterritorially. The vendor's parent company may be US-headquartered and subject to CLOUD Act extraterritorial access. The metadata (timestamps, user identifiers, query classifications) routes through cloud infrastructure that doesn't honor Swiss borders. Article 321 looks through the hosting claim. The firm has a documented misconception that requires correction.

**4. Mid-Year Carrier Check-Ins — Documentation Aged Within Days**
Mid-year carrier check-ins are now standard for professional liability coverage in Geneva. The carrier is asking questions between renewals. The ICP's documentation is dated within days of the previous renewal. The mid-year check-in produces a documented evidence point that the documentation is not current. The ICP treats the check-in as anomalous. It is structural. The gap between policy documentation cycle and carrier check-in cycle widens with every mid-year intervention.

**5. Public Attestation Visibility — Peer Signatures Are Now Public**
Geneva bar conference season (June 2026) produced public disclosures of what individual firms attested to in their carrier forms. The managing partner now has visibility into what specific peer firms declared. They are discovering their attestation was less specific, less defensible, and less well-documented than their peers'. The comparison trap from C310 has now moved from peer inference to direct peer disclosure. The gap is visible in writing. Firms that didn't sign as much as peers are now living with that visibility.

**6. AI Vendor Succession Exposure — Bankruptcy / Acquisition / Termination Creates Article 321 Continuity Problem**
The AI vendor may go bankrupt, get acquired, terminate the service, or pivot the offering. When this happens, the firm's client matter data — attorney work product, privileged communications, research notes — transfers with the vendor's asset sale to a new controller who did not negotiate the firm's Article 321 obligations. The firm is now in privity with a new data controller without their consent. Article 321 doesn't terminate on vendor insolvency. The succession gap is unnamed in the market and unrepresented in current vendor contracts.

## Failure Modes (C313 Specific)

**Form-completion loop failure:** Firm uses AI to complete carrier form. AI processes matter descriptions through vendor servers. The attestation answer creates the exposure the form is asking about. Renewal finds firm has been answering the form with the exposure they attested against.

**Authorization handover failure:** New associate starts using AI on day one. Managing partner doesn't authorize specific tools. Carrier mid-year check-in asks for "authorization documentation for all AI tools used in the firm in the last 90 days." Managing partner cannot produce documentation for the new associate's tool selection. Personal liability crystallizes.

**Residency assumption failure:** Firm selects Swiss-hosted vendor on residency grounds. Vendor discloses — in fine print, after selection — that sub-processors operate extraterritorially and parent company is subject to CLOUD Act. Firm believes they made Article 321-compliant selection. Article 321 obligation is breached by the selection itself. Firm carries the professional secrecy obligation they believed they satisfied.

**Mid-year check-in failure:** Firm completes annual attestation in February. Carrier sends check-in in July. Documentation is five months old. Check-in asks "what changed in the last 90 days." Firm has no documentation of changes in the last 90 days. Carrier checks the documentation cycle as part of risk assessment.

**Peer disclosure failure:** Firm's attestation was binary, peer firm's was specific. Bar conference publishes summary. Client notes the difference. Client asks firm why their attestation was less specific. Firm has no calibrated response.

**Vendor succession failure:** Vendor files for bankruptcy protection. Asset purchaser acquires client matter data as part of asset sale. Firm's clients are now being processed by a successor who didn't inherit the firm's obligations. Article 321 doesn't terminate. Firm is professionally responsible for data protection the successor isn't providing.

## The C313 Structural Problem

The C312 structural problem was: the carrier form created documented standards at the moment of signing, but the evidence to support those standards was never created.

The C313 structural problem adds six new layers to that foundation:

1. The form is now being answered by AI, creating a loop exposure
2. New hires create authorization gaps within weeks of arrival
3. Swiss hosting creates a documented Article 321 misconception
4. Mid-year carrier interventions make documentation within days old
5. Peer signatures are publicly visible, making comparison direct
6. Vendor exit creates succession exposure no current contract addresses

The C313 Geneva legal ICP is not facing the same problem they faced in C312. They are facing the same problem with six new dimensions layered on top.

The service that addresses all twelve exposures — the six C312 layers plus the six C313 layers — is the C313 service. The service that addresses only the original six is the C312 service with a fresh paint job.

## Why the 31-Day Pause Matters

The pause between C312 and C313 (May 31 → July 1) was not market stasis. It was market shift masked as market pause. Three things happened in the pause:

- Summer associate hiring cycle started (July 1 hire dates are common at Geneva firms)
- Bar conference season produced public peer attestation disclosures
- The Swiss Federal Council consultation moved into submission phase, with bar-association position submissions becoming visible

The ICP returning from the pause doesn't see these as new forces. They see them as "things that happened while I wasn't paying attention." The C313 service is the service that names what happened during the pause.
