### Finding 01: Form-Completion Vector Exposure — Attestation Loop

**The finding:** The carrier is now offering (directly or through broker platforms) AI-assisted form completion. The Geneva firm is using the form-completion tool to draft responses to the attestation by entering matter descriptions, associate tool lists, supervision summaries — exactly the categories of information the form is asking about. The form-completion tool processes this through its own AI vendor's servers. The attestation answer is now being produced through a vendor the firm has not separately contracted with, with data flows the firm's Article 321 review has not addressed. The exposure loop is closed: the AI tool used to answer the form creates the same Article 321 / privilege / supervision exposure the form is asking about.

**Mechanism:** Carrier offers AI form-completion tool → firm enters matter details → form-completion vendor processes descriptions through its AI → answers generated → form signed → carrier form architecture now incorporates content that was processed through a separately-disclosed vendor → Article 321 vector is doubled.

**Implication:** Form-completion vendor review is a discrete, named service. It audits which form-completion tool the firm uses, what data that tool processes, whether its terms create Article 321 exposure, and what the alternative is (answering the form directly with the firm's own evidence architecture). The first service to publish the form-completion vector review owns the category. The market will reach for this service the moment a carrier asks "what tool did you use to complete our form?"

**Ready-to-use language:** "The AI tool you used to complete the carrier form creates the same Article 321 exposure the form is asking about. The attestation answer now passes through the very vendor the attestation covers. I'm showing you how to break the loop."

### Finding 02: Summer Associate Authorization Discontinuity — The Temporal Authorization Gap

**The finding:** July 1 associate arrival at Geneva boutiques creates the most concrete, time-stamped authorization gap in the legal market. New associates begin using AI tools on day one (some before their first client matter). The authorization documentation reflects pre-arrival state. The firm cannot produce, on demand, the authorization status for the new associates' tool selection — because the documentation was written before the new arrivals were onboarded. The handover window also includes leavers: their AI tools are deactivated (sometimes), but their AI-generated work product remains in the firm's matters. Article 321 obligations begin when data flows, not when contracts are reviewed — and data flows the moment a new associate opens an AI tool with a client matter.

**Mechanism:** New associate starts (July 1) → opens AI tool with first matter → authorization documentation reflects pre-arrival state → carrier mid-year check-in (typically June/July) asks "what is your current AI authorization documentation for all firm personnel in last 90 days" → firm cannot produce → personal liability crystallizes at personnel-change cadence the firm did not document at.

**Implication:** Authorization documentation tied to personnel-change cadence — not annual attestation cadence. The service produces authorization documentation refreshed at the rate of personnel change, with documented handover for both arrivals and leavers. This is the discrete, named service the C313 ICP needs and no competitor has built.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your authorization documentation was written before your new associates arrived. Their AI use is your liability. The documentation cycle is annual; the personnel cycle is faster. I'm refreshing the authorization chain at the cadence your firm actually changes."

### Finding 03: Vendor Data Residency as False Reassurance — The Sub-Processor Lens

**The finding:** The Geneva firm that selected a "Swiss-hosted" AI vendor on residency grounds has a documented Article 321 misconception. Article 321 is not satisfied by data residency alone. The Swiss-hosted vendor's sub-processors (logging, telemetry, authentication, monitoring) operate extraterritorially. The vendor's parent company's cloud infrastructure (when non-Swiss) inherits CLOUD Act extraterritorial access. The metadata — query classifications, user identifiers, timestamps — routes through infrastructure that doesn't honor Swiss borders. The residency claim is a marketing claim. The firm's Article 321 obligation reads through the marketing.

**Mechanism:** Firm selects Swiss-hosted vendor on residency grounds → vendor's terms disclose sub-processors (extraterritorially) → metadata routes through parent-company infrastructure → firm believes Article 321 satisfied → exposure persists → carrier asks vendor contract review → vendor disclosure reveals sub-processor chain → Article 321 exposure visible → firm's documented misconception is documented.

**Implication:** Swiss-hosted vendor sub-processor audit is a discrete service. It maps the vendor's sub-processor chain (logging, telemetry, auth, monitoring) against Article 321 requirements, identifies which sub-processors create extraterritorial exposure, and produces a sub-processor audit memo the firm can attach to its authorization documentation. The service is bounded, named, and triggerable at any vendor review or new vendor selection moment.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your Swiss-hosted vendor still routes metadata through U.S. cloud infrastructure. Article 321 reads through the marketing. I'm auditing the sub-processors your vendor didn't disclose at the front of the contract."

### Finding 04: Mid-Year Carrier Check-In Documentation Truncation — Between-Renewal Staleness

**The finding:** Mid-year carrier check-ins are now structural in professional liability coverage. Carriers send structured inquiries between annual renewals — typically June/July for many Geneva carriers, matching the post-pause timing. The Geneva firm's documentation is dated within days of the prior annual renewal. The mid-year check-in asks "what changed in the last 90 days" — the firm's documentation cycle is annual, but the check-in cadence is now twice-yearly. Documentation aged within 90 days is the new freshness standard. Firms treating documentation as annual are now visibly behind at mid-year check-in moments. The gap widens with every check-in. By year-end the firm is two formal evidence points behind the carrier's question.

**Mechanism:** Firm completes annual attestation (e.g., February) → carrier sends mid-year check-in (June/July) → documentation is 4-5 months old → check-in asks "what changed in last 90 days" → firm has no documentation of changes in last 90 days → carrier checks documentation cycle as part of risk assessment → annual documentation cycle now structurally behind mid-year check-in cadence.

**Implication:** Mid-year documentation refresh is a discrete service. It produces documentation aged within days, refreshed quarterly, updated at personnel change and vendor change moments, designed for the mid-year check-in request. This is the documentation cycle the C313 ICP needs and no current service delivers. The first service to publish a mid-year documentation refresh component owns the C313 mid-year category.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your carrier is now asking twice a year. Your documentation is annual. You're behind at every mid-year check-in. I'm refreshing the documentation at the cadence your carrier actually asks."

### Finding 05: Peer Signature Visibility — The Bar-Conference Benchmark

**The finding:** The June 2026 Geneva bar-conference season produced public disclosures of attestation content from named Geneva firms (with firm consent). The managing partner now has visibility into what specific peer firms declared — not inference from peer-group averages, but direct reading of named-firm attestation language. They are discovering their attestation was less specific, less defensible, and less well-formed than what named peers produced. The comparison trap (C310 Finding 02) has moved from peer inference to direct peer disclosure. The gap is now visible in writing. The firm carries the visibility whether they want it or not.

**Mechanism:** Geneva bar-conference (June 2026) → named firms consent to disclosure of attestation content → public summary published → competitors, clients, and peers read each other's statements → managing partner reads peer A's attestation (specific, defensible, well-formed) → managing partner reads their own attestation (binary, brief, less documented) → gap visible in writing → visibility creates pressure on next attestation round.

**Implication:** Peer-attestation gap analysis is a discrete service. It maps the firm's current attestation against the public benchmark, identifies specific gaps in specificity, defensibility, and formation, and produces a documented comparison the firm can use to update their attestation position before the next carrier cycle. The service is peer-benchmarked, documented, and bounded — a discrete, named C313 offering.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You saw what your peer firms declared. The benchmark is now public. I'm mapping the gap between your attestation and theirs — in writing, with documented recommendations for closing it."

### Finding 06: AI Vendor Succession / Data Portability — The Continuity Gap

**The finding:** The AI vendor may go bankrupt, get acquired by a larger player, terminate the service, or pivot the offering. When this happens, the firm's client matter data — attorney work product, privileged communications, research notes, draft memoranda, AI conversation history — transfers with the vendor's asset sale to a new controller who did not negotiate the firm's Article 321 obligations. The firm is now in privity with a new data controller without their consent. Article 321 doesn't terminate on vendor insolvency — the obligation transfers to the legal entity that holds it (the firm), and the firm becomes accountable for the data's protection downstream of the asset transfer. This exposure is unnamed in vendor contracts, unspecified in carrier attestations, and unaddressed by any current service.

**Mechanism:** Vendor files for bankruptcy / signs asset purchase / terminates service → client matter data transfers with asset sale to new controller → new controller did not inherit Article 321 obligations → firm's clients are now being processed by a successor who didn't inherit the firm's professional secrecy requirements → Article 321 obligation transfers to firm (still legal entity) → firm is professionally responsible for data protection the successor isn't providing → carrier learns of vendor exit → carrier attestation now includes vendor-exit clause firm cannot satisfy.

**Implication:** Vendor succession / data portability planning is a discrete service. It includes: pre-vendor-selection succession terms in vendor contracts (what happens on insolvency, acquisition, termination — including data export obligations, transition period, controller-change disclosure); mid-vendor-relationship succession monitoring (vendor financial signals, acquisition signals, service-termination signals); post-vendor-exit response (data portability execution, controller-change documentation, successor-controller audit). The service is novel, discrete, and triggerable at any vendor exit signal. The first service to publish a vendor succession methodology owns the category.

**Ready-to-use language:** "When your AI vendor goes bankrupt or gets acquired, your client data transfers to the buyer. Article 321 doesn't transfer with it. Your obligations stay. I'm building the succession terms into your vendor contracts now, before the exit happens."
