## English

### The Post-Pause ICP: "I Thought Things Had Calmed Down"

They hadn't.

The pause — the four weeks where the Geneva legal market stopped actively asking about AI governance — wasn't recovery. It was the summer associate hiring season starting, mid-year carrier check-ins arriving, and the Swiss Federal Council's AI consultation submissions being published. The market didn't pause. The market moved into a different phase.

Now the managing partner is returning from the pause and the world has moved without them. New associates. New carrier questions. New vendor claims. New peer disclosures. The same exposure, but with new mechanisms creating it.

**The pause betrayed them. The market kept moving.**

### The Language They Use (July 2026 register)

**"My carrier sent something else. Are these check-ins normal now?"**
Mid-year carrier interventions. The carrier that sent the annual attestation is now sending mid-year inquiries — the documentation gap is being measured between renewals, not just at renewal. The managing partner didn't expect another attestation request in the same year. They treat it as anomalous. It isn't. It's a new cycle.

**"I'm using AI to fill in the carrier form. Is that a problem?"**
Form-completion dematerialization. The managing partner has started using AI tools to draft responses to the carrier attestation itself. They enter matter descriptions, associate tool lists, and supervision summaries into AI to produce carrier-ready answers. The carrier forms are now being answered with the same AI exposure they're attesting about. The loop is closed. The exposure has been doubled.

**"I switched to a Swiss-hosted AI vendor. I thought that fixed the professional secrecy issue."**
The residency fallacy. The Geneva firm that moved from a US-hosted AI vendor to a Swiss-hosted one because they read about Swiss data residency believes they have resolved their Article 321 exposure. They haven't. The sub-processors still extraterritorially transit. The metadata still routes through US-headquartered parent companies' logging infrastructure. The data residency claim is a marketing claim. Article 321 looks through it.

**"We just hired two new associates. They started using AI tools the first week."**
Authorization handover discontinuity. The summer associate cycle creates a documented gap in the firm's authorization chain. New associates begin using AI tools immediately. The managing partner has not authorized the specific tools. The authorization documentation reflects pre-arrival tools, not post-arrival tools. The gap is temporal and structural — every year, every new associate cycle, the documentation becomes stale.

**"I saw what three other Geneva firms declared at the bar conference. I didn't sign something like that."**
Public attestation visibility. Bar-conference season has produced public declarations of what Geneva firms attested to. The managing partner now has visibility into what peers actually said. They are discovering their attestation was less specific, less defensible, less well-formed than what other firms produced. The comparison trap (C310 Finding 02) has moved from inference to direct comparison. The gap is now visible in writing.

**"What happens to my client data if my AI vendor disappears?"**
Vendor succession exposure. The vendor question now includes the worst-case scenario: what happens when the AI vendor goes bankrupt, gets acquired, or terminates the service? The data the firm has been processing — client matter details, attorney work product, privileged communications — transfers with the vendor's asset sale. Article 321 obligations don't terminate on vendor insolvency. The new controller inherits client matter data without inheriting the firm's professional secrecy obligations. The succession gap is unnamed in the market.

## The Emotional State (C313 Update)

The Geneva legal ICP at C313 is not worried about what they were worried about in C312. They are worried about:

- The form being answered by AI creating a loop they didn't realize
- New hires creating new AI surface they didn't authorize
- Their "Swiss-hosted" vendor not being what they thought
- Mid-year carrier interventions they didn't expect
- Peer disclosures making their attestation look light
- The vendor disappearing and leaving their data behind

The C312 anxiety (carrier form architecture, post-attestation reconstruction, unauthorized delegation, OaF two-tier, vendor compliance, "adequate" paralysis) is still present. But it is layered under six new C313 anxieties. The ICP is now carrying two sets of concerns simultaneously, and the new ones are the ones that have surfaced post-pause.

The service that names the seven hinge phrases and immediately responds to the specific situation creates the engagement. The C313 ICP doesn't want a general AI governance conversation. They want the C313-specific problem addressed.

### The Trust Formation (C313 Lens)

In a normal cycle, the trust formation goes: institutional → peer → broker → document.

In C313, the trust formation is interrupted by the pause. The peer benchmark is now public, but the managing partner only sees it after they have already signed. The institutional signals (FSN, FDPIC, OaF) are quieter because the survey period is over. The broker referral channel is the same. The document (carrier form) is now being asked twice a year.

The engagement trigger is not a single document. The engagement trigger is the visible peer benchmark. The C313 managing partner who saw a peer's attestation at a bar conference and realized theirs was thinner has the highest conversion probability.

The lead magnet opening that names this: "You saw what other Geneva firms declared. Now you need to know what yours actually committed to."
