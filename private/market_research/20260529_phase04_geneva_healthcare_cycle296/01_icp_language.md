# ICP Language — Phase 04 C296 Geneva Healthcare
## Independent Practitioners, Small Group Practices (2–10), Outpatient Clinics

*How the ICP talks about AI problems — documented from market signals, not invented.*

---

## Primary Accountability Language

**"I'm the one who signs."**
The personal signature is the focal point. Not abstract regulatory exposure — the moment a carrier form arrives, the physician knows their name is on it and the document commits them to something they haven't evaluated.

**"The machine made a recommendation. I made the call."**
This formulation — "machine recommendation, physician decision" — is how independent physicians already frame their AI relationships. It reflects genuine physician self-conception but the documentation doesn't capture the distinction. Records show the outcome, not the recommendation-evaluation-rejection chain.

**"I don't even know what's running in my own practice."**
Incremental activation through software updates and staff-initiated features has created an AI surface the physician did not consciously authorize. This phrase surfaces consistently: not active adoption resistance but passive unawareness of what has accumulated.

**"The patient came in having already asked the AI."**
Consumer AI-generated patient health analyses arriving at consultation are creating a documented pattern. Physicians describe this as tension between clinical independence and patient-provided AI input they didn't request and can't fully evaluate.

**"If something goes wrong, it's my name on the record."**
The medical record as the accountability artifact. Physician accountability is encoded in the record's authority under their name — regardless of what contributed to the content.

**"Swiss hosted" as insufficient answer**
Physicians who have engaged with AI governance questions have absorbed "Swiss hosted" as a sufficient answer to data protection concerns. When pressed further, uncertainty emerges — they sense the answer may not be complete but have no framework to identify what is missing.

## Documentation Problem Language

**"My records don't show what the AI actually did."**
Medical records show conclusions, not recommendation chains. AI-influenced decisions look identical to decisions made without AI assistance. The documentation doesn't capture the differential.

**"I can't prove what I overrode."**
When a physician exercises independent judgment against an AI recommendation — making a different clinical decision — there is no documentation category for that specific moment. The record shows the decision made, not the evaluation performed.

**"Multi-physician practice and nobody owns the AI question."**
Small group practices without practice-level AI governance coordination. Each physician independently activating different tools, different features. No one has enumerated the aggregate AI surface.

**"Incident documentation doesn't have a place for AI."**
Existing incident documentation infrastructure is designed for human clinical decision-making. When an adverse outcome occurs, there is no category for AI system state, AI recommendation at time of event, or physician's AI-influenced vs. independent decision.

## Carrier/Compliance Language

**"The carrier form asks questions I don't know how to answer."**
Attestation questionnaires arriving from carriers. Physicians signing documents committing to "adequate AI governance controls" with no Geneva-calibrated definition of what that means at independent practice scale.

**"I signed something. I don't know what I committed to."**
Post-signature awareness that carrier attestation creates personal liability commitment. No pre-signing review service available in Geneva medical market.

**"The urgency to respond doesn't leave time to do it properly."**
Carrier attestation windows with 2-4 week response requirements. Physicians scrambling to assemble documentation retroactively — vendor-downloaded templates, internet searches — creating documentation that doesn't accurately represent actual AI practice.

**"Training certificates don't map to what I'm actually responsible for."**
AI competency documentation built from vendor training certificates and online course completions. Evidence assembled for compliance purposes that would not survive carrier or FMH substantive challenge.

## Regulatory/Liability Language

**"FMH says oversight, doesn't say what that looks like on a Wednesday."**
FMH human oversight principle stated without operational definition. No documentation requirement, no review frequency, no competency standard. Principle provides no operational endpoint for small independent practice.

**"Art.22 reversal right and medical record integrity — these push in opposite directions."**
nFADP Art.22 patient reversal right creates obligation to reverse AI-informed decisions in records, but medical records cannot be casually altered without clinical process and record integrity obligations. Structural incompatibility with no Geneva protocol.

**"If I'm the last discloseable entity, I'm responsible for everything downstream."**
Physician understanding of nFADP Art.9 processor chain responsibility — that accepting vendor terms extends their professional secrecy obligations to vendor data flows they cannot stop.

**"Swissdamed — I looked at it, it doesn't talk about my situation."**
Swissdamed classification criteria written for manufacturers, not independent outpatient physicians. Tier 1/Tier 2 distinctions have operational consequences the framework was not designed to address at independent practice scale.

## French-Language ICP Patterns

**"C'est moi qui signe, c'est moi qui réponds."**
The personal signature as accountability anchor. French-speaking physicians use the signature as the irreducible point of personal responsibility.

**"Je ne sais même pas ce qui tourne dans mon cabinet."**
Incremental AI accumulation described through inability to enumerate what's active. "Ce qui tourne" — what's running — reflects passive accumulation awareness.

**"Le patient arrive avec ce que l'IA a déjà dit."**
Patient-arrives-with-AI-analysis pattern. Consumer AI health analyses arriving pre-consultation described as structural friction.

**"Le formulaireCarrier demande des choses que je ne sais pas répondre."**
Carrier form creates compliance obligation the physician cannot discharge with confidence. "Je ne sais pas répondre" — I don't know how to answer — reflects the documentation gap.

**"FMH dit surveillance, ne dit pas ce que ça veut dire concrètement."**
Human oversight principle described without concrete operational meaning. "Concrètement" — concretely — marks the gap between stated principle and implementation reality.
