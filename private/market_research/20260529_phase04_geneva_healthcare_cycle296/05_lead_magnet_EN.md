## What the Carrier Form Actually Commits You To

When you sign the carrier attestation, you're making a personal representation about your AI governance. Not your practice's governance — yours. Your name is on the form and the document commits you to "adequate AI governance controls" without defining what that means for an independent physician in Geneva.

The form was designed by an underwriting team to evaluate carrier risk. It's not designed to protect you. That's the first thing to understand.

The second thing to understand: there's no Geneva-calibrated pre-signing review service for medical practitioners. Most physicians sign without evaluating what they're committing to. The ones who do evaluate usually do it after signing — when the document is already in effect.

**What I see happening:** Practices receiving the form, having 2-4 weeks to respond, scrambling to assemble documentation using vendor templates and internet searches. The documentation gets signed and sent. It doesn't accurately represent what's actually happening in the practice. But it's now the documented standard of care for that practice — evidence that will be used against the signing physician if a problem ever arises.

The urgency to respond is creating a stampede. And the stampede is producing documentation that works against the people signing it.

## The Seven Named Problems I've Found in Independent Practices

### 1. You Don't Know What's Running in Your Practice

Incremental AI accumulation through software updates, staff activation, embedded features — AI has arrived in most independent practices before any governance decision was made. Physicians are now responsible for AI usage they didn't consciously authorize.

There's no enumeration. No one has documented the aggregate AI surface of the practice. When the carrier form asks about your AI governance, you're certifying to something you haven't actually enumerated.

This isn't a hypothetical problem. It's the actual starting point for most independent practices I work with.

### 2. Your Records Don't Show What the AI Actually Did

Medical records show clinical conclusions, not recommendation chains. AI-influenced decisions look identical to decisions made without AI assistance. The documentation doesn't capture the differential.

When a patient arrives with a consumer AI-generated health analysis and you evaluate it and make a different clinical decision, your record documents the patient AI input and your decision — but not the specific moment where you evaluated the AI recommendation and chose differently. That's a discrete documentation category that doesn't exist in Geneva.

The same problem operates in reverse: if something goes wrong and you're asked to reconstruct what happened, you can't show the recommendation-evaluation-rejection chain. You can only show the outcome.

### 3. Multi-Physician Practice Has No One Owning the AI Question

In small group practices — three to six physicians — each physician independently adopts different AI tools, activates different EHR features, implements different documentation practices. No practice-level AI governance coordination exists.

When the carrier attestation arrives and one physician signs it, that physician is certifying to an AI surface they cannot fully enumerate. The signing physician is personally accountable for AI governance decisions made independently by colleagues they don't coordinate with.

No Geneva methodology exists for addressing this internal accountability diffusion at small-group practice scale. The problem exists, has consequences, and no one has built a solution for it.

### 4. Switching AI Tools Mid-Record Creates Attribution Chaos

When you switch AI tools mid-patient-record, the record becomes a continuous stream of content from multiple AI systems with no transition documentation. You cannot identify which AI generated which clinical content. Your name is on the record. The AI's contribution is invisible.

Every tool switch compounds the attribution problem. After two or three switches, the record contains untraceable contributions from multiple systems, all under your professional authority.

No Geneva documentation standard exists for mid-record AI tool transitions. When carrier or FMH requests documentation of your AI governance, you cannot reconstruct which AI system contributed which content — because that documentation was never created.

### 5. Incident Documentation Has No Category for AI

When an adverse clinical outcome occurs, your existing incident documentation infrastructure is designed for human clinical decision-making. It has no category for: AI system state at time of event, AI tool version, AI recommendation made, your AI-influenced vs. AI-independent decisions.

Your name is on the record. The AI's contribution is invisible to the review process. No Geneva medical body or carrier has published AI-associated adverse outcome documentation requirements proportionate to independent practice scale.

If an incident occurs and you're asked to document what the AI was doing at the time, you don't have the architecture to do it.

### 6. Your Competency Documentation Is Probably the Wrong Evidence

Most independent practices have built AI competency documentation: vendor training certificates, staff attendance records, online course completions. This documentation was assembled to satisfy implied FMH AI competency requirements.

The problem: FMH has never operationally defined what those requirements are. Your documentation may be inadequate to the evidentiary purpose while creating false confidence of compliance.

This is the wrong-standard compliance paradox. You're building evidence that doesn't actually satisfy the requirement it's trying to meet. Attempting compliance creates documentation that looks like compliance but wouldn't survive carrier or FMH substantive challenge.

### 7. The Urgency Is Producing Self-Incriminating Documentation

The carrier attestation wave is creating urgency-driven documentation across Geneva independent practices. Practices receiving questionnaires under 2-4 week response windows, assembling documentation retroactively using vendor templates, internet searches, and whatever is available.

This documentation doesn't accurately represent actual AI practice. It creates commercial representations to the carrier that cannot be supported with evidence. It's signed by physicians who haven't evaluated whether it's accurate.

Attempt to comply creates documentation that will be used against the signing physician if a challenge occurs. The urgency to respond is creating evidence that works against the people producing it.

## What Doing This Properly Actually Requires

The honest answer is that "adequate AI governance" at independent practice scale requires:

**An enumeration** — knowing what's running in your practice, which tools, which versions, which features activated, what data each accesses.

**An attribution architecture** — knowing which AI contributed to which clinical content in your records, with documentation that survives carrier or FMH review.

**A transition protocol** — when you switch tools or activate new features, documentation that maintains record integrity and establishes clear attribution.

**An override documentation category** — for the moments when you evaluate patient-provided AI analysis or your AI system's recommendation and exercise independent clinical judgment making a different decision.

**A multi-physician coordination framework** — if you have colleagues, documentation that establishes practice-level AI governance and clarifies individual physician accountability.

**A post-incident AI documentation protocol** — for adverse outcomes, capturing AI system state, version, recommendations, and physician's AI-influenced vs. independent decisions.

**A competency documentation standard** — proportionate to FMH oversight requirements, accepted by Geneva carriers, built for 2-10 physician scale.

**A carrier attestation pre-signing review** — evaluating what the form commits you to, what evidence you need to support it, and what personal liability exposure the signature creates.

None of this exists as a packaged solution in the Geneva market for independent medical practices. What exists are generic frameworks, institutional models, vendor templates, and carrier instruments — none designed for your scale, your liability structure, or your situation.
