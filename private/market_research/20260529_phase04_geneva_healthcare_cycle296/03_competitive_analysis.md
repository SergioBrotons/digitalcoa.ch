# Competitive Analysis — Phase 04 C296 Geneva Healthcare
## What Exists in Market vs. What's Missing

*Market landscape assessment for Geneva independent medical practice AI governance services.*

---

## What Exists

### 1. Generic AI Governance Frameworks
Large international consultancies and compliance firms offer AI governance frameworks — designed for enterprise, hospitals, and larger group practices. These frameworks exist but are not calibrated for independent physician scale (2-10 physicians), personal liability structure, or Geneva cantonal specificities.

**Gap:** Scale mismatch. Enterprise frameworks require infrastructure independent practices don't have — compliance departments, dedicated IT staff, board-level oversight committees.

### 2. Swissdamed Classification Pathway
The Swiss authorized representative and registration pathway exists for medical device AI — but criteria are written for manufacturers, not outpatient independent physicians. Tier 1/Tier 2 classification has operational consequences for manufacturers that don't translate to independent practice governance.

**Gap:** Classification pathway exists but doesn't address independent physician scale. No Geneva-calibrated methodology for determining whether a given AI tool requires Swissdamed classification at independent practice level.

### 3. FMH Human Oversight Principle
FMH has stated human oversight as the operative standard for AI in medical practice — but without operational definition: no documentation requirement, no review frequency, no competency standard, no endpoint.

**Gap:** Principle exists without operational translation. No Geneva-specific implementation guidance for what oversight means at independent practice scale, in a two-physician practice, on a Wednesday with ten patients.

### 4. Carrier Attestation Instruments
Carriers have developed AI governance attestation questionnaires — but designed for underwriting risk assessment, not physician professional liability protection. Forms create personal liability commitments for physicians without defining what adequate means.

**Gap:** Instruments exist but are misaligned with professional liability framework. No pre-signing review market for physicians evaluating carrier instruments before signature.

### 5. nFADP Compliance Documentation
General data protection compliance documentation exists — DPA templates, processing registers, consent forms — but not calibrated for AI-specific data processing scenarios specific to medical practice: AI-influenced decisions, patient-generated AI content, AI system state documentation.

**Gap:** Generic nFADP compliance documentation doesn't address the specific AI documentation scenarios in medical practice. No AI-specific DPIA methodology for clinical AI tools at independent practice scale.

### 6. Vendor-Supplied Training Documentation
AI vendors provide training materials and completion certificates — but these don't map to FMH oversight requirements and are not designed as evidentiary documentation for carrier attestation.

**Gap:** Vendor documentation exists but is the wrong evidence category for FMH oversight purposes and carrier attestation evidence requirements.

## What's Missing — Named White Space

### Missing 1: Geneva-Calibrated Independent Practice AI Governance Methodology
No service exists that provides a Geneva-calibrated, independent-practice-proportionate AI governance methodology specifically for 2-10 physician outpatient practices. What exists is either:
- Generic international frameworks scaled down, or
- Hospital/institutional governance models adapted, or
- Finance/legal vertical AI governance adapted.

**What's needed:** A methodology designed from independent practice scale, Geneva cantonal specifics, physician personal liability structure, and FMH accountability framework.

### Missing 2: Pre-Signing Carrier Attestation Review for Medical Practitioners
No service in Geneva market offers physicians pre-signing analysis of carrier AI attestation instruments — evaluation of what the form commits them to, what evidence they need to support those commitments, what personal liability exposure the signature creates.

**What's needed:** A review service calibrated to medical practitioner liability framework, not underwriting risk assessment framework.

### Missing 3: Medical Record AI Attribution Architecture
No documentation standard or methodology exists for attributing AI contribution within medical records — which AI generated which content, which clinical decisions were AI-influenced vs. independent, how to document the recommendation-evaluation-rejection chain.

**What's needed:** A documentation architecture for medical records that captures AI contribution without disrupting clinical workflow, proportionate to independent practice scale.

### Missing 4: Post-Incident AI Documentation Protocol
No Geneva medical body or carrier has published AI-associated adverse outcome documentation requirements proportionate to independent practice scale. Existing incident documentation infrastructure has no category for AI system state at time of event.

**What's needed:** A documentation protocol for post-incident review that captures AI system state, version, recommendations, and physician's AI-influenced vs. independent decisions.

### Missing 5: Multi-Physician Practice Internal AI Governance Coordination
No methodology exists for small group practices (3-6 physicians) to coordinate AI governance across independent physician decision-makers who each independently adopt different AI tools. No enumeration framework, no coordination protocol, no shared accountability documentation.

**What's needed:** A coordination methodology for small group practices that addresses internal accountability diffusion while preserving individual physician autonomy.

### Missing 6: AI Tool Switching Mid-Record Documentation Protocol
No documentation standard exists for mid-record AI tool transitions — how to document that the record contains content from multiple AI systems, how to establish AI attribution when switching tools mid-patient-record.

**What's needed:** A documentation protocol for AI tool transitions that maintains record integrity and establishes clear AI attribution for carrier/FMH review.

### Missing 7: Patient-Override Documentation Category
No discrete documentation category exists for the moment when a physician evaluates patient-provided consumer AI analysis and exercises independent clinical judgment making a different decision. The AI-influenced-but-not-AI-determined decision has no documentation standard.

**What's needed:** A documentation category and protocol for AI-influenced-but-not-AI-determined clinical decisions, specific to the patient-provided AI content scenario.

### Missing 8: AI Competency Documentation Standard for Independent Practice
No Geneva-calibrated standard defines what AI competency documentation a physician must maintain, what form it should take, how it should be organized for carrier or FMH review. Vendor training certificates don't map to FMH requirements.

**What's needed:** A competency documentation standard specifically calibrated to independent practice FMH oversight obligations, accepted by Geneva carriers, proportionate to 2-10 physician scale.

### Missing 9: nFADP Art.22 / Medical Record Integrity Proportionate Protocol
No Geneva protocol exists for satisfying Art.22 reversal right without compromising medical record integrity. No methodology for proportionate clinical notation that satisfies both obligations simultaneously.

**What's needed:** A protocol that operationalizes Art.22 reversal rights in clinical record contexts with record integrity preservation as a design constraint.

---

## Summary: Market Position

**What exists:** Generic frameworks, institutional models, carrier instruments, vendor documentation — none calibrated to independent practice scale or Geneva cantonal specifics.

**What's missing:** Nine discrete service categories, all unnamed in Geneva market, all addressable with proportionate Geneva-calibrated methodology.

**The opportunity:** Build services for the white space. The market has frameworks that don't fit, instruments that misalign, principles without operational endpoints. Independent physicians are managing AI accountability with tools designed for different scales and different liability structures.
