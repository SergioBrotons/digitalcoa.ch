## Finding 1: AI Tool Switching Mid-Record Attribution Exposure

**The problem:** When an independent practice switches AI tools mid-patient-record, the medical record becomes a continuous stream of AI-generated content from multiple systems with no transition documentation. The physician cannot identify which AI generated which clinical content. The record is authoritative under the physician's name with no AI attribution architecture.

**Why this is distinct:** This is not first-adoption AI governance (addressed in prior cycles), not accumulation without enumeration (addressed separately), not tool deprecation (different mechanism). This specifically names mid-record tool switching as a discrete liability scenario that creates an attribution impossibility distinct from both initial adoption and deprecation. Each tool switch compounds the attribution problem. After two or three switches, the record contains untraceable contributions from multiple systems all under the physician's professional authority — with no documentation standard existing in Geneva for this scenario.

**The exposure:** Carrier or FMH request for documentation of AI governance cannot be satisfied because reconstruction of which AI contributed which content is structurally impossible without documentation that was never created.

## Finding 2: Patient-Override-of-AI Documentation Void as Named Clinical Category

**The problem:** When a patient arrives with consumer AI-generated health analyses and the physician exercises independent clinical judgment making a different decision than AI recommended, the record documents patient AI input and physician decision but not the specific deliberation moment where the AI input was evaluated and rejected. This is a discrete documentation category — AI-influenced-but-not-AI-determined clinical decision — with no Geneva protocol, no naming, no defined requirement.

**Why this is distinct:** Different from patient-generated AI content entering the record (addressed in prior cycles) — this specifically names the physician override documentation moment as distinct from the content-ingestion moment. Also distinct from physician-deployed AI decision documentation (different trigger, different accountability attribution). The override moment has high clinical accountability but zero documentation infrastructure.

**The exposure:** Physician who exercised sound clinical judgment against AI recommendation may be unable to demonstrate the evaluation process in retrospective review. The record shows the decision made, not the recommendation that was considered and rejected.

## Finding 3: Multi-Physician Practice Internal Accountability Diffusion as Named Structural Problem

**The problem:** In small group practices (3-6 physicians), each physician independently adopts different AI tools, activates different EHR AI features, implements different AI-assisted documentation practices without practice-level AI governance coordination. When the practice receives carrier attestation, the signing physician certifies to an AI surface they cannot fully enumerate.

**Why this is distinct:** Prior findings addressed single-physician governance impossibility (one person holding all roles). This specifically names multi-physician internal accountability diffusion — the problem that emerges when independent physician decision-makers in the same group practice each make separate AI governance decisions without coordination, creating an aggregate AI surface no single physician can enumerate or own.

**The exposure:** Carrier attestation signed by one physician for a multi-physician practice creates personal liability documentation that does not accurately represent the practice's AI surface. Internal accountability diffusion becomes external personal liability through the attestation signature mechanism.

## Finding 4: AI-Associated Adverse Outcome Documentation Architecture Absence as Discrete Service Category

**The problem:** When an adverse clinical outcome occurs in an independent practice, existing incident documentation infrastructure designed for human clinical decision-making has no category for: AI system state at time of event, AI tool version at time of event, AI recommendation made, physician's AI-influenced vs. AI-independent decisions. The physician's name is on the record. The AI's contribution is invisible to the review process.

**Why this is distinct:** Different from medical record AI authorship ambiguity (which addressed records going forward), this specifically names post-incident review architecture as a discrete service category. The documentation architecture needed for retrospective post-incident review of AI contribution is different from — and currently absent alongside — documentation needed for prospective record integrity. No Geneva medical body or carrier has published proportionate requirements for this scenario.

**The exposure:** If an incident occurs and physician is asked to document what the AI was doing at the time, no architecture exists to do it. The absence is invisible until the moment it becomes critical.

---

## Finding 5: AI Competency Documentation Wrong-Standard Paradox

**The problem:** Independent practices building AI competency documentation (vendor training certificates, staff attendance records, online course completions) to satisfy implied FMH AI competency requirements FMH has never operationally defined. Documentation assembled is inadequate to the evidentiary purpose while creating false confidence of compliance. The practice has evidence of compliance that would not survive carrier or FMH substantive challenge.

**Why this is distinct:** Different from training documentation absence (addressed in prior cycles). This specifically names the wrong-standard compliance paradox — the mechanism by which attempting compliance creates compliance-looking evidence that does not actually comply. False adequacy of wrong-standard compliance is more dangerous than no documentation because it creates the appearance of protection where actual protection is absent.

**The exposure:** Practice with vendor training certificates believing it has demonstrated FMH AI competency oversight — carrier or FMH substantive challenge reveals documentation maps to no defined requirement, creates evidence of effort without evidence of compliance.

---

## Finding 6: Carrier Attestation Stampede Effect as Self-Incriminating Documentation Pattern

**The problem:** As carrier AI attestation arrives in Geneva healthcare, independent practices experiencing stampede response — receiving questionnaires, attempting to document AI governance retroactively under 2-4 week time pressure using vendor-downloaded templates and internet searches — creates documentation that does not accurately represent actual AI practice, creates commercial representations to carrier that cannot be supported with evidence, signed by physicians who have not evaluated whether documentation is accurate.

**Why this is distinct:** Different from carrier attestation pressure (named in prior cycles) and different from pre-signing review absence (named separately). This specifically isolates the stampede response pattern as the mechanism creating self-incriminating documentation. Urgency-driven documentation creation producing evidence that will be used against the signing physician if a challenge occurs. The attempt to comply creates the documentation that becomes the liability.

**The exposure:** Carrier attestation creates the documented standard of care for the practice. Documentation assembled under urgency becomes the benchmark against which future adequacy is evaluated. If that documentation doesn't accurately represent actual AI practice, the physician has created a worse evidentiary position than if they'd never responded.
