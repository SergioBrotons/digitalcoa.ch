## NET-NEW Findings C145 Phase 02 Geneva Finance

### Finding 01: FINMA Guidance 01/2026 Stablecoin Framework — Greenfield Licensing Advisory
FINMA Guidance 01/2026 (effective January 12, 2026) establishes Swiss stablecoin issuer framework: payment instrument institution license pathway for CHF/EUR/USD stablecoins; foreign custodian equivalence provisions; monthly attestation requirements. Two new FinIA license categories (payment instrument institutions + crypto-institutions) in legislative pipeline. Advisory opportunity: FinIA stablecoin license application advisory; Swiss stablecoin authorization strategy; DLT facility compliance for tokenized assets; EU passporting pathway analysis for Swiss stablecoin issuers targeting EU markets. Distinct from Geneva legal (banking/crypto licensing was covered but stablecoin-specific is new angle).

*Different from Phase 01 Geneva Legal findings:* C145 Phase 01 covered FINMA stablecoin guidance as a single finding (Finding 6); C145 Phase 02 sharpens the advisory angle — specifically the greenfield FinIA licensing advisory opportunity, EU passporting strategy, and DLT facility compliance as a distinct service line for Geneva finance advisors.

### Finding 02: DORA-Parity via ISA Amendments — Operational Resilience Advisory Gap
EU Digital Operational Resilience Act (DEMA/DORA effective January 2025) creates extraterritorial pressure on Swiss FIs with EU market access. Switzerland has no standalone DORA equivalent — the Federal Council is using ISA amendments to create parity obligations: ICT risk management frameworks, incident reporting timelines, third-party AI vendor oversight, and digital operational resilience testing. Geneva FIs (asset managers, private banks) with EU cross-border operations face dual compliance: Swiss ISA + DORA requirements. Advisory opportunity: DORA-parity compliance gap analysis for Swiss FIs with EU operations; ISA incident reporting framework design; third-party AI vendor risk management advisory; operational resilience testing (threat-led penetration testing for Swiss FIs). This is a 2026 live advisory topic not yet addressed by Geneva boutiques.

*Different from Phase 01 findings:* DORA parity covered in Phase 01 as a general regulatory convergence item; this Phase 02 finding specifically addresses the ISA amendments creating DORA-equivalent obligations and the advisory gap for Geneva FIs — specifically the gap between existing ISA requirements and the new ISA amendment requirements, plus the specific service line of DORA-parity compliance advisory.

### Finding 03: CRS 2.0/CARF January 2026 — Crypto-Asset Reporting Compliance Wave
Switzerland implementing CRS 2.0 and Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework (CARF) from January 1, 2026. All Swiss financial institutions (banks, asset managers, crypto institutions) face new tax due diligence and reporting obligations for crypto-assets held by reportable persons. Reporting to Federal Tax Administration (FTA) by June 2026 deadline. Geneva private banks, asset managers, and crypto custody providers face acute compliance burden — crypto positions require new classification, due diligence, and reporting infrastructure. Advisory opportunity: CRS 2.0/CARF compliance gap analysis for Geneva FIs; crypto-asset classification and reporting process design; FALD implications for Geneva-based UHNW clients with international crypto holdings; cross-border reporting coordination with EU/EAA jurisdictions. Deadline-driven urgency creates natural first conversation for Geneva compliance advisors.

*Different from Phase 01 findings:* CRS 2.0/CARF mentioned in Phase 01 C115 (Finding 5: CRS 2.0/CARF); this Phase 02 finding sharpens to: (1) June 2026 reporting deadline urgency; (2) FALD implications for Geneva UHNW clients; (3) specific advisory service line of CRS 2.0/CARF compliance gap analysis + reporting infrastructure design.

### Finding 04: FINMA Circular 2026/1 Nature-Related Financial Risks — New ESG Disclosure Service Line
FINMA Circular 2026/1 (effective January 1, 2026) mandates systematic integration of nature-related financial risks into governance and risk management for Swiss banks and insurers under supervision. Disclosure requirements aligned with TNFD (Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures) framework. Greenwashing defense and transition plan advisory is a genuinely new service line not yet widely offered in Geneva. Affected entities: Geneva-based private banks, asset managers, insurance companies managing nature-exposed portfolios (agriculture, real estate, forestry, water infrastructure). Advisory opportunity: TNFD-aligned nature-related risk disclosure framework; greenwashing defense documentation; transition plan development; nature-related risk assessment for Geneva investment portfolios. Geneva financial institutions are lagging EU counterparts on nature-related risk preparedness.

*Different from Phase 01 findings:* FINMA Circular 2026/1 mentioned in Phase 01 C114 (Finding 1: nature-related financial risks); this Phase 02 finding sharpens to: (1) TNFD framework as the specific disclosure standard; (2) FALD implications for Geneva UHNW clients with international crypto holdings; (3) specific advisory service line of CRS 2.0/CARF compliance gap analysis and reporting infrastructure design for Geneva FIs.

### Finding 05: AI Governance Framework for Geneva Asset Managers — Underserved Advisory Vertical
Federal Council AI civil liability bill (Q1 2026) proposes Art. 41 OR strict liability for high-risk AI systems with a compliance defense pathway. FINMA AI survey (Nov 2024-Jan 2025): 50% of Swiss FIs using AI; 91% of AI-enabled using GenAI. Yet no Geneva boutique advisor has positioned specifically as AI governance advisor for asset managers. Gaps: (1) AI model risk in investment decisions; (2) AI-generated investment recommendation liability; (3) AI documentation requirements for FINMA examinations; (4) AI vendor due diligence for third-party AI tools (robo-advisory, ESG scoring, risk models). Advisory opportunity: AI governance framework for Geneva asset managers; compliance defense documentation; AI model risk assessment; FINMA examination preparedness for AI-related findings. Target buyer: CCO, CRO, Head of Technology at Geneva asset managers (CHF 1B-50B AUM).

*Different from Phase 01 findings:* AI governance for law firms covered extensively in Phase 01 (C113 Finding 1: AI governance liability; C145 Finding 2: AI civil liability framework; Finding 4: SBA AI guidelines); this Phase 02 finding specifically addresses AI governance for asset managers — a distinct ICP, distinct regulatory framework (FINMA vs. OGE/SBA), distinct liability exposure (investment decisions vs. legal advice), and distinct buyer (CCO/CRO/Head of Technology at asset managers vs. Managing Partner/GC at law firms).

### Finding 06: Geneva Fintech Cluster — Payment Institution and Embedded Finance Advisory
Geneva fintech ecosystem (360T, Klarna Switzerland, Sonect, Amnis, Remate, Bity, Mtile, Relai, and others) is concentrated in payment institutions, embedded finance, and crypto-to-fiat on/off ramps. Post-FinIA revision, these firms face: (1) new payment instrument institution license category; (2) enhanced capital requirements; (3) AML obligations under revised GwG; (4) potential EU PSD2 passporting implications. Advisory opportunity: FinIA payment institution license application; AML compliance framework for Geneva payment institutions; embedded finance advisory for Geneva commodity trading firms embedding payments; EU PSD2 passporting strategy for Swiss payment institutions targeting EU merchants. Geneva fintech cluster (compared to Zurich's crypto exchange concentration) is underserved for regulatory advisory.

*Different from Phase 01 findings:* FinIA revision covered in Phase 01 C115 (Finding 4: FinIA revision consultation); Geneva fintech cluster as specific ICP is new — Phase 01 focused on Geneva legal sector. This Phase 02 finding specifically addresses the fintech ecosystem as a distinct buyer segment with distinct needs (payment institution licensing, AML, embedded finance) that differ from the Geneva financial institution ICP (asset managers, private banks, family offices).
