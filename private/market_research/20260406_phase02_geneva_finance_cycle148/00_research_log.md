### Finding 01: FINMA Guidance 01/2026 Stablecoin Framework — Greenfield Licensing Advisory
FINMA Guidance 01/2026 (effective January 12, 2026) establishes Swiss stablecoin issuer framework: payment instrument institution license pathway for CHF/EUR/USD stablecoins; foreign custodian equivalence provisions; monthly attestation requirements. Two new FinIA license categories (payment instrument institutions + crypto-institutions) in legislative pipeline. Advisory opportunity: FinIA stablecoin license application advisory; Swiss stablecoin authorization strategy; DLT facility compliance for tokenized assets; EU passporting pathway analysis for Swiss stablecoin issuers targeting EU markets. Distinct from Geneva legal (banking/crypto licensing was covered but stablecoin-specific is new angle).

*Different from Phase 01 Geneva Legal findings:* C145 Phase 01 covered FINMA stablecoin guidance as a single finding (Finding 6); C145 Phase 02 sharpens the advisory angle — specifically the greenfield FinIA licensing advisory opportunity, EU passporting strategy, and DLT facility compliance as a distinct service line for Geneva finance advisors.

### Finding 02: DORA-Parity via ISA Amendments — Operational Resilience Advisory Gap
EU Digital Operational Resilience Act (DEMA/DORA effective January 2025) creates extraterritorial pressure on Swiss FIs with EU market access. Switzerland has no standalone DORA equivalent — the Federal Council is using ISA amendments to create parity obligations: ICT risk management frameworks, incident reporting timelines, third-party AI vendor oversight, and digital operational resilience testing. Geneva FIs (asset managers, private banks) with EU cross-border operations face dual compliance: Swiss ISA + DORA requirements. Advisory opportunity: DORA-parity compliance gap analysis for Swiss FIs with EU operations; ISA incident reporting framework design; third-party AI vendor risk management advisory; operational resilience testing (threat-led penetration testing for Swiss FIs). This is a 2026 live advisory topic not yet addressed by Geneva boutiques.

*Different from Phase 01 findings:* DORA parity covered in Phase 01 as a general regulatory convergence item; this Phase 02 finding specifically addresses the ISA amendments creating DORA-equivalent obligations and the advisory gap for Geneva FIs — specifically the gap between existing ISA requirements and the new ISA amendment requirements, plus the specific service line of DORA-parity compliance advisory.

### Finding 03: CRS 2.0/CARF January 2026 — Crypto-Asset Reporting Compliance Wave
Switzerland implementing CRS 2.0 and Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework (CARF) from January 1, 2026. All Swiss financial institutions (banks, asset managers, crypto institutions) face new tax due diligence and reporting obligations for crypto-assets held by reportable persons. Reporting to Federal Tax Administration (FTA) by June 2026 deadline. Geneva private banks, asset managers, and crypto custody providers face acute compliance burden — crypto positions require new classification, due diligence, and reporting infrastructure. Advisory opportunity: CRS 2.0/CARF compliance gap analysis for Geneva FIs; crypto-asset classification and reporting process design; FALD implications for Geneva-based UHNW clients with international crypto holdings; cross-border reporting coordination with EU/EAA jurisdictions. ### Finding 01: Basel IV SA/BIA Capital Calibration — RWA Optimization Advisory for Geneva Private Banks
Switzerland implementing Basel IV via revised FINMA ordinance (FINMA-BO 2026 revision expected H1 2026). Unlike the EU's staggered Basel IV timeline, Switzerland is calibrating its own output floor scalar and SA/BIA requirements through FINMA's revised supervisory framework. Key impact on Geneva private banks: Standardized Approach (SA) replacing IRB for institutions without FINMA-approved internal model status; RWA increases of 15–30% projected for private banks with corporate bond portfolios and Lombard lending exposure; domestic mortgage and Lombard loan books subject to revised SA credit risk buckets. Geneva banks with non-Swiss counterparty exposures face particularly complex SA calibration. Advisory opportunity: Basel IV SA/BIA gap analysis for Geneva private banks (CHF 1B–50B AUM); RWA optimization strategies within SA parameters; SA credit risk bucket alignment advisory; capital planning advisory for Basel IV transition period; internal ratings alignment with SA counterparty categories. This is a distinct Basel IV advisory angle from Phase 01's TBTF/banking consolidation coverage.

*Distinct from C145 Phase 02 findings:* C145 Phase 02 covered stablecoin FinIA licensing, DORA-ISA parity, CRS 2.0/CARF, nature-related TNFD risk, asset manager AI governance, and Geneva fintech payment institutions. Basel IV capital calibration for Geneva private banks was not addressed in any prior cycle — it requires specific SA/BIA expertise, RWA optimization advisory, and a distinct buyer (CFO/CEO at Geneva private banks vs. CCO at asset managers or fintech payment institutions).

### Finding 02: FINMA Guidance 01/2026 Crypto Custody — Asset Manager Segregation and Third-Party Custodian Obligations
FINMA Guidance 01/2026 (effective January 12, 2026) on crypto-based asset custody goes beyond the stablecoin licensing angle of C145 Phase 02 Finding 1. The guidance creates specific custody obligations for Swiss institutions holding crypto assets: (1) bankruptcy protection applies only when crypto assets are held at all times in readiness for customers, in individual or collective segregated custody with documented customer shares; (2) foreign custodians must be prudentially supervised AND foreign law must guarantee equivalent bankruptcy protection — the equivalence test creates an ongoing due diligence obligation; (3) asset managers providing individual portfolio management that includes crypto assets must use custodians ensuring segregation AND bankruptcy protection equivalent to Swiss standards; (4) DLT facility licensing prerequisites for institutions operating their own crypto custody infrastructure. Geneva asset managers with existing third-party crypto custody arrangements face immediate review obligations: custodian equivalence assessment, contractual audit rights, exit concept requirements. Advisory opportunity: crypto custody compliance gap analysis; foreign custodian equivalence assessment; DLT facility licensing roadmap; portfolio management crypto custody compliance review. This is a distinct advisory service line from the stablecoin issuer FinIA licensing advisory covered in C145 Phase 02.

*Distinct from C145 Phase 02 Finding 1:* C145 Phase 02 Finding 1 focused on stablecoin issuers obtaining FinIA payment instrument institution licenses and EU passporting. C148 Finding 2 addresses the custody obligations for all Swiss institutions holding crypto assets — including asset managers, private banks, and family offices that use third-party custodians — specifically the segregation, bankruptcy protection, and foreign custodian equivalence requirements.

### Finding 03: BVG Reform and Total Portfolio AI Investing — Pension Fund Board AI Governance Advisory
The BVG reform (effective January 1, 2026) transforms Swiss second-pillar pension fund governance while Geneva institutions embrace total portfolio investing and AI-assisted investment decisions. Key intersection: FINMA Guidance 08/2024 (December 2024) AI governance requirements explicitly apply to Swiss financial institutions including supervised pension funds — requiring data quality controls, model explainability, human oversight, and continuous monitoring of AI systems. The BVG reform introduces enhanced governance obligations for Stiftungsräte (pension fund boards) who must oversee AI-assisted investment decisions without necessarily possessing the technical expertise to do so. Specific gaps: (1) Geneva pension fund boards lack AI governance expertise — most Stiftungsräte are employer/labour representatives without AI/technology backgrounds; (2) AI documentation for BVG audit and FINMA examination readiness; (3) liability allocation when AI systems recommend portfolio allocation changes affecting insured members; (4) third-party AI vendor risk management for pension fund AI tools (risk models, ESG scoring, alternative investment monitoring). Advisory opportunity: AI governance framework for Geneva pension fund Stiftungsräte; board AI literacy training program design; AI documentation for BVG audit compliance; AI vendor risk management for Geneva pension fund technology decisions. Target buyer: Stiftungsrat president, pension fund CEO, and head of risk at Geneva-based pension funds (BVG-compliant institutions with CHF 500M–10B+ assets under administration).

*Distinct from C145 Phase 02 Finding 5:* C145 Phase 02 Finding 5 addressed AI governance for Geneva asset managers (CCO/CRO/Head of Technology buyer). C148 Finding 3 specifically addresses pension fund AI governance — a distinct buyer (Stiftungsrat/governance board), distinct regulatory framework (BVG reform + FINMA Guidance 08/2024), distinct liability exposure (board member liability for AI oversight vs. investment model risk), and a distinct Geneva concentration (Geneva has a high density of institutional pension funds alongside private banks and asset managers).

### Finding 04: Geneva Commodity Trading AI Compliance — Surveillance, AML, and DORA Vendor Risk
Geneva is a major European commodity trading hub, home to multinational commodity trading houses (Swiss-based trading firms across energy, metals, agricultural commodities). These firms face a distinct AI compliance profile that was not addressed in C145 Phase 02: (1) AI-enabled trading models used for price discovery, arbitrage, and risk management require FINMA trading surveillance compliance frameworks — the Swiss Financial Market Infrastructure Act (FinfrA) self-regulation and the Geneva Trading & Shipping Association (GTS) frameworks are the applicable standards; (2) AML obligations compound when AI trading systems execute high-frequency commodity transactions — transaction monitoring for AI-traded volumes under revised Swiss Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA) GwG obligations; (3) DORA-equivalent third-party AI vendor risk applies when Geneva commodity firms use third-party AI platforms for commodity price prediction, logistics optimization, or counterparty risk scoring; (4) Export control and sanctions compliance for AI-traded commodities — SECO sanctions oversight applies regardless of AI execution. Advisory opportunity: AI trading model compliance framework for Geneva commodity trading firms; AML transaction monitoring for AI-enabled commodity trading; DORA-equivalent third-party AI vendor risk management for commodity trading firms; AI-driven sanctions compliance review. This is an underserved vertical — no Geneva boutique is positioned as AI compliance advisor specifically for commodity trading firms.

*Distinct from C145 Phase 02 findings:* No C145 finding addressed commodity trading AI compliance. C145 Phase 02 covered Geneva fintech (payment institutions), stablecoin licensing, DORA-ISA parity (banking focus), asset manager AI governance, and CRS 2.0/CARF (tax reporting). ### Finding 05: Swiss-EU Sustainable Finance Divergence — Dual Taxonomy Alignment Advisory
Switzerland is developing its own sustainable finance framework (Federal Council Sustainable Finance Action Plan, Swiss Platform for Sustainable Finance / SFA/SSF label) rather than adopting the EU taxonomy or SFDR directly. This creates a specific compliance complexity for Geneva financial institutions with cross-border EU distribution: EU Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR) Article 8 and Article 9 fund labeling requirements apply when Swiss funds are marketed to EU investors; Swiss SFA/SSF labeling does not automatically satisfy SFDR disclosure requirements; Swiss fund managers face dual-compliance burden — SFA classification for Swiss regulatory purposes and SFDR classification for EU distribution. The divergence is substantive (not merely technical): Swiss taxonomy covers additional sectors not in EU taxonomy (e.g., hydropower, certain nuclear), uses different thresholds and measurement methodologies, and lacks EU's MiFID II sustainability preferences integration. Advisory opportunity: Swiss-EU sustainable finance taxonomy harmonization advisory; dual SFDR Article 8/9 + Swiss SFA labeling strategy for Geneva funds; cross-border sustainable finance product framework design; EU distribution strategy for Swiss ESG-labelled funds under SFA. This is distinct from C145 Phase 02 Finding 4 (nature-related TNFD risk disclosure), which focused on disclosure compliance; this finding focuses on taxonomy harmonization, product labeling strategy, and the dual-jurisdiction compliance challenge.

*Distinct from C145 Phase 02 Finding 4:* C145 Phase 02 Finding 4 addressed FINMA Circular 2026/1 nature-related TNFD risk disclosure (regulatory disclosure obligation). C148 Finding 5 addresses Swiss-EU sustainable finance taxonomy divergence (product strategy and dual-jurisdiction labeling challenge) — a distinct advisory service line requiring knowledge of both EU SFDR/ taxonomy and Swiss SFA frameworks, targeting the product development and distribution strategy buyer (CEO, Head of Product, Distribution Director at Geneva asset managers and fund promoters).

### Finding 06: Geneva Private Banking AI Advisory Liability — FinSA + FINMA + Federal Council AI Bill Triple Exposure
Geneva private banks offering AI-powered wealth planning, tax optimization, and portfolio rebalancing tools face a converging three-layer liability exposure not addressed in any prior cycle: (1) FinSA obligations — when AI provides personalized investment advice under FinSA, institutions must meet the client categorization, suitability assessment, and disclosure requirements applicable to all financial services; AI systems that generate "personalized" recommendations must document the basis for personalization and ensure the recommendation is suitable; (2) FINMA Guidance 08/2024 AI governance requirements — 11 specific observation areas including AI model risk, explainability, human oversight, and documentation standards; (3) Federal Council AI civil liability bill (Q1 2026) — proposed Art. 41 OR strict liability for high-risk AI systems with a compliance defense pathway, requiring institutions to demonstrate conformity with AI governance requirements. Geneva private banks face all three simultaneously: FinSA advisory obligations layered over FINMA AI governance requirements layered over new civil liability exposure. The advisory gap: no Geneva boutique is positioned to advise private banks on the integrated FinSA + FINMA + AI civil liability compliance framework. Advisory opportunity: integrated AI advisory liability framework for Geneva private banks; FinSA AI suitability documentation; FINMA AI governance gap analysis; AI civil liability compliance defense preparation; AI vendor due diligence for private banking wealth management tools. Target buyer: CCO, Head of Private Banking, and General Counsel at Geneva private banks and family offices.

*Distinct from C145 Phase 02 Finding 5:* C145 Phase 02 Finding 5 addressed AI governance for Geneva asset managers (FINMA AI survey context, AI model risk in investment decisions, FINMA examination preparedness). C148 Finding 6 specifically addresses Geneva private banking AI advisory liability — a distinct buyer (private bank CCO/GC vs. asset manager CCO/CRO), distinct liability profile (FinSA personalized advisory + FINMA AI governance + Federal Council AI civil liability triple layer vs. single FINMA AI governance layer), and distinct product context (wealth planning and advisory vs. asset management).
