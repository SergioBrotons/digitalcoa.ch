## Current Problems, Complaints, Failures — C309

## Signal 01: The Policy Gap — Written Policy vs. Carrier Form Specification Are Structurally Different Documents

**Signal:** The Geneva small firm that responded to the initial carrier attestation with a written AI governance policy has discovered, at renewal, that the policy does not satisfy the carrier's actual questions. The policy was written to answer "do you have an AI policy?" — a yes/no question. The renewal form asks for evidence of authorization, vendor contract status, matter-level AI use records, and supervision protocols. These are not policy questions — they are evidence architecture questions. The policy the firm produced is the wrong document for the question being asked. The carrier is asking for operational evidence, not policy intent.

**Market signal:** Managing partners reporting that they produced something, the carrier asked follow-up questions they couldn't answer, and they don't know what the carrier actually needs. The policy gap is a structural mismatch between what carriers are now asking and what firms have produced.

**Commercial angle:** "Your policy answers the wrong question. The carrier is asking for evidence — not a document that says you have a policy."

## Signal 02: The Documentation Paradox as Primary Action Blocker — Not Ignorance, Fear

**Signal:** The Geneva legal ICP that has begun to understand AI governance obligations is now stopped by a specific fear: that documenting AI use creates a record that can be used against the firm in litigation or regulatory proceedings. This is not ignorance — the ICP understands they need records. The paradox is that creating records feels self-incriminating. The ICP has concluded: "If I write down what I did, and something goes wrong, that record proves what I didn't do adequately." This is the documentation paradox — the tool that protects the firm (contemporaneous records) feels like the tool that exposes the firm (created records). No Geneva service has resolved this paradox for the ICP.

**Market signal:** Managing partners reporting that they know they need documentation, know they should record AI use in client matters, but have concluded that the risk of creating the record exceeds the risk of not having it. The documentation paradox is generating active avoidance behavior, not mere inaction.

**Commercial angle:** "The fear that documentation will be used against you is the same fear that leaves you with nothing to show when the carrier asks. I resolve the documentation paradox — I'll show you what to write and how to write it so it protects you, not exposes you."

## Signal 03: Managing Partner AI Competence Gap Is Now an Active Accountability Problem

**Signal:** At C309, the managing partner at the small Geneva firm has acknowledged a specific problem: they cannot personally evaluate the AI output their associates produce. The associate is more AI-fluent than the managing partner. The managing partner is personally accountable for decisions made using AI output they cannot evaluate. This creates a structural supervision problem: the managing partner must authorize AI-assisted work they cannot substantively review, and must document that authorization as "reasonable supervision" in carrier attestation responses. The gap between AI-fluent associates and AI-incompetent managing partners is now a specific accountability exposure — not a training gap, but a personal liability gap.

**Market signal:** Managing partners reporting they sign off on AI-assisted work they don't understand. No Geneva service addresses the documentation architecture for supervising AI output you cannot evaluate. The problem is not that managing partners are unaware of AI — they are aware and they are accountable. The problem is that accountability without competence creates a specific documentation need: evidence of reasonable supervision of incomprehensible output.

**Commercial angle:** "You are personally responsible for AI-assisted decisions you cannot evaluate. The answer is not to understand AI — it is to document the supervision you did of the associate who did understand it. I'll build the supervision architecture."

## Signal 04: Vendor Compelled Records — The Third-Party Privilege Exposure Pathway

**Signal:** The Geneva legal ICP is beginning to understand that AI vendor records are not protected by attorney-client privilege. If a matter goes to litigation, opposing counsel can subpoena the AI vendor. The vendor's records of what AI was used, on what data, with what inputs, are discoverable through the vendor — not through the attorney. The privilege gap is structural: client-attorney privilege protects communications between attorney and client, not between attorney and third-party AI vendor. Every AI tool used on a client matter potentially creates a vendor record that can be compelled, revealing client matter data and AI use patterns without attorney consent. This is a privilege pathway the ICP has not previously identified.

**Market signal:** Managing partners beginning to surface the question: "If there's a dispute, can the other side get the vendor to produce records of what the AI did on the file?" The question is surfacing independently at C309 — the ICP is discovering this exposure through their own analysis, not through regulatory guidance.

**Commercial angle:** "Your vendor can be compelled to produce records about your client matters. That is a privilege exposure your current contracts don't address. I'll show you the exposure and the contract fix."

## Signal 05: AI Billing in Fee Agreements — The Unresolved Commercial Question

**Signal:** The Geneva legal ICP is now confronting a question that has not previously surfaced in AI governance discussions: is it permissible to bill clients for AI-assisted work, and if so, how should it be disclosed in the fee agreement? Managing partners are using AI tools to draft briefs, conduct research, and process documents — and these activities are being billed to clients. The AI tools may reduce attorney time, or they may not. The billing question intersects with AI governance in two ways: (1) disclosure obligations to clients about the use of AI in their matter, (2) fee agreement language that protects the firm if a client challenges AI-assisted billing. No Geneva bar guidance exists on AI billing disclosure. No standard fee agreement AI clause exists. This is a live commercial question that the C309 ICP is navigating without guidance.

**Market signal:** Managing partners beginning to ask: "Can I bill for AI-assisted work?" and "What do I tell the client?" The billing question is surfacing as a client relationship problem — clients who discover AI use in billing records and question it.

**Commercial angle:** "The fee agreement is the document that governs the entire client relationship. If it doesn't have an AI clause, you have no documented basis for AI-assisted billing. I'll draft the clause."

## Signal 06: The Renewal Carrier Form is More Sophisticated — The Calibration Gap Is Widening

**Signal:** Carriers serving the Geneva legal market are demonstrably improving their AI attestation forms faster than firms are improving their AI governance documentation. The gap between what carriers know to ask and what firms can answer is widening at each renewal cycle. The carrier that sent a simple yes/no attestation form in 2024 is now sending forms with specific vendor contract questions, matter-level AI use questions, associate tool authorization questions, and supervision protocol questions. The sophistication of carrier forms is now a market signal — it demonstrates that carriers are learning from claims experience and regulatory pressure, and are specifically calibrating their questions to expose inadequate documentation.

**Market signal:** Managing partners reporting that the renewal form was "harder" or "more specific" than the initial form. The carrier is raising the documentation standard at each cycle. Firms that haven't improved their documentation since initial attestation are now materially under-prepared relative to carrier expectations.

**Commercial angle:** "Your carrier's form is more sophisticated than it was two years ago. Your documentation hasn't kept up. I read carrier forms for a living — I'll show you what they're actually asking for and how to answer it."
