# Compliance Context: Geneva Legal — C309 Phase 01

## Geneva/Switzerland Specific Regulatory — C309

---

## Regulatory Landscape Overview (C309)

The Geneva legal ICP operates in a multi-authority accountability environment where no single authority has issued comprehensive AI guidance for attorneys, and where the authorities that have issued guidance (carriers, FDPIC) have done so in language that creates documentation obligations without defining the documentation standard. The C309 regulatory landscape has evolved from C308 in one critical respect: the C309 ICP has begun to understand that the documentation obligations are real, even in the absence of clear regulatory standards. The "adequate" undefined by the OFL is now understood by the ICP to be a gap that does not eliminate the obligation — it only shifts the definition to whatever the carrier decides at renewal.

---

## 1. Ordre des Avocats de Genève (OFL) — The Silence That Creates Uncertainty

**Current state:** The OFL has not issued formal guidance on AI use by attorneys in client matters. No OFL advisory opinion exists on: (1) what constitutes adequate supervision of AI-assisted legal work, (2) whether attorneys must disclose AI use to clients, (3) what AI use records attorneys must maintain, (4) how to handle privilege when AI tools process privileged client data.

**What this means practically:** The managing partner cannot point to OFL guidance and say "I did what the Order requires." The Order has not defined the standard. This creates a specific problem: the managing partner cannot demonstrate compliance with a standard that does not exist, but the carrier at renewal is asking documentation questions that assume a standard does exist. The OFL silence is not a permission structure — it is an absence of safe harbor.

**C309 signal:** The C309 ICP is no longer waiting for OFL guidance with patience. The phrase "Without guidance, I don't know what adequate means" now surfaces with active frustration, not passive acceptance. The ICP understands that "adequate" will be defined by the carrier at renewal, not by the OFL. The documentation gap is not eliminated by OFL silence — it is defined by carrier expectation.

**Implication:** The service must address what "adequate" means operationally — specifically against carrier form requirements — when the OFL has not defined the term. "Adequate" is not a regulatory definition; it is the evidence that satisfies the carrier's current questions.

---

## 2. Carrier Attestation — The De Facto Standard

**Current state:** The carrier attestation form is the closest thing to an AI governance standard that the Geneva boutique legal market has. The carrier form defines: (1) what questions must be answered, (2) what documentation must exist, (3) what evidence must be producible at renewal. The carrier form functions as the de facto standard for the Geneva legal AI accountability obligation — not because carriers set professional standards, but because they have the enforcement power (non-renewal, coverage denial) that makes their form the operative requirement.

**C309 signal:** The C309 ICP now recognizes the carrier form as a specification, not just a compliance checklist. The language "I didn't realize the form was a design specification" surfaces at C309 — the ICP has begun to understand that answering the carrier form requires the same engineering rigor as building to a technical specification. The policy they wrote in response to the initial attestation was not built to the carrier's specification. The carrier's specification has now been clarified through follow-up questions at renewal that the firm's documentation cannot satisfy.

**Implication:** Reading the carrier form as a design specification — identifying exactly what evidence is needed, in what form, at what level of detail — is the primary compliance design activity. The evidence architecture is not a general document; it is a carrier-form-mapped evidence production system.

---

## 3. Article 321 CC (Code Civil) — Professional Secrecy and AI Vendor Exposure

**Current state:** Article 321 CC establishes the professional secrecy obligation for attorneys in Switzerland. The obligation covers all information disclosed in professional confidence. The question that has not been resolved — and that the C309 ICP is beginning to ask explicitly — is: when an AI tool processes privileged client information on a matter, does that processing constitute a disclosure to a third party? The vendor's employees, servers, and data processing infrastructure have access to the client data. The vendor is not bound by Article 321 CC. The vendor's contractual terms may permit them to use data for model training. The compelled disclosure question (Signal 04 from market signals) intersects with Article 321 CC: if a court compels the vendor to produce records of AI use on a privileged matter, the privilege is potentially compromised through the vendor.

**C309 signal:** The C309 ICP is no longer asking "could this be a problem?" The question has shifted to: "What exactly happens to our client data in the AI vendor's system, and what can be compelled from them?" The privilege-through-vendor concern is now specific enough to generate discrete service inquiries.

**Implication:** Article 321 CC mapping for AI vendor relationships is a discrete compliance deliverable. The output is a vendor-by-vendor privilege exposure assessment: which vendors have access to privileged data, what their data practices are, what their compelled disclosure obligations are, and what contract modifications are required to reduce privilege exposure.

---

## 4. nFADP (Swiss Data Protection Act) — The DPA Requirement

**Current state:** The Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP, revised 2020, in force since 2024) requires a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) between any entity that processes personal data and its data processors. If the Geneva law firm uses AI tools to process client matters containing personal data, the firm must have a DPA with each AI vendor. The C309 reality: most Geneva boutique firms using personal AI subscriptions (Signal 02 from C309 market signals) do not have firm-level DPAs with AI vendors — the associate accepted the vendor's Terms of Service personally, not through a firm-level DPA.

**C309 signal:** The C309 ICP has begun to recognize the DPA gap but is uncertain about what to do. The question "Do I need a specific agreement with the AI vendor?" surfaces frequently. The answer is clearly yes under nFADP — but the implementation (getting vendors to sign firm-level DPAs, negotiating terms) is operationally complex for boutique firms that lack procurement leverage.

**Implication:** The DPA audit and remediation service is a discrete, bounded deliverable — identify all AI vendors processing personal data in client matters, assess current contractual status, recommend contract modifications or vendor alternatives, and produce a firm-level DPA framework.

---

## 5. Code of Civil Procedure (CCP) — The Litigation Disclosure Vacuum

**Current state:** The Swiss Federal Code of Civil Procedure (CPC) governs discovery and evidence disclosure in civil litigation in Switzerland. The CPC requires disclosure of documents material to the dispute. The specific question of whether AI-assisted work product, AI-processed documents, or AI use records must be disclosed in litigation has not been answered by any Geneva court. No cantonal guidance exists. No OFL advisory exists. No academic commentary has reached definitive consensus.

**C309 signal:** The C309 ICP is now actively concerned about the litigation disclosure vacuum. The documentation paradox (fear that records will be used in litigation) and the litigation disclosure question are two sides of the same problem: in the absence of clear rules about what AI use must be disclosed, the managing partner faces a binary risk: document everything (and potentially be compelled to disclose it) or document nothing (and have inadequate governance inferred from the absence of records). Neither position is clearly correct.

**Implication:** Pre-litigation AI governance documentation is the only defensible strategy. The managing partner who builds a documentation system and can demonstrate appropriate supervision of AI use in client matters has a more defensible position in litigation than the managing partner who has no documentation — regardless of what the CCP ultimately requires. The service must frame pre-litigation documentation as litigation preparation, not compliance exercise.

---

## 6. Fee Agreement Requirements — The Unresolved AI Billing Question

**Current state:** No Geneva bar requirement exists for disclosing AI use in billing or fee agreements. No OFL guidance exists on whether attorneys must inform clients that AI tools were used in their matter. No standard AI clause exists for engagement letters. The commercial question — can an attorney bill for AI-assisted work, and what disclosure is required — is genuinely open under current Geneva professional rules.

**C309 signal:** The C309 ICP is now actively asking the billing question: "Can I bill for AI-assisted work, and what do I tell the client?" The question is surfacing independently across multiple ICP conversations, suggesting it is a live concern for the market. English-speaking clients are beginning to ask directly. The managing partner has no calibrated response.

**Implication:** The fee agreement AI clause is a discrete service deliverable that resolves a live commercial question. The clause should address: (1) disclosure of AI use in the matter, (2) client consent to AI processing of their data, (3) basis for AI-assisted billing, (4) limitations on AI use in sensitive matters. The clause protects the firm commercially and advances AI governance objectives simultaneously.

## 7. EU AI Act — Awareness Without Applicability

**Current state:** The EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) applies to AI systems used within the EU. Geneva law firms advising EU clients or processing EU personal data may face indirect obligations under the Act. However, the EU AI Act's provisions are designed for deployers and providers of AI systems — not for professional services firms using general-purpose AI tools in legal practice. The Act's risk-based approach categorizes AI applications by risk level. General-purpose AI tools used by attorneys for research, drafting, and document review are not classified as high-risk under the Act's Annex III list.

**C309 signal:** The C309 ICP has heard of the EU AI Act but does not know whether it applies to their practice. The question is typically asked in the context of "EU clients are asking about it." The EU AI Act creates client communication pressure, not direct compliance obligations, for the Geneva boutique legal ICP.

**Implication:** EU AI Act awareness is a client communication competency, not a compliance driver, for Geneva boutique firms. The service should address what the EU AI Act means for attorney-client relationships (minimal direct application) and how to respond to client EU AI Act inquiries (client communication language). Do not overstate applicability to Geneva attorneys.

---

## 8. Swissdamed — Medical Devices, Not Legal AI

**Current state:** Swissdamed (Swiss Medical Device Registry) is the implementation of the Therapeutic Products Act (TPA) for medical devices. The September 2026 deadline covers medical device registration and AI systems that qualify as medical devices under the TPA. Legal AI tools used by attorneys in client matters do not fall within Swissdamed's scope.

**C309 signal:** The C309 ICP that was waiting for Swissdamed clarification at C308 has either acted on the distinction (C308 Signal 04) or has moved to a new confusion: some ICPs now believe that because Swissdamed does not apply, their AI governance obligations are less pressing. This is a false inference. nFADP, Article 321 CC, and carrier attestation obligations exist independently of Swissdamed. The Swissdamed confusion has resolved into either clarity or dismissal — both of which require follow-up calibration.

**Implication:** Swissdamed clarification is a bounded service deliverable (confirm scope, confirm what doesn't apply, identify what does apply) that releases firms from the waiting posture and creates urgency around existing obligations.
