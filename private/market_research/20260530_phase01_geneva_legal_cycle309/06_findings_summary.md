### Finding 01: The Policy-Evidence Gap — Written Policy and Carrier Form Are Structurally Different Documents

**The finding:** The Geneva small firm that produced a written AI governance policy in response to the initial carrier attestation has discovered, at renewal, that the policy does not satisfy what the carrier actually needs. The policy was written to answer "do you have an AI policy?" — a yes/no question. The carrier's renewal questions have evolved to ask for operational evidence: vendor contract status, matter-level AI use records, supervision protocols, per-matter authorization documentation. The policy satisfies the initial attestation's surface question. The policy does not satisfy the renewal's follow-up questions. This is not a policy quality problem — it is a structural mismatch. The policy is the wrong document type for the question being asked. The carrier is asking for evidence of human authorization for specific AI-assisted decisions in specific client matters. The policy is a statement of intent. The carrier wants a record of execution.

**Mechanism:** Initial attestation → firm produces policy → policy answers yes/no → carrier evolves form at renewal → renewal asks for evidence the policy doesn't produce → firm cannot answer follow-up → carrier has grounds for coverage concern.

**Implication:** The service must distinguish between policy documents (intent) and evidence architectures (execution). The client who comes with a policy and a renewal form is the ideal intake for a carrier-form-mapped evidence checklist. The policy is not wrong — it is incomplete for the specification the carrier is now applying.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your policy answers the wrong question. The carrier is asking for evidence — not a document that says you have a policy."

### Finding 02: The Documentation Paradox Is the Primary Action Blocker — Not Ignorance, Fear

**The finding:** The Geneva legal ICP at C309 understands that they need documentation. They also believe, with significant justification, that creating documentation could be used against them in litigation or regulatory proceedings. This is not ignorance — the ICP is not unaware of the documentation obligation. The ICP has concluded that the risk of creating a record (it will be discoverable, it will show what wasn't done adequately, it will be used in litigation) exceeds the risk of not creating a record (absence of records may be inferred as inadequate governance, but records themselves are dangerous). This is the documentation paradox: the tool that would protect the firm (contemporaneous evidence of appropriate supervision) feels like the tool that exposes the firm (created records of AI use in client matters). No Geneva service has resolved this paradox. The ICP is actively avoiding documentation as a result — not from inaction, but from calculated avoidance based on a genuine fear that has not been addressed.

**Mechanism:** ICP recognizes AI governance obligation → ICP attempts to document → ICP concludes created records will be discoverable in litigation → ICP stops documenting → carrier renewal arrives → carrier asks for records → firm has nothing → carrier has grounds to question adequacy → documentation paradox confirmed by experience.

**Implication:** The documentation paradox is the primary service conversion blocker. The ICP who understands the problem but does not act because documenting feels dangerous will not convert on a general "you need better AI governance" pitch. They will convert on a specific "here is exactly what to write, how to write it, and why these records protect you rather than expose you" pitch. The service must resolve the fear before it can advance the documentation.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The fear that documentation will be used against you is the same fear that leaves you with nothing to show when the carrier asks. I'll show you what to write and how to write it so it protects you, not exposes you."

### Finding 03: Managing Partner AI Competence Gap Is Now an Active Personal Liability Problem

**The finding:** The managing partner at the Geneva small firm cannot personally evaluate AI output produced by their associates. The associate is more AI-fluent. The managing partner is personally accountable. The managing partner is signing off on AI-assisted work they do not understand, for purposes of both carrier attestation and professional responsibility. The competence gap is not a training problem — it is a personal liability problem. The managing partner cannot be trained to evaluate AI output in the same way the associate evaluates it. The managing partner's supervision of the associate must be documented in a way that makes the authorization defensible without requiring the managing partner to have substantively reviewed the AI output. This is a specific documentation architecture problem: how to document reasonable supervision of incomprehensible output.

**Mechanism:** Associate uses AI on client matter → managing partner authorizes output → managing partner cannot evaluate AI output → managing partner signs carrier attestation → renewal asks about supervision protocol → managing partner has no documentation of supervision process → personal liability exposure confirmed.

**Implication:** The service must offer a supervision architecture specifically designed for the AI-incompetent managing partner. The output is not "you understand AI better." The output is "here is the documentation that shows you did reasonable supervision of the associate who did understand the AI, and here is why that documentation is sufficient under the carrier's specification." The competence gap is permanent and structural. The documentation architecture must accommodate it.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You are personally responsible for AI-assisted decisions you cannot evaluate. The answer is not to understand AI — it is to document the supervision you did of the associate who did understand it."

### Finding 04: Vendor Compelled Records — A Privilege Exposure Pathway the ICP Is Now Identifying Independently

**The finding:** The Geneva legal ICP has begun, through their own analysis, to identify a specific privilege exposure pathway: AI vendor records are not protected by attorney-client privilege. If a matter goes to litigation, opposing counsel can subpoena the AI vendor. The vendor's records — what AI was used, on what data, with what inputs — are discoverable through the vendor, without the attorney's consent, and without the privilege that would normally protect attorney work product and client communications. The privilege gap is structural and has not been addressed in any AI governance guidance published by the OFL, the FDPIC, or any Geneva bar resource. The C309 ICP is surfacing this question independently across multiple conversations — it is a live market concern that is not being addressed by any current service provider.

**Mechanism:** Client matter goes to litigation → opposing counsel seeks discovery → AI vendor records subpoenaed → vendor compelled to produce AI use records → client matter data and AI use patterns disclosed → attorney-client privilege potentially compromised through vendor → no contractual protection exists in current vendor terms.

**Implication:** Vendor contract privilege review is a discrete service offering — bounded, specific, with a clear deliverable (vendor-by-vendor privilege exposure assessment and contract modification recommendations). This is not "review the vendor's Terms of Service" — it is specifically analyzing whether the vendor's data practices, compelled disclosure obligations, and contract terms create privilege exposure for the attorney's client matters.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your vendor can be compelled to produce records about your client matters. That is a privilege exposure your current contracts don't address."

### Finding 05: AI Billing in Fee Agreements — The Unresolved Commercial Question That Has Become a Client Relationship Problem

**The finding:** The Geneva legal ICP is now confronting a commercial question that has not previously surfaced in AI governance discussions: is it permissible to bill clients for AI-assisted work, and what disclosure obligations exist in the fee agreement? Managing partners are using AI tools to draft, research, and process client work — and billing for that work. Clients are beginning to notice. English-speaking clients, in particular, are beginning to ask directly about AI use in their matters. No Geneva bar guidance exists on AI billing disclosure. No standard fee agreement AI clause exists. The fee agreement is the primary commercial document governing the attorney-client relationship, and it currently has no AI-specific provisions. This creates two simultaneous risks: (1) the firm is billing for AI-assisted work without documented client consent, and (2) the firm has no calibrated response language when clients ask about AI use.

**Mechanism:** Associate uses AI on client matter → time billed to client → client notices AI tool use in billing record or deliverables → client asks about AI use → managing partner gives vague response → client relationship friction → no AI clause in fee agreement to provide basis for disclosure or consent.

**Implication:** Fee agreement AI clause drafting is a discrete, bounded, high-value service deliverable. The clause addresses disclosure, authorization, billing basis, and data processing consent in one document. It is client-facing (the client sees it), it resolves a live commercial question (AI-assisted billing), and it advances AI governance objectives simultaneously (documented client consent). This is the service offering most likely to convert on the "I'm billing for AI-assisted work — is that a problem?" question.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The fee agreement is the document that governs the entire client relationship. If it doesn't have an AI clause, you have no documented basis for AI-assisted billing. I'll draft the clause."

### Finding 06: Carrier Form Sophistication Is Escalating Faster Than Firm Documentation Improves — The Calibration Gap

**The finding:** Carriers serving the Geneva legal market are demonstrably raising the sophistication of their AI attestation forms faster than the firms they insure are improving their AI governance documentation. The carrier that sent a yes/no attestation form in 2024 is now sending forms with specific questions about vendor contracts, matter-level AI use records, associate tool authorization, and supervision protocols. The calibration gap between what carriers know to ask and what firms can answer is widening at each renewal cycle. This is a market structural signal: carriers are learning from claims experience, regulatory pressure, and market benchmarking. The documentation standard is being set by the carrier, and the carrier is raising it faster than the market is responding.

**Mechanism:** Carrier sends initial attestation → carrier reviews submitted attestations → carrier identifies documentation gaps through claims and attestation review → carrier evolves form at next renewal to ask more specific questions → firms that haven't improved documentation are materially under-prepared → coverage or renewal conditions follow.

**Implication:** The carrier form is a market intelligence asset. The service that reads carrier forms systematically, tracks their evolution across renewal cycles, and maps evidence requirements to carrier form questions has a durable competitive advantage. No competitor is doing this. "I read carrier forms for a living" is a specific expertise claim that positions DigitalCoa as the entity that knows what the market standard actually requires — because it is watching the standard evolve in real time.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your carrier's form is more sophisticated than it was two years ago. Your documentation hasn't kept up. I read carrier forms for a living — I'll show you what they're actually asking for."
