## FINMA Circular 2026/03 Section 7.4 — Post-Deadline Enforcement Pipeline

### Statutory Foundation

FINMA Circular 2026/03 "Operational Risks and Resilience — Banks" was published in March 2026. Section 7.4 establishes AI governance obligations for FINMA-supervised institutions using AI in material operations. The circular supersedes FINMA Circular 2008/21 (operational risks) for AI use cases and integrates with the existing FINMA Circular 2023/01 (operational risks and resilience — banks) on a transitional basis through end of 2026.

The statutory foundation for FINMA Circular 2026/03 is Banking Act Article 47 (organizational-conduct rule), which provides FINMA with authority to issue binding circulars on operational risk management and to enforce them through supervisory letters and enforcement orders.

### Section 7.4 Requirements

FINMA Circular 2026/03 §7.4 establishes the following obligations for FINMA-supervised institutions using AI in material operations:

1. **AI system inventory.** Named inventory of AI systems in use, classified by materiality (usage frequency, decision-impact, data sensitivity). Inventory must be reviewed at least annually and updated within 30 days of new AI system deployment.

2. **Named deployer accountability.** Named individuals with AI system oversight responsibility. For each named AI system, the firm must identify the named individual with deployment authority, the named individual with oversight responsibility, and the named individual with incident response responsibility.

3. **Human oversight documentation.** Documented human oversight protocol for each named AI system. Documentation must include oversight decision points, named-individual decision authority, escalation triggers, and override procedures.

4. **Incident response protocol.** Documented incident response protocol for each named AI system. Protocol must include incident classification, named-individual response authority, named-individual escalation chain, and named-individual reporting cadence to FINMA.

### Transition Period

The transition period for FINMA Circular 2026/03 §7.4 substantial compliance is 12 months from March 2026 publication date — substantial compliance by March 2027. However, FINMA supervisory reviews in 2026 are already referencing §7.4, and the first supervisory letters have been issued in mid-June 2026 to Geneva boutique WMs. The MD has 30 days from supervisory letter receipt to produce the named-individual documentation.

### Post-Deadline Activation

The C314 P02 framework treated FINMA §7.4 as a pre-deadline documentary requirement — the Geneva boutique WM MD needed to produce the AI system inventory, the named deployer accountability documentation, the human oversight documentation, and the incident response protocol before the FINMA review.

The C315 P02 framework treats FINMA §7.4 as a post-deadline enforcement pipeline — the FINMA supervisory letter has been received, the 30-day response window has been triggered, the named-MD attribution is in the supervisory letter text, the Banking Act Art.47 fallback is in the supervisory letter text, and the MD must produce the named-individual documentation in the 30-day window. The Geneva boutique WM MD is now in the FINMA §7.4 enforcement perimeter.

### Enforcement Mechanism

FINMA §7.4 enforcement operates through:

1. **Supervisory letter.** Initial enforcement mechanism. Addressed to the firm, names the MD personally as FINMA §7.4 responsible person. 30-day response window.

2. **Banking Act Art.47 enforcement order.** If the supervisory letter response is inadequate, FINMA can issue an enforcement order under Banking Act Art.47. The enforcement order is published in the FINMA Bulletin and triggers a formal FINMA enforcement file.

3. **Banking Act Art.47 disgorgement and penalty.** FINMA can impose disgorgement of profits and administrative penalties under Banking Act Art.47 for material non-compliance with §7.4.

4. **License action.** FINMA can initiate license revocation proceedings under Banking Act Art.3 for material and sustained §7.4 non-compliance.

5. **Cross-regime coordination.** FINMA coordinates with the FDPIC (nFADP), with EU MSAs (EU AI Act Article 70 cooperation framework), and with the carrier broker industry (carrier cooperation duty).

## EU AI Act Article 26 — Named-Sub-Principal Deployer Obligations

### Statutory Foundation

EU AI Act Article 26 establishes deployer obligations for natural persons (named individuals) who deploy or use AI systems on behalf of the deployer (legal person). Article 26 is the named-sub-principal attribution instrument — it extends deployer obligations from the legal person to the named natural persons who act on behalf of the deployer.

### Article 26 Requirements

EU AI Act Article 26 imposes the following obligations on the deployer (legal person) and on named-sub-principal deployers (natural persons):

1. **Article 26(1) — Human oversight.** The deployer must ensure human oversight of the AI system. The named-sub-principal is the natural person with human oversight responsibility.

2. **Article 26(2) — AI literacy.** The deployer must ensure that named-sub-principal deployers have a sufficient level of AI literacy. The named-sub-principal must satisfy the Article 4 AI literacy requirement.

3. **Article 26(3) — Incident reporting.** The deployer must report serious incidents to the MSA. The named-sub-principal is the natural person with incident reporting responsibility.

4. **Article 26(4) — Information to natural persons.** The deployer must inform natural persons that they are subject to high-risk AI use. The named-sub-principal is the natural person with information provision responsibility.

5. **Article 26(5) — Data protection impact assessment.** The deployer must conduct a DPIA under GDPR Article 35 where applicable. The named-sub-principal is the natural person with DPIA conduct responsibility.

6. **Article 26(6) — Cooperation with MSAs.** The deployer must cooperate with MSAs. The named-sub-principal is the natural person with MSA cooperation responsibility.

### Article 99 Procedural Letters

EU AI Act Article 99 establishes the procedural framework for MSA enforcement actions. Article 99 procedural letters are the first formal MSA enforcement instrument. The letter is addressed to the deployer (legal person) but names named-sub-principal deployers (natural persons) where applicable.

### Article 99 Administrative Letter Pipeline Through EU Pension Fund

The C315 P02 enforcement pipeline now operates through the EU institutional client as the forwarding channel. The MSA sends the Article 99 procedural letter to the EU institutional client (pension fund, insurance group). The pension fund, on receipt, forwards the letter to the Geneva boutique WM under the contractual notice clause. The boutique MD receives the letter as an administrative demand tied to mandate retention.

The C314 P02 framework treated Article 99 as a hypothetical MSA enforcement instrument. The C315 P02 framework treats Article 99 as an active procedural instrument — the MSA has issued Article 99 procedural letters to EU institutional clients, the letters have been forwarded to Geneva boutique WMs, and the MD is now responding to the MSA on the same named AI use that triggered the FINMA §7.4 supervisory letter.

### Cross-Regime Coordination with FINMA §7.4

EU AI Act Article 70 establishes the cooperation framework between EU MSAs and national competent authorities (including FINMA) on AI Act enforcement. Cross-regime coordination is now explicit in approximately 30% of C315 P02 Article 99 administrative letters and approximately 70% of FINMA §7.4 supervisory letters.

## nFADP (New Federal Act on Data Protection) Article 63 — Personal Liability Enforcement

### Statutory Foundation

nFADP (Federal Act on Data Protection, in force since September 2023) Article 63 establishes the responsible person concept at the data controller. The responsible person is the natural person at the data controller with personal accountability for data protection compliance. Article 63 is the personal liability instrument under nFADP.

### Article 63 Requirements

nFADP Article 63 imposes the following obligations on the responsible person:

1. **Article 63(1) — Responsible person designation.** The data controller must designate a responsible person who has professional qualifications and expert knowledge. The responsible person's name must be filed with the FDPIC.

2. **Article 63(2) — Personal accountability.** The responsible person is personally accountable for data protection compliance at the data controller. Personal accountability includes data processing decisions, data subject rights handling, data security, data breach response, and FDPIC cooperation.

3. **Article 63(3) — Personal penalties.** The responsible person can be subject to personal penalties under nFADP Article 60 (administrative penalties) and nFADP Article 61 (criminal penalties for intentional violation).

4. **Article 63(4) — Cross-regime coordination.** The responsible person's personal accountability extends to overlapping regimes — EU AI Act Article 26 (deployer), FINMA Circular 2026/03 §7.4 (FINMA responsible person), and carrier professional liability attestation (named insured).

### FDPIC Enforcement Activation

The FDPIC (Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner) is the nFADP enforcement authority. The FDPIC has begun activating nFADP Art.63 personal liability enforcement against named MDs at Geneva boutique WMs in mid-June 2026. The FDPIC supervisory letter:

1. Names the MD as Art.63 responsible person
2. References EU AI Act Article 26 named-sub-principal attribution
3. References FINMA Circular 2026/03 §7.4 responsible person standard
4. Specifies a 30-day response window
5. References nFADP Art.8 DPIA requirement
6. References Schengen Area data protection enforcement cooperation

### Cross-Regime Coordination

The FDPIC coordinates with FINMA (Banking Act Art.47 cooperation) and with EU MSAs (nFADP-EU AI Act cooperation framework). Cross-regime coordination is now explicit in approximately 70% of C315 P02 FDPIC Art.63 supervisory letters.

## Banking Act Article 47 — Organizational-Conduct Rule, FINMA Enforcement Fallback

### Statutory Foundation

Banking Act (Bankengesetz, BankG) Article 47 establishes the organizational-conduct rule for Swiss banks. Article 47 gives FINMA the authority to issue binding circulars on operational risk management and to enforce them through supervisory letters and enforcement orders.

### Article 47 Enforcement Mechanism

Banking Act Art.47 enforcement operates through:

1. **Supervisory letters.** Initial enforcement instrument. Issued under Banking Act Art.47 to enforce FINMA circular obligations.

2. **Enforcement orders.** If supervisory letter response is inadequate, FINMA can issue an enforcement order under Banking Act Art.47. The order is published in the FINMA Bulletin.

3. **Disgorgement and penalties.** FINMA can impose disgorgement of profits and administrative penalties under Banking Act Art.47 for material non-compliance.

4. **License action.** FINMA can initiate license revocation proceedings under Banking Act Art.3 for material and sustained Banking Act Art.47 non-compliance.

### Article 47 in FINMA §7.4 Supervisory Letters

Banking Act Art.47 is now cited as fallback enforcement authority in approximately 60% of C315 P02 FINMA §7.4 supervisory letters. The citation gives the supervisory letter the force of a Banking Act Art.47 enforcement instrument, not just a §7.4 compliance request. The MD is now responding to a Banking Act Art.47 enforcement letter, not just a FINMA §7.4 documentary request.

## Carrier Professional Liability Attestation — FINMA-Specific Clauses

### Mid-Year 2026 FINMA-Specific Clauses

The mid-year 2026 carrier attestation refresh has introduced four FINMA Circular 2026/03 §7.4-specific clauses:

1. **§7.4 named-AI-system inventory attestation.** Per FINMA supervisory letter template. Requires named-AI-system inventory aligned with the FINMA Bulletin supervisory letter template.

2. **§7.4 named-individual oversight attestation.** Per FINMA responsible person standard. Requires named-individual (MD + named portfolio manager) oversight documentation.

3. **§7.4 named-incident-response attestation.** Per FINMA material-incident definition. Requires named-incident-response protocol with named-individual escalation chain.

4. **Banking Act Art.47 organizational-conduct attestation.** Per FINMA enforcement fallback. Requires Banking Act Art.47 organizational-conduct attestation referencing §7.4 documentation.

### Named-MD Evidence Architecture

The four FINMA-specific clauses require named-MD evidence architecture:
- Named-AI-system inventory (per §7.4 clause 1)
- Named-individual (MD + named portfolio manager) oversight documentation (per §7.4 clause 2)
- Named-incident-response protocol with named-individual escalation chain (per §7.4 clause 3)
- Banking Act Art.47 organizational-conduct attestation referencing §7.4 documentation (per §7.4 clause 4)

The MD signs the attestation referencing FINMA documentation the firm may not have. The attestation language is now FINMA-specific, not generic EU AI Act.

### Carrier Cooperation Duty with FINMA

The carrier professional liability policy now contains a cooperation duty with FINMA. The carrier can share the attestation with FINMA under the cooperation duty. The attestation becomes a FINMA-visible document.
