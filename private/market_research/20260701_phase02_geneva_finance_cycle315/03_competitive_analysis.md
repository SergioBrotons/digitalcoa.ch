## Market Map (C315 P02)

The Geneva boutique wealth management AI accountability market in July 2026 is structurally similar to the C314 P02 market (pre-deadline readiness) but with a critical operational shift: the FINMA supervisory pipeline, the MSA Article 99 administrative letter pipeline, the FDPIC Art.63 personal liability enforcement, the carrier FINMA-specific attestation clauses, the Geneva Private Banking Week peer disclosure cycle, and the sub-processor vendor enforcement audit are now active signals, not hypothetical scenarios.

The C314 P02 market offered pre-deadline readiness frameworks. The C315 P02 market needs post-deadline FINMA-Article 26 enforcement navigation.

## Competitive Positioning (C315 P02)

### Position 1: FINMA Compliance Consulting for Large Banks (Not Verticalized)

The FINMA compliance consulting firms serving large Swiss banks (UBS, Credit Suisse remnants, Julius Bär, Pictet, Lombard Odier) have FINMA Circular 2026/03 §7.4 response architectures. These architectures are not verticalized to Geneva boutique WMs. They assume a 500+ employee organization, a Chief Compliance Officer function, a General Counsel function, and an internal audit function. The Geneva boutique WM has 50-200 employees, no CCO function, no GC function, and an MD who personally signs the regulatory response. The large-bank architecture does not fit.

**C315 P02 gap:** No FINMA §7.4 response architecture is designed for the 50-200 employee Geneva boutique WM with MD-named attribution and no internal compliance function.

### Position 2: Generic AI Governance Consultancies (Not FINMA-Specific)

The AI governance consultancies (large international firms and boutique AI ethics shops) offer generic AI governance frameworks that reference EU AI Act, nFADP, and FINMA in passing. These frameworks are not FINMA-specific. They do not reference Circular 2026/03 §7.4 by section number. They do not integrate Banking Act Art.47 fallback language. They do not produce named-MD attribution documentation. They do not produce named-AI-system inventory aligned with the FINMA Bulletin supervisory letter template.

**C315 P02 gap:** No generic AI governance consultancy produces FINMA-Circular-specific named-MD attribution documentation aligned with the July 2026 FINMA Bulletin supervisory letter template.

### Position 3: Carrier Broker Pre-Attestation Services (Not FINMA-Clause-Specific)

The carrier broker firms (Marsh, Aon, Willis Towers Watson, and Geneva-based boutique brokers) offer pre-attestation services for the annual carrier attestation renewal. The mid-year 2026 FINMA-specific attestation refresh has introduced four new clauses that the broker services are scrambling to translate. The broker services are not designed to produce the named-MD evidence (named-AI-system inventory, named-individual oversight, named-incident-response) that the FINMA-specific clauses now require. The broker services offer language review, not evidence architecture.

**C315 P02 gap:** No carrier broker service produces the named-MD evidence architecture for the four mid-year 2026 FINMA Circular 2026/03 §7.4-specific attestation clauses.

### Position 4: Portfolio Construction AI Vendor Compliance Claims (Contradicted by MSA Audit Reality)

The Swiss-hosted portfolio construction AI vendors have built compliance claim architectures around nFADP Art.9 Swiss-hosted positioning, EU AI Act readiness, and sub-processor transparency. The MSA sub-processor enforcement audits have begun revealing that the vendor's sub-processor chain routes mandate metadata extraterritorially, that the named-portfolio-manager activity is captured in vendor logs, and that the vendor's cooperation duty with the MSA exposes the Geneva boutique's identity. The vendor's claim architecture is now contradicted by the MSA audit reality.

**C315 P02 gap:** No vendor produces a response architecture that integrates the MSA sub-processor enforcement audit pipeline with the named-portfolio-manager activity disclosure and the Geneva boutique's cooperation framework.

### Position 5: GPBA / Swiss WM Informal Peer Networks (Unstructured Disclosure)

The Geneva Private Bankers Association (GPBA) and Swiss WM offer informal peer disclosure through Geneva Private Banking Week and mailing list communications. The September 2026 panel disclosure was the first structured informal disclosure benchmark. The second wave of self-disclosure is unstructured — MDs are talking to peers but have no formal response methodology for what to disclose, when to disclose, to whom to disclose, and how to coordinate disclosure with FINMA supervisory letter response architecture.

**C315 P02 gap:** No GPBA / Swiss WM service produces a formal disclosure response methodology for the second-wave disclosure pressure created by Geneva Private Banking Week 2026.

### Position 6: FDPIC Complaint Response Services (Not MD-Named-Attribution-Specific)

The FDPIC complaint response services (consumer-facing and SME-facing) handle nFADP complaints. They do not handle nFADP Art.63 supervisory letters naming MDs at Geneva boutique WMs. The cross-regime coordination architecture (nFADP Art.63 + EU AI Act Article 26 + FINMA §7.4) is not in their scope. The nFADP Art.8 DPIA integration is not in their scope. The Schengen Area data protection enforcement cooperation is not in their scope.

**C315 P02 gap:** No FDPIC complaint response service produces an Art.63 supervisory letter response architecture for named MDs at Geneva boutique WMs with cross-regime coordination documentation.
