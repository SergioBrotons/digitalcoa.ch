## 05_lead_magnet.md

# The Boutique Wealth Manager's AI Governance Readiness Checklist
### A 3-Layer Compliance Framework for Geneva's Independent WM Firms and Family Offices

---

## Executive Summary

CARF and CRS 2.0 have been operative since January 1, 2026. FATF's mutual evaluation of Switzerland is underway. AMLA revision arrives mid-2026. If your firm uses AI tools to manage client portfolios, analyze risk, or generate reports — and you don't have a formal AI governance framework — you're exposed on multiple layers simultaneously.

This checklist gives you a clear path to:
1. Assess your 3-layer compliance posture
2. Identify Shadow AI gaps
3. Build a CARF/FATF-aligned AI governance framework

**CARF is already operative. The FATF evaluators are already in Switzerland. Your window is now.**

---

## Section 1: The 3-Layer Compliance Stack

**Layer 1 — Swiss Domestic (FINMA)**
- FINMA Circ. 08/2024: AI in customer-facing applications — transparency, documentation, explainability
- FINMA Circ. 2026/1: Nature-related financial risks (Jan 1 2026) — AI in ESG/sustainability contexts

**Layer 2 — Swiss International (CARF/CRS)**
- CARF/CRS 2.0 operative since Jan 1, 2026 (~3 months already)
- AI systems used in tax reporting, client classification, account reporting — in scope
- Common Reporting Standard: automatic exchange of financial account information
- Country-by-Country Reporting: augmented due diligence requirements

**Layer 3 — International Pressure (FATF)**
- FATF mutual evaluation: Switzerland 2026–2027/2028
- AMLA revision: mid-2026 — enhanced client due diligence, AI transaction monitoring
- LETA Transparency Register: 30-day discrepancy reporting, H2 2026

**The intersection:** AI tools used for client portfolio management, risk analysis, or reporting touch all three layers simultaneously.

---

## Section 2: The Shadow AI Problem in Wealth Management

**What Shadow AI looks like in WM:**
- Portfolio managers using ChatGPT to summarize client meeting notes → client data leaves firm systems
- Risk analysts using AI tools to model portfolio scenarios → financial data processed externally
- Client service teams using AI to draft performance reports → client P&L data shared with AI providers
- Advisors using AI to research investment opportunities → firm intellectual property shared externally

**The exposure:**
1. **AML/KYC:** AI tools processing client financial data without documented due diligence
2. **FATF:** AI transaction monitoring systems — if not governed, may not meet FATF standards
3. **CARF:** AI systems in scope for CRS reporting — reporting errors = international exposure
4. **FADP/nFADP:** Client financial data processed by AI tools without documented consent/dPIA
5. **FINMA:** AI in customer-facing applications — documentation and explainability obligations

**Phase 01 finding (cross-phase):** 69% of professionals use AI individually. Only 34% of firms have formal AI policies. In WM (76% AI adoption — highest sector), the Shadow AI gap is widest.

---

## Section 3: CARF and AI — What You Need to Know

**CARF/CRS 2.0 operative since Jan 1, 2026:**

**AI systems in scope include:**
- Automated client classification systems (CRS due diligence)
- AI-driven transaction monitoring for reportable transactions
- Portfolio data aggregation tools used for FATCA/CRS reporting
- Machine-readable jurisdiction classifications

**CARF compliance obligations:**
- Document AI systems used in the reporting pipeline
- Ensure AI outputs are accurate, auditable, and explainable
- Maintain audit trails for AI-assisted classifications
- Report discrepancies within 30 days

**The FATF connection:**
- FATF evaluators will assess AI use in financial crime compliance
- AI transaction monitoring systems must meet FATF standards
- Manual override capabilities must be documented
- Governance framework for AI in AML must be demonstrable

## Section 4: The AI Governance Readiness Checklist

### A. CARF/FATF AI Governance (Immediate)

- [ ] Inventory all AI tools used in client reporting, portfolio analysis, and transaction monitoring
- [ ] Document AI systems in the CRS/FATCA reporting pipeline
- [ ] Assess whether AI tools processing client financial data meet FINMA Circ. 08/2024 requirements
- [ ] Verify AI transaction monitoring systems have documented FATF alignment
- [ ] Confirm 30-day discrepancy reporting process for CARF errors
- [ ] Assess AI tools for CRS due diligence (client classification, account reporting)

### B. AML/KYC AI Governance (Near-Term)

- [ ] Map AI tools used in client onboarding and ongoing due diligence
- [ ] Document AI risk-scoring models used in client classification
- [ ] Assess AI tools for PEP/sanctions screening
- [ ] Verify AI-enhanced transaction monitoring has adequate human oversight
- [ ] Prepare for AMLA revision (mid-2026) — enhanced due diligence requirements
- [ ] Assess LETA Transparency Register readiness (H2 2026)

### C. Shadow AI Audit (Decision 0)

- [ ] Survey portfolio managers, analysts, client service staff: what AI tools are you using?
- [ ] Identify which tools process client financial data
- [ ] Assess data flows: where does client data go when processed by AI?
- [ ] Identify gaps between approved AI tools and actual AI use
- [ ] Flag AI tools that may not meet FADP requirements for financial data processing
- [ ] Document purpose, functionality, data sources for each AI tool (FDPIC three-part test)

### D. DPIA for High-Risk AI (Required)

- [ ] Conduct DPIA for AI tools processing client financial data (FADP Art. 22)
- [ ] AI in contract analysis for investment advisory = likely high-risk
- [ ] AI in client risk profiling = likely high-risk
- [ ] AI in transaction monitoring = likely high-risk
- [ ] Document mitigation measures for identified risks

## Section 5: FATF Evaluation — What to Expect

**Timeline:** Switzerland 2026–2027/2028 (mutual evaluation)

**What FATF evaluators assess:**
- AML/CFT framework effectiveness
- Financial crime compliance across all regulated entities
- AI use in financial crime compliance (increasingly in scope)
- Transaction monitoring systems and their governance
- SAR (Suspicious Activity Report) filing practices

**FATF and AI:** FATF is increasingly focused on AI use in financial crime compliance. AI transaction monitoring, risk scoring, and client classification systems will be in scope.

**What FATF wants to see:**
- Documented AI governance framework
- Evidence of human oversight in AI-assisted decisions
- Audit trails for AI-assisted classifications
- Evidence of regular AI system testing and validation
- Staff training on AI compliance obligations

## Section 6: SRO Membership and AI Obligations

**Who needs SRO membership:**
- Independent financial advisors (non-bank)
- Family offices (depending on structure)
- Trustees, notaries, lawyers (if providing WM services)

**SRO obligations:**
- Membership in FINMA-recognized SRO (Swiss Financial Assn, AOOS, OAR-G, etc.)
- AML compliance program (including AI transaction monitoring)
- Client due diligence (including AI-enhanced KYC tools)
- Suspicious activity reporting
- Annual audit

**AI obligations under SRO membership:**
- Document AI tools used in client onboarding and monitoring
- Assess AI tools against AML/CFT standards
- Ensure AI systems have adequate human oversight
- Report AI-related incidents to SRO
