### English Version

**The question I keep hearing from Geneva managing partners isn't "Should we use AI?"**

It's "How do I sleep at night knowing my associates are using AI on client matters?"

That question tells me everything. You've moved past the theoretical. You're not asking whether AI is coming — you're asking how to use it without losing your license, your carrier coverage, or your reputation in the Geneva legal community.

Here's what I know to be true after working with law firms on this exact problem:

**The SBA Guidelines exist. But they're not a checklist.**

The Swiss Bar Association published AI guidelines in June 2024. They gave us three paths: internal deployment, compliant outsourcing, or client consent. That's useful as a framework. But a framework doesn't tell you whether the AI tool you're already using — the one your associate signed up for on a personal account — actually complies.

If you've been waiting for a definitive list of approved tools, I'm telling you: it doesn't exist. And it won't, not until after the Council of Europe AI Convention implementation in 2027 at the earliest. You're operating in the meantime.

**Your carrier is starting to ask about AI. Are you ready to answer?**

Professional indemnity carriers have begun including AI-specific attestation questions. If your carrier asked you today what AI tools your firm uses, whether you have DPAs in place, and whether you have a written AI governance policy — could you answer all three without hesitation?

If you're like most 2–5 attorney firms I speak with, the honest answer is: not fully.

That's not a criticism. It's the gap I'm trying to close.

**The question isn't whether AI is a risk. It's whether you're managing that risk deliberately.**

I've watched firms take two approaches. Some bury their heads — no policy, no documentation, hoping nothing goes wrong. Others freeze entirely, convinced they can't touch AI until legislation provides complete clarity.

Neither works.

What does work: understanding where the actual liability sits, building the minimum documentation your carrier needs, and making deliberate tool choices rather than ad-hoc ones.

**Here's what I'd do if I were in your position:**

First, I'd audit every AI tool currently being used in your practice. That includes tools individual attorneys may have subscribed to on personal accounts. You can't govern what you don't know.

Second, I'd get a Data Processing Agreement in place with every AI vendor touching client data. Not eventually — now. If a vendor can't provide one, that's your answer: find another tool.

Third, I'd write a one-page AI governance policy. Not a 30-page framework designed for a 50-lawyer firm. A practical, honest document that reflects what your firm actually does. It doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to exist and be honest.

Fourth, I'd document how you're choosing tools and which SBA compliance path each one falls under. If your carrier asks, you want a clear answer — not a defensive pause.

**The exposure is real. But so is the path forward.**

I've seen the Federal Supreme Court decision. External AI providers are your "auxiliaries" under professional secrecy law. You remain responsible for what they do with client data, even if you didn't intend a breach. That's a significant personal exposure.

But it's not unmanageable. It requires deliberation, documentation, and a refusal to use AI tools that can't meet minimum compliance standards.

If you're a managing partner or senior associate at a small Geneva firm, and you're trying to figure out what "adequate" AI governance actually looks like at your scale — I'm working on exactly this problem. Reach out if you want to talk it through.

The goal isn't to avoid AI. It's to use it without it using you.
