## Applicable Regulatory Framework (as of May 2026)

### 1. The Regulatory Architecture

**No Dedicated Swiss AI Legislation (yet)**
Switzerland has not enacted AI-specific legislation as of May 2026. This creates a principles-and-guidelines environment rather than a rules-based one.

**Council of Europe AI Convention — Signed March 2025**
- Switzerland signed on March 27, 2025
- Covers AI systems, human rights, democracy, rule of law
- Requires implementation via sector-specific amendments and cross-sectoral fundamental rights regulation
- Consultation draft for implementation: **not expected before end of 2026**
- Timeline gap: the vacuum will persist through at least 2026-2027

**Existing Laws That Apply to AI:**

| Law | Application to AI |
|---|---|
| Federal Act on Data Protection (FADP) | Applies to AI-supported data processing directly — confirmed by FDPIC, May 2025 |
| Federal Act on Attorneys (FMLA) | Professional obligations, including professional secrecy, apply to AI use |
| Swiss Code of Professional Conduct (SCPC) | Articles on diligence, confidentiality apply to AI-assisted work |
| Article 321 Swiss Criminal Code | Criminal liability for breach of professional secrecy — applies to AI-related leaks |
| Product Liability Act (PLA) | Technology-neutral — applies to AI-related harm |
| Federal Copyright Act (CopA) | AI training on copyrighted material — open legal questions, likely amendments |

### 2. Swiss Bar Association Guidelines on AI (June 2024)

**Adopted:** June 2024 by the Council of the Swiss Bar Association (SBA)

**Core Principle:** Lawyers remain fully accountable for their professional obligations — including professional secrecy — even when using AI. The use of AI does not reduce or modify these obligations.

**Three Acceptable Compliance Paths (as defined by SBA):**

**Path 1: Internal Solution**
- AI application installed and operated locally within the law firm's own network
- Data never leaves firm infrastructure for storage or processing
- Most compliant but requires advanced technical expertise
- Realistic cost: significant IT investment; not realistic for most 2-5 attorney firms

**Path 2: Compliant Outsourcing**
- External AI service provider meets Swiss data protection and professional secrecy standards
- Requires robust Data Processing Agreement (DPA) with the vendor
- Must comply with SBA Cloud Computing Guidelines
- Critical precedent: Federal Supreme Court recognized external provider as "auxiliary" under Art. 13(2) FMLA — lawyer remains liable for ensuring third party respects professional secrecy

**Path 3: Informed Consent**
- Client signs express, formal declaration partially waiving professional secrecy and FADP protections
- Client must be fully informed of risks
- Practical challenge: clients rarely understand technical implications of consent forms
- Risk: consent may not be truly "informed" if client doesn't understand AI processing risks

**What the Guidelines Don't Provide:**
- No approved vendor list
- No technical standards or certifications
- No compliance checklist
- No definition of "adequate" at small-firm scale
- No carrier-specific guidance

### 3. Attorney-Client Privilege / Professional Secrecy Framework

**Legal Basis:**
- Article 13 FMLA: attorneys must maintain confidentiality
- Article 321 Swiss Criminal Code: deliberate breach = criminal offense (monetary penalty or up to 3 years custody)
- Article 398 Code of Obligations: mandate confidentiality duty
- Article 160 Swiss Code of Civil Procedure: right to refuse testimony and document production
- Article 264 Swiss Criminal Procedure Code: same protection in criminal matters
- Since January 1, 2025: in civil proceedings, parties and third parties also have right to refuse testimony regarding in-house legal services activities

**What Is Covered:**
- All information entrusted to a lawyer for the purpose of carrying out their mandate
- Direct and indirect communications
- Physical and electronic documents
- Activities considered part of typical lawyer duties: legal advice, court representation, contract drafting, internal investigations

**Implication for AI:**
- Any client information entered into an AI system is potentially covered by professional secrecy
- Breach occurs if data leaves the firm in an unauthorized way — which can happen through cloud-based AI processing
- The lawyer is always primarily liable — even if the breach is caused by the AI vendor
- No AI-specific safe harbor or carve-out exists

### 4. FADP and AI Data Processing (May 2025 Update)

**FADP Application to AI — Confirmed May 2025:**
- The Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner (FDPIC) confirmed FADP applies directly to AI-supported data processing
- Key FADP principles applicable to AI: lawfulness, purpose limitation, proportionality

**What This Means in Practice:**
- AI data processing must be lawful (base, purpose)
- Data used for AI processing must be purpose-limited (not repurposed)
- Processing must be proportional (minimum necessary data)
- Anonymization standards must meet FADP requirements — not clearly defined for legal documents

**Intersection with Professional Secrecy:**
- FADP compliance does not automatically satisfy professional secrecy obligations
- A tool that is FADP-compliant may still breach professional secrecy if it processes confidential client data through servers outside Switzerland without adequate safeguards
- The two frameworks interact — small firms need guidance on both simultaneously

### 5. Carrier / Insurance Attestation Context

**Current Market Reality:**
- PI (Professional Indemnity) carriers are beginning to ask AI-specific questions on attestation forms
- Questions typically cover: what AI tools are used, whether DPAs are in place, whether a firm AI policy exists
- No standardized industry response — each carrier has its own form
- Small firms without documentation are at risk of either non-renewal or coverage gaps in the event of a claim

**The Attestation Problem:**
- Carriers want evidence of "adequate" AI governance
- No one has defined what "adequate" means for a 2-5 attorney firm
- A carrier asking this question cannot itself define what a satisfactory answer looks like
- Creates circular uncertainty: firms don't know what to attest to; carriers don't know what to require

### 6. Geneva-Specific Considerations

**Canton of Geneva Context:**
- Geneva is the largest legal market in Switzerland
- French is the primary language of legal practice
- Geneva Bar requires compliance with SBA guidelines at minimum
- No separate Geneva-specific AI guidance beyond SBA framework

**Geneva Professional Culture:**
- Strong tradition of lawyer confidentiality
- Geneva legal community is tight — incidents travel fast and damage reputation significantly
- Managing partners in Geneva are personally known to each other — reputational stakes are high
- Boutique culture means no anonymity for mistakes

**Practical Challenge for Small Geneva Firms:**
- French-language AI tools are limited; most compliance resources are in English or German
- Geneva-based lawyers need French-contextualized guidance — most Swiss legal tech content is German-centric
- Small firm + French-speaking + AI compliance = underserved segment

## Summary: Compliance Context for Small Geneva Firms

**The Problem:** No AI-specific legislation. SBA Guidelines are principles without checklists. FADP applies but lacks AI-specific guidance. Carrier attestation asks questions no one can answer.

**The Exposure:** Personal liability under Article 321 CP. Carrier coverage risk. No safe harbor for AI use. The lawyer is always responsible.

**The Opportunity:** A practical, Swiss, small-firm-appropriate AI governance framework doesn't exist — creating white space for one that does.
