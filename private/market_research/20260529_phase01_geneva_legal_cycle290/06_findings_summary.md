# Findings Summary: Geneva Small Law Firm AI Accountability

## Phase 01 — Cycle 290

---

## Key Insights

### Insight 1: The Regulatory Vacuum Is Structural, Not Temporary

Switzerland signed the Council of Europe AI Convention in March 2025 but won't produce an implementation consultation draft before end of 2026. Combined with no domestic AI-specific legislation, small law firms are operating in a principles-only environment. The SBA Guidelines (June 2024) are the current ceiling of formal guidance. This vacuum is structural — it will persist at least through 2027.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You're not waiting for clarity. You're operating in a regulatory vacuum — and everyone else is too."

---

### Insight 2: Professional Accountability Has No AI Carve-Out

Article 321 Swiss Criminal Code makes breach of professional secrecy a criminal offense. There is no AI-specific safe harbor. The Federal Supreme Court confirmed external AI providers are lawyer "auxiliaries" — meaning the lawyer remains personally liable for vendor mishandling of client data. This personal, criminal-level exposure is not widely understood by small firm managing partners.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your carrier may cover the financial loss. The criminal exposure under Article 321, your license — that's yours alone."

---

### Insight 3: Carrier Attestation Is the Trigger Point

PI carriers are now asking AI-specific attestation questions. This is the most immediate pressure point for small firms — more pressing than legislation, more immediate than bar guidance. A firm that cannot answer three questions (what AI tools, do you have DPAs, do you have a written AI policy) faces coverage risk. This creates a concrete, near-term problem to solve.

**Ready-to-use language:** "If your carrier asked today what AI tools your firm uses — could you answer without hesitation? Most can't."

---

### Insight 4: The FADP–Professional Secrecy Intersection Is Unmanaged

The FDPIC confirmed in May 2025 that the FADP applies directly to AI data processing. But FADP compliance does not equal professional secrecy compliance. A tool can be FADP-compliant and still breach professional secrecy if it processes data through servers that don't meet Swiss professional standards. Small firms don't understand this distinction — or the fact that they need both frameworks simultaneously.

**Ready-to-use language:** "FADP compliance is not the same as professional secrecy compliance. Most AI vendors sell you the first; none guarantee the second."

---

### Insight 5: The Market Has Produced No Small-Firm-Appropriate Solution

Every existing AI governance framework, tool, and template is designed for enterprises or generic use. No one has defined "adequate AI governance at boutique scale" — not the SBA, not carriers, not legal tech vendors. The white space is significant: a practical, carrier-ready, 2-5 attorney Swiss law firm AI governance package does not exist in the market.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Enterprise frameworks don't translate. There's no off-the-shelf solution for a 2-to-5 attorney Geneva firm — because no one bothered to build one."

---

### Insight 6: Small Firm Decision Dynamics Are Peer-Driven and Fear-Based

Small Geneva firms watch their peers. Incidents travel fast in the Geneva legal community. The decision to adopt AI (or avoid it) is driven more by fear of an incident than by positive ROI arguments. This means that credible, anonymized positive case studies from similar-sized Swiss firms would be highly persuasive — and are currently nonexistent.

**Ready-to-use language:** "In Geneva legal community, your reputation is everything. The question isn't whether AI will touch your practice — it's whether you'll be the firm that handled it responsibly, or the one that became a cautionary tale."
