## C115 Phase 04 NET-NEW Regulatory Findings (2026-04-04)

### C115 Regulatory Finding 1: Humanitarian technology impact measurement — regulatory and donor compliance dimension
- Donors and impact investors increasingly requiring quantified humanitarian outcomes tied to tech investments — not just output metrics
- ICRC, UNHCR, WFP developing outcome-based technology assessment methodologies with donor-facing reporting requirements
- **Advisory implication:** Geneva NGOs/IOs need frameworks aligning technology deployment with measurable humanitarian outcomes; distinct from commercial ROI frameworks
- **Regulatory adjacency:** Not a hard regulatory requirement but a de facto donor compliance standard increasingly enforced through funding conditions

### C115 Regulatory Finding 2: Swiss private philanthropy regulatory distinctiveness
- Swiss family office and private foundation funding operates under different regulatory frameworks than government/multilateral donors
- Swiss Foundations Act (ZGB Art. 80ff) governs Swiss foundations; cross-border philanthropy advisory requires understanding of Swiss regulatory distinctiveness
- **Advisory implication:** Geneva NGOs seeking Swiss private philanthropy need regulatory navigation beyond standard donor compliance — foundation governance, perpetual capital restrictions, impact reporting norms
- **French language dimension:** Key Swiss philanthropists (Lombard Odier Foundation, Radcliffe, Suyan) operate in French-speaking context; EN+FR advisory materials essential

### C115 Regulatory Finding 3: Domestic Swiss political risk to IO privileges and immunities
- Geneva's IO privileges and immunities (derived from Swiss-IO headquarters agreements) face domestic political scrutiny
- Reduced Swiss contributions to IOs could trigger renegotiation of hosting terms; threat to IO operational autonomy
- **Advisory implication:** IO financial sustainability planning must incorporate political risk scenarios; scenario modeling for reduced Swiss host-country support
- **Distinct from UN80 reform:** UN80 is UN internal; domestic Swiss political pressure is external and ongoing

### C115 Regulatory Finding 4: Digital Public Goods (DPG) certification — emerging regulatory-adjacent standard
- ITU/WSIS DPG framework advancing as open standard for software, data, AI models — Geneva-originated
- DPG certification becoming de facto requirement for EU development funding access and IO procurement qualification
- **Advisory implication:** Geneva tech firms seeking IO procurement need DPG readiness assessment; DPG-aligned open-source contribution strategy
- **Regulatory status:** DPG is voluntary certification but functionally required for certain IO and EU funding pathways

### C115 Regulatory Finding 5: Geneva AI talent immigration — Swiss work permit regulatory considerations
- US AI talent relocating to Switzerland requires understanding of Swiss work permit system (B/L/欧盟 Blue Card equivalents)
- Canton de Genève immigration competence for EU/EFTA vs. third-country nationals different; startup visa programs relevant
- **Advisory implication:** Geneva startups and IOs recruiting international AI talent need work permit navigation advisory; Swiss startup visa Fast Track (FONGIT-EDIU) available
- **Regulatory dimension:** AI talent immigration is work permit / residency permit navigation, not AI-specific regulation

### C115 Regulatory Finding 6: UN agency AI procurement compliance — vendor qualification and accountability frameworks
- WHO, UNHCR, WFP, WTO AI procurements subject to UN Financial Regulations and Rules (FRR) — distinct from commercial procurement
- UN vendor registration (UNGM) and AI-specific vendor due diligence requirements creating barriers for Geneva startups
- **Advisory implication:** Geneva tech firms need UN procurement navigation and vendor qualification support; AI implementation governance for IO deployment contexts
- **Distinct from EU AI Act:** UN procurement compliance is a separate regulatory layer for Geneva firms targeting IO clients
