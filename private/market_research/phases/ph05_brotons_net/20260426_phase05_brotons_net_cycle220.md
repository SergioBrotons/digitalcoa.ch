## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### How Readers Describe Their AI Governance Information Needs — Real Language

The Geneva professional reading Brotons.net has usually been thinking about AI governance for at least a year. They are past the introductory stage. They have initial frameworks, some policy documents, and a growing sense that what they built initially may not hold. What they need now is more specific than what the existing content environment provides.

**Core reading interests in their own register:**

- "I have read most of the frameworks. What I do not have is a clear picture of what good actually looks like in Geneva practice — what the firms that are doing this well are actually doing." — The maturity-stage reader needs reference points, not introductions.

- "The conversation about AI governance at conferences is at a level of abstraction that does not help me make decisions for my firm on Monday morning." — Geneva's AI knowledge production is perceived as disconnected from practitioner-relevant application.

- "I need to understand what the Federal Council's mid-2026 decision means for what I am supposed to be doing right now — not in theory, but in my practice." — Regulatory calendar as a decision-timing driver. The reader wants to know what to do before and after a specific date.

- "When a client sends me an AI governance questionnaire, I do not know what a good answer looks like. I do not know what the standard is." — Counterparty verification escalation. The ICP is being asked to demonstrate AI governance practice, not just document it.

- "The question I get from clients is not 'do you use AI' — it is 'what standard do you apply.' I do not have a good answer." — The framing question. The ICP needs to know how to characterize their AI governance practice to clients and counterparties.

- "I want to understand what the Geneva-specific dimension is. Generic AI governance content applies everywhere. I need to know what is specific to operating in Geneva." — Swiss institutional specificity as a credibility filter. The ICP discounts advisors whose framing could apply to any jurisdiction.

## 02 — Market Signals

### What's Happening in the Swiss AI Governance Information Space

**Signal 1 — The Maturity-Stage Reader Has Arrived**
The Geneva professional who reads Brotons.net has typically been at AI governance for 18–24 months. They have initial frameworks. They have policies. They are now asking different questions: What does good look like? What should we be doing that we are not doing? How do we know if our practice is defensible? The content environment has not shifted to serve this reader. Most Swiss AI governance content is introductory or framework-focused.

**Signal 2 — Counterparty Verification Has Escalated Beyond Questionnaires**
The era of AI governance questionnaires is ending. Counterparties — clients, insurance underwriters, institutional partners — are now asking for evidence of practice, not just policy documents. The shift from "do you have a policy" to "demonstrate your practice" is a new genre of demand. No publisher has produced practitioner-facing content on what evidence of AI governance practice looks like.

**Signal 3 — Federal Council Mid-2026 as Decision Reference Point**
The Federal Council's mid-2026 window on binding AI legislation is creating a decision-making reference point for practitioners. The question is not "what does the law say" — it is "how should I be preparing for what the law will say." This creates a time-bounded editorial opportunity around what practitioners should be doing right now, before the deadline, as opposed to after.

**Signal 4 — Geneva Institutional Activity vs. Practitioner-Relevant Output Widening**
Geneva hosts more AI standard-setting activity than any other European city. The pipeline from Geneva platform activity to practitioner-relevant guidance is broken. Practitioners experience this as a gap between "lots of AI talk" and "nothing that helps my practice on Monday." The credibility opportunity is in translating Geneva platform activity into practitioner-relevant content.

**Signal 5 — AI Governance Terminology Precision as Professional Exposure**
The specific words practitioners use to describe their AI governance practices matter — in client communications, in insurance applications, in counterparty responses, in bar association contexts. Imprecise language creates exposure that is invisible until it surfaces in a dispute or a review. No publisher has produced terminology precision guidance specific to the Geneva professional context.

**Signal 6 — Brotons.net Voice as a Differentiator**
In a content environment where AI governance claims are generic and hard to verify independently, the calm, precise, practitioner-first voice of Brotons.net is itself a credibility signal. The voice communicates something no framework can: that the author understands what the practice actually requires.

## 04 — Compliance/Editorial Context

### Swiss Regulatory Developments and Editorial Timing

**Federal Council mid-2026 decision:** The window for binding AI legislation is approaching. Practitioners need to be making governance decisions before the deadline, not after. The pre-decision period is the time for governance architecture — not post-hoc compliance.

**nFADP existing obligations:** Articles 12, 21, and 22 are already in force. The false security of "we have time to comply" is a present-tense exposure. Experienced practitioners know this; the documentation debt is already accumulating.

**Counterparty verification escalation:** The shift from questionnaires to evidence requests is a market signal that the ICP cannot ignore. Practitioners who cannot demonstrate operational AI governance practice will lose commercial opportunities.

**Terminology precision:** FINMA, bar association, and legal practice contexts all require specific terminology. The wrong word in the wrong document creates exposure that is invisible until it matters.
