## 02 — Market Signals: What's Happening in the Swiss AI Governance Information Space

**What is resonating right now:**

The problem of AI outputs persisting in client files and becoming reinterpretable over time has emerged as a distinct second-generation AI governance exposure. Unlike paper documents, which age in predictable ways and whose meaning is relatively stable, AI outputs can be reread, reanalyzed by newer AI tools, or compared against current best practice in ways that surface discontinuities the firm did not know existed. A client who re-reads an AI-assisted analysis from two years ago using today's AI tools may arrive at different conclusions about the recommendation than the client who read the original output — and the firm may have no mechanism for knowing that this reinterpretation has occurred. The persistence and reinterpretability of AI outputs creates an exposure window that is structurally different from the exposure created by static documents, and no Swiss publisher has addressed this specifically.

The AI reasoning entanglement question is now established as a specific, lived problem — not a philosophical one, but a practical accountability one. Practitioners who have been using AI for eighteen months or more are discovering that their own professional reasoning has been shaped by the AI tools they use, in ways they cannot fully reconstruct. The first-generation frameworks did not anticipate this problem, because they assumed AI was a tool being applied to a problem, not a reasoning environment reshaping how the professional approaches problems.

The EU AI Act jurisdictional question is arriving through client pressure rather than regulatory enforcement. Swiss professional firms serving EU clients are being asked, increasingly directly, to confirm alignment with EU AI Act standards. The question is imprecise, but it is beginning to shape what AI governance documentation Geneva firms need to produce.

Content that addresses the AI reasoning entanglement problem in operational terms — not the concept (which is beginning to circulate), but the specific methodology for managing it at the practice level, including the reasoning attribution standard and the periodic entanglement review.

## 05 — Editorial Content Draft: Article Proposal in Brotons.net Voice

**Title (EN):**
**"The Persistence Problem: Why AI Outputs in Client Files Age Differently Than Paper — and What You Owe to the Work You Can't Stand Behind Anymore"**

**Title (FR):**
**« Le problème de la persistance : pourquoi les productions d'IA dans les dossiers clients vieillissent différemment du papier — et ce que vous devez au travail que vous ne pouvez plus défendre »**

**Angle:**

There is a second-generation AI governance problem that no Swiss publisher has addressed in operational terms, and it is distinct from the tool-use problem, the configuration governance problem, and the reasoning entanglement problem. The problem is this: AI outputs in client files do not age the way paper documents age. A paper memo from two years ago is stable — its meaning is fixed, its context is understood, its limitations are visible in retrospect. An AI output from two years ago is not stable. It persists in the client's file. It can be reread. It can be compared against current best practice. It can be rerun through today's AI tools and produces different conclusions than the original reader drew from it. The AI output that the firm produced two years ago, under the governance standards of two years ago, with the AI configuration of two years ago, is still in the client's file — and it is doing work the firm may no longer be able to stand behind.

This is not a documentation problem. Documentation problems can be remediated with better documentation. This is a structural problem: the outputs exist, they persist, and the firm does not have the ability to reconstruct what they meant when produced, what they were based on, or what the AI was configured to do. The firm cannot reach back into the past and fix the outputs. It can only decide what to do about them now — and the decision is not simple, because auditing every past AI-assisted output is not operationally feasible, and doing nothing is not defensible.

This article is about the persistence problem: why AI outputs in client files are structurally different from paper documents in their aging behavior, what the reinterpretation risk looks like in practice, what the triage methodology is for accumulated AI outputs, and what a responsible approach to the past work product looks like when the firm can no longer stand behind it in its original form. It is written for the Geneva professional who has been using AI long enough to have accumulated a body of AI-assisted work in client files, and who is beginning to understand that the accumulation itself is a governance problem.

**Structure:**

*Opening:* The scenario. A Geneva managing partner receives a client call. The client has been reviewing an AI-assisted market analysis from eighteen months ago — a recommendation the firm produced with AI support. The client is not complaining. The client is asking questions: the recommendation looked different when they reread it in the context of current market conditions, and they want to understand what the firm actually knew and relied on when it was produced. The partner realises they cannot reconstruct the original context: they do not know what AI configuration was in place, what model version was running, what the firm's review standard was at the time, or what the partner themselves actually reviewed and understood about the analysis. The AI output is in the client's file. The client is reading it. The partner cannot explain what it meant when produced. This is the persistence problem, and it is beginning to surface across Geneva professional practices.

*Section 1 — Why AI outputs age differently than paper*
Paper documents age predictably: the ink fades, the context shifts, the limitations are visible in retrospect, and the reader's expectations adjust accordingly. AI outputs do not fade — they persist, they can be accessed in their original form, and they can be read in new contexts by readers who bring new questions to them. A client who rereads an AI-assisted analysis from two years ago may use today's AI tools to interrogate the analysis, compare it against current data, or identify discontinuities between the recommendation and what the client now understands to be true. The original output was produced under conditions — AI configuration, model version, firm governance standard, professional's review — that have since changed. The output did not change. The conditions did. The client is reading the unchanged output in the context of changed conditions, and forming conclusions about the firm's recommendation that may not reflect what the firm actually knew or relied on. This is not a failure of the firm's past work. It is a structural feature of AI outputs that no governance framework has addressed: they persist without aging, and they can be reinterpreted without warning.

*Section 2 — The reinterpretation risk in practice*
The reinterpretation risk is not hypothetical. It manifests in three specific ways. First, the client rereads the output in a new factual context — market conditions have changed, a regulatory development has occurred, a strategic assumption has shifted — and the recommendation appears inadequate or misleading in ways the original output did not signal. Second, the client uses a different AI tool to analyze the same data the firm used two years ago and arrives at different conclusions, raising questions about the firm's methodology that the firm cannot answer because it does not know what the AI was configured to do at the time. Third, the client's own AI literacy has increased since the original output was produced, and the client now reads the output with a more sophisticated understanding of what the AI was and was not designed to do — and what the firm disclosed (or did not disclose) about the AI's involvement becomes material in a way it was not when the output was produced. In each case, the exposure is not that the firm did something wrong — it is that the firm cannot reconstruct what it actually did, and cannot stand behind the output in its original form.

*Section 3 — What you owe to the work you can no longer stand behind*
The question of what a firm owes to its past AI-assisted work product is not answered by any Swiss regulatory guidance or professional practice standard. The practical framework is as follows. The firm does not owe a retroactive audit of every past AI-assisted output — that is not operationally feasible and the records to conduct it may no longer exist. What the firm does owe is a systematic approach to the accumulated inventory that distinguishes between three categories of past outputs: outputs in active client files where the recommendation or analysis is still doing work — these require the most immediate attention and the documentation review should establish what the AI was configured to do, what the model version was, and what the review standard was at the time; outputs in archived client files where the matter is closed but the client retained the records — these require a documentation note in the current file that flags the original AI configuration context and current firm position; and outputs in client files where the matter is active but the AI-assisted work was routine and not consequential — these can be noted as a category and reviewed on a periodic basis rather than individually. The goal is not to eliminate the accumulated governance debt — it is to have a defensible position when the reinterpretation moment arrives: the firm can describe its approach to the accumulated inventory, can show that it made a deliberate, documented decision about what to address and what to note, and can demonstrate that its current AI governance practice is sound.

*Section 4 — The reasoning attribution standard and the entanglement review*
The persistence problem and the reasoning entanglement problem are related: both arise from the firm's inability to reconstruct what it actually did with AI in specific client matters. The response to both is the same at the practice architecture level: establish a reasoning attribution standard for current and recent AI-assisted work, and conduct a periodic entanglement review to assess the degree to which the firm's approach to specific practice areas has been shaped by AI in ways that make past reasoning harder to reconstruct. The reasoning attribution standard means that when AI-assisted work appears in a client matter, the professional maintains a contemporaneous record of what the AI was used for, what it produced, how the professional interpreted and used the output, and what the professional decided and why. This is not a burdensome administrative requirement — it is a one-to-three sentence annotation in the matter record that captures the role the AI played in the professional's reasoning process. The periodic entanglement review means that at least annually, the professional reviews their AI use patterns and assesses which practice areas have the highest concentration of AI-assisted work, which of those areas involve significant consequential recommendations, and which of those consequential recommendations are in active client files or files the client still retains. The review produces a prioritized list of past outputs that require documentation attention before the reinterpretation moment arrives.

*Section 5 — The EU AI Act client question and the documentation gap it reveals*
Swiss professional firms serving EU clients are being asked to confirm that their AI governance documentation meets EU AI Act standards. The question is imprecise — the EU AI Act's specific requirements for professional services firms are not fully defined, and Swiss domestic law is not aligned. But the client's compliance function is asking, and the Geneva firm needs to understand what the question actually requires before it answers. The practical reality is that the EU AI Act question is revealing a documentation gap that goes beyond EU AI Act alignment. The gap is the firm's general inability to produce a specific, substantively grounded account of what the AI was configured to do, what it produced, what the professional reviewed, and what the professional decided in specific client matters. The EU AI Act framing is the client pressure that is making the gap visible. The answer to the client's question requires the firm to be able to answer a set of questions that it currently cannot answer in operational detail. Building the capacity to answer those questions — through the reasoning attribution standard, the reasoning log, and the accumulated output triage — is what positions the firm to respond to both the EU AI Act client question and the general AI governance accountability questions that are arriving through carriers and counterparties.

*Closing:* The persistence problem is not a documentation failure. It is a structural consequence of working with AI tools in professional practice, and it cannot be remediated retroactively. What can be done is to establish a defensible position going forward: a reasoned, documented approach to the accumulated inventory that demonstrates the firm understood what it owed to its past work and made a deliberate, operationally grounded decision about how to manage it. And to build the reasoning attribution and entanglement review capacity into the current practice architecture so that the outputs being produced today are outputs the firm can stand behind in five years, when the client reads them again in whatever context has arrived by then.

**Intended reader outcome:**

A Geneva managing partner finishes the article understanding why AI outputs in client files age differently than paper documents (persistence without aging, reinterpretation without warning), what the reinterpretation risk looks like in practice (factual context shift, AI tool comparison, client literacy increase), what they owe to the accumulated inventory (a defensible systematic approach, not a retroactive audit of everything), what the triage methodology is for past outputs (active files, archived files, routine work), how the reasoning attribution standard and periodic entanglement review address both the persistence problem and the entanglement problem simultaneously, and how the EU AI Act client question reveals the documentation gap and what building the capacity to answer it looks like in practice. They leave with a clear-eyed view of the structural nature of the persistence problem and a concrete methodology for managing accumulated AI outputs that they can implement without reconstructing the past.

*Distinct from C214–C230 Phase 05.*

**Insight 1 — AI Outputs in Client Files Age Structurally Differently Than Paper Documents and Create a Reinterpretation Risk No Swiss Framework Has Addressed**
Paper documents fade, shift context, and become readable in retrospect as limited by their time. AI outputs persist in their original form, can be reread without aging, and can be interrogated by newer AI tools or compared against current best practice in ways that surface discontinuities the firm did not know existed. This is a structural property of AI outputs, not a documentation failure, and no Swiss publisher has addressed it specifically. This is distinct from C230 Phase 05 (liability tail) by identifying the reinterpretation risk as a distinct mechanism (AI outputs can be reread and reanalyzed in ways paper cannot), not just a duration/timing problem — the liability tail focuses on the time dimension of accumulated outputs; the persistence problem focuses on the behavioral dimension of how those outputs act in the client's ongoing possession.

**Insight 2 — The Reinterpretation Risk Manifests in Three Specific Patterns That Geneva Practitioners Are Beginning to Encounter**
The risk is not hypothetical and presents in three specific forms: the client rereads the output in a changed factual context (market conditions, regulation, strategy shift) and the recommendation appears inadequate in ways the original did not signal; the client uses a different AI tool to analyze the same underlying data and arrives at different conclusions, raising methodology questions the firm cannot answer; and the client's own AI literacy has increased since the original output, making what the firm disclosed (or did not disclose) about AI involvement material in a new way. Each pattern creates exposure that is not about the firm's past work being deficient — it is about the firm's inability to reconstruct what it actually did, configure, and reviewed in the original production. This is distinct from C230 Phase 05 (liability tail) by identifying three specific reinterpretation patterns with distinct exposure profiles, not just the general liability tail from accumulated outputs.

**Insight 3 — The Tripartite Triage Framework for Accumulated AI Outputs Provides an Operationally Feasible Response to the Persistence Problem**
The firm does not owe a retroactive audit of every past AI-assisted output — that is not operationally feasible and the records may no longer exist. This is distinct from C227 Phase 05 (governance documentation temporal decay) by providing a specific three-tier triage methodology for accumulated AI outputs in client files, not a general documentation refresh framework — the tripartite framework is specific to the client file inventory problem and its operational constraints.

**Insight 4 — The Reasoning Attribution Standard and the Periodic Entanglement Review Address Both the Reasoning Entanglement Problem and the Persistence Problem Simultaneously**
Both problems arise from the same structural inability: the firm cannot reconstruct what it actually did with AI in specific client matters. The response to both is the same at the practice architecture level. The reasoning attribution standard (a contemporaneous one-to-three sentence annotation in the matter record capturing what the AI was used for, produced, how the professional interpreted and used the output, and what was decided and why) builds the record forward from the point of production. The periodic entanglement review (annual review of AI use patterns, identifying which practice areas have the highest concentration of consequential AI-assisted work in active or retained files) builds the record backward into the accumulated inventory. Together, they provide a defensible position for both current outputs and past outputs — not by retroactively fixing the past, but by building a documented approach that shows the firm understood its obligations and acted on them. This is distinct from C230 Phase 05 (reasoning attribution standard as missing element) by identifying the attribution standard and entanglement review as a unified response to both the entanglement and the persistence problems simultaneously, not as a single-problem solution.
