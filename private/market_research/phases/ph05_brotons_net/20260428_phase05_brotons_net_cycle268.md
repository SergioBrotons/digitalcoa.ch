## 06 — Findings Summary: 6 Net-New Content/Editorial Insights for Brotons.net

**Finding 01 — AI Convention Ratification as Present-Tense Private Law Implementation Event, Not Future Regulatory Consideration**

The AI Convention ratification (completed February 2026) is now operative through private law mechanisms — professional liability standard of care, carrier underwriting criteria, client contract obligations — not pending as a future regulatory instrument. The convention's obligations flow into private sector accountability through mechanisms that are already active: the managing director who signed a carrier attestation is now operating under a standard of care that includes AI use adequacy in a way it did not before ratification. No Geneva publication has treated the AI Convention ratification as a present-tense private law implementation event with specific operative consequences for professional services firms. This is distinct from all prior Phase 05 AI Convention coverage which treated it as a public international law instrument and future regulatory consideration, not as a now-operative accountability reference point.

**Finding 02 — AI Vendor Terms Conflict with Article 321 CP as Structural Exposure Operating Simultaneously Across All Active Matters**

AI platform vendor terms — specifically model improvement provisions allowing client data processing for training purposes — create a structural conflict with Article 321 CP professional secrecy obligations that is operating simultaneously across all active matters involving AI-assisted work. No Geneva publication has named this as a discrete structural category: a conflict that is present-tense, compounding, and structurally irresolvable at the firm level (the firm's only options are to accept terms it cannot negotiate, or stop using the tool). This is distinct from C268 Phase 01 which named the conflict as a research/authentication problem. This finding names it as a structural professional liability exposure operating across the full active matter portfolio with no clean resolution pathway available to individual firms.

**Finding 03 — Geneva AI Governance Standard-Setting Fragmentation as Named Editorial Beat Requiring Cross-Framework Systematic Tracking**

Geneva AI governance standards are being set through five separate channels simultaneously — UNAIWG (UN agency AI governance positions), GAIGI (Geneva Process on AI Principles), Federal Chancellery AI contact point decisions, carrier underwriting criteria, and client counterparty requirements — each operating through different institutional logic with different consequences and different publication cadences. No Geneva publication is tracking these five channels systematically as an integrated editorial subject. Brotons.net treating the cross-framework standard-setting landscape as a named editorial beat would provide a service no other Geneva publication provides: a map of where accountability standards are actually being set, in real time, across all five channels. This is distinct from all prior Phase 05 institutional architecture findings which addressed individual actors without treating the multi-channel fragmentation itself as the editorial subject.

**Finding 04 — Documentation Shelf-Life Problem as Structural Product Design Failure Distinct from Documentation Quality**

Documentation adequate for AI governance accountability last month is now structurally insufficient because the AI system evolved in the interim — new tool features, model updates, configuration changes, vendor terms modifications. The documentation was built for a specific system state that no longer exists. This is a structural product design failure: documentation built as a point-in-time record is architecturally incompatible with a continuously evolving system. No Geneva publication has treated this as a distinct structural problem — the market treats it as a documentation quality or hygiene issue that can be solved by updating documents. This finding names it as an architectural incompatibility that cannot be resolved through documentation improvement alone. This is distinct from all prior Phase 05 documentation findings which addressed adequacy, coherence, and format, not the specific structural incompatibility between point-in-time documentation architecture and continuously evolving AI systems.

**Finding 05 — Federal Council Consultation Submission Period as Specific Editorial Timing Occasion Requiring Pre-Positioning Content**

The formal Federal Council consultation submission period — the window when the consultation draft is published and private sector firms submit positions — creates a specific editorial timing occasion distinct from the pre-draft positioning window and distinct from post-publication analysis. During the submission period, Geneva institutional actors and private sector firms are both focused on the same questions simultaneously: what language to use in submissions, what positions to take on specific provisions, how to frame accountability commitments in a way that is both accurate and strategically appropriate. Brotons.net pre-positioning content (published before the submission window opens) addressing how to read the consultation draft, what language commits and what does not, and what specific accountability positions Geneva principals should consider taking, would serve an immediate operational need no competitor is addressing. This is distinct from all prior Phase 05 event-based editorial timing findings which addressed the July 2026 Geneva event concentration and carrier renewal cycles, not the consultation submission period as a discrete editorial occasion.

**Finding 06 — Geneva AI Governance Information Supply-Demand Mismatch as Structural Editorial Positioning Variable**

The Geneva AI governance information market has a structural supply-demand mismatch: the most exposed principals (those with highest personal accountability, most complex AI deployments, most internationally visible client bases) have the least access to substantive governance intelligence, because sharing that intelligence publicly carries risk and sharing it privately carries competitive cost. This structural condition means the information supply curve and the exposure distribution curve are inversely related.
