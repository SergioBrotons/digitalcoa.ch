## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: The adequacy standard problem — Geneva firms are attesting to undefined adequacy**
Carrier attestation language requires principals to confirm their AI governance is "adequate." No Geneva authority, carrier, or professional body has published a definition of adequate AI governance for Geneva professional services firms. The principal signs personally, without knowing what they are attesting to. The gap between attestation and adequacy definition creates personal liability exposure the principal cannot price, manage, or reduce through any available guidance. This is not a documentation problem — it is a definitional problem. No documentation set resolves undefined adequacy.

**Signal 2: Multiple non-synchronized accountability venues requiring incompatible documentation architecture**
Carrier examination, board accountability review, and institutional counterparty RFP processes evaluate AI governance against different evidence standards, different timing, and different consequences. A documentation set built to satisfy carrier examination may fail board review. A documentation set coherent in Q2 may be incoherent by Q4 when new accountability occasions arrive. No Geneva firm is producing documentation coherent across all three venues simultaneously — because the problem is structural, not technical. The venues are not designed to be mutually coherent. They are separate accountability mechanisms with separate evidentiary standards and no coordination requirement between them.

**Signal 3: Retrospective accountability reconstruction becoming operationally impossible**
Geneva firms are approaching a structural threshold where 2023 AI decisions cannot be retrospectively reconstructed: original decision-makers have departed, tool configurations have drifted, vendor terms have changed, memory of original rationale has faded. When something goes wrong and the principal is asked to explain what was decided, why, and how — the reconstruction window has closed. The documentation window close is not just about building new documentation going forward. It is about the permanent loss of the ability to reconstruct what was decided and why during the period when AI tools were adopted without systematic documentation requirements.

**Signal 4: Geneva's EU AI Act proximity creating compliance paradox, not compliance clarity**
Geneva professional services firms operating near the EU border face a specific confusion: proximity suggests alignment, but proximity is not membership. The AI Act applies to EU-established entities. Swiss firms serving EU clients through Geneva operations may encounter AI Act obligations without clear jurisdictional mapping. Carriers are beginning to ask about EU AI Act compliance status — treating it as evidence of governance quality — even though Swiss firms are not required to comply. The proximity creates pressure to perform compliance with a framework that does not formally apply, and carriers are treating that performance as a governance quality signal. This is creating compliance theater that does not actually produce accountability protection.

**Signal 5: No Geneva reference institution for AI governance adequacy**
Geneva lacks a single institution with the credibility, reach, and authority to make AI governance adequacy legible for the professional services community. The Federal AI Coordination Office is Bern-based and oriented toward federal coordination, not Geneva professional services. FINMA applies supervisory standards without issuing adequacy guidance for consulting practices. International organizations have their own frameworks. Professional associations (ORDRE, SVA) are not AI governance authorities. The vacuum means Geneva principals navigate multiple partial authorities — none of which provides the definitive reference point the market needs. This is an editorial opportunity: the absence of a Geneva AI governance reference institution means Brotons.net can perform a reference institution function without competition from formal institutional actors who have declined the role.

**Signal 6: Liability absorption capacity emerging as distinct from governance documentation**
Carrier fourth-generation questions are beginning to probe not just current governance practices but the firm's capacity to absorb liability if practices fail. This is a different analytical question from "do you have documentation." It is asking: if something goes wrong, what happens? The answer requires firms to think about risk distribution, client contract liability allocation, professional indemnity coverage limits, and carrier coverage conditions — not just whether the documentation satisfies an evidentiary standard. The shift from governance documentation to liability absorption capacity represents a material change in what carriers are actually trying to assess.

**Title: Adequate to What? The Geneva Accountability Definition Problem Nobody Is Naming**

**Angle:**
Geneva professional services principals are signing carrier attestations to undefined adequacy standards. They are building documentation against requirements nobody has precisely named. They are navigating multiple accountability venues with incompatible evidentiary standards. And they are doing all of this while the documentation window for 2023-2026 decisions is approaching permanent closure.

The Geneva AI accountability problem is not primarily a documentation problem. It is a definitional problem. The question is not how to build adequate documentation — it is what adequate means, who defines it, and what happens to the principal who attested to it when the definition arrives and does not match what was built.

Brotons.net's role in this window is to name the definitional problem precisely, map which accountability venues are applying which standards, and explain what the documentation window close actually means for firms that have not yet built retrospective reconstruction capability.

**Structure:**

*Opening — The attestation problem:*
A managing director signed a carrier attestation confirming that the firm's AI governance was adequate. The attestation was required for coverage renewal. The form was written by the carrier's lawyers. The managing director signed personally. Six months later, the firm cannot define what adequate means or whether the documentation they have built satisfies the definition. The problem is not that they lack documentation. The problem is that they attested to a standard nobody has defined.

*Section 1 — The adequacy definition vacuum:*
No Geneva authority, carrier, or professional body has published a definition of adequate AI governance for Geneva professional services firms. The Federal AI Coordination Office does not define adequacy. FINMA does not define adequate governance for consulting practices. The professional associations (ORDRE, SVA) are not AI governance authorities. The vacuum is structural — no institution has claimed the definitional function. Principals are navigating undefined adequacy standards while personal liability exposure is attached to their attestations.

*Section 2 — The five accountability venues and their incompatible standards:*
Carrier fourth-generation examination is applying a standard the carrier has not published — it is observable only through what the questionnaire asks and what the carrier challenges. Board accountability review applies a personal knowledge standard — the principal must know enough to represent what the firm does personally. Institutional counterparty RFP processes apply their own requirements, calibrated to their own risk tolerance and their own liability exposure. These three venues do not agree on what adequate means. A documentation set coherent for one may fail another. No Geneva firm is producing documentation coherent across all three — because the problem is structural, not technical.

*Section 3 — The retrospective reconstruction problem:*
The 2023-2026 documentation window is approaching permanent closure. The AI decisions made during this period — tool adoption, configuration choices, client service methodology — cannot be retrospectively reconstructed after a specific threshold. The people who made the decisions have departed. The tool configurations have drifted. The vendor terms have changed. The memory has faded. After the window closes, a firm asked to explain how it used AI in a specific client matter in 2024 will have nothing to offer except that the tools seemed reasonable at the time. This is not a documentation backlog — it is a permanent structural condition that arrives after a specific point, and that point is approaching.

*Section 4 — The liability absorption question:*
Carrier fourth-generation questions are beginning to probe something different from documentation adequacy: liability absorption capacity. The question is not whether documentation exists — it is what happens to the firm's liability position if the documented practices fail to prevent a client loss. This requires the firm to evaluate its risk distribution architecture: client contract liability allocation, professional indemnity coverage limits, carrier coverage conditions, and the specific gap between what the firm can absorb and what the firm actually faces. This is a different analytical exercise from building documentation to satisfy an evidentiary standard.

*Section 5 — What to do with the window that remains:*
The documentation window that is still open will close. Before it closes: define which accountability venue is primary for the firm's specific situation (not which one is most prominent — which one has the fastest iteration velocity and the highest consequence when the standard shifts). Build documentation against that venue's implicit standard while the other venues are observable. Accept that documentation coherent across all three venues simultaneously is architecturally impossible — and build accordingly.

**Intended reader outcome:**
The reader understands that the Geneva accountability problem is primarily definitional, not technical. They understand why attesting to undefined adequacy creates personal liability exposure that no documentation set can resolve — because the definition of adequate has not been named by any authority with Geneva credibility. They understand which three accountability venues are applying which standards and why those standards are not designed to be coherent. They understand the retrospective reconstruction threshold as a structural event with a specific timing — not a compliance deadline. They understand the shift from documentation adequacy to liability absorption capacity as a material change in what carriers are actually trying to assess. And they understand what to do before the documentation window closes.
