## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Carrier third-generation questionnaire as standard-setting instrument**
The carrier's evolution from framework documentation to methodology evidence is the market's most active standard-setting mechanism. The questionnaire is forming the standard faster than regulatory process.

**Signal 2: FDPIC pattern reading as intelligence circuit**
Attorneys in the informal circuit are reading FDPIC patterns as intelligence — what the FDPIC is actually examining, not what it publishes. The distinction between published guidance and investigative intelligence is the informal circuit's primary value.

**Signal 3: Client DD questionnaire as peer benchmark**
Client due diligence questionnaires are functioning as peer benchmarks. When a sophisticated client asks a question, the answer reveals what the market is beginning to expect from well-governed firms.

**Signal 4: Geneva bar informal session content formation**
Geneva bar informal sessions are forming the actual content of professional AI governance norms. This formation is not being recorded or published. The informal record is the real standard.

**Signal 5: Two-tier information environment as structural market failure**
The market has two tiers: an informal tier that knows what is actually happening, and a formal tier that only gets published versions months later. The structural failure means the published record is perpetually behind.

**Signal 6: EFTA and international org intelligence gap**
EFTA Court developments and international organization AI governance decisions are creating intelligence that Swiss domestic practitioners cannot access. The intersection of EFTA and Swiss AI governance is uncharted territory.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Named informal standard formation mechanism**
The mechanism through which Geneva sets AI governance standards informally is nameable and describable: carrier questionnaires, bar sessions, FDPIC pattern observation, and sophisticated client questions. This mechanism is distinct from formal regulatory formation.

**Finding 2: Two-tier information environment as structural market failure**
The market has a structural information failure: the informal tier knows what is actually happening; the formal tier only gets published versions months later. The two-tier environment is not a temporary condition — it is structural.

**Finding 3: FDPIC pattern as intelligence vs guidance distinction**
FDPIC patterns are being read as intelligence, not just guidance. The intelligence function — what the FDPIC is actually examining — is different from what the FDPIC publishes. The distinction is the informal circuit's primary analytical value.

**Finding 4: Geneva EFTA/international org intersection editorial beat**
The EFTA Court and international organization AI governance decisions intersect with Swiss AI governance in ways that domestic practitioners cannot follow through official channels. This intersection is a specific editorial territory that no Swiss publication covers.

**Finding 5: Carrier questionnaire progression as historical record**
The evolution of the carrier AI questionnaire from first generation to third is a real-time historical record of how the market's understanding of AI governance has developed. This progression is itself publishable intelligence.

**Finding 6: Editorial translation function as distinct Brotons.net positioning**
The function of translating informal circuit intelligence into publishable, actionable analysis is a distinct editorial positioning. Brotons.net fills the gap between what the informal circuit knows and what the public tier can access. The translation function is the publication's core value proposition.
