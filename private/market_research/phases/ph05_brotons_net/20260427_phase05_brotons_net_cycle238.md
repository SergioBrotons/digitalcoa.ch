## 02 — Market Signals: What's Happening in the Swiss AI Governance Information Space

### What's Resonating

**The unreadable signature problem.** The carrier underwriting cycle is now probing not just whether AI tool use was documented, but whether the work was actually reviewed by a professional with the capacity to evaluate it. The language being used in carrier questionnaires and follow-up exchanges is revealing a market-formed view that the supervisory signature — the attestation that work was reviewed by a qualified professional — is structurally inadequate when the supervisor cannot independently verify the AI's contribution. The signature is real. The review capacity behind it is increasingly in question. This is being read by firms as a professional liability exposure they did not know they had.

**The Geneva peer silence paradox as a market signal.** The Geneva professional community is generating AI governance intelligence through informal channels — carrier meetings, bar sessions, peer dinners, client advisory conversations — at a pace that significantly outstrips the published record. But the same liability concerns that make the informal standard formation necessary also make it private. The result is a structural information asymmetry: the firms that are most active in informal knowledge formation are also the firms least likely to publish what they know. The information value of Geneva AI governance expertise is being maintained precisely through non-publication. This is a self-reinforcing trust moat that Brotons.net can name and partially bridge.

**Post-examination revelation as primary discovery mechanism.** The Geneva firms that have encountered AI governance questions in FINMA supervisory examinations are discovering their documentation gaps under examination conditions — in real time, with evidentiary consequence. The examination is functioning as an accountability revelation moment, not an accountability compliance moment. Firms are learning what adequate looks like by failing it first. This pattern is generating informal intelligence that is circulating among Geneva peers but has no publication venue.

**The nFADP Article 21 explanation obligation now active.** Swiss clients who received AI-assisted advice are beginning to exercise their Article 21 right to explanation. The first cases of clients requesting reconstruction of AI-assisted decisions — not just the decision outcome but the process — are surfacing in Geneva professional conversations. The nFADP explanation obligation was designed for human decision processes; its application to AI-assisted decisions is revealing a reconstruction gap: firms can explain what they decided, but cannot always demonstrate how the AI contributed to the decision process in a way that satisfies the nFADP's granularity expectation.

### What's Missing

**The supervisory capacity question as a distinct legal exposure.** No Geneva publication has addressed the structural difference between signing a supervisory attestation and having the cognitive capacity the attestation claims. This is not a training problem — it is a professional responsibility architecture problem. The supervisor's signature is real; the review capacity the signature certifies may not be. This gap is being probed by carriers and is beginning to surface in professional liability discussions, but it has not been addressed in the Geneva professional press.

**The nFADP explanation reconstruction problem as a specific obligation.** The application of Article 21 nFADP to AI-assisted decisions is a specific, live question with real evidentiary consequence. Practitioners are discovering that their file documentation does not contain what the explanation obligation requires — not because the files are incomplete, but because the documentation was designed for a different evidentiary standard. This gap is not on the radar of most Geneva firms until a client asks the question.

**The Geneva informal standard formation operating at a pace the published record cannot track.** The Geneva AI governance standard is being formed through mechanisms that are structurally inaccessible to practitioners who are not embedded in the relevant informal networks: carrier underwriting negotiations, bar association sessions, post-examination debriefs, peer accountability conversations. The pace of standard formation in these informal environments significantly exceeds the pace of publication. This creates a persistent information lag for practitioners who rely on published sources.

**Title (EN):** The Unreadable Signature: What Your Carrier's AI Questionnaire Is Actually Asking

**Title (FR):** La Signature Illisible : Ce que votre questionnaire d'assureur demande réellement sur l'IA

---

**Angle:**

The carrier questionnaire is probing something the formal language doesn't make explicit: whether the supervisory signature represents an actual review capacity or a formal attestation that cannot be substantiated when the work was AI-assisted and the supervisor cannot independently verify what the AI contributed.

This is not a documentation problem. It is a professional responsibility architecture problem. And it is the question the carrier is actually asking when it asks whether the work was reviewed.

---

**Structure:**

**Opening:** A Geneva managing partner receives his carrier's 2026 AI governance questionnaire. He can answer the tool inventory questions. He can answer the version tracking questions. But there's one question he keeps returning to: "Describe the supervisory review process for AI-assisted work. Confirm that the reviewing professional had the capacity to evaluate the AI's contribution."

He signs the attestation. He sends it. Then he thinks about it. Can he confirm what the question is actually asking? Did the reviewer have the capacity to evaluate what the AI contributed? And if the answer is no — what does that mean for the attestation he just signed?

**The structural gap:** The supervisory architecture that Geneva professional firms have developed was designed for human-generated work. The supervisor reviews what the junior produces. The supervision is meaningful because the supervisor can evaluate the work against their own professional knowledge and judgment.

When the work is AI-assisted, this architecture breaks down in a specific way: the supervisor can review the output, but cannot always evaluate what the AI contributed to producing it. The AI's reasoning is not visible in the output. The output looks like professional work product. The question of how it was produced — what role the AI played, what reasoning it applied, what alternatives it weighed and rejected — is not answered by looking at the result.

This is not a training gap. It is a structural problem. No amount of AI literacy training resolves it. The supervisor's cognitive capacity does not scale with the AI's complexity. The more capable the AI tool, the less visible its contribution, the less the supervisor can independently verify.

**The carrier's reading:** The carrier underwriting team is aware of this problem. The question about supervisory review capacity is not accidental. It is the question the carrier most needs answered — because the attestation it produces is only as reliable as the review capacity behind it. An attestation signed without the capacity to verify what was reviewed is not insurance-grade evidence of supervision. It is a formal statement that cannot be substantiated.

**What "adequate review" means in practice:** There is no published standard for what adequate supervisory review of AI-assisted work looks like in Geneva. The Geneva bar has not defined it. FINMA has not defined it. The carrier questionnaires are forming the market standard before any authority publishes one.

The emerging market standard has two components. First, the firm needs to be able to describe its supervisory process — not just that it happened, but what it involved. Second, the firm needs to be able to demonstrate that the supervisory process was adequate to the specific AI tool used — meaning: the reviewer had enough information about what the AI was doing to evaluate whether its contribution was appropriate.

Neither component is satisfied by a signature alone. Both require documentation of the supervisory process and the reviewer's basis for evaluation.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva professional firms face this problem at both the firm-internal level (supervisory review of AI-assisted work by juniors) and the external accountability level (the firm's own accountability to clients and counterparties who are asking about AI governance without a formal framework to reference). The Geneva bar's informal guidance is forming the internal standard. The carrier questionnaire is forming the external standard. Neither is published. Both are operational.

**What the article does:** This article names and explains the unreadable signature problem — the structural gap between the supervisory attestation and the review capacity it claims — and connects it to the carrier questionnaire language that is making this problem present-tense for Geneva firms. It explains what the carrier is actually asking, what the attestation actually certifies, and what adequate review actually requires when the work was AI-assisted. It gives Geneva professionals a precise language for a problem they have been feeling without being able to name.

**Intended reader outcome:** The reader finishes the article knowing whether their supervisory attestation is defensible, what they need to be able to demonstrate, and what questions to ask about their own AI-assisted work review process. They have a precise framework for a problem that has been operating below the surface of their professional consciousness.

**Title (FR):** La Signature Illisible : Ce que votre questionnaire d'assureur demande réellement sur l'IA

**Angle :**

Le questionnaire de l'assureur teste quelque chose que le langage formel ne rend pas explicite : si la signature de révision représente une capacité réelle de révision ou une attestation formelle qui ne peut pas être substantivée quand le travail était assistée par IA et que le réviseur ne peut pas vérifier indépendamment ce que l'IA a contribué.

Ce n'est pas un problème de documentation. C'est un problème d'architecture de responsabilité professionnelle. Et c'est la question que l'assureur pose réellement quand il demande si le travail a été révisé.

**Structure :**

**Ouverture :** Un associé gérant genevois reçoit le questionnaire de gouvernance IA de son assureur 2026. Il peut répondre aux questions sur l'inventaire des outils. Il peut répondre aux questions sur le suivi des versions. Mais il y a une question à laquelle il revient sans cesse : « Décrivez le processus de révision superviseuse pour le travail assistée par IA. Confirmez que le professionnel qui a révisé avait la capacité d'évaluer la contribution de l'IA. »

Il signe l'attestation. Il l'envoie. Ensuite il y réfléchit. Peut-il confirmer ce que la question demande réellement ? Le réviseur avait-il la capacité d'évaluer ce que l'IA a contribué ? Et si la réponse est non — qu'est-ce que cela signifie pour l'attestation qu'il vient de signer ?

**L'écart structurel :** L'architecture de révision que les cabinets professionnels genevois ont développée a été conçue pour le travail généré par l'homme. Le superviseur revise ce que le collaborateur junior produit. La révision a du sens parce que le superviseur peut évaluer le travail par rapport à ses propres connaissances et jugement professionnels.

Quand le travail est assistée par IA, cette architecture se brise d'une manière spécifique : le superviseur peut réviser le résultat, mais ne peut pas toujours évaluer ce que l'IA a contribué à le produire. Le raisonnement de l'IA n'est pas visible dans le résultat. Le résultat ressemble à un travail professionnel. La question de comment il a été produit — quel rôle l'IA a joué, quel raisonnement elle a appliqué, quelles alternatives elle a pesées et rejetées — n'est pas répondue en regardant le résultat.

Ce n'est pas un problème de formation. C'est un problème structurel. Aucune formation en littératie IA ne le résout. La capacité cognitive du superviseur ne s'adapte pas à la complexité de l'IA. Plus l'outil d'IA est capable, moins sa contribution est visible, moins le superviseur peut vérifier indépendamment.

**La lecture de l'assureur :** L'équipe d'assurance du carrier est consciente de ce problème. La question sur la capacité de révision superviseuse n'est pas accidentelle. C'est la question que l'assureur a le plus besoin de voir répondue — parce que l'attestation qu'il produit n'est aussi fiable que la capacité de révision derrière elle. Une attestation signée sans la capacité de vérifier ce qui a été révisé n'est pas une preuve d'assurance de qualité superviseur. C'est une déclaration formelle qui ne peut pas être substantivée.

**Ce que « révision adéquate » signifie en pratique :** Il n'existe pas de norme publiée sur ce à quoi ressemble une révision superviseuse adéquate d'un travail assistée par IA à Genève. Le barreau de Genève ne l'a pas définie. La FINMA ne l'a pas définie. Les questionnaires d'assureur forment la norme du marché avant que toute autorité n'en publie une.

La norme du marché émergente a deux composantes. Premièrement, le cabinet doit pouvoir décrire son processus de révision — pas seulement qu'il a eu lieu, mais ce qu'il impliquait. Deuxièmement, le cabinet doit pouvoir démontrer que le processus de révision était adéquat à l'outil d'IA spécifique utilisé — ce qui signifie : le réviseur avait assez d'informations sur ce que l'IA faisait pour évaluer si sa contribution était appropriée.

Aucune des deux composantes n'est satisfaite par une signature seule. Les deux nécessitent une documentation du processus de révision et de la base du réviseur pour son évaluation.

**La dimension genevoise spécifique :** Les cabinets professionnels genevois font face à ce problème à la fois au niveau interne au cabinet (révision superviseuse de travail assistée par IA par des juniors) et au niveau de la responsabilité externe (la propre responsabilité du cabinet envers les clients et contreparties qui posent des questions sur la gouvernance de l'IA sans cadre formel de référence). La guidance informelle du barreau de Genève forme la norme interne. Le questionnaire de l'assureur forme la norme externe. Aucune n'est publiée. Les deux sont opérationnelles.

**Ce que l'article fait :** Cet article nomme et explique le problème de la signature illisible — l'écart structurel entre l'attestation de révision et la capacité de révision qu'elle réclame — et le connecte au langage du questionnaire de l'assureur qui rend ce problème actuel pour les cabinets genevois. Il explique ce que l'assureur demande réellement, ce que l'attestation certifie réellement, et ce que révision adéquate nécessite réellement quand le travail était assistée par IA. Il donne aux professionnels genevois un langage précis pour un problème qu'ils ressentaient sans pouvoir le nommer.

**Résultat souhaité pour le lecteur :** Le lecteur termine l'article en sachant si son attestation de révision superviseuse est défendable, ce qu'il doit pouvoir démontrer, et quelles questions poser sur son propre processus de révision de travail assistée par IA. Il dispose d'un cadre précis pour un problème qui opérait sous la surface de sa conscience professionnelle.
