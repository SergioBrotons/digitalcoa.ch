## 02 — Market Signals: What's Happening in the Swiss AI Governance Information Space

### What's Resonating

**Carrier questionnaire as primary market signal.** The 2026 carrier underwriting cycle is the most concrete and dateable market mechanism forcing Geneva firms to confront their AI governance documentation. The questionnaires are probing specific dimensions: tool inventory with version specificity, decision attribution methodology, client communication records, FADP audit trail substance, and reassessment protocol. Firms that cannot answer these questions are experiencing the carrier interaction as an accountability revelation moment, not merely an insurance process.

**FDPIC enforcement pattern language becoming legible.** Practitioners who track FDPIC decisions systematically — a small but growing population — are beginning to read the FDPIC's investigation priorities from the procedural choices in published decisions: which AI systems draw scrutiny first, what audit trail characteristics trigger investigation, what documentation adequacy standard the FDPIC is implicitly applying. This pattern-reading practice is generating informal intelligence that has no publication venue but circulates through Geneva professional networks.

**Client counterparty questionnaire arrival as peer benchmark.** When a Geneva firm receives its first substantive AI governance DD questionnaire from a client, two things happen simultaneously: the firm discovers its own documentation gaps, and it receives the first concrete evidence of what a sophisticated counterparty considers adequate. The questionnaire itself becomes a benchmark that spreads through Geneva professional peer networks — shared at events, referenced in bar association conversations, occasionally forwarded directly between managing partners at comparable firms.

**Geneva bar informal session content formation.** The SVA (Geneva Bar Association) and the Ordre des-avocats de Genève have begun holding informal sessions where AI governance questions are discussed. The content of these sessions — what the bar acknowledges as the problem, what it declines to resolve, what guidance it offers provisionally — is not published. But Geneva practitioners who attend are forming a private map of the bar's implicit positions. This informal standard formation is operating faster and with more practical authority than any published guidance.

### What's Missing

**No public record of the informal standard formation.** The most consequential AI governance standard-forming conversations in Geneva are happening in carrier underwriting meetings, bar association sessions, peer dinners, and client advisory conversations. None of these are published. Practitioners who do not attend these conversations — or whose peer networks do not include participants — are operating without access to the actual standard being formed.

**Geneva EFTA/UN AI governance intelligence gap.** Geneva is the seat of EFTA, UN Geneva, WHO, WMO, ITU, and multiple other international organisations that are actively developing AI governance frameworks. The intersection of these international frameworks with Swiss domestic obligations for Geneva-based professional firms is a live question with significant practical consequence. No Geneva professional publication is systematically tracking this intersection.

**FDPIC pattern translation gap.** The FDPIC's published decisions contain implicit guidance about what adequate AI governance documentation looks like under Swiss law. But the translation from published decision to Tuesday morning operational practice is not being made explicit anywhere in the Geneva professional information ecosystem. Practitioners are left to read the tea leaves themselves.

**Professional community informal intelligence circuit.** The Geneva AI governance knowledge economy operates through informal channels that are not accessible to practitioners who are not embedded in the relevant professional communities. This creates a two-tier information environment where the actual standard of care — the shared understanding of what adequate AI governance means in practice — is held by a network of practitioners who are not publishing it.
