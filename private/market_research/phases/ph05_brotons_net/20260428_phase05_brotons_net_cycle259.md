## 05 — Editorial Content Draft: Brotons.net Article Proposal

**English Version**

**Title:**
*The Geneva AI Governance Conversation Has Three Speeds. Most Publications Are Only Covering One.*

**Angle:**
The Geneva AI governance landscape is not one conversation — it is three simultaneously operating tracks that use overlapping vocabulary but address different obligations, different accountability structures, and different consequences. The diplomatic track (negotiated at international forums hosted in Geneva), the regulatory track (Federal Council deliberation, FINMA guidance, nFADP application), and the commercial track (carrier questionnaires, client counterparty requirements, EU AI Act service provider obligations imposed through commercial relationships) are operating in parallel with no reconciliation mechanism. Geneva professional services principals are navigating all three simultaneously without a publication that maps the intersections.

The editorial function here is disaggregation — showing the reader precisely where these tracks diverge, where they create conflicting documentation requirements, and what "adequate" means under each track. This is not a general AI governance explainer. It is a structural mapping exercise specifically calibrated to what a Geneva principal needs to understand before July 2026 changes the vocabulary again.

**Structure:**

*Opening:* The July 2026 Geneva AI summit concentration as a concrete occasion — within seventy-two hours, Geneva will host the UN Global Dialogue on AI Governance and the AI for Good Global Summit. The intelligence that emerges from those events will reshape how Geneva thinks about AI governance obligations. But the conversation was already in three speeds before the summits arrived.

*Track One — The Diplomatic Track:* How the international AI governance conversation, conducted in Geneva's multilateral venues, produces accountability vocabulary that flows into Geneva professional services through international client relationships and Geneva's own institutional positioning. The confidence-building framing, the multilateral obligation architecture. Why this track matters for domestic firms — not as theory, but as a specific set of obligations arriving through client relationships.

*Track Two — The Regulatory Track:* The Federal Council deliberation, the AI Convention ratification, the nFADP/FINMA collision as a present-tense operational problem. The documentation requirements that pull in different directions at boutique scale. What FINMA has formally published versus what carriers are requiring — the divergence as the primary operative standard-formation mechanism.

*Track Three — The Commercial Track:* Carrier questionnaires as the de facto Geneva boutique AI governance standard. Client counterparty requirements arriving independently of Swiss domestic determination. EU AI Act service provider obligations imposed through commercial relationships. The August 2, 2026 commercial accountability clock running ahead of the regulatory clock.

*The Intersection Problem:* Where these three tracks require contradictory documentation from the same firm, simultaneously, for the same AI system.

*What a Geneva Principal Should Do With This:* The specific documentation architecture that would produce adequate positions under all three tracks — or, more honestly, the prioritization framework for when perfect simultaneous compliance is not achievable. The pre-July 2026 positioning window.

**Intended Reader Outcome:**
The reader should leave with a precise understanding of which Geneva AI governance conversation is relevant to their specific situation — and clear-eyed recognition that the answer is usually "all three." They should understand where the tracks diverge, where they create conflicting requirements, and what "adequate" means under each track. The article should generate the specific discomfort that precedes a decision to act, not the general comfort of having read something informative.
