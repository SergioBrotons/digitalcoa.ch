## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### English

**Reader on the Geneva AI governance conversation having fragmented into incompatible registers:**
"I have been going to events all year where AI governance comes up in different contexts. Now I am trying to figure out whether these are the same conversation or different conversations wearing the same vocabulary. The vocabulary is the same. The underlying problems are different. Nobody is naming the incompatibility."

**On the silence where the translation function should be:**
"Between sessions at conferences, in the informal conversations, people are using the same words and meaning completely different things. The people who should be translating — the advisors, the bar associations, the regulators — are mostly just adding to the noise. The translation function is absent. That is what Brotons.net could do. Name what the translation function should produce."

**On what has happened to the Geneva informal circuit:**
"The informal circuit used to be where you learned what was actually happening. Now it has become a performance. People talk about AI governance in general terms because they do not want to be specific about their own situation. The information content of the informal circuit has collapsed. I am getting less from it than I was two years ago."

**On the timing of the Federal Council deliberation:**
"The Federal Council is going to make a decision in June. Most people I know have not read the consultation response. They are waiting to see what the Council decides. But the decision the Council makes will not be the thing that determines what happens next — it will be the starting point for a longer process. The waiting is a misallocation of attention."

**On what adequate preparation actually means:**
"I have been trying to figure out what adequate preparation looks like for the last several months. Every framework I look at is designed for a different scale. I cannot find anything that tells me what the standard is for a two-to-five person firm operating in Geneva with specific client types and specific AI tools. The question is specific. The answer does not exist."

**On the gap between what regulators say and what carriers do:**
"FINMA published guidance. The carriers sent questionnaires. The guidance and the questionnaires are not the same document. Nobody has mapped the relationship between them. The carriers are forming the operative standard through the questions they ask, not through the guidance FINMA published. That is the thing nobody is naming."

## 05 — Editorial Content Draft

### English

**The Geneva AI Governance Conversation Has Three Speeds**

Most publications covering AI governance in Geneva are covering one speed. The actual conversation happening among Geneva practitioners, regulators, and institutions operates at three different speeds simultaneously — and the vocabulary is the same across all three, which makes the incompatibility invisible.

**The three speeds:**

The first speed is diplomatic. This is the conversation happening in international organizations, multilateral institutions, and Geneva-based diplomatic bodies. The concerns are systemic stability, cross-border coordination, and the architecture of international AI governance. The vocabulary is high-level and frameworks-focused. The timeline is long.

The second speed is regulatory. This is the conversation happening in FINMA, the SVA, ORDRE, the FDPIC, and the federal administration. The concerns are supervisory standards, examination methodology, and the translation of international frameworks into Swiss domestic obligations. The vocabulary is compliance-focused and obligation-driven. The timeline is medium-term regulatory.

The third speed is commercial. This is the conversation happening in boutique firms, family offices, wealth management practices, and small consulting operations. The concerns are what the carrier asks in the next questionnaire, what the client asked in the last meeting, and whether the firm's documented position will survive the next examination. The vocabulary is operational and accountability-focused. The timeline is immediate.

**The translation problem:**

The same words appear across all three speeds. Words like "accountability," "governance," "transparency," and "risk management" mean different things in each context. An accountability mechanism that makes sense at the diplomatic level may be entirely misaligned with what regulatory supervision actually examines. A governance framework that satisfies a commercial client's requests may not address the obligations that FINMA actually enforces.

Most Geneva practitioners are trying to operate in all three speeds simultaneously without anyone producing the translation between them. They read documents from each track and assume the vocabulary is consistent. It is not. The misreading produces governance structures that are misaligned with actual accountability requirements.

**What Brotons.net does:**

Brotons.net exists to produce the translation. To name what the same vocabulary means in each context, where the conflicts between contexts are unresolvable, and what a Geneva practitioner operating across all three tracks should actually do.

The Geneva AI governance conversation has three speeds. Most publications are only covering one. Brotons.net covers all three.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Vocabulary overlap across Geneva's three AI governance tracks masking structural incompatibility**
The same words — accountability, governance, transparency, risk management — appear across diplomatic, regulatory, and commercial tracks with different meanings in each context. The overlap creates the appearance of a coherent conversation where none exists. No Geneva publication has named this specific configuration.

**Finding 2: Informal circuit information collapse under performance dynamics as structural information deprivation**
Geneva's informal circuit has become a performance space where participants conceal their actual situations. The information content has collapsed. Practitioners who relied on it for real market intelligence are now structurally information-deprived without knowing why. No publication has named this dynamic or its consequences.

**Finding 3: Carrier questionnaire standard-formation displacing published FINMA doctrine as operative standard source**
Firms are being measured against what carriers ask in questionnaires, not against what FINMA has published in guidance. The displacement means the standard is being formed by a document firms cannot read until they receive the questionnaire. The mechanism is unnamed in the market.

**Finding 4: Federal Council deliberation as misallocation of reader attention and editorial opportunity**
Readers are allocating attention to the Federal Council's June decision as if it resolves something. It starts a longer process. The misallocation creates an editorial occasion to redirect attention to what matters after the deliberation, not before it.

**Finding 5: Boutique-scale adequate preparation standard absent from Geneva AI governance publishing**
No Geneva publication has produced the specific standard for small-firm, specific-tool, specific-client-type preparation. The question is precise and the answer is absent. The gap is an editorial occasion — Brotons.net can produce what no one else has.

**Finding 6: Translation function as structural editorial positioning for Brotons.net across all three tracks**
The structural role for Brotons.net is producing the translation between Geneva's three AI governance tracks — naming what the vocabulary means in each context, where the conflicts are unresolvable, and what practitioners should actually do.
