## 02 — Market Signals: What's Happening in the Swiss AI Governance Information Space

### What's Resonating

**1. The carrier questionnaire as accountability reckoning**
The third-generation carrier AI questionnaire (2026) is landing differently than the first two iterations. Where earlier versions could be satisfied with generic policy language, the 2026 version is probing specific: model version history, documented reassessment triggers, client communication records for AI-assisted decisions. Practitioners who treated earlier questionnaires as box-checking exercises are discovering that the third-generation version has teeth. The market signal is that the carriers are converging on a specific documentation standard — not through regulatory mandate but through the natural selection of underwriting risk.

**2. Client due diligence questionnaires arriving from EU counterparties**
EU-based clients and counterparties are sending AI governance questionnaires to Swiss firms with increasing frequency. These questionnaires reference EU AI Act high-risk system obligations, specific documentation requirements under Annex VIII, and post-market monitoring obligations that Swiss firms have no established framework to answer against. The market signal: EU clients are treating Swiss firms as de facto EU AI Act service providers, regardless of the Swiss firm's understanding of their own obligations.

**3. The documentation archaeology problem entering general awareness**
The problem of reconstructing AI governance documentation for the 2023-2024 early-adoption period is no longer confined to the most exposed early adopters. Boutique firms that deployed AI tools in 2023 without governance documentation are now receiving carrier questionnaires that ask for exactly that history. The gap between what happened and what can be documented is becoming a named problem in Geneva professional circles.

**4. Zurich-Geneva AI governance information divergence**
A measurable divergence has opened between Zurich and Geneva professional communities in how AI governance questions are discussed, documented, and resolved. Zurich AI governance discourse is oriented toward the Federal Council's AI policy direction and Federal Institute of Technology research input. Geneva AI governance discourse is oriented toward international organization accountability frameworks, EU client pressure, and cantonal bar guidance. This divergence creates a specific opportunity for content that addresses the Geneva-specific intersection without simply importing Zurich or international frameworks.

### What's Missing

**1. Honest editorial language for the early-adoption-period gap**
Most published AI governance content assumes the reader knows they should have been documenting AI tool use from the beginning. No one is publishing honest analysis of what it means to discover, in mid-2026, that your firm's 2023-2024 AI governance decisions left no documentation record — and what a realistic reconstruction path looks like.

**2. Geneva peer accountability information circuit — functional but informal and inaccessible**
The most useful information about how AI governance accountability is actually being set in Geneva circulates privately: in carrier underwriting conversations, in bar association sessions, in peer-to-peer professional exchanges at events. This circuit is functional but inaccessible to practitioners who don't have established Geneva peer networks or who are new to the AI governance question. The market signal is that informal intelligence is outpacing formal publication by 3-6 months — and nobody is systematically translating the informal circuit into accessible editorial content.

**3. Documentation adequacy standard for Geneva professional services**
There's no Geneva-specific reference for what adequate AI governance documentation looks like for a small law firm, boutique wealth management practice, or consulting boutique. International frameworks (ISO 42001, NIST AI RMF, EU AI Act Annex VIII) provide general structure but not Geneva-specific guidance. The gap between general framework language and Geneva professional practice expectations is unaddressed in published content.

**4. The AI model version documentation problem at boutique scale**
Firms that deployed AI tools in 2023-2024 may have used multiple AI models or model versions without tracking which version produced which output. As client matters from that period come under carrier review or counterparty scrutiny, the version documentation problem becomes acute. No published content addresses this specifically for small professional services firms.

**5. The professional silence problem as editorial opportunity**
Geneva senior professionals are experiencing AI governance problems privately that they cannot discuss publicly — carrier questionnaire responses, personal liability questions, supervisory capacity issues. The silence is rational given liability exposure but creates an information vacuum. Editorial content that names the problems accurately without requiring practitioners to identify themselves fills a genuine market gap.

### Angle

The Federal Council's June 2026 AI policy deliberation will not resolve the Geneva professional services AI governance documentation problem. It will clarify Switzerland's institutional direction while leaving the specific question of what adequate AI governance documentation looks like for small law firms, boutique wealth management practices, and consulting boutiques unresolved at the federal level. ### Structure

**Opening: The Problem That Exists Before the Regulatory Decision**

The Federal Council's June deliberation will set Switzerland's AI governance direction. It will not set the documentation adequacy standard for Geneva boutique law firms, wealth management practices, or consulting boutiques. This gap is structural. Regulatory direction defines the frame; it does not fill the frame with specific documentation requirements for small professional services firms. In this gap, firms are left without a reference point — and the documentation window is closing.

**Section 1: What the Documentation Window Is, and Why It Has a Closing Date**

The 2023-2024 early-adoption AI governance documentation problem is not a forward-looking concern. It is a present-tense accountability exposure. Client matters from that period are still active. Carrier questionnaires are probing that period specifically. AI model versions used in 2023-2024 have changed or been discontinued. Staff who configured AI tools in 2023 have departed. The documentation window for that period is closing because the conditions for reconstructing what happened are deteriorating systematically: model versions change, configurations drift, institutional memory fades.

The closing mechanism is not regulatory deadline. It is operational decay. And it is happening now.

**Section 2: What the Carrier Questionnaire Is Actually Measuring**

The third-generation carrier AI questionnaire (2026) is the most specific market signal of what adequate AI governance documentation requires for Geneva professional services firms. It is not perfect — it is a underwriting instrument, not a regulatory standard. But it is the closest thing to a working adequacy standard that exists for small professional services firms in Geneva.

The questionnaire probes four documentation categories that matter for this analysis:
1. Tool inventory with model version tracking (not just tool names)
2. Documented reassessment triggers for AI tool configurations
3. Client communication records for AI-assisted decisions
4. Evidence of review or supervision of AI outputs

These four categories are not arbitrary. They are a reconstruction of the accountability surface that a Geneva professional services firm presents to counterparties, regulators, and carriers. The questionnaire is measuring whether that surface exists — and firms that cannot document these four categories have a gap that the questionnaire will surface.

**Section 3: Why Standard Frameworks Don't Fill the Geneva Gap**

ISO 42001, the EU AI Act Annex VIII documentation requirements, and NIST AI RMF provide general documentation structure. They do not answer the Geneva-specific question: what does adequate documentation look like for a two-to-five-attorney Geneva law firm that deployed AI tools in 2023 without a governance policy?

The answer is not in the frameworks. The answer is in the carrier questionnaire — because the carrier's underwriting interest is precisely in the documentation that would allow them to assess the firm's AI governance accountability exposure. The questionnaire is a proxy for what adequate documentation means at boutique professional services scale.

**Section 4: A Documentation Architecture Geneva Firms Can Build Now**

The proposed architecture has three components, corresponding to the three documentation problems Geneva firms face:

**Component 1: Retroactive reconstruction for the 2023-2024 period**
Firms cannot recover what was never documented. But they can document the reconstruction. The retroactive reconstruction component consists of: an inventory of AI tools and configurations in use during 2023-2024 (obtained through staff interviews, email records, billing records for AI tool subscriptions); a reconstruction map showing which AI tools were used for which client matter categories; and a documented assessment of the gaps between what was done and what can now be documented.

This is not a retroactive governance policy. It is a documentation of what exists and what does not exist — which is itself a governance artifact.

**Component 2: Present-tense documentation architecture**
The documentation architecture for ongoing AI tool use consists of: a version-tracked tool inventory; a documented reassessment trigger protocol (what causes a review of an AI tool configuration, and who is responsible); client communication records for AI-assisted decisions; and evidence of supervision or review of AI outputs in client files.

This architecture does not require sophisticated governance infrastructure. It requires disciplined file note practice and a version-tracking system — both of which are achievable for small professional services firms.

**Component 3: Forward-looking reassessment protocol**
The documentation window that exists now will also close for the present. What firms do now will become the 2026-2027 documentation record that future carriers and counterparties will examine. The forward-looking reassessment protocol establishes: a regular review cycle for AI tool configurations; a trigger-based review mechanism (model version change, new AI use case, client matter type change); and a documentation discipline that treats AI governance decisions as discrete recordable events rather than background infrastructure.

**Section 5: The Federal Council Decision Will Not Resolve This**

The Federal Council's June deliberation will address Switzerland's AI institutional policy direction. It will not resolve what adequate AI governance documentation means for a specific Geneva law firm practice. That resolution is happening through market mechanisms — carrier questionnaires, counterparty due diligence forms, peer practice norms — and it is happening now.

Firms that wait for the Federal Council to resolve the documentation adequacy question will find, when the decision arrives, that the documentation window for the intervening period has closed without their awareness.

**Closing**

The documentation window does not announce itself. It closes quietly, through model version changes and staff departures and the accumulation of client matters to which no AI governance documentation was attached. The firms that will be best positioned when the Federal Council announces its direction are not those that waited for regulatory clarity. They are those that acted in the window — building documentation architecture using the market-formed standard as their reference, rather than waiting for formal guidance that will arrive after the window has closed.

Brotons.net's role is to make this timing visible before it becomes another named problem.

## 06 — Findings Summary: 6 Net-New Content Insights (Distinct from C214–C238 Phase 05)

### Finding 01: Documentation Decay Has a Closing Date That Is Not a Regulatory Deadline

The 2023-2024 AI governance documentation problem is not a forward-looking concern that firms can defer until regulatory clarity arrives. It is a present-tense accountability exposure with a structural closing mechanism: operational decay. AI model versions used in 2023-2024 are being discontinued by vendors, staff who configured AI tools have departed, and client matters from that period remain active in ways that make them current accountability surfaces. The documentation window closes through decay, not through regulatory mandate — and this distinction matters for how firms should respond. The only available response is present-tense documentation architecture that treats AI governance decisions as discrete recordable events, not background infrastructure. This finding is distinct from C235 Phase 05 (early-adoption-period accountability gap), which framed the problem as a named governance failure without identifying operational decay as the closing mechanism.

### Finding 02: The Carrier Questionnaire Is the Best Available Working Adequacy Standard for Boutique Geneva Firms

ISO 42001, EU AI Act Annex VIII, and NIST AI RMF provide general documentation structure but do not answer the Geneva-specific question of what adequate AI governance documentation means for a small boutique law firm, wealth management practice, or consulting boutique. The carrier AI questionnaire — specifically its third-generation 2026 iteration — is the most specific working adequacy standard available for boutique Geneva firms, because it probes the four documentation categories (tool inventory with version tracking, reassessment triggers, client communication records, AI output review evidence) that constitute the accountability surface a firm presents to counterparties and carriers. The questionnaire is an underwriting instrument, not a regulatory standard — but it is the market's convergence on what adequate AI governance documentation means at boutique professional services scale. This finding is distinct from C238 Phase 05 (carrier questionnaire as diagnostic tool), which framed the questionnaire as a diagnostic input to governance review rather than as the best available working adequacy standard for firms without regulatory guidance.

### Finding 03: The Federal Council's June 2026 Deliberation Will Not Resolve Documentation Adequacy for Geneva Boutiques

The Federal Council's June 2026 AI policy deliberation will set Switzerland's institutional direction on AI governance but will not resolve what adequate AI governance documentation means for specific Geneva professional services firm types. This gap is structural: regulatory direction defines the frame; it does not fill the frame with firm-type-specific documentation requirements for 2-5 attorney law firms, 50-200 employee wealth management boutiques, or 2-15 employee consulting boutiques. Firms that wait for the Federal Council's decision to define their documentation obligations will face a double gap: an unresolved regulatory question plus a closed documentation window for the intervening period. The market-formed standard (carrier questionnaire) and the documentation architecture described in Finding 02 are the available references until formal regulatory guidance arrives — and the window for acting on them is open now. This finding is distinct from C236 Phase 05 (EU AI Act high-risk documentation requirements), which addressed the EU regulatory dimension without addressing the structural inadequacy of waiting for regulatory resolution to define boutique-scale documentation adequacy.

### Finding 04: The EU Client Questionnaire Is Creating an Obligation for Swiss Firms That Operates Independently of Swiss Regulatory Status

Swiss firms serving EU-based clients or counterparties are receiving EU AI Act high-risk system questionnaires asking for documentation of post-market monitoring, conformity assessment, and Annex VIII technical documentation. These questionnaires create an obligation for Swiss firms that operates independently of the Swiss firm's regulatory understanding of their own EU AI Act status. From the EU client's perspective, the Swiss firm is a service provider whose AI system documentation they are required to maintain under EU AI Act obligations — regardless of whether the Swiss firm considers itself subject to the EU AI Act. This is a structural change in the Swiss professional services accountability environment happening through client pressure rather than regulatory enforcement. Swiss firms that do not have documentation architecture capable of responding to EU client questionnaires are accumulating undisclosed accountability exposure through client relationships they believe are governed by Swiss law only. This finding is distinct from C236 Phase 05 (EU AI Act high-risk documentation arriving via client questionnaires), which identified the mechanism without analyzing the structural accountability exposure that arises independently of the Swiss firm's own regulatory classification.

### Finding 05: Geneva Professional Services Firms Face a Three-Layer Documentation Architecture Problem, Not a Single Documentation Gap

The AI governance documentation problem for Geneva boutique professional services firms is not a single gap to be filled. It is a three-layer architecture problem: retroactive reconstruction for the 2023-2024 early-adoption period (where documentation was not created), present-tense documentation architecture for current AI tool use (where the infrastructure to create adequate documentation does not exist in most boutiques), and forward-looking reassessment protocol (where no trigger-based review mechanism is in place). Each layer requires different tools and different organizational disciplines. Most published AI governance guidance addresses the second layer (present-tense documentation) without addressing the retroactive reconstruction problem or the forward-looking reassessment protocol — leaving firms with a partial solution that does not close the full accountability exposure. The documentation archaeology approach (C235 Phase 05) addressed the retroactive layer but did not integrate it with present-tense architecture or forward-looking protocol as a three-layer system. This finding is distinct from C235 Phase 05 (documentation archaeology protocol) and C233 Phase 05 (lateral integration protocol), which addressed individual components without naming the three-layer architecture as a structural system that firms must address simultaneously.

### Finding 06: Brotons.net's Editorial Positioning Opportunity Is the Translation Function Between Market-Formed Standard and Formal Regulatory Record

The most significant editorial opportunity for Brotons.net in the current period is not forward-looking regulatory analysis or framework comparison — both of which are covered by larger publishers. It is the translation function between what is forming in Geneva's informal AI governance information circuit (carrier underwriting conversations, bar sessions, peer exchanges, client due diligence questionnaires) and what appears in formal published record. The informal circuit is operating 3-6 months ahead of formal publication, and no Geneva publisher is systematically translating the informal circuit's output into accessible editorial content. The consequence is that the Geneva professionals who most need this intelligence — mid-career practitioners in small firms, who do not have access to senior Geneva peer networks — are underserved. Brotons.net's bilingual EN/FR positioning and Geneva-specific institutional depth make it the natural publication for this translation function. This finding is distinct from C237 Phase 05 (Geneva informal standard formation mechanism), which named the phenomenon without identifying the translation function itself as a specific Brotons.net editorial positioning opportunity with distinct reader outcome and competitive moat value.
