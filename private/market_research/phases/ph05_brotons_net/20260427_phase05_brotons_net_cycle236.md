## 02 — Market Signals: What's Happening in the Swiss AI Governance Information Space

**What is resonating right now:**

Three converging signals indicate that the EU AI Act high-risk documentation requirements are arriving in the Geneva professional services market as a present-tense problem — not as regulatory theory, but as a specific documentation and compliance question that is producing operational pressure on Swiss firms serving EU clients.

First, the EU client questionnaire has arrived in Geneva. Swiss professional service firms with EU-based clients are receiving structured questionnaires from their clients' legal and procurement departments asking about post-market monitoring plans, conformity assessment documentation, and the specific AI systems used in EU-related work. These questionnaires are not generic AI governance questions — they are specific EU AI Act high-risk compliance questions that flow from the EU deployer's own regulatory obligations under Articles 9 and 72-74 of the EU AI Act. The Geneva firm receiving these questionnaires is on the service side of an EU AI Act obligation it did not know it had until the questionnaire arrived.

Second, the Swiss Federal Council's parallel AI policy framework — currently in consultation draft — is being read by Swiss firms with EU exposure as an anticipation document: a signal of how Swiss domestic regulation will align with the EU AI Act, and what the documentation requirements will look like for Swiss firms that serve EU clients. The June 2026 consultation deadline is creating a decision window — firms are asking what the Swiss framework will require before committing to documentation infrastructure, and finding that the answer is not available yet. This is producing a documentation investment deferral problem: the EU obligations are present-tense; the Swiss framework is not yet final; and the documentation infrastructure that would satisfy both is not being built because the final Swiss frame is unknown.

Third, the ISO 42001 certification pathway is being read by Swiss firms as a potential documentation solution — a standardized framework that might produce the documentation structure required by the EU AI Act without requiring each firm to construct its own documentation architecture from the regulatory text. The certification is being purchased by some Geneva firms as a pre-emptive documentation investment. But the question of whether ISO 42001 certification constitutes conformity evidence under the EU AI Act — or whether it is a best-practice framework that sits alongside rather than satisfying the regulatory requirement — is not resolved by any Swiss authority or publication.

The EU AI Act's high-risk AI system provisions — Articles 6 through 9, and the post-market monitoring obligations under Articles 72 through 74 — are now in effect for systems classified as high-risk under Annex III. The conformity assessment procedures are operational. The first enforcement actions under the high-risk regime are beginning to arrive in EU member states. The market signal is clear: the EU AI Act is not a future regulatory development. It is a present-tense enforcement reality for organizations with high-risk AI systems in the EU.

For Swiss firms serving EU clients, the EU AI Act applies to their EU clients — the EU deployers — who have regulatory obligations that flow back to their service providers as documentation requirements. The Swiss firm that advises EU clients using high-risk AI systems is on the service side of an EU AI Act obligation it did not consent to, was not consulted on, and is not subject to directly — but which is producing real documentation pressure as EU clients ask their Swiss service providers to confirm their AI tool conformity and produce post-market monitoring documentation.

The Geneva Bar Association and the Swiss Bar Association (SVA/ORDRE) have not issued guidance on the Swiss professional secrecy — EU AI Act audit trail collision. **Structure:**

*Opening:* The scenario. A Geneva advisor receives a structured questionnaire from an EU-based client's legal department. The questionnaire is about EU AI Act compliance. It asks specifically about post-market monitoring plans for AI systems used in the client's work. It asks about conformity assessment documentation. It asks about the specific AI tools used, their configuration state, and the records kept of their outputs. *Section 1 — What the EU AI Act actually requires from a Swiss service provider*
The EU AI Act applies to providers and deployers of high-risk AI systems within the EU. Swiss firms are not directly subject to the EU AI Act — Switzerland is not an EU member. But Swiss firms that serve EU clients and use AI systems in that work are on the service side of their EU clients' regulatory obligations. The EU client — as a deployer of a high-risk AI system — is required to maintain post-market monitoring documentation under Article 9 and Articles 72-74, to conduct conformity assessments under the procedures in Annexes IV and VII, and to produce this documentation on request from EU market surveillance authorities. These obligations flow back to the Swiss service provider as documentation requirements: the EU client needs documentation from its service providers to satisfy its own EU AI Act obligations. The Swiss firm that does not have this documentation cannot respond to the EU client's questionnaire — and the EU client that cannot produce the documentation cannot satisfy its own regulatory obligation. The documentation gap has a regulatory consequence in the EU, even though the Swiss firm is not directly regulated.

*Section 2 — The specific documentation gap: what nFADP compliance does not cover*
The Swiss nFADP documentation architecture — data processing records, tool inventories, client communication documentation — is designed to satisfy the Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection's requirements. It is not designed to satisfy the EU AI Act's post-market monitoring documentation requirements for high-risk AI systems. The gap is specific and operational. The nFADP documentation captures: what data was processed, by which tools, for what purpose. The EU AI Act post-market monitoring documentation requires: how the AI system performed against its defined parameters during use, what deviations or anomalies were detected, what the outputs looked like in relation to the system's intended purpose, what incidents occurred and how they were addressed, and what the record of this monitoring activity shows over time. These are different documentation questions. One is about data processing compliance. The other is about AI system performance and deviation monitoring over time. A Swiss firm that has invested in nFADP compliance has not invested in EU AI Act post-market monitoring compliance — and the two investments are not interchangeable.

*Section 3 — The Geneva professional secrecy collision*
The EU AI Act's post-market monitoring documentation requirement creates a structural tension with Geneva professional secrecy. The documentation requirement implies maintaining records that capture, for specific client matters: how AI contributed to the work, what the AI produced, what the professional reviewed, and what the professional decided. This is a detailed, client-matter-specific record of AI-assisted professional work — exactly the kind of record that Geneva professional secrecy is designed to protect from disclosure. The collision is not hypothetical: the EU AI Act's audit trail requirement assumes that the documentation it requires will be available for regulatory review; Geneva professional secrecy assumes that client-specific records of professional work will not be disclosed to third parties. The question of whether these two obligations can be reconciled — and if so, how — is not resolved by any current Geneva bar guidance. The Geneva professional who is building EU AI Act-compliant documentation infrastructure is building it inside a structural collision that has not been officially acknowledged or resolved.

*Section 4 — Provider or deployer: the Swiss firm's role in the EU AI Act chain*
The EU AI Act distinguishes between providers of high-risk AI systems (those who place the system on the market or put it into service) and deployers (those who use the system under their own responsibility). Swiss firms that use AI tools provided by third-party vendors are likely deployers under the EU AI Act, not providers — the vendor placed the system on the market. But the Swiss firm's role as a service provider to an EU client adds a further layer: the Swiss firm is providing professional services to an EU deployer, and the EU deployer's post-market monitoring obligations require documentation from the Swiss firm about how AI was used in the services provided. This creates a documentation obligation that is specific to the service relationship — the Swiss firm needs to document its use of AI in client services, not just its use of AI tools. The documentation requirement is client-matter-specific, not just tool-specific.

*Section 5 — The ISO 42001 question: conformity evidence or best practice*
ISO 42001 certification — the AI management system standard — is being adopted by some Geneva firms as a pre-emptive documentation investment for EU AI Act purposes. The certification provides a structured management system for AI governance: documented policies, procedures, monitoring protocols, and incident management processes. It produces the kind of documentation structure that the EU AI Act post-market monitoring requirement implies. But the question of whether ISO 42001 certification constitutes conformity evidence under the EU AI Act — formally satisfying the regulatory documentation requirement — is not definitively resolved. The EU AI Act refers to harmonized standards as the basis for conformity presumption, and ISO 42001 has not been formally designated as a harmonized standard under the EU AI Act. Geneva firms investing in ISO 42001 certification are investing in a best-practice framework that may provide significant documentation substance — and that may not constitute formal conformity evidence until or unless ISO 42001 is formally referenced in the EU AI Act's harmonized standards framework. The practical value of ISO 42001 for EU AI Act documentation purposes is real. Its formal status as conformity evidence is not yet established.

*Section 6 — What to build now, before the Swiss framework is final*
The Swiss Federal Council's parallel AI policy framework will resolve the question of how Swiss domestic regulation aligns with the EU AI Act for Swiss firms serving EU clients. That resolution is not yet available — the consultation runs until June 2026. But the EU AI Act obligations are present-tense now. The EU client questionnaires are arriving now. And the Geneva professional who is waiting for the Swiss framework to be final before building documentation infrastructure is allowing the documentation window to close on decisions that have already been made and work that has already been done. What can be built now — in advance of the Swiss framework — is the documentation architecture for the service relationship: client-matter-specific records of AI use, output logs for AI-assisted work, deviation monitoring records, and incident logs. These records are not yet required by Swiss domestic law. They are required by the EU AI Act obligations that have flowed back to the Swiss service provider through the EU client relationship. Building them now — before the documentation window closes on the decisions and work already performed — is the appropriate action for the Geneva professional who is receiving the EU client questionnaires and discovering they cannot answer them.
