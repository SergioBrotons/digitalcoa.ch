The protocol has three stages: inventory (what records exist from 2023–2024, what AI tools were in use); gap analysis (what those records capture about AI contribution and what they do not capture); and reconstruction mapping (which decisions can be reconstructed from available evidence, and what the evidence gap looks like for the rest). The archaeology does not close the gap — it maps it. The map is what the carrier questionnaire is asking for, and what the professional needs before they can answer the reconstruction question. This is distinct from C227 Phase 05 (reassessment protocol as missing operational methodology) by addressing the backward-looking reconstruction methodology for the early-adoption-period documentation gap, not the forward-looking trigger-based reassessment protocol for ongoing AI governance practice.

**Insight 5 — The Documentation Window Is Closing: Model Version Evolution, Tool Configuration Change, and Staff Departure Are Degrading Early-Adoption-Period Evidence at a Predictable Rate**
The reconstruction question has a temporal constraint: model versions have changed since 2023 and continue to evolve; tool configurations have been updated, overwritten, or abandoned; and the individuals who made early-adoption-period decisions are beginning to move in their careers. The evidence that would permit reconstruction of early-adoption-period AI contribution is degrading at a predictable rate — and the rate is not slowing. The documentation archaeology protocol is urgent in proportion to how much time has passed since the early-adoption period ended. Before the window closes, the Geneva professional needs to inventory what exists, map what the evidence can and cannot support, and document the reconstruction mapping in a form that is maintained and updateable. This is distinct from C226 Phase 05 (accountability infrastructure gap as second-generation problem) by identifying the specific temporal urgency of the early-adoption-period documentation window — the predictable evidence degradation that makes reconstruction increasingly impossible over time — not just observing that accountability infrastructure is a second-generation problem.

**Insight 6 — The Geneva Professional Advising Clients on AI Governance Is Encountering the Early-Adoption-Period Reconstruction Problem as a Dual Obligation: Forward-Looking Governance and Retrospective Accountability**
The Geneva professional who advises clients on AI governance is now facing a dual obligation: designing adequate forward-looking governance frameworks for ongoing AI use, and being able to reconstruct accountability for AI-assisted decisions made during the early-adoption period. These are different problems with different methodologies, different time horizons, and different documentation requirements. The professional obligation to give competent advice on AI governance is not discharged by forward-looking governance design alone — the retrospective accountability question is now part of the professional obligation, and the professional who cannot answer the reconstruction question for their own early-adoption-period decisions is not in a position to advise clients on what the reconstruction obligation requires. This is a specific professional competence gap — the inability to reconstruct early-adoption-period accountability — that is distinct from the general AI governance competence questions that have been the focus of the professional development conversation to date.
