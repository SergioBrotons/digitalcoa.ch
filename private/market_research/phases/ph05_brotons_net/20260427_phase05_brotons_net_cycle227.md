## 02 — Market Signals: What's Happening in the Swiss AI Governance Information Space

**What is resonating right now:**

The temporal decay of AI governance documentation has become the live problem for experienced practitioners. The frameworks adopted in 2023–2024 are becoming structurally stale — not because of neglect, but because the AI tool ecosystem and professional practice using AI are evolving faster than the documentation cadence. The problem is not that firms stopped caring about governance — it is that governance documentation was designed with a static object in mind, and AI tool dynamics are creating a moving target that static documentation cannot track. Practitioners are recognising that "we updated our AI governance policy" is not the same as "our governance documentation reflects current practice" — and the gap is becoming visible when counterparties and carriers ask specific questions.

The human reasoning entanglement question is surfacing as a distinct professional identity problem — not a liability question yet, but a question that experienced practitioners are beginning to articulate: if my professional reasoning about client matters is now shaped by AI tools in ways I cannot fully reconstruct, what does that mean for how I attribute my professional conclusions? This is distinct from the supervision question (is AI output adequately reviewed) and the liability question (who is responsible for AI-assisted decisions) — it is a question about the integrity of the professional's own reasoning process when AI has been part of it over an extended period. This is not being addressed in any Swiss governance content.

Geneva's AI governance knowledge economy has a structural paradox: the practitioners who have developed genuine AI governance expertise are simultaneously becoming more valuable and more exposed. They are valuable because the AI governance standard is rising and the gap between experienced and inexperienced practitioners is becoming more commercially consequential. They are exposed because the same peer network that recognises their expertise is the venue in which sharing specific AI governance practice details creates liability documentation. The informal Geneva AI governance knowledge economy is operating under constraints that limit knowledge transfer — which means the firms that have figured out how to govern AI responsibly cannot easily teach others how they did it, and the firms that need to learn cannot easily find the education they're looking for.

The AI vendor sustainability question is beginning to register as a business continuity risk in Geneva professional practice — not in the abstract (AI vendor going away is a known risk) but in the specific sense of what happens to client matter documentation and AI-assisted work product when a tool is deprecated, a provider changes terms, or a model is fundamentally updated. The governance documentation that firms built for specific AI tools does not transfer cleanly when those tools change — and the reassessment process for what to do when the underlying tool changes is not part of any standard AI governance framework.

**What is missing:**

Content that addresses AI governance documentation as a temporally dynamic problem — not just "keep your policy updated" but the specific methodology for building governance documentation that can track a moving AI tool and practice reality without requiring a full policy revision every time a tool is updated.

Content that addresses the human reasoning entanglement question as a distinct professional accountability surface — what it means for professional identity, for decision attribution, and for the integrity of professional conclusions when AI has been part of the reasoning process over time.

Content that addresses the Geneva knowledge economy paradox — why genuine AI governance knowledge transfer is harder than it should be in a community of peers, and what that means for how the Geneva standard is being formed and who is being left behind in the formation process.

The relevant dynamic for the experienced Geneva practitioner is not the regulatory timeline but the temporal problem: the AI governance documentation produced under one regulatory frame may need to be rebuilt when the regulatory frame clarifies or when the AI tool ecosystem changes. The regulatory uncertainty compounds the documentation decay problem — firms are governance-documenting in a moving regulatory environment with moving AI tools, and the documentation they have is further from current reality than they assume.

The FDPIC is looking at substance — and the AI governance documentation built in 2023–2024 was often built to capture fact, not substance. The gap is not about having been negligent; it is about documentation design having been calibrated to a different question than the one the regulator is now asking.

The Geneva cantonal bar continuing education requirements are incorporating AI competence incrementally, but the competence being required remains at the level of general awareness, not at the level of the operational accountability knowledge the experienced practitioner needs. The CE credit market continues to incentivise exposure over capability — and the gap between CE-certified AI literacy and the AI governance knowledge required to practise responsibly in 2026 is not being addressed by the professional development system.

The AI tools have been updated, the use cases have evolved, and the governance documentation captures a version of reality that has moved on. ## 05 — Editorial Content Draft: Article Proposal in Brotons.net Voice

**Title (EN):**
**"The Governance Decay Problem: Why Your AI Documentation Is Becoming Stale Faster Than You Think — and What to Do About It"**

**Title (FR):**
**« Le problème de déterioration de la gouvernance : pourquoi votre documentation IA devient obsolète plus vite que vous ne le pensez — et quoi faire à ce sujet »**

**Angle:**

There is a specific problem that every Geneva professional who adopted an AI governance framework in 2023 or 2024 is confronting now, in April 2026, and that no Swiss publisher has addressed in operational terms. The problem is not that the framework was wrong, or that the governance effort was misplaced. The problem is that AI governance documentation has a structural decay problem — it was designed to document a static state, and the state it documents is no longer static. The AI tools have been updated. The use cases have evolved. The review processes have changed in ways that were never formally documented. And the documentation that was built to demonstrate accountable AI practice now documents a version of reality that no longer exists.

This article is about the temporal governance problem — why AI governance documentation decays, what the decay looks like in a Geneva professional firm, and what a methodology for managing ongoing documentation accuracy looks like when the object of governance is moving. It is written for the Geneva professional who has already done the first-generation AI governance work and who is now discovering that the work they did needs a different kind of maintenance than they anticipated.

**Structure:**

*Opening:* The scenario. A Geneva managing partner reviews the firm's AI governance documentation before a counterparty meeting and realises that the documentation they would produce — the policy, the tool inventory, the review process — describes a version of practice that the firm has already moved past. The AI tools have been updated twice. The client communication approach has been adjusted based on what clients are actually asking. The review workflow has evolved to handle volume. None of these changes were formally documented because none of them required a policy revision — they were operational adaptations. But the documentation now captures a firm that no longer exists in exactly that form. This is the governance decay problem, and it is structural, not accidental.

*Section 1 — Why AI governance documentation has a different decay problem from other professional documentation*
Professional documentation has always had a staleness risk — policies get outdated, procedures drift from practice. But AI governance documentation has a different and more acute decay problem because the object of governance is changing at a rate that other professional documentation does not experience. An AI tool that was implemented eighteen months ago may now be a different product with different output characteristics, different configuration options, and different behaviour at the boundaries of its capability. The governance documentation that was built to describe that tool describes the product as it was, not as it is. And the governance decision about how much human review is appropriate for outputs from that tool was made in the context of the tool's previous performance profile — which may have changed without the firm being aware of it. This is not a documentation discipline problem. It is a structural mismatch between the cadence of governance documentation and the rate of change in AI tool capability and use patterns.

*Section 2 — The three sources of governance decay in a Geneva professional firm*
The first source is tool update drift — when an AI tool is updated (a new model version, a configuration change, a new capability), the governance documentation that was built for the previous version describes a tool that no longer exists in that form. The firm continues operating under documentation that describes the old tool. The second source is use case differentiation — when the firm begins using the same AI tool in different practice areas, the governance documentation that was built for the initial use case is applied to use cases it was not designed for. The review adequacy standard that was appropriate for one practice area may not be appropriate for another, but the documentation is the same. The third source is client expectation evolution — as clients become more sophisticated in their own understanding of AI, the firm's client communication about AI use needs to reflect a more specific and accurate picture of what is actually happening in their matters. The client communication language documented six months ago may be insufficient for the expectations the client now has. These three sources compound each other, and the result is a documentation state that is further from current practice than the firm realises.

*Section 3 — The Geneva-specific dimension: why the international client portfolio accelerates decay*
Geneva professional firms serving international clients or EU-exposed counterparties are operating under a compound documentation standard that adds urgency to the governance decay problem. When a client's counterparty AI governance questionnaire asks specific questions about AI practice in their matters, the questions are calibrated to current market expectations — which are rising as the standard evolves. A Geneva boutique's AI governance documentation, built to answer the questions the market was asking eighteen months ago, may not answer the questions the market is asking now. The decay is not just about the tool changing — it is about the standard the documentation is being measured against changing faster than the documentation can be updated. And for firms with EU-exposed counterparties, the standard is rising continuously as the EU AI Act's risk-based obligations begin to shape what counterparties expect from their service providers.

*Section 4 — The reassessment protocol: how to govern a moving target*
The governance decay problem cannot be solved by updating documentation more frequently — the cadence would need to be too high to be operationally sustainable, and the updates would become bureaucratic exercises rather than genuine governance. The solution is a reassessment protocol — a structured process for re-evaluating AI governance documentation when specific trigger events occur, rather than on a fixed calendar. The trigger events that should initiate a governance documentation reassessment are: a model version change in an AI tool in active use, a new use case for an existing AI tool in a different practice area, a substantive client question about AI practice in their matters that the existing documentation cannot answer, a carrier underwriting questionnaire that asks questions the existing documentation cannot address, and a counterparty AI governance questionnaire response that the firm cannot independently evaluate. These five trigger events cover the primary sources of governance decay. When any of them occurs, the reassessment protocol requires the firm to evaluate whether the existing governance documentation accurately reflects current practice — and if it does not, to rebuild the relevant documentation before the trigger event creates an accountability gap in a professional moment.

*Section 5 — What the reassessment protocol produces and why it matters for the accountability infrastructure*
The reassessment protocol does not produce a new policy document every time a trigger event occurs. It produces targeted documentation updates that address the specific gap created by the trigger event. When an AI tool is updated, the tool documentation is reassessed and updated. When a new use case is added, the use-case-specific review standard is defined and documented. When a client asks a question the existing documentation cannot answer, the client communication record is rebuilt to reflect current practice. The reassessment protocol is designed to keep the four components of the accountability infrastructure (tool documentation, decision attribution, client communication record, FADP audit trail) accurate and current without requiring a complete governance framework rebuild. The protocol is a maintenance methodology, not a governance design — it assumes the framework is in place and focuses on keeping the framework's documentation aligned with the reality it is supposed to describe.

*Section 6 — The competitive consequence of governance decay and the reassessment protocol as differentiation*
Firms that have a reassessment protocol in place — that can demonstrate their governance documentation is current because they have a structured process for keeping it current — are beginning to be distinguished from firms that have governance documentation that was accurate when it was produced but has since drifted from practice. The distinction is not visible in the policy document itself — both firms have a policy. The distinction is visible when a carrier asks a specific question, when a counterparty probes the detail, when a client asks what happens in their specific matter. The firm with current documentation has the answer. The firm with decayed documentation does not — and the gap between the two is becoming more commercially consequential as the standard rises. The reassessment protocol is a competitive infrastructure investment that most Geneva boutique firms have not yet made. The firms that make it now will be better positioned when the standard requires it.

*Closing:* The governance decay problem is structural, not a failure of diligence. It is a consequence of having built governance documentation for a moving target — and the target is moving faster now than it was when the frameworks were first adopted. The solution is not more frequent documentation projects. It is a reassessment protocol — a trigger-based process for keeping the accountability infrastructure accurate as the AI tool ecosystem and professional practice evolve. The firms that build this maintenance methodology now will be in a different position when the next carrier questionnaire arrives, the next counterparty probe comes in, or the next client question exposes a gap in documentation they thought was adequate.

**Intended reader outcome:**

A Geneva managing partner who has been working on AI governance for 18–24 months finishes the article understanding why their documentation has been drifting from practice without any specific failure on their part, what the three sources of governance decay are in their firm, what the five trigger events are that should initiate a reassessment, and what the reassessment protocol methodology looks like as a practical maintenance process — not a full governance redesign, but a structured way of keeping the accountability infrastructure accurate as the target moves.

## 06 — Findings Summary: 6 Net-New Content or Editorial Insights

*Distinct from C214–C226 Phase 05.*

**Insight 1 — AI Governance Documentation Has a Structural Temporal Decay Problem Distinct from Documentation Negligence**
The first-generation AI governance documentation was designed to document a static target — a specific AI tool, a specific use case, a specific review process. AI tool ecosystems are structurally dynamic: model versions update, capabilities expand, output characteristics change, and use cases proliferate across practice areas. The result is that governance documentation built for an AI tool in its initial deployment state becomes inaccurate not through negligence but through the normal evolution of the underlying tool and its use. This is distinct from C222 Phase 05 (documentation debt from past adoption) and C224 Phase 05 (version-aware documentation architecture) by framing the problem as a structural decay mechanism rather than a documentation gap to be closed or a version-control practice to be implemented — the decay is inherent to the governance documentation design, and the solution requires a reassessment protocol, not better documentation discipline.

**Insight 2 — The Human Reasoning Entanglement Problem Is a Distinct Professional Accountability Surface That No Swiss Publisher Is Addressing**
Experienced practitioners who have been working with AI tools over an extended period are beginning to recognise that their professional reasoning about client matters is entangled with AI reasoning in ways they cannot fully reconstruct. This is not the supervision question (is AI output adequately reviewed) or the liability question (who is responsible for AI-assisted decisions) — it is a question about the integrity of the professional's own reasoning process when AI has been part of it over time, and what that means for decision attribution when the professional is asked to explain their conclusions. The editorial opportunity is to frame this as a distinct accountability surface and to provide the methodology for documenting professional reasoning in a way that accounts for AI's role in it — not to replace professional judgment with process documentation, but to create a record of how professional judgment was formed in AI-assisted practice. This is distinct from C221 Phase 05 (where AI ends and professional accountability begins — the boundary question) by focusing on the entanglement of reasoning over time, not the discrete boundary at a point in time.

**Insight 3 — The Geneva AI Governance Knowledge Economy Has a Structural Paradox That Is Constraining Standard Formation**
The Geneva community has an informal tier structure of AI governance practice — but the peer network that could transfer knowledge from the firms that have figured out how to govern AI responsibly to the firms that are still building is constrained by the same liability and competitive concerns that make AI governance documentation necessary. Genuine knowledge transfer requires sharing specific practice details, and sharing specific practice details creates documentation that could be used against the sharing firm in a liability context. The result is that the firms most capable of teaching AI governance practice are the least free to do so publicly. This structural paradox means the Geneva standard is forming through market mechanisms (carrier questions, counterparty probes, client expectations) rather than through peer knowledge transfer — which makes the standard formation less efficient and less accessible to firms that are earlier in their AI governance journey. This is distinct from C223 Phase 05 (Geneva as living laboratory for international standards) by focusing on the knowledge economy constraint on standard formation rather than the institutional mechanisms of standards formation.

**Insight 4 — The Reassessment Protocol Is the Missing Operational Methodology in AI Governance Practice**
Geneva professional firms have adopted AI governance frameworks and built accountability infrastructure — but they do not have a methodology for keeping that infrastructure accurate as the AI tool ecosystem and practice reality evolve. The missing operational concept is the reassessment protocol: a trigger-based process (model update, new use case, client question the documentation cannot answer, carrier questionnaire, counterparty probe) that initiates targeted documentation reassessment without requiring a complete governance framework rebuild. The protocol is a maintenance methodology, not a governance design — it assumes the framework is in place and focuses on keeping documentation aligned with the reality it describes. This is distinct from C219 Phase 05 (workflow integration as next frontier) by addressing the documentation maintenance methodology rather than the workflow integration of governance into professional practice.

**Insight 5 — The Three-Source Decay Model Explains Why AI Governance Documentation Drift Is Systematic, Not Accidental**
The three sources of governance decay in a Geneva professional firm — tool update drift, use case differentiation, and client expectation evolution — combine to create a systematic documentation drift that is faster than any calendar-based review cadence can manage. Tool update drift means the documented tool is not the tool in production. Use case differentiation means the governance standard appropriate for one practice area is being applied to use cases it was not designed for. Client expectation evolution means the documented client communication no longer reflects the standard the client now expects. These three sources are not independent — they compound each other. The editorial opportunity is to provide the three-source decay model as a diagnostic tool that allows a Geneva managing partner to assess where the documentation decay is concentrating in their own firm and which trigger events are most likely to surface it in a professional moment. This is distinct from C226 Phase 05 (accountability infrastructure gap as second-generation problem) by focusing on the decay mechanism and its systematic character, not the structural components of the infrastructure.

**Insight 6 — The Reassessment Protocol as Competitive Infrastructure: Why the Firms That Build It Now Will Be Differentiated When the Standard Rises**
The competitive consequence of the governance decay problem is not about having an AI governance framework — almost every Geneva professional firm of any size now has one. It is about being able to demonstrate that the accountability infrastructure is current — that the documentation accurately reflects what the firm is actually doing, at the level of detail that carriers, counterparties, and clients now require.
