**Title:** The Geneva AI Accountability Map: Where the Actual Obligations Actually Are

**Angle:** Geneva professional services principals are being told to prepare for Swiss AI regulation. The Federal Council is developing a consultation draft, the AI Convention is moving toward ratification, and Geneva hosts two major international AI governance events in July 2026. Meanwhile, the carrier attestation they signed in Q1 2026 — personally — is the operative document that will define their accountability exposure. These are not the same conversation. The regulatory framework is being built. The accountability mechanism is already operating. This article maps where the actual obligations actually sit, and why the difference matters for Geneva principals managing personal exposure.

**Structure:**

**Section 1 — The Accountability Architecture Nobody Published**

The Federal Chancellery established an AI contact point on February 1, 2026. It is the designated point of coordination for federal AI matters. What decisions does it make? Who is accountable for them? How does it interact with FDPIC, FINMA, and sector-specific regulators? These questions have no published answers. This is not a gap in public communication — it reflects a genuine ambiguity in the institutional architecture. Brotons.net names the Federal AI Coordination Office contact point as a specific named accountability actor that Geneva principals need to track, even if its specific function is still being defined.

**Section 2 — The AI Convention's Public-Private Boundary**

The Council of Europe AI Convention will primarily apply to public-sector actors when implemented into Swiss law. Geneva's professional services firms are not public authorities. The convention does not directly create obligations for them. This sounds like good news. It is not. The convention creates obligations for the public authorities Geneva firms interact with — cantonal governments, federal administration, public entities. Those obligations will flow into private sector accountability through a different mechanism: private law. Client contracts will increasingly reference AI Convention compliance obligations. Professional liability exposure will be calibrated against the standard of care that AI Convention-compliant public institutions establish. Carrier underwriting criteria will reflect AI Convention implementation status. The convention is a public instrument with private sector consequences that nobody has named as a structural condition.

**Section 3 — The Binding/Non-Binding Structure Problem**

The Federal Council is developing two things simultaneously: a consultation draft for legally binding AI regulation measures, and an implementation plan for non-legally binding measures (self-declarations, industry solutions, voluntary commitments). Both are being developed by the same departments (FDJP, DETEC, FDFA, EAER) in the same policy process. The structural relationship between binding obligations and non-binding measures — which is mandatory, which is recommended, which creates accountability and which does not — is not defined in any public source. Geneva firms preparing for AI governance adequacy cannot determine from public sources what actually binds them. This is a specific accountability preparation problem with a specific name: the binding/non-binding structural ambiguity. It is not a gap in communication. It is a gap in the architecture.

**Section 4 — Where the Actual Accountability Sits**

The carrier third-generation questionnaire is the most operationally specific AI governance standard operating in the Geneva private sector professional services market. It is not published doctrine. It has no public comment period. It is administered by insurance carriers through commercial underwriting. But it is the document that determines whether a Geneva principal's AI governance practices are adequate or inadequate, and it does so through a personal signature. The regulatory framework being built by the Federal Council will eventually provide clarity. The carrier questionnaire is providing it now. Geneva principals managing personal accountability exposure need to understand this distinction: the regulatory framework is being developed. The operative standard is already in effect.

**Intended reader outcome:** A Geneva professional services principal reads this article and understands that the accountability they are managing is not primarily defined by the Federal Council's developing regulatory framework — it is defined by the carrier attestation they signed, the client contracts they hold, and the professional liability standard established by their practice. The regulatory framework matters. But it is not the primary operative accountability mechanism. Understanding which instrument is actually governing your exposure is the first step to managing it.
