## 02 — Market Signals: What's Happening in the Swiss AI Governance Information Space

**What is resonating right now:**

The accountability infrastructure question has moved past the policy stage. Practitioners who adopted AI governance frameworks 18–24 months ago are discovering that the frameworks described a target state, not an operational system. The gap between "we have a policy" and "we can demonstrate accountable AI practice in a specific matter" is now the primary tension for the experienced Geneva professional. Carriers, counterparties, and clients are increasingly precise in what they ask about AI — and the answers practitioners can give are falling short of the questions being posed.

The carrier underwriting signal is sharpening. Three developments are converging: carriers are cross-referencing AI governance documentation against the FDPIC's investigation patterns (treating the regulator's enforcement record as a map of what creates exposure); the questions asked in underwriting questionnaires are increasingly specific about what happens at the tool-and-workflow level; and brokers are beginning to advise clients that AI governance documentation quality will affect coverage terms, not just availability. The insurance market is becoming a secondary regulatory mechanism for AI governance standards — not through published underwriting guidelines, but through the specific questions being asked and the responses that are being accepted.

The counterparty questionnaire has become an accountability gap, not a compliance signal. Geneva firms are receiving AI governance questionnaires from international counterparties and discovering that they cannot evaluate the responses — they collect the documents but lack the internal reference framework to assess whether the answers represent genuine practice or surface compliance. This is creating a market inefficiency: the questionnaire is supposed to provide assurance, but the receiving firm cannot use it as intended. Sophisticated counterparties are aware of this limitation and are beginning to probe AI governance practice through structured interviews or requests for specific documentation samples rather than policy documents.

Geneva's international positioning is creating an AI governance asymmetry. Geneva-based international organisations, foreign diplomatic missions, and multinational corporations operating in Geneva are subject to AI governance standards from multiple jurisdictions simultaneously — EU AI Act requirements, home-country regulations, and institutional policies. Geneva boutique firms serving these clients are being asked to demonstrate AI governance practice that meets the standards these clients apply to themselves. The implication is that Geneva professional firms need to be operating at the level of the most rigorous standard in their client portfolio, not the minimum Swiss domestic requirement.

**What is missing:**

Content that maps the accountability infrastructure components — the specific documentary, verification, and institutional mechanisms that a Geneva boutique firm needs to have in place to be able to demonstrate accountable AI practice in a specific matter. No Swiss publisher is addressing the structural question: what does the accountability infrastructure actually consist of, how do the components interact, and what is the sequence of building it.

Content that addresses the evaluation gap in counterparty AI governance due diligence — the fact that Geneva firms are receiving AI governance questionnaires they cannot evaluate, and what it means for how the firm should be building its own AI governance practice to become a credible counterparty rather than a questionnaire recipient.

Content that maps what the FDPIC's investigation patterns reveal about AI governance enforcement priorities — not as regulatory commentary, but as practical intelligence for the practitioner who needs to know where the regulator is looking and why.

The Federal Council's three-track AI policy continues to define the structural frame — ratifying the Council of Europe AI Convention, maintaining sector-specific regulation, developing non-binding measures alongside targeted legal changes. The consultation draft on AI Convention integration remains anticipated by end of 2026. For Geneva professional firms, the relevant dynamic is not the regulatory timeline but the commercial pressure mechanism: contractual requirements from EU-exposed clients, carrier underwriting questions derived from FDPIC investigation patterns, and counterparty AI governance questionnaires carrying EU AI Act expectations into Swiss practice are all creating operational obligations that are arriving before domestic law requires them.

The FDPIC's enforcement record is becoming a practical intelligence source, not just a regulatory document. Practitioners who understand what the FDPIC has been investigating and why can infer what the regulator considers the accountability infrastructure to consist of. This inference is imperfect but more operationally useful than waiting for published guidance.

The nFADP's existing obligations (Articles 12, 21, and 22) are creating a retrospective accountability problem. AI systems that have been in use since before the nFADP entered force were not implemented with FADP documentation requirements in mind. The gap between what the nFADP requires and what the current AI governance documentation can demonstrate is now surfacing as a specific compliance exposure for firms that adopted AI tools in the 2022–2024 period without contemporaneous FADP documentation.

Clients who lack AI governance capacity are being asked by their own counterparties, carriers, and regulators to demonstrate AI governance practice — and they are turning to their service providers for the demonstration. The accountability chain runs from the international counterparty to the Geneva boutique firm in a way that was not anticipated when the firm's AI governance framework was designed.

Cantonal bar continuing education requirements are incrementally incorporating AI competence — but the competence being required is general AI literacy, not the specific accountability infrastructure knowledge that the experienced practitioner needs. The continuing education credit market is incentivising exposure to AI concepts, not the operational capability to demonstrate accountable AI practice.

**Editorial timing assessment:**

The current moment has a specific quality: the experienced practitioner has completed the first-generation AI governance work (policy adoption, framework implementation, initial tool deployment) and is now confronting the accountability infrastructure gap — the difference between what the framework describes and what the market requires in specific professional moments. ## 05 — Editorial Content Draft: Article Proposal in Brotons.net Voice

**Title (EN):**
**"The Accountability Infrastructure Gap: What Geneva Professional Firms Are Actually Building When They Move Past the AI Governance Framework"**

**Title (FR):**
**« L'écart d'infrastructure de responsabilité : ce que les cabinets professionnels genevois construisent réellement lorsqu'ils passent au-delà du cadre de gouvernance de l'IA »**

**Angle:**

There is a specific problem that the experienced Geneva professional is confronting now, in April 2026, that no Swiss publisher has addressed in practitioner-relevant terms. The problem is not about whether to have an AI governance framework — that question has been answered, at least in principle, by the practitioners who have been working on this for 12–24 months. The problem is about what comes after the framework is in place: what the accountability infrastructure actually consists of, why the framework alone is not sufficient, and what specific documentary, verification, and institutional mechanisms a Geneva boutique firm needs to have in place to be able to demonstrate accountable AI practice in a specific professional moment — when a carrier asks, when a counterparty probes, when a client asks a direct question, when a regulator looks.

This article maps the accountability infrastructure gap — the structural difference between "we have a policy" and "we can demonstrate accountable AI practice in this specific matter, to the level of detail the market now requires." It is written for the Geneva professional who has the framework in place and who now needs to understand what the next layer of work actually is.

**Structure:**

*Opening:* The scenario. A Geneva managing partner receives a carrier underwriting questionnaire that asks specific questions about AI tool versions, prompt configurations, expected output variance, and human review documentation. The firm's AI governance policy is on file. None of the carrier's questions can be answered from it. The gap is not a compliance failure — it is an accountability infrastructure gap. The framework was designed to answer a different question than the one the carrier is now asking. This is the specific problem this article addresses.

*Section 1 — Why the framework is not the infrastructure*
The first-generation AI governance framework was designed to answer the question "do we have AI governance." It was not designed to answer the question "what specifically happens in this matter when AI is in the loop, can we reconstruct it, and can we show that a professional judgment was applied." These are different questions with different documentation requirements. The framework tells you what you should be doing. The accountability infrastructure is what you have when a third party asks you to demonstrate that you actually did it.

*Section 2 — The four components of accountability infrastructure in a Geneva professional firm*
The accountability infrastructure is not one thing — it is a system of four components that interact. (1) Tool documentation — not just that an AI tool is in use, but what version, what configuration, what purpose it is being used for, and what the output characteristics are. (2) Decision attribution — not just that human review occurred, but who reviewed what, what judgment was applied, and why the decision was made at the point of the AI's involvement. (3) Client communication record — what the client was told about AI use in their matter, in language specific enough that it creates an accurate record of the expectation that was set. (4) FADP audit trail — the documentation that allows the firm to demonstrate, retrospectively, that the AI system's processing complied with the lawful basis, purpose limitation, and data minimisation requirements at the time of processing. These four components are not equally developed in most Geneva boutique firms — tool documentation is the most developed, FADP audit trail is the least developed, and the gap between them is where the exposure concentrates.

*Section 3 — The Geneva-specific dimension: what international exposure adds to the infrastructure requirement*
Geneva professional firms serving international clients, EU counterparties, or organisations with EU AI Act obligations are operating under a compound standard — the Swiss domestic requirement plus the standard applied by the most rigorous jurisdiction or institutional policy in the client portfolio. This is not a theoretical exposure. It surfaces in counterparty AI governance questionnaires, in carrier underwriting questions, and in client due diligence requests that probe AI practice at the level of detail the FDPIC has been investigating. The implication for the accountability infrastructure is specific: the tool documentation, decision attribution, client communication record, and FADP audit trail need to be at the level of specificity that the most demanding standard in the client portfolio requires. For many Geneva boutique firms, this means operating at EU AI Act documentation standards for AI systems used in client matters involving EU-exposed counterparties, even though Swiss domestic law does not require this.

*Section 4 — Carrier underwriting as secondary regulatory mechanism: what the questions reveal*
The specific questions carriers are now asking in underwriting — about tool version, configuration, output variance, and human review documentation — are not random. They are derived from the FDPIC's investigation patterns and from claims experience in other jurisdictions. The carrier is building a view about what adequate AI governance documentation looks like through the specific questions it asks and the responses it accepts. Practitioners who understand this can read the carrier's questions as intelligence about the standard, not just as underwriting requirements to be satisfied. The article maps the specific question categories carriers are using and explains what each reveals about the accountability infrastructure expectation.

*Section 5 — Building the infrastructure in a boutique firm: the sequence and the time cost*
The accountability infrastructure is not built in a single project — it is built in a sequence that reflects the risk profile of the firm's practice. The article proposes a three-phase approach: (1) immediate — establish tool documentation and client communication record standards for the highest-exposure practice areas; (2) near-term — build decision attribution documentation into matter workflow for AI-assisted work; (3) ongoing — establish the FADP audit trail documentation standard and integrate it into the firm's AI tool onboarding process. Each phase has a specific output, a specific time cost, and a specific reduction in the accountability infrastructure gap. The article is specific about what each phase produces and why it matters in the sequence.

*Section 6 — What the Geneva professional community will distinguish: the competitive consequence of the infrastructure gap*
Firms that have the accountability infrastructure in place are beginning to be distinguished by counterparties, carriers, and clients in ways that are not formally measured but are real in commercial consequence. The distinction is not about having AI governance — almost everyone has a policy now. It is about being able to demonstrate accountable AI practice at the level of specificity the market now requires. This is a quiet competitive differentiation that will become more visible as the standard rises — and the standard is rising because the carriers, counterparties, and clients who are asking specific questions are raising the bar for everyone they engage with.

*Closing:* The framework was the first generation. The accountability infrastructure is the second. The practitioners who understand what the infrastructure consists of — and who begin building it now — will be in a different position when the regulatory standard clarifies, when the carrier questions get sharper, and when the counterparty questionnaires require actual answers rather than policy documents. The gap between framework and infrastructure is the work of this moment.

**Intended reader outcome:**

A Geneva managing partner who has been working on AI governance for 12–24 months finishes the article understanding exactly what the accountability infrastructure consists of, why it is distinct from the framework they already have, what the Geneva-specific dimension adds to the requirement, and what sequence to follow in building it. They leave with a clear-eyed assessment of where their firm is relative to where the market is forming — and a specific, sequenced set of steps to close the gap.
