## 02 — Market Signals: What's Happening in the Swiss AI Governance Information Space

**What is resonating right now:**

The Geneva AI governance information space has moved into a second phase where the dominant question is no longer "should we be using AI" or "what policy do we need" — it is "what do we now owe to the AI-assisted work we already produced under conditions we can no longer fully reconstruct." The market is developing a vocabulary for this problem, but the vocabulary is imprecise: firms describe it as a documentation gap, a retrospective exposure, a past-practice problem. What they are actually describing is a structural accountability gap arising from the fact that AI-shaped reasoning cannot be cleanly separated from professional judgment, and AI outputs in client files persist in ways that create reinterpretation exposure over time. The market has not yet produced a precise analytical framework for this problem — it has produced a felt problem that practitioners experience but cannot name with precision.

The carrier questionnaire cycle is accelerating. The underwriters who began asking about AI tool inventory two years ago are now asking about AI governance methodology — what the firm can demonstrate about the specific contribution of AI to professional reasoning in specific client matters, what the review standard looks like in practice, and what the firm has done about AI outputs from prior periods that are still in client files. The carrier underwriting process is forming a market standard through what it asks, and what it is beginning to ask is more substantively demanding than what the first-generation AI governance frameworks prepared practitioners for.

**What is missing:**

Content that addresses the distinction between AI governance documentation theater — documentation that exists but does not provide the evidence the accountability function requires — and substantive AI governance documentation that can actually answer the questions that carriers, clients, and counterparties are beginning to ask. The documentation theater problem is different from the documentation gap problem: the firm has documents, but the documents do not provide the accountability evidence.

Content that addresses the specific problem of AI configuration state reconstruction: what it means to be unable to verify the AI configuration state at the time a past output was produced, why that inability creates a specific accountability exposure, and what a defensible approach to the reconstruction problem looks like when complete reconstruction is not operationally feasible.

Content that addresses the professional judgment reconstruction problem — what it means that AI-shaped reasoning cannot be fully reconstructed, why this is a distinct accountability surface from the documentation gap, and what the professional owes to the accountability function when the reasoning itself is the governance surface that has been affected.

The Federal Council's three-track AI policy framework defines the structural frame. The AI Convention ratification process and the anticipated consultation draft by end of 2026 create a specific pre-publication editorial window: the period before the draft arrives is when anticipatory guidance has the highest value for practitioners making governance decisions in advance of regulatory clarification. The regulatory uncertainty compounds the accountability reconstruction problems — practitioners who have been using AI for two years are operating in a context where the accountability standards for AI-shaped reasoning are undefined by Swiss domestic law, and where the Geneva professional community is forming informal standards through what carriers ask and clients require.

The enforcement direction — toward specificity, substantiveness, and matter-specific documentation — is consistent with where the AI configuration state reconstruction problem and the professional judgment reconstruction problem will land when enforcement attention reaches them. The enforcement record is not yet addressing these specific problems, but the direction is clear: documentation must demonstrate specific, current, substantively grounded answers about AI processing in specific matters, and documentation that captures the fact of a governance framework without providing the underlying evidence is not substantive compliance.

The Geneva professional community's informal standard formation continues through carrier questionnaires, counterparty probes, client expectations, and peer conversations. But the structural silence problem persists: the firms that have most carefully developed their AI governance practice are the most careful about what they say publicly, because the peer community that respects their expertise is the community where any public acknowledgment of a governance gap could be used against them. The standard formation is happening, but it is not being recorded in a form that makes it accessible.

**Editorial timing assessment:**

The documentation theater problem and the AI configuration state reconstruction problem are the defining editorial opportunities of this moment. The documentation theater problem — the gap between documentation that exists and documentation that provides the accountability evidence the market is now asking for — is specific, operationally recognizable, and not addressed by any Swiss publisher. The editorial contribution is to name the problem precisely (AI-shaped reasoning that cannot be fully reconstructed), to explain why it is different from the documentation gap, and to provide the professional with a methodology for managing the accountability function when the reasoning itself has been affected in ways that cannot be fully documented retroactively.

## 05 — Editorial Content Draft: Article Proposal in Brotons.net Voice

**Title (EN):**
**"The Documentation Theater Problem: Why Having AI Governance Documents Is Not the Same as Having AI Governance Evidence — and What the Gap Is Costing You"**

**Title (FR):**
**« Le problème de la gouvernance spectacle : pourquoi avoir des documents de gouvernance de l'IA n'est pas la même chose que d'avoir des preuves de gouvernance de l'IA — et ce que l'écart vous coûte »**

**Angle:**

There is a specific problem that Geneva professional firms are beginning to encounter, and that no Swiss publisher has addressed with precision: the gap between AI governance documentation that exists and AI governance evidence that can actually answer the questions the accountability function is now asking. Firms have AI governance policies. They have AI use disclosure language. They have tool inventories and review process descriptions. They do not have, in any systematic way, the evidence that demonstrates what the AI actually contributed to professional reasoning in specific client matters, what the AI configuration state was at the time past outputs were produced, and what the firm has done about the accumulated AI output inventory in client files. The documentation exists. The evidence does not.

This is the documentation theater problem. It is different from the documentation gap problem. A documentation gap means you don't have documents. Documentation theater means you have documents that look like governance but that do not provide the accountability evidence the situation actually requires. The carrier questionnaire that asks about AI governance methodology is not asking whether the firm has a policy. The carrier is asking what the firm can demonstrate about how AI actually functioned in specific client matters, and the documents the firm has do not answer that question. The client who asks about a past AI-assisted recommendation is not asking whether the firm has a disclosure policy. The client is asking what the AI was configured to do when it produced that recommendation, and the firm's documentation does not provide that answer.

This article is about the documentation theater problem: why it is different from the documentation gap, what specific evidence the accountability function is now asking for that most Geneva firms cannot currently provide, what the AI configuration state reconstruction problem means in practice, and what a defensible approach to the evidence gap looks like when complete reconstruction is not operationally feasible. It is written for the Geneva professional who has AI governance documentation and who is beginning to suspect that the documentation does not provide what the situation actually requires.

**Structure:**

*Opening:* The scenario. A Geneva managing partner receives a carrier questionnaire that asks, in specific terms, to describe the firm's AI governance methodology for the past two years: what AI tools were in use, what model versions were running, what the review standard looked like for AI-assisted work in client matters, and what the firm has done about AI outputs from prior periods that remain in client files. The partner has documentation — an AI governance policy, a tool inventory, a client disclosure template. None of it answers the specific questions the carrier is asking. The documentation exists. The evidence does not. This is the documentation theater problem, and it is more common in Geneva professional practices than the firms that have it will acknowledge.

*Section 1 — The difference between documentation and evidence*
Documentation that captures the existence of a governance framework is not the same as evidence that demonstrates the framework is functioning. An AI governance policy documents that the firm has a policy. A tool inventory documents that the firm has considered what tools are in use. A client disclosure template documents that the firm has language for informing clients about AI use. None of this documentation answers the questions the accountability function is now asking: what did the AI actually do in this specific matter, what was the AI configured to do at the time, what did the professional actually review, and what did the professional decide and why. The documentation exists. The firm cannot produce the evidence.

*Section 2 — What the accountability function is now asking for*
The carrier underwriting questions have become more specific. The first-generation questionnaires asked whether the firm used AI and whether the firm had a policy. The current-generation questionnaires ask about AI governance methodology in specific operational terms: what AI tools contributed to client matter work, how the firm's professionals supervised AI outputs, what documentation exists about AI reasoning contribution in specific matters, and what the firm has done about AI outputs from prior periods. The questions require evidence — specific, substantively grounded accounts of AI use in specific client matters — not documentation that captures the existence of a governance framework. The firms that can answer the current-generation questions are being distinguished in the underwriting process. The firms that cannot are beginning to experience the documentation theater problem as an underwriting outcome.

*Section 3 — The AI configuration state reconstruction problem*
The AI configuration state at the time a past output was produced is the specific configuration of AI tools, model versions, prompt libraries, and review standards that applied when the output was created. Most Geneva firms cannot reconstruct the AI configuration state for outputs from two years ago — the records were not kept, the model version logs may no longer be accessible, and the prompt libraries have been updated since then without a preservation record. The AI configuration state reconstruction problem is not a documentation failure — it is a structural consequence of not having designed the governance system to preserve configuration state records from the point of production. The firm cannot reconstruct what it cannot reconstruct. But the firm can decide what to do about the reconstruction problem now, and the decision is not the same as simply documenting going forward.

*Section 4 — What a defensible approach to the reconstruction problem looks like*
The firm does not owe the impossible: it cannot reconstruct AI configuration states from a period when it was not preserving those records. What the firm does owe is a defensible approach to the accumulated inventory that acknowledges the reconstruction limitation and demonstrates what it has done about it. The specific approach is the tripartite triage framework: outputs in active client files where the recommendation is consequential require the most immediate attention — the documentation review should establish what can be reconstructed about the original AI configuration and review standard, and where reconstruction is not possible, the firm should document its current position on the output and the basis for its current relevance; outputs in archived client files where the matter is closed but records retained require a documentation note in the current file that flags the original AI configuration context and the firm's current position on the output; and outputs in files where the AI-assisted work was routine and not consequential can be noted as a category and reviewed on a periodic basis. The goal is to be able to demonstrate that the firm understood the reconstruction problem and acted on it deliberately, not that it retroactively reconstructed every past output.

*Section 5 — The professional judgment reconstruction problem as a distinct surface*
The AI configuration state reconstruction problem and the professional judgment reconstruction problem are related but distinct. The configuration state problem is about what the AI was doing: which model version, which tool configuration, which prompt library. The professional judgment reconstruction problem is about what the professional was doing: how the AI-shaped reasoning process incorporated AI outputs into professional decisions, and whether the professional can reconstruct that process if asked. The professional judgment reconstruction problem is harder to address than the configuration state problem, because the records for it were never kept in documentary form, and because the entanglement of AI and professional reasoning means that the reconstruction may not be fully possible even with good intentions. The response is the reasoning attribution standard: a contemporaneous one-to-three sentence annotation in the matter record that captures what role the AI played in the professional's reasoning process. Building this record going forward is the only remediation that is actually available, because the professional judgment reconstruction problem cannot be fully addressed retroactively.

*Section 6 — The carrier underwriting signal and what it requires*
The carrier underwriting signal is clear: the market is forming a standard through what it asks, and what it is asking is specific evidence of AI governance methodology in operation, not documentation of the existence of a governance framework. The firms that can provide that evidence are being distinguished in underwriting. The firms that cannot are experiencing the documentation theater problem as a market outcome. The practical implication is that the documentation gap is not only a professional obligation issue — it is a business continuity issue, because insurance capacity is becoming contingent on the ability to demonstrate substantive AI governance evidence. The defensible approach to the reconstruction problem, combined with the reasoning attribution standard for going forward, is what positions the firm to answer the current-generation carrier questions and to maintain the insurance capacity the practice requires.

*Closing:* The documentation theater problem is not a documentation failure. It is a structural consequence of the gap between documentation that captures the existence of a governance framework and evidence that demonstrates the framework is functioning in specific matters. The remediation is not to produce more documentation — it is to understand what evidence the situation actually requires, to triage the accumulated inventory with a deliberate, documented approach, and to build the reasoning attribution standard into the current practice architecture so that the outputs being produced today are outputs the firm can stand behind with evidence, not just documentation.

**Intended reader outcome:**

A Geneva managing partner finishes the article understanding what the documentation theater problem actually is (the gap between documentation that exists and evidence that can answer the accountability questions), why the carrier questionnaires have become more specific in what they ask (methodology evidence, not framework documentation), what the AI configuration state reconstruction problem means (the firm cannot verify past AI configuration states), what a defensible approach to the reconstruction problem looks like (tripartite triage framework), what the professional judgment reconstruction problem is and how it differs from the configuration state problem, what the reasoning attribution standard is and why building it forward is the only actual remediation available, and how the carrier underwriting signal is functioning as a market standard that requires substantive evidence. They leave knowing what they need to do about their current documentation and what they need to build going forward.

## 06 — Findings Summary: 6 Net-New Content or Editorial Insights

*Distinct from C214–C231 Phase 05.*

**Insight 1 — The Documentation Theater Problem Is a Distinct AI Governance Failure Mode Separate from the Documentation Gap Problem**
Most Geneva firms have AI governance documents — policies, tool inventories, disclosure templates. They do not have AI governance evidence — specific, substantively grounded accounts of what AI contributed to reasoning in specific client matters, what the AI configuration state was at the time past outputs were produced, and what the firm has done about the accumulated AI output inventory in client files. The documentation theater problem is the gap between documentation that exists and evidence that can answer the questions the accountability function is now asking. This is distinct from C227 Phase 05 (governance documentation temporal decay) by identifying the documentation theater problem as a distinct failure mode from documentation absence — firms have documentation that does not provide evidence, not a documentation gap in the ordinary sense; the theater problem requires a different remediation from the documentation gap problem.

**Insight 2 — The Carrier Underwriting Cycle Is Forming a Market Standard That Requires Methodology Evidence, Not Framework Documentation**
The first-generation carrier AI questionnaires asked whether the firm used AI and whether it had a policy. The current-generation questionnaires ask about AI governance methodology in operational terms: what AI contributed to client matter work, how professionals supervised AI outputs, what documentation exists about AI reasoning contribution in specific matters, and what the firm has done about past AI outputs in client files. The market is forming a standard through what it asks, and what it asks is evidence of substantive AI governance in operation — not documentation of the existence of a framework. This is distinct from C228 Phase 05 (carrier questionnaire as standard formation) by identifying the specific shift from framework documentation requirements to methodology evidence requirements in the current underwriting cycle, not just the carrier questionnaire as a general standard-formation mechanism.

**Insight 3 — The AI Configuration State Reconstruction Problem Is a Structurally Distinct Accountability Exposure Arising from Undocumented AI Configuration History**
The AI configuration state at the time a past output was produced — the specific model version, tool configuration, and prompt library — cannot be reconstructed by most Geneva firms that adopted AI without preserving configuration state records. This is structurally different from the documentation gap: the documentation gap means records were not created; the configuration state reconstruction problem means the records to reconstruct what the AI was doing at a specific past time are not available even though records of current state may exist. The accountability exposure is that the firm cannot verify what the AI was configured to do when past outputs were produced, which means it cannot verify whether the AI configuration was appropriate to the task. This is distinct from C230 Phase 05 (liability tail from accumulated outputs) by identifying the configuration state reconstruction problem as a specific evidence problem with its own mechanics, not as part of the general liability tail from accumulated outputs.

**Insight 4 — The Professional Judgment Reconstruction Problem Is a Distinct Accountability Surface from the Configuration State Reconstruction Problem**
The configuration state reconstruction problem is about what the AI was doing: which model version, which tool configuration, which prompt library. The professional judgment reconstruction problem is about what the professional was doing: how the professional's reasoning was shaped by AI tools in ways that cannot be fully reconstructed, and whether the professional can account for what they decided and why when the decision process incorporated AI-shaped reasoning. The professional judgment reconstruction problem is harder to address because the records for it were never kept in documentary form, and because the entanglement means full reconstruction may not be possible even with good intentions. The only available remediation is building the reasoning attribution standard forward: a contemporaneous one-to-three sentence annotation in the matter record capturing what role the AI played in the professional's reasoning process. This is distinct from C231 Phase 05 (reasoning attribution standard and periodic entanglement review) by identifying the professional judgment reconstruction problem as a structurally distinct problem from the configuration state reconstruction problem — the two problems require different framings and different responses even though they are related.

**Insight 5 — The Documentation Theater Problem Has a Specific Business Continuity Implication: Insurance Capacity Is Becoming Contingent on Evidence, Not Documentation**
The carrier underwriting signal is functioning not only as a professional obligation indicator but as a business continuity signal: firms that cannot demonstrate substantive AI governance evidence are experiencing constrained insurance capacity, not just professional obligation exposure. Insurance capacity is becoming a competitive variable in the Geneva professional services market in a way it was not before the current underwriting cycle. The documentation theater problem, if unaddressed, is not only a governance failure — it is a business continuity risk, because insurance capacity affects the firm's ability to serve clients and to operate at certain engagement scales. This is distinct from C226 Phase 05 (accountability infrastructure gap) by identifying the specific business continuity mechanism through which the documentation theater problem creates competitive consequence, not just the general accountability infrastructure gap.

**Insight 6 — The Geneva Structural Silence Problem in AI Governance Standard Formation Has a Specific Market Mechanism That Is Being Underappreciated**
The Geneva firms that have made the most progress on AI governance are the firms that are most silent about what they have learned, because the peer community that respects their expertise is the same community where any public acknowledgment of a governance gap could be used competitively against them. The result is that the AI governance standard is forming through market mechanisms — what carriers ask, what clients require, what counterparties probe — but the content of the standard is not being recorded or shared in a form that makes it accessible. The firms that need to know what the standard actually requires do not have access to the information, because the firms that best understand the standard are the firms with the strongest incentives to remain silent. This structural silence is the specific editorial contribution available to Brotons.net: providing the analytical framework that allows the broader community to understand what the market standard formation process is producing — without requiring the leading firms to speak publicly. This is distinct from C231 Phase 05 (Geneva structural silence in standard formation mechanism) by focusing on the specific market mechanism (carrier underwriting, client requirements, counterparty probes) through which the standard is forming and the specific way the structural silence operates within that mechanism, not just the general silence problem in standard formation.
