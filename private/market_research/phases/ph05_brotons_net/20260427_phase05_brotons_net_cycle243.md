## 02 — Market Signals: What's Happening in the Swiss AI Governance Information Space

### What's Resonating

**The accountability chain question arriving from clients, not regulators.** The most acute AI governance pressure arriving in Geneva professional services firms right now is coming through client relationships, not regulatory enforcement. Sophisticated clients — family offices, institutional investors, international organizations — are asking questions about AI methodology, version tracking, and output review that their Geneva advisors are structurally unprepared to answer. The signal this sends: the accountability chain question is not a compliance box to check. It is a client relationship question with immediate commercial consequences.

**Mandate letter silence on AI as newly visible exposure.** The professional services mandate letter — the document governing the attorney-client or consultant-client relationship — has historically been silent on AI. This silence is being recognized as a specific structural gap: there is no documented agreement between professional and client on AI tool use, AI output review, or AI-related authority. Firms that have been delivering AI-assisted work product for years have been doing so without any written basis establishing the terms. This is resonating because it is actionable: firms can update their mandate letter template today.

**Infrastructure AI accountability gap entering professional vocabulary.** The Swiss financial infrastructure — SIX, SwissRepoNet, SIC — operates AI-driven systems that Geneva wealth management firms depend on without evaluating. The accountability chain running through this infrastructure has been invisible to boutique firms because it sits below their operational horizon. It is now entering professional vocabulary because clients are beginning to ask about it, and firms have no prepared answer.

**Diplomatic community AI governance expectations circulating below formal publication.** Geneva's concentration of international organizations and permanent missions creates a specific environment for AI governance standard formation: informal cross-border comparison happening at the diplomatic level. Delegations compare AI governance expectations across jurisdictions in Geneva in ways they do not in their home capitals. The resulting shared expectations function as a de facto standard for Geneva firms with international organization clients — before any formal publication.

### What's Missing

**Cross-framework documentation architecture.** nFADP Art.21, FINMA model risk governance, and EU AI Act Annex VIII each impose distinct documentation requirements that Geneva firms operating across multiple regulatory environments must satisfy simultaneously. No publication has mapped the overlap between these frameworks — what documentation serves multiple frameworks, what documentation is framework-specific, and what a coherent cross-framework documentation architecture would look like for a Geneva boutique firm.

**Infrastructure accountability as named exposure category.** The accountability chain running through Swiss financial infrastructure AI systems — from client to boutique firm to infrastructure operator — has not been named as a specific exposure category. Boutique firms do not have a framework for thinking about what they owe their clients in terms of infrastructure AI oversight, or what documentation would demonstrate adequate oversight of systems they do not control.

**Mandate letter AI provisions as discrete practice document category.** No Geneva publisher has produced practical guidance on what AI-specific provisions should appear in professional services mandate letters — provisions on AI tool authority, AI output review, client communication about AI involvement, and AI-related liability allocation. This is a present-tense gap: firms are delivering AI-assisted work product under mandate letters silent on AI.

**Geneva diplomatic community as informal standard-setting environment.** The mechanism by which the Geneva diplomatic and international organization community generates de facto AI governance standards through informal cross-border comparison has not been named or analyzed. Firms with international clients are subject to expectations formed in this environment without any visibility into how those expectations formed or what they require.

## 04 — Compliance/Editorial Context: Swiss Regulatory Developments, Professional Obligation Shifts, Editorial Timing

### Current Regulatory Landscape

**nFADP Art.21 operational specificity increasing.** The FDPIC has made the nFADP Art.21 reconstruction obligation increasingly operationally specific through enforcement pattern evidence. The obligation is not merely that documentation exists — it is that the documentation chain allows reconstruction of what the automated system did, why, and with what data. For firms deploying AI tools without documentation architecture from 2023-2024 onward, this is a present-tense exposure. The FDPIC's enforcement pattern is converging toward systematic examination of AI-assisted professional decisions, not just complaint-driven investigation.

**FINMA model risk governance expectations formalizing.** FINMA has been building its model risk governance expectations for supervised institutions through supervisory communication rather than formal circular. The expectations address: model inventory with version tracking, documented validation methodology, model performance monitoring, and senior review adequacy. For Geneva wealth management boutiques supervised by FINMA, these expectations are becoming examination standards even without formal publication — which means boutique firms are discovering their model governance gaps only in examination context.

**EU AI Act Annex VIII high-risk documentation requirements operational for EU client relationships.** The EU AI Act's high-risk system documentation requirements (Annex VIII) are creating present-tense obligations for Swiss firms serving EU clients — not through Swiss regulatory enforcement but through EU client contractual due diligence. The documentation requirements cover: technical documentation of the AI system, post-market monitoring plans, incident reporting protocols, and conformity assessment evidence. Swiss firms receiving EU client questionnaires are discovering they have no documentation architecture capable of responding.

**Federal Council AI strategy deliberation — June 2026.** The Federal Council is expected to deliberate on Swiss AI governance direction in mid-2026. This will set institutional frames but will not resolve the specific documentation adequacy question for Geneva boutique firms. The regulatory record will lag the market-formed standard by twelve to eighteen months minimum after the deliberation.

### Professional Obligation Shifts

**Mandate letter silence creating implicit AI authorization.** The professional services mandate letter in Geneva practice has historically not addressed AI tool use, output review standards, or AI-related authority allocation. The consequence is that firms have been delivering AI-assisted work product under implicit authorization — the client has not agreed to AI-assisted delivery, but neither has the engagement contract excluded it. This creates a specific accountability gap: when something goes wrong, the firm has no documented basis for what was authorized and what was not. The gap is actionable today: firms can update their mandate letter templates.

**Carrier renewal questionnaire now testing market adequacy, not just policy existence.** The 2026 carrier renewal questionnaire has moved from testing whether firms have AI governance policies to testing whether those policies produce evidence of substantive review. The third-generation questions probe: version tracking records, reassessment trigger documentation, client communication records for AI-assisted decisions, and output review evidence. A firm that can demonstrate a policy but cannot produce the evidence the policy is supposed to generate is now visibly inadequate under the carrier's standard.

**Client accountability chain questions arriving before firms have prepared answers.** Sophisticated clients — family offices, institutional investors, international organizations — are asking Geneva advisors about the accountability chain for their work: who reviewed the AI output, what model version was used, what infrastructure does the work depend on. These questions are arriving at the client relationship level before firms have prepared answers, and the answers firms are improvising are not producing consistent or defensible positions.

## 05 — Editorial Content Draft: Specific Brotons.net Article Proposal

### EN — The Article Proposal

**Title:** The Accountability Chain Nobody Mapped: What Geneva Firms Owe Their Clients When the Infrastructure Runs on AI

**Angle:** Geneva professional services firms are operating inside accountability chains that extend well beyond their own AI tool deployments. Swiss financial infrastructure — SIX, SwissRepoNet, SIC — runs AI systems that process, execute, and clear client work product. Boutique firms are accountable to clients for outcomes that flow through infrastructure they did not choose, cannot fully evaluate, and have never documented. This piece names the infrastructure accountability chain as a distinct exposure category, maps where it sits in the Geneva professional services context, and proposes what adequate infrastructure accountability documentation looks like for a boutique firm that cannot control the infrastructure it depends on.

**Structure:**

*Section 1 — What the Accountability Chain Looks Like*

A Geneva wealth management boutique makes an investment recommendation. The recommendation is executed through SIX's trading infrastructure, which uses AI-driven order routing and execution algorithms. The position is cleared through SIC, which operates AI-assisted matching. The collateral management runs through SwissRepoNet, which uses AI for intraday liquidity optimization. The client sees the outcome — the position, the return — and attributes it to the boutique's recommendation. The boutique, in the event of a problem, is accountable for the recommendation. But the execution, clearing, and collateral management — all AI-driven — sit inside infrastructure accountability chains the boutique has not mapped.

*Section 2 — Why This Is a Different Kind of Accountability Problem*

The standard AI governance discussion for Geneva boutiques focuses on the firm's own AI tool deployments: which tools, which versions, which outputs, which review. The infrastructure accountability problem is different in kind: the AI systems in question are not the firm's tools. The firm did not select them, cannot reconfigure them, and has no documentation of their operating parameters. The firm's accountability is for what it knew or should have known about the infrastructure — which is, for most boutiques, essentially nothing, because nobody has told them they need to know anything.

*Section 3 — What the Mandate Letter Should Say About Infrastructure*

The professional services mandate letter — the document governing the client relationship — is the appropriate place to establish what the firm does and does not account for with respect to infrastructure AI. The proposed AI provisions for the mandate letter include: (1) a specification of the infrastructure through which client work product will be processed and the firm's role in selecting and monitoring that infrastructure; (2) a description of what the firm has done to satisfy itself that the infrastructure is adequate for the client's purposes; (3) a description of what the firm will do if infrastructure AI produces an outcome that requires investigation or correction; (4) a specification of what the firm does and does not warrant with respect to AI-driven infrastructure outcomes.

*Section 4 — The Documentation That Would Demonstrate Adequate Infrastructure Accountability*

For a boutique firm that cannot control Swiss financial infrastructure AI, adequate documentation of infrastructure accountability consists of: (1) a record of which infrastructure systems the firm's client work product flows through — obtained through the firm's own operational records, not through infrastructure operator disclosure; (2) a record of what due diligence the firm has conducted on infrastructure AI adequacy — which, for most boutiques, will be limited to understanding the infrastructure operator's public disclosures about their AI systems; (3) a record of what the firm has communicated to clients about infrastructure AI dependencies and what the client has agreed to by proceeding with the engagement.

*Section 5 — Why This Is Not Optional*

The accountability chain question is arriving through client relationships now. Sophisticated clients — family offices, institutional investors, international organizations — are beginning to ask Geneva advisors about infrastructure AI accountability. Firms that have not thought through this question will improvise answers that are inconsistent, inadequately documented, and potentially expose the firm to liability for infrastructure outcomes it did not control. The mandate letter is the appropriate place to establish the scope of the firm's infrastructure accountability — before the question arises in a client meeting or a dispute.

**Intended Reader Outcome:** A Geneva managing partner or senior professional reads this article and realizes that the accountability chain for their work extends through infrastructure they have not mapped. They understand that this is a distinct exposure category with a specific documentation response: infrastructure provisions in the mandate letter, a record of infrastructure due diligence, and a record of client communication about infrastructure AI dependencies. The article gives them a framework for thinking about this problem and specific steps for addressing it.

### FR — La Proposition Éditoriale

**Titre :** La Chaîne de Responsabilité Que Personne N'A Cartographiée : Ce Que Les Cabinets Genevois Doivent à Leurs Clients Quand L'Infrastructure Fonctionne à L'IA

**Angle :** Les cabinets de services professionnels genevois opèrent à l'intérieur de chaînes de responsabilité qui s'étendent bien au-delà de leurs propres déploiements d'outils d'IA. L'infrastructure financière suisse — SIX, SwissRepoNet, SIC — fait fonctionner des systèmes d'IA qui traitent, exécutent et compensent les produits du travail des clients. Les petits cabinets sont responsables devant leurs clients pour des résultats qui circulent à travers une infrastructure qu'ils n'ont pas choisie, qu'ils ne peuvent pas pleinement évaluer, et qu'ils n'ont jamais documentée. Cet article nomme la chaîne de responsabilité de l'infrastructure comme une catégorie d'exposition distincte, cartographie où elle se situe dans le contexte des services professionnels genevois, et propose ce à quoi ressemble une documentation adéquate de la responsabilité de l'infrastructure pour un petit cabinet qui ne peut pas contrôler l'infrastructure dont il dépend.

**Structure :**

*Section 1 — À Quoi Ressemble la Chaîne de Responsabilité*

Un cabinet genevois de gestion de patrimoine fait une recommandation d'investissement. La recommandation est exécutée à travers l'infrastructure de négociation de SIX, qui utilise des algorithmes de routage et d'exécution d'ordres basés sur l'IA. La position est compensée par SIC, qui exploite la correspondance assistée par l'IA. La gestion des garanties passe par SwissRepoNet, qui utilise l'IA pour l'optimisation de la liquidité intrajournalière. Le client voit le résultat — la position, le rendement — et l'attribue à la recommandation du cabinet. Le cabinet, en cas de problème, est responsable de la recommandation. Mais l'exécution, la compensation et la gestion des garanties — toutes assistées par l'IA — se trouvent à l'intérieur de chaînes de responsabilité de l'infrastructure que le cabinet n'a pas cartographiées.

*Section 2 — Pourquoi C'est Un Type Différent de Problème de Responsabilité*

La discussion standard sur la gouvernance de l'IA pour les petits cabinets genevois se concentre sur les propres déploiements d'outils d'IA du cabinet : quels outils, quelles versions, quelles sorties, quelle révision. Le problème de responsabilité de l'infrastructure est différent par sa nature : les systèmes d'IA en question ne sont pas les outils du cabinet. Le cabinet ne les a pas sélectionnés, ne peut pas les reconfigurer et n'a aucune documentation sur leurs paramètres de fonctionnement. La responsabilité du cabinet est pour ce qu'il savait ou aurait dû savoir sur l'infrastructure — ce qui, pour la plupart des petits cabinets, est essentiellement rien, parce que personne ne leur a dit qu'ils doivent savoir quelque chose.

*Section 3 — Ce Que la Lettre de Mandat Devrait Dire Sur L'Infrastructure*

La lettre de mandat des services professionnels — le document gouvernant la relation client — est l'endroit approprié pour établir ce que le cabinet fait et ne fait pas en matière de responsabilité de l'infrastructure d'IA. Les dispositions d'IA proposées pour la lettre de mandat comprennent : (1) une spécification de l'infrastructure à travers laquelle les produits du travail des clients seront traités et le rôle du cabinet dans la sélection et la surveillance de cette infrastructure ; (2) une description de ce que le cabinet a fait pour se satisfaire que l'infrastructure est adéquate aux fins du client ; (3) une description de ce que le cabinet fera si l'infrastructure d'IA produit un résultat nécessitant une enquête ou une correction ; (4) une spécification de ce que le cabinet garantit et ne garantit pas concernant les résultats de l'infrastructure d'IA.

*Section 4 — La Documentation Qui Démontrerait Une Responsabilité d'Infrastructure Adéquate*

Pour un cabinet de taille boutique qui ne peut pas contrôler l'infrastructure d'IA financière suisse, une documentation adéquate de la responsabilité de l'infrastructure consiste en : (1) un enregistrement des systèmes d'infrastructure through which les produits du travail des clients circulent — obtenu through les propres dossiers opérationnels du cabinet, pas through la divulgation de l'opérateur d'infrastructure ; (2) un enregistrement de la due diligence que le cabinet a menée sur l'adéquation de l'infrastructure d'IA — ce qui, pour la plupart des petits cabinets, sera limité à comprendre les divulgations publiques de l'opérateur d'infrastructure sur ses systèmes d'IA ; (3) un enregistrement de ce que le cabinet a communiqué aux clients sur les dépendances à l'infrastructure d'IA et ce que le client a accepté en procédant à l'engagement.

*Section 5 — Pourquoi Ce N'Est Pas Optionnel*

La question de la chaîne de responsabilité arrive à travers les relations clients maintenant. Des clients sophistiqués — family offices, investisseurs institutionnels, organisations internationales — commencent à poser aux conseillers genevois des questions sur la responsabilité de l'infrastructure d'IA. Les cabinets qui n'ont pas réfléchi à cette question improviseront des réponses qui sont incohérentes, insuffisamment documentées, et potentiellement exposent le cabinet à des responsabilité pour des résultats d'infrastructure qu'il ne contrôlait pas. La lettre de mandat est l'endroit approprié pour établir la portée de la responsabilité de l'infrastructure du cabinet — avant que la question ne surgisse lors d'une réunion client ou d'un différend.

**Résultat lecteur prévu :** Un associé gérant ou professionnel senior genevois lit cet article et réalise que la chaîne de responsabilité pour leur travail s'étend à travers une infrastructure qu'il n'a pas cartographiée. Ils comprennent que c'est une catégorie d'exposition distincte avec une réponse documentaire spécifique : des dispositions sur l'infrastructure dans la lettre de mandat, un dossier de due diligence sur l'infrastructure, et un dossier de communication client sur les dépendances à l'infrastructure d'IA. L'article leur donne un cadre pour réfléchir à ce problème et des étapes spécifiques pour y répondre.

### Finding 1: The Cross-Framework Documentation Overlap Problem Is a Discrete Unnamed Category

nFADP Art.21, FINMA model risk governance, and EU AI Act Annex VIII each impose distinct documentation requirements on Geneva firms operating across multiple regulatory environments. nFADP requires an explanation trail for automated decisions. FINMA expects model inventory, version tracking, validation documentation, and senior review evidence. EU AI Act Annex VIII requires technical documentation for high-risk systems, post-market monitoring plans, and conformity assessment evidence. These are not three separate documentation exercises — they are three documentation requirements imposed on the same firm for the same AI-assisted work product. The secondary problem: documentation built to satisfy one framework may not satisfy the others. A FINMA-compliant model governance record does not automatically constitute an nFADP Art.21 explanation trail, because the explanation trail must describe the specific decision made for the specific client, not just the model's general operating parameters. And neither necessarily constitutes EU AI Act Annex VIII technical documentation. No Geneva publisher has mapped the cross-framework documentation overlap — what documentation simultaneously serves multiple frameworks, what is framework-specific, and what a coherent cross-framework documentation architecture requires. This is a present-tense documentation architecture problem with no published resolution.

**Editorial implication:** An article mapping the cross-framework documentation overlap between nFADP Art.21, FINMA model risk governance, and EU AI Act Annex VIII — with a specific documentation architecture that serves all three frameworks simultaneously — fills a present-tense gap that no Geneva publisher is addressing.

### Finding 6: The Vocabulary Gap Between Internal "Adequate" and Market "Adequate" Has Not Been Named or Mapped

When a Geneva firm says its AI governance is "adequate," that word means something different from what it means when a sophisticated client uses the word, when a carrier underwriter uses the word, or when a counterparty uses the word. The firm's internal standard for "adequate" is formed by its own resources — what it has built, what it believes is sufficient, what its carrier's prior questionnaires did not challenge. The market's external standard for "adequate" is formed by what sophisticated clients have encountered in comparable firms, what carriers have learned through three generations of underwriting, what counterparties have determined through their own AI governance standards, and what the diplomatic community has converged on through informal cross-border comparison. These two standards are not the same. A firm can be fully "adequate" by its own internal measure and fully inadequate by the market's external measure — and the firm typically discovers this gap only at the moment of carrier renewal, client questionnaire, or supervisory examination. The specific vocabulary distinguishing what "adequate" means in these different contexts — which client matters trigger elevated adequacy standards, what the carrier is testing for in third-generation questions, what counterparty AI governance questionnaires are probing — has not been published in the Geneva professional information space. This is the primary reason firms discover their governance is inadequate at the examination moment rather than before it.

**Editorial implication:** Producing the specific vocabulary that distinguishes internal "adequate" from market "adequate" — a practical language guide for Geneva professionals navigating the gap between what they have built and what the market expects — is a sustained editorial opportunity.
