## 02 — Market Signals: What's Happening in the Swiss AI Governance Information Space

**What is resonating right now:**

The carrier underwriting signal is emerging as the clearest real-time map of the Geneva AI governance standard. When Swiss professional liability carriers began asking specific AI governance questions in underwriting questionnaires, they created a market-formed standard — not a regulatory standard, but a standard calibrated by what the carriers have determined to be the indicators of acceptable risk. What is now becoming visible is that the carriers are not asking the same questions they were asking twelve months ago. The questionnaire questions are becoming more specific, more technically grounded, and more focused on documentation substance rather than documentation existence. A firm that built its AI governance documentation to answer the carrier questions of 2024–2025 is finding that the 2026 carrier questionnaire is asking different questions — and that the documentation gap that seemed acceptable when the first questionnaire was answered is no longer acceptable when the second questionnaire probes the same territory at greater depth. The carrier questionnaire is not just a risk assessment tool; it is a standard-formation mechanism operating in real time.

The counterparty AI governance questionnaire is arriving in Geneva professional firms through a pattern that is becoming structurally predictable: a counterparty — often a larger financial institution, an international corporate, or a firm with EU exposure — sends an AI governance questionnaire as part of due diligence for a new engagement or a contract renewal. The questions are calibrated to what the counterparty's legal or compliance function has determined to be the indicators of responsible AI practice in a service provider. The Geneva boutique receiving the questionnaire discovers that their AI governance documentation, which felt adequate when it was built, cannot answer the counterparty's questions in any specific detail. The gap is not about having been negligent about AI governance — it is that counterparty questionnaires are forming a de facto standard that is more technically specific and more documentation-substantive than what most Geneva boutiques have built. The counterparty questionnaire is the second market mechanism creating real-time standard formation.

The AI vendor sustainability question is surfacing with a new urgency: the question is not whether an AI vendor might discontinue a product (this has always been a background risk) but what happens to client matter documentation and AI-assisted work product when a specific tool's model is updated in a way that changes its output characteristics. The governance documentation a firm built for "ChatGPT for client memo drafting" in 2024 is not describing the tool that same tool runs in 2026 — and the firm has no systematic process for detecting when the tool it is using is no longer the tool it documented. The model version tracking gap is a specific documentation exposure that is not being addressed in any standard AI governance framework.

**What is missing:**

Content that reads the carrier underwriting questionnaire as a real-time standard-formation mechanism and that provides the methodology for understanding what questions the next questionnaire will ask before it arrives — not by predicting the carrier's questions, but by understanding the pattern of what carriers have determined to be the indicators of acceptable AI governance risk and what that pattern suggests about where the standard is going.

Content that addresses the counterparty questionnaire as a specific professional moment with specific documentation requirements — what the questionnaire is actually probing, why the documentation built for internal governance purposes cannot answer it without reassessment, and what a methodology for preparing counterparty-questionnaire-ready AI governance documentation looks like.

Content that addresses the model version tracking gap as a specific documentation exposure — what it means to govern an AI tool that is changing silently (through model updates that the vendor does not explicitly announce or that the firm does not systematically track), and what a documentation maintenance process looks like that can detect and respond to tool-level changes before they create accountability gaps in professional moments.

## 04 — Compliance/Editorial Context: Swiss Regulatory Developments, Professional Obligation Shifts, and Editorial Timing

**Current regulatory landscape (late April 2026):**

The Federal Council's three-track AI policy framework continues to define the structural frame — AI Convention ratification, sector-specific regulation, non-binding measures. The anticipation of the AI Convention integration consultation draft by end of 2026 creates a specific editorial timing consideration: the period before the consultation draft arrives is the period when practitioners are most aware of the gap between what they have built and what the regulatory frame is becoming. This is the pre-publication window when the editorial value of anticipatory guidance is highest — and when content that helps practitioners understand where the standard is going before it arrives is most valuable.

The FDPIC's enforcement record under nFADP Articles 12, 21, and 22 continues to operate as the clearest signal of what substantive compliance looks like. What the enforcement record is increasingly establishing is that documentation substance — the specific processing purpose, the data minimisation applied, the lawful basis in context — is what distinguishes adequate documentation from documentation that captures the fact of compliance without capturing its substance. The AI governance documentation built in 2023–2024 was often designed to capture the fact that a governance framework existed and that AI use was disclosed. The FDPIC is now looking at whether the documentation can support specific answers to specific questions about what processing occurred, on what basis, with what safeguards applied. The gap between fact-capture and substance-capture is the documentation quality distinction that is becoming more consequential.

**Professional obligation shifts:**

The Geneva professional community's informal tier structure is beginning to have real commercial consequences that are not formally measured but are becoming more visible as the standard rises. The firms that can answer carrier and counterparty AI governance questions with specific, current, substantively grounded documentation are being distinguished from firms that have governance documentation that was adequate when it was built but has since drifted from practice. The distinction is forming through market mechanisms — carrier questionnaires, counterparty probes, sophisticated client questions — rather than through formal certification or published rankings. This means the standard is rising through the quality of the questions that are being asked, not through the publication of a threshold.

The Geneva cantonal bar continuing education requirements for AI competence continue to operate at the awareness level. The CE credit market continues to incentivise exposure over capability. The gap between CE-certified AI literacy and the operational AI governance knowledge required to practise responsibly in 2026 is not being addressed by the professional development system — and the practitioners who need operational guidance are not finding it through the channels the professional development system provides.

**Editorial timing assessment:**

The carrier underwriting questionnaire evolution is the defining editorial intelligence of this moment. The questions carriers are asking in mid-2026 are different from the questions they were asking in 2024–2025, and the trajectory is toward greater technical specificity, greater documentation substance requirements, and a more detailed probe of the firm's actual AI practice in client matters. The editorial opportunity is to read this trajectory and to provide the methodology for understanding what the next carrier questionnaire will ask before it arrives — by understanding the pattern of what carriers have determined to be the indicators of acceptable risk and what that pattern reveals about where the standard is going.

The counterparty questionnaire arrival as a specific professional moment is the second editorial anchor. When a counterparty AI governance questionnaire arrives with an engagement letter or a contract renewal, it is a specific accountability moment — the firm is being asked to demonstrate its AI governance practice in a context that is different from internal governance review. The editorial opportunity is to address the counterparty questionnaire as a distinct genre with distinct documentation requirements, and to provide the methodology for preparing for that moment before it arrives.

The model version tracking gap is the third editorial anchor — it is a specific documentation exposure that is not addressed in any standard framework and that is creating silent accountability gaps in professional firms that do not have a systematic process for tracking when the AI tool they are governing has changed. The editorial opportunity is to address this exposure before it becomes an incident.

**Structure:**

*Opening:* The scenario. A Geneva managing partner receives their annual professional liability renewal questionnaire and notices that the AI governance section has expanded significantly from the previous year. The questions are more specific — they probe not just whether the firm has an AI governance policy, but how the policy was built, when it was last reviewed, what specific AI tools are in use, how the firm tracks model version changes, and what the firm's process is for reassessing governance documentation when the underlying tool changes. *Section 1 — Why the carrier underwriting questionnaire is a standard-formation mechanism, not just a risk assessment tool*
The carrier's AI governance questionnaire is often read as a compliance obligation — something to be satisfied so that the renewal proceeds without friction. This reading misses what the questionnaire is actually doing. The carrier is using the questionnaire to determine whether the firm they are insuring represents an acceptable AI governance risk — and to make that determination, they have had to develop their own view of what adequate AI governance looks like. That view is reflected in the questions they ask. And because the carrier is seeing a broad portfolio of Geneva professional firms, the pattern of questions they ask is a more comprehensive and more practically grounded map of the standard than any individual firm's governance documentation or any published regulatory framework. The carrier's questions are forming the standard in real time, through the accumulation of underwriting decisions — and the firm that can read those questions as a signal is better positioned than the firm that only reads them as a compliance obligation.

*Section 2 — What the questionnaire evolution reveals about where the standard is going*
The first-generation carrier AI governance questionnaire (2024) asked about the existence of AI governance policy, the disclosure of AI use to clients, and the presence of a review process for AI-assisted work product. These questions established that the firm had considered AI governance and had documented something. The second-generation questionnaire (2025) added questions about specific AI tools in use, the basis for tool selection, and the documentation of the review process in client files. These questions established that the firm's governance was grounded in specific practice, not just general policy. The third-generation questionnaire (mid-2026) is beginning to ask about model version tracking, the process for reassessing governance documentation when a tool is updated, the specific safeguards applied to different use cases, and the firm's process for detecting when the tool they are using has changed materially. These questions establish that the carrier is now looking for evidence of a maintenance methodology — not just a governance framework, but a process for keeping the framework's documentation current as the AI tool ecosystem changes. The trajectory from existence to groundedness to maintenance methodology is the map of where the standard is going.

*Section 3 — The five documentation requirements the carrier is beginning to probe that most Geneva firms cannot answer*
The first requirement is tool-specificity: the carrier wants to know not just that the firm uses AI tools, but which specific tools, in which configurations, for which client matter types. Generic disclosure ("we use AI tools in client matters") is no longer sufficient. The second requirement is model version tracking: the carrier wants to know whether the firm has a process for tracking when the AI tool they are using has been updated to a new model version, and whether that tracking triggers a governance documentation reassessment. Most firms have no systematic process for this. The third requirement is use-case differentiation: the carrier wants to know whether the firm's review standard varies by use case — whether the appropriate human review for AI-assisted contract drafting is different from the appropriate human review for AI-assisted research, and whether the governance documentation reflects that differentiation. Most firms have one policy document for all use cases. The fourth requirement is FADP audit trail substance: the carrier wants to know not just that the firm has an audit trail, but whether the audit trail can support specific answers to specific questions about what processing occurred, on what lawful basis, with what data minimisation applied, for what specific purpose in the specific client matter. The fifth requirement is the reassessment protocol: the carrier wants to know whether the firm has a process for initiating a governance documentation review when a specific trigger event occurs — a model update, a new use case, a client question the documentation cannot answer, a counterparty probe — rather than waiting for a calendar-based review cycle. Most firms have calendar-based review, not trigger-based reassessment.

*Section 4 — The methodology for reading the next carrier questionnaire before it arrives*
The carrier's questions do not arrive without pattern. The pattern of what carriers ask is determined by what they have determined to be the indicators of acceptable risk in the preceding underwriting cycle. To read the next questionnaire before it arrives, a Geneva firm needs to understand what the carrier saw in the current cycle that caused them to ask new or more specific questions. The firm that has built a relationship with their broker and that reviews the carrier's underwriting guidance (even in its general form) can detect the direction of the carrier's questions before the next questionnaire arrives. The methodology is: monitor the carrier's underwriting guidance for any changes in framing or specificity; track what the carrier asked in the most recent questionnaire and what follow-up questions they asked when the responses were reviewed; assess which of the five documentation requirements the current questionnaire addressed and which it did not, and assume the gaps will be filled in the next cycle; and maintain a governance documentation readiness assessment that specifically evaluates whether the firm can answer each of the five requirements in detail before the next questionnaire arrives. This is not about predicting the carrier's questions — it is about understanding the pattern that produces them.

*Section 5 — The counterparty questionnaire as a second market mechanism operating on the same trajectory*
The counterparty AI governance questionnaire — arriving with new engagements and contract renewals from international counterparties and EU-exposed clients — is operating on the same trajectory as the carrier questionnaire. The questions are calibrated by what the counterparty's legal or compliance function has determined to be the indicators of responsible AI practice in a service provider, and that determination is being refined with every questionnaire cycle. The firm that can read the carrier's questions as a map can often read the counterparty's questions through the same lens — the two mechanisms are looking for the same underlying indicators, from slightly different angles. The counterparty questionnaire adds a specific dimension that the carrier questionnaire does not have: the counterparty is asking about AI practice in their specific matter, not just in the abstract. The counterparty questionnaire is probing whether the firm's AI governance documentation reflects what the firm is actually doing in the counterparty's engagement — and the documentation that was built for general internal governance purposes may not answer those matter-specific questions without reassessment. The methodology for preparing for the counterparty questionnaire is the same as for the carrier questionnaire: maintain a governance documentation readiness assessment that specifically evaluates whether the firm can answer in detail, before the questionnaire arrives.

*Section 6 — The competitive consequence: why the firms that can read the signal are separating from the firms that cannot*
The firms that can read the carrier underwriting questionnaire as a map — that understand the pattern of what the carrier is looking for, that maintain their governance documentation to the level of specificity the carrier's questions are reaching, and that have a reassessment protocol in place to keep that documentation current — are beginning to be distinguished from firms that have governance documentation that was adequate for earlier questionnaire cycles but is now being found insufficient. The distinction is not visible from the outside — both firms have AI governance documentation, both firms responded to the carrier's questions in previous cycles. The distinction is visible in the quality of the answers the firm can give when the carrier probes at greater depth. The firm with current, substantively grounded documentation has answers. The firm with documentation that was accurate when it was produced but has since drifted does not — and the gap between the two is becoming more commercially consequential as the standard rises.
