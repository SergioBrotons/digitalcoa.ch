## 02 — Market Signals

### What is happening in the Swiss AI governance information space

**The Federal Council June 2026 deliberation is producing anticipatory paralysis, not anticipatory action.**
Geneva professional services firms are aware of the timeline but are reading it as a reason to wait rather than a reason to act. The informal circuit reports that firms are deferring AI governance documentation decisions until after the Federal Council announces its direction. This creates a compounding exposure window: the deliberation will clarify direction but will not retroactively construct documentation that does not exist for the period between initial AI adoption and the decision date.

**Carrier AI questionnaires have moved from generic to specific.**
The third-generation carrier questionnaire now probes five specific documentation categories with evidence requirements that Geneva firms cannot currently meet: tool version tracking records, reassessment trigger protocols, client communication records with AI methodology references, AI output review evidence, and configuration change logs. The questionnaire is operating as a market-standard formation mechanism: what the carrier asks for is becoming what "adequate" means in the Geneva professional services market, regardless of what any published standard says.

**FDPIC enforcement patterns are becoming readable.**
The FDPIC's investigation pattern — financial services firms first, professional services under current mandate scope, data minimization as the primary enforcement angle — is being read by sophisticated Geneva practitioners as actionable intelligence about where enforcement attention will concentrate. This creates a two-tier market: firms with Geneva informal circuit access are positioning defensively based on pattern reading, while firms without circuit access are waiting for published guidance that will arrive after enforcement has already begun.

**EU client questionnaires are arriving at Swiss firms independently of Swiss regulatory status.**
The EU AI Act's high-risk provisions are creating documentation obligations for Swiss firms serving EU clients, regardless of Swiss domestic regulatory status. Geneva professional services firms are encountering EU client questionnaires that impose obligations the firms did not know they had and cannot currently discharge. The obligation is real, the guidance is absent, and the Swiss domestic framework has not addressed the intersection.

**Geneva informal circuit is operating as the primary standard-formation environment.**
The informal circuit — carrier underwriting conversations, peer dinners, bar association sessions, counterparty questionnaire exchanges — is where the actual standard of care is forming. Formal published guidance from Swiss authorities is arriving 3–6 months after the circuit has already established working norms. The circuit handles what-is-happening; it structurally cannot handle what-cannot-be-explained, which is the actual exposure surface Geneva firms face.

### What is resonating

- Personal liability language: executives respond to personal exposure framing, not organizational compliance framing
- The reconstruction question: "can you show what happened on this specific matter" as the operative accountability test
- Carrier renewal as forcing function: predictable, dateable, requiring documented position

- EU client questionnaire as independent obligation mechanism
- The documentation archaeology problem: reconstructing what you didn't document at the time

### What is missing

- No Geneva publisher is naming what the carrier questionnaire's evidence requirements actually mean operationally
- No analytical distinction between documentation that satisfies internal adequacy standards and documentation that would protect in a dispute or examination
- No practical guidance on the specific documentation categories the carrier's third-generation questionnaire probes
- No analysis of the Geneva-specific accountability chain components that Swiss professional services firms need to build
- No editorial coverage of the anticipatory paralysis problem: firms waiting for the deliberation that will not reconstruct their past

**Title:** The Geneva Accountability Gap: What You're Actually Being Asked to Show

**Angle:**
Most Geneva professional services firms have a working AI governance approach. Very few have a documentation record that would survive a specific accountability question about a specific matter. This is not a framework adoption problem. It is an accountability reconstruction problem — and it is the question the carrier's third-generation questionnaire is now asking for evidence of, across five specific documentation categories, before any Geneva regulatory standard has defined what adequate means.

The article argues that Geneva firms are managing a documentation accountability gap that accumulated between 2023 and 2026, during a period when AI tools were adopted before governance documentation protocols existed. The Federal Council's June 2026 deliberation will set direction for the future. It will not reconstruct the past. The carrier's questionnaire is asking about the past now. This gap — between what firms know they should have built and what they have built — is the specific problem this article addresses.

**Structure:**

**Part 1 — What the Questionnaire Is Actually Asking For**
The third-generation carrier AI questionnaire probes five specific documentation categories: tool version tracking records, reassessment trigger protocols, client communication records referencing AI methodology, AI output review evidence, and configuration change logs. These are not abstract governance categories. They are specific records about specific decisions. The article explains what each category requires operationally and why most Geneva firms cannot currently produce evidence in each category.

**Part 2 — Why the Standard Form Has No Safe Harbor**
The Geneva informal circuit is where the actual standard of care is forming. Carrier underwriting conversations, peer exchanges, bar sessions, and counterparty questionnaire responses have established working norms 3–6 months ahead of any published regulatory standard. The article explains why waiting for published guidance is not a protective strategy — it is a deferral that compounds exposure while the formal standard catches up to what the circuit already knows.

**Part 3 — The Documentation Archaeology Problem**
The period from 2023 to mid-2026 has a specific structural property: AI tools were adopted before documentation protocols existed. The documentation that did not exist during that period cannot be retroactively constructed in the way that future documentation can be. The article defines what bounded reconstruction is possible for that period and what remains as genuine gap — and why that distinction matters for how firms describe their current position.

**Part 4 — What Adequate Accountability Documentation Actually Requires**
The article defines the four components of a Geneva professional services accountability documentation system: tool documentation (what AI tools were used, in what configuration, during what period), decision attribution records (which specific recommendation or output was produced with AI contribution, on what client matter, with what human review), client communication records (what clients were told about AI methodology, in what form, at what point in the engagement), and FADP audit trail substance (the substantive content of the nFADP Art. 21 reconstruction obligation, not just compliance form).

**Intended reader outcome:**
A Geneva managing partner, principal, or compliance officer finishes this article understanding what the carrier questionnaire's five evidence categories mean operationally, why the Geneva informal circuit defines the operative standard ahead of formal guidance, what documentation archaeology can and cannot accomplish for the 2023–2026 period, and what the four-component accountability documentation system actually requires them to build.

**Angle:**

Très peu ont un dossier de documentation qui survivrait à une question de reddition de comptes spécifique sur un dossier spécifique. Ce n'est pas un problème d'adoption de cadre. C'est un problème de reconstruction de la reddition de comptes — et c'est la question que le questionnaire de troisième génération de l'assureur demande maintenant des preuves, dans cinq catégories de documentation spécifiques, avant que toute norme réglementaire genevoise n'ait défini ce que signifie « adéquat ».

L'article soutient que les cabinets genevois gèrent un retard de documentation de reddition de comptes qui s'est accumulé entre 2023 et 2026, pendant une période où les outils d'IA ont été adoptés avant l'existence de protocoles de documentation de gouvernance. La délibération du Conseil fédéral en juin 2026 fixera la direction pour l'avenir. Elle ne reconstruira pas le passé. Le questionnaire de l'assureur demande sur le passé maintenant. Ce retard — entre ce que les cabinets savent qu'ils auraient dû construire et ce qu'ils ont réellement construit — est le problème spécifique que cet article aborde.

**Structure:**

**Première partie — Ce que le questionnaire demande réellement**
Le questionnaire d'IA de troisième génération de l'assureur examine cinq catégories spécifiques de documentation : les enregistrements de suivi des versions d'outils, les protocoles de déclenchement de réévaluation, les enregistrements de communication client référençant la méthodologie d'IA, les preuves de révision des productions d'IA, et les journaux de modification de configuration. Ce ne sont pas des catégories de gouvernance abstraites. Ce sont des enregistrements spécifiques sur des décisions spécifiques. L'article explique ce que chaque catégorie exige opérationnellement et pourquoi la plupart des cabinets genevois ne peuvent actuellement pas produire de preuves dans chaque catégorie.

**Deuxième partie — Pourquoi la norme formelle n'offre aucun refuge sûr**
Le circuit informel genevois est l'endroit où la norme de soin réelle se forme. Les conversations de souscription d'assureur, les échanges entre pairs, les sessions du barreau et les réponses aux questionnaires des contreparties ont établi des normes opérationnelles 3 à 6 mois avant toute norme réglementaire publiée. L'article explique pourquoi attendre la publication de directives n'est pas une stratégie protectrice — c'est un report qui aggrave l'exposition pendant que la norme formelle rattrape ce que le circuit sait déjà.

**Troisième partie — Le problème de l'archéologie de la documentation**
La période de 2023 à mi-2026 a une propriété structurelle spécifique : les outils d'IA ont été adoptés avant l'existence de protocoles de documentation. La documentation qui n'existait pas pendant cette période ne peut pas être construite de manière aléatoire de la façon dont une documentation future peut l'être. L'article définit quelle reconstruction limitée est possible pour cette période et ce qui reste comme un véritable retard — et pourquoi cette distinction est importante pour la façon dont les cabinets décrivent leur position actuelle.

**Quatrième partie — Ce que nécessite réellement une documentation de reddition de comptes adéquate**
L'article définit les quatre composantes d'un système de documentation de reddition de comptes pour les services professionnels genevois : documentation des outils (quels outils d'IA ont été utilisés, dans quelle configuration, pendant quelle période), enregistrements d'attribution des décisions (quelle recommandation ou production spécifique a été produite avec une contribution d'IA, sur quel dossier client, avec quelle révision humaine), enregistrements de communication client (ce que les clients ont été informés sur la méthodologie d'IA, sous quelle forme, à quel moment du mandat), et substance de la piste d'audit nFADP (le contenu substantiel de l'obligation de reconstruction de l'Art. 21 nFADP, pas seulement la forme de conformité).

It is a period boundary problem with specific remediation architecture. What can be reconstructed from that period (bounded by what evidence exists in client files, engagement records, and communication logs) is distinct from what cannot be reconstructed (the specific reasoning steps and AI configuration states that were never recorded). Naming documentation archaeology as a specific problem — with specific boundaries and specific residual gaps — gives Geneva practitioners a more precise frame than "documentation gap" for describing their current position.

The actual accountability exposure Geneva firms face is the problem of producing documentation for AI-assisted decisions that were made without documentation architecture. The informal circuit can share that this is a problem; it cannot resolve the documentation problem for firms that need to reconstruct what was never recorded. This structural limitation of the informal circuit as a standard-setting environment is the specific gap that Brotons.net's editorial translation function is positioned to fill.

Swiss firms serving EU clients are encountering EU AI Act high-risk documentation obligations as client-imposed requirements, independently of Swiss domestic regulatory status. The Swiss nFADP documentation framework is not equivalent to EU AI Act post-market monitoring documentation requirements — they address different obligations using different frameworks. No Geneva publisher has addressed the specific documentation problem created by this intersection: what Swiss firms owe EU clients under EU AI Act high-risk provisions, and how that obligation relates (or does not relate) to Swiss nFADP obligations. This is distinct from prior coverage of EU AI Act exposure as a general Swiss market problem.

**Finding 6 — Personal liability discharge as the primary audience development signal for Brotons.net:**
Geneva professional services principals and managing partners respond to personal liability framing — what they personally need, what personally exposes them, what personally discharges their obligation — more than organizational compliance framing. Prior Brotons.net Phase 05 editorial content has addressed organizational accountability infrastructure.
