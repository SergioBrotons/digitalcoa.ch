- How to think about AI competence as a professional standard — is it like continuing education, or is it a fundamental change in what "competence" means?

- Vendor-produced content about AI governance that does not engage with Swiss professional responsibility standards
- Regulatory commentary that addresses what exists without explaining what is actually missing from their professional protection
- Content that treats AI governance as a technology decision rather than a professional responsibility decision
- Any AI governance guidance that assumes they have IT support, compliance departments, or dedicated AI governance staff

## 05 — Editorial Content Draft: Brotons.net Article Proposal

### Proposal A (Lead Essay Format — English)

**Title:** The Accountability Chain: How to Think About Responsibility When AI Is Involved in a Client Matter

**Angle:** The accountability question is the one that keeps Geneva senior professionals up at night. They use AI tools in their practice, they supervise junior colleagues who use AI tools, they rely on AI-assisted analysis from vendors, and at some point — either because of a client problem, a liability question, or an insurance inquiry — someone will ask: who was responsible for the decision that this AI-assisted analysis was sound?

This article provides the structural framework that no other English-language publication has offered for Swiss professional practice: an accountability chain analysis for AI-assisted client matters. It maps where responsibility sits at each stage — from initial AI tool selection, to AI-assisted analysis generation, to professional evaluation and reliance, to client communication, to file documentation — and explains how Geneva professional responsibility standards apply at each handoff. It is calm, precise, and practitioner-first. It does not give a false sense of certainty. It gives a usable map.

**Structure:**
1. Why the traditional accountability model breaks down when AI is involved: the linear accountability assumption and where it fails
2. The accountability chain framework: five stages where responsibility must be assigned and documented when AI is used in a client matter
3. Stage 1 — AI tool selection and onboarding: what the professional is vouching for when they choose an AI tool for a specific purpose
4. Stage 2 — AI-assisted analysis generation: where the professional's responsibility begins and ends for the AI output itself
5. Stage 3 — professional evaluation and reliance: what "reasonable professional evaluation" means when the professional is relying on AI-generated analysis
6. Stage 4 — client communication: how and what to communicate to clients about AI use, and what documentation protects the professional
7. Stage 5 — file documentation: what the AI file note should contain at each stage, and why the file note is the professional's primary protection
8. The Geneva-specific dimension: how Geneva professional culture and peer practice norms are shaping the informal accountability chain standards that are emerging

**Intended reader outcome:** The reader leaves with a structural map of where their accountability sits when AI is involved in a client matter. They understand that accountability is not a single point — it is a chain — and that each link in the chain requires a different kind of attention and documentation. They feel more equipped to answer the question "who was responsible" when it eventually comes.

**C217 Phase 05 — Net-new findings:**

**1. The accountability chain framework for AI-assisted professional services**
No English-language publisher has produced a structural accountability chain analysis for Swiss professional practice. The insight is that accountability in AI-assisted work is not a single point — it is a distributed chain with distinct stages (AI tool selection, AI output generation, professional evaluation and reliance, client communication, file documentation) where responsibility must be separately assigned and documented. This is distinct from C216's "accountability chain" language in ICP language (finding 1 there) because it produces a full practitioner framework, not just a naming of the problem. Brotons.net can publish the first actionable accountability chain methodology for Geneva professional practice — this becomes the structural foundation piece that supports all subsequent AI governance content.

**2. AI file note methodology: the specific documentation practice that protects the professional**
The informal move toward AI documentation notations in client files is a recognized trend — but nobody has produced a practitioner methodology for what a well-constructed AI file note should contain. The specific AI file note elements that provide real professional protection are: AI tool identification and version, purpose-specific use notation, professional evaluation of AI output before reliance, client communication record, and outcome-based reliance documentation. This is distinct from C215's "documentation imperative" (which was about the shift from frameworks to files) by providing the specific methodology for constructing protective AI file documentation. It is also distinct from C214's "AI incident response gap" (which was about reactive documentation after something goes wrong) by addressing proactive, ongoing file documentation practice.

**3. The AI supervision gap: reasonable supervision of AI-assisted work by professionals who were not trained as AI evaluators**
The unspoken anxiety at the center of Geneva senior professionals' AI governance concerns is the supervision question: how do you supervise and take responsibility for AI-assisted work when you were never trained to evaluate AI outputs? This question is present in the ICP's language but has not been addressed as a distinct professional responsibility topic. The analysis that "reasonable AI supervision" requires the professional to understand AI outputs at the level necessary to evaluate their soundness — not at the technical level — is a genuinely useful reframing. It resolves the supervision anxiety without pretending the professional must become an AI engineer. This is a distinct editorial angle that speaks directly to the anxiety driving much of the ICP's AI governance behavior.

**4. Geneva professional culture as regulatory substitute: mapping the informal standard-setting mechanism**
Geneva professional culture is functioning as a de facto AI governance regulatory substitute — peer practice norms set through conference conversations, bar association discussions, and professional networks are more determinative of actual AI governance behavior than formal regulation. Nobody has mapped this mechanism clearly: how it works, what standards it produces, what its limitations are, and what it means for the standard of care. This analysis is distinct from C216's identification of the peer practice norm (finding 2 there) by providing the structural analysis of how and why this regulatory substitution is happening, and what its implications are for practitioners and for Brotons.net's content strategy. The key insight: when professional culture substitutes for formal regulation, the standard of care is set by what peers do — which means the ICP is operating in a norm-setting environment, whether they know it or not.

**5. AI vendor management as a professional practice discipline: the ongoing relationship, not the initial choice**
Existing AI governance content addresses vendor evaluation and selection — but nobody addresses what comes after: the ongoing professional practice management of AI vendor relationships. This includes monitoring AI tool updates and their implications for reliance decisions, managing AI tool version documentation in client files, understanding AI vendor communication obligations and what to require in contracts, and establishing internal review triggers for when AI output changes materially. This is distinct from C214's "AI incident response gap" (reactive) and from C216's "AI vendor contract provisions" (contract-level) by addressing the ongoing operational discipline of managing AI as a professional practice tool over time. Brotons.net can position this as a distinct editorial vertical — AI vendor management for professionals — that no competitor has claimed.

**6. The AI practice architecture question: structural design for senior professionals building AI-integrated practices**
Senior Geneva professionals are beginning to think about building sustainable AI-integrated practices — not just adopting AI tools into existing workflows, but designing the accountability architecture for AI-assisted service delivery from the ground up. This includes questions of firm structure (who oversees AI use), workflow design (where AI enters the mandate), supervision protocols (how senior professionals supervise AI-assisted work), documentation standards (what the file must show), and client communication design (how AI use is described and agreed). Nobody is publishing about this structural design question for Geneva professional services. This is distinct from all prior phase 05 findings because it addresses the forward-looking design question — how to build an AI practice — rather than the defensive questions (how to avoid liability, how to document, how to comply) that dominate current content. It is a natural Brotons.net editorial angle because it requires both Geneva institutional knowledge and practitioner-level structural thinking.
