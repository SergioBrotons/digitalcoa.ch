## 04 — Compliance/Editorial Context: Swiss Regulatory Developments and Editorial Timing

**Key timing factors:**
- **Federal Council June 2026 deadline:** The Federal Council set end-of-2026 for AI measures in sensitive areas (healthcare, autonomous transport). The midpoint of 2026 is a natural editorial moment — the market is actively wondering whether concrete measures will emerge or whether the deadline will slip. Brotons.net can address the scenario planning angle before the deadline arrives.
- **Council of Europe AI Convention ratification:** The parliamentary ratification process is underway. The Convention creates binding due diligence and accountability obligations for operators of AI systems — this applies to Swiss firms that operate AI systems in Convention member states. This is more immediately relevant than most Geneva professionals realize.
- **FINMA AI supervision intensification:** FINMA continues to embed AI model risk governance into existing supervisory expectations. There is no single FINMA AI circular — rather, AI expectations are being integrated into existing supervisory frameworks across wealth management, asset management, and fintech licensing. This is a quieter but more persistent pressure.
- **nFADP enforcement trajectory:** The FDPIC has active investigation powers and has published penalty amounts. The interaction between nFADP existing provisions and forthcoming AI-specific obligations is becoming a practical question for firms that use AI in client-facing processes.
- **Cantonal divergence (residual):** Geneva has historically operated at the forward edge of Swiss regulatory interpretation. The cantonal layer is less actively discussed than in prior cycles, but it remains operationally relevant for firms with multi-cantonal practice.

**Professional obligation shifts:**
- Documentation imperative: The shift from "AI framework" to "file documentation" is the most significant behavioral change in the ICP. Professionals are now asking "what do I put in writing" rather than "what framework should I adopt."
- Client communication about AI: External pressure from EU counterparties is creating more urgency around AI governance documentation in client communications than Swiss regulatory requirements alone.
- Professional liability exposure: AI-assisted decisions that cause client harm are generating liability questions that existing insurance frameworks do not cleanly answer.
