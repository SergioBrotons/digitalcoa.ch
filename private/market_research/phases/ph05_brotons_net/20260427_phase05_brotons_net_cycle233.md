## 02 — Market Signals: What's Happening in the Swiss AI Governance Information Space

**What is resonating right now:**

The Geneva AI governance market has moved into a third distinct phase. The first phase was about whether to use AI. The second phase was about what governance framework to adopt. The third phase — the one the market is in now — is about what firms owe to the outputs produced during the first two phases, and about how firms are managing the growing inventory of AI governance surfaces that were created before governance methodology was mature.

Three specific signals are converging. First, the AI vendor consolidation wave is producing a new category of governance exposure: firms that built AI governance practices around specific tools are discovering that those tools are being deprecated, absorbed into different product architectures, or discontinued — and that the governance documentation they have describes systems that no longer exist in their documented form. This is creating a category of documentation obsolescence that is distinct from both the configuration state reconstruction problem and the temporal decay problem: the governance architecture was built around tools that have been removed from the landscape.

Second, the lateral movement of AI-experienced professionals between Geneva firms is creating a new category of governance inheritance that is not being systematically managed. When firms acquire lateral partners or teams with established AI practices, they are inheriting AI configuration histories, tool inventories, and governance approaches that have not been integrated into the acquiring firm's governance architecture — and that the acquiring firm may not have the knowledge to evaluate. This lateral integration problem is becoming a vector for governance exposure that the acquiring firm doesn't know it has absorbed.

Third, the carrier underwriting cycle is entering a third-generation phase. The first generation asked about AI tool inventory. The second generation asked about AI governance methodology. The third generation is beginning to probe the firm's position on its early-adoption-period AI outputs — the period from 2023 through mid-2024 — specifically asking what the firm can demonstrate about its AI configuration states and review standards during the period when most firms were building their AI practices without documentation infrastructure. This is a new and specific underwriting question that most Geneva firms cannot currently answer.

**What is missing:**

Content that addresses the AI vendor exit problem: what it means that the governance documentation for a deprecated or acquired AI tool describes a system that no longer exists in its documented form, why this creates a distinct documentation obsolescence problem, and what the firm should do about governance frameworks that were built around tools that have since been removed from the market.

Content that addresses the lateral hire AI governance integration problem: what it means that firms are absorbing AI practices through lateral movement without systematically integrating the governance histories of those practices into the acquiring firm's architecture, why this creates an exposure the acquiring firm may not know it has, and what a defensible lateral integration approach looks like.

Content that addresses the Geneva silence paradox as a governance opportunity: why the firms most capable of defining what adequate AI governance looks like are the firms least likely to share that knowledge publicly, and what the specific editorial contribution is that breaks this silence without requiring the leading firms to speak.

Second, the AI vendor exit problem is creating a documentation obsolescence surface that is not addressed by any current professional guidance — the question of what the firm owes to governance documentation that describes tools that no longer exist in their documented form has not been addressed by any Swiss professional body or regulatory authority.

Third, the carrier underwriting cycle is beginning to probe the early-adoption-period problem specifically — asking firms what their AI governance methodology looked like during 2023 through mid-2024, when most firms were building AI practices without documentation infrastructure. The Geneva silence paradox is the editorial anchor: the piece should be framed not as a solution from a single source, but as an analytical framework that reflects what the Geneva market is actually producing — through carrier renewals, client requirements, and counterparty pressures — without requiring any firm to speak publicly about its specific practice.

## 05 — Editorial Content Draft: Article Proposal in Brotons.net Voice

**Title (EN):**
**"The Three Governance Gaps Geneva Firms Are Discovering Too Late: Vendor Exits, Lateral Hires, and the Early-Adoption-Period Problem"**

**Title (FR):**
**"Les trois écarts de gouvernance que les cabinets genevois découvrent trop tard : sorties de fournisseurs, recrutements latéraux et le problème de la période d'adoption initiale"**

**Angle:**

There is a specific moment in the lifecycle of a Geneva professional firm's AI governance practice when the firm discovers it has been absorbing exposure it did not knowingly take on. It happens in three recurring scenarios, and none of them have been addressed with precision by any Swiss publisher.

The first is the vendor exit scenario. The firm built its AI governance practice around a tool that served it well for eighteen months or two years. The vendor was acquired. The product is being deprecated. The governance documentation — the tool inventory entry, the configuration records, the review process description — now describes a system that no longer exists in the form that was documented. The documentation is technically current. It is substantively obsolete. And when a carrier asks about the firm's AI governance methodology, the firm has documentation for an AI tool that the vendor no longer supports in its original form.

The second is the lateral hire scenario. The firm integrates a lateral partner or team with an established AI practice. Six months or a year into the integration, the firm realises it has absorbed an AI governance history it did not evaluate and cannot fully account for. The lateral practice was using AI tools. The configuration states, model version histories, and review standards of that practice were not documented in a form compatible with the acquiring firm's governance architecture. The firm absorbed the practice. It did not absorb the governance record. And the outputs from the lateral practice's AI-assisted work are now in client files that the acquiring firm is accountable for.

The third is the early-adoption-period problem. The carrier renewal asks, specifically, to describe the firm's AI governance methodology for 2023 through mid-2024. Not the current methodology. The methodology the firm had when it was building its AI practice without documentation infrastructure, without model version tracking, and without a clear understanding of what adequate governance looked like. The firm cannot answer this question, not because it failed, but because the question asks for something most firms could not have produced at the time.

This article is about the three governance gaps: what they are, why they are distinct from the governance problems firms expected to face, how they arise through market mechanisms rather than regulatory failure, and what a defensible position looks like in each case. It is written for the Geneva professional who has been managing AI governance actively and who is now discovering that the governance surface they need to manage is larger than the surface they designed governance to cover.

**Structure:**

*Opening:* Three scenarios, one structural problem. A Geneva firm receives a carrier renewal questionnaire that asks specifically about the firm's AI governance methodology during its early adoption period. A managing partner integrates a lateral team and discovers the AI governance history they absorbed. An AI tool the firm has been using for two years is deprecated after its vendor is acquired. In each case, the firm has governance documentation for a situation that no longer exists in its documented form. This is the three-gap problem, and it is distinct from the governance gaps firms prepared for.

*Section 1 — The AI vendor exit documentation problem*
When a firm builds AI governance around a specific tool, the governance documentation becomes partially a record of that tool's configuration state. When the vendor exits the market, is acquired, or substantially changes the product architecture, the governance documentation that described the tool at a specific point in time now describes a system that may no longer be recoverable in its documented form. This is different from the configuration state reconstruction problem — the configuration state problem means the firm cannot reconstruct what the AI was doing; the vendor exit problem means the firm has documentation for a tool whose original form no longer exists. The response is the documentation archaeology protocol: a systematic review of governance documentation for deprecated tools that establishes what the tool was documented to do, what the current state of the tool or its replacement is, and what the firm's current position is on outputs produced under the original documented configuration. The goal is not to maintain obsolete documentation — it is to ensure that the firm's governance record accurately reflects what it can actually demonstrate about AI use in client matters, including where that demonstration requires acknowledging that a documented tool no longer exists in its original form.

*Section 2 — The lateral hire AI governance integration problem*
When a firm integrates a lateral partner or team with an established AI practice, it absorbs that practice's AI governance history — the tool inventory, configuration decisions, review standards, and output records — without having evaluated that history in the context of the acquiring firm's governance standards. This is a specific and named governance exposure that most firms do not manage through a deliberate protocol. The lateral integration problem is structurally distinct from the general governance gap because it arises through talent movement rather than through the firm's own AI adoption decisions. The response is the lateral integration protocol: a bounded due diligence process at the point of lateral integration that establishes what AI tools and configurations the lateral practice was using, what documentation exists about those tools, what the review standard looked like for AI-assisted client work, and what outputs from that practice's AI-assisted work are in client files now under the acquiring firm's accountability. The integration protocol converts an unevaluated governance inheritance into a documented position the firm can defend.

*Section 3 — The early-adoption-period AI outputs problem*
The carrier renewal questionnaire that asks specifically about the firm's AI governance methodology during 2023 through mid-2024 is asking for something most firms could not have produced at the time. This is not a documentation failure — it is a structural consequence of the period in which the firm was building its AI practice without documentation infrastructure. The early-adoption-period outputs are still in client files. The firm cannot reconstruct the configuration states from that period. And the carrier is now asking questions about what the firm can demonstrate about those outputs. The response is the period boundary documentation: a contemporaneous record that establishes the firm's position on its early-adoption-period AI outputs — what the firm knows about its AI use during that period, what it cannot reconstruct, and what it has done to address the outputs that remain in client files. The period boundary documentation is not a reconstruction of what the firm cannot reconstruct. It is a documented acknowledgment of the reconstruction limitation and the firm's deliberate response to it.

*Section 4 — Why the Geneva silence paradox makes these problems harder to see*
The firms that have made the most progress on managing these three governance gaps are the firms that are most careful about what they say publicly. The Geneva professional culture — which operates through discrete relationships and quiet conversations rather than public best-practice sharing — means that the firms with the most sophisticated responses to the vendor exit problem, the lateral integration problem, and the early-adoption-period problem are not publishing what they have learned. The result is that the broader Geneva professional community is encountering these problems later and less prepared than it needs to be, because the firms that best understand the problems are maintaining silence. This is the Geneva silence paradox, and it is the specific market failure that editorial analysis can address without requiring any firm to speak publicly about its own practice.

*Section 5 — The market mechanism that is accelerating the pressure*
The three gaps are becoming acute not primarily because of regulatory intervention but because of market mechanism pressure: carrier renewals probing the early-adoption period, lateral hiring continuing to move AI-experienced professionals between firms, and AI vendor consolidation continuing to remove tools from the market. The market is creating the pressure that regulatory guidance has not yet produced. The firms that develop deliberate responses to the three gaps before a specific incident or underwriting outcome forces the issue will be in a materially different position from the firms that wait for the pressure to become unavoidable. The defensible position — period boundary documentation, lateral integration protocol, documentation archaeology for deprecated tools — is available to firms that choose to build it now.

*Closing:* The three governance gaps are not failures of governance design. They are structural consequences of building an AI governance practice in a market that was moving faster than the documentation infrastructure could follow, that continues to move through talent and vendor changes that create new governance surfaces, and that is now being asked to account for what it built during a period when adequate governance methodology had not yet been defined. The response is not retroactive reconstruction of what cannot be reconstructed. It is deliberate, documented positions on what the firm knows, what it cannot know, and what it has done about the outputs that remain in client files under each of the three scenarios.

**Intended reader outcome:**

A Geneva managing partner finishes the article understanding what the three governance gaps actually are — the vendor exit documentation problem, the lateral hire integration problem, and the early-adoption-period problem — and why each is distinct from the governance gaps they prepared for. They understand why the Geneva silence paradox makes these problems harder to see and address than they need to be. They understand how market mechanism pressure — carrier renewals, lateral hiring, vendor consolidation — is accelerating the urgency of each gap without regulatory intervention. They leave knowing what the period boundary documentation requires, what the lateral integration protocol should cover, and what the documentation archaeology protocol for deprecated tools looks like. They know what to do now and what to build going forward so that the next vendor exit, the next lateral hire, and the next carrier renewal do not catch them unprepared.

## 06 — Findings Summary: 6 Net-New Content or Editorial Insights

*Distinct from C214–C232 Phase 05.*

**Insight 1 — The AI Vendor Exit Documentation Problem Is a Distinct Governance Exposure Arising from AI Market Consolidation That No Swiss Publisher Has Addressed**
As the AI vendor market consolidates, firms that built governance practices around specific tools are discovering that their governance documentation describes systems that no longer exist in their documented form. The tool was deprecated, acquired, or substantially changed after the product architecture shifted. The documentation is technically current — it reflects what the tool was when documented. It is substantively obsolete — it no longer reflects anything that exists in the market. This is distinct from the configuration state reconstruction problem (C232): the configuration state problem means the firm cannot reconstruct what the AI was doing at a specific past time; the vendor exit problem means the firm has documentation for a tool whose current form or existence cannot be verified. The remediation is the documentation archaeology protocol — a systematic review process for governance documentation covering deprecated tools that establishes what the tool was documented to do, what its current state is, and what the firm's position is on outputs produced under the original configuration.

**Insight 2 — The Lateral Hire AI Governance Integration Problem Is a Specific and Unmanaged Governance Exposure Arising from Professional Talent Movement Between Geneva Firms**
When a Geneva firm integrates a lateral partner or team with an established AI practice, it absorbs that practice's AI governance history — tool inventories, configuration decisions, review standards, and output records — without having evaluated that history against the acquiring firm's governance standards. This exposure is not being managed through any systematic protocol by most Geneva firms. It is structurally distinct from the general governance gap because it arises through talent movement rather than through the firm's own AI adoption decisions, and because the firm is accountable for outputs it did not produce under a governance architecture it did not design. This is distinct from C222 Phase 05 (board-level personal liability as growing upstream demand signal) by identifying the lateral hire integration problem as a specific, named governance exposure with its own mechanics and its own remediation protocol, not as a manifestation of the general accountability chain problem.

**Insight 3 — The Geneva Professional Culture Silence Paradox Has a Specific Market Mechanism: The Leading Firms Are Silent Because Geneva Peer Accountability Operates Through Reputation, Not Publication**
The Geneva firms that have developed the most sophisticated responses to the three governance gaps are maintaining silence not primarily because of competitive concern but because Geneva professional culture operates through discrete reputation rather than public best-practice sharing. In a community where what you know and what you say publicly are governed by relationship-level accountability rather than published standards, the most knowledgeable firms are the most careful about public claims. The silence is rational within the Geneva professional culture frame, but it creates a market failure: the firms that most need guidance cannot access the knowledge of the firms best positioned to provide it. This is distinct from C231 Phase 05 (Geneva structural silence in market standard formation mechanism) by identifying the specific cultural mechanism — reputation-based peer accountability rather than publication-based standard formation — that produces the silence, and by framing the silence paradox as a market failure with a specific editorial remedy rather than as a structural characteristic of the Geneva environment.

**Insight 4 — The Carrier Underwriting Cycle Has Entered a Third-Generation Phase Probing the Early-Adoption-Period Problem Specifically**
The first generation of carrier AI questionnaires asked about AI tool inventory. The second generation asked about AI governance methodology. The third generation — now arriving in Geneva carrier renewals — asks specifically about the firm's AI governance methodology during 2023 through mid-2024. This is a new and specific underwriting question that most firms cannot answer, not because they failed, but because the question asks for something most firms could not have produced during that period. The early-adoption-period problem is distinct from the general liability tail problem (C230) because it identifies a specific market mechanism — the carrier renewal questionnaire — that is producing a specific documentation requirement for a specific time period that the firm cannot reconstruct. The editorial contribution is to name the early-adoption-period problem, to explain why the inability to answer is not a professional failure, and to provide the period boundary documentation methodology as the defensible response.

The convergence of vendor consolidation, lateral talent movement, and third-generation carrier underwriting is producing a market in which Geneva firms are being tiered by their AI governance capability without any regulatory intervention or published standard. Firms that have developed deliberate responses to the vendor exit, lateral integration, and early-adoption-period problems are being distinguished in carrier underwriting outcomes, in client negotiations, and in counterparty due diligence. This is distinct from C226 Phase 05 (Geneva informal tier structure with competitive consequence) by identifying the specific market mechanism — vendor consolidation, lateral hiring, third-generation carrier underwriting — through which the tier structure is being produced in real time, not just observing that a tier structure exists.
