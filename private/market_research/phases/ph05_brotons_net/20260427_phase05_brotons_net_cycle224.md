## 02 — Market Signals: What's Happening in the Swiss AI Governance Information Space

**What is resonating right now:**

The carrier underwriting question has changed in character. Two years ago, carriers were asking whether firms used AI. Now they are asking specific questions about what AI does in the workflow, what documentation exists, and what happens when the AI tool changes or produces an unexpected output. The shift from "do you use AI" to "what specifically happens when AI is in the loop" is a market signal that the insurance market is forming a view about what adequate AI governance documentation looks like — and that practitioners who cannot answer the specific questions will face different underwriting outcomes than those who can.

The counterparty AI governance questionnaire has become a permanent feature of professional services procurement, not a temporary phase. Firms with international operations or EU counterparties are being asked to demonstrate AI governance practice as part of standard due diligence. The gap between having a policy and being able to demonstrate it in a structured questionnaire format is now a commercial consequence, not just a theoretical exposure.

Geneva's professional community is developing informal AI governance tiers — firms that have done significant work, firms that have done surface-level work, and firms that have done nothing. These tiers are not visible through published rankings or formal standards. They are visible through the informal knowledge that circulates in the professional community — through client feedback, carrier questions, and peer conversations. The tier distinction is real and it is beginning to affect how firms are positioned in the market.

The question of AI's role in client deliverables is surfacing as a commercial expectation problem. Clients who are themselves subject to AI governance obligations are beginning to ask service providers about the AI in their work — not as a compliance question but as a quality question. They want to know what the AI did, how it was supervised, and whether the output meets the standard they are holding their own boards accountable for.

**What is missing:**

Content that addresses the second-phase AI governance practitioner — the person who has been working on this for 12–24 months and who has moved past framework adoption into operational confrontation with the hard questions. Almost all available content is pitched at an earlier stage. The experienced practitioner has run out of relevant reading material.

The Geneva-specific version of "reasonable professional practice" for AI governance is not being articulated by any publisher. Practitioners are being asked to demonstrate compliance with a standard that does not yet exist in Swiss doctrine — the standard is forming through informal professional community consensus, through carrier underwriting signals, and through FDPIC investigative patterns. Content that maps this formation process and translates it into practitioner-relevant terms is missing.

The AI knowledge transfer problem — how firms capture the AI governance decisions made by people who later leave — is surfacing as a structural exposure. This is not about junior staff using AI without supervision. It is about the institutional knowledge problem: when someone who made significant AI tool decisions leaves the firm, what they knew leaves with them, and the firm has no mechanism for capturing it.

## 05 — Editorial Content Draft: Article Proposal in Brotons.net Voice

**Title (EN):**
**"The Second-Phase AI Governance Practitioner: What 'Reasonable' Looks Like After the Framework Is Built"**

**Title (FR):**
**"Le praticien de la gouvernance de l'IA en deuxième phase : à quoi ressemble « raisonnable » une fois le cadre construit"**

**Angle:**

There is a category of Geneva professional that has not been served by any publisher: the practitioner who has been working on AI governance for 12–24 months, who has a framework in place, who has the tools deployed, who has the policies documented — and who has discovered that having the framework is not the same as having the answers. This article is for that practitioner. It addresses the specific collision that occurs when first-generation AI governance meets operational reality: when the tool changes and the documentation no longer matches, when the client asks a specific question and the policy does not cover it, when the carrier asks about AI in a way the policy never anticipated.

The angle is not about building a better framework. It is about understanding what "reasonable AI governance practice" means in Geneva right now — not as a regulatory definition, but as an operational standard that is being formed through carrier underwriting signals, FDPIC investigation patterns, counterparty questionnaire responses, and informal professional community consensus. The practitioner who understands what is forming — and why — is better positioned than the one who is waiting for a published standard to tell them where the line is.

**Structure:**

*Opening:* The scenario. A Geneva managing partner who has been working on AI governance for 18 months receives a carrier underwriting questionnaire that asks questions she cannot answer from her existing documentation. The policy is in place. The answers are not. The article starts here — in the gap between having a framework and being able to demonstrate what the framework means in a specific professional moment.

*Section 1 — Why the first-generation framework is colliding with operational reality*
The transition from AI governance policy adoption to AI governance operational confrontation. The framework was designed to answer the question "do we have AI governance." It was not designed to answer the question "what specifically does our AI do in each matter, and can we show that a professional judgment was applied to the output." These are different questions. The first-generation framework answers the first. The second question is where the exposure now lives.

*Section 2 — What 'reasonable AI governance practice' is actually made of in Geneva right now*
The four components of what is forming as the Geneva professional standard: (1) documented tool configuration — what AI tools are in use, what version, what settings; (2) documented purpose limitation — what the tool is being used for and why that use is appropriate; (3) documented human review — what review happened, by whom, and what judgment was applied; (4) documented client communication — what the client was told about AI use in their matter. These four components are not invented — they are derivable from the FDPIC's investigation patterns, from carrier underwriting questions, from counterparty questionnaire structures, and from informal professional community consensus. The article shows how each component is being tested by the market right now.

*Section 3 — The AI tool volatility problem: what to do when the tool changes underneath your documentation*
The specific operational problem that is surfacing most acutely for second-phase practitioners: the AI tools in use are updating faster than documentation cycles can track. A prompt configuration documented six months ago may no longer produce the same output. A model version referenced in a file note may have been updated without the firm noticing. The article addresses how to maintain documentation accuracy for tools that are continuously changing — not by updating documentation faster, but by designing a version-aware documentation architecture that can accommodate tool change without requiring a full documentation rebuild.

*Section 4 — The carrier underwriting signal: what the insurance market is actually asking for and why*
The carrier underwriting questions have shifted from "do you use AI" to "what happens when the AI produces an unexpected output." The article maps the specific questions carriers are now asking and explains what they reveal about the standard the insurance market is forming. The insight is not that carriers have defined the standard — they have not — but that the questions they are asking are diagnostic of what a defensible AI governance practice needs to be able to demonstrate. Practitioners who can answer the carrier's questions in a coherent, documented way are being treated differently in underwriting than those who cannot. The article explains why and what a defensible carrier response looks like.

*Section 5 — The client AI expectation problem: when AI in deliverables becomes an implied quality assumption*
Clients who are themselves subject to AI governance obligations — boards, trading entities, regulated firms — are beginning to hold their service providers to the same standard they hold themselves. The question is not "did you use AI" but "what did your AI do in this matter and how do you know it was right." The article addresses how this expectation is surfacing in the client-service relationship, what it means for how AI use should be documented in client matters, and what the communication obligation actually is when AI is involved in a deliverable.

*Section 6 — What the Geneva professional community's informal tier structure means for competitive positioning*
The Geneva professional community is developing informal AI governance tiers through the informal knowledge that circulates in the community. Firms with genuine AI governance practice are being distinguished from firms with surface-level documentation by clients, carriers, and counterparties in ways that are not formally measured but are real in their consequences. The article explains how this tier structure is forming and what it means for firms that have done the work versus those who have not — and why the firms that have done the work are not yet being properly recognised for it.

*Closing:* The framework was the beginning, not the end. The second-phase practitioner has done the necessary first work. The work now is not to build a better framework — it is to make the existing framework operational by filling the specific gaps that operational reality has exposed. The four documentation components, the version-aware architecture, the carrier response, the client communication standard — these are not new requirements. They are the operationalisation of what the first-generation framework was always pointing toward.

**Intended reader outcome:**

A Geneva managing partner who has been working on AI governance for 12–24 months finishes the article understanding exactly what is missing from their current AI governance documentation, why the missing elements matter in specific professional contexts (carrier underwriting, client questions, counterparty questionnaires, FDPIC investigation), and what specific steps to take in the next 30 days to move from framework possession to operational defensibility. They leave with a clear-eyed view of where they are relative to where the market is forming, and what concrete work needs to happen to close the gap.

**Insight 1 — The Second-Phase AI Governance Practitioner is an Underserved Audience**
The dominant assumption in Swiss AI governance content is that the audience is either early-stage (beginning AI governance work) or policy-level (boards and executives receiving AI governance briefings). The second-phase practitioner — someone who has been working on AI governance for 12–24 months and has a framework in place but is now confronting the hard operational questions that the framework does not answer — is not being served by any Swiss publisher. Content pitched at this audience's specific level of experience and specific operational questions (AI tool volatility, carrier underwriting specificity, client expectation management, version-aware documentation) is a distinct editorial opportunity. This is distinct from C219/C220 Phase 05 which identified maturity-stage content needs generally — C224 identifies the specific second-phase operational collision and the specific documentation and practice gaps that define this phase.

**Insight 2 — The Four-Component Geneva AI Governance Standard is Forming Through Market Mechanisms**
The "reasonable AI governance practice" standard for Geneva professional firms is not being defined by published regulation — it is being formed through the convergence of four market mechanisms: carrier underwriting questions, FDPIC investigative patterns, counterparty AI due diligence questionnaires, and informal professional community consensus. The net-new insight is that these four mechanisms are converging on the same four documentation components (tool configuration, purpose limitation, human review, client communication), which means the standard is becoming internally consistent across different enforcement surfaces even without published prescriptive rules. Content that maps this convergence and shows practitioners how to read it is distinct from all prior Phase 05 findings, none of which addressed the market-mechanism standard-formation process itself as the editorial subject.

**Insight 3 — AI Tool Volatility Creates a Version-Aware Documentation Architecture Problem**
The problem is not that documentation is missing — it is that documentation accuracy cannot be maintained when tools change continuously underneath fixed documentation cycles. The net-new insight is that the solution is not faster documentation but a different documentation architecture: one designed to capture tool configuration and output at the point of use rather than in periodic review cycles. This is distinct from C215 Phase 05 (documentation imperative — the shift from policy to file), C216 Phase 05 (AI tool version control documentation — the problem of version tracking), and C217 Phase 05 (AI supervision gap) by addressing specifically the architectural question: not how to document more, but how to design a documentation system that can accommodate tool volatility without requiring continuous manual updates.

**Insight 4 — The Carrier Underwriting Signal Reveals a Market-Formed Standard**
Professional liability carriers are asking more specific AI governance questions in underwriting than 12 months ago — questions about what happens when AI produces unexpected output, what documentation exists at the point of use, and what human review actually occurred. These questions are not random — they are diagnostic of what the carrier has determined creates AI governance exposure. The net-new insight is that the carrier underwriting process is forming a market-based standard for what adequate AI governance documentation looks like, distinct from and ahead of regulatory definition. This is distinct from C215 Phase 05's "PL insurance gap" (which identified that the coverage question was unresolved) and from C221 Phase 05's "personal professional liability" finding (which addressed the individual exposure question, not the carrier market signal methodology).

**Insight 5 — The AI Knowledge Transfer Problem is a Structural Exposure Distinct from Supervisory Gap**
The junior staff AI use problem is not primarily a supervisory failure — it is a knowledge capture failure. The AI governance decisions being made by departing staff (tool choices, prompt configurations, workflow integrations) represent institutional knowledge that is not being captured and that leaves with the person. The net-new insight is that the AI knowledge transfer problem is a distinct structural exposure from the supervisory gap: it is not about controlling unsanctioned AI use, it is about capturing sanctioned AI decisions before they disappear with the person who made them. This is distinct from C217 Phase 05 (AI supervision gap), C218 Phase 05 (AI knowledge loss on departure), C220 Phase 05 (institutional knowledge capture), and C221 Phase 05 (structural supervisory gap design problem) — C224 frames the problem as knowledge capture architecture, not supervision architecture or knowledge loss description.

**Insight 6 — Geneva's Informal AI Governance Tier Structure Has Competitive Consequence**
The Geneva professional community is informally distinguishing between firms that have done genuine AI governance work and firms with surface-level documentation — through client feedback, carrier questions, counterparty responses, and peer conversation. This informal tier structure has real market consequence: firms in the upper tier are being distinguished positively in client selection, carrier underwriting, and counterparties assessments. The net-new insight is that the informal tier structure is itself a competitive positioning signal for Geneva boutique firms — demonstrating genuine AI governance practice is becoming a positive commercial differentiator, not just a defensive compliance measure. This is distinct from C223 Phase 05's "knowledge economy" insight (which addressed AI governance expertise as an exportable service) by framing the competitive positioning angle specifically: visible, credible AI governance practice is attracting clients and retaining talent, not just avoiding liability.
