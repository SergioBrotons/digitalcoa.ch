## 02 — Market Signals

What's happening in the Swiss AI governance information space. What's resonating. What's missing.

### Active Market Dynamics

**The AI explanation gap at the model-output level: nFADP Art.21 meets unauditable reasoning.** The nFADP Article 21 automated decision explanation right is now operating as a present-tense exposure, not a future compliance exercise. Firms are encountering situations where clients request explanation of AI-processed outcomes and the underlying model cannot produce the requested reasoning trace. This is structurally distinct from a documentation gap — it is a technical architecture incompatibility between explanation obligation and model design. The market has not developed an operational protocol for this specific failure mode: how to discharge an explanation obligation when the explanation is technically unavailable from the system that generated the outcome.

**Board-level personal AI use as unmanaged governance surface with direct personal liability consequence.** Geneva board members are using personal AI tools — for board communications, research, document preparation — outside any organizational governance framework. This personal AI use creates Swiss data protection liability for the individual board member under nFADP, independent of the organization's AI governance policy. Boards reviewing organizational AI governance policies are not reviewing their own personal AI use surface. This gap is structurally distinct from the organizational governance policy scope: board members in their personal capacity are processing data under obligations they cannot describe, through tools they did not evaluate, with no organizational infrastructure to support them.

**Geneva court procedural AI questions arriving as unpublished case-specific exposure.** Geneva civil procedure is generating AI-related procedural questions at the case level — authentication of AI-generated documents, procedural status of AI-assisted filings, model version disclosure in discovery, judicial requests for methodology explanation in proceedings. These questions are being resolved case-by-case without published doctrine. The informal circuit is circulating these experiences, but no Geneva publication is treating unpublished procedural resolution as a first-class editorial subject requiring systematic aggregation and analysis.

**EU AI Act August 2026 threshold as structural forcing function for Swiss firms with EU market exposure.** The EU AI Act becomes fully applicable for high-risk systems by August 2, 2026. Swiss firms with EU clients, EU-facing services, or AI outputs used within the EU are approaching the point where EU AI Act service provider obligations are operative independently of any Swiss domestic regulatory determination. The market is not treating this as an imminent operational deadline requiring present-tense documentation architecture. It is treating it as a regulatory question to be answered when Swiss domestic law clarifies the intersection.

**The carrier attestation as personal liability instrument with no discharge methodology: third-generation deepening.** Carrier AI questionnaires have progressed through three generations of probing substantive review adequacy. The current generation is asking about methodology substance, not just policy existence. The personal signature requirement on carrier attestations creates personal liability exposure for the attesting professional. No Geneva market participant has provided a discharge methodology: what substantive review looks like operationally, what evidence demonstrates adequate review, what happens when the attesting professional cannot produce that evidence under questioning.

### Resonating Themes
- nFADP Art.21 explanation obligation as live operational problem, not future compliance scenario
- Board-level personal AI use governance as the ungoverned surface that organizational policies miss
- Geneva court procedural questions as the accountability surface that compliance checklists don't address
- EU AI Act August 2026 threshold approaching before Swiss domestic regulatory intersection is resolved
- Carrier attestation substantive review deepening without operational discharge methodology

### What's Missing from the Market
- No Geneva publication is treating nFADP Art.21 explanation obligations as a model-architecture problem, not a documentation problem
- No analysis of board member personal AI use as a distinct personal liability surface requiring its own governance surface
- No systematic aggregation of unpublished Geneva court procedural AI questions as an editorial beat

## 05 — Editorial Content Draft

A specific Brotons.net article proposal in Sergio's voice — bilingual EN + FR.

### Article Title (EN)

**The Explanation Gap: When Your AI System Cannot Tell You What It Decided — and the Law Requires It To**

### Article Title (FR)

**Le Fossé de l'Explication : Quand Votre Système IA Ne Peut Pas Vous Dire Ce Qu'Il a Décidé — et Que le Droit l'Exige**

### Angle

The nFADP Article 21 right to explanation of automated decisions is presenting Geneva firms with a technical architecture problem that documentation cannot resolve. When a client requests explanation of an AI-processed outcome and the underlying model does not retain per-instance reasoning traces, the explanation obligation is technically discharged before the firm becomes aware it exists. The market has not named this as a distinct structural problem requiring a different remediation logic than disclosure templates and consent forms. This article names it.

The piece does not propose that firms replace AI tools. It examines what the explanation obligation actually requires operationally, why many current AI model architectures are structurally incompatible with that requirement, what the firm's position is when explanation is technically impossible, and what a defensible discharge protocol looks like when the model cannot explain itself.

This is not a hypothetical concern. It is a present-tense exposure that is activating for firms that have had clients request explanations of AI-processed outcomes and discovered the underlying system cannot produce the requested reasoning.

### Structure

**Opening: The moment the explanation is requested**

A Geneva firm receives a client request for explanation of an automated decision. The client has a right under nFADP Article 21. The firm begins preparing the explanation and discovers the AI system that generated the decision does not retain per-instance reasoning traces. The model can confirm it processed the data and produced the output. It cannot reconstruct the specific reasoning path for the specific decision. The obligation exists. The discharge path does not.

This is the explanation gap.

**Section 1: What Article 21 actually requires**

The nFADP explanation right is not satisfied by disclosure that an AI system was used. It requires substantive explanation of the logic involved in the automated decision. FDPIC guidance makes this clear. The obligation is for the specific decision, not the general system. A client requesting explanation of a specific AI-processed outcome is entitled to explanation of that specific outcome's logic. When the model does not retain the reasoning path for the specific decision, the obligation is technically incompatible with the system's architecture.

**Section 2: Why many AI model architectures create this incompatibility**

Explainable AI (XAI) is a research field specifically because most production AI models — including those embedded in the tools Geneva professional services firms are using — do not preserve per-instance reasoning traces. The model may be accurate. It may be well-governed. It may produce correct outcomes. It may not be able to reconstruct the specific reasoning path for a specific outcome in a form that satisfies an explanation obligation.

This is not a vendor failure. It is a technical property of many model architectures. The firms that deployed these tools in 2023 and 2024 did not deploy tools they knew would create explanation gaps. They deployed tools that solved problems. The explanation gap is a downstream consequence that the deployment decision did not anticipate.

**Section 3: The firm's position when explanation is technically impossible**

When a client requests explanation and the model cannot produce it, the firm's position is not that it failed to document — it is that the technical architecture of the system in use is incompatible with the explanation obligation. These are different positions with different implications. Documentation improvement addresses the first position. It does not address the second.

The firm's defensible position when the model cannot explain itself requires: acknowledgment that the specific explanation cannot be provided, description of what the model does and how it was governed, evidence that the system was operated within its authorized scope, demonstration that human review was applied at the appropriate level. This is a different discharge protocol than an explanation of reasoning — it is a position that explains why the explanation cannot be provided and demonstrates governance discipline in the absence of the requested information.

**Section 4: What a defensible discharge protocol looks like**

The protocol has three components: pre-deployment evaluation of explanation capability as a governance requirement, not a technical preference; documentation architecture that preserves what the model does and how it was governed, even when it cannot explain specific outcomes; a client communication protocol that manages explanation requests through a structured response that acknowledges the obligation, describes the system's explanation architecture honestly, and demonstrates the governance surrounding the system's use.

A firm that can say: "The model you are asking about does not retain per-instance reasoning traces and cannot provide the specific explanation you are requesting. Here is what the system does, how it was authorized, what governance applies to its use, and how human review was applied to this outcome" — that firm has a defensible position. A firm that cannot say any of those things has a different and more acute exposure.

**Closing: The architectural question**

The explanation gap is not a documentation problem. It is an architectural problem that documentation can partially address but not resolve. Firms that deployed AI tools before this question was live are now discovering that the question is live. The appropriate response is not to document harder — it is to understand what the explanation obligation actually requires, evaluate their current systems against that requirement, and build governance architecture that is defensible in the absence of the explanation the regulation calls for.

The firms that will be in the strongest position are those that understood the question before it became an enforcement question.

### Intended Reader Outcome

After reading this article, a Geneva professional services principal understands:
- Why the nFADP Article 21 explanation obligation is technically incompatible with many AI model architectures in current use
- Why this is an architectural problem, not a documentation problem
- What the firm's defensible position looks like when the model cannot explain a specific outcome
- What a discharge protocol requires when explanation is technically impossible
- Why pre-deployment evaluation of explanation capability is a governance requirement, not a technical preference
- What the August 2026 EU AI Act threshold adds to this analysis for Swiss firms with EU client exposure
