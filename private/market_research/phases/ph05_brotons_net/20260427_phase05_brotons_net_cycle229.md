## 02 — Market Signals: What's Happening in the Swiss AI Governance Information Space

**What is resonating right now:**

The prompt library question has arrived as a specific governance surface. Firms that invested significant time in 2023–2024 configuring AI tools for their practice areas — building prompt libraries, setting custom instructions, tuning output parameters for their specific document types and client communication standards — are beginning to recognise that these configurations are practitioner-generated intellectual property operating inside vendor-controlled infrastructure. The governance question this creates is distinct from both tool selection and output review: the prompt library represents a set of deliberate methodological choices about how AI should behave in the firm's practice, and those choices are currently governed neither by the vendor (whose model changes without notice) nor by the firm (whose governance documentation does not include the configuration decisions). The prompt library governance gap is a specific, identifiable exposure that is surfacing in carrier questionnaires and counterparty probes.

The Geneva international client portfolio is creating AI governance expectation divergence. International clients — particularly those with EU exposure, diplomatic community affiliation, or institutional accountability structures — are applying AI governance expectations to their Geneva service providers that are calibrated to their own institutional contexts rather than to Swiss domestic regulatory standards. The gap this creates is not about whether the Geneva firm has AI governance; it is about whether the firm's governance documentation is in a language and format that the international client's accountability function can evaluate. The international client is often looking for evidence of governance practice that is framed in terms the client's own governance framework recognises — and most Geneva boutique documentation is not calibrated for cross-institutional evaluation.

The post-incident accountability question is surfacing with new urgency. A small number of Geneva professional firms have had AI-assisted client matters that produced outcomes requiring explanation — not necessarily errors, but outcomes where the AI's role in the recommendation or decision was opaque when the client asked questions. The firms that have been through this are not publishing their experiences, but the informal professional network is beginning to carry information about which firms have encountered this and how they handled it. The post-incident accountability question — what a Geneva firm needs to have in place before an incident occurs, and what the response protocol looks like when one does — is beginning to register as a specific governance need rather than a theoretical risk.

**What is missing:**

Content that addresses the prompt library as a distinct governance object — what it is, why it is practitioner intellectual property operating inside vendor infrastructure, what the governance attention it requires looks like, and what happens when the underlying model changes in ways that affect how the library behaves. This is a specific, operationally actionable editorial contribution that no Swiss publisher has addressed.

Content that addresses the Geneva international client portfolio as a distinct AI governance context — how international clients evaluate AI governance practice differently from Swiss domestic clients, what the cross-institutional evaluation language looks like, and what a Geneva firm needs to have in its governance documentation to be legible to an international counterparty's accountability function.

Content that addresses the post-incident accountability question for Geneva professional firms — not the general risk management literature, but the specific protocol for what a Geneva boutique firm does when an AI-assisted client matter produces an outcome that requires explanation, and what the pre-incident governance investment looks like that makes that response possible.

## 04 — Compliance/Editorial Context: Swiss Regulatory Developments, Professional Obligation Shifts, and Editorial Timing

The anticipated consultation draft on AI Convention integration by end of 2026 creates a specific pre-publication editorial window: the period before the draft arrives is when anticipatory guidance has the highest value for practitioners who are making governance decisions in advance of regulatory clarification. The regulatory uncertainty compounds the prompt library governance problem — firms are making configuration decisions about AI tools without knowing what the regulatory frame will require those decisions to look like in documentation terms.

The FDPIC's enforcement record under nFADP Articles 12, 21, and 22 continues to be the primary practical map of what substantive compliance looks like. What the enforcement record is increasingly establishing is that documentation must be capable of answering specific questions about specific processing — what the AI system did, on what basis, with what data, for what purpose in the specific matter. The prompt library is the most specific documentation surface for this question: the custom instructions that govern how an AI tool behaves in a specific client matter are the direct expression of the firm's deliberate choices about what the AI system does, on what basis, and for what purpose. ## 05 — Editorial Content Draft: Article Proposal in Brotons.net Voice

**Title (EN):**
**"The Prompt Library Problem: Why the AI Governance Configuration Your Firm Built Is the Most Ungoverned Part of Your Practice — and How to Fix It"**

**Title (FR):**
**« Le problème de la bibliothèque de prompts : pourquoi la configuration de gouvernance de l'IA que votre cabinet a construite est la partie la moins gouvernée de votre pratique — et comment y remédier »**

**Angle:**

There is a specific AI governance problem that every Geneva professional firm that invested in AI tool configuration in 2023 or 2024 is now confronting, and that no Swiss publisher has addressed in operational terms. The problem is not about which AI tool the firm uses, or whether the firm has an AI governance policy, or whether senior review of AI-assisted work is documented. The problem is the prompt library — the collection of custom instructions, tuned parameters, output configurations, and methodology-specific setups that the firm built to make a general-purpose AI tool behave in specific ways appropriate to the firm's practice areas.

The prompt library is, without exaggeration, the most operationally significant AI governance document most Geneva firms have produced. It contains the firm's specific instructions about how AI should behave in client matters — what it should produce, what it should refuse, what level of detail is expected, what format the output should take, what the firm considers acceptable and unacceptable from the AI in their specific practice context. These are deliberate methodological choices. They are professional practice decisions. And they are currently governed — if they are governed at all — as an afterthought to the AI governance policy rather than as the primary expression of what the firm has decided about how AI should operate in its practice.

This article is about the prompt library problem: what it is, why it has escaped governance attention, why it is now becoming a specific accountability exposure, what the governance attention it requires looks like, and what happens when the underlying model changes in ways that affect how the library behaves. It is written for the Geneva professional who configured their AI tools with care, who invested significant time in getting the tool to behave the way their practice requires, and who is now discovering that this investment is sitting outside their accountability infrastructure.

**Structure:**

*Opening:* The scenario. A Geneva managing partner is preparing for a carrier underwriting questionnaire and realises that one of the most specific documentation assets the firm has — the prompt library the team spent months building and refining — is not part of the firm's AI governance documentation. The policy document mentions that the firm uses AI tools in client matters. The prompt library describes, in operational detail, exactly how those tools are configured to behave in specific practice areas and for specific client matter types. The gap is not that the firm has been careless about governance. It is that the most operationally significant AI governance document the firm has is not being treated as a governance document at all. This is the prompt library problem, and it is more common than the Geneva professional community acknowledges.

*Section 1 — What the prompt library actually is: practitioner methodology encoded in vendor infrastructure*
The prompt library is not a list of prompts. It is a set of deliberate methodological choices about how AI should behave in the firm's practice — encoded in the configuration settings of a tool the firm does not control. When a Geneva law firm spends three months tuning an AI tool for contract review, they are making dozens of specific decisions: how detailed the AI output should be, what contract provisions should receive the most attention, what level of risk language the AI should flag, what the firm considers a material deviation, how the AI should handle provisions outside its jurisdiction. These are professional methodology decisions. They are as specific to the firm's practice as the firm's document templates, its filing conventions, or its billing conventions — and yet they are sitting inside a vendor-controlled tool with no internal governance documentation, no version tracking, no change log, and no defined process for what happens when the underlying model changes the tool's behaviour.

*Section 2 — Why the prompt library has escaped governance attention until now*
The prompt library has escaped governance attention for a structural reason: it was built by the people who were doing the work, in the tool's interface, as a practical response to getting the AI tool to produce useful output. It was not built as a governance deliverable. It was built as a workflow optimisation. The people who built it were not thinking about AI governance documentation — they were thinking about making the tool work for the practice. As a result, the governance implications of the configuration choices were never formally examined at the time the choices were made. The prompt library accumulated as a practice-side adaptation, and it was never brought into the accountability infrastructure because the infrastructure was being built in a different part of the firm, by different people, on a different timeline.

*Section 3 — The three specific exposures created by an ungoverned prompt library*
The first exposure is the model change problem: when an AI vendor updates the underlying model, the prompt library that was built for the previous model's behaviour may produce different outputs under the same instructions — without the firm receiving explicit notice, and without the governance documentation capturing that a change has occurred. The prompt library was tuned to produce specific outputs from a specific model. When the model changes, the library may no longer produce those outputs. The firm is operating under the assumption that the AI is behaving as configured, when in fact the configuration may no longer be producing the intended behaviour. The second exposure is the professional methodology documentation problem: the prompt library represents the firm's most specific set of decisions about how AI should behave in client matters — and it is the least documented of any of the firm's practice methodologies. If the firm needs to demonstrate what its AI is instructed to do in specific client matters, the prompt library is the answer. But it is not in the file, it is not version-tracked, and it is not maintained as a governance document. The third exposure is the cross-institutional evaluation problem: when an international client or counterparty asks specific questions about the firm's AI practice in their matters, the custom instructions that govern how the AI behaves in those matters are precisely what the counterparty is asking about — whether they use that language or not. The firm that cannot produce its prompt library on request is in the same position as the firm that cannot produce its review process documentation: it has the governance in practice but not in evidence.

*Section 4 — The methodology for bringing the prompt library within the accountability infrastructure*
The governance solution is not to stop using the prompt library or to simplify it to the vendor's defaults. It is to treat the prompt library as a governance document — to maintain it as the firm maintains its other practice methodology documents, with the same attention to version tracking, change documentation, review cycles, and professional sign-off. The specific steps are: conduct a prompt library audit to identify every custom configuration currently active in the firm's AI tools, including both explicit prompt library entries and parameter settings that affect output behaviour; document each configuration as a deliberate methodological choice — what the firm decided, why it decided it, what it was designed to produce, and what the review standard is for outputs from that configuration; establish a model version impact assessment process — when the vendor announces a model update, the impact on the active prompt library configurations should be assessed before the update is accepted into production use; integrate the prompt library into the firm's client matter documentation standard — when AI-assisted work appears in a client file, the prompt library configuration used in that matter should be referenceable; and assign prompt library governance responsibility to a specific role — someone who is responsible for maintaining the library, tracking changes, conducting model impact assessments, and keeping the library in a state that would survive professional scrutiny.

*Section 5 — The Geneva international dimension: why the prompt library matters for cross-institutional evaluation*
Geneva professional firms serving international clients — diplomatic missions, international organisations, multinational corporations with Geneva offices, EU-exposed counterparties — are subject to AI governance evaluation from institutions that have their own AI governance frameworks and that evaluate their service providers against those frameworks. The prompt library is precisely what these clients are evaluating when they probe AI governance practice at the level of detail: what is the AI instructed to do in our matters, how was that instruction set, what does it produce, how do you know it is producing what you expect. The firm that can present its prompt library as a governed, version-tracked, professionally reviewed document is in a fundamentally different position than the firm that cannot. The prompt library is not just a governance document — it is the cross-institutional evaluation-ready expression of the firm's AI governance methodology. This is a Geneva-specific insight: the international client portfolio that characterises Geneva professional practice creates a specific requirement for governance documentation that is legible across institutional frameworks, and the prompt library is the most specific expression of that documentation.

*Section 6 — What firms that govern their prompt library are quietly doing differently*
The firms that have begun treating the prompt library as a governance document are not publishing this. The professional community is aware, through informal conversation, that some firms have made significant progress — but the specific governance moves those firms are making are not being shared publicly, both because the firms are cautious about creating documentation that could be used against them and because the governance of the prompt library is still too new for a consensus about best practice to have formed. What is becoming visible is the difference between firms that have brought the prompt library within the accountability infrastructure and firms that have not: the former can answer specific questions about AI behaviour in client matters, can demonstrate what the AI was instructed to do, and can track when that instruction set changed. The latter cannot — and the gap is becoming more consequential as the carrier questions sharpen and the counterparty probes deepen.

*Closing:* The prompt library is the most ungoverned significant document in most Geneva professional firms' AI practice. It contains the firm's most specific decisions about how AI should behave in client matters — and it is sitting inside a vendor-controlled tool with no governance documentation, no version tracking, and no defined process for when the underlying model changes. The solution is not to abandon the prompt library or to reduce its specificity. It is to treat it as what it actually is: practitioner methodology, encoded in vendor infrastructure, requiring the same governance attention the firm gives to its other practice methodology documents. The firms that bring the prompt library within the accountability infrastructure now will be in a different position when the next carrier questionnaire arrives, when the next international counterparty probes, and when the next client asks what the AI was instructed to do in their matter.

**Intended reader outcome:**

A Geneva managing partner finishes the article understanding what the prompt library actually is (practitioner methodology in vendor infrastructure), why it has escaped governance attention, what the three specific exposures are (model change, methodology documentation, cross-institutional evaluation), what the methodology is for bringing it within the accountability infrastructure (audit, document, assess model impact, integrate into file, assign responsibility), and why the prompt library is specifically important for Geneva firms serving international clients. They leave with a clear-eyed view of where their firm's most significant AI governance gap is and what a specific remediation process looks like.

## 06 — Findings Summary: 6 Net-New Content or Editorial Insights

*Distinct from C214–C228 Phase 05.*

**Insight 1 — The Prompt Library Is the Most Significant AI Governance Document Most Geneva Firms Have Produced and the Least Governed**
The prompt library — custom instructions, tuned parameters, output configurations built to make a general-purpose AI tool behave in specific practice-area ways — represents the firm's most specific set of deliberate methodological choices about how AI should operate in client matters. It is more operationally significant than the AI governance policy document, more specific than the tool inventory, and more directly connected to what the AI actually does in any given matter than any other governance artifact the firm has produced. Yet it is ungoverned in almost every Geneva boutique firm — not tracked for version changes, not maintained as a governance document, not integrated into the accountability infrastructure, and not assigned to anyone responsible for its maintenance. This is distinct from C228 Phase 05 (model version tracking gap) by focusing on the practitioner methodology encoded in the library, not the model version change detection problem — the library is a deliberate intellectual product requiring governance in its own right, not just a mechanism for detecting when a tool changes.

**Insight 2 — The Prompt Library Is Practitioner Intellectual Property Operating Inside Vendor-Controlled Infrastructure**
The custom instructions a Geneva firm builds into its AI tool for contract review, due diligence, client communication, or research are methodology decisions — as specific to the firm's practice as its document templates or billing conventions. They are made by the firm's professionals, reflect the firm's specific approach to its work, and produce the outputs the firm uses in client matters. They are therefore the firm's intellectual property. But they are encoded in vendor-controlled infrastructure — inside a tool whose underlying model can change without explicit notice, whose terms of service give the vendor significant control over the tool's behaviour, and whose configuration data the firm may not own in any legally clear sense. The intellectual property question is distinct from the governance question: the firm has made methodology decisions it cannot cleanly document, version-tracked, or maintain independently of a tool it does not control. This is distinct from C226 Phase 05 (accountability infrastructure gap) by addressing the specific intellectual property dimension of the prompt library as a governance object, not the four-component accountability infrastructure more broadly.

**Insight 3 — Geneva's International Client Portfolio Creates a Cross-Institutional Evaluation Requirement for Prompt Library Documentation**
Geneva professional firms serving diplomatic missions, international organisations, EU-exposed corporations, and multinational clients are subject to AI governance evaluation from institutions applying their own AI governance frameworks. These institutional clients evaluate service providers against standards calibrated to their own accountability contexts — not Swiss domestic regulatory standards. The prompt library is precisely what these clients are probing when they ask specific questions about AI practice in their matters: what is the AI instructed to do, how was that instruction set, what does it produce, how do you know. The firm that can present its prompt library as a governed, version-tracked, professionally reviewed document is legible to the cross-institutional evaluation. The firm that cannot is not. This cross-institutional evaluation dimension is distinct from C226 Phase 05 (Geneva compound standard for accountability infrastructure) by focusing on the specific documentation object — the prompt library — that makes the firm legible across institutional frameworks, not the general accountability infrastructure components.

**Insight 4 — The Geneva AI Governance Knowledge Economy Has an Informal Precedent Formation Mechanism That Is Not Being Documented**
The Geneva professional community is forming AI governance precedents informally — through how firms respond to carrier questionnaires, how they handle counterparty probes, how they manage client incidents involving AI output. These precedents are not published. They are circulating in the informal professional network — which firms encountered post-incident situations, how they handled the response, which carrier questions are producing real underwriting differentiation. The knowledge transfer is constrained by the same liability and competitive concerns that make AI governance documentation necessary in the first place. The result is that the precedent formation is happening without being recorded — the community is creating a body of practice that will eventually define what adequate AI governance looks like in Geneva, but that body of practice is not being documented in a way that makes it accessible to firms that need the guidance. This is distinct from C227 Phase 05 (Geneva knowledge economy paradox) by focusing on the precedent formation mechanism — how the community is quietly resolving AI governance questions through practice — not the general knowledge transfer constraint.

**Insight 5 — The Post-Incident Accountability Protocol Is the Missing Component in Geneva Boutique AI Governance Architecture**
A small number of Geneva boutique firms have encountered AI-assisted client matters that produced outcomes requiring explanation — not necessarily errors, but situations where the AI's role in the recommendation or decision was opaque when the client asked questions. These firms are not publishing their experiences, but the informal network is carrying information about who has encountered this and how they handled it. What is emerging is that no Geneva guidance exists for what a boutique firm should do in the immediate aftermath of an AI-assisted incident: what to tell the client, what to document, what to preserve, what not to destroy, when to notify the carrier, what the bar association guidance is (there is none in operational terms), and what the distinction is between a response that demonstrates accountability and a response that creates additional exposure. The post-incident protocol is the missing component in Geneva boutique AI governance architecture — it is the one governance question that most firms have not thought about until they need it.
