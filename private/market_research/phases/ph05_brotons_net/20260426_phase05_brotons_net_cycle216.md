## 01 — ICP Language: How Readers Describe Their AI Governance Information Needs

### English

**What they are trying to understand:**
- "We have attended several AI governance events this year in Geneva. What do we now do with what we heard — there was no implementation roadmap attached."
- "Our clients are asking us whether we use AI in their matters. We do not have a firm position on what to say or whether we need a policy."
- "I have read three different Swiss AI governance frameworks in the past six months. They all say different things. Which one actually matters for my practice?"
- "Our AI tools were updated last month and the output changed. Are we supposed to document that? How?"
- "The Geneva international organizations seem to have more developed AI governance policies than we do. Should we be looking at what they are doing?"
- "We are getting pressure from two directions — clients asking about AI competence, counterparties asking about AI due diligence. These feel like different conversations."

**What confuses them:**
- Why Geneva hosts so many AI governance conversations but produces so little concrete guidance for practitioners — they experience the gap between Geneva's international platform role and domestic professional reality
- How to distinguish between AI governance as a genuine professional obligation and AI governance as a marketing exercise practiced by firms that want to appear modern
- Whether their existing professional conduct obligations (confidentiality, competence, loyalty) already cover what AI governance requires, or whether AI creates genuinely new obligations
- The gap between what AI governance frameworks recommend and what Geneva professional culture will actually accept — they are aware that some practices they have adopted are not yet shared by their peers
- Why Geneva's AI governance information environment feels so fragmented — legal, finance, consulting, and NGO professionals have almost no shared reference points
- How to explain AI governance decisions to clients without either overpromising AI capability or undermining confidence in human judgment

**What they distrust:**
- Content that frames AI governance as a technology compliance checklist rather than a professional responsibility practice
- Vendor-produced "AI governance" content that turns out to be basic data protection compliance with a new label
- Regulatory trackers that document what exists without helping professionals decide what to do next
- Content that assumes Geneva professionals already have dedicated AI governance resources, IT teams, or compliance departments
- Overly optimistic narratives about Geneva's AI leadership that do not reflect the genuine uncertainty professionals experience when they try to apply international standards locally
- Content that treats Geneva's AI governance situation as equivalent to Zurich's or Bern's without acknowledging Geneva's specific international complexity

**Language they actually use:**
- "AI competence" — not "AI literacy" or "AI awareness" — they mean the standard of professional competence as it applies to AI tool use in their domain
- "Going to need a policy" — the informal recognition that AI use in their practice needs some form of documented governance
- "What are our peers doing" — the real reference group is Geneva senior professionals, not global best practice standards
- "AI accountability chain" — who is responsible when AI is used in a multi-step process involving several advisors or tools
- "Client has their own AI" — the new situation where clients are arriving with AI-generated documents, AI-assisted research, or AI-derived advice that the professional must now evaluate
- "What did Geneva actually decide" — frustration with the gap between Geneva's platform role and concrete outcomes for local practice

## 05 — Editorial Content Draft: Brotons.net Article Proposal

### Proposal A (Lead Essay Format — English)

**Title:** The Client Arrives with AI: Geneva Professionals and the Emerging Problem of AI-Generated Work Product

**Angle:** A quiet professional responsibility problem is emerging in Geneva's legal, financial, and advisory practices. Clients — particularly those working with international counterparties, EU institutions, or multinationals — are increasingly arriving at meetings and in communications with AI-generated documents, AI-assisted analysis, and AI-derived recommendations. The professional must now evaluate, respond to, rely on, or correct this material. Nobody in Swiss professional practice has addressed what this situation requires in terms of professional responsibility, documentation, or client communication. This article names the problem clearly, maps the professional responsibility questions it creates, and provides Geneva practitioners with a practical framework for handling client-generated AI work product.

This is not about using AI in your own practice. It is about the distinct professional responsibility questions that arise when the professional is on the receiving end of AI-generated content — evaluating it, building on it, or responding to it — and what Swiss professional obligations require in that context.

**Structure:**
1. The problem as it is actually manifesting: what Geneva professionals are experiencing when clients arrive with AI-generated documents and analysis
2. The professional responsibility questions that arise: competence obligation when evaluating AI output, confidentiality when reviewing client-submitted AI material, loyalty obligation when client AI may be incomplete or biased, documentation of the evaluation process
3. What Swiss professional conduct standards require — or do not yet require — when a Geneva professional evaluates AI-assisted client submissions
4. A practical framework for Geneva practitioners: how to document the evaluation of client-generated AI material, what questions to ask, what to record in the file
5. What to communicate to clients about how their AI-generated submissions will be handled — and why this communication itself is becoming a professional practice requirement
6. The Geneva-specific dimension: how Geneva's international professional community is handling this problem, and what peer practice is revealing about emerging professional norms

**Intended reader outcome:** The reader leaves with a clear understanding of the specific professional responsibility questions created by client-generated AI, and a practical framework for handling these situations in their practice. They understand that this is a distinct problem from AI tool use in their own work — and that it requires its own documentation and client communication approach. They feel more equipped to handle the next client who arrives with AI-generated material.

## 06 — Findings Summary: 6 Net-New Content and Editorial Insights (Distinct from C214 and C215 Phase 05)

**C214 Phase 05 produced:** July 2026 Geneva event concentration narrative, Geneva AI sovereignty operationalization for professional services, humanitarian AI governance editorial vertical, newsletter structured briefing format, AI incident response gap (reactive), Apertus AI Swiss domestic infrastructure narrative.

**C215 Phase 05 produced:** Documentation imperative shift (framework → file), post-deadline scenario planning, EN/FR professional information divide, Geneva jurisdiction complexity, PL insurance AI documentation gap, Geneva as living laboratory for international standards.

**C216 Phase 05 — Net-new findings:**

**1. The "client arrives with AI" professional responsibility problem**
When clients submit AI-generated documents, analysis, or recommendations for a Geneva professional to evaluate, rely on, or respond to — this creates a distinct category of professional responsibility question that no Swiss publisher has addressed. The professional must evaluate AI output they did not generate, may be responsible for errors they did not introduce, and has no guidance on what documentation the evaluation process requires. This is distinct from both the "AI incident response gap" (C214) and the "documentation imperative" (C215) — those address AI use within the professional's own practice. The client-generated AI problem addresses what happens when the professional is on the receiving end of AI output. Brotons.net can name and frame this problem before any other Swiss publisher.

**2. The Geneva peer practice norm as the actual working AI governance standard**
In the absence of formal regulation, Geneva's professional culture is developing informal AI governance norms through conference conversations, bar association discussions, and peer networks. These norms — what "responsible AI use" means in Geneva legal, financial, and advisory practice — are more determinative of actual professional behavior than formal frameworks. Nobody is documenting this phenomenon or analyzing what it means for the evolution of Swiss AI governance practice. This is a genuine insight about how Geneva professional culture is functioning as a regulatory substitute, and it has significant implications for how Brotons.net positions its content and community.

**3. AI tool version control and update documentation as an emerging professional practice requirement**
Geneva professionals are noticing that AI tool updates change outputs — sometimes materially. They have no framework for deciding when and how to document these changes in their professional files. This is distinct from the general "documentation imperative" (C215) because it addresses a specific, recurring operational problem: what the professional must record when the AI system they rely on changes mid-matter. The version control documentation question is beginning to surface in professional liability conversations and is not addressed in any Swiss AI governance content. Brotons.net can produce the first practitioner-oriented piece on this specific problem.

**4. Geneva's international professional community as a distinct AI governance environment**
The AI governance questions facing Geneva's IOs, NGOs, diplomatic missions, and multinationals based in Geneva are structurally different from those facing Swiss domestic firms. International organizations face donor accountability AI, cross-border data localization requirements from member states, and AI procurement policies mandated by headquarters. These institutions are ahead of Swiss domestic firms on AI governance maturity — but nobody is documenting what Geneva professionals can learn from how the international community handles AI governance, or what the structural differences mean for professional responsibility. This is distinct from C214's "humanitarian AI governance vertical" — which was about content for the humanitarian sector — by addressing what the humanitarian and international sector AI governance experience can teach Geneva domestic practitioners.

**5. AI vendor contract provisions as a professional practice document**
Geneva professionals are signing AI vendor agreements without understanding the provisions that most affect their professional liability position: data retention terms, model update disclosure, liability carve-outs, indemnification scope, and audit rights. No Swiss publisher has produced practitioner-facing content on what these contract provisions mean in the context of professional services — not as legal review checklists but as professional practice documents. This is distinct from C214's "AI incident response gap" (what to do when something goes wrong) by addressing the contractual provisions that determine whether anything is covered when something goes wrong. It is also distinct from C215's "PL insurance AI gap" (coverage questions) by addressing vendor contract terms rather than insurance policy terms.

**6. The Geneva AI governance platform credibility gap: what the city's international role has actually delivered for practitioners**
Geneva hosts more AI governance standard-setting activity than any other European city. But the gap between what Geneva produces at the international level and what Geneva practitioners need at the domestic level has become a source of quiet frustration. Nobody has published a clear-eyed, honest assessment of this gap — what Geneva's platform role has genuinely delivered for local professional practice, and what it has not. This kind of honest, critical editorial voice is exactly what Brotons.net is positioned to provide. It is distinct from C215's "Geneva as living laboratory" finding because that finding was optimistic — the laboratory produces standards that flow to domestic practice. This finding is more critical: the laboratory produces standards that often do not reach practitioners who need them. The credibility gap framing is a stronger editorial position because it matches the ICP's actual experience.
