## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### English

**Executive director on program-team AI governance absorption:**
"Our program team manages AI as part of their program delivery. When we need to respond to a donor report or a data subject request, the program team handles that too. The governance work is not a separate function — it is absorbed into program time. We do not have a governance department. The people doing governance are the same people delivering programs."

**On beneficiary nFADP rights requests arriving without infrastructure to respond:**
"Beneficiaries have started asking what data we hold about them and what decisions were made about them by AI. We do not have a process for answering those questions. We are trying to figure out what we are supposed to produce and in what timeframe. The legal obligation is clear. The infrastructure to discharge it does not exist."

**On the AI tool adopted for monitoring now being used for beneficiary communication:**
"We selected the AI tool for monitoring and data collection. The tool also has communication capabilities. Staff started using it for beneficiary communication because it was available. The governance review we did for monitoring did not cover communication use. The same AI tool is now making decisions about how it communicates with beneficiaries. We did not approve that use."

**On donor visibility requirement and what disclosing AI outputs to donors actually means for beneficiary confidentiality:**
"The donor wants visibility into how AI is used in the program. We need to show what the AI produces. But the AI output is about specific beneficiaries. When we show the donor what the AI decided about a beneficiary, we are disclosing beneficiary information to the donor. We are caught between donor accountability and beneficiary confidentiality. The grant agreement does not resolve this — it asks us to do both."

**On implementing AI governance when the same staff are responsible for program delivery:**
"We have a governance problem. The person who needs to govern the AI is the same person running the program. They do not have separate governance capacity. When the AI system changes, that person is the only one who knows what changed. When they leave, we lose what we know about the system."

## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: AI governance work absorbed into program delivery time with no category distinction**
Governance tasks — donor report preparation, data subject request responses, vendor update evaluations — absorbed into program team time without separate budget category. Organizations with most complex AI deployments experiencing largest governance-driven capacity displacement invisible in program metrics. The absorption is structural, not a staffing problem.

**Signal 2: Dual-use AI tool scope creep from monitoring into communication without governance review**
AI tools selected for specific functions (monitoring, data collection) expanding into adjacent functions (beneficiary communication, decision notification) without separate governance review. The scope expansion is staff-driven, not management-directed. The governance review done at selection does not cover use evolution.

**Signal 3: Donor visibility and beneficiary confidentiality structurally in conflict for AI outputs**
Donor accountability requiring AI output visibility while beneficiary confidentiality requiring data protection operate on the same AI outputs simultaneously. No Geneva NGO has a framework for resolving this — the grant agreement assumes both can be satisfied, but AI outputs about specific beneficiaries cannot be disclosed to donors without disclosing beneficiary data.

**Signal 4: Data subject request response infrastructure entirely absent at Geneva NGO scale**
nFADP data subject requests for AI-inferred data arriving without any response infrastructure. Organizations do not have process for determining what data exists, what was inferred, what decisions were made, or in what timeframe to respond. The legal obligation is operative; the discharge mechanism is not.

**Signal 5: Program-staff AI knowledge concentration creating succession-level governance gap**
AI configuration and operational knowledge concentrated in the program staff who configured and run systems. No protocol for transferring this knowledge separately from general program operations. Staff departure = AI knowledge departure with no organizational copy.

**Signal 6: Geneva humanitarian AI standards operating as compliance substitute without legal basis**
UNAIWG/ICRC/OCHA frameworks applied as compliance framework in grant agreements and donor assessments, interpreted as equivalent to Swiss regulatory compliance. The frameworks are operational guidelines, not legal compliance certifications. Organizations following Geneva AI standards believing they satisfy nFADP obligations may be不知道自己 in compliance gap.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: NGO-specific AI governance infrastructure design service**
No service offers governance infrastructure design calibrated to NGO staffing reality — where the same staff do program delivery and governance without separate functions. The design would need to address how to build governance without governance department, how to create review triggers that do not require additional staff, and how to document decisions without taking program time.

**Gap 2: Dual-use AI tool scope governance review as discrete service**
No service exists for reviewing AI tools operating across multiple functions and whether use has expanded beyond approved scope. The review would need to detect staff-driven scope expansion before it creates compliance exposure and document the evaluation for donor accountability purposes.

**Gap 3: Donor visibility / beneficiary confidentiality conflict resolution framework for AI outputs**
No framework exists for resolving the structural conflict between donor AI visibility requirements and beneficiary data protection when both apply to the same AI outputs. The framework would need to address what can be disclosed to donors without violating beneficiary confidentiality, how to document the resolution, and how to design programs to avoid the conflict from program design stage.

**Gap 4: nFADP data subject request response process for AI-inferred beneficiary data**
No process architecture exists for small NGO operations to respond to nFADP data subject requests for AI-inferred data. The architecture would need to cover data inventory (what was inferred), decision explanation (what the AI decided and why), timeframe compliance, and documentation that satisfies the legal obligation without requiring dedicated compliance staff.

**Gap 5: AI knowledge succession protocol for program staff concentration**
No protocol exists for managing AI knowledge transfer when configuration knowledge is held by the program staff who built the system. The protocol would need to address how to document AI configuration knowledge separately from general program knowledge, how to create organizational knowledge that survives staff departure, and how to maintain this without requiring governance staff.

**Gap 6: Geneva humanitarian AI standards vs Swiss regulatory compliance gap mapping**
No mapping exists between Geneva humanitarian AI standards (UNAIWG/ICRC/OCHA) and Swiss nFADP/FADP obligations. Organizations following Geneva standards for compliance purposes may be不知道自己 whether those standards satisfy Swiss law. The mapping would need to identify where the frameworks diverge, where following one creates exposure under the other, and how to satisfy both simultaneously.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**ZEWO certification covers financial governance, not AI governance:**
ZEWO certification examines organizational structure, financial management, and use of funds against declared purpose. It does not examine AI governance adequacy, data protection architecture for beneficiary data, or AI decision documentation for program delivery. NGOs with valid ZEWO certification may have active AI governance gaps certification does not surface. Donors interpreting ZEWO certification as adequate AI governance signal are operating on incorrect assumptions.

**Institutional donor AI conditions creating simultaneous incompatible obligations:**
Institutional donors inserting AI governance conditions into grant agreements on different timelines, with different evidentiary formats, sometimes requiring disclosure of precisely the information beneficiary confidentiality obligations protect. The cascade effect means aggregate obligations no individual donor intended. Organizations receiving multi-donor funding navigating non-harmonized requirements simultaneously without coordination mechanism.

**nFADP Articles 13 and 21 specifically applicable to AI-inferred beneficiary data:**
Art. 13 requires explanation of automated decisions affecting data subjects. Art. 21 requires documentation of AI-assisted decision logic. For beneficiary data that was not directly collected but inferred through AI analysis, these obligations activate in ways directly collected data does not. NGOs have not designed systems with these obligations in mind. The data subject request arriving for AI-inferred beneficiary data will expose the compliance gap.

**FADP (status as of 2024-2025 transition) NGO operational obligations:**
FADP likely in force or approaching implementation, creating Swiss domestic law obligations alongside or replacing nFADP for some organizations. The transition creates compliance ambiguity: which framework applies, what transitional obligations exist, whether existing AI systems need redesign to satisfy new requirements. Small NGOs with limited legal capacity navigating the transition without advisory support designed for their specific situation.

**Art. 75 ZGB board member personal liability for AI governance oversight:**
Board members approving AI strategies face personal liability under Art. 75 ZGB for inadequate oversight. The liability attaches regardless of whether the organization has a governance department. Board members approving AI strategies they do not have mechanism to review between annual presentations face active personal exposure. The board governance gap is a fiduciary duty problem, not a documentation problem.

**Geneva humanitarian AI standards ecosystem operating as compliance substitute without legal equivalence:**
UNAIWG/ICRC/OCHA frameworks developed in Geneva and applied globally create a parallel accountability ecosystem operating independently of Swiss regulatory review. Organizations following Geneva standards for donor compliance purposes may be不知道自己 in Swiss law compliance position. No published mapping exists of the relationship between Geneva standards and Swiss obligations. The gap is structural and unnamed.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Program-team AI governance absorption as structurally invisible governance failure**
Governance tasks absorbed into program delivery time without separate budget category or function distinction. Organizations with most complex AI deployments experiencing largest governance-driven capacity displacement invisible in program metrics. The absorption is structural, not a staffing problem — it results from NGO operational design, not from lack of governance awareness. Distinct from AI cost invisibility (C272 Finding 5) which addressed cost classification; this finding addresses governance work displacement, a different failure mode.

**Finding 2: Dual-use AI tool scope expansion without governance review as named structural exposure**
AI tools selected for one function (monitoring) expanding into adjacent functions (beneficiary communication) without separate governance review. Staff-driven scope expansion operates below management visibility. The governance review done at tool selection does not cover use evolution over time. Distinct from field AI override documentation (C271 Finding 6) which addressed manual overrides of AI outputs; this finding addresses organic scope expansion of the tool's own functions without override.

**Finding 3: Donor visibility and beneficiary confidentiality structural conflict for AI outputs as discrete governance problem**
Donor AI visibility requirements and beneficiary data protection obligations applying to the same AI outputs create a structural conflict no Geneva NGO has resolved. Grant agreements assuming both can be satisfied simultaneously are operating on incorrect premises. The conflict cannot be resolved at implementation — it must be resolved at program design stage. Distinct from donor visibility inversion (C262) which named documentation creating evidentiary records; this finding names the specific structural incompatibility between donor accountability and beneficiary confidentiality as applied to AI outputs, a different mechanism.

**Finding 4: nFADP data subject request response infrastructure entirely absent at small NGO scale**
Data subject requests for AI-inferred beneficiary data arriving without any response infrastructure. Organizations do not have process for determining what data was inferred, what decisions were made, or in what timeframe to respond. The legal obligation is operative; the discharge mechanism does not exist. Distinct from AI-inferred data disposal (C266) which addressed disposal obligations; this finding addresses the specific response infrastructure absence for data subject requests, a different operational moment and mechanism.

**Finding 5: Program-staff AI knowledge concentration creating succession-level governance gap without organizational copy**
AI configuration knowledge held by program staff with no organizational backup. Staff departure = AI knowledge departure. No protocol exists for separating AI configuration knowledge from general program knowledge and creating an organizational copy. Distinct from staff departure AI institutional knowledge loss (C262 Finding 2) which named the problem; this finding identifies the specific concentration mechanism and the absence of any organizational knowledge transfer protocol as structural gap requiring discrete solution architecture.

**Finding 6: Geneva humanitarian AI standards functioning as compliance substitute without legal equivalence to Swiss law**
UNAIWG/ICRC/OCHA frameworks applied as compliance framework in grant agreements and donor assessments, interpreted as satisfying Swiss regulatory obligations. The frameworks are operational guidelines, not legal compliance certifications. Organizations following Geneva standards may be不知道自己 in Swiss law compliance gap. No published mapping exists between Geneva standards and nFADP/FADP obligations. Distinct from Geneva humanitarian AI standards ecosystem (C269 Finding 5) which named the parallel framework existence without Swiss law mapping; this finding identifies the specific compliance substitution mechanism: organizations using Geneva standards as if they were legally equivalent to Swiss data protection obligations, creating undisclosed compliance exposure.
