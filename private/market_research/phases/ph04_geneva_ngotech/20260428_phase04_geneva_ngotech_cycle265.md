## 01 — ICP Language: How This ICP Actually Describes AI Problems

### English

**On AI as program mandate assumption:**
"We received a grant that required our AI-assisted targeting system to identify beneficiaries. The AI was central to how the program operated. We signed the grant agreement. The AI vendor then changed the model. The new model identifies different people. We cannot deliver the program as described in the grant. We did not negotiate any AI continuity protection in the grant agreement. We did not know we needed to."

**On field worker AI judgment override as undocumented personal exposure:**
"Field staff are making decisions that the AI recommended against. They override the AI because they see something the model does not see. When something goes wrong, the organizational record shows we had an AI system and the staff made a different decision. We have no documentation of what the staff saw that the AI missed. We are exposed for decisions we cannot reconstruct."

**On AI evidence as donor reporting currency:**
"Donors are asking for evidence that AI-assisted programs are achieving results. We can show outputs. We cannot show the reasoning chain that produced them. We are being asked to demonstrate program integrity when our main tool for generating program integrity evidence is itself not verifiable. The AI generates metrics we cannot reconstruct in a way that satisfies the evidentiary standard donors are beginning to require."

**On Geneva office AI authority versus operational reality:**
"We are the Geneva office. Decisions about AI tools are made at headquarters. We implement what headquarters decides. When something goes wrong in the field because of an AI decision made at headquarters, the accountability arrives at the Geneva office. We do not have the authority to change what headquarters has decided. We are accountable for decisions we did not make and cannot reverse."

**On AI governance overhead as program displacement:**
"We hired a compliance consultant to help us with AI governance. The consultant produced 40 pages of documentation. The documentation sits in a folder. Our programs did not become more accountable. The time and budget spent on governance documentation did not produce governance. We have less capacity to deliver programs because governance absorbed resources that programs needed. The problem is not that we lack documents. The problem is that governance overhead is consuming the capacity governance was supposed to protect."

**On beneficiary AI output contestation:**
"A beneficiary contested an AI-assisted eligibility decision. The AI had assessed the beneficiary and recommended non-eligibility. Staff agreed and the beneficiary was not enrolled. The beneficiary asked for an explanation. We could not provide one in the form the law requires. The AI produced a score. The score did not come with a reason. We enrolled the beneficiary manually after the fact. The AI system is still operating in the same way. The next contestation will arrive without any architecture for responding."

## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

**Signal 1: Grant agreements are embedding AI continuity risk that NGOs do not know they are accepting**
Grant agreements committing to AI-assisted program delivery do not contain AI continuity provisions. When vendors update, deprecate, or exit, the NGO is in breach of grant terms. The exposure is invisible at the time of signature.

**Signal 2: Field worker AI override documentation is a personal liability surface with no organizational protocol**
Field workers overriding AI recommendations are making organizational decisions without documentation architecture. When override decisions produce adverse outcomes, the undocumented reasoning chain becomes the liability exposure. No Geneva NGO has an organizational protocol for this.

**Signal 3: Donor AI evidence standards are arriving before any methodology for AI-assisted evidence production exists**
Donors beginning to require verifiable reasoning chains for AI-assisted program outcomes face a structural problem: the AI tools generating program evidence do not produce reconstructable reasoning chains. The evidentiary gap is methodological, not technological.

**Signal 4: Geneva office AI authority gap is a specifically Swiss structural phenomenon**
Geneva offices of international organizations are accountable for AI decisions made by parent entities they cannot govern. The accountability-without-authority configuration has no published resolution pathway under Swiss association law.

**Signal 5: AI governance overhead is producing documents not governance, consuming program capacity**
The compliance consultant model produces documentation without operational change. NGOs are spending governance budgets on documents that sit in folders while programs remain ungoverned. The overhead problem is distinct from the cost problem — it is a structural displacement of program capacity.

**Signal 6: AI-assisted eligibility contestation architecture is absent and activating**
Beneficiary contestation of AI-assisted eligibility decisions is activating nFADP Art.13 explanation rights NGOs cannot discharge. The next wave of contestation will arrive without any response architecture. The structural failure is that AI scoring systems are not designed with explanation architecture as an operational requirement.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Specific Obligations

### ZEWO Certification AI Accountability Requirements
ZEWO certification for Swiss humanitarian and development NGOs includes accountability standards that intersect with AI program delivery. Current ZEWO audit practice reviews financial accountability documentation; AI-assisted program decisions are not yet a formal audit category. The intersection of AI-assisted beneficiary selection, program delivery metrics, and financial reporting creates a documentation architecture gap that ZEWO audits do not yet probe systematically — but that institutional donors increasingly do through AI governance questionnaires attached to grants.

### Institutional Donor AI Framework Obligations
Institutional donors — including SDC, cantonal development funds, and international funders requiring AI governance documentation — are introducing AI conditions in grant agreements. The conditions require documented AI governance positions, evidence of board-level oversight, and AI system inventory documentation. These obligations are arriving in grant agreements now, before the Federal Council AI regulatory framework crystallizes, and before any Geneva methodology for AI governance documentation for resource-constrained NGOs exists. The binding date is grant renewal, not regulatory announcement.

### FADP (nFADP) AI-Inferred Beneficiary Data
Under the revised Federal Data Protection Act (nFADP), AI-inferred beneficiary profiles constitute personal data requiring lawful basis, purpose limitation, and data subject rights compliance. For NGOs deploying AI for beneficiary identification, eligibility assessment, or program targeting, the synthetically inferred data activates Art.13 explanation rights and Art.15 access rights that most AI systems cannot discharge in the form required. The compliance gap is structural — AI tool architecture predates nFADP design assumptions — and activates now, not upon Federal Council deliberation.

### Swiss Association Law (ZGB) Board Accountability for AI Decisions
Articles 75–79 ZGB establish board member personal fiduciary duty that extends to oversight of organizational AI deployment. Board members approving AI programs they cannot substantively evaluate face personal liability exposure without a defined discharge mechanism. The Geneva NGO configuration — part-time board members, limited IT capacity, AI programs designed elsewhere — creates a structural exposure that current board documentation practice does not address.

### International Organization Geneva Office AI Accountability
Geneva offices of international organizations face a specific multi-framework accountability gap: Swiss association law obligations apply to the Geneva entity, while AI tool selection and governance frameworks are determined by headquarters in other jurisdictions. The Geneva office bears accountability for AI decisions it did not make and cannot govern. No published Swiss law resolution pathway exists for this structural configuration.

## 06 — Findings Summary: Six Net-New Insights

**Finding 01: AI continuity risk embedded in grant agreements is a pre-signature exposure no advisory addresses**
Grant agreements committing to AI-assisted program delivery are accepting AI vendor continuity risk without AI continuity provisions. The exposure crystallizes silently at signature and activates on vendor model change, tool deprecation, or vendor exit — not on any Geneva-defined trigger. No Geneva market participant offers pre-signature grant agreement AI continuity review as a discrete service. The service requires combining grant agreement review, AI vendor stability assessment, and migration obligation mapping — a specific combination no current advisory addresses. This is distinct from all prior C247–C264 Phase 04 findings on NGO AI governance gaps.

**Finding 02: Field AI override documentation is a named personal liability surface for field workers with no organizational protocol**
Field workers overriding AI recommendations are making undocumented decisions at the point of highest program consequence. The decisions are organizational — they affect beneficiaries — but the documentation architecture is absent. When override decisions produce adverse outcomes, the organization cannot reconstruct what the worker observed that the AI missed. The gap is distinct from incident reporting: it is a real-time documentation requirement for a named category of decisions that is structurally separate from both AI governance and field operations reporting. No Geneva NGO has a protocol. No Geneva advisory addresses this gap specifically.

**Finding 03: Donor AI evidence standards are arriving before any methodology for AI-assisted evidence production exists**
Institutional donors are beginning to require verifiable reasoning chains for AI-assisted program outcomes. The requirement is methodological before it is technological: donors want to understand how the AI produced its conclusions, not just what the conclusions were. AI tools generating program evidence do not produce reconstructable reasoning chains by design. The methodology gap — how to architect AI-assisted program delivery to produce donor-quality evidentiary documentation — is absent from the Geneva market. This is distinct from the AI governance documentation gap covered in prior cycles: it concerns evidence production architecture specifically, not general governance documentation.

**Finding 04: Geneva international org HQ/branch AI accountability attribution mapping is a specifically Swiss structural exposure with no published resolution**
Geneva offices of international organizations are accountable under Swiss association law for AI decisions made by parent entities outside Swiss jurisdiction. The accountability-without-authority configuration has no published resolution pathway: Swiss courts cannot reach HQ decisions, Swiss law applies to the Geneva entity, and the Geneva entity cannot govern what HQ decides. This is distinct from the general "accountability without agency" finding covered in prior cycles — it is specifically the multi-jurisdictional dimension that makes the problem structurally unsolvable under current frameworks without a named resolution approach.

**Finding 05: AI governance overhead displacement is a distinct failure mode from governance absence, consuming program capacity without producing accountability**
The compliance consultant model — producing documents while programs remain ungoverned — represents a structural displacement failure distinct from governance absence. NGOs are spending governance budgets on documentation that does not produce governance, while program operations continue without governance architecture. The problem requires a diagnostic that distinguishes documentation theater from operational governance and measures governance overhead against program capacity. This is distinct from the AI cost invisibility finding covered in prior cycles: it concerns the efficiency and displacement of governance investment, not the visibility of AI costs.

**Finding 06: AI-assisted eligibility explanation discharge architecture is a pre-deployment design requirement that NGOs are discovering reactively**
Beneficiary nFADP Art.13 explanation rights for AI-assisted eligibility decisions require explanation architecture designed before deployment — not retrospective documentation. NGOs deploying AI scoring systems without explanation architecture are accumulating Art.13 exposure that activates on each contestation. The architecture requires that AI tool evaluation include explanation capacity as a procurement requirement, not an afterthought. No Geneva advisory addresses pre-deployment explanation architecture review as a discrete service. This is distinct from the beneficiary consent form architecture gap covered in prior cycles: it concerns the AI tool's explanation output specifically, not the consent form language.
