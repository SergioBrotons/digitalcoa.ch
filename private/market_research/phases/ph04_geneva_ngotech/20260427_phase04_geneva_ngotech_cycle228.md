## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

1. **Inherited AI debt from institutional mandate decisions — field absorbs HQ AI choices** — International organizations headquartered in Geneva make AI tool decisions at the institutional level. Field offices — including Geneva-based program offices — are required to use tools they did not select, cannot modify, and cannot govern. The accountability for those AI decisions runs to the field while the control remains at headquarters. This creates a structurally distinct AI governance problem: the entity accountable cannot govern, and the entity governing does not bear accountability.

2. **ZEWO certification AI expectations arriving as external forcing function, not internal initiative** — Swiss nonprofit certification (ZEWO) is beginning to include AI governance expectations in audit scopes, without publishing specific standards. NGOs with ZEWO certification face an audit question they cannot prepare for because the adequacy standard does not exist yet. The forcing function is external and imprecise — they will know they failed only when the audit reveals the gap.

3. **Aggregate donor reporting and FADP anonymization in direct conflict at the data layer** — Institutional donors require beneficiary-level aggregate reporting — anonymized but granular enough to demonstrate program impact. Swiss data protection (FADP) requires anonymization standards that may prevent re-identification even for legitimate program reporting purposes. These requirements conflict not in disclosure language but in the data architecture itself. The organization is trying to satisfy both by constructing datasets that are architecturally incompatible.

4. **Personal AI infrastructure becoming organizational infrastructure through informal use patterns** — Staff bring personal AI tools — on personal devices, personal subscriptions — into organizational workflows. Over time, these tools become the standard communication channel with beneficiaries without any governance review. The organization now depends on personal AI infrastructure for program delivery. The governance surface is invisible because it lives on personal devices and personal accounts.

5. **FADP 72-hour breach notification window structurally impossible for NGO detection capacity** — Swiss data protection requires breach notification within 72 hours of discovery. NGOs serving vulnerable populations process beneficiary data through cloud-based AI tools with no monitoring infrastructure. A breach can occur and persist indefinitely before detection. The compliance obligation is not merely difficult — it is architecturally impossible without monitoring infrastructure that does not exist at NGO scale.

6. **Participation paradox: Geneva-based NGOs subject to AI standards they help author** — Geneva is the global center for humanitarian AI standards-setting: ICRC AI ethics frameworks, UNAIWG vendor governance guidelines, WHO digital health AI standards. Geneva-based NGOs participate in developing these standards through institutional memberships and working groups. Those same organizations are not applying the frameworks they help produce to their own AI tool decisions. The gap between participation in standards-setting and internal compliance is unacknowledged and unmanaged.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### Gaps — What No One Is Addressing for Geneva NGO/Tech

- **Inherited AI debt governance framework for field-level accountability without field-level control** — No framework exists for managing the specific problem of AI decisions made at institutional level that field offices are required to implement. The accountability inversion — accountable without control — is not addressed in any Geneva governance guidance. Organizations are absorbing inherited AI debt without a vocabulary or methodology for managing it.

- **ZEWO certification AI readiness gap before the audit question arrives** — No advisory service prepares ZEWO-certified NGOs specifically for the AI governance expectations that will appear in their next audit. The timing gap — between the question arriving and the preparation needed to answer it — creates a specific advisory opportunity with a defined external deadline.

- **Data architecture conflict resolution between FADP anonymization and institutional donor aggregate reporting** — The conflict between FADP anonymization standards and institutional donor data requirements is architectural, not documentary. No methodology exists for resolving it at the data layer rather than the disclosure layer. Organizations are managing the symptom (disclosure language) while the structural conflict persists.

- **Personal AI tool governance migration pathway for informal organizational infrastructure** — When personal AI tools have become de facto organizational infrastructure, the governance response cannot simply be "stop using personal tools." A migration pathway is needed: from personal infrastructure to organizational infrastructure with continuity of program function. No advisory methodology addresses this specific transition challenge for NGOs.

- **AI incident detection infrastructure for capacity-constrained NGOs** — The FADP breach notification obligation requires detection capacity. No product or advisory service addresses the specific problem of building minimal viable AI incident detection for organizations that have no IT security infrastructure. The compliance obligation assumes infrastructure that does not exist and no one is building the bridge.

- **Standards participation vs. internal compliance gap for Geneva AI governance bodies** — Geneva-based NGOs that participate in international AI standards-setting have a specific governance gap: they help define what adequate AI governance looks like for the sector, without having applied those standards to their own operations. This creates reputational exposure distinct from other NGO AI governance gaps — the organization that helps define the standard and then fails to meet it.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Specific Obligations

1. **ZEWO certification AI governance expectations in audit scope without published standard** — The Swiss nonprofit certification body is beginning to examine AI governance as part of certification reviews, without having published specific adequacy criteria. ZEWO-certified NGOs face an imprecise but real expectation: the next audit may ask questions the organization cannot answer because the standard is being formed through the audit process itself. This is distinct from FADP compliance — it is certification-specific and Swiss-domestic.

2. **FADP Art. 26 breach notification: 72-hour window without detection infrastructure** — The Swiss data protection requirement to notify within 72 hours of discovering a breach assumes monitoring infrastructure that most NGOs do not have. The architectural gap: the obligation is present-tense, the capacity to fulfill it is absent, and no Geneva advisory service addresses this specific NGO-scale compliance impossibility.

3. **Inherited AI governance obligations from institutional mandate decisions** — International organizations headquartered in Geneva make AI governance decisions at the institutional level — AI tool mandates, vendor selections, data processing agreements. The obligations flow downstream to field offices and Geneva program offices without corresponding decision authority. The field office bears accountability for AI systems it did not choose and cannot modify.

4. **Aggregate beneficiary reporting vs. FADP anonymization conflict at data architecture level** — Institutional donors require beneficiary-level aggregate data demonstrating program impact. Swiss data protection requires anonymization standards that prevent re-identification even for legitimate program reporting. These requirements conflict at the data architecture level, not the disclosure level. The organization is architecturally unable to satisfy both simultaneously.

5. **Personal AI tool use in beneficiary-facing communication creating undocumented data processing** — When staff use personal AI tools for beneficiary communication, personal data enters AI systems operating under personal subscriptions. The organizational data processing that appears to be informal is legally significant: beneficiary data is being processed by AI tools with no organizational data processing agreement, no legal basis documentation, and no organizational awareness of what is being processed or where.

6. **Swiss association law Art. 69ff. board liability for inherited AI decisions** — Board members of Geneva-based international NGOs carry personal liability under Swiss association law for organizational AI decisions. When those decisions were made at institutional headquarters without Geneva board involvement, the liability exposure for the Geneva board member is present-tense and documented: the decision exists, the consequences exist, the board member was accountable for it without having participated in it.

7. **UNAIWG vendor governance alignment vs. bilateral funder AI conditions** — Geneva-based international organizations participating in UN AI governance frameworks (UNAIWG) face specific alignment obligations. Those same organizations face bilateral funder AI conditions that may conflict with UNAIWG guidance. The two requirement sets are not harmonized at the organizational level. Compliance with one may constitute breach of the other.

8. **ICRC/WHO AI ethics framework internal compliance gap for Geneva humanitarian NGOs** — Geneva-based humanitarian NGOs helped develop ICRC AI ethics guidelines and WHO digital health AI standards through working group participation. Those same organizations are not applying the frameworks they helped author to their own AI tool decisions. The gap between sector standard-setting and internal compliance is a specific reputational risk when the organization that authors the standard is found non-compliant by its own donors.

## 06 — Findings Summary

### 6 Net-New Findings (Distinct from C214–C227 Phase 04)

1. **Inherited AI debt is a structurally distinct governance problem from adoption-led governance debt** — Prior cycles identified adoption-led governance debt (C224): NGOs adopting AI tools without governance because the tools were useful. C228 identifies the distinct problem of inherited AI debt: institutional headquarters making AI decisions that Geneva-based field offices are required to implement without governance participation. The accountability inversion — accountable without control — is not a matter of adoption pace. It is a consequence of organizational structure. The field office did not adopt the AI; it was assigned the AI debt. This structural distinction requires a different remediation logic: field offices need a governance framework for inherited decisions, not just a framework for adoption decisions.

2. **ZEWO certification AI expectations are arriving through the audit process before any published standard exists** — ZEWO is examining AI governance in certification audits without having published specific adequacy criteria. The standard is being formed through the audit process — NGOs will learn what adequate means by failing the question, not by reading guidance. This creates a specific advisory opportunity: pre-audit preparation based on observed ZEWO audit patterns rather than published standards. The external deadline (next audit) is imprecise but real. The preparation window is now. This is distinct from prior cycle findings about ZEWO pressure (C214) — that addressed general ZEWO AI disclosure expectations; this specifically addresses the timing paradox of an audit standard forming through its own application.

3. **Aggregate donor reporting and FADP anonymization conflict is a data architecture problem, not a disclosure design problem** — Prior cycles identified the donor visibility vs. beneficiary autonomy conflict at the disclosure layer (C225): NGOs cannot disclose AI use without causing participation drop. C228 identifies a distinct conflict at the data architecture level: institutional donors require beneficiary-level aggregate data that can demonstrate program impact, while FADP anonymization standards prevent re-identification even for legitimate program reporting purposes. These requirements are not reconcilable through improved disclosure language. The organization is trying to construct datasets that are architecturally incompatible. The problem is not what to disclose — it is what data can legally be constructed and reported at all.

4. **Personal AI tool infrastructure becoming de facto organizational infrastructure through informal use is a data processing problem with no governance surface** — Staff using personal AI tools on personal devices for beneficiary communication is not an IT problem. It is a legally significant data processing arrangement: beneficiary personal data enters AI systems operating under personal subscriptions, with no organizational data processing agreement, no legal basis documentation, and no organizational awareness of the processing scope. The migration away from personal tools cannot be a simple prohibition — program function depends on them. A migration pathway is needed that maintains program continuity while resolving the data processing illegality. This is distinct from board member personal AI use (C226) — that addressed board-level organizational exposure; this addresses the organizational-wide problem of personal AI tools becoming embedded program infrastructure through informal use patterns.

5. **AI incident detection infrastructure is a compliance prerequisite that no one is building for NGO scale** — The FADP 72-hour breach notification obligation requires detection capacity. No product or advisory service addresses the specific problem of building minimal viable AI incident detection for organizations with no IT security infrastructure, no monitoring budget, and no IT staff. The compliance obligation assumes infrastructure that does not exist at NGO scale. The bridge — between the obligation and the capacity to fulfill it — is not being built by anyone in the Geneva advisory market. This is distinct from the breach notification gap identified in prior cycles (C215, C222) — those framed the problem as NGO inability to respond within 72 hours; this frames it specifically as the missing detection infrastructure, which is the logically prior problem.

6. **Geneva-based NGOs face a standards participation paradox distinct from the framework proportionality gap** — Geneva is the global center for humanitarian AI standards-setting. Geneva-based NGOs participate in developing frameworks through ICRC working groups, UNAIWG vendor governance processes, and WHO digital health AI standard committees. Those same organizations are not applying the frameworks they help author to their own AI decisions. The paradox: the organization that defines what adequate AI governance looks like for the humanitarian sector is itself non-compliant with frameworks it helped write. This is distinct from framework proportionality gap (C223 — frameworks assume too much capacity) and from knowledge economy gap (C226 — Geneva produces knowledge for external consumption). This is specifically about the reputational exposure that arises when an organization authors the standard and is then found non-compliant by its own donors. The conflict is between participation in standards-setting and internal compliance — not between the framework and organizational capacity.
