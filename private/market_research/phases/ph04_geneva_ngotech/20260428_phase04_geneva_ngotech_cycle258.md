## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Institutional donor AI audit convergence as a simultaneous multi-party governance stress event**

Multiple institutional donors are now sending AI governance questionnaires within overlapping time windows. The structural property is not merely the volume — it is that each questionnaire reflects a different institutional framework, asks questions calibrated to a different accountability standard, and produces answers that cannot easily be made coherent across donors without an underlying documentation architecture the NGO does not have. The NGO is answering three or four AI questionnaires simultaneously, under time pressure, without a Geneva-level response architecture that would produce coherent representations across all of them. The downstream risk: answers given under these conditions become the organizational record. If any of those donors later asks a more specific question — which donors with active AI governance programs will — the answers provided in this window will be the reference point. This is distinct from the general funder questionnaire burden identified in prior cycles. The specific dynamic is the convergence of multiple different frameworks in the same window, creating an answer coherence problem that individual questionnaire response training does not address.

**Signal 2: Post-grant AI infrastructure disposition as an unnamed live exposure with no donor framework**

When grant funding ends, the AI infrastructure funded by the grant enters a disposition question that no current grant agreement or donor AI governance framework addresses. The question encompasses: what happens to AI-inferred beneficiary profiles that were generated during the program — synthetic conclusions about individuals that exist in the AI system but may not be retrievable or deletable through standard data disposal protocols; what governance obligations survive the grant regarding AI-assisted decisions made during the program; and what continuity framework applies to beneficiaries whose ongoing case status is managed through AI inference. Donors are not currently asking this question in grant closure protocols. The NGO is not currently answering it. The eight-month pre-closure window is the moment when the question becomes addressable, but the organizational awareness that it is a named governance question has not arrived in most Geneva-based NGOs operating field programs. This is distinct from post-program data disposal as named in prior cycles — the specific dynamic here is that AI-inferred profiles survive grant termination as a live operational and legal question, not merely a data hygiene question.

**Signal 3: Legal personality ambiguity creating differential AI governance obligation mapping for Geneva international orgs**

International organizations headquartered in Geneva — those operating under international framework agreements, hosting arrangements with the Swiss government, or functional immunity configurations — face a specific structural problem when answering donor AI questionnaires and FADP data subject requests. The problem is not that Swiss AI governance frameworks apply or do not apply. It is that the organization's legal personality determines which Swiss law obligations actually apply, and this determination is non-obvious for organizations that are not purely Swiss domestic entities. Different legal personality configurations produce different obligation mappings for the same AI deployment. The donor AI questionnaire arriving in Geneva is not designed to handle this ambiguity — it asks the question as if the answer is the same across all Geneva-hosted organizations. The NGO is giving technically imprecise answers because the questions do not accommodate its legal structure. The structural property: Swiss law creates AI governance obligations for entities whose legal personality determines which obligations those are, and the frameworks that create those obligations have not been designed for organizations falling between Swiss domestic and international legal categories.

**Signal 4: Zero-resource AI governance design failure distinct from governance capacity gap**

The smallest Geneva-based NGOs — ten to thirty people, part-time IT, no dedicated compliance function — face a governance design problem that is structurally distinct from the governance capacity gap identified in prior cycles. Capacity gap framing assumes the governance architecture exists and the NGO lacks the resources to implement it. The actual problem for the smallest NGOs is that the governance architecture was never designed to fit organizations of their size and structure. Existing AI governance frameworks — whether Swiss domestic, institutional donor, or international org — assume a level of compliance infrastructure that does not exist in these organizations. The governance question arrives as if there is a design to implement. The actual starting condition is that no design fits. This is distinct from resource-constrained AI governance as named in prior cycles. The specific dynamic is the structural impossibility of fitting existing frameworks to organizations that do not have the compliance architecture the frameworks assume — a design problem, not a capacity problem.

**Signal 5: AI inference explainability failure as present-tense data subject rights exposure with no discharge pathway**

When a beneficiary asks what the AI inferred about their case priority and whether that inference influenced their services, the NGO must answer. The AI tool vendor has updated the model since the beneficiary's assessment. The original inference record — the specific reasoning chain — is not preserved in any system the NGO controls. The answer the NGO must give is that they cannot answer. This is a present-tense FADP Articles 13 and 21 exposure. The beneficiary has not filed a formal complaint. The NGO knows the question is unanswerable and has not built a protocol for what to do when the answer is that the record does not exist. The exposure compounds daily as more beneficiaries are assessed and more inferences are generated that will become unexplainable when the next model update arrives. The structural property: the combination of model update cadence and inference record preservation architecture means the window for preserving the record that would answer the question closes before the organization knows it needs to preserve it. This is distinct from post-negative-outcome explainability as named in prior cycles. The specific dynamic is that the explainability failure is not triggered by a negative outcome — it is a structural property of how AI inference records are generated and destroyed in current NGO tool configurations, operating independently of whether any particular outcome was negative.

**Signal 6: Operational AI adoption chronology as governance history the organization cannot accurately reconstruct**

AI tools were adopted to solve immediate operational problems — beneficiary intake was too slow, case management was failing, reporting was unreliable. The adoption was not documented as a governance event because it was not experienced as a governance decision. The tools were configured, deployed, updated, and modified through an informal operational chronology that produced the current AI environment. The current AI environment is the result of a sequence of decisions that were made without governance framing, by people who did not know they were making governance-relevant decisions. When the donor AI questionnaire arrives, the organization must represent its AI governance history. The governance history it must represent was never created as a governance record. This is distinct from the general documentation archaeology problem identified across prior cycles. The specific dynamic is that the AI deployment chronology is not merely undocumented — it was experienced by the organization as a sequence of operational problem-solving decisions, not as governance decisions, which means the people who made those decisions did not know they were creating the governance record that would later be required.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Geneva and Swiss-specific obligations for the NGO/Tech ICP:**

**ZEWO Certification and AI Governance Documentation**

ZEWO certification for Swiss charitable NGOs includes a requirement for governance frameworks that apply to the organization's AI deployments as those deployments affect beneficiary data and program delivery. ZEWO's certification audit verifies that a governance policy exists. The certification audit does not verify that actual AI governance practices conform to the policy. For a Geneva-based NGO that has passed ZEWO certification with an AI governance policy written before current AI deployments, the certification creates a documented AI governance framework that does not describe actual AI practices. This is a specific structural property of ZEWO's current audit methodology as it applies to AI: documentation completeness is audited, governance adequacy is not. The NGO should understand that ZEWO certification creates a documented position that will be the reference point if donors or other parties ask about AI governance adequacy, and that position may not accurately describe what the organization actually does with AI.

**FADP Articles 13 and 21 — Data Subject Rights for AI-Inferred Beneficiary Information**

Articles 13 and 21 of the revised Federal Act on Data Protection create obligations for NGOs that process beneficiary data through AI-assisted systems. Article 21 specifically addresses the right to explanation for automated decisions, including AI inference about case priority, service eligibility, and program placement. The structural complexity for NGOs is that AI inference records — the specific reasoning chains that led to AI-generated conclusions about beneficiaries — may not be preserved after vendor model updates. The obligation to provide an explanation does not have a sunset clause that corresponds to the model's update cycle. The NGO bears the obligation to produce an explanation for an AI inference that was made under conditions that no longer exist in the current system. The FADP does not currently provide a safe harbor for NGOs that cannot produce inference records due to vendor architecture choices. Geneva NGOs using AI-assisted case management should treat AI inference record preservation as a present-tense operational obligation, not a documentation exercise.

**Institutional Donor AI Audit Frameworks — SDC, FDFA, EU Development Donors**

Institutional donors funding Geneva-based NGOs — including SDC, FDFA, and EU development funding mechanisms — are developing AI governance questionnaire frameworks that impose accountability requirements on grantees. These frameworks are not harmonized. They arrive with different questions, different evidence standards, and different accountability implications. For the Geneva NGO responding to multiple donor AI questionnaires, the specific risk is answer incoherence: representations made to different donors about the same AI deployments may be technically inconsistent because each donor's framework asks for different aspects of the same underlying governance position. Donors are beginning to cross-reference questionnaire responses against program reports and site visit findings. An NGO that has not built a coherent underlying governance position will find that its donor responses do not hold up under cross-reference scrutiny.

**Swiss Neutrality and AI Data Sovereignty in International Org Context**

Geneva-based international organizations operating under Swiss hosting arrangements face a specific AI governance constraint not applicable to Swiss domestic NGOs: the intersection of Swiss neutrality law, international organization privileges, and Swiss domestic AI governance law creates an accountability configuration where Swiss law obligations fall on entities whose AI decisions may be controlled by parent bodies outside Swiss jurisdiction. The specific problem is that Swiss data protection law applies to the Geneva entity's AI processing, but the AI tool configuration, model updates, and inference methodology may be controlled by an international body that does not itself operate under Swiss law. The Geneva entity bears Swiss law obligations for AI decisions it did not independently make. No current framework addresses this specific structural configuration.

**ICT4Health and Swiss Health Data Sovereignty for NGOs with Health Programs**

NGOs operating health programs in Switzerland or processing health-adjacent beneficiary data through AI tools face specific obligations under Swiss health data frameworks, including ICT4Health standards where applicable to Swiss-based health data processing. The intersection of health data protection requirements and AI-assisted beneficiary case management creates a documentation obligation that standard NGO AI governance frameworks do not address. The specific complexity is that AI tools designed for case management may process data that falls within health data categories, triggering additional Swiss law obligations that the organization's general AI governance documentation does not contemplate.

**Grant Agreement AI Provisions — Pre-Signature Capacity Assessment**

Current grant agreements are beginning to include AI governance provisions as conditionality requirements. These provisions are often designed without a methodology for verifying that the grantee has the capacity to satisfy them. The structural problem is that the obligation is imposed before the capacity assessment occurs — the grantee signs the agreement and accepts the AI governance conditions without a pre-signature review that would identify whether the conditions are structurally satisfiable given the organization's actual AI infrastructure. The Geneva NGO should treat AI governance conditionality in grant agreements as a pre-signature review obligation, not a post-signature compliance exercise. Accepting AI governance conditions in a grant agreement without a documented capacity assessment creates a documented obligation the organization may not be structurally capable of meeting.

**What this is**

A ninety-minute structured briefing for Geneva-based NGO executive directors and program managers who are navigating simultaneous institutional donor AI governance questionnaires, FADP data subject requests concerning AI-inferred beneficiary information, or the approaching termination of a grant-funded AI program.

This is not a training session. It is not a documentation template. It is a specific, structured conversation about the specific problems your organization is facing right now — what the actual exposure is, what the structural properties of the problem are, and what a defensible organizational position would look like given where you are.

The briefing maps where your donors' frameworks are compatible and where they are not, identifies what you can represent consistently across all of them, and produces a documented response framework that does not create downstream exposure when donors compare notes.

The briefing provides a protocol for managing AI inference explainability requests when the complete answer cannot be produced — and documentation practices that maximize your capacity to answer future requests before the model updates again.

*Zero-resource governance design.* If your organization is fifteen to thirty people with part-time IT and no dedicated compliance function, existing AI governance frameworks were not designed for you. The briefing works from your actual constraints backward — what governance architecture actually fits your organizational structure, what your AI tool inventory and evaluation protocol should look like, and what donor reporting on AI accountability is actually feasible to produce.

Ninety minutes. Executive director or program manager. Individual or small cohort (maximum four organizations per session, with agreement on data handling). Delivered in English or French, by appointment.

The output is a documented organizational position — not a template, not a policy document you fill in, but a specific written account of where your organization stands on the questions that matter, calibrated to your actual AI infrastructure and your actual donor relationships.

Le briefing identifie ce que vous devriez faire dans la fenêtre de subvention restante, quelle documentation vous devriez produire à la clôture, et ce que vous devriez demander à vos donateurs avant la fin de la subvention — pas après.

*Demandes d'accès aux inférences IA au titre de la nFADP.* Quand un bénéficiaire demande ce que l'IA a inféré sur son cas et comment cela a influencé ses services, et que l'enregistrement d'inférence original n'est plus disponible parce que le modèle a été mis à jour, votre organisation doit savoir quoi dire et comment le dire. Le briefing fournit un protocole pour gérer les demandes d'explicabilité des inférences IA quand la réponse complète ne peut pas être produite — et des pratiques de documentation qui maximisent votre capacité à répondre aux demandes futures avant la prochaine mise à jour du modèle.

*Conception de gouvernance à ressources nulles.* Si votre organisation compte quinze à trente personnes avec un service informatique à temps partiel et aucune fonction conformité dédiée, les cadres existants de gouvernance IA n'ont pas été conçus pour vous. Le briefing part de vos contraintes réelles à rebours — quelle architecture de gouvernance correspond réellement à votre structure organisationnelle, à quoi ressemble votre protocole d'inventaire et d'évaluation des outils IA, et quel reporting aux donateurs sur la reddition de comptes IA est réellement réalisable à produire.

## 06 — Findings Summary

Six net-new insights from C258 Phase 04, distinct from C214–C257 Phase 04:

**Finding 01: Multi-donor AI questionnaire convergence as a simultaneous coherence problem distinct from questionnaire response burden**

The market signal is not merely that institutional donors are asking AI governance questions — it is that multiple donors are asking different AI governance questions within overlapping windows, producing answer obligations that cannot be reconciled without an underlying coherence architecture the NGO does not have. The prior-cycle framing addressed funder questionnaire burden as a volume and capacity problem. The specific C258 finding is that the simultaneous arrival of non-harmonized frameworks creates an answer incoherence risk that is distinct from the burden of answering any individual questionnaire. An NGO that responds expertly to each individual questionnaire may still produce representations that are mutually inconsistent — and donors who compare notes, which sophisticated institutional donors increasingly do, will find the inconsistencies. No Geneva market participant has addressed the coherence architecture as a discrete service category.

**Finding 02: Grant-cycle AI disposition as a named governance occasion with specific obligations that no current grant agreement addresses**

The grant termination window creates a discrete governance occasion that has not been named as such in the Geneva market. The specific obligations are: what happens to AI-inferred beneficiary profiles when the grant ends; what governance documentation the NGO should produce at closure to preserve its accountability position regarding AI-assisted decisions made during the program; what continuity framework applies to beneficiaries whose ongoing case status is managed through AI inference; and what the NGO should be asking institutional donors about AI disposition expectations before the grant ends. Donors are not systematically asking these questions in grant closure protocols. The NGO is not systematically preparing for them. The eight-month pre-closure window is the specific timing occasion. This is distinct from post-program data disposal as named in prior cycles — the specific framing is the grant closure event as a named governance occasion with specific documentation obligations and timeline, not merely the general end-of-program data hygiene question.

**Finding 03: Legal personality differential obligation mapping as the prior question for Geneva international org AI governance**

For Geneva-based international organizations, the question of which Swiss AI governance obligations apply is not answered by asking what Swiss law says — it is answered by determining the organization's legal personality under Swiss international law, which determines which Swiss obligations actually apply. This determination is non-obvious for organizations operating under international framework agreements, hosting arrangements with the Swiss government, or functional immunity configurations. The prior-cycle framing addressed Geneva HQ accountability without authority for international orgs. The specific C258 finding is that the legal personality determination is the prior question — until you know which Swiss obligations actually apply to your organization, you cannot build a governance architecture that correctly maps to your obligations, and any AI governance representation you make to donors or data subjects may be technically imprecise in ways that create exposure. No Geneva market participant has built a legal personality differential mapping service for the international org segment.

**Finding 04: AI inference explainability failure as a structural property of current NGO tool architecture, not a consequence of negative outcomes**

The C257 finding on post-negative-outcome AI explainability framed the exposure as triggered by adverse beneficiary outcomes. The specific C258 finding is that the explainability failure is structurally inherent to current NGO AI tool configurations — it is not conditional on whether any particular outcome was negative. Every AI inference made under a model version that subsequently updates becomes unexplainable once the update occurs, regardless of whether the inference was correct, reasonable, or favorable to the beneficiary. The FADP Articles 13 and 21 obligations are present-tense and structural — they apply to every inference, not only to inferences associated with negative outcomes. The compounding dynamic is that each model update destroys the inference records that would have answered future data subject questions, and the organization has no protocol for preserving them. No Geneva market participant has framed AI inference explainability as structurally inherent to current NGO tool architecture, not as a consequence of adverse outcomes.

**Finding 05: Operational AI adoption chronology as undocumented governance history requiring a different reconstruction methodology**

Prior-cycle documentation archaeology framing treated undocumented AI history as a documentation gap — the records were never created and now need to be reconstructed. The specific C258 finding is that the AI deployment chronology for NGOs that adopted AI to solve operational problems is not merely undocumented — it was made by people who did not know they were making governance-relevant decisions. The reconstruction problem is not just that records are missing — it is that the decisions that produced the current AI environment were not experienced as governance decisions, which means there is no decision record to reconstruct, only an operational outcome. This requires a different reconstruction methodology than legal or financial AI documentation archaeology: not what did you decide and when, but what did you do operationally and what did it produce, reconstructed from operational records rather than governance records. No Geneva market participant has built a reconstruction methodology specific to the operational adoption chronology problem that characterizes NGO AI deployments.

**Finding 06: ZEWO certification documentation divergence as a structurally invisible adequacy signal for institutional donors**

ZEWO certification creates a documented AI governance position for the NGO that is structurally divergent from actual AI practices — the policy was written before the AI deployments it nominally governs, and the certification audit confirms policy existence, not conformance. The specific C258 finding is that this divergence creates a structurally invisible adequacy signal: the NGO has passed an authoritative external certification for AI governance, and this fact is available to institutional donors considering funding the organization. The certification is accurate as a documentation completeness measure and inaccurate as a governance adequacy measure. The NGO is not aware that the certification creates a false adequacy signal in the donor community. Institutional donors who rely on ZEWO certification as evidence of AI governance adequacy are relying on a signal that does not mean what it appears to mean. No Geneva market participant has addressed the specific dynamic of ZEWO certification as a structurally invisible adequacy signal in the institutional donor context.
