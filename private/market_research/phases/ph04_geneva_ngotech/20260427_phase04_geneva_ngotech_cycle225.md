# Phase 04 — Geneva NGO/Tech | C225
**Filed:** 2026-04-27 | **Cycle:** C225 Phase 04/06
**ICP:** Executive director or program manager at a Geneva-based NGO (10–100 employees), international organization, or tech nonprofit deploying AI for program delivery, data management, or communications. Core concerns: donor accountability, data sovereignty, reputational risk, limited IT capacity, donor visibility vs. confidentiality tension.

## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

1. **AI vendor infrastructure decay as silent data sovereignty erosion** — NGOs selected AI tools three or four years ago under different terms. Vendor infrastructure has changed — servers relocated, model training updated, terms revised — without organizational awareness. The data sovereignty the organization believed it had when signing contracts has been quietly invalidated. The organization cannot detect when or how this has happened.

2. **AI harm liability chain exposure without prevention documentation** — When donors hold NGOs responsible for AI-assisted harm to beneficiaries, the NGO has no documentation of the prevention process it followed. The absence of process documentation is indistinguishable from the absence of concern. Both look the same in an audit.

3. **Donor visibility and beneficiary autonomy in direct structural conflict** — Some donors require visibility into AI tool operations as a condition of funding. Some beneficiaries, particularly in humanitarian and vulnerable population contexts, have genuine autonomy interests in avoiding AI-processed decisions. These are not reconcilable through disclosure design. They are in direct conflict and NGOs are managing them simultaneously without a framework for resolution.

4. **Board AI mandate staleness as unmanaged governance surface** — Board approvals of AI approaches were made in an earlier technology environment. No process exists for updating those approvals when the technology changes. The board is accountable for decisions it made under conditions that no longer exist, without knowing what has changed.

5. **Compliance theater as reputation management separate from governance** — NGOs produce AI governance documents for donor requirements that have no connection to what the organization actually does. The documents serve a reputation management function, not a governance function. This is known and not resolved.

6. **AI vendor lock-in as program continuity risk at NGO scale** — AI tools selected for program delivery become embedded in operational workflows. When vendors change pricing, terminate services, or are acquired, NGOs have no fallback. The program continuity exposure is real but not priced or planned for.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### Gaps — What No One Is Addressing for Geneva NGO/Tech

- **AI infrastructure decay detection for NGO-selected tools** — No one has built a methodology for small NGOs to detect when the data sovereignty assumptions embedded in their original AI vendor selection have been quietly invalidated by vendor infrastructure changes. The detection gap is structural.

- **AI harm prevention documentation for donor accountability chains** — When harm occurs, NGOs need to demonstrate that they had a prevention process. No one has designed an AI harm prevention documentation standard appropriate for NGO scale and capacity. Existing frameworks assume legal or compliance infrastructure that does not exist.

- **Donor visibility vs. beneficiary autonomy conflict resolution framework** — The incompatibility between donor AI visibility requirements and beneficiary autonomy interests is not being addressed as a genuine conflict. No framework exists for resolving it rather than managing it.

- **Board AI mandate staleness review process** — No process exists for periodic board-level review of AI mandates as technology changes. The governance gap is temporal — decisions made under old conditions remain in force despite changed conditions.

- **AI vendor lock-in program continuity planning for NGOs** — No advisory or consulting service addresses AI vendor lock-in as program continuity risk for NGOs. The problem is treated as an IT issue, not a program governance issue.

- **Compliance theater as discrete organizational behavior** — The phenomenon of NGOs producing governance documents unconnected to actual practice is not addressed as a discrete behavior with its own risk profile. It is treated as a governance gap when it is more accurately a reputation management behavior.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Specific Obligations

1. **Donor AI governance representation audit rights** — Institutional donors (SDC, EU DEVCO, ECHO, UN agencies, major private foundations) include AI governance representations in grant agreements with contractual audit rights. Misrepresentation is a distinct exposure from non-compliance — the organization signed that it had controls it may not have.

2. **FADP Art. 22 individual rights for AI-assisted beneficiary decisions** — Beneficiaries have enforceable rights to explanation, review, and human intervention for AI-assisted decisions affecting them. NGOs serving vulnerable populations have the most acute exposure: the population with the strongest rights is the one least likely to exercise them without organizational support.

3. **ZEWO certification AI disclosure expectations** — Swiss nonprofit certification body (ZEWO) beginning to expect AI governance narrative in certification and annual reporting. The standard is developing without published guidance, creating uncertainty about what adequate disclosure means.

4. **Board personal fiduciary liability under Swiss association law Art. 69ff.** — Individual board members carry personal liability for organizational decisions under Swiss association law. AI governance decisions are board-level decisions. The personal exposure is present-tense and not addressed by standard NGO board insurance.

5. **FADP Art. 26 breach notification 72-hour window vs. NGO detection capacity** — Swiss data protection law requires notification within 72 hours of discovering a breach. Most NGOs do not have monitoring systems that can detect a breach within any timeframe, let alone 72 hours. The compliance obligation is untethered from the detection capacity required to fulfill it.

6. **AI-enhanced beneficiary due diligence under Swiss anti-terrorism financing rules** — When AI tools are used in beneficiary screening or prioritization, the organization is making decisions that may trigger enhanced due diligence obligations. The intersection of AI-assisted decision-making and Swiss anti-terrorism financing compliance is not mapped for NGO operational contexts.

7. **International org UNAIWG AI governance alignment vs. funder AI conditions** — Geneva-based international organizations face a specific tension between UN AI governance frameworks (UNAIWG) and bilateral funder AI conditions. The two requirement sets are not harmonized. Organizations answer to both without a mechanism for reconciliation.

8. **Swiss foundation Art. 83a/b/c governance obligations** — Swiss foundations with public benefit mandates have specific governance obligations that intersect with AI tool deployment. The AI governance dimension of foundation governance obligations is not published or operationalized.

## 06 — Findings Summary

### 6 Net-New Findings (Distinct from C214–C224 Phase 04)

1. **AI vendor infrastructure decay is creating silent data sovereignty erosion that NGOs cannot detect** — Organizations selected AI tools under different vendor terms and infrastructure configurations three or four years ago. Vendor infrastructure has changed — servers relocated, model training updated, sub-processors added — without organizational awareness. The data sovereignty assumptions embedded in the original vendor selection have been quietly invalidated. This is distinct from the vendor term monitoring failure identified in prior cycles; it is not about noticing changes when they are announced, but about detecting structural changes in vendor infrastructure that may not be announced.

2. **AI harm prevention documentation is the critical gap in the donor accountability chain, not AI governance policy** — Donors hold NGOs responsible for AI-assisted harm to beneficiaries. NGOs cannot demonstrate they had a prevention process because no such documentation standard exists at NGO scale. The absence of prevention documentation is indistinguishable from the absence of concern. This is distinct from prior cycle findings about FADP beneficiary rights operationalization or donor representation conformance; it is specifically about the prevention documentation that makes the accountability chain legible, not the rights themselves.

3. **Donor visibility and beneficiary autonomy are in direct structural conflict that disclosure design cannot resolve** — Some donors require AI visibility as a contract condition. Some beneficiaries, particularly in humanitarian contexts, have legitimate autonomy interests in avoiding AI-processed decisions. These requirements are not reconcilable through improved disclosure language. They are in direct conflict and NGOs are managing them simultaneously without acknowledging the conflict or having a resolution framework. This is distinct from prior findings about consent form participation drop; that was an operational symptom, this is the structural incompatibility.

4. **Board AI mandate staleness is an unmanaged governance surface with personal liability consequences** — Board approvals of AI approaches were made in an earlier technology environment. No process exists for updating those approvals when the technology changes. The board remains accountable for decisions that were made under conditions that no longer exist. This is distinct from the board AI literacy deficit addressed in prior cycles; it is not about the board's ability to understand AI, but about the temporal governance gap created when approved AI mandates become stale without review processes.

5. **Compliance theater as discrete organizational behavior has its own risk profile that is not being addressed** — NGOs produce AI governance documents for donor requirements that have no connection to what the organization actually does. The behavior is functional for reputation management in the short term but creates a specific risk: when a real governance event occurs, the gap between documented practice and actual practice becomes visible and damaging. This is distinct from prior findings about documentation gaps; it identifies compliance theater as a behavior with its own risk logic, not just a symptom of governance weakness.

6. **AI vendor lock-in as program continuity risk is structurally unaddressed in Geneva NGO advisory markets** — AI tools embedded in program delivery create dependency that becomes strategic fragility when vendors change pricing, terminate services, or are acquired. The program continuity exposure is real but treated as an IT issue rather than a governance and program integrity issue. No advisory service in Geneva addresses AI vendor lock-in for NGOs as a program governance risk with board-level implications. This is distinct from the AI vendor concentration risk identified in finance ICP cycles; the NGO manifestation is program dependency without continuity planning, not financial concentration risk.
