## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

### 2.1 AI Vendor Consolidation M&A Wave Creating Present-Tense Data Sovereignty Gaps

The 2024–2026 AI vendor consolidation wave has reached the NGO technology stack with a specific intensity that has not been named as a discrete market signal. Humanitarian AI vendors — platforms purpose-built for field data collection, beneficiary matching, crisis mapping, and program optimization — have been absorbed into larger technology conglomerates at a rate that exceeds the commercial sector average. The structural consequence for Geneva NGOs is a data sovereignty disruption arriving silently, mid-program, without notification architecture adequate to trigger a governance response.

The specific failure mode: organizations that conducted vendor due diligence at program design are operating under data processing terms negotiated with entities that no longer exist in their prior form. The acquirer inherits the processing relationship but not the governance documentation the organization built around the original terms. The gap between what organizations believe their data processing architecture to be and what it actually is compounds with each acquisition cycle. No Geneva advisory service has addressed AI vendor consolidation M&A as a specific humanitarian sector data sovereignty event.

### 2.2 Humanitarian AI Impact Assessment Format Optimizing for Metric Visibility Over Harm Reduction

Institutional donors have standardized AI impact assessment requirements across their grant application processes. The assessment formats in use optimize for improvement metric demonstration — showing that selected indicators improved during the program period. This creates a specific paradox: a program can show measurable improvement on selected metrics while causing unmeasured harm through AI-assisted decisions that the metrics do not capture.

The structural problem is that humanitarian harm is not the absence of metric improvement. Harm includes dignity violations, allocation errors that prioritize metric-compliant beneficiaries over more vulnerable populations, predictive policing effects that redirect services away from genuine need, and dependency creation that outlasts the program period. The assessment format in current use cannot surface these harm categories because it was designed to demonstrate improvement, not to detect harm. Organizations are producing assessments that satisfy the format requirement while the actual harm-reduction question goes unaddressed. The gap between format compliance and actual harm reduction is invisible until a specific incident surfaces it.

### 2.3 Post-Program AI Decision Continuity as Structural Ungoverned Territory

The program close question for AI-assisted programs has been partially addressed in prior cycles as a data disposal problem. The more acute and unnamed problem is the continuity of AI-shaped decisions after program termination. When an AI system has influenced beneficiary access to services, resource allocation, prioritization, or risk classification, those shaped decisions do not terminate when funding terminates. Downstream systems — government social services, integrated health networks, other NGOs receiving beneficiary referrals — continue to operate on the basis of AI inferences generated during the program period.

The specific market signal: Geneva-based organizations coordinating humanitarian AI deployments have no transfer-out protocol for AI-shaped decisions. The absence of a transfer-out architecture means that AI-influenced beneficiary classifications persist in systems the originating organization no longer governs, for beneficiaries the originating organization no longer serves, producing accountability for decisions that can no longer be explained or corrected. This is distinct from data disposal. Data disposal concerns records. Decision continuity concerns the operational consequences of AI-shaped categorizations that have escaped the originating governance context.

### 2.4 Geneva Humanitarian AI Coordination Concentration Without Local Accountability

Geneva hosts the highest concentration of international organization headquarters and humanitarian AI coordination functions in the world. The frameworks developed here — AI deployment standards, data sharing protocols, beneficiary data governance norms for crisis contexts — are applied across the global humanitarian AI estate. The concentration creates an accountability paradox: the decisions with the broadest humanitarian consequences are made in the jurisdiction with the least developed local accountability infrastructure for AI governance.

No Geneva-based institution reviews the adequacy of the AI governance frameworks developed by Geneva-based humanitarian organizations. No Geneva-based professional body certifies AI governance competency for humanitarian operations. The informal Geneva humanitarian AI coordination circuit operates as the de facto standard-setting mechanism for global humanitarian AI without the transparency, review, or accountability functions that formal standard-setting bodies provide. Organizations operating in this circuit receive social reassurance from peer engagement without substantive accountability evaluation.

### 2.5 Zero Professional Body Equivalent for Humanitarian AI Governance

ZEWO provides Swiss NGOs with a financial governance certification that functions as the baseline accountability signal for institutional donors evaluating organizational reliability. No equivalent certification infrastructure exists for AI governance adequacy in the humanitarian sector. The absence means that an organization's AI governance practices — the quality of its beneficiary data protection, the rigor of its algorithmic impact assessment, the adequacy of its consent architecture — carry no third-party validation signal available to donors or beneficiaries.

The structural consequence is adverse selection operating in both directions. Donors cannot differentiate between organizations with genuinely rigorous AI governance and organizations with adequate-looking documentation. Organizations with serious AI governance practices cannot distinguish themselves from organizations that have invested in presentation over substance. The market for AI governance credibility has no certification mechanism to perform the sorting function that certification performs for financial governance.

### 2.6 Multi-Year AI Infrastructure Commitment in Grant Architectures Not Designed for Governance Overhead

The standard grant agreement architecture in the humanitarian sector was designed before AI governance overhead was a recognized cost category. Multi-year AI infrastructure commitments are embedded in grant agreements that budget for technology costs as capital expenditure — hardware, software licenses, platform subscriptions — without budgeting for the human capacity required to govern AI systems adequately over the commitment period.

The specific failure mode: organizations sign AI infrastructure commitments that are fixed for the grant period while governance costs accumulate at a rate that was never projected and is not recoverable from the grant budget. The governance overhead — documentation maintenance, vendor update review, data subject response, impact reassessment — is real, recurring, and unbudgeted. Organizations discover the governance gap only when governance obligations collide with program delivery capacity. By that point, the infrastructure commitment is signed and the governance deficit is structural.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Specific Obligations

### 4.1 Federal Act on Data Protection (FADP / nFADP) — Humanitarian Operations Derogation Analysis

The nFADP applies to Swiss-based organizations processing personal data of beneficiaries, regardless of where the humanitarian operation occurs. The Act contains specific derogations for humanitarian operations that remain incompletely mapped for AI-assisted humanitarian programs. The specific compliance question for Geneva humanitarian AI deployments: which nFADP provisions apply to AI-influenced beneficiary decisions made in crisis contexts where the data subject's capacity to exercise data rights is compromised by the crisis itself?

The practical compliance gap: organizations cannot discharge Art.13 (disclosure obligation) or Art.21 (right to object to automated decision-making) obligations in crisis contexts where the beneficiary may lack access to the communications infrastructure required to exercise those rights, may lack the language capacity to understand what the rights are, or may be in a legal status that creates ambiguity about which organization is the controller for purposes of the Act. No Geneva authority has published guidance on how these obligations translate to the humanitarian context. Organizations are operating with compliance obligations they cannot practically discharge.

### 4.2 ZEWO Certification and AI Governance Adequacy Signal Gap

ZEWO certification evaluates financial governance adequacy for Swiss charitable organizations. It does not evaluate AI governance adequacy. The practical consequence for institutional donors evaluating ZEWO-certified organizations with AI-assisted programs: ZEWO certification creates a false adequacy signal for AI governance. Donors reasonably interpret ZEWO certification as evidence of organizational reliability, including reliability in AI governance practices, when the certification covers no such thing.

The compliance context implication: organizations holding ZEWO certification are implicitly representing AI governance adequacy through a certification that evaluated something else entirely. This creates a specific exposure for organizations that have invested in ZEWO certification as their primary accountability signal but have not built AI governance practices proportionate to what donors are inferring from the certification.

### 4.3 Institutional Donor AI Governance Requirements and Swiss Law Intersection

Major institutional donors — institutional development banks, UN agencies, bilateral government aid agencies — have issued AI governance requirements for grant recipients that may not be simultaneously satisfiable with Swiss law obligations. The specific intersection points where conflicts arise:

- Donor AI transparency requirements may require documenting inference chains about beneficiaries in ways that create nFADP Art.21 exposure when those inference chains constitute evidence of automated decision-making without adequate disclosure.
- Donor AI impact assessment requirements may require producing assessments of AI system behavior in ways that require disclosing AI methodology that the vendor has designated as confidential or that the organization does not have the technical capacity to verify.
- Donor data sharing requirements for AI oversight purposes may require transferring beneficiary data to donor systems that do not meet Swiss data protection adequacy standards.

No Geneva advisory service has mapped the specific intersection of institutional donor AI governance requirements with Swiss law for Geneva-based humanitarian organizations. Organizations are responding to donor requirements without understanding the Swiss law consequences of compliance.

### 4.4 Humanitarian AI Standard-Setting Without Geneva-Based Accountability Mechanism

Geneva-based international organizations and humanitarian coordination bodies are developing AI deployment standards, data sharing protocols, and beneficiary data governance frameworks that are applied globally. These standards are developed through informal coordination processes that do not include public consultation mechanisms, independent review, or accountability pathways for affected populations.

The Swiss compliance context implication: Swiss-based organizations participating in or implementing these standards are operating under governance frameworks that have no Swiss law accountability mechanism. The standards are developed by Geneva-based entities but are not subject to Swiss regulatory review, Swiss judicial oversight, or Swiss administrative law accountability pathways. Organizations implementing these standards from Geneva are accountable to accountability mechanisms that exist only within the informal humanitarian coordination circuit.

### 4.5 Swiss Host State Obligations and Geneva Humanitarian AI Coordination

Swiss international obligations — including obligations arising from the European Convention on Human Rights as applied by Swiss courts, the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, and the Swiss Federal Council's human rights due diligence obligations — apply to Geneva-based humanitarian organizations in their capacity as Swiss-domiciled entities. The specific AI governance implications of these obligations for humanitarian organizations have not been mapped in any public source.

The practical gap: organizations believe that their humanitarian mandate and their Swiss domicile create a protective framework that insulates them from the Swiss law accountability obligations that apply to commercial organizations. The human rights due diligence obligations applicable to Swiss organizations under the Federal Council's framework do not have a humanitarian operations carve-out that is documented as applying to AI governance. Organizations are operating under assumptions about the scope of their Swiss law obligations that may not be accurate.

## 06 — Findings Summary: Six Net-New Insights

**Finding 01: AI Vendor Consolidation M&A as a Specific Humanitarian Sector Data Sovereignty Event Distinct from General Vendor Risk**

The 2024–2026 AI vendor consolidation wave has affected humanitarian AI platforms with specific structural intensity that has not been named as a discrete market phenomenon. The specific characteristic distinguishing the humanitarian sector consolidation from the commercial sector: humanitarian organizations are operationally dependent on AI infrastructure that was acquired and restructured without notification, operating under processing terms they did not negotiate, and are unable to switch vendors mid-grant without grant breach. The combination of operational dependency, mid-program structural change, and switching constraint creates a data sovereignty exposure that is categorically different from general vendor risk. No Geneva advisory service has addressed this combination as a discrete service occasion. The exposure is present-tense, compounding, and invisible until a data subject request arrives or a donor asks specific questions about data processing continuity. Distinct from C265/C266 tool migration findings which addressed documentation discontinuity and mid-program vendor switches as process failures — this finding names the specific structural mechanism (acquisition-driven terms change with no switching option) as a distinct exposure category with its own advisory service occasion.

**Finding 02: Humanitarian AI Impact Assessment Format Optimization Producing Harm Visibility Deficit Rather Than Harm Reduction Evidence**

The standardized AI impact assessment formats required by institutional donors optimize for metric improvement demonstration, not harm reduction evidence. The format architecture creates a specific paradox: organizations that invest seriously in rigorous impact assessment may produce assessments that look identical to organizations producing format-compliant documents that avoid engaging with the actual harm-reduction question. The optimization for metric visibility is not a documentation quality problem — it is a format design problem that no individual organization can solve by improving its own assessment practices. The format gives donors evidence that selected metrics improved. It does not give donors evidence that harm was reduced. No Geneva service has named this as a discrete structural problem requiring a format redesign conversation rather than an organizational practice improvement conversation. Distinct from all prior C214–C266 Phase 04 findings addressing AI impact assessment adequacy — which treated the assessment as a documentation exercise to be improved — this finding identifies the format design itself as the source of the harm visibility deficit.

**Finding 03: Post-Program AI Decision Continuity as an Ungoverned Accountability Domain Structurally Distinct from Data Disposal**

The program-close AI governance question has been addressed in prior cycles as a data disposal problem: what happens to beneficiary data when the grant ends. The unnamed and more acute problem is what happens to AI-shaped beneficiary decisions when the program ends. AI-influenced categorizations — risk classifications, eligibility determinations, prioritization rankings, service intensity recommendations — persist in downstream systems after program termination, shaping beneficiary access to services in government systems, integrated health networks, and referral chains to other organizations. The originating organization has no transfer-out protocol, no documentation architecture for communicating what decisions were AI-influenced and what the influence consisted of, and no accountability framework for decisions that continue to operate outside its governance. The distinction from data disposal is structural: disposal concerns records, decision continuity concerns operational consequences that outlast the program period and operate in systems the originating organization does not govern. Distinct from C266 Finding 01 (program-close dependency debt) which named the dependency problem without identifying the specific mechanism of downstream system inheritance of AI-shaped decisions; distinct from C261/C262 grant-cycle AI disposition findings which addressed obligations about data and profiles not decision consequences.

**Finding 04: Geneva Humanitarian AI Coordination Concentration as a Structural Accountability Condition Without Local Accountability Infrastructure**

Geneva hosts the highest density of international organization headquarters and humanitarian AI coordination functions globally. The frameworks developed in Geneva — AI deployment standards, cross-organizational data sharing protocols, beneficiary data governance norms for crisis contexts — are applied across humanitarian AI deployments on four continents. The coordination concentration creates a specific structural accountability condition: the decisions with the broadest humanitarian consequences are made in the jurisdiction with the least developed local accountability infrastructure for AI governance. No Geneva-based institution reviews whether the AI governance frameworks developed here are adequate. No Geneva-based professional body certifies AI governance competency for humanitarian operations. The absence of local accountability infrastructure is not a gap waiting to be filled — it is a structural condition that defines the operating environment for every Geneva-based humanitarian organization. Distinct from all prior C214–C266 Phase 04 findings addressing individual organizational governance failures — this finding identifies Geneva's specific role as a global coordination hub as a structural amplifier of the accountability vacuum rather than a governance actor with obligations.

**Finding 05: Zero Professional Body Equivalent for Humanitarian AI Governance Creating Structural Certification Vacuum for the Sector**

ZEWO certification covers financial governance for Swiss charitable organizations. No equivalent certification infrastructure exists for AI governance adequacy in the humanitarian sector. The absence creates a specific structural paradox: the organizations with the most complex AI deployments — processing beneficiary data across conflict zones, making allocation decisions with life-consequence weight, operating in contexts where algorithmic errors produce compounding harm — are operating under the weakest external accountability certification framework. ZEWO creates a financial governance adequacy signal that institutional donors use as a proxy for general organizational reliability, including reliability in AI governance practices. The proxy is inaccurate. Organizations that hold ZEWO certification and have serious AI governance practices cannot demonstrate that AI governance adequacy through any third-party certification. Organizations with inadequate AI governance practices and ZEWO certification carry the same certification signal. The market for AI governance credibility in the humanitarian sector has no sorting mechanism. Distinct from C259 Finding 06 (ZEWO certification documentation divergence) which identified that ZEWO documentation adequacy does not measure governance adequacy — this finding takes the finding further by naming the certification vacuum as a structural market failure requiring a certification pathway solution, not merely a documentation quality observation.

**Finding 06: Multi-Year AI Infrastructure Commitment in Grant Architectures Not Designed for Governance Overhead Producing Structural Under-Governance from Commitment Date**

The standard humanitarian grant agreement architecture predates AI governance overhead as a recognized budget category. Multi-year AI infrastructure commitments are embedded in grant agreements that budget technology costs as capital expenditure without budgeting for the recurring human capacity required to govern AI systems adequately. The specific structural dynamic: the governance overhead — documentation maintenance, vendor update review, data subject response, AI impact reassessment, model version tracking — is real, recurring, and structurally unbudgeted. Organizations discover the governance deficit only when governance obligations collide with program delivery capacity mid-grant. By that point the infrastructure commitment is fixed and the governance deficit is structural. The problem is not cost visibility failure — organizations may be aware that governance costs exist. The problem is that grant architecture has no category for governance overhead, meaning governance costs compete with program delivery for the same unrestricted budget allocation, with governance losing that competition by default. Distinct from C265 Finding 01 (AI governance shelf-life collapse as budget cycle failure) which identified that multi-year budgets cannot accommodate continuous governance evolution — this finding names the specific mechanism: the grant architecture itself has no governance overhead category, creating structural under-governance from the commitment date regardless of organizational awareness of the governance costs.
