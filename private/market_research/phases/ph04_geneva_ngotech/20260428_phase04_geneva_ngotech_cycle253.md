## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Multi-donor AI scoring as uncontested parallel accountability records**

NGOs receiving multiple simultaneous AI-generated performance scores from different donor platforms. Each score is uncontested, unexplainable, and creates accountability records the NGO does not own or control. The NGO cannot reconcile the scores, cannot query the methodology, cannot challenge the data. The structural property: donor AI platforms are generating authoritative accountability data about the NGO's program without the NGO having any standing to examine the process. This is distinct from the transparency concern — the NGO is not asking for more visibility into donor scoring, they are reporting three incompatible readings as equally valid data points to their own board.

**Signal 2: Grant agreement AI condition design as structural trap**

Donors imposing AI governance conditions on NGOs through grant agreements without infrastructure verification. The conditions are real, binding, and create legal obligations. The infrastructure to fulfill them does not exist in the NGO. The donor does not verify capacity before funding. The NGO signs because funding is needed. This is architecturally distinct from a compliance gap — it is a structural trap built into the grant design process. The compliance gap implies the NGO could comply if it tried harder; the structural trap means the NGO cannot comply regardless of effort because the obligations were designed without reference to its actual capacity.

**Signal 3: Post-program beneficiary AI inference disposal as nameless obligation**

Beneficiaries who completed programs requesting deletion and explanation of what AI systems inferred about them. The NGO cannot retrieve the original inference — tool updates have made it unavailable. The obligation under FADP Articles 13 and 21 is present-tense and active. The NGO can delete the source data but cannot confirm what synthetic profile existed or what decisions it influenced. The structural problem: consent was obtained before AI deployment, the inference was made under that consent, but the inference itself — as a distinct artifact — cannot be reconstructed or explained. The NGO is liable for what it cannot produce.

**Signal 4: Board resolution language as vendor-drafted governance approval**

Board members approving AI tool vendors through resolutions written by the vendor or vendor-provided templates. The board's fiduciary duty under Art. 75 ZGB requires substantive review of what is being approved. The resolution language was designed by an entity that benefits from approval. The board member signing is personally liable for the decision. The structural capture: the governance document that should protect the board is the same document that was designed to achieve approval.

**Signal 5: Beneficiary consent form architectural mismatch with AI deployment**

Consent forms designed pre-AI, now in use for AI-assisted decisions. The form covers data collection and storage. It does not cover AI inference, profiling, decision influence, or model updates. Beneficiaries consent to data handling that does not correspond to what the AI actually does. The NGO is obtaining valid consent for a different system than the one in operation. When the AI changes, the consent does not change with it. This is not a documentation problem — it is an architectural mismatch that no form revision can close without redesigning the form around the AI's actual behavior.

**Signal 6: Vendor acquisition consent discontinuity as unresolvable operational gap**

When AI vendors are acquired, privacy policies change. Beneficiaries whose data was processed under the old policy remain in the system. Their consent was given under terms that no longer exist. The NGO cannot re-consent them operationally — the beneficiary relationship may not support re-contact, the original consent context cannot be replicated, and the new policy may describe processing that cannot be voluntarily explained to beneficiaries in a way that would constitute informed re-consent. This is structurally unresolvable at the NGO level. The discontinuity was created by the vendor acquisition, not by the NGO's decisions.

This is not a data disposal problem — it is an AI inference accountability problem without a Geneva-specific protocol.

**Gap 4: Board member AI resolution personal liability discharge methodology does not exist**

This is a technical-legal-design problem requiring the combination of AI systems knowledge, Swiss data protection law, and program design competence. No Geneva advisor currently occupies this intersection for the NGO segment.

**Gap 6: Vendor acquisition consent continuity management does not exist**

No Geneva service addresses the specific problem of beneficiary consent continuity when AI vendor acquisitions change privacy terms. This is distinct from general vendor management or sub-processor management. ## 04 — Compliance Context

**Geneva / Swiss specific obligations and intersections for NGO AI governance:**

**ZEWO Certification AI Governance Requirements**

ZEWO certification requires NGOs to demonstrate responsible use of resources and accountable governance. AI governance is not explicitly codified in ZEWO standards, but AI deployment creates accountability exposure that ZEWO certification processes are beginning to probe. The structural tension: ZEWO certification audit examines documentation completeness; AI governance quality resists documentation measurement. NGOs with AI policies that exist on paper but do not govern actual tool deployment face a specific exposure — they have documentation that fails under scrutiny of what the documentation claims to cover.

**Institutional Donor AI Conditions**

Major institutional donors — EU institutions, UN agencies, bilateral development agencies — are increasingly attaching AI governance conditions to grants. The conditions vary in specificity, referencing frameworks such as the EU's AI principles for development cooperation, donor-specific AI guidelines, or general "responsible AI" requirements. The compliance architecture problem: conditions are imposed without capacity verification, and the NGO's signing binds the organization to obligations it cannot independently fulfill. The intersection with Swiss domestic obligations: fulfilling donor AI conditions may require data handling that conflicts with nFADP purpose limitation, creating simultaneous compliance obligations that cannot both be satisfied.

**nFADP / FADP Article 13 and Article 21 — Beneficiary Rights in AI Context**

Articles 13 and 21 create obligations for AI-assisted beneficiary data processing that NGOs are not operationally prepared to discharge. Article 13 access rights require the organization to be able to explain what AI systems concluded about the beneficiary. Article 21 automated decision rights require the organization to be able to explain what decisions were AI-influenced and on what basis. The operational gap: the AI inference, as a distinct artifact, may not be retrievable if tool updates have occurred. The consent-based lawful basis may not cover what the AI actually does under current tool configuration. The intersection with donor visibility: donor reporting requirements may require data processing that conflicts with purpose limitation, creating a structural legal conflict between donor accountability and FADP compliance.

**Data Sovereignty in International NGO Operations**

Geneva-based NGOs operating internationally face data sovereignty questions without resolution infrastructure. Data processed in field operations may be subject to local law. AI tools processing that data may store it in jurisdictions the NGO cannot control. Donor platforms receiving data may hold it under terms the NGO cannot negotiate. The NGO bears accountability for data sovereignty compliance in infrastructure it does not own or govern. No Swiss legal framework specifically resolves the NGO's accountability when the data sovereignty violation occurs in a third-party system the NGO is contractually required to use.

**Art. 75 ZGB Board Member Personal Liability for AI Governance**

Swiss Association Law Art. 75 creates personal liability for board members who fail to exercise the care required in the management of association affairs. Board members approving AI vendors through resolutions drafted by the vendor are not protected by reliance on the executive director's recommendation. Swiss law requires the board member to be able to demonstrate that they conducted a substantive review. The documentation record — the resolution language, the board meeting record — must show what the board member understood about what they were approving. This is distinct from the organization's AI governance obligation — it is a personal liability question for individual board members that current NGO board practices do not address.

**Limited IT Capacity as Structural Governance Constraint**

Geneva NGO AI governance cannot be designed as if IT capacity is scalable. The constraint is structural, not temporary. Governance solutions must fit within part-time IT capacity, program staff without technical backgrounds, and executive directors managing multiple compliance areas simultaneously. Solutions that require ongoing IT infrastructure monitoring, regular technical updates, or specialized compliance technology are not implementable in this context. The compliance context is: the obligation exists and is real; the capacity to discharge it through conventional governance infrastructure does not exist; the structural constraint must be designed for, not worked around.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Six net-new insights distinct from C214–C252 Phase 04:**

**Finding 01: Multi-donor AI scoring is a structural accountability paradox, not a transparency problem**

The NGO problem with multi-donor AI scores is not that scores are opaque — it is that the NGO has no standing to challenge or query any of them while being held accountable for results that the scores measure. This creates a specific documentation and board communication problem that no current Geneva advisory service addresses. The NGO is reporting three incompatible performance readings to its board as equally valid data points, which is rational given the NGO's position but creates board governance exposure when board members cannot explain why the same program produces three different evaluations. This is structurally distinct from the earlier NGO AI transparency and donor accountability concerns in prior Phase 04 cycles.

**Finding 02: Grant agreement AI conditions are structurally designed as traps, not compliance failures**

The problem is not that NGOs cannot comply with donor AI conditions — it is that the conditions are imposed without capacity verification, making compliance structurally impossible regardless of effort. The architectural fix is pre-signature capacity assessment, not post-signature compliance management. This is distinct from the grant agreement AI condition interpretation burden described in C252 Phase 04. The C252 finding focused on interpretation burden after signing; this finding focuses on the trap built into the grant design process before signature — a structurally different problem requiring a different service design.

**Finding 03: Post-program AI inference disposal is a distinct FADP obligation category without protocol**

The obligation to explain what AI systems inferred about beneficiaries who completed programs is active present-tense under FADP Articles 13 and 21, and it is structurally distinct from source data disposal. The AI inference — as a synthetic profile, a conclusion, a scoring output — may not be retrievable even when the underlying data exists. The NGO can delete what it collected but cannot produce what it computed. No Geneva-specific protocol exists for this scenario, and no Geneva market participant has named it as a discrete obligation requiring a discrete methodology. This is distinct from the beneficiary identity reconstruction risk described in C249 Phase 04 — which focused on AI-inferred profiles at program wind-down generally — by isolating the specific FADP Art. 13/Art. 21 discharge problem as a named category requiring its own protocol.

**Finding 04: Board resolution language sourced from the vendor creates structural governance capture by design**

The board approving vendor-drafted resolution language is not a due diligence failure — it is a structural governance capture mechanism operating through the normal board approval process. The document that should protect board members from liability is the same document designed to achieve their approval. Art. 75 ZGB requires substantive review; the resolution was designed to appear substantive without requiring it. This is distinct from the board-approved AI policy staleness problem in C242/C248 Phase 04 by isolating the specific problem of vendor-authored governance documents as a discrete structural capture mechanism, not a staleness or policy quality problem.

**Finding 05: Beneficiary consent forms architecturally cannot cover AI-assisted decisions by design**

The consent form mismatch is not a drafting gap — it is an architectural impossibility. Consent forms are designed around what data is collected and how it is stored. AI inference, profiling, decision influence, and model update behavior are architecturally outside what consent forms can cover by design. The problem cannot be closed by better drafting; it requires the consent architecture to be redesigned around the AI's actual behavior, which requires knowing what that behavior is. This is distinct from the consent form AI disclosure lag described in C233 Phase 04 — which identified the sequential timing gap — by identifying the architectural impossibility as a structural property of pre-AI consent design applied to AI-assisted decisions.

**Finding 06: Vendor acquisition consent discontinuity is structurally unresolvable at the NGO level and nobody has named it**

When AI vendor acquisitions change privacy terms, beneficiaries whose consent was given under the old terms remain in the system. Re-consent is operationally impossible in most field contexts. The NGO bears liability for a discontinuity it did not create and cannot resolve. No Geneva market participant has named this as a discrete structural problem requiring a discrete service. The service gap is: documentation architecture for vendor acquisition scenarios designed prospectively, and a discharge protocol for the specific retrospective case where re-consent is structurally impossible. This is distinct from the processor-of-record drift described in C248/C252 Phase 04 — which focused on sub-processor chain opacity and processor relationship changes — by isolating the specific beneficiary consent continuity problem created by vendor acquisition events as its own named category requiring its own methodology.
