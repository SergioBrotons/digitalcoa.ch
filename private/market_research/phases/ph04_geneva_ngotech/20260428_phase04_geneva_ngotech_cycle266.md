## 01 — ICP Language: How This ICP Actually Describes AI Problems

### English

**On AI dependency debt at program close:**
"We ran AI-assisted beneficiary assessments for three years. The AI generated risk scores and eligibility recommendations that shaped which beneficiaries received services and at what intensity. Now the grant is ending. The AI vendor updated the model three times during the program period. We do not have a clean record of which model produced which beneficiary assessment. When the program closes, we have beneficiaries who were enrolled based on AI recommendations. The AI-influenced decisions do not stop when the funding stops. We have no architecture for managing the AI dependency we created for these beneficiaries."

**On tool migration as undocumented program break:**
"We switched AI tools mid-program because the original vendor changed their pricing structure beyond what the grant budget could absorb. The new tool is different. The beneficiary profiles from the old tool do not transfer cleanly to the new tool. We manually migrated what we could. The donors are asking for program continuity data across the transition. The records do not connect. We cannot show that the same beneficiaries received equivalent services across the migration. The gap is invisible to us until we try to produce the continuity report."

**On AI-inferred beneficiary data disposal as an inventory problem nobody built:**
"The AI inferred conclusions about beneficiaries that we never collected directly — risk levels, vulnerability scores, intervention priority rankings. We never documented which fields in our records were AI-inferred versus directly reported. When a data subject requested deletion of their file, we had to figure out what was AI-inferred and whether deleting the source data also deletes the inference. We could not answer the question. The data disposal obligation is present-tense. We do not have the inventory."

**On AI configuration knowledge as organizational debt departing with staff:**
"One staff member configured our entire AI-assisted targeting system. They understood how the model decisions connected to our program design, what adjustments had been made for local context, and which parameters had been calibrated to our specific beneficiary population. They left six months ago. The system is still running. The new person cannot describe what it does or why it makes the recommendations it makes. We have a governance record that says the system was configured. We do not have the configuration knowledge. The board signed off on the system. The board has no way of knowing the knowledge departed with the staff member."

**On donor visibility as a structural data exposure paradox:**
"When we show donors the AI transparency they ask for, we are showing them inference chains about beneficiaries that beneficiaries did not consent to share with donors. We built the AI governance documentation because donors require it. The documentation itself is now the exposure. The more visible we make our AI governance for donors, the more data we may be required to disclose about beneficiaries under our own data protection obligations. We are caught between donor accountability and beneficiary confidentiality, and the documentation architecture we built to satisfy one makes the other worse."

**On AI training as institutional knowledge transfer obligation nobody discussed:**
"Donors are asking what AI skills our staff have. We trained field staff on AI tools as part of program delivery. The training happened. The training content was whatever the vendor provided or what we figured out internally. Nobody documented what the training covered, what competency threshold we were targeting, or whether staff actually developed the skills the AI use requires. We can show that training occurred. We cannot show what was transferred or whether it was adequate. The documentation gap is invisible until someone asks the specific question."

## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

**Signal 1: AI dependency debt at program close is an ungoverned structural liability, not a program transition question**
When a program closes, the organization has beneficiaries whose enrollment was influenced by AI recommendations. The AI dependency does not terminate with the grant. No Geneva NGO has a program-close AI dependency review protocol. The liability is invisible until a beneficiary contests eligibility after the program has ended and funding has stopped.

**Signal 2: Tool migration mid-program creates a documentation discontinuity that donors are beginning to request records for**
When NGOs switch AI vendors mid-program due to pricing changes, the beneficiary profiles from the old system do not transfer cleanly to the new system. The resulting documentation discontinuity is not a data migration problem — it is an accountability gap. Donors are now requesting continuity records across tool transitions, and NGOs cannot produce them cleanly.

**Signal 3: AI-inferred data disposal is a compounding present-tense exposure with no inventory**
The nFADP data subject deletion right applies to AI-inferred conclusions, not just directly collected data. The disposal obligation is present-tense, but most NGOs have not inventoried which fields in their records are AI-inferred. The inventory problem compounds with each AI tool adoption. No Geneva market participant addresses this inventory architecture.

**Signal 4: AI configuration knowledge departing with staff is an organizational debt invisible until the person leaves**
Staff members who configured AI systems carry institutional knowledge that has not been transferred to the organization. The organization has a system running, a board approval on file, and no internal capacity to describe what the system does or why. The knowledge gap is not a personnel matter — it is a governance deficit that operates continuously until someone names it.

**Signal 5: Donor AI transparency requirements and beneficiary data protection create structural data exposure paradox that documentation cannot resolve**
The documentation architecture built to satisfy donor AI visibility requirements is now the instrument through which beneficiary data may become more visible to donors than the organization's own data protection obligations permit. The paradox is structural: satisfying donor accountability creates conditions for violating beneficiary data protection. No Geneva NGO has a framework for managing this simultaneously.

**Signal 6: AI staff training records show attendance not competency, creating a documentation gap when donors ask the specific question**
Donors asking about staff AI competency encounter training records showing that training occurred. Training records document attendance, not skill transfer. When a donor asks whether staff have the AI competencies the program requires, the documentation does not answer the question. The gap is invisible until the specific question arrives.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis: Gaps, What No One Is Addressing

**Gap 1: Program-close AI dependency review as a discrete governance occasion**
No service addresses the program-close AI dependency review: the structured assessment of which beneficiaries have AI-influenced enrollment decisions, what the organization's continuing obligations are to those beneficiaries, what data disposal obligations have accumulated, and what documentation the organization needs to maintain after program closure. This is distinct from post-program data disposal — it is a structured review of the AI dependency that was created, not just the data that was held.

**Gap 2: AI tool migration documentation architecture for mid-program transitions**
No methodology exists for documenting AI tool transitions mid-program in a way that preserves continuity accountability. The architecture would need to address how to record the state of the old system at transition, how to document the migration process, and how to produce continuity records that donors can audit. This is distinct from vendor continuity provisions — it addresses what happens when the transition has already occurred without adequate documentation.

**Gap 3: AI-inferred data inventory architecture for nFADP disposal compliance**
No service exists for building the AI-inferred data inventory that nFADP disposal compliance requires. The inventory architecture would need to identify which fields in existing records are AI-inferred versus directly collected, what the inference methodology was, and whether deletion of source data eliminates the inference or whether the inference must be separately disposed. This is distinct from general data inventory work.

**Gap 4: AI configuration knowledge transfer protocol for staff departures**
No protocol exists for documenting AI system configuration knowledge in a way that survives staff departure. The protocol would need to capture what the system does, why it makes the recommendations it makes, what local calibrations have been applied, and what the organizational knowledge base needs to maintain in order to govern the system independently of the person who configured it. This is distinct from general knowledge management — it is a specific structured protocol for AI configuration knowledge.

**Gap 5: Donor AI transparency and beneficiary data protection simultaneous compliance architecture**
No framework exists for simultaneously satisfying donor AI transparency requirements and beneficiary data protection obligations when the transparency documentation may itself be the mechanism through which beneficiary data becomes accessible to donors. The framework would need to address how to structure disclosure to satisfy donor accountability without creating additional beneficiary data exposure. This is a structural design question, not a documentation gap.

**Gap 6: AI competency documentation framework showing skill transfer not just training attendance**
No framework exists for documenting AI staff competency in a way that demonstrates skill transfer, not just training attendance. The framework would need to define competency levels, how to assess whether competency was achieved, and how to document the assessment. This is distinct from training records management — it addresses the specific question of what training records do not show.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Specific Obligations

### ZEWO Certification and AI-Assisted Program Delivery Documentation
ZEWO certification audits financial accountability documentation but does not yet systematically examine AI-assisted program decisions as a distinct audit category. However, institutional donors are now attaching AI governance questionnaires to grant agreements that require the same organizational documentation that ZEWO audits do not probe. Organizations with ZEWO certification may have a documentation gap for institutional donor requirements that certification did not surface.

### Institutional Donor AI Framework Obligations Arriving in Grant Agreements
SDC, cantonal development funds, and multilateral donors are introducing AI governance conditions in grant agreements with increasing specificity. The conditions require documented AI governance architecture, evidence of board-level AI oversight, AI system inventory, staff competency documentation, and program-close AI dependency management. These obligations are arriving now, before any Swiss Federal Council AI regulatory framework crystallizes. The binding date is grant renewal, not regulatory announcement.

### nFADP AI-Inferred Data Disposal Obligations (Art. 13 and Art. 15)
Under the revised Federal Data Protection Act, AI-inferred beneficiary data constitutes personal data requiring lawful basis and subject to data subject rights including deletion. For NGOs that have adopted AI tools without inventoried data fields, disposal requests cannot be correctly discharged: the organization cannot determine what data exists, what is AI-inferred, and whether deletion of source data eliminates the inference. The compliance gap is present-tense and compounding with each tool adoption.

### Swiss Association Law (ZGB Art. 75-79) Board Accountability for AI Configuration Knowledge Departure
Board members approving AI systems are accountable for organizational oversight. When AI configuration knowledge departs with the staff member who configured the system, the board's oversight function is structurally compromised — the system operates, the board approval is on file, and the organizational capacity to understand what the system does is absent. The board liability exposure is present-tense and unaddressed in current board documentation practice.

### International Organization Geneva Office Accountability for AI Decisions Made Outside Swiss Jurisdiction
Geneva offices of international organizations face a compounding accountability exposure under Swiss association law for AI systems configured by headquarters in other jurisdictions. The Geneva office implements AI decisions it did not design and cannot modify. Staff departures at headquarters can change AI system behavior without Geneva office knowledge. The Geneva office bears accountability for system behavior it cannot govern and may not be aware of.

## 06 — Findings Summary: Six Net-New Insights

**Finding 01: AI dependency debt at program close is a named structural liability distinct from data disposal — the organization carries ongoing obligations to beneficiaries enrolled through AI-assisted processes after funding ends**
When a grant terminates, beneficiaries whose enrollment was AI-influenced do not revert to a pre-AI state. The AI system may continue operating on the same beneficiary population. The organizational obligations do not terminate with the funding. No Geneva market participant addresses program-close AI dependency review as a discrete governance occasion. The liability is structurally distinct from post-program data disposal — disposal concerns data held, dependency review concerns decisions made and ongoing obligations created. This is distinct from the grant-windown AI disposition finding in C259 and C261 Phase 04, which addressed data disposal at grant close, not the ongoing AI dependency and beneficiary continuity question.

**Finding 02: Tool migration documentation discontinuity is a present-tense accountability gap that donors are now requesting records for, with no Geneva methodology for mid-program AI tool transitions**
When NGOs switch AI vendors mid-program due to pricing, the beneficiary profiles and program continuity records do not transfer cleanly. Donors are now requesting continuity documentation across these transitions, and NGOs cannot produce clean records. The gap is not a data migration problem — it is an accountability documentation failure. No Geneva market participant offers a methodology for documenting AI tool transitions mid-program in a way that preserves accountability continuity. This is distinct from the AI continuity risk in grant agreements (C265 Phase 04 Finding 01), which addressed pre-signature protection, not post-hoc documentation of transitions that already occurred.

**Finding 03: AI-inferred data disposal under nFADP requires an inventory that most Geneva NGOs have not built, making correct disposal compliance structurally impossible at the moment requests arrive**
nFADP Art. 13 and Art. 15 rights apply to AI-inferred beneficiary data. NGOs that deployed AI without inventoried data fields cannot correctly discharge disposal requests: they cannot determine what is AI-inferred, what the inference methodology was, or whether deletion of source data eliminates the inference. The inventory gap is present-tense and compounding with each tool adoption. No Geneva market participant offers AI-inferred data inventory architecture as a discrete service. This is distinct from the post-program inference disposal finding in C253 Phase 04, which addressed the question of whether AI-generated inferences survive source data deletion — this finding addresses the prior question of whether the organization knows what AI-inferred data it holds.

**Finding 04: AI configuration knowledge departing with staff creates an organizational accountability deficit that board approval documents do not bridge — the governance record says the system is approved, the organization cannot describe what it does**
Staff members who configured AI systems carry configuration knowledge that has not been transferred to the organization as an institution. The board approval remains on file. The system continues operating. The organizational capacity to govern what the system does is absent. The gap is not a personnel matter — it is a structural governance deficit that operates continuously from departure until someone names it. No Geneva market participant addresses AI configuration knowledge transfer protocol as a discrete service. This is distinct from the general staff departure knowledge loss finding in C262 Phase 04 — that finding addressed knowledge management, this finding specifically isolates the AI configuration knowledge gap as a distinct category from general institutional knowledge.

**Finding 05: Donor AI transparency requirements and beneficiary nFADP data protection create a structural data exposure paradox where the documentation built to satisfy donor accountability becomes the mechanism of beneficiary data exposure to donors**
The documentation architecture built for donor AI visibility may itself be the instrument through which beneficiary data becomes accessible to donors in ways that violate the organization's own nFADP obligations to those beneficiaries. The paradox is structural: satisfying one accountability creates conditions for violating another. No Geneva framework exists for managing donor transparency and beneficiary data protection simultaneously. This is distinct from the donor visibility inversion finding in C261 Phase 04, which identified that donor documentation creates evidentiary records — this finding specifically addresses the structural contradiction where the same documentation mechanism serves donor accountability and potentially violates beneficiary data protection simultaneously.

**Finding 06: AI staff training documentation shows attendance not competency, creating a specific documentation gap when institutional donors ask whether staff have the AI skills the program requires**
Donors are asking about staff AI competency as part of grant accountability. Training records document that training occurred — they do not document skill transfer. When a donor asks whether staff have the AI competencies the program requires, the documentation cannot answer the question. No Geneva market participant addresses AI competency documentation framework as a discrete service. This is distinct from all prior Phase 04 findings on staff capacity and training — those addressed capacity gaps and training needs, this finding specifically isolates the documentation gap between training records and competency evidence as a discrete market failure.
