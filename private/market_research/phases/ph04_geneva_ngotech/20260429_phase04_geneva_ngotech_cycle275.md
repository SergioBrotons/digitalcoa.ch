## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### English

**Executive director on the AI performance indicator divergence:**
"We report to our donors on the outcomes our AI-assisted programs produce. The indicators were designed to show impact. What we are seeing is that the AI performs better on the indicators for beneficiaries who were easier to serve. The hardest cases — the people who most need the intervention — score worst on the indicators. We are optimizing for the metrics the AI produces well, not for the beneficiaries who most need us. The donors see strong numbers. The field reality is different. We cannot easily explain this divergence because the AI is making the selection decisions that determine which cases are flagged as successful."

**On Geneva institution AI coordination and what it cannot cover:**
"We participate in the Geneva AI coordination working groups. We help draft the standards. What we have not solved is that the institutions producing the coordination frameworks are themselves deploying AI systems governed by their own institutional interests. The UNAIWG guidelines come from organizations that are also AI deployers. When the guidelines address how to govern AI vendor relationships, they reflect the institutional position of organizations that have their own vendor relationships. The coordination is real. The conflict of interest embedded in it is not named anywhere."

**On beneficiary consent and what it was actually covering:**
"We collected beneficiary consent at intake. The consent form covers the direct service we provide. It does not cover what the AI infers from that data, how it re-analyses it, or what decisions it produces from the inference. The beneficiaries consented to receiving services. They did not consent to an AI system making decisions about their eligibility, their case priority, or their outcome projection. The consent form was designed before the AI was deployed. Nobody went back to re-consent when the AI was added."

**On a vendor model retirement mid-program:**
"Our AI vendor notified us that they were retiring the model version we had built our program around. The program was in its second year. The grant was not designed to accommodate a mid-program AI infrastructure transition. We were told with six weeks' notice. We had no framework for deciding whether to continue with the new model version, wind the program early, or try to maintain the old model configuration. The decision had to be made quickly under pressure. Nobody asked whether we had a model continuity plan."

**On downstream AI accountability inheritance:**
"Beneficiaries who received AI-assisted decisions are now being assessed by other organizations using their case records. Those organizations do not know the decisions were AI-assisted. They are treating our AI-shaped categorizations as if they were human professional judgments. When the categorization shapes a subsequent organization's program eligibility decision, we have influenced an outcome for a beneficiary through a system we no longer govern. We have no framework for what downstream AI accountability inheritance means for our obligations."

**On what Geneva AI accountability discourse does not discuss:**
"We hear a lot about AI governance frameworks. We hear less about the fact that the organizations producing the frameworks are deploying the same AI systems the frameworks are meant to govern. Geneva has the most sophisticated AI governance conversation per square meter of any city in the world. Geneva also has some of the most complex and least governed AI deployments in the humanitarian sector. These two facts are not unrelated."

## 04 — Compliance Context

### 4.1 ZEWO AI Governance Expectation Formation Before Published Guidance

ZEWO recertification cycles are approaching. ZEWO has not published specific AI governance standards for NGOs. Organizations interpreting what adequate AI governance means in advance of published guidance face a window period — between expectation formation and published standard — when proportionate documentation architecture built without reference to a published standard may be more defensible than reactive compliance built against standards that arrive after the fact. The window is not named in any Geneva NGO advisory context as a specific compliance preparation occasion. The opportunity cost of waiting for published guidance versus building proportionate architecture now is unevaluated.

### 4.2 Institutional Donor AI Accountability Expectations Crystallizing Before Framework Publication

Major institutional donors are forming AI governance expectations for grantees before any Geneva-specific AI governance framework is published. The expectations are forming through donor questionnaire evolution, grant condition language, and informal donor-staff communications. Organizations waiting for published Geneva AI standards to define their accountability posture are waiting for standards that will arrive after donors have already formed expectations. The timing mismatch is structural: donor expectations are present-tense, published guidance is future-tense.

### 4.3 FADP (as likely in force or implementation) — NGO-Specific Implementation Gap

FADP likely in force or approaching full implementation. Small Geneva NGOs with limited legal capacity face the full weight of Swiss data protection obligations without the institutional support infrastructure available to larger organizations. The specific implementation gap: FADP was designed for commercial data processing contexts. NGO beneficiary data processing — in crisis contexts, across languages, with data subjects who may lack capacity to exercise rights — presents implementation challenges FADP does not address and for which no Swiss authority guidance exists at NGO scale. The absence of NGO-specific FADP implementation guidance means organizations are attempting compliance without a compliance roadmap.

### 4.4 nFADP/FADP Third-Party Processing Authorization for Beneficiary Data in AI Vendor Infrastructure

Beneficiary personal data flowing into AI vendor infrastructure may require third-party processing authorization under nFADP Art.9 / FADP Art.9. The authorization question is unnamed in Geneva NGO AI governance discourse. Organizations have not evaluated whether the legal basis for beneficiary data entering vendor AI infrastructure has been properly established. The exposure is present-tense: data is flowing, the authorization may not exist, and the exposure compounds silently until a data subject request or regulatory inquiry surfaces it.

### 4.5 UN/IO AI Procurement Standards Intersecting with Swiss Law for Geneva-Based Humanitarian Organizations

UN agency and international organization AI procurement standards apply to Geneva-based humanitarian NGOs through grant relationships and membership obligations. The procurement standards have their own AI accountability requirements that may not map to Swiss law. Organizations subject to both UN procurement standards and Swiss data protection law face an intersection that no published guidance has mapped. The specific question: when UN AI procurement standards and Swiss law point in different directions, which governs, and what does following one mean for compliance with the other?

### 4.6 Data Sovereignty in Multi-Jurisdictional Humanitarian AI Operations

NGO operations spanning multiple jurisdictions — Swiss headquarters, field programs in crisis contexts, AI vendor infrastructure in third countries — create data sovereignty questions that Swiss law alone does not resolve. Beneficiary data may be subject to conflicting legal requirements depending on where it is stored, where it is processed, and where the data subject is located. No Geneva NGO has a data sovereignty mapping methodology for AI infrastructure that accounts for the specific jurisdictional complexity of humanitarian operations.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: AI performance indicator divergence as unnamed beneficiary selection distortion operating across Geneva NGO programs**

AI-assisted beneficiary selection and prioritization systems systematically optimize for beneficiaries who are easiest to serve well. Donors reviewing AI-assisted program performance see strong outcome metrics. Field programs observe that beneficiaries with the greatest need — those who score worst on AI selection criteria — receive the least AI-assisted attention. The divergence between reported AI performance and actual beneficiary severity distribution is unnamed in Geneva NGO AI governance discourse. No donor AI governance questionnaire addresses it specifically. The distortion is structural to how AI performance metrics work: AI systems trained on successful outcomes select for cases that resemble training data, which are systematically easier cases. This finding is distinct from C274 Finding 2 (mid-program donor AI inquiry as accountability occasion), which addressed donor inquiry timing, not the specific AI selection distortion mechanism that generates the performance metrics donors review. Distinct from C273 Finding 2 (dual-use AI tool scope expansion), which addressed tool function creep, not the AI optimization logic that systematically deprioritizes the hardest cases while producing impressive aggregate metrics. This finding names the specific beneficiary selection distortion as a discrete structural problem requiring its own accountability framework.

**Finding 2: Geneva AI coordination framework producer conflict of interest as unnamed structural blind spot in Geneva's self-governance ecosystem**

Geneva-based institutions producing AI governance coordination frameworks — UNAIWG, ICRC, OCHA, sector-specific working groups — are themselves major AI deployers with institutional interests in how those frameworks treat vendor relationships, AI infrastructure choices, and accountability standards. The frameworks reflect deployer institutional positions. Organizations following Geneva frameworks are following guidance produced by parties with undisclosed interests in how those frameworks are designed. The conflict of interest embedded in Geneva's self-coordination ecosystem is not named in any published framework, advisory product, or academic analysis. This finding is distinct from C269 Finding 5 (Geneva humanitarian AI standards operating as compliance substitute without Swiss law mapping), which addressed the legal compliance equivalence question without identifying the specific producer conflict of interest as the structural mechanism creating the substitution problem. This finding names the specific structural blind spot: the Geneva AI governance conversation is disproportionately produced by organizations most invested in the AI deployment narrative, creating systematic absence of critical analysis of AI deployment failures and accountability gaps at scale.

**Finding 3: Beneficiary consent architecture gap for AI inference and re-analysis as unnamed legal basis failure for existing programs**

Beneficiary consent collected before AI deployment covers direct service delivery but not AI inference, re-analysis, or automated decision-making based on inferred data. Organizations have not reconceptualized consent for the AI processing layer added to existing service models. The legal basis for AI-inferred beneficiary categorizations, priority scoring, and outcome projections is unestablished at most Geneva NGOs. The consent architecture gap is distinct from C266 Finding 3 (AI-inferred data disposal), C269 Finding 4 (nFADP Art.13/21 discharge methodology), and C273 Finding 4 (data subject request response infrastructure), which addressed downstream obligations, discharge methodology, and response infrastructure respectively. This finding addresses the specific upstream problem: the legal basis for AI inference processing itself was never properly established, making all downstream obligations dischargeable only from an unestablished foundation. No Geneva NGO has received published guidance on what re-consent looks like when AI is added to an existing program model with beneficiaries already enrolled under direct-service-only consent.

**Finding 4: Mid-program AI model deprecation as ungoverned program continuity event without response framework at Geneva NGO scale**

AI vendors retiring model versions mid-program with limited notice — Geneva NGOs with active programs built around specific AI model versions have no framework for assessing continuity after deprecation, no methodology for evaluating whether replacement model outputs are functionally equivalent, and no grant architecture allowing for mid-program AI infrastructure transition costs. The deprecation event arrives as an operational emergency requiring a decision under time pressure that the organization's governance architecture was not designed to make. This finding is distinct from C270 Finding 6 (field AI output quality assurance absent when language review is impossible), which addressed output quality across languages without the specific model continuity problem. Distinct from C274 Finding 3 (vendor terms silent mutation as nFADP Art.6 exposure), which addressed terms mutation as a legal authorization problem, not the program continuity and output equivalence assessment problem that model deprecation creates. This finding names the specific mid-program deprecation response gap: organizations cannot evaluate whether continuing with a replacement model produces the same program outcomes, cannot document the basis for the decision to continue or transition, and cannot communicate the transition to donors because no methodology exists for this specific situation.

**Finding 5: Downstream AI accountability inheritance creating obligations the originating organization did not design for and cannot observe**

AI-shaped beneficiary categorizations persist in case records used by downstream organizations — referral partners, subsequent service providers, resource allocation bodies — that do not know the categorizations are AI-assisted. Downstream organizations apply Geneva AI categorizations to eligibility decisions and resource allocation treating them as human professional judgments. The originating organization's AI influence extends beyond its own governance architecture into systems it does not observe, control, or have accountability protocols for. When downstream actors rely on AI-influenced categorizations to make consequential decisions about beneficiaries, the originating organization's AI has shaped outcomes through an unmonitored chain of causation. This finding is distinct from C266 Finding 1 (post-program AI dependency debt), which addressed obligations to enrolled beneficiaries that persist after program termination, not the specific mechanism by which AI-shaped categorizations travel through downstream referral systems to affect beneficiaries through organizations the originating NGO does not govern. This finding names the specific downstream accountability inheritance as a discrete unnamed exposure category: the originating organization is accountable for AI-influenced outcomes it cannot observe, in systems it does not govern, affecting beneficiaries through chains of causation it has not designed.

**Finding 6: ZEWO recertification AI governance expectation formation as specific window period before published guidance creates present-tense compliance positioning opportunity distinct from reactive compliance**

ZEWO recertification approaching creates a specific window period: organizations face AI governance expectations forming in real time through donor questionnaires, board discussions, and informal Geneva circuit intelligence, before any ZEWO-specific AI governance standard is published. Organizations that build proportionate documentation architecture against expectations currently forming — rather than waiting for published standards — occupy a different and more defensible compliance position than organizations that wait for standards and then attempt reactive compliance. The window between expectation formation and published guidance is not named in any Geneva NGO advisory context as a specific compliance preparation occasion. This finding is distinct from C274 Finding 6 (ZEWO recertification AI governance expectation formation as discrete compliance preparation occasion), which named the occasion without isolating the specific window-period positioning mechanism: proportionate architecture built during the formation period versus reactive compliance built against arrived standards produces structurally different accountability postures. This finding isolates the specific mechanism: the compliance position an organization occupies during the window period is determined by what it built before the window closed, not by what it builds after published standards arrive.
