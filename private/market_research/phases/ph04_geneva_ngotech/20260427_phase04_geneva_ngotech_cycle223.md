## 02 — Market Signals

1. **Donor AI documentation requirements creating audit exposure without organizational capacity** — Donors are asking for AI governance documentation the NGO cannot produce. The documentation gap is structural, not a matter of effort.

2. **FADP beneficiary rights operational impossibility for AI-assisted decisions** — Individual rights under FADP Art. 22 apply to AI-assisted beneficiary decisions. The NGO cannot operationalize these rights at scale.

3. **Board AI governance oversight cannot be exercised without a translatable framework** — Board members have fiduciary responsibility for AI oversight but no evaluable framework to apply. The governance conversation is managed, not informed.

4. **Vendor term monitoring failure creates silent legal basis invalidation** — AI vendor term changes accumulate without organizational awareness, potentially invalidating legal bases for data processing without anyone knowing.

5. **Multi-donor AI framework fragmentation creating documentation demands the NGO cannot satisfy** — Each institutional donor applying a different AI governance framework multiplies the documentation burden without providing a single implementable standard.

6. **AI framework proportionality gap keeping NGOs from governance despite intent** — Geneva's AI frameworks assume compliance infrastructure, staff, and budgets that small NGOs do not have. The intent to govern is present; the implementation capacity is absent.

## 06 — Findings Summary

### 6 Net-New Findings (Distinct from C214–C222 Phase 04)

1. **Donor AI documentation requirements as structural capacity gap, not effort gap** — The NGO cannot produce AI governance documentation not because it lacks will but because it lacks the structural capacity. The gap is organizational, not motivational.

2. **FADP beneficiary rights operational impossibility is a present-tense compliance failure** — AI-assisted decisions affecting beneficiaries are already subject to FADP Art. 22. The NGO is non-compliant now, not at some future regulatory moment.

3. **Board AI governance oversight cannot be exercised without a framework the board can apply** — Fiduciary responsibility for AI oversight requires an evaluable framework. Without it, the board is accountable for a decision it cannot make informed judgments about.

4. **Vendor term monitoring failure creates silent exposure accumulation across time** — Each vendor term change potentially invalidates legal bases for data processing without organizational awareness. The exposure compounds silently and is discovered only at audit.

5. **Multi-donor AI framework fragmentation multiplies documentation burden without providing direction** — The NGO faces incompatible documentation demands from multiple donors applying different frameworks. The result is compliance theater — producing documents that do not reflect actual governance.

6. **Framework proportionality gap is the structural mechanism keeping NGOs from governance** — Geneva's AI governance knowledge production is designed for organizations with compliance infrastructure. The gap between framework ambition and NGO implementation capacity is the core structural problem, not a problem of priorities or motivation.
