# Phase 04 — Geneva NGO/Tech | C233
**Filed:** 2026-04-27 | **Cycle:** C233 Phase 04/06
**ICP:** Executive director or program manager at a Geneva-based NGO (10–100 employees), international organization, or tech nonprofit deploying AI for program delivery, data management, or communications. Core concerns: donor accountability, data sovereignty, reputational risk, limited IT capacity, donor visibility vs. confidentiality tension.

## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### Real Language — How the ICP Actually Describes AI Problems

- "The donor sent the AI questionnaire. I have three days to answer it. I do not have an AI inventory. I have a list of tools people mentioned in staff meetings." — The gap between donor AI governance reporting deadlines and organizational AI governance documentation readiness: the questionnaire arrives under time pressure, and the organization discovers it does not have the foundational inventory that the questionnaire assumes.

- "We hired a consultant to help with the AI governance policy. They wrote a policy. Nothing changed operationally." — The documentation-production problem: AI governance consulting that produces a policy document without affecting operational AI governance, leaving organizations with correct documents and unchanged tool deployments.

- "Our tools are fine. The problem is that when something goes wrong, I cannot explain what the tool did. I cannot show the decision trail. I cannot show who reviewed it." — The post-incident accountability gap: the AI tool worked acceptably in normal operation but produced a decision that, when questioned, cannot be reconstructed because the decision trail was never documented.

- "Our AI tools are from 2022. The vendor has changed models twice since then. We do not know which model was running when." — The model version continuity problem: AI tools deployed in 2022, with vendor model updates applied silently, and no organizational record of which model version was in operation during which program period.

- "When I leave, there is no record of which AI decisions were made in this system, under which model version, and with what inputs. It is just gone." — The organizational memory problem: AI-assisted decisions made under a specific tool configuration that leaves no organizational record when the person who managed the tool departs.

- "We use AI for beneficiary communications. Our consent forms do not mention AI. We did not think to add it." — The consent form lag: FADP-compliant consent frameworks designed before AI tools were deployed, now in use for AI-assisted beneficiary communications without amendment.

## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

1. **Grant agreement AI reporting obligations arrive as time-bound deadlines before organizations have the AI inventory to respond — donor AI questionnaires arrive under 72-hour response windows that expose the documentation gap rather than allowing organizations to close it** — The most common immediate AI governance crisis in Geneva NGOs is not the absence of AI governance — it is the timing of donor AI governance requests: the questionnaire arrives with a short response deadline, the organization scrambles to answer questions about AI tool deployments it has not inventoried, and the response documents whatever the organization can reconstruct under deadline pressure rather than what the governance picture actually is. The compliance exposure is not in the failure to have AI governance — it is in the grant agreement structure that allows donors to issue time-bound AI governance requests before organizations have had time to build the infrastructure to respond accurately. The paradox is that the organizations most behind on AI governance are the ones most likely to be caught by surprise by donor AI reporting deadlines.

2. **AI governance consulting that produces documents without changing operational AI governance — policy documents approved and filed while tool deployments remain ungoverned** — The most common consulting failure mode in Geneva NGO AI governance is the policy production engagement: a consultant is engaged to develop AI governance policy, produces a comprehensive document, the document is approved by the board, and the operational AI tool deployments remain unchanged, ungoverned, and undocumented. The organization ends up with a correct-looking policy document that describes governance for AI tools it does not actually have documented governance processes for — producing a false conformance impression that is more dangerous than no policy at all, because it would be surfaced as governance inadequacy in a donor audit that examined operational reality rather than document existence.

3. **Post-incident AI decision reconstruction: the organization cannot explain what the AI tool did because the decision trail was never documented in the first place** — The most acute AI governance failure in operational terms is the post-incident reconstruction problem: an AI-assisted decision produced a poor outcome, a donor asks for an explanation, a beneficiary challenges a decision, and the organization discovers it cannot reconstruct the decision because the trail does not exist. The AI tool made the decision, the decision was implemented, and no record was created of what the tool was asked to do, what it produced, and how a human reviewed and acted on the output. The problem is not that the AI was wrong — it is that the organization cannot demonstrate the process that produced the decision, which means it cannot demonstrate that the process was adequate.

4. **AI tool model version continuity: silent vendor model updates creating documentation obsolescence without organizational awareness** — Geneva NGOs operating AI tools from 2021–2023 are facing a specific documentation obsolescence problem: the AI tool configuration that was in operation when the tool was adopted is no longer the current configuration, because the vendor has updated the underlying model one or more times since deployment. The organization has no record of when model updates occurred, which model version was in operation during which program period, and whether governance documentation written for the original configuration still describes the current configuration. Silent model updates create a chronic documentation drift problem: governance documents describe a tool configuration that no longer exists.

5. **Organizational memory loss on AI tool departure: knowledge about AI-assisted decisions embedded in individual practice rather than organizational systems** — When the program manager or operations staff who managed an AI tool deployment leaves the organization, the AI governance knowledge they held leaves with them: which tool was in use, what decisions were made with AI assistance, how the tool was configured, what the review process was, what incidents occurred and how they were handled. This organizational memory problem is distinct from general staff departure knowledge loss: AI governance knowledge is embedded in operational practice rather than documented in organizational systems, and it disappears when the person who held it leaves — along with the accountability trail for AI-assisted decisions made during their tenure.

6. **Consent form lag: FADP-compliant consent frameworks deployed before AI tools, now in use for AI-assisted decisions without being updated to reflect AI processing** — Geneva NGOs that deployed FADP-compliant consent frameworks in 2020–2022 are now using those same frameworks for AI-assisted beneficiary communications and decisions without amendment. The consent forms describe manual data processing. The actual processing now includes AI-assisted components. The consent framework does not reflect this. When a beneficiary challenges an AI-assisted decision, or when a regulator examines the consent architecture against actual data processing, the consent form does not disclose AI processing. The lag is not from deliberate concealment — it is from the sequential gap between consent framework deployment and AI tool adoption: organizations built consent infrastructure before AI tools were deployed, and the consent infrastructure was never rebuilt to reflect the new processing reality.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### Gaps — What No One Is Addressing for Geneva NGO/Tech

- **Grant agreement AI obligation pre-acceptance review: a methodology for Geneva NGOs to evaluate what donor AI audit and reporting obligations they are accepting before signing, and what the realistic compliance timeline is** — No advisory product addresses the specific pre-signing review problem for AI governance obligations in grant agreements: organizations need to know, before signing, what AI audit rights they are conceding, what documentation obligations those rights create, and what the realistic timeline is for building the infrastructure to satisfy those obligations. The product design target is not a legal review — it is an operational readiness assessment that maps the gap between what the donor's AI clause requires and what the organization can currently demonstrate, and produces a signed implementation commitment that the organization can actually deliver on rather than one that will create compliance exposure when the donor exercises its audit rights.

- **Post-incident AI decision reconstruction protocol: a first-72-hour methodology for Geneva NGOs to document AI-assisted decisions when something goes wrong, before the decision trail disappears** — No advisory methodology addresses the specific problem of post-incident AI decision reconstruction in small NGOs: when an incident involving an AI-assisted decision occurs, program staff can often still reconstruct what happened in the first 48-72 hours before memory degrades and configurations change. After that window, reconstruction becomes impossible. The product design target is a post-incident protocol that gives program managers a structured way to capture the decision trail while it still exists — not a legal document, but an operational reconstruction methodology that preserves the information needed to explain what the AI did and how it was reviewed before the window closes.

- **Model version continuity documentation for resource-constrained NGOs: a lightweight method for tracking AI tool changes over time that does not require technical infrastructure or dedicated IT capacity** — No advisory methodology addresses the specific problem of AI tool version tracking for organizations without dedicated IT: organizations need to know when their AI tools changed, which model version was running during which program period, and whether governance documentation written for the original configuration still applies. The product design target is a version continuity method that uses existing organizational record-keeping — tool selection decisions documented at the time they are made, configuration change records when they occur — rather than creating a new technical documentation infrastructure. The key constraint: the solution must be implementable by a program manager without technical IT expertise.

- **Organizational AI governance knowledge retention: a methodology for documenting AI governance decisions at the time they are made, so that knowledge does not disappear when the person who held it leaves** — No advisory methodology addresses the specific problem of AI governance knowledge retention in small Geneva NGOs: the person who manages an AI tool deployment holds knowledge about how the tool was configured, what decisions were made with AI assistance, and how the review process worked — and that knowledge disappears when they leave. The product design target is a one-page AI governance decision record that gets created when an AI tool is adopted and updated when the configuration changes — a living organizational record rather than a personal memory artifact — that survives staff departures and builds organizational AI governance capability over time.

- **Consent form AI disclosure gap remediation: a methodology for Geneva NGOs to audit existing consent frameworks against actual AI processing and develop compliant amendments without requiring full consent architecture reconstruction** — No advisory product addresses the specific consent form lag problem: organizations have FADP-compliant consent forms that do not disclose AI processing, and they need a methodology for determining what amendments are required, what the compliant disclosure language is, and how to implement the amendments without requiring a full consent framework redesign. The product design target is a consent architecture audit that identifies where AI-assisted decisions are being made under consent forms that do not disclose them, and produces amendment language that closes the disclosure gap without requiring the organization to rebuild its entire consent framework from scratch.

- **Geneva NGO AI governance infrastructure vs. IT infrastructure: distinguishing AI governance documentation from technology documentation, and building governance capacity that does not require expanded IT resources** — No advisory practice has addressed the specific distinction between AI governance capacity and IT capacity for Geneva NGOs. Most organizations understand that they have limited IT capacity. Fewer understand that AI governance is a distinct capability — it requires documentation, process design, and accountability mapping that is separate from IT infrastructure. The advisory gap is helping organizations understand that AI governance capacity can be built within existing operational workflows without requiring IT infrastructure expansion — by embedding AI governance documentation into existing program management processes rather than building a parallel governance IT system.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Specific Obligations

1. **ZEWO audit evolution toward operational AI governance adequacy: certification audits increasingly check whether AI governance documents match operational reality, not just whether documents exist** — ZEWO certification audits are tightening their examination of AI governance: audits that previously checked for document existence are now probing whether AI governance documents describe the tools actually in operation, whether reviews have been conducted when tool configurations changed, and whether the governance documents are current relative to the AI deployment. For small Geneva NGOs with AI governance documents that were approved properly but have not been reviewed since tool deployments changed, the adequacy gap is now being detected in audits. The compliance risk is not in the absence of a policy — it is in the documentation staleness relative to operational reality that adequacy auditing surfaces.

2. **Grant agreement AI reporting obligations as binding commitments: when a donor AI questionnaire is answered without an AI inventory, the response creates representations the organization may not be able to support** — Swiss contract law principles mean that answers to donor AI questionnaires create representations about the organization's AI governance that have contractual effect: if the organization represents that it has AI governance documentation for specific tool deployments, and it does not, that misrepresentation creates legal exposure under the grant agreement. The compliance risk is not in the questionnaire itself — it is in organizations responding to donor AI questionnaires without having first established the AI inventory the questionnaire assumes. The 72-hour response window that donors often impose makes this worse: organizations produce responses under deadline pressure that create contractual representations they cannot support.

3. **FADP Art. 21 human review rights for AI-assisted beneficiary decisions: the operational mechanism for providing human review on request does not exist in most Geneva NGOs** — FADP Art. 21 gives data subjects the right to request human review of solely automated decisions producing legal or similarly significant effects. When a Geneva NGO uses an AI tool to assist in beneficiary eligibility determinations, service matching, or resource allocation, and a beneficiary requests human review, the NGO must be able to provide it through a defined operational process. Most Geneva NGOs have not built this process: the AI system makes or assists the decision, the beneficiary requests review, and the organization has no defined procedure for conducting it. This is a specific compliance gap that requires an operational process, not a policy statement.

4. **FADP Art. 13 transparency disclosure: consent forms must inform data subjects about AI-assisted processing, and most existing consent frameworks do not** — FADP Art. 13 requires that data subjects be informed about the logic involved in automated processing, in intelligible form. When an NGO uses an AI tool to assist in decisions affecting beneficiaries, and the consent form does not disclose that AI is involved in the decision, the consent form does not meet the Art. 13 transparency standard. The compliance gap is not in the consent form language itself — it is in the operational reality that most Geneva NGO consent forms were designed before AI tools were deployed, and the AI-assisted processing component was added to operations without amending the consent framework to disclose it.

5. **AI tool model version change documentation under FADP Art. 31 data processing agreement obligations: silent vendor model updates may constitute material processing changes that require notification** — When a Geneva NGO uses an AI tool under a data processing agreement with the vendor, and the vendor updates the underlying model, this may constitute a material change in processing that requires the NGO to assess whether the change affects its own compliance obligations, and potentially to notify affected data subjects. Most data processing agreements do not specify what happens when the vendor changes the model — the change occurs silently, and the NGO may not know to reassess. This creates a compliance gap: the organization continues operating the tool under an outdated model version assumption while the vendor has already updated the model, and the organization has not conducted the reassessment that the change in processing should have triggered.

6. **Board member AI governance ongoing oversight under Swiss association law: approving a policy once does not discharge the ongoing obligation as tool deployments evolve** — Swiss association law holds board members to an ongoing oversight obligation for technology decisions. Approving an AI governance policy once creates a baseline — it does not discharge the ongoing obligation to maintain governance currency as AI tool deployments evolve. Board members who approved AI governance documents two years ago, and have not reviewed them as tool deployments changed, cannot demonstrate that the oversight obligation was maintained. The personal liability exposure is not in the policy's existence — it is in the absence of documented ongoing oversight as the operational AI reality changed.

7. **AI governance documentation staleness as an independent compliance risk: governance documents that were adequate when approved but have not been updated create a false conformance impression that is more dangerous than no documents** — AI governance documentation that was properly approved but has not been reviewed since tool deployments changed creates a specific compliance risk: it produces the impression of conformance without the substance. In a donor audit or ZEWO certification review, the organization can show a properly approved AI governance document. The auditor then examines operational reality and finds that the document does not describe the tools actually in operation. The documentation gap — the fact that the governance document is stale relative to operational reality — is more damaging than if no document existed, because it demonstrates that the organization had governance documentation and did not maintain it, rather than simply not having gotten to AI governance yet.

8. **AI tool vendor wind-down as program continuity risk: when a vendor discontinues an AI tool or is acquired, the Geneva NGO may face program disruption with no contractual protection for continuity** — Small Geneva NGOs frequently use AI tools through vendor subscription arrangements that have no program continuity protections: if the vendor discontinues the tool, is acquired, or changes terms in ways that make the tool unaffordable, the NGO faces program disruption with no contractual right to continuity. FADP Art. 31 requires specific provisions in data processing agreements for what happens when processing relationships change, but small NGOs often accept vendor terms without negotiating these provisions. The compliance risk is not just data protection — it is program continuity: AI tools embedded in program delivery may have no contractual fallback if the vendor relationship changes.

## 06 — Findings Summary

### 6 Net-New Findings (Distinct from C214–C232 Phase 04)

1. **Donor AI reporting deadlines arriving before organizations have the AI inventory to respond — time-bound questionnaire deadlines that expose the documentation gap rather than allowing organizations to close it** — Prior cycles identified grant agreement AI audit rights pre-signing gaps (C232, C231), donor AI representation falsification exposure (C224), and multi-donor documentation fragmentation (C231). C233 identifies the specific temporal problem: donor AI questionnaires arrive under short response deadlines (72 hours is common) that do not give organizations time to establish the AI inventory the questionnaire assumes, forcing organizations to respond without inventory — producing representations they cannot support. This is distinct from prior cycles by focusing on the deadline structure as the mechanism creating the problem, not just the obligation itself: the compliance risk is not that donor AI clauses exist, but that the response window is too short for organizations to answer accurately.

2. **AI governance consulting producing correct documents without changing operational AI governance — policy documents approved and filed while tool deployments remain ungoverned and undocumented** — Prior cycles identified governance documentation theater (C228, C229) and compliance theater as a discrete organizational behavior (C225). C233 identifies the specific mechanism: AI governance consulting engagements that produce technically correct, properly approved policy documents that do not affect operational AI governance because the engagement did not address operational tool deployments. This is distinct from governance documentation theater by identifying the consulting delivery model as the mechanism producing the failure, not just the outcome. The advisory entry point is changing the consulting engagement scope from policy production to operational AI inventory — not a better document, but a different document that describes what is actually running.

3. **Post-incident AI decision reconstruction as the primary operational AI governance failure mode — the organization cannot explain what the AI tool did because the decision trail was never documented** — No prior cycle addressed post-incident AI decision reconstruction as a discrete failure mode. C233 identifies this as the primary operational AI governance failure: the AI tool operated normally, a decision was made, an incident occurred, and the organization cannot reconstruct the decision process because no trail was created. This is distinct from the general AI governance documentation gap by specifically identifying the temporal dimension: the reconstruction window is narrow (48-72 hours), after which the trail disappears regardless of what governance infrastructure the organization later builds. The advisory entry point is not preventing incidents — it is preserving the reconstruction capacity to explain them.

4. **Silent AI tool model version updates creating chronic documentation obsolescence — vendor model changes applied without organizational awareness, producing governance documents that describe configurations that no longer exist** — Prior cycles identified AI vendor wind-down contingency (C223, C224) and AI vendor infrastructure decay (C225). C233 identifies a distinct chronic problem: the silent model update, where the vendor changes the underlying model and the organization does not know, continues operating under the original configuration assumption, and has governance documents that describe a tool configuration that no longer exists. This is distinct from wind-down by specifically identifying the silent steady-state drift problem, not the acute discontinuation problem: organizations may have had their model updated twice without knowing, and their governance documentation describes configurations two generations obsolete.

5. **Organizational AI governance memory loss as a distinct failure mode on staff departure — knowledge embedded in individual practice rather than organizational systems, disappearing when the person who held it leaves** — Prior cycles identified knowledge continuity risk (C223, C224, C226) and prompt library governance as firm IP succession problem (C233 Phase 03). C233 identifies the specific organizational memory loss failure mode for NGOs: the person who managed an AI tool deployment holds knowledge about which decisions were AI-assisted, how the tool was configured, and what the review process was — and that knowledge leaves with them when they depart, leaving no organizational record of AI-assisted decisions made during their tenure. This is distinct from knowledge continuity risk by specifically identifying the NGO memory loss mechanism: not strategic IP erosion, but the disappearance of operational AI governance knowledge when the person who held it leaves.

6. **Consent form lag as a systemic FADP disclosure gap — consent frameworks designed before AI tools were deployed, now in use for AI-assisted decisions without amendment** — Prior cycles identified consent form architecture disconnection (C229), FADP Art. 13 beneficiary rights conflict (C230), and FADP beneficiary comprehension gap (C223, C226). C233 identifies the specific sequential mechanism: organizations deployed FADP-compliant consent frameworks before AI tools were adopted, and those frameworks are now in use for AI-assisted beneficiary decisions without amendment. The disclosure gap is not from deliberate concealment — it is from the sequential gap between consent framework deployment and AI tool adoption, which most organizations did not close because they did not know they needed to. This is distinct from consent form disconnection by identifying the lag mechanism as a systemic sequential problem rather than a design flaw: the forms were correct when deployed, they became inadequate when AI tools were added without corresponding consent framework updates.
