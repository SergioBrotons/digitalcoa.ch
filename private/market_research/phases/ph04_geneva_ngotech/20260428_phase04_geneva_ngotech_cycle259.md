## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Donor AI governance questionnaire as organizational discovery mechanism**
The questionnaire surfaces governance gaps that have been compounding silently. The NGO did not know it had the gap because there was no mechanism for discovering it before the questionnaire arrived.

**Signal 2: Beneficiary nFADP rights questions arriving before compliance architecture**
Beneficiaries are asking about AI-inferred data and correction rights. The questions activate nFADP obligations the NGO did not design for. The architecture for responding does not exist.

**Signal 3: Vendor AI updates as unevaluated program integrity events**
Vendor updates change AI tool behavior without evaluation protocol. The change may affect program delivery without the NGO knowing. The unmanaged event creates exposure invisible until something goes wrong.

**Signal 4: Board-approved AI strategy as historical document without review trigger**
Board-approved strategies that are not reviewed become historical documents. The review gap is structural; board governance is not designed to trigger AI strategy review.

**Signal 5: AI cost invisibility as governance paralysis**

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Donor AI governance questionnaire preparation service**
No advisory service exists for preparing NGOs to respond to donor AI governance questionnaires before the deadline. The preparation would need to address what documentation exists, what is missing, and what to do about the gap.

**Gap 2: Beneficiary nFADP rights response architecture for AI-inferred data**
No architecture exists for responding to beneficiary nFADP rights questions about AI-inferred data. The architecture would need to address what data exists, what rights are activated, and how to discharge them.

**Gap 3: Vendor AI update evaluation protocol for NGOs**
No protocol exists for evaluating vendor AI updates and their effect on program delivery. The protocol would need to specify what to assess when updates arrive and how to document the evaluation.

**Gap 4: AI strategy board review trigger architecture**
No architecture exists for triggering board AI strategy review at appropriate intervals. The architecture would need to define what triggers review and how to document that it occurred.

**Gap 5: AI cost visibility architecture for NGO budget governance**
No architecture exists for making AI costs visible as a governance object. The architecture would need to address how to classify and aggregate costs across budget lines.

**Gap 6: Field AI output quality assurance for multi-language delivery**
No protocol exists for quality assurance of AI outputs in languages the organization cannot review. The protocol would need to address how to maintain quality when the review function is structurally absent.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**ZEWO certification and AI governance documentation:**
ZEWO certification examines documentation completeness, not governance adequacy. NGOs with documentation satisfying ZEWO requirements may still have governance gaps that certification does not surface.

**Institutional donor AI conditions in grant agreements:**
Institutional donors are inserting AI governance conditions into grant agreements. The conditions create obligations NGOs sign without evaluating. The interpretation burden falls on the NGO with least capacity to assess it.

**nFADP Articles 13 and 21 beneficiary rights for AI-inferred data:**
Art. 13 requires explanations of automated decisions affecting data subjects. Art. 21 requires documentation of AI-assisted decision logic. AI-inferred beneficiary data activates these obligations in ways directly collected data does not.

**Art. 75 ZGB board member personal liability for AI oversight:**
Board members approving AI strategies without adequate review mechanisms face personal liability under Art. 75 ZGB. The liability is active and specific. The board governance gap is a fiduciary duty problem, not a documentation problem.

**Data sovereignty in international NGO operations:**
NGO operations spanning multiple jurisdictions create data sovereignty questions that nFADP alone does not resolve. The intersection of Swiss data protection law with field country requirements creates compliance complexity no framework addresses.

**Vendor AI updates as missing trigger in governance frameworks:**
No Geneva NGO governance framework includes vendor AI updates as a governance trigger. The omission means the most significant changes to AI tool behavior are not reviewed as governance events.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Donor AI governance questionnaire as organizational discovery mechanism for governance gaps that compound silently**
The questionnaire surfaces governance gaps that have been compounding silently. The NGO did not know it had the gap because there was no discovery mechanism before the questionnaire arrived. The discovery is costly because the gap has had time to grow.

**Finding 2: Beneficiary nFADP rights questions arriving before compliance architecture for AI-inferred data**
AI-inferred beneficiary data activates nFADP rights that NGOs have no architecture for responding to. The data exists; the rights exist; the response capability does not. The gap is structural and present-tense.

**Finding 3: Vendor AI update as program integrity event with no evaluation protocol and no documentation requirement**
Vendor AI updates are program integrity events that go unevaluated and undocumented. The update may have changed how the AI tool works in ways that affect program delivery. The unmanaged event creates exposure invisible until something surfaces it adversarially.

**Finding 4: Board-approved AI strategy as historical document with no trigger for review and no architecture for conducting it**
Board-approved AI strategies become historical documents without a trigger for review or an architecture for conducting it. The vendor landscape changes; the strategy does not. Board governance is not designed to address this structural gap.

**Finding 5: AI cost dispersion as governance visibility failure creating budget governance paralysis**
AI costs dispersed across budget lines create governance visibility failure. The board cannot make decisions about AI investment because it cannot see total AI costs. The paralysis is organizational; the consequence is governance failure.

**Finding 6: Field AI output quality assurance as structurally absent function with no market solution for multi-language delivery**
Field AI outputs in languages the organization cannot review create a structurally absent quality assurance function. No market solution exists for multi-language AI output quality assurance in resource-constrained NGO operations. The gap is architectural, not operational.
