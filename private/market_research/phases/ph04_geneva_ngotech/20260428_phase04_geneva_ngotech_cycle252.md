## 02 — Market Signals

Current problems, failure modes, paradoxes, and complaints heard in this ICP's own language.

### Signal 1: Field Team AI Deployment as Organizational Inventory Blind Spot

Geneva NGOs are discovering that field teams have adopted AI tools for program communication, beneficiary engagement, and content production — tools configured and deployed without central IT or program management awareness. The tools are processing beneficiary data, generating outputs that enter donor reporting chains, and creating data protection obligations the organization is not managing. When ZEWO reviews or donor AI questionnaires ask for an inventory of AI tools in use, the field-deployed tools are invisible to the central function. The discovery happens reactively: when a tool changes terms, when a beneficiary exercises rights, when a donor asks a specific question. The pattern is not new in principle, but the specific organizational dynamics that produce it in NGO contexts — field teams operating with high autonomy, program urgency overriding governance considerations, limited Geneva-based IT oversight capacity — are consistent across the sector and constitute a structural inventory problem.

### Signal 2: Donor Platform AI Scoring as Uncontested Accountability Input

Geneva NGOs submitting data to donor-operated platforms that use AI to score program indicators are treating the AI-generated scores as objective performance data without examining the methodology. When a score drops, the NGO absorbs the drop as a program performance fact — reporting it to the board, incorporating it into donor communications — without knowing whether the drop reflects actual program performance or an artifact of how the platform's AI processes the data. The signal is distinct from general AI explainability concerns: it concerns specifically the dynamic where the NGO has no visibility into a platform operator's AI, cannot challenge the output, and cannot reconstruct the methodology if the score is later used against the organization. The donor platform AI becomes a de facto accountability instrument that the NGO did not evaluate and cannot contest.

### Signal 3: Beneficiary nFADP Rights Activation Against AI-Assisted Case Management Outputs

Geneva NGOs receiving beneficiary data subject access requests under nFADP — particularly in AI-assisted case management, protection, or benefit eligibility contexts — are encountering a specific problem that general data protection guidance does not address: the AI system produces synthesized conclusions about the beneficiary (risk classifications, vulnerability scores, need rankings) that go beyond the source data. When the beneficiary asks what data the organization has and what the AI concluded, the NGO cannot separate the source data it can disclose from the AI-generated inference it may not be required to disclose, or cannot explain. The rights activation is increasing as beneficiary awareness of data protection rights grows, and the NGO's operational response capacity has not developed in pace with the obligation.

### Signal 4: Donor AI Condition Interpretation Burden Falling on Least-Resourced Party

Institutional donors imposing AI governance conditions in grant agreements — using language drawn from EU AI principles, SDC guidelines, UNDP AI frameworks — are creating an interpretation obligation on Geneva NGOs that the NGOs are least equipped to carry. The donor's AI condition language was written for a different institutional context and assumes institutional capacity that a 10–100 person Geneva NGO does not have. The NGO signs the grant agreement, absorbing the obligation, without mechanisms for determining what the condition requires in its specific operational context. The donor is not actively monitoring compliance in most cases — but the obligation is contractual and binding, and the interpretation burden compounds silently until a due diligence review, a program audit, or a funder AI questionnaire surfaces the gap.

### Signal 5: AI-Generated Beneficiary Communications Without Geneva-Based Review

Field teams at Geneva NGOs are using AI tools to draft beneficiary communications — in displacement contexts, protection case updates, benefit notifications — and distributing them directly without Geneva-based editorial review. The communications are going to beneficiaries in contexts where the stakes are high: asylum processes, resettlement decisions, benefit eligibility determinations. The AI-generated text is in languages the Geneva team does not read fluently. The field team making the distribution decision has no escalation pathway to Geneva for AI-generated content review. The pattern is driven by operational efficiency and distance from beneficiaries, not by governance choice. The signal is the beneficiary reading AI-generated text about their own case in Arabic or Farsi, produced by a system nobody in Geneva has reviewed.

### Signal 6: Board AI Agenda Items Using Vendor-Provided Approval Language

Geneva NGO boards approving AI-related agenda items are receiving resolution language proposed by executives working from vendor-provided templates — language written by the entity that benefits from the board's approval, reviewed by no one with independent judgment about what the language commits the organization to. The board member signs a resolution in a language they did not draft and cannot evaluate, creating Art. 75 ZGB personal liability for governance decisions they are structurally unable to assess. The paradox is structural: the governance event that should produce the strongest board-level scrutiny produces the least, because the board lacks the specific technical and legal background to identify vendor-biased approval language.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

Gaps, and what no one is addressing specifically for this ICP and geography.

### Gap 1: AI Tool Discovery and Inventory Protocol for Field-Heavy NGOs

No market participant has developed an AI tool discovery and inventory protocol specifically for Geneva NGOs with field teams operating with high autonomy and limited Geneva-based IT oversight. The protocol would need to specify: how to identify AI tools field teams have adopted without central awareness, how to assess whether those tools are processing beneficiary data, what minimum immediate action is required when an undocumented tool is discovered, and how to establish an ongoing inventory mechanism that does not require IT infrastructure. The specific organizational dynamics of NGO field operations — high distance between field and Geneva, program urgency, limited communications bandwidth — make standard IT discovery tools inapplicable. The gap is actionable and immediate: every Geneva NGO with field operations has undocumented AI tools in use.

### Gap 2: Donor Platform AI Scoring Accountability Mapping

No market participant has developed an accountability mapping methodology for Geneva NGOs submitting data to donor-operated AI-assisted platforms. The methodology would specify: what the submitting organization's accountability position is when the platform's AI produces a disputed score, what minimum information the NGO should obtain from the platform operator before submitting data, what the NGO's obligation is to disclose to its own board that program performance data has been AI-processed by a third party, and how to contest a platform AI score when the methodology is not disclosed. The gap affects every NGO participating in donor data platforms, and the problem is accelerating as donors increase AI-assisted data quality scoring.

### Gap 3: AI-Generated Beneficiary Communication Review Protocol

No protocol exists for Geneva NGOs to maintain editorial oversight of AI-generated beneficiary communications when field teams operate in languages Geneva staff do not read. The protocol would need to specify: what minimum review standard applies to AI-generated beneficiary communications before distribution, how to establish a review mechanism that does not create operational paralysis, what the field team's authorization is to distribute AI-generated content without Geneva-based review, and how to manage the risk when beneficiaries receive AI-generated communications in high-stakes contexts without prior human review of the specific content. The market gap is specific to the language and distance constraints that NGO field operations create.

### Gap 4: Beneficiary Rights Discharge Methodology for AI-Inferred Content

No market participant has developed a beneficiary rights discharge methodology specifically for Geneva NGOs in AI-assisted program contexts where the AI system produces synthesized conclusions about beneficiaries. The methodology would need to specify: how to separate source data disclosure from AI inference disclosure when a beneficiary exercises access rights, what the NGO's obligation is to explain AI-generated conclusions about the beneficiary, how to handle requests for deletion of AI-inferred classifications that are not stored as discrete data fields, and how to document the discharge of Art. 21 nFADP explanation obligations in program delivery contexts. The methodology must be implementable by program staff without legal background and must not require access to AI system internals that the NGO does not have.

### Gap 5: Grant Agreement AI Condition Pre-Signing Interpretation Protocol

No pre-signing interpretation protocol exists for Geneva NGOs evaluating institutional donor AI governance conditions before signing grant agreements. The protocol would need to specify: how to identify which AI governance conditions in a grant agreement create binding obligations that the organization can and cannot fulfill, how to assess whether the organization's current AI governance state meets the conditions as the donor likely interprets them, what minimum interpretation requests should be made to the donor before signing, and how to structure grant agreement AI language to create accountability for the funder as well as the grantee. The protocol would need to work within the power asymmetry of funder-grantee relationships where challenging donor language during negotiation is structurally difficult.

### Gap 6: Vendor-Provided Board Resolution Language Audit

No market participant has developed a specific audit methodology for Geneva NGO boards to evaluate vendor-provided or vendor-influenced AI approval language before signing board resolutions. The methodology would need to specify: how to identify when board resolution language for AI approvals was sourced from vendor templates, what minimum content a board resolution for AI deployment should contain independently of vendor-proposed language, what the board member's personal liability exposure is under Art. 75 ZGB when approving AI governance language they did not independently evaluate, and how to establish an internal review function that does not require external legal counsel for every board resolution. The specific problem — vendor templates creating the appearance of governance while containing terms that benefit the vendor — is structural and unaddressed in the Geneva NGO advisory market.

## 04 — Compliance Context

Geneva / Swiss-specific obligations material to this ICP.

### ZEWO Certification — Field-Level AI Tool Discovery as Certification Exposure

ZEWO audit processes for Geneva-certified NGOs are beginning to probe AI governance at the field operations level, not only at the Geneva headquarters level. The audit scope is expanding to include program delivery sites where AI tools may be in use without Geneva-based IT awareness. A ZEWO audit that identifies undocumented AI tools in field operations creates a certification exposure that organizations are not prepared to address: the organization cannot demonstrate governance over tools it did not know were in use. The compliance context is specific to Geneva's ZEWO framework: the certification audit is probing governance infrastructure, not just policy existence, and the discovery of field-deployed AI tools without corresponding governance documentation constitutes an audit finding before any assessment of whether the tools themselves are appropriate.

### Institutional Donor AI Conditions — EU Framework Language and Interpretation Burden

Institutional donors funding Geneva NGOs — including EU development funding streams, SDC bilateral programs, and large private foundations with AI governance policies — are increasingly using AI condition language drawn from EU and international frameworks that assume institutional capacity beyond what Geneva NGOs at the 10–100 employee scale possess. The specific interpretation burden falls on the Geneva NGO: each funder's AI language references a framework the NGO must interpret and implement without access to interpretive guidance. The compliance context is contractual: the obligation is binding through the grant agreement regardless of whether the NGO has the capacity to interpret it. The non-coordination across institutional donors means a Geneva NGO with multiple funders may face AI conditions that are not reconcilable with each other, and the NGO bears the interpretation burden without support.

### nFADP — Active Obligations Across Multiple Articles

The nFADP creates specific active compliance exposures for Geneva NGOs in AI-assisted program contexts:

**Art. 8 Purpose Limitation:** AI tools deployed in field operations to solve immediate operational problems — beneficiary communications, case management, program monitoring — are processing beneficiary data for purposes that were not evaluated at deployment. The purpose limitation obligation requires that data collected for specific program purposes not be used for different purposes without separate authorization. In NGO field operations where AI tool adoption is driven by operational need rather than compliance planning, the purpose limitation gap is structural from the point of deployment.

**Art. 13 Right of Access:** Beneficiaries in AI-assisted program contexts exercising the right to access their data may receive outputs that include AI-generated summaries, risk classifications, or vulnerability assessments — synthesized content that goes beyond the source data and that the NGO may not be obligated to disclose, or may not be able to disclose coherently. No Geneva NGO has developed an operational protocol for managing Art. 13 access requests in AI-assisted program contexts.

**Art. 21 Right to Explanation:** Beneficiaries receiving AI-assisted decisions or communications in program delivery contexts have a right to explanation under nFADP Art. 21. The obligation requires that the NGO be able to explain, in intelligible form, how the AI contributed to the decision or communication affecting the beneficiary. Program staff cannot fulfill this obligation because they do not have access to AI system reasoning, and the operational context — field-based communications in languages Geneva staff do not read — makes verification of AI-generated content before distribution structurally difficult.

**Art. 26 Data Disposal:** At program wind-down, when beneficiary data disposal obligations activate, the specific question of AI-inferred classifications — derived profiles that go beyond source data — has no Geneva-specific guidance. The disposal obligation under nFADP Art. 26 was drafted before AI inference became a standard program tool and does not address the specific disposal question for synthesized beneficiary profiles.

### Art. 75 ZGB — Board Approval Language Under Vendor Influence

Board members at Geneva NGOs approving AI-related resolutions are often approving language drafted from vendor-provided templates — language written by the entity that benefits from the approval. Under Art. 75 ZGB, board members bear personal liability for fiduciary decisions made without adequate information. The specific structural problem is that AI approval language sourced from vendor templates creates the appearance of a proper governance decision while the substantive content of the decision was written by a party with commercial interest in the outcome. The compliance context is specific to Geneva's NGO governance culture: board resolutions are often prepared by executives with limited independent legal background, reviewed briefly, and approved — a process that is adequate for routine governance items and structurally inadequate for AI approvals that create personal liability under Art. 75 ZGB.

### Cross-Border Data Protection in Field Operations

Geneva NGOs operating programs in displacement, conflict, and post-conflict contexts face specific data protection obligations that are compounded by AI tool use. The data protection law of the country of operation may impose requirements that conflict with Swiss nFADP compliance assumptions, and the AI tool processing beneficiary data in a field context may be subject to both frameworks simultaneously. The Geneva-specific compliance consideration is the intersection: the NGO's Geneva headquarters is subject to nFADP, the field operations are subject to local data protection law, and the AI tool may be subject to both — creating compliance architecture that requires coordination the organization does not have.

## 06 — Findings Summary

Six net-new insights from this cycle, distinct from all C214–C251 Phase 04 findings.

### Finding 1: Field AI Inventory Blind Spot as Structural Organizational Discovery Problem

Geneva NGOs with field operations are discovering that AI tools have been adopted by field teams without Geneva-based awareness — tools processing beneficiary data, generating outputs that enter donor reporting chains, and creating data protection obligations the organization is not managing. This finding is distinct from prior "staff-initiated AI deployment without organizational awareness" findings (C250) and "policy-without-enforcement" findings (C242, C249) because it addresses specifically the inventory discovery problem: the organization doesn't know what it has, and when it discovers a field-deployed tool, the assessment of whether it's appropriate must happen under conditions of existing deployment — the tool has already processed data, the outputs are already in use. The prior cycles addressed awareness and governance response; this finding addresses the specific organizational discovery mechanism that makes field AI deployment structurally invisible until a triggering event surfaces it.

### Finding 2: Donor Platform AI Scoring as Uncontested Accountability Input

Geneva NGOs submitting data to donor-operated AI-assisted platforms are treating AI-generated performance scores as objective program data without evaluating the platform AI methodology. This finding is distinct from prior "donor dashboard AI dependency" findings (C249) which addressed the structural dependency problem — owning accountability records in infrastructure you don't control. This finding addresses specifically the accountability consequence when the platform's AI produces a contested score: the NGO reports the score to its board as program performance data without knowing whether the drop is real or an AI processing artifact, and without a mechanism for contesting the score when the platform operator doesn't disclose the methodology. The distinct problem is the uncontested nature of the AI-generated input — the NGO absorbs the score as fact because it has no basis for evaluating or challenging it.

### Finding 3: Beneficiary Rights Activation Against AI-Inferred Content Without Discharge Methodology

Geneva NGOs in AI-assisted program contexts are receiving beneficiary data subject access requests under nFADP that require the organization to disclose AI-generated conclusions about the beneficiary — synthesized risk classifications, vulnerability scores, need rankings — that go beyond source data. This finding is distinct from prior "AI-inferred beneficiary profile disposal" findings (C249, C240) which addressed the program wind-down disposal question. This finding addresses the present-tense operational problem: when a beneficiary asks what the AI concluded about them, what is the NGO obligated to disclose, what can it withhold, and how does it explain AI-generated conclusions that the NGO cannot itself explain? No Geneva NGO has an operational discharge methodology for Art. 13 and Art. 21 requests in AI-assisted program delivery contexts.

### Finding 4: Grant Agreement AI Condition Interpretation Burden as Structural Capacity Mismatch

Institutional donors imposing AI governance conditions in grant agreements are creating an interpretation obligation on Geneva NGOs that falls on the least-resourced party in the relationship. This finding is distinct from prior "institutional donor AI framework implementation gap" findings (C241, C250) which described the accountability asymmetry — donor frameworks assuming infrastructure the NGO doesn't have — without naming the specific mechanism of the capacity mismatch: the interpretation burden is linguistic and analytical before it is operational. The NGO must read AI condition language written for a different institutional context and translate it into operational requirements for a 10–100 person organization without access to the interpretive guidance the donor used when writing the conditions. The problem is present-tense at the moment of grant signing, not a downstream implementation gap.

### Finding 5: AI-Generated Beneficiary Communications Without Geneva-Based Editorial Review

Field teams at Geneva NGOs distributing AI-generated beneficiary communications — in displacement, protection, and benefit eligibility contexts — without Geneva-based editorial review are creating a specific exposure that is distinct from general AI content quality concerns. This finding is distinct from prior "AI-assisted beneficiary communications accountability" findings (C237) which addressed the accountability chain for beneficiary-facing AI communications. This finding addresses specifically the language barrier and distance constraint: the field team distributes AI-generated text in Arabic or Farsi that Geneva staff cannot read, creating a distribution decision made without the review capacity that would be considered minimum standard for any other communications channel. The exposure is not about content quality — it's about the structural impossibility of editorial review across the language and distance constraints that NGO field operations create.

### Finding 6: Vendor-Provided Board Resolution Language as Structural Governance Capture

Geneva NGO boards approving AI-related resolutions are operating under Art. 75 ZGB personal liability while approving language drafted from vendor-provided templates — language written by the entity that commercially benefits from the approval. This finding is distinct from prior "board-approved AI policy staleness" findings (C242, C249) and "board member personal liability" findings (C240, C248) which addressed board liability and policy obsolescence. This finding addresses specifically the structural governance capture mechanism: the board's approval capacity is compromised not by insufficient diligence but by the source of the language itself — the board cannot evaluate what it did not write, and what it approved was written by a party with commercial interest in the outcome. The distinct insight is that governance appearance (board approved, resolution signed) is produced by a process that structurally precludes the informed judgment that Art. 75 ZGB fiduciary duty requires. No Geneva market participant has named vendor-provided board language as a discrete governance capture mechanism requiring a specific countermeasure.
