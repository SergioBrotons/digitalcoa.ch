## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: AI performance indicators diverging from actual beneficiary need without detection**
AI performance indicators used for beneficiary selection diverge from actual beneficiary need. The divergence is invisible in the indicator itself. Programs make allocation decisions based on rankings that do not reflect current reality.

**Signal 2: Board-approved AI governance as document not reflecting current state**
Board-approved AI governance documents become historical documents between annual presentations. Vendor updates, staff changes, and program drift accumulate without triggering review.

**Signal 3: Beneficiary nFADP rights questions arriving before any compliance architecture**
AI-inferred beneficiary data activates nFADP rights that NGOs have no architecture for responding to. The questions are arriving from more sophisticated beneficiaries than expected.

**Signal 4: AI cost invisibility across budget lines**
AI costs absorbed into program budgets, staff time, and general operations. The board cannot see total AI costs in a form that enables governance decisions.

**Signal 5: Donor AI governance questionnaire surfacing governance decisions that were never made**
Questionnaires arriving and surfacing that governance decisions were never made. The NGO is asked to produce a governance record for a process that did not exist.

**Signal 6: Vendor AI updates changing tool behavior without evaluation protocol**
Vendor updates change AI tool behavior without evaluation protocol. The change may affect program delivery without the NGO knowing.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: AI performance indicator audit for beneficiary selection**
No service exists for auditing AI performance indicators used for beneficiary selection. The audit would need to assess whether indicators reflect current reality and what the divergence means for program decisions.

**Gap 2: Board AI governance review trigger architecture**
No architecture exists for triggering board AI governance review at appropriate intervals. The architecture would need to define what triggers review between annual presentations.

**Gap 3: Beneficiary nFADP rights response architecture for AI-inferred data**
No architecture exists for responding to beneficiary nFADP rights questions about AI-inferred data. The architecture would need to address what data exists, what rights are activated, and how to discharge them.

**Gap 4: AI cost visibility architecture for NGO budget governance**
No architecture exists for making AI costs visible as a governance object. The architecture would need to address how to classify and aggregate costs across budget lines.

**Gap 5: Vendor AI update evaluation protocol for NGOs**
No protocol exists for evaluating vendor AI updates and their effect on program delivery. The protocol would need to specify what to assess when updates arrive.

**Gap 6: Field AI output quality assurance for multi-language delivery**
No protocol exists for quality assurance of AI outputs in languages the organization cannot review.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**ZEWO certification and AI governance documentation:**
ZEWO certification examines documentation completeness, not governance adequacy. NGOs with documentation satisfying ZEWO requirements may still have governance gaps that certification does not surface.

**Institutional donor AI conditions in grant agreements:**
Institutional donors are inserting AI governance conditions into grant agreements. The conditions create obligations NGOs sign without evaluating. The interpretation burden falls on the NGO with least capacity to assess it.

**nFADP Articles 13 and 21 beneficiary rights for AI-inferred data:**
Art. 13 requires explanations of automated decisions affecting data subjects. Art. 21 requires documentation of AI-assisted decision logic. AI-inferred beneficiary data activates these obligations in ways directly collected data does not.

**Art. 75 ZGB board member personal liability for AI oversight:**
Board members approving AI strategies without adequate review mechanisms face personal liability under Art. 75 ZGB. The liability is active and specific.

**Data sovereignty in international NGO operations:**
NGO operations spanning multiple jurisdictions create data sovereignty questions that nFADP alone does not resolve. The intersection of Swiss data protection law with field country requirements creates compliance complexity no framework addresses.

**Vendor AI updates as missing trigger in governance frameworks:**
No Geneva NGO governance framework includes vendor AI updates as a governance trigger.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: AI performance indicator divergence as beneficiary selection distortion mechanism**
AI performance indicators used for beneficiary selection diverge from actual beneficiary need through data staleness and model update gaps. The distortion mechanism is different from indicator quality — it is a structural property of how AI systems maintain relevance over time. No Geneva NGO has a protocol for detecting when AI performance indicators have drifted from reality.

**Finding 2: Geneva framework producer conflict of interest as structural blind spot in standard-setting**
Geneva institutional actors producing AI governance standards are often also consumers of those standards in their own operations. The conflict of interest is structural, not incidental. No Geneva publication has named this conflict as a governance problem.

**Finding 3: Beneficiary consent gap for AI inference as upstream legal basis failure**
Beneficiary consent for data collection does not cover AI inference on that data. The upstream legal basis failure means all downstream AI-assisted decisions inherit the failure. No Geneva NGO has a consent architecture that addresses AI inference separately from data collection.

**Finding 4: Mid-program AI model deprecation as ungoverned continuity event**
When an AI model is deprecated mid-program, the program's recommendations may no longer be reproducible under the new model. The continuity event has no governance protocol. No Geneva NGO has a continuity plan for mid-program model deprecation.

**Finding 5: Downstream AI accountability inheritance through unmonitored referral chains**
AI-influenced decisions create accountability that inherits through referral chains NGOs do not monitor. When a beneficiary is referred to another organization, the AI-influenced categorization follows. No framework exists for managing this inherited accountability.

**Finding 6: ZEWO recertification window period as positioning mechanism for AI governance expectations**
Between ZEWO recertification cycles, AI governance expectations crystallize without published guidance. Organizations that position during this window have an advantage over those that react after recertification. The window period is a named positioning occasion.
