## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Grantee AI output inheritance as donor-chain accountability without review**

Geneva lead NGOs absorbing partner AI-generated outputs and passing them upward through the donor chain without reviewing the underlying methodology. The partner's AI tool, training data, and inference logic are unknown. The lead NGO is including AI-generated conclusions about its own program in reports to donors as if they were internally produced analytical conclusions. The structural property: the accountability record entering donor reports is generated by a system the lead NGO has not evaluated, cannot explain, and has no standing to challenge. The donor receives an AI-generated program assessment that the NGO cannot substantiate. This is distinct from the general implementing partner cascade problem — the specific issue is AI output inheritance through the donor reporting chain creating accountability records the NGO cannot stand behind.

**Signal 2: AI-assisted beneficiary communications in unreadable languages as operational liability**

Field AI-assisted beneficiary communications in languages Geneva staff cannot verify, approved on the basis of communication intent rather than verified content accuracy. The AI translation may preserve or alter meaning without Geneva-level detection capability. The approval decision was made without the information required to make it. The structural property: the organization approved and distributed AI-generated communications it cannot independently verify, creating beneficiary communications that are operational facts but not verified content. This is distinct from the general beneficiary communication accountability concern — the specific issue is the combination of language verification incapacity and AI translation opacity that makes accuracy invisible to those responsible for approval.

**Signal 3: Vendor model update re-evaluation trigger as structurally unaddressed governance event**

When AI tool vendors update their models, no governance protocol activates within the NGO to evaluate whether the update affects prior assessments, changes the organization's obligations to beneficiaries, or alters what the organization can represent about its AI-assisted decisions. The tool updates; the board is informed that the tool updated; the operational and legal implications of what changed in the model's decision logic are never evaluated. The structural property: model updates are treated as IT maintenance events rather than as governance events that should trigger a documentation and obligation review. The NGO continues operating under AI-generated conclusions that were produced under different model conditions than the current version.

**Signal 4: Board member personal AI tool use as unmanaged governance surface with personal liability**

Board members using personal AI tools for professional work, unaware that their personal AI tool use creates organizational data protection surface. Board member devices used for both personal AI tools and work-related files create a data governance boundary that was never defined. Swiss law creates personal liability for board members for decisions affecting organizational data protection. The organization has no visibility into this surface and no governance protocol for it. The structural property: the highest-governance body in the organization has an unmanaged AI surface that operates outside organizational governance infrastructure, creating personal liability for board members who do not know to manage it.

**Signal 5: Post-negative-outcome AI inference explainability as active exposure without formal complaint**

Beneficiaries receiving negative outcomes under AI-assisted triage systems asking for explanation of AI conclusions, with no producible answer. The exposure is present-tense and active — the question is unanswerable without the original inference record. The beneficiary has not filed a formal complaint, so the exposure is below organizational attention threshold. Swiss law and FADP create the obligation regardless of whether a formal complaint has been filed. The structural property: the organization knows it cannot answer the question if asked formally, has not addressed the underlying capacity gap, and is operating under AI-assisted decision systems that produce unexplainable outcomes while the exposure remains latent.

**Signal 6: AI governance policy-practice divergence undetected by certification audit**

NGOs passing ZEWO certification audits with AI governance documentation that was written before current AI deployments and does not describe actual AI practices. The policy exists; the certification audit confirms the policy exists; the divergence between what the policy describes and what the AI tools actually do is not measured by the audit. The structural property: certification audits measuring documentation completeness rather than governance adequacy create a false adequacy signal that protects the organization from scrutiny while the divergence between policy and practice is widest. This is distinct from general compliance theater — the specific exposure is that the organization's most authoritative external validation confirms adequate governance when actual governance is structurally absent.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Geneva / Swiss specific obligations and intersections for NGO AI governance:**

**ZEWO Certification AI Governance Audit Limitations**

ZEWO certification processes are beginning to probe AI governance, but audit methodology measures documentation completeness rather than governance adequacy. NGOs with AI policies written before current deployments can pass certification while actual governance practice diverges from documented policy. The structural exposure: ZEWO certification creates a formal adequacy signal that does not measure what it claims to measure. The audit confirms the policy exists; it does not confirm the policy governs actual AI operations. For NGOs preparing for institutional donor AI audits, this divergence is the primary exposure — the organization's most authoritative external validation may confirm governance that does not exist in practice.

**Institutional Donor AI Audit Preparation as Distinct Obligation**

Major institutional donors are increasingly conducting AI governance audits of funded NGOs. The audits examine: what AI tools the organization uses, how they were approved, what beneficiary data they process, how the organization can account for AI-generated decisions, and what governance documentation exists. The compliance architecture problem: donor AI audit standards are not harmonized, each donor has different specific requirements, and the NGO's AI governance documentation was not designed with any specific donor audit standard in view. The intersection with ZEWO certification: passing ZEWO certification does not confirm adequacy for donor AI audit purposes, creating a situation where the NGO's most authoritative Swiss domestic validation is not transferable to the donor audit context.

**FADP Articles 13 and 21 — Explanation Obligation When AI Inference Is Unavailable**

Articles 13 and 21 create obligations for AI-assisted beneficiary decisions that NGOs are not operationally prepared to discharge. When a beneficiary receives a negative outcome and asks for an explanation of what the AI concluded and why, the organization must be able to provide it. The structural gap: AI inference records may not be retrievable after model updates, making the explanation obligation present-tense and unmanageable for assessments produced under prior model versions. The intersection with donor reporting: the organization's donor reports may include conclusions generated by AI systems under conditions the organization cannot reproduce or explain, creating parallel accountability exposure in both donor and regulatory contexts.

**Board Member Personal Liability Under Swiss Data Protection Law**

Swiss data protection law creates personal liability for board members who fail to exercise appropriate oversight of data processing operations. Board member personal AI tool use — where personal AI tools process work-related data or create data governance surfaces the organization cannot monitor — is a specific exposure that current NGO board governance practices do not address. The structural problem: board members do not know to manage this surface, the organization has no protocol for governing it, and personal liability under Swiss law is not mitigated by ignorance. The intersection with Art. 75 ZGB: board member personal liability for AI governance oversight compounds with personal liability for data protection failures, both created by decisions made without adequate information.

**AI Model Update Governance as Missing Trigger Event**

Swiss data protection law and NGO governance best practice require organizations to maintain accountability for AI-assisted decisions over time. When AI tool vendors update their models, the organization's prior assessments were produced under different conditions than current model operation. No Geneva-specific protocol exists defining what governance actions should occur at model update events: which prior assessments are affected, what the organization owes beneficiaries assessed under prior versions, what donor reporting representations remain valid, and what board notification standard applies. The compliance context is: the obligation to maintain coherent accountability across AI tool changes is real; the methodology for doing so is absent; the organizational response is to report that the tool updated without evaluating the implications.

**Beneficiary Communication in Unverifiable Languages**

When NGOs use AI-assisted translation for beneficiary communications in languages Geneva staff cannot verify, the organization approves communications it cannot independently confirm. Swiss consumer protection principles and donor expectations require that beneficiary communications be accurate. AI translation opacity creates a structural verification gap: the organization's approval process cannot detect errors that would change what the communication conveys. The compliance context: the organization is accountable for what its communications say to beneficiaries; the approval process does not provide the information required to make an informed approval decision; the communication was distributed as an organizational fact before the verification gap was identified.

**Art. 75 ZGB Board Member Liability for Unmonitored AI Surfaces**

Swiss Association Law Art. 75 creates personal liability for board members who fail to exercise appropriate oversight of association affairs. Board members with unmanaged personal AI tool use are maintaining data governance surfaces outside organizational awareness. The board's fiduciary duty requires oversight of data processing operations; personal AI tool use that processes work-related data is within the scope of that duty. The structural exposure: the organization cannot demonstrate oversight of a governance surface it does not know exists; the board member cannot demonstrate oversight of a surface they did not know to monitor. No Geneva-specific guidance exists for board members on this specific exposure.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Six net-new insights distinct from C214–C253 Phase 04:**

**Finding 01: Grantee AI output inheritance through the donor reporting chain creates accountability records the lead NGO cannot stand behind**

When implementing partners share AI-generated monitoring reports and the lead NGO includes them in donor reports, the accountability record entering the donor chain is generated by a system the lead NGO has not evaluated and cannot explain. This is distinct from the general implementing partner cascade tort liability described in prior cycles — which focused on legal liability for harm caused by partner AI decisions — by isolating the specific accountability inheritance problem: the lead NGO passing partner AI outputs upward as if they were internally produced analytical conclusions, creating accountability representations in donor reports that the NGO cannot substantiate. No Geneva market participant has named this as a discrete donor-chain documentation problem requiring a discrete methodology.

**Finding 02: AI-assisted beneficiary communications in unreadable languages create approval decisions made without the information required to make them**

When Geneva headquarters approves AI-assisted beneficiary communications in languages staff cannot verify, the approval decision is made on the basis of communication intent rather than verified content accuracy. The AI translation may have altered meaning without detection by those responsible for approval. This is distinct from the AI-generated beneficiary communications accountability problem in C237 Phase 04 — which focused on organizational responsibility for AI-generated content generally — by isolating the specific verification incapacity problem: approval decisions made by people who cannot read what they are approving, creating a structural gap between the organization's approval authority and the information required to exercise it. No Geneva-specific protocol exists for managing this verification gap in capacity-constrained NGOs.

**Finding 03: AI vendor model update re-evaluation triggers are structurally unaddressed governance events that nobody has named**

When AI tool vendors update their models, no governance protocol activates within Geneva NGOs to evaluate whether the update affects prior assessments, changes organizational obligations to beneficiaries, or alters donor reporting representations. No Geneva market participant has named vendor model update re-evaluation triggers as a discrete governance category requiring a discrete protocol.

**Finding 04: Board member personal AI tool use is an unmanaged governance surface creating personal liability that current NGO board practices do not address**
