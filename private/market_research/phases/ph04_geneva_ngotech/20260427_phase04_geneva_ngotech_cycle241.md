## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

**Problem 1 — The Program Design Debt**

AI is deployed into existing program designs without those programs being re-architected around AI operation. The result is a structural mismatch: AI outputs are fed into decision processes designed for human-generated information, with no governance layer bridging the two. The gap is not in the AI tool — it is in the program logic that receives AI output without changed protocols. This generates what program managers describe as "the tool works fine, but something always goes wrong in what happens next."

**Problem 2 — The Donor Accountability Inversion**

Institutional donors measure AI compliance through documentation checkpoints. An NGO passes its donor's AI review by presenting its policy. What the donor does not measure is whether the program team actually operates according to that policy. The NGO therefore invests in policy documentation rather than operational governance — because documentation is what gets audited, not practice. The compliance audit and the actual governance are structurally disconnected.

**Problem 3 — The AI Measurement Paradox**

Donors require AI-assisted programs to demonstrate impact through data. The AI tools used to deliver programs collect and infer beneficiary data to produce those impact metrics. But FADP Article 13 gives beneficiaries rights over automated decisions affecting them, and donors themselves have AI frameworks requiring data minimization. The NGO is caught between a donor's demand for evidence and both the donor's and Swiss law's limitations on how that evidence can be generated. The metric the donor wants cannot be ethically or legally produced within the constraints the donor also imposes. No resolution methodology exists.

**Problem 4 — The Geneva Institutional Immunity Accountability Gap**

International organizations based in Geneva deploying AI systems do so under institutional immunity frameworks that limit Swiss regulatory reach. But Swiss law applies to operations conducted in Geneva regardless of immunity status — a distinction not clearly articulated in any published guidance. The result is accountability without enforcement: Swiss law creates obligations that Geneva international orgs nominally bear, but that no Swiss authority can practically enforce. The exposure is real and unaddressed.

**Problem 5 — The Compliance Overhead Compounding Mechanism**

AI governance compliance costs are absorbed as organizational overhead — line items in budgets, not named governance liabilities. ZEWO certification costs, FADP compliance reviews, donor AI framework implementation: each is absorbed without being recognized as a discrete governance debt. The accumulation is invisible because it is filed under "compliance costs," not "unaddressed governance exposure." When a board asks "what is our AI liability," the answer is not in any single budget line because no single line names it.

**Problem 6 — Resource-Constrained AI Deployment as Distinct Failure Mode**

Organizations with the least IT capacity to monitor AI are precisely the organizations deploying AI most heavily in program delivery. This is not the same as the "limited IT capacity" framing that has been noted in prior cycles. It is a specific structural dynamic: the organizations that cannot audit their own AI deployments are the ones most dependent on those deployments being adequate. When a capacity-constrained NGO's AI-assisted program produces a harmful outcome, the reconstruction window is 48–72 hours, and the organization lacks both the tooling and the personnel to execute it.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva / Swiss Specific Obligations

**ZEWO Certification (SwissNGO Fundraising Accountability Standard)**

ZEWO certification requires organizations to demonstrate financial stewardship and accountability to donors. AI deployment creates specific certification exposure points: donor funds used for AI tool procurement must be traceable and justified; AI-assisted decision-making affecting beneficiaries must be demonstrably accountable; the organization's AI governance must be transparent to donors without compromising beneficiary data protection. ZEWO's 2025–2026 AI audit protocol is probing these intersections without published guidance on what adequate compliance looks like. Organizations are being assessed against standards that do not exist in documented form.

**Institutional Donor AI Frameworks (SDC, SDC, multilateral donors)**

Major institutional donors — SDC, EU instruments, multilateral development banks — have introduced AI governance conditions in grant agreements. These conditions impose obligations that often exceed what Swiss domestic law requires. The specific problem: institutional donors measure compliance through questionnaire responses and documentation submission, not through operational verification. The compliance measurement mechanism is structurally misaligned with the compliance obligation itself.

**FADP (SR 235.1 — Swiss Data Protection Act)**

The nFADP (in force since September 2023) creates specific obligations for NGOs processing beneficiary data with AI systems:

- Article 13: Automated decision-making rights for individuals affected by AI-assisted decisions — NGO programs making beneficiary decisions via AI must be able to explain the logic and provide human review options
- Article 21: Documentation obligations proving lawful basis for AI-assisted data processing — the organizational record must demonstrate what data was processed, for what purpose, with what legal basis, and with what beneficiary rights exercised
- Article 22: Data minimization obligations that may conflict with donor impact measurement requirements — the purpose limitation principle limits what data can be collected and retained beyond the original program purpose

The specific FADP-NGO intersection: program delivery requires detailed beneficiary data, donor reporting requires aggregate impact metrics, AI systems need the same data to produce those metrics, but FADP purpose limitation restricts data reuse for new purposes. No resolution framework has been published by any Swiss authority.

**Swiss Association Law — Board Member Personal Liability**

Articles 75–79 ZGB (Swiss Civil Code) establish board member fiduciary duties including duty of care (Sorgfaltspflicht). For Geneva NGOs with board members using AI tools personally for organizational work, the question of personal liability for AI-assisted governance decisions is unresolved. No published Swiss authority has defined the specific content of the duty of care with respect to AI oversight at the board level. Board members are personally exposed for decisions they do not have an adequate framework to evaluate.
