## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Multi-donor AI framework incoherence as active present-tense exposure**
NGOs operating with three or more institutional donors receive AI governance requirements that are not harmonized. Each framework asks for different evidence, different documentation formats, different timing. When an NGO responds to all frameworks simultaneously, the responses may not cohere. The incoherence is a donor relationship risk that has not been named as a discrete category. The NGO does not know which response will be operative when a donor reviews its file.

**Signal 2: AI program logic embedded in system configurations outside governance documents**
NGOs deploying AI for beneficiary prioritization, service allocation, or eligibility determination have operational rules encoded in AI system configurations that do not appear in any governance document. The rules exist in the system. The system is not the governance record. No Geneva NGO has a protocol for extracting and documenting the program logic embedded in its AI configurations as a governance exercise.

**Signal 3: Beneficiary rights activation for AI-inferred data with no retrieval methodology**
When beneficiaries request access to AI-inferred data or correction of AI-assisted decisions, NGOs discover that the inferences were not stored in retrievable form. The data was computed; the computation record was not retained. The nFADP obligation is present-tense. The retrieval capability is structurally absent. The gap is not a documentation gap; it is an architecture gap.

**Signal 4: Board AI approval scope drift through operational decisions below board visibility**
Board-approved AI use cases expand through operational decisions that do not return to the board. The expansion is not tracked. The board does not know the difference between what it approved and what has been added operationally. The governance document and the operational reality have diverged without a mechanism for detecting the divergence.

**Signal 5: Multi-party beneficiary communications with no accountable author**
AI-generated beneficiary communications pass through multiple organizational hands before reaching the beneficiary. Each organization in the chain modifies, translates, or re-platforms the communication. No single entity owns the accountability for the final form the beneficiary receives. When a communication causes harm or confusion, the accountability chain cannot reconstruct who said what.

**Signal 6: Advisory organizations deploying AI internally under different standards than those they advise clients on**
NGOs and tech nonprofits that provide AI governance advisory services to external clients are operating their own AI systems internally without equivalent governance rigor. The advisory-accountability incoherence is active. It has not been named. It surfaces when an organization is audited by a donor or regulator and the governance standards it recommended to clients are not the standards it applied to itself.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Multi-donor AI governance coherence mapping service**
No advisory service exists for mapping the incoherence between multiple institutional donor AI governance frameworks simultaneously. The service would identify where frameworks require different evidence, where satisfying one framework creates evidence of non-compliance with another, and how to build a coherent response across frameworks without compromising any single framework's requirements.

**Gap 2: AI program logic extraction and documentation as a discrete governance deliverable**
No service exists for extracting, documenting, and reviewing the program logic embedded in AI system configurations as a distinct governance exercise. The deliverable would produce a governance-accessible record of what rules the AI system is actually applying to beneficiary eligibility and resource allocation — separate from the system's technical documentation and separate from the organization's general AI policy.

**Gap 3: Multi-party beneficiary consent architecture for the aid chain**
No architecture exists for designing beneficiary consent processes that bind all parties in the aid chain simultaneously — the Geneva NGO, its institutional donors, and its implementing partners. The architecture would need to address which party's consent form governs, how consent travels across organizational boundaries, and what happens when parties have incompatible data protection standards.

**Gap 4: Geneva institutional AI decision documentation protocol**
No protocol exists for formally documenting AI decisions made through informal Geneva operational and diplomatic channels. The protocol would create a pathway for converting institutional memory about AI decisions into governance records, addressing the specific problem that Geneva organizational culture favors informal coordination over formal documentation.

**Gap 5: Evidence standard reconciliation across donor accountability, nFADP, and humanitarian harm frameworks**
No methodology exists for reconciling evidence requirements that diverge across donor accountability, Swiss data protection, and humanitarian harm standards for the same AI-assisted decision. The methodology would need to identify the irreconcilable tensions, document them, and build an evidence strategy that satisfies each framework's distinct evidentiary standard to the extent possible.

**Gap 6: Advisory organization internal-external AI governance coherence audit**
No service exists for auditing advisory organizations' internal AI governance against the standards they recommend to clients. The audit would identify the specific gaps between what the organization advises and what it practices, providing the basis for resolving the incoherence before it surfaces adversarially.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Multi-donor AI governance framework non-harmonization as structural exposure:**
Institutional donors — SDC, ECHO, FCDO, bilateral agencies, and private foundations — are developing independent AI governance requirements for grantees. The frameworks are not harmonized. They arrive simultaneously. They require different evidence formats, different documentation depths, and different timing. The NGO that tries to satisfy all frameworks finds that a single action may be documented differently across frameworks, generating answer incoherence that creates donor relationship risk independent of the underlying governance quality.

**ZEWO certification as documentation completeness measure, not governance adequacy measure:**
ZEWO certification audits documentation completeness against the ZEWO standard. The certification does not assess whether the documented governance is adequate for the NGO's actual AI deployment. NGOs with ZEWO certification may present a documentation-adequate profile to institutional donors while operating AI systems with governance gaps the certification did not surface. The false adequacy signal flows into the donor community through certification status as a governance quality proxy.

**nFADP Articles 13 and 21 for AI-inferred beneficiary data:**
Article 13 requires that data subjects receive explanations of automated decisions affecting them. Article 21 requires documentation of the logic underlying AI-assisted decisions. When NGOs use AI to infer beneficiary profiles, prioritize service delivery, or determine eligibility, both obligations activate. The inferences were not designed to be retrievable or explainable at the individual level. The architecture gap is present-tense and specific to AI-inferred data — different from the obligation that applies to directly collected data.

**Art. 75 ZGB board member personal liability for AI oversight without adequate review architecture:**
Board members approving AI programs face personal fiduciary liability under Art. 75 ZGB when the programs cause harm without adequate oversight. The oversight obligation requires substantive review of AI system behavior. Board members do not have access to a methodology for conducting substantive AI review at board scale. The personal liability is specific and active. The discharge mechanism is absent from Geneva NGO governance practice.

**Data sovereignty in international NGO multi-jurisdictional operations:**
NGOs operating across multiple countries face data sovereignty questions that nFADP alone does not resolve. Donor data may be subject to funder-country regulations. Field data may be subject to host-country laws. Geneva HQ AI systems process data from multiple jurisdictions simultaneously under configurations that may not be compliant with all applicable regimes. No framework exists for mapping the multi-jurisdictional data sovereignty obligations of a Geneva-based international NGO operating AI systems for country programs.

**Vendor AI updates as governance triggers that no framework includes:**
No Geneva NGO governance framework — internal policy, donor requirement, or certification standard — includes vendor AI model updates as a governance trigger. The most significant changes to how an AI system behaves arrive through vendor product updates that NGOs treat as IT maintenance events. The update may change program logic, eligibility criteria, or output quality without organizational awareness. The governance gap is structural to how AI governance frameworks are designed.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Multi-donor AI framework incoherence as a named structural exposure distinct from individual framework compliance**
NGOs with multiple institutional donors receive AI governance requirements that are not harmonized across frameworks. Each framework generates its own evidence standard, its own documentation format, and its own timing. When an NGO responds to all frameworks simultaneously, the responses may not cohere. The NGO is structurally unable to satisfy all frameworks with identical evidence, and may not know which response will be operative when a specific donor reviews its file. The incoherence is not a documentation failure; it is a structural property of operating across non-harmonized frameworks simultaneously. The exposure has not been named as a discrete category in any Geneva advisory market output. No advisory service addresses multi-framework coherence mapping for NGOs.

**Finding 2: AI program logic encode problem as a distinct governance gap from data governance and policy governance**
NGOs deploying AI for beneficiary eligibility determination, service prioritization, or resource allocation have operational rules governing these decisions embedded in AI system configurations that do not appear in any governance document. The program logic — the actual decision rules the AI applies in borderline cases — exists in the system. It is not in the governance record. Board members approving AI use are approving an abstraction; the actual program logic is in the configuration. No Geneva NGO has a protocol for extracting embedded program logic as a discrete governance deliverable. The gap is distinct from data governance documentation and from AI policy documentation; it is governance of the decision rules themselves, which requires a specific methodology.

**Finding 3: Multi-party beneficiary consent architecture as a structurally absent category for the aid chain**
Beneficiaries in international aid chains interact with AI-generated communications and decisions from multiple organizations simultaneously — the Geneva-based NGO that designed the program, the implementing partner operating in the field, the institutional donor that funded the program, and possibly a third-party platform. Effective consent would require all parties in the chain to be bound by the same consent terms, with compatible data protection standards, for the same beneficiary's data and AI-assisted decisions. No consent architecture exists for binding multiple parties in the aid chain simultaneously. The absence means that beneficiary consent, as practiced, does not cover the full AI-assisted decision chain. The obligation under nFADP and humanitarian harm standards is present-tense; the architecture to discharge it is absent.

**Finding 4: Geneva institutional memory gap for AI decisions made through informal coordination channels**
Geneva-based organizations — particularly international organizations and NGOs with diplomatic and operational coordination functions — make AI-related decisions through informal coordination channels that do not produce formal governance records. The decisions are made. They are not documented. The institutional knowledge of what was decided exists in personal relationships and operational memory, not in governance documents. When governance frameworks require documentation of AI decision history — for nFADP compliance, donor accountability, or internal review — the record does not exist. The gap is specifically Geneva: it results from organizational culture that favors informal coordination over formal documentation, not from negligence. No Geneva advisory service addresses the conversion of informal AI decisions into governance records as a discrete methodology.

**Finding 5: Evidence standard divergence across donor accountability, nFADP, and humanitarian harm as irreconcilable tensions from the same decision**
A single AI-assisted humanitarian decision is simultaneously subject to three distinct evidence standards: donor accountability frameworks require process evidence demonstrating that approved AI governance was followed; nFADP requires inference explainability demonstrating that automated decisions affecting data subjects can be explained; and humanitarian harm standards require outcome documentation demonstrating that the decision was appropriate for the specific beneficiary context. These standards do not require the same evidence. Process compliance does not produce explainability. Explainability does not demonstrate outcome appropriateness. An NGO with strong process documentation may have no explainability record. An NGO with explainable decisions may be unable to demonstrate outcome appropriateness. The divergence is not resolvable by improving any single framework's compliance. It requires an evidence strategy that acknowledges the irreconcilability and builds documentation that serves each standard to the extent possible without compromising the others.

**Finding 6: Tech nonprofit advisory-accountability incoherence as a named structural reputational exposure**
Organizations that provide AI governance advisory services to external clients while operating their own AI systems internally are holding those clients to governance standards they do not apply to themselves. The incoherence is active and structural: the organization is visibly recommending governance practices it has not implemented internally. The exposure surfaces when a donor, institutional partner, or regulator reviews the organization's own AI operations against the standards it has publicly recommended. At that point, the organization faces reputational harm that is self-generated — it recommended the standard, it did not meet it. No Geneva advisory market participant has named this incoherence as a discrete structural exposure or offered a methodology for resolving it before adversarial disclosure. The exposure is particularly acute for tech nonprofits with tech-forward identities, where the gap between advisory claim and operational reality is most visible.
