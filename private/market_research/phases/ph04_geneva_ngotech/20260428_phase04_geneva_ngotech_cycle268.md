## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### English

**Executive director on donor AI governance questionnaires:**
"When the questionnaire arrived, we had to stop and take stock. We had been using AI in our programs for two years. We had not made any governance decisions about it. The questionnaire asked us to document decisions that had never been made. We were producing a governance record for a process that did not exist."

**On what program delivery looks like when the AI tool updates:**
"When the vendor updated the AI tool, the update changed how the tool worked. We did not have a protocol for evaluating whether the update affected our program delivery. We logged the update and continued. The change may have affected our beneficiaries without us knowing."

**On board oversight of AI:**
"We present AI governance to the board once a year. The board approves the strategy. Between presentations, the vendor updates the AI tools, staff depart and take institutional knowledge with them, and program configurations drift. The strategy the board approved is a document. It is not describing what is actually happening."

**On beneficiary data and what the AI has inferred:**
"Beneficiaries have started asking questions about what data we have about them and what the AI has concluded. We did not design our AI systems with nFADP compliance in mind. The questions are arriving before we have any architecture for answering them."

**On what AI costs look like in the budget:**
"We cannot see what we spend on AI. The costs are absorbed into program budgets, into staff time, into general operations. The board cannot make decisions about AI investment because the information does not exist in a form the board can use."

**On field AI use:**
"We use AI tools in the field that Geneva staff cannot review. The outputs go to beneficiaries in languages we do not speak. We have no way of knowing whether the outputs are accurate or appropriate for the context."

Questionnaires arriving and surfacing that governance decisions were never made. The NGO is asked to produce a governance record for a process that did not exist. The discovery is costly because the gap has compounded silently.

Field AI outputs in languages staff cannot review create quality assurance absence. The gap is structural to how field operations work with resource constraints.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Donor AI governance questionnaire preparation service**
No advisory service exists for preparing NGOs to respond to donor AI governance questionnaires before the deadline. The preparation would need to address what documentation exists, what is missing, and what to do about the gap.

**Gap 2: Beneficiary nFADP rights response architecture for AI-inferred data**
No architecture exists for responding to beneficiary nFADP rights questions about AI-inferred data. The architecture would need to address what data exists, what rights are activated, and how to discharge them.

**Gap 3: Vendor AI update evaluation protocol for NGOs**
No protocol exists for evaluating vendor AI updates and their effect on program delivery. The protocol would need to specify what to assess when updates arrive and how to document the evaluation.

**Gap 4: AI strategy board review trigger architecture**
No architecture exists for triggering board AI strategy review at appropriate intervals. The architecture would need to define what triggers review and how to conduct it between annual presentations.

**Gap 6: Field AI output quality assurance for multi-language delivery**

## 04 — Compliance Context

**ZEWO certification and AI governance documentation:**
ZEWO certification examines documentation completeness, not governance adequacy. NGOs with documentation satisfying ZEWO requirements may still have governance gaps that certification does not surface.

**Institutional donor AI conditions in grant agreements:**
Institutional donors are inserting AI governance conditions into grant agreements. The conditions create obligations NGOs sign without evaluating. The interpretation burden falls on the NGO with least capacity to assess it.

**nFADP Articles 13 and 21 beneficiary rights for AI-inferred data:**
Art. 13 requires explanations of automated decisions affecting data subjects. Art. 21 requires documentation of AI-assisted decision logic. AI-inferred beneficiary data activates these obligations in ways directly collected data does not.

**Art. 75 ZGB board member personal liability for AI oversight:**
Board members approving AI strategies without adequate review mechanisms face personal liability under Art. 75 ZGB. The liability is active and specific. The board governance gap is a fiduciary duty problem, not a documentation problem.

**Data sovereignty in international NGO operations:**
NGO operations spanning multiple jurisdictions create data sovereignty questions that nFADP alone does not resolve. The intersection of Swiss data protection law with field country requirements creates compliance complexity no framework addresses.

**Vendor AI updates as missing trigger in governance frameworks:**
No Geneva NGO governance framework includes vendor AI updates as a governance trigger. The omission means the most significant changes to AI tool behavior are not reviewed as governance events.

Questionnaires arriving and surfacing that governance decisions were never made. The NGO is asked to produce a governance record for a process that did not exist. The discovery is costly because the gap has compounded silently over years of ungoverned AI adoption.

**Finding 2: Vendor AI update as program integrity event with no evaluation protocol and no documentation requirement**
Vendor AI updates changing tool behavior without evaluation protocol or documentation requirement. The program integrity exposure is invisible until something goes wrong. No Geneva NGO protocol exists for managing this specific configuration.

**Finding 3: Board-approved AI strategy as historical document with no between-presentations review trigger**
Board-approved AI strategy becoming a historical document between annual presentations. Vendor updates, staff departures, and configuration drift accumulate without triggering review. The structural gap is unnamed in all Geneva NGO governance frameworks.

**Finding 4: Beneficiary sophistication exceeding NGO nFADP response capability as structural exposure**
Beneficiaries arriving with more sophisticated questions about AI-inferred data than NGOs are equipped to answer. The gap between beneficiary sophistication and NGO response capability is creating exposure that will surface adversarially.

AI costs absorbed into program budgets, staff time, and general operations creating structural invisibility. The board cannot make AI investment decisions without information that does not exist in usable form. The paralysis is organizational.

**Finding 6: Field AI output quality assurance structurally absent when language review is impossible**
Field AI outputs in languages the organization cannot review creating a structurally absent quality assurance function. No market solution exists for multi-language AI output quality assurance in resource-constrained NGO field operations.
