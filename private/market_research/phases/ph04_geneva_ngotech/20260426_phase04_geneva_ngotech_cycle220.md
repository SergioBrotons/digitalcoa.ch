## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### How the ICP Actually Describes AI Problems — Real Language

The executive director at a mid-size Geneva NGO has a different relationship with AI governance than the private sector ICP. They are not asking "how do we adopt AI efficiently." They are asking: "how do we govern AI in a context where our funders have expectations, our beneficiaries have rights, our board has fiduciary obligations, and our team has almost no technical capacity."

**Core concerns in their own register:**

- "Our donor requires us to confirm in writing that AI is used in compliance with applicable data protection law. We are not sure what that means, and we do not have anyone who can write that confirmation with confidence." — Donor conditions as commercial gatekeeping. The NGO has signed a contract with AI-related conditions they cannot affirmatively satisfy.

- "We use AI in our beneficiary intake process. We have not updated our consent forms for this. If a beneficiary's data is processed by an AI tool we did not fully understand when we signed up, what are our obligations?" — FADP Art. 25/26 individual rights for vulnerable populations creating operational unsolvable tension.

- "We have been using an AI tool for program monitoring and evaluation for two years. The vendor changed its terms last month. We did not notice until a staff member flagged it. We have no process for reviewing vendor terms." — AI dependency without exit strategy as program continuity risk.

- "Our board asks us about AI at every meeting, but they cannot evaluate our answers because they do not have a framework. We are providing information that does not help them govern." — Board AI literacy deficit as personal liability vector for individual board members.

- "Some of our proposals to donors are substantially AI-generated. We are not hiding it, but we also are not sure how to characterize it. Is it our proposal if an AI wrote most of it?" — AI-generated proposal authenticity and donor expectation management.

- "There are more AI frameworks and guidelines than we can read. Most of them assume we have compliance staff. We do not. The gap between what is written and what is doable is enormous." — Geneva AI knowledge production disconnected from field realities. Structural incentive misalignment.

## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

**Signal 1 — Donor AI Conditions as Commercial Gatekeeping**
Institutional donors (SDC, EU, UN agencies, private foundations) are increasingly including AI governance conditions in grant agreements and partnership contracts. The conditions are concrete and written. They include representations about AI use, data protection compliance, and sometimes AI-specific reporting. The NGO cannot affirmatively satisfy these conditions because they do not have an AI governance framework — they have AI tools in use. The gap is between a commercial requirement they signed and an operational reality they cannot document.

**Signal 2 — FADP Beneficiary Rights vs. Operational Impossibility**
FADP Articles 25 and 26 create individual rights for vulnerable populations — the exact population NGOs serve. Beneficiary data processed by AI tools creates obligations the NGO cannot practically satisfy: right of access, right of correction, right of objection, right to human review of automated decisions. The NGO lacks the technical infrastructure and governance processes to operationalize these rights at scale. The operational impossibility is real and present-tense.

**Signal 3 — AI Dependency Without Exit Strategy as Program Continuity Risk**
NGOs have adopted AI tools for program delivery, monitoring, evaluation, and communications. The tools are embedded in critical workflows. Vendor changes — pricing, terms, service discontinuation — are now a program continuity risk. The NGO has no AI program dependency audit, no exit strategy, no vendor concentration assessment. When the tool changes or disappears, the program workflow breaks with no contingency.

**Signal 4 — Board AI Literacy Deficit as Personal Liability Vector**
Individual board members at Geneva NGOs face undefined personal fiduciary exposure for AI decisions under Art. 69ff. OR (Swiss Association Law). They do not know what questions to ask, what answers are adequate, or whether their governance practices expose them personally. The board AI literacy deficit is not an organizational training problem — it is a distinct personal liability question for individual directors.

**Signal 5 — AI-Generated Proposal Authenticity Compounding Through Commitment Chain**
Proposals submitted to donors are increasingly AI-generated — partially or substantially. The NGO may not fully understand what they are submitting. Donors receiving AI-generated proposals may not know they are evaluating AI-assisted work. The commitment chain — from AI-generated proposal to donor-approved program to beneficiary delivery — carries compounding accountability gaps with no framework for managing attribution.

**Signal 6 — Structural Incentive Misalignment in Geneva AI Knowledge Production**
Geneva hosts more AI standard-setting and governance knowledge production than any other European city. The knowledge produced does not reach mid-tier Geneva NGOs in a usable form. The structural incentive misalignment: Geneva institutions produce frameworks for large organizations; the NGO sector cannot implement them. The knowledge production is disconnected from the populations and organizations it is nominally designed to serve.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### What Exists, What Gaps Remain

**What exists:**
- EPFL AI Initiative — technical research and AI tool development, not NGO governance
- UNAIWG framework — institutional framework for UN agencies, not operationalized for mid-tier NGOs
- ZEWO guidance — Swiss nonprofit financial oversight, beginning to address AI narrative reporting expectations
- ICRC/UNHCR/Sphere guidelines — humanitarian AI ethics guidance, designed for large institutional deployment, too complex for 15–80 person NGOs to operationalize without dedicated staff

**What no one is addressing for Geneva NGOs:**

1. **Donor AI condition proportionality negotiation service** — No one helps the NGO negotiate or interpret donor AI conditions to a proportionality the NGO can actually satisfy. The NGO either accepts impossible conditions or walks away from funding.

2. **FADP beneficiary rights operationalization framework** — No one has produced a practical operationalization of FADP individual rights for NGOs that serve vulnerable populations. The gap between legal obligation and operational capacity is unaddressed.

3. **AI program dependency audit and exit strategy** — No service product exists for AI program dependency assessment and continuity planning for mid-size NGOs. Vendor concentration, exit costs, and alternative tool assessment are not on anyone's service menu.

4. **Board AI literacy briefing for NGO board members** — No one is providing Geneva NGO board members with a personal fiduciary briefing that translates AI governance into their specific accountability language. Individual board liability for AI decisions is a distinct underserved need.

5. **AI-generated proposal accountability review** — No one is helping NGOs develop a methodology for disclosing or managing AI-generated content in donor proposals. The authenticity question and the accountability attribution question are unresolved.

6. **Geneva framework proportionality translation service** — No one is translating Geneva's abundant AI frameworks into NGO-scale implementation guidance. The proportionality gap between what is written and what is doable is the core operational failure no one is solving.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations for NGOs

1. **Institutional donor AI conditions — proportionality gap** — SDC, EU, UN, and private foundation grants increasingly include AI governance conditions. No proportionality mechanism exists for mid-tier NGOs to satisfy these conditions at their scale.

2. **FADP Articles 25 and 26 — vulnerable population individual rights** — NGO beneficiaries have enhanced data protection rights. AI processing of beneficiary data creates obligations the NGO lacks capacity to fulfill.

3. **ZEWO AI narrative reporting expectations** — Swiss nonprofit oversight body beginning to ask about AI use in annual reporting. Not yet a formal requirement but direction of travel is clear.

4. **Cross-border AI data flows** — International NGO program data flows across borders processed by AI tools. GDPR intersection, Swiss-Hosted AI infrastructure, and FADP cross-border obligations create layered compliance requirements.

5. **Board personal fiduciary duties under Art. 69ff. OR** — Individual board members have personal liability exposure for organizational AI decisions. The standard of care for AI governance at the board level is undefined.

6. **NGO consortium AI supply chain accountability** — Lead NGOs in consortium structures are absorbing governance burdens created by implementing partners' AI infrastructure choices. The accountability chain through consortium architecture is not mapped.
