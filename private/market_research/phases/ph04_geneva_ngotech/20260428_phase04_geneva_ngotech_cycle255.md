## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Donor AI governance questionnaire as organizational discovery event**
The donor AI governance questionnaire is functioning as a discovery event for NGOs that did not know they had governance gaps. The questionnaire surfaces the gap by asking questions the organization cannot answer. The discovery is uncomfortable and expensive.

**Signal 2: AI-inferred beneficiary data as nFADP rights activation surface**
AI systems inferring beneficiary characteristics that were never directly collected creates a data inventory problem. Beneficiaries have rights over data the NGO did not know it had. The rights activation surface is present-tense and growing as AI tools are used more extensively.

**Signal 3: Vendor AI update as unmanaged program integrity event**
Vendor AI updates that change how tools work create program integrity events that NGOs do not have protocols for. The update may change outputs in ways that affect program delivery. The unmanaged event creates exposure that is invisible until something goes wrong.

**Signal 4: Board-approved AI strategy as historical document without review mechanism**
Board-approved AI strategies that are not reviewed become historical documents. The vendor landscape has changed; the strategy does not reflect current state. The review gap is structural — board governance is not designed to trigger AI strategy review.

**Signal 5: AI cost dispersion across budget lines as governance visibility failure**
AI costs dispersed across multiple budget lines cannot be seen as a single cost. The visibility failure prevents governance decisions that require understanding total AI investment. The problem is organizational accounting, not AI governance, but the consequence is governance failure.

**Signal 6: Field AI output without Geneva-based editorial review as program quality problem**
Field AI outputs that Geneva staff cannot review create a program quality problem. The outputs go to beneficiaries in languages the organization does not speak. The quality assurance function is structurally absent for these outputs.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Donor AI governance questionnaire preparation service**
No advisory service exists for preparing NGOs to respond to donor AI governance questionnaires before the deadline arrives. The preparation would need to address what documentation exists, what documentation is missing, and what to do about the gap before the questionnaire is due.

**Gap 2: AI-inferred beneficiary data inventory protocol**
No protocol exists for creating an inventory of AI-inferred beneficiary data. The protocol would need to address: what data has been inferred, where it is stored, what the nFADP obligations are, and how to design beneficiary rights response processes.

**Gap 3: Vendor AI update evaluation protocol for NGOs**
No protocol exists for evaluating vendor AI updates and their effect on program delivery. The protocol would need to specify what to assess when an update arrives, how to determine whether outputs have changed, and what documentation is required.

**Gap 4: AI strategy board review trigger architecture**
No architecture exists for triggering board AI strategy review at appropriate intervals. The architecture would need to define what triggers review, what the review should examine, and how to document that the review occurred.

**Gap 5: AI cost visibility architecture for NGO budget governance**
No architecture exists for making AI costs visible as a single governance object. The architecture would need to address how to classify AI costs across budget lines in a way that supports governance decision-making.

**Gap 6: Field AI output quality assurance protocol for multi-language delivery**
No protocol exists for quality assurance of AI outputs in languages that the organization's staff cannot review. The protocol would need to address how to maintain quality when the review function is structurally absent.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**ZEWO certification and AI governance documentation:**
ZEWO certification examines documentation completeness, not governance adequacy. NGOs that have documentation that satisfies ZEWO requirements may still have governance gaps that the certification does not surface.

**Institutional donor AI conditions in grant agreements:**
Institutional donors are inserting AI governance conditions into grant agreements. The conditions create obligations that NGOs sign to without evaluating what compliance requires. The interpretation burden falls on the NGO with least capacity to assess it.

**nFADP Articles 13 and 21 beneficiary rights for AI-inferred data:**
Art. 13 requires that data subjects receive explanations of automated decisions affecting them. Art. 21 requires documentation of AI-assisted decision logic. AI-inferred beneficiary data activates these obligations in ways that directly collected data does not.

**Art. 75 ZGB board member personal liability for AI oversight:**
Board members who approve AI strategies without adequate review mechanisms face personal liability under Art. 75 ZGB. The liability is active and specific. The board governance gap is not a documentation problem; it is a fiduciary duty problem.

**Data sovereignty in international NGO operations:**
NGO operations spanning multiple jurisdictions create data sovereignty questions that nFADP alone does not resolve. The intersection of Swiss data protection law with field country requirements creates compliance complexity that no framework addresses.

**Vendor AI update as missing trigger in governance frameworks:**
No Geneva NGO governance framework includes vendor AI updates as a governance trigger. The omission means that the most significant changes to AI tool behavior are not reviewed as governance events.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Donor AI governance questionnaire as organizational discovery mechanism for governance gaps that compound silently**
The questionnaire surfaces governance gaps that have been compounding silently. The NGO did not know it had the gap because there was no mechanism for discovering it before the questionnaire arrived. The discovery is costly because the gap has had time to grow.

**Finding 2: AI-inferred beneficiary data as nFADP rights activation surface with no response architecture in place**
AI-inferred beneficiary data activates nFADP rights that NGOs have no architecture for responding to. The data exists; the rights exist; the response capability does not. The gap is structural and present-tense.

**Finding 3: Vendor AI update as program integrity event with no evaluation protocol and no documentation requirement**
Vendor AI updates are program integrity events that go unevaluated and undocumented. The update may have changed how the AI tool works in ways that affect program delivery. The unmanaged event creates exposure that is invisible until something surfaces it adversarially.

**Finding 4: Board-approved AI strategy as historical document with no trigger for review and no architecture for conducting it**
Board-approved AI strategies become historical documents without a trigger for review or an architecture for conducting it. The vendor landscape changes; the strategy does not. Board governance is not designed to address this structural gap.

**Finding 5: AI cost dispersion as governance visibility failure creating budget governance paralysis**
AI costs dispersed across budget lines create governance visibility failure. The board cannot make decisions about AI investment because it cannot see total AI costs. The paralysis is organizational, but the consequence is governance failure.

**Finding 6: Field AI output quality assurance as structurally absent function with no market solution for multi-language delivery**
Field AI outputs in languages the organization cannot review create a structurally absent quality assurance function. No market solution exists for multi-language AI output quality assurance in resource-constrained NGO operations. The gap is architectural, not operational.
