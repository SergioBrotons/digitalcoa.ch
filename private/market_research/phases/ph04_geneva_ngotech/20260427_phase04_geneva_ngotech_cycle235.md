The recurring tension in different language: *"Our donor sends us a questionnaire asking how we use AI. We have to answer it. We answer it as honestly as we can. But the honest answer is that we don't have the documentation to answer the questions they're actually asking."*

## 02 — Market Signals

Current problems, failure modes, paradoxes, and recurring complaints.

### Signal 1: The Program Manager Personal Accountability Discharge Problem

The mid-career Geneva NGO program manager holds personal exposure under Swiss association law (Art. 75 ff. ZGB) for AI-assisted decisions embedded in program design, beneficiary allocation, and resource distribution. That accountability obligation exists. The mechanism to formally discharge it does not. This is distinct from board-level liability (which is about fiduciary oversight) and from the Geneva office accountability-without-authority problem (which is about organisational structure). The program manager's exposure is personal, immediate, and structurally without resolution. No Geneva advisory service currently addresses this specific persona and this specific gap: the individual professional who is accountable for decisions they cannot fully explain, with no institutional pathway to reduce that exposure.

### Signal 2: The Humanitarian Data Residue Disposition Problem

When Geneva-based NGOs terminate programs — a routine lifecycle event — they confront a disposition problem that no Geneva-specific guidance resolves: what happens to beneficiary data that was processed, analysed, or categorised by AI systems during the program? Swiss law and Geneva cantonal guidance on data disposal apply. But the specific question of AI-processed beneficiary data — data where the AI's inference, classification, or scoring contributed to decisions about individuals — has no published resolution pathway. Retention implies ongoing responsibility. Transfer implies the receiving entity inherits the AI-derived inferences. Anonymisation may not be technically feasible for data scored by AI. Destruction eliminates a potentially valuable program record. No Geneva framework addresses this terminal-state data problem for AI-processed beneficiary information.

### Signal 3: The Geneva Domestic NGO HQ-Office Governance Split

The accountability-without-authority problem is not confined to international organisations with institutional immunity. Swiss domestic NGOs headquartered in Zurich or Basel, operating programs in Geneva, face a parallel structure: Swiss law applies to the Geneva operational office; AI decisions about program design, beneficiary selection, or resource allocation are made at headquarters; the Geneva office implements but does not govern. The Geneva office bears Swiss legal exposure for decisions made elsewhere, under a governance model designed for a pre-AI era. This is distinct from the international org accountability problem because the accountability chain is governed by domestic Swiss institutional hierarchy rather than international legal personality — but the accountability gap is structurally identical.

### Signal 4: Donor AI Governance Questionnaire Asymmetry as Structural Procurement Problem

Large institutional donors — bilateral agencies, private foundations, EU instruments — have begun including AI governance questionnaires in standard due diligence and reporting packs. Geneva NGOs must complete these questionnaires. The questions — about AI tool inventories, model version tracking, use-case documentation, FADP compliance architecture — presuppose an AI governance documentation infrastructure that most respondents do not have. The gap between what donors ask and what NGOs can document is not a communications failure. It is a structural asymmetry: donors with compliance teams are asking questions that require an AI governance function to answer, and most Geneva NGOs of 10–100 employees do not have an AI governance function. The questionnaire response becomes evidence of governance inadequacy rather than proof of governance effort. This is creating a specific, dateable procurement and reporting problem: every new grant cycle requires a questionnaire response, and every response exposes the documentation gap.

### Signal 5: The AI Governance Budget Architecture Failure

Governance costs — the staff time, external advisory, and process design required to document and manage AI use — have no natural budget home in Geneva NGO financial architecture. They are not program costs (they are not directly attributable to program delivery). They are not IT costs (they predate the IT function's AI responsibilities). They are not compliance costs in the regulatory sense (the NGO is not a regulated entity). As a result, they fall into an undefined overhead category that Finance does not recognise and that the executive director cannot approve without an budget line. The structural result: every AI governance investment requires a one-off budget negotiation, which means governance investment is episodic, discretionary, and insufficient. The problem is not priority — it is architecture. No Geneva NGO of 10–100 employees has resolved this, because the accounting framework does not provide a category for it.

### Signal 6: The Routine AI Derivation Reconstruction Failure

The post-incident reconstruction problem — identified in prior cycles as a donor accountability risk — is not only an incident-response problem. It is a routine operational exposure. Every time a Geneva NGO program manager uses AI to prepare a beneficiary assessment, a resource allocation recommendation, or a program design option, the AI derivation chain is generated and then decays within 48–72 hours: chat context expires, file versions update, model versions change, and the trail of how the AI reached its recommendation becomes structurally unrecoverable. The problem is not that NGOs will face an incident and need to reconstruct. The problem is that every significant AI-assisted decision is, by default, unreconstructable within a short period of generation. This is not a crisis mode. It is normal operations with a structural information loss built in.

## 04 — Compliance Context

Geneva/Swiss specific obligations.

### Swiss Association Law — Personal Accountability of Program Managers

Articles 75 ff. ZGB establish that persons charged with the management of a Swiss association bear personal duties of care. For Geneva NGO program managers who have approved program designs incorporating AI-assisted recommendations, this duty applies to the decision to use the AI output — not only to the AI tool selection. The specific question of what due diligence a program manager must perform before acting on an AI recommendation, and how that due diligence must be documented, has no published Swiss authority guidance. The obligation exists. The adequacy standard does not.

### FADP — Program Wind-Down and AI-Processed Beneficiary Data

The nFADP (SR 235.1) establishes retention, access, and disposal obligations for personal data. When a Geneva NGO program terminates, beneficiary data processed by AI systems must be disposed of in accordance with Art. 25 nFADP (data minimisation and purpose limitation). The specific problem: AI inference data — scores, classifications, derived attributes — may not be clearly categorised as "personal data" in the NGO's data inventory, and the disposal of AI-derived inferences about individuals, where the inference contributed to decisions about those individuals, is not addressed in any published FADP guidance specific to the humanitarian or development NGO context. Geneva NGOs are operating without a disposal protocol for this data category.

### FADP — Beneficiary Consent Architecture for AI-Assisted Decisions

Art. 6 nFADP requires lawful basis for data processing, including consent where applicable. Geneva NGOs using AI to assist in beneficiary selection, resource allocation, or program design have in many cases collected beneficiary consent under consent forms designed for pre-AI program delivery. The consent forms do not describe AI-assisted processing. The gap between the consent actually obtained and the AI-assisted processing actually occurring is a structural nFADP compliance exposure that activates upon any FADP subject access request or FDPIC inquiry. No Geneva NGO sector guidance addresses the required consent form architecture for AI-assisted program decisions.

### ZEWO Certification — AI Accountability Expectations

ZEWO (the Swiss certification body for humanitarian aid organisations) has begun incorporating AI governance expectations into its certification review process, but has not published the specific standard against which AI governance adequacy is assessed. Geneva NGOs preparing for ZEWO certification or recertification face a documentation requirement of uncertain scope. The practical consequence: organisations must produce AI governance evidence without knowing what adequate evidence looks like, and ZEWO reviewers assess it without a published benchmark. The gap between "ZEWO expects AI governance" and "the published standard for what ZEWO expects from AI governance" is a documentation planning problem for every Geneva NGO in the certification cycle.

### Institutional Donor AI Governance Conditions

Large institutional donors — including SDC, EU instruments, and major private foundations — have begun incorporating AI governance conditions into grant agreements. These conditions vary in specificity: some require AI use policies; others require AI risk assessments; others require donor notification of AI-assisted decisions affecting beneficiaries. The enforcement mechanisms also vary: some require documentation at reporting intervals; others are event-triggered. Geneva NGOs managing multi-donor portfolios face a cumulative compliance obligation where different donors have different AI governance requirements, no two of which were designed with the same NGO AI governance architecture in mind. The result is a compliance management problem — tracking which donor has which AI condition, what evidence satisfies each, and how to produce it — that has no sector tool or guidance document.

### Geneva Cantonal Data Protection

Geneva's Commission cantonale de la protection des données (CCPD) has jurisdiction over personal data processing within the canton of Geneva. NGOs operating in Geneva fall under CCPD oversight for data processing activities occurring in Geneva — which, for AI-assisted beneficiary processing, may include decisions made by AI systems even where the AI tool is hosted outside Geneva. The question of whether an AI inference made outside Geneva about Geneva-residents' data falls under CCPD jurisdiction is not settled. Geneva NGOs are operating under cantonal oversight for which the jurisdictional scope, in the context of AI-assisted processing, is genuinely unclear.

## 06 — Findings Summary

Six net-new insights, distinct from C214–C234 Phase 04.

---

**Finding 1: The Program Manager Personal Accountability Discharge Failure Is Structurally Distinct from Board Liability**

Prior cycles identified board-level personal AI governance liability (C218, C226, C230) and the Geneva office accountability-without-authority problem (C234). C235 identifies a specifically different gap: the mid-career program manager who holds personal exposure under Swiss association law for AI-assisted decisions embedded in program design, but has no institutional mechanism — no process, no document template, no governance protocol — to formally reduce or discharge that accountability. The obligation exists in law. The adequacy standard does not exist anywhere in Geneva advisory or NGO sector guidance. This is the highest-risk, least-served persona in the Geneva NGO AI governance problem, and no current market position addresses it specifically.

*Distinction from prior cycles:* Board liability (C218/C226) is about fiduciary oversight failures. Accountability-without-authority (C234) is about organisational structure. The program manager personal discharge failure is about the individual professional's personal liability for decisions they made with AI assistance, with no mechanism to demonstrate adequacy.

---

**Finding 2: Humanitarian Data Residue at Program Wind-Down Is a Distinct AI-Specific Disposal Problem with No Geneva Guidance**

Prior cycles identified donor visibility versus FADP beneficiary rights conflicts (C224, C230), FADP consent form disconnection (C233), and general data disposal gaps at program termination (C231). C235 identifies a specifically different problem: AI inference residue — the derived data, scores, classifications, and AI-generated attributes about beneficiaries that exist in organisational records after program termination, and for which no Geneva-specific guidance addresses the specific question of what lawful disposition looks like. The intersection of AI inference inheritance, FADP purpose limitation at program wind-down, and Geneva cantonal data protection oversight is not addressed by any published Swiss framework. This is a specific, recurring operational problem for Geneva NGOs with program lifecycle management, and it is currently ungoverned.

*Distinction from prior cycles:* General FADP data disposal guidance (C231) does not address the AI inference category. Donor visibility conflicts (C224/C230) address reporting obligations, not data terminal state. Consent form disconnection (C233) addresses prospective disclosure, not retrospective disposition.

---

**Finding 3: The Domestic NGO HQ-Office Governance Split Extends the Geneva Accountability Problem Beyond International Organisations**

Prior cycles focused on the Geneva accountability-without-authority problem in the context of international organisations with institutional legal personality (C234, C233). C235 identifies that Swiss domestic NGOs headquartered in other cantons face a parallel but legally distinct version of the same structural problem: Swiss association law applies to the Geneva operational office; AI decisions are made at cantonal or national headquarters; the Geneva office implements but does not govern; the accountability exposure under Swiss law runs through the Geneva office under domestic Swiss hierarchy rather than through international legal personality. The governance gap is structurally identical. The legal resolution pathway — if one exists — is different. No current advisory addresses this specific domestic Swiss NGO variant of the Geneva accountability problem.

*Distinction from prior cycles:* International org accountability-without-authority (C234/C233) operates through institutional immunity and international legal personality. The domestic NGO variant operates through Swiss association law hierarchy without the institutional immunity dimension. The structural governance failure is the same; the legal resolution is different.

---

**Finding 4: Donor AI Governance Questionnaire Asymmetry Is Creating a Systematic Documentation Evidence Problem**

Prior cycles identified donor AI questionnaire pressure as a documentation gap trigger (C233) and institutional donor AI risk framing shifts (C230). C235 identifies a specifically different dynamic: the structural asymmetry between what institutional donor questionnaires ask and what Geneva NGOs of 10–100 employees can actually document is not a temporary gap that closes as governance matures — it is a structural feature of the size and resource distribution of the ICP. The questions presuppose an AI governance function. The NGO does not have one and cannot justify one under current budget architecture. The response to the questionnaire therefore either over-commits (producing governance claims without evidence) or under-commits (documenting gaps without a remediation narrative). Neither response serves the donor relationship. Neither response reduces the NGO's exposure. This asymmetry is systemic, recurring, and not addressed by any current donor guidance or Geneva NGO advisory.

*Distinction from prior cycles:* Donor questionnaire deadline pressure (C233) identified the timing problem. Donor AI risk framing shift (C230) identified the framing change. C235 identifies the structural response asymmetry — the fact that the questionnaire format itself is incompatible with the NGO's structural capacity, regardless of governance maturity.

---

**Finding 5: AI Governance Budget Architecture Failure Is an Accounting Problem, Not a Priority Problem**

Prior cycles identified governance cost equity problems (C216) and the structural underinvestment in AI governance infrastructure (C224, C229, C231). C235 identifies a specifically different dimension: the reason Geneva NGO AI governance investment remains insufficient is not that executives undervalue it (though they may), and not that donors don't require it (though they increasingly do). It is that the NGO's financial accounting architecture provides no recognised budget line for AI governance costs. IT manages tool procurement. Programs manage direct delivery costs. HR manages staff compliance training. There is no budget category for the ongoing documentation, review, and discharge activity that AI governance requires. As a result, every governance investment is a one-off negotiation rather than a standard budget item. The structural consequence is episodic, insufficient, and organisationally invisible governance investment. This is an accounting architecture problem that no Geneva NGO sector tool, no ZEWO guidance, and no Geneva advisory engagement currently addresses.

*Distinction from prior cycles:* Governance cost equity (C216) framed the problem as who pays. Structural underinvestment (C224/C229/C231) framed it as insufficient priority. C235 frames it as an accounting architecture failure: there is no recognised cost category, which means the governance investment cannot be made systematically regardless of priority.

---

**Finding 6: The Routine AI Derivation Decay Problem Is a Normal Operations Exposure, Not an Incident Response Problem**

Prior cycles identified post-incident AI decision reconstruction as a donor accountability and compliance problem (C232, C233, C234). C235 identifies a specifically different framing: the derivation reconstruction problem is not primarily an incident-response problem that activates when something goes wrong. It is a routine operational exposure that is active every time a significant AI-assisted decision is made, whether or not any incident occurs. The AI derivation trail decays within 48–72 hours of generation through ordinary operational processes: context window expiration, file versioning, model updates, staff departure. The program manager who used AI to prepare a beneficiary allocation recommendation in March 2025 cannot reconstruct the AI's reasoning in March 2026, not because an incident occurred, but because ordinary operations destroyed the trail. This means every significant AI-assisted program decision in the organisation's recent history is, by default, unreconstructable. This is not a crisis-mode problem. It is the ordinary-state problem of operating AI systems without a derivation preservation protocol. No Geneva NGO of 10–100 employees has a systematic solution to this, because the problem has not been framed as a routine operational exposure rather than an exceptional incident-response problem.

*Distinction from prior cycles:* Post-incident reconstruction (C232/C233/C234) frames the problem as episodic and triggered by adverse events. C235 frames it as continuous and inherent to ordinary AI-assisted operations without a preservation protocol. The implication for advisory is different: the solution is not an incident response plan, it is a derivation chain preservation practice integrated into routine program management processes.
