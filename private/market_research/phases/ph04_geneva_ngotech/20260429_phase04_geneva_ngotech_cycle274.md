## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

### Current operational problems in-market

**AI tool proliferation debt**
NGOs that adopted AI tools reactively during 2023–2025 acceleration now operate with tool sprawl no one planned. Multiple tools across program teams, different vendor terms for each, beneficiary data flowing through infrastructure the organization cannot fully describe. Staff who adopted tools have moved on or shifted roles. The tool inventory exists as institutional memory, not documented infrastructure.

**Mid-program donor AI governance inquiry**
Institutional donors — particularly those with their own AI governance frameworks — are beginning to send pre-award AI due diligence questionnaires and mid-grant AI accountability requests. NGOs discover mid-grant that they cannot produce the evidence donors are requesting because the documentation architecture was never built. The inquiry arrives before the documentation exists.

**AI-generated impact data credibility gap**
AI tools generating program metrics, beneficiary needs assessments, and outcome projections — the outputs look credible because they are precisely formatted. Donors are beginning to ask about validation methodology. The credibility of the format is being interpreted as credibility of the underlying data quality. Organizations cannot separate the two.

**Staff AI knowledge concentration**
The staff member who configured and manages the AI tool is also the program officer who uses it. Departure of that person creates a dual loss: program knowledge and AI configuration knowledge simultaneously. The organization cannot reconstruct the AI system state because no organizational copy of the configuration exists. This is structurally different from general knowledge management — AI configuration is specific and non-obvious.

**Donor visibility versus beneficiary confidentiality structural tension**
Institutional donors increasingly require visibility into AI-assisted decisions affecting beneficiaries. The same AI outputs that must be disclosed to satisfy donor accountability obligations may contain beneficiary personal data that nFADP protects from unnecessary disclosure. These are simultaneous obligations that apply to the same artifact. No Geneva NGO has published a methodology for resolving this structural conflict at the documentation level.

### Failure modes

**Documentation theater without governance function**
Documentation produced in response to donor requests (visibility function) does not contain governance information that would allow accountability reconstruction. Organizations produce substantial documentation folders while remaining structurally unable to answer the question: "What did the AI actually decide, and could you show us how?" The visibility function is satisfied. The governance function is absent.

**Compliance adequacy signal false positive**
ZEWO certification, institutional donor confidence, board approval of AI use policy — each signals adequacy. None addresses AI governance specifically. Organizations with valid ZEWO certification and board-approved AI policies are operating with AI governance exposures no external certification or governance process has evaluated. The signal provides false assurance at the moment of an actual AI-related inquiry.

**Field AI override as silent liability accumulation**
Field staff override AI recommendations in individual cases without documentation. Each override is individually defensible. The aggregate pattern is invisible: the organization's AI-assisted decisions do not reflect the AI system's outputs, and no record of the divergence exists. When a donor asks about AI governance, the answer requires reconstructed field decisions the organization cannot produce.

**Vendor terms silent mutation as present-tense undisclosed contract modification**
Multiple Geneva NGOs are operating with AI tools whose vendor terms have silently changed through platform updates. The organization's authorization basis for processing beneficiary data may have changed without organizational awareness. The consent architecture, the data processing agreement, and the actual vendor terms are no longer aligned. This is not a hypothetical risk — it is a present-tense condition operating across the sector.

### Paradoxes and complaints

"We have AI tools we bought because we needed them — but we can't describe to donors what they do or how they affect beneficiaries because nobody documented the system."

"We want to be accountable to our donors. We also need to protect beneficiary data. We are being asked to demonstrate both simultaneously and the requirements seem to pull in opposite directions."

"Our staff are using AI tools they selected because they needed them for program delivery. We don't have a process for deciding which tools to use — we just started using them."

"We have a board-approved AI policy. Nobody has tested whether it would actually produce the documentation we would need if a donor asked questions today."

"Our biggest fear isn't that we did something wrong — it's that we wouldn't be able to show what we did if someone asked."

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Specific Obligations

### ZEWO certification and AI governance

ZEWO certification is the primary quality standard for Swiss humanitarian NGOs. It covers financial governance, organizational accountability, and use of funds. It does not cover AI governance. Institutional donors with ZEWO requirements interpret valid certification as adequate AI governance signal — it is not. This creates a structural false adequacy signal in the Geneva NGO market. ZEWO has not published AI-specific governance standards, and its recertification process does not specifically evaluate AI use. Organizations with valid ZEWO certification have unevaluated AI governance exposures.

*No specific ZEWO AI governance guidance exists as of April 2026. This is both the exposure and the opportunity.*

### Institutional donor AI accountability requirements

Major institutional donors (foundations, multilateral programs, government aid programs) are developing AI accountability requirements that flow through grant agreements. These requirements typically ask for:
- Documented AI use policy
- Evidence of human oversight over AI-assisted decisions
- Data protection documentation for AI tools
- Beneficiary consent architecture for AI-assisted decisions
- Incident response protocol for AI-related events

These requirements arrive in grant agreements before Geneva NGOs have the documentation architecture to satisfy them. The compliance timeline is donor-driven, not organization-driven.

### FADP / nFADP applicability to NGO AI deployments

The revised Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) applies to Geneva NGOs processing personal data — including AI-inferred data about beneficiaries. Key operative obligations:
- Art.6(1)(b): Purpose limitation — AI inference use cases must be documented and consistent with original collection purpose
- Art.13/21: Automated decision explanation — organizations must be able to explain AI-assisted decisions affecting beneficiaries; the explanation must be accessible and timely
- Art.9: Processing of special categories (health, social services recipient status) requires explicit consent or specific legal basis; AI inference on special category data activates Art.9
- Data subject access requests: Beneficiaries can request disclosure of what data the organization holds about them, including AI-inferred data; response infrastructure does not exist at most small NGO scale

The FDPIC published guidance confirming nFADP applies to AI systems (December 2024). The guidance does not address the specific intersection of NGO program delivery, beneficiary data protection, and donor accountability. Organizations are operating in a gap between the statute and operational guidance.

### Geneva-specific institutional context

Geneva is home to the highest concentration of international organizations using AI in humanitarian operations. This creates specific dynamics:
- Geneva-based NGOs often operate in adjacent program contexts to UN/IO agencies, using similar tools and facing similar accountability questions
- Geneva humanitarian AI standards ecosystem (UNAIWIG/ICRC/OCHA) is developed locally and applied globally — Swiss law mapping remains absent
- Institutional donors with Geneva presence apply accountability standards that reflect Geneva institutional norms
- The Geneva IO ecosystem functions as an informal testing ground for operative AI governance standards before formal regulatory doctrine arrives

Geneva NGOs are exposed to a compounding accountability geometry: Swiss regulatory obligations + institutional donor accountability requirements + Geneva humanitarian AI framework standards + nFADP — operating simultaneously, not harmonized.
