## 01 — ICP Language: How This ICP Actually Describes AI Problems

### English

**On what Geneva knows versus what the field is doing:**
"We approve AI deployment decisions here in Geneva. The board reviews the strategy. The committee signs off on the approach. What the field teams actually do with the AI tools — which outputs they act on, which they override, how they interpret the confidence levels — we do not know in any systematic way. When something goes wrong in the field, we cannot reconstruct what the AI actually contributed to the decision that caused the problem. We approved the system. We do not govern it."

**On the vendor contract and what it doesn't cover:**
"The contract with our AI vendor has an indemnification clause. We provide indemnification to the vendor for claims arising from how we use the tool. The vendor does not indemnify us for claims arising from how the tool behaves. We are responsible for the downstream consequences of a system we do not control, operated by staff we cannot fully supervise, in contexts the board has not evaluated. The contract was reviewed by our lawyer. The indemnification asymmetry was not flagged."

**On receiving three different donor AI governance letters in the same quarter:**
"We received AI governance requirements from three institutional donors within the same quarter. The requirements overlap but are not identical. Each donor asks for different things, in different formats, on different timelines. We cannot give Donor A what Donor B is asking for because the questions are different. We cannot give any of them what they are asking for because we did not build our AI governance to a specification — we built it to run programs. The documentation we have does not fit the documentation they want. We are now spending governance capacity trying to produce documentation for three donors simultaneously when that capacity should be running programs."

**On what nFADP requires in a displacement camp context:**
"We operate in contexts where the people we serve have been displaced, are in acute crisis, and may not have functional access to the communications infrastructure required to exercise data rights even if they knew those rights existed. The law says we must provide explanations of automated decisions and the right to contest them. We do not have a methodology for doing this in a displacement camp context. No Geneva authority has told us what this looks like. We are doing our best. Best effort is not the same as compliance."

**On following Geneva AI coordination standards versus Swiss regulatory requirements:**
"We participate in the UNAIWG working groups. We follow the ICRC AI ethics guidelines. We use the OCHA data responsibility toolkit. These are the frameworks developed here in Geneva for humanitarian AI. When we map what these frameworks require against what Swiss data protection law requires, they do not always point in the same direction. We are following Geneva frameworks. We are not certain whether we are following Swiss law. Nobody has mapped the relationship between the two."

**On producing governance documentation for donors versus governing AI:**
"We have a folder of AI governance documents. The documents exist because donors ask for them. The board approves the folder annually. The folder is not connected to how the AI is actually configured. When something goes wrong, the folder does not tell us what happened. It tells us what we said about what we were planning to do. The documentation is evidence of a plan. It is not governance."

## 02 — Market Signals

### Signal 1: Geneva HQ approval architecture misaligned with field-level AI accountability

The Geneva-based approval process for AI deployment creates a specific accountability gap: the approving body has the authority but not the contextual knowledge; the operating context has the knowledge but not the authority. When something goes wrong in the field, Geneva cannot reconstruct what the AI contributed because the approval architecture was never designed to capture field-level AI behavior as an accountability object.

### Signal 2: Vendor AI indemnification asymmetry in NGO contracts — unnamed contractual exposure

NGO AI vendor contracts contain indemnification provisions that indemnify the vendor for the NGO's use of the tool but do not indemnify the NGO for the vendor's tool behavior. The asymmetry is present in standard contracts reviewed by legal counsel without flag. The exposure is unnamed in Geneva NGO governance discourse.

### Signal 3: Multi-institutional donor AI condition cascade creating fragmented, simultaneously impossible obligations

Institutional donors are issuing non-harmonized AI governance conditions that arrive simultaneously, require different evidentiary formats, and sometimes demand evidence that would expose the organization to nFADP liability if produced. Organizations with multiple institutional donors face compounding, non-harmonized obligations that cannot be simultaneously satisfied.

### Signal 4: nFADP Art.13/21 discharge methodology absent for crisis context operations

Art.13 and Art.21 FADP obligations require disclosure of automated decision logic and the right to contest decisions. Organizations operating in displacement contexts, conflict zones, and acute crisis settings have no methodology for discharging these obligations when data subjects lack the capacity or infrastructure to exercise rights. The gap is unnamed in published guidance from any Swiss authority.

### Signal 5: Geneva humanitarian AI standards ecosystem operating as parallel framework to Swiss regulatory requirements

The Geneva humanitarian AI coordination ecosystem — UNAIWG, ICRC guidelines, OCHA frameworks, sector-specific protocols — operates as a self-contained standards environment with no published mapping to Swiss law obligations. Organizations following Geneva standards exclusively may be operating in a compliance position that is unevaluated against Swiss law.

### Signal 6: AI governance documentation produced for donor visibility function rather than governance function

The primary driver of AI governance documentation production at many Geneva NGOs is donor request arrival, not governance need identification. Documentation accumulates to satisfy the visibility function while the actual governance function — tracking what the AI is doing, why, and what the consequences are — remains unbuilt. The documentation displacement is structural, not behavioral.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Specific Obligations

### 4.1 nFADP Articles 13 and 21 in Crisis Context Humanitarian Operations

Art.13 FADP requires disclosure of logical reasoning behind automated decisions. Art.21 grants data subjects the right to object to automated decision-making and to request human review. For organizations operating in displacement camps, conflict zones, and acute crisis settings, both obligations present practical discharge problems that Swiss authorities have not addressed.

The specific crisis context complications: data subjects may lack access to the communications infrastructure required to submit Art.21 objections; language barriers prevent comprehension of what the right even is; functional capacity to exercise rights is compromised by the crisis itself; no Geneva authority has published guidance on what "adequate discharge" means in these contexts. Organizations are attempting to comply with obligations that have no operational translation methodology.

### 4.2 Institutional Donor AI Conditions — Non-Harmonized Obligations and Cascade Effect

Major institutional donors — development banks, UN agencies, bilateral government aid agencies — are inserting AI governance conditions into grant agreements with increasing specificity. The conditions are not harmonized across donors. The specific cascade problem: a multi-donor funded AI program may simultaneously be subject to three different donor AI governance frameworks that require different evidentiary formats, different documentation timelines, and different audit rights. The cost of compliance with non-harmonized donor conditions exceeds the governance capacity of most 10–100 person Geneva NGOs.

The Swiss law intersection: donor AI transparency requirements may require producing documentation about AI-inferred beneficiary data that activates nFADP Art.13 disclosure obligations to data subjects. Donor audit rights may require granting external access to beneficiary data in ways that create data transfer obligations under nFADP. No published guidance maps the intersection of institutional donor AI conditions with Swiss law for Geneva-based humanitarian organizations.

### 4.3 ZEWO Certification Scope and AI Governance Adequacy Signal

ZEWO certification evaluates financial governance completeness for Swiss charitable organizations. It does not evaluate AI governance adequacy. The practical consequence: institutional donors evaluating ZEWO-certified organizations interpret the certification as evidence of organizational reliability, including AI governance reliability. The inference is reasonable but the evidentiary basis is absent. Organizations with serious AI governance practices and ZEWO certification carry the same adequacy signal as organizations with inadequate AI governance practices and ZEWO certification.

The specific ZEWO-AI intersection that has not been named: ZEWO's financial governance requirements may require organizations to document AI-related expenditures in ways that create a partial AI cost visibility record. This record exists within the financial governance documentation system, not within an AI governance system, and the two documentation streams are not integrated.

### 4.4 Art.75 ZGB Personal Liability for Board Members in Geneva HQ AI Decisions

Swiss Art.75 ZGB establishes board member personal liability for breach of fiduciary duties in organizational management. Board members at Geneva-based NGOs who approve AI deployment strategies without adequate comprehension of what the strategy means in field operating contexts face specific personal liability exposure that fiduciary duty law addresses but that no Geneva NGO board training or advisory service has specifically mapped for the AI context.

The specific gap: Geneva board members are not being advised that approving AI deployment is a fiduciary act with personal liability consequences that requires the same substantive evaluation standard as approving financial investments or major programmatic commitments. The absence of this framing means board approval is given with inadequate information about what the approval actually commits the board member to.

### 4.5 Geneva Humanitarian AI Standards Ecosystem and Swiss Law Independence

The Geneva-based humanitarian AI coordination ecosystem — UNAIWG working groups, ICRC AI ethics handbook, OCHA data responsibility toolkit, sector-specific humanitarian AI deployment guidelines — operates as a self-contained standard-setting environment. These standards are developed by Geneva-based international organizations with global reach but are not subject to Swiss regulatory review, Swiss administrative law accountability, or Swiss judicial oversight.

The specific compliance implication: organizations following Geneva standards exclusively may be operating in compliance positions that are unevaluated against Swiss law. The relationship between Geneva humanitarian AI standards and Swiss regulatory requirements is not published in any consolidated form. Organizations have no reference document for understanding when following Geneva standards satisfies Swiss law, when it diverges from Swiss law, and when the relationship is simply unevaluated.

## 06 — Findings Summary: Six Net-New Insights

**Finding 01: Geneva HQ AI Decision Authority Structurally Misaligned with Field-Level AI Accountability — Approval Architecture Does Not Govern**

Geneva-based board approval of AI deployment strategies creates a specific structural accountability misalignment: the approving body holds decision authority but lacks field-level contextual knowledge; the field operating context holds the knowledge but lacks decision authority and reporting protocols. When an AI-assisted decision produces adverse consequences in the field, Geneva cannot reconstruct what the AI contributed because the approval architecture was never designed to capture field-level AI behavior as an accountability object. The board approved a description of an AI system. The board did not approve a governance architecture for how that system operates in contexts the board has not evaluated. This is distinct from C268 Finding 03 (board-approved AI strategy as historical document), which named the staleness problem without identifying the specific misalignment between HQ decision authority and field accountability as a structural design problem requiring a dedicated governance architecture solution.

**Finding 02: AI Vendor Contract Indemnification Asymmetry as Unnamed Contractual Exposure in Standard NGO AI Agreements**

NGO AI vendor contracts contain indemnification provisions that indemnify the vendor for the NGO's use of the tool while remaining silent on vendor indemnification for the NGO for tool behavior. The NGO accepts downstream liability for AI-assisted decisions through contracts reviewed by legal counsel without flagging this specific asymmetry. The exposure is unnamed in Geneva NGO governance discourse — organizations are not aware they are accepting downstream liability without corresponding vendor obligation. The problem is structural: vendor standard terms do not change because individual NGOs request changes, and most NGOs do not have the commercial leverage to negotiate terms with large AI platform vendors. The naming of this asymmetry as a discrete contractual exposure is distinct from all prior Phase 04 findings addressing vendor risk through the lens of data processing, data sovereignty, or tool migration — this finding names the specific contractual liability allocation problem that none of those prior findings addressed.

**Finding 03: Multi-Institutional Donor AI Condition Cascade Creating Fragmented Simultaneously Impossible Obligations Across Multi-Donor Portfolios**

Geneva NGOs with multiple institutional donors face non-harmonized AI governance conditions that arrive simultaneously, require incompatible evidentiary formats, and sometimes demand evidence that would expose the organization to nFADP liability if produced. The cascade effect — where each donor's conditions compound the aggregate compliance burden and the aggregate compliance gap — is distinct from prior findings about donor questionnaire burden and multi-framework incoherence. The specific mechanism this finding names: institutional donors are functioning as de facto AI governance standard-setters through grant conditions without coordination between themselves, creating aggregate obligations on grantees that no individual donor's conditions were designed to produce. The accountability for AI governance across a multi-donor portfolio is fragmented across multiple grant relationships, with no single donor owning the governance coherence question. This is distinct from C259 Finding 01 (multi-donor AI framework incoherence), which addressed framework incompatibility without identifying the cascade mechanism by which individual donor conditions compound into aggregate impossible obligations.

**Finding 04: nFADP Art.13/21 Crisis Context Discharge Methodology Entirely Absent for Displacement and Conflict Zone Operations**

Art.13 FADP requires disclosure of logical reasoning behind automated decisions. Art.21 grants data subjects the right to object to automated decision-making and request human review. No Swiss authority has published guidance on what these obligations require or how they can be discharged in displacement camp contexts, conflict zones, or acute crisis settings where data subjects may lack functional communications infrastructure, language capacity, or physical safety to exercise data rights. Organizations are attempting to comply with obligations that have no operational translation methodology for their specific operating context. The naming of this specific discharge methodology gap as distinct from general nFADP compliance is new to C269 Phase 04 — prior findings addressed nFADP in terms of beneficiary data rights, data inventory, and AI-inferred data, but none identified the specific crisis context discharge impossibility as a discrete unnamed compliance gap requiring its own methodology development.

**Finding 05: Geneva Humanitarian AI Standards Ecosystem Operating as Parallel Self-Contained Framework Without Published Swiss Law Mapping**

Geneva-based humanitarian AI coordination — UNAIWG working groups, ICRC AI ethics handbook, OCHA data responsibility toolkit, sector-specific protocols — functions as a self-contained standards environment developed by Geneva-based international organizations with global reach but operating independently of Swiss regulatory review, Swiss administrative law accountability, or Swiss judicial oversight. Organizations following Geneva standards exclusively may be operating in compliance positions unevaluated against Swiss law obligations. The specific gap this finding names: no published source maps the relationship between Geneva humanitarian AI standards and Swiss regulatory requirements, meaning organizations cannot determine whether following Geneva standards satisfies Swiss law, diverges from it, or operates in territory that is simply unevaluated. **Finding 06: AI Governance Documentation Functional Displacement — Visibility Function Satisfied While Governance Function Remains Unbuilt**

The primary driver of AI governance documentation production at many Geneva NGOs is donor request arrival, not governance need identification. Documentation is produced to satisfy the donor visibility function — to demonstrate that the organization is managing AI risk — while the actual governance function — to produce records that would allow the organization to reconstruct what the AI did, why, and what the consequences were when something goes wrong — remains unbuilt. The two functions are structurally different: visibility documentation demonstrates that a governance process was followed; governance documentation would allow actual accountability reconstruction. Organizations with substantial visibility documentation folders may have zero effective governance documentation. The naming of documentation functional displacement as a discrete structural problem is distinct from all prior Phase 04 findings addressing documentation adequacy, documentation completeness, and documentation shelf-life — those findings addressed the quality and longevity of documentation produced within an assumed governance function. This finding identifies that the governance function itself may be absent while the visibility function is satisfied, meaning documentation volume provides no evidence of governance substance.
