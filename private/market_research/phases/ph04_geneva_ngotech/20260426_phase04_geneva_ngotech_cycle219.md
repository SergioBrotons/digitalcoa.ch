## 02 — Market Signals

### Current problems, failure modes, complaints, and paradoxes

---

**Signal 1: Institutional Donor AI Conditions as Commercial Gatekeeping — Grant Agreements Now Containing AI Requirements That NGOs Cannot Practically Implement**

A structural shift in the institutional donor-NGO relationship: major institutional donors (EU DEVCO, ECHO, SDC, UN agencies) are embedding specific AI governance conditions in grant agreements. These conditions reference UNAIWG alignment, require completion of AI due diligence questionnaires, mandate AI incident response documentation, and in some cases require alignment with the EU AI Act for EU-funded programs. The conditions are now appearing in grant agreements in a more specific and enforceable form than was the case two years ago. The commercial consequence: compliance with donor AI conditions is becoming a gatekeeping mechanism for institutional funding — NGOs that cannot demonstrate AI governance capacity are losing competitive advantage in grant processes. The operational consequence: the conditions are written by donor compliance teams without proportionality calibration to the organizational scale of the implementing NGOs. An EU DEVCO AI annex referencing EU AI Act high-risk system classifications is not actionable for a forty-person organization. No donor proportionality framework exists. No advisory provider has developed donor-facing AI governance proportionality negotiation guidance for NGOs. The market signal: the gap between what institutional donors require and what implementing NGOs can deliver is now a live commercial risk, not just an abstract governance concern.

---

**Signal 2: FADP Beneficiary Rights vs. Operational Capacity — A Compliance Gap That NGOs Cannot Close Without Resources They Do Not Have**

The revised FADP gives beneficiaries stronger rights to contest AI-assisted decisions affecting them (Arts. 8, 15, 16, 22). For Geneva NGOs serving vulnerable populations — refugees, asylum seekers, survivors of trafficking — these rights carry heightened practical weight because the populations served face the greatest barriers to exercising them: language barriers, limited digital access, fear of consequences for challenging organizational decisions, and limited access to legal advice. The FADP does not differentiate rights based on vulnerability. The practical standard for honoring those rights is structurally higher for organizations serving vulnerable populations — without any formal recognition of this distinction in donor governance requirements or ZEWO guidance. The result: Geneva NGOs are legally obligated to honor beneficiary AI rights that their operational reality cannot support in a legally compliant form. The compliance gap is not a matter of organizational will — it is a resource problem that no amount of organizational effort can close without additional capacity. This is distinct from the FADP breach notification problem in C215: that signal addressed the 72-hour notification obligation. This signal addresses the individual rights obligations and the structural impossibility of operationalizing them for vulnerable populations at current organizational scale.

---

**Signal 3: AI Program Dependency Without Exit Strategy — The Embedded AI Risk That NGOs Have Not Acknowledged as a Continuity Problem**

A failure mode that has not been identified in prior NGO AI governance cycles: Geneva NGOs are building programmatic dependencies on AI tools without contingency planning for vendor failure, pricing changes, API discontinuation, or model degradation. The monitoring and evaluation infrastructure of a mid-sized Geneva NGO is now frequently AI-dependent in ways that would halt program tracking if the AI vendor becomes unavailable. Case management systems, beneficiary communication tools, field translation workflows — all increasingly built on AI components whose continuity depends on third-party vendor relationships that NGOs have not reviewed for sustainability or exit risk. This is distinct from the AI vendor deprecation problem identified in C216: C216 addressed vendor discontinuation as a technical IT problem. This signal addresses the specific program continuity risk — the scenario where an AI tool becomes so embedded in core program delivery that losing it would stop the program, not just create an inconvenience.

---

**Signal 4: Board AI Literacy Deficit Creating Personal Liability Without Awareness — A Training Gap That No Advisory Provider Has Addressed**

Board members of Geneva NGOs are approving AI-related plans without the technical literacy to understand what they are approving. Unlike the board fiduciary blind spot identified in C216 — which addressed the absence of AI governance on board agendas — this failure mode addresses the specific absence of board AI literacy: board members who want to fulfill their duty of care on AI decisions but cannot because they do not understand the technology well enough to ask the right questions. In a Swiss association law context where personal fiduciary duties attach to individual board members (Art. 69ff. OR), approving an AI system without understanding its function or risks is a personal liability exposure — not because the board member was negligent in a technical sense, but because they lacked the information environment to exercise meaningful oversight. No Geneva advisory provider has developed a board AI literacy briefing specifically calibrated for NGO board members serving in a personal capacity. The market gap: a discrete, affordable, plain-language board briefing product that helps NGO board members understand what their duty of care requires in the context of AI, what questions to ask, and what documentation protects them versus what creates liability.

---

**Signal 5: AI-Generated Donor Proposals and the Assumptions Accountability Gap — Errors Compounding From Proposal to Implementation**

Donor proposals from Geneva NGOs increasingly contain AI-generated outcome projections, beneficiary statistics, and theory of change language. The accountability failure: AI-generated content inherits assumptions that were not consciously selected by program staff — assumptions about baseline conditions, intervention logic, outcome trajectories, and causal relationships that the AI selected from data without human deliberation. Those assumptions then become the basis for donor-approved program designs, which become the basis for implementation plans, which become the basis for donor reporting. When the assumptions were wrong, the entire chain of commitments is built on an unexamined AI output. Donor perception of AI "sound" in proposals — a homogenized, smooth quality — is beginning to affect how evaluators assess proposal authenticity. The reputational consequence: organizations whose proposals are perceived as AI-generated lose credibility that cannot be easily recovered. The operational consequence: organizations cannot always explain their own proposal commitments because they do not know what assumptions the AI made. This is distinct from the AI program design entanglement problem identified in C218: C218 addressed AI becoming embedded in program methodology. This signal addresses the specific downstream accountability problem of AI-generated proposal assumptions that bind organizations to commitments they cannot defend.

---

**Signal 6: The Structural Incentive Misalignment in Geneva's AI Governance Knowledge Production — Knowledge Producers Do Not Bear Implementation Costs**

Geneva's AI governance knowledge producers (large institutions, academic centers, international organizations) create frameworks in an environment where they do not bear the implementation costs of what they produce. UNAIWG, ICRC AI ethics frameworks, OECD AI Principles — each produced by institutions with dedicated compliance infrastructure, for consumption by organizations that do not have that infrastructure. The structural result: knowledge production is calibrated to the production environment, not the implementation environment. The frameworks get more sophisticated over time, but the sophistication reflects the institutional capacity of the producers, not the implementation capacity of the consumers. The gap between Geneva's AI knowledge production and the field realities of implementing NGOs is widening — not because the knowledge is wrong, but because the incentive structure of knowledge production does not reward producing frameworks that are proportionate and actionable at the scale of mid-tier NGOs. This is the systemic cause of the implementation gap identified in prior cycles. No knowledge producer in Geneva has an explicit incentive to produce a simplified, proportionate version of their framework — the reputational reward for producing sophisticated frameworks exceeds the reward for producing useful ones.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss specific regulatory and professional obligations relevant to this ICP

---

**1. Institutional Donor AI Governance Conditions as Contractual Compliance Obligations — Proportionality Gap Creating Structural Liability**

SDC, EU DEVCO, ECHO, UN agencies, and Swiss foundations impose AI governance conditions in grant agreements. These conditions have become more specific and enforceable over the past 18 months: UNAIWG alignment requirements, AI due diligence questionnaires, AI incident documentation standards, and — for EU-funded programs — EU AI Act alignment references. Under Swiss contract law, grant conditions become binding contractual obligations once the grant agreement is signed. The proportionality problem: donor AI conditions are written by institutional compliance teams without calibration to the organizational scale of implementing NGOs. An EU DEVCO AI annex referencing EU AI Act high-risk classifications is not actionable for a thirty-person organization. No standard grant agreement language addresses proportionality. The commercial and legal consequence: NGOs are signing grant agreements that contain compliance obligations they cannot practically fulfill, creating contractual liability for non-compliance that has no proportionate relationship to the organization's actual governance capacity.

**2. FADP Individual Rights Obligations for Vulnerable Populations — Operational Capacity Gap With No Formal Recognition**

The revised FADP applies to all personal data processing, including AI-assisted processing of beneficiary data. For NGOs serving vulnerable populations (refugees, asylum seekers, survivors of trafficking), the FADP's individual rights provisions (Arts. 8, 15, 16, 22) carry heightened practical weight. Vulnerable populations face additional barriers to exercising data subject rights: language barriers, limited digital access, fear of consequences for challenging organizational decisions, and limited access to legal advice. The FADP does not differentiate rights based on vulnerability — the practical compliance standard is structurally higher for organizations serving vulnerable populations, without any formal recognition of this distinction in donor governance requirements, ZEWO guidance, or FADP implementing guidance. The result: Geneva NGOs face legal obligations that their operational reality cannot meet in a compliant form — not because of organizational failure, but because of a resource gap that compliance effort alone cannot close.

**3. ZEWO AI Narrative Reporting Expectations — Documentation Obligations Creating Honest Reporting Tension**

ZEWO-certified NGOs must report under SWISS GAAP FER 21, which includes narrative reporting requirements. ZEWO's evolving guidance signals that AI governance will increasingly be a narrative reporting expectation — organizations will be expected to describe their AI usage, data protection practices, and governance approach in annual reporting. For many smaller ZEWO-certified NGOs, this creates a documentation problem: they do not have the internal AI governance inventory to describe their AI posture honestly and credibly. The tension: ZEWO expects credible narrative reporting; honest reporting from an organization without a formal AI governance framework reveals governance gaps that may affect future certification decisions. No practical guidance exists for how a small ZEWO-certified NGO should approach AI narrative reporting — what to disclose, what to commit to, and how to present governance limitations in a way that is honest without being damaging.

**4. Cross-Border AI Data Flows in Multi-Jurisdictional Humanitarian Programs — FADP Adequacy Ambiguity in Practice**

Geneva NGOs operating cross-border humanitarian programs transfer beneficiary data across multiple jurisdictions through shared program management platforms. AI tools processing this data in multi-jurisdictional environments create cross-border data flow compliance questions under FADP adequacy rules and, for EU-connected programs, GDPR. The FADP's international transfer provisions are still developing; Geneva NGOs are making case-by-case judgments about data transfer legality without clear regulatory guidance. For AI-assisted processing specifically — where data is not just transferred but analyzed, profiled, and acted upon by AI systems across jurisdictions — the adequacy analysis is more complex than for standard data transfers. Geneva NGOs operating in fragile states or conflict zones face additional complexity: data transferred to jurisdictions with inadequate data protection frameworks may create FADP violations that the organization cannot easily remediate because the transfer is a programmatic necessity.

**5. Board Personal Fiduciary Duties and AI Oversight — The Undefined Standard Creating Personal Liability Uncertainty**

Under Swiss association law (Art. 69ff. OR), board members have personal fiduciary duties — duty of care (Sorgfaltspflicht) and duty of loyalty (Loyalitätspflicht) — that attach to individuals. For AI-related decisions: approving AI systems, approving annual plans with AI-assisted program elements, or failing to maintain oversight over AI deployments — each creates potential personal fiduciary exposure. Unlike corporate governance, where AI board duties are beginning to be articulated in regulatory guidance, NGO board members serving in a personal capacity have no formal standard to reference. The practical consequence: board members who want to fulfill their duty of care cannot find a plain-language, Geneva-specific resource that explains what AI oversight requires in practice. The liability uncertainty is personal and unquantified — and no Geneva advisory provider has addressed it directly for the NGO board context.

**6. Swiss Association Law and AI Supply Chain Accountability — No Framework for Allocating Incident Liability in Consortium Structures**

Large Geneva-based international NGOs structure programs as consortiums with smaller implementing partners. These consortiums involve shared AI-assisted platforms, AI-powered monitoring tools, and AI-informed beneficiary management systems — with smaller partners required to use the lead organization's AI infrastructure. Under Swiss contract law and association law, contractual liability flows through consortium agreements in ways that have not been tested for AI-specific incidents. When an AI system produces a harmful outcome, the accountability chain runs through the contractual structure of the consortium — which typically allocates liability to implementing partners for data quality and program delivery, without addressing AI system failures that are outside the partner's control or visibility. No Swiss legal framework or Geneva advisory guidance addresses the specific problem of AI supply chain accountability in NGO consortium architectures.

## 06 — Findings Summary

### Six net-new insights for C219 Phase 04 — distinct from C214 Phase 04, C215 Phase 04, C216 Phase 04, and C218 Phase 04

---

**Finding 1: Institutional Donor AI Conditions as Commercial Gatekeeping — Grant Agreements Now Containing AI Requirements That Function as Funding Access Conditions Without Proportionality Calibration**

Major institutional donors (EU DEVCO, ECHO, SDC, UN agencies) have embedded specific AI governance conditions in grant agreements in a form that is materially more enforceable than two years ago: UNAIWG alignment requirements, AI due diligence questionnaires, AI incident documentation standards, and EU AI Act references for EU-funded programs. These conditions are functioning as commercial gatekeeping mechanisms — NGOs that cannot demonstrate AI governance capacity are losing competitive positioning in grant processes. The structural problem: donor AI conditions are written by institutional compliance teams without proportionality calibration to the organizational scale of implementing NGOs. No donor proportionality framework exists. No advisory provider has developed donor-facing proportionality negotiation guidance for NGOs. The commercial consequence: NGOs are accepting compliance obligations in grant agreements that they cannot practically fulfill, creating contractual liability for non-compliance that has no proportionate relationship to their actual governance capacity. The opportunity: institutional donors who write AI conditions without expecting proportionality pushback may be more responsive to legitimate, evidence-based proportionality arguments than the current environment suggests — because donors also face reputational and operational consequences for funding non-compliant implementations.

*Distinct from C214 Finding 5 (UNAIWG contractual downstream): C214 identified UNAIWG as a contractual downstream obligation for NGOs. This finding addresses the distinct commercial mechanism through which AI conditions now operate as funding gatekeeping — specifically the shift from voluntary framework adoption to contractual compliance obligation embedded in grant agreements, with more specific and enforceable conditions than was the case two years ago.*

---

**Finding 2: FADP Beneficiary Rights vs. Operational Capacity — A Compliance Gap That Is Structurally Impossible to Close Without Resources NGOs Do Not Have**

The revised FADP gives beneficiaries stronger rights to contest AI-assisted decisions (Arts. 8, 15, 16, 22). For Geneva NGOs serving vulnerable populations, these rights carry heightened practical weight because the populations served face the greatest barriers to exercising them: language barriers, limited digital access, fear of consequences, limited legal literacy. The FADP does not differentiate rights based on vulnerability — the practical compliance standard is structurally higher for organizations serving vulnerable populations, without formal recognition of this distinction anywhere in the regulatory or donor guidance ecosystem. The result: Geneva NGOs face legal obligations that their operational reality cannot meet in a compliant form. The compliance gap is not a matter of organizational will — it is a resource problem. This is distinct from C215 Finding 6 (multi-vendor attribution gap): that finding addressed attributing harm when multiple AI vendors are involved. This finding addresses the specific operational impossibility of honoring FADP individual rights obligations for vulnerable populations at current organizational scale.

*Distinct from C215 Finding 4 (72-hour FADP breach notification vs NGO detection): C215 addressed the breach notification timing problem. This finding addresses the individual rights obligations — the right to explanation, the right to correction, the right to object to automated decision-making — and the structural impossibility of operationalizing those rights for vulnerable beneficiary populations at current resource levels.*

---

**Finding 3: AI Program Dependency Without Exit Strategy — The Embedded Continuity Risk That NGOs Have Not Acknowledged as a Program Design Problem**

Geneva NGOs are building programmatic dependencies on AI tools without contingency planning for vendor failure, pricing changes, API discontinuation, or model degradation. Monitoring and evaluation infrastructure, case management systems, beneficiary communication tools, and field translation workflows are increasingly built on AI components whose continuity depends on third-party vendor relationships that NGOs have not reviewed for sustainability risk. The specific failure mode: an AI tool becomes so embedded in core program delivery that losing it would halt the program — not create an inconvenience but a program-stopping continuity crisis. No Geneva advisory provider has developed an AI program dependency audit and exit strategy product for the NGO context. This is distinct from C216 Finding 3 (AI vendor deprecation): C216 addressed vendor discontinuation as an IT operational problem. This finding addresses the specific program design problem: AI dependencies that have become load-bearing in program architecture without deliberate architectural choice or contingency planning.

*Distinct from C216 Finding 3 (AI vendor deprecation as operational continuity risk): C216 framed vendor deprecation as a technical IT risk. This finding frames the same underlying risk differently — not as an IT problem but as a program design problem: the gradual, undiscussed embedding of AI dependencies into core program delivery architecture without a deliberate decision to treat those dependencies as load-bearing risks requiring contingency planning.*

---

**Finding 4: Board AI Literacy Deficit as a Personal Liability Vector — Approving AI Systems Without the Technical Environment to Exercise Meaningful Oversight**

Board members of Geneva NGOs are approving AI-related plans without the technical literacy to understand what they are approving. This is distinct from the board fiduciary blind spot identified in C216: that finding addressed the absence of AI from board agendas. This finding addresses the specific absence of board AI literacy — board members who want to fulfill their duty of care on AI decisions but cannot because they lack the conceptual environment to ask the right questions. In a Swiss association law context where personal fiduciary duties attach to individual board members (Art. 69ff. OR), approving an AI system without understanding its function or risks creates personal liability exposure — not because the board member was negligent in a general sense, but because they lacked the information necessary to exercise the specific duty of care that applies to their role. The market gap: a discrete, affordable, plain-language board AI literacy briefing for NGO board members that addresses what AI oversight requires in practice, what questions to ask, what documentation protects versus creates liability, and what reasonable oversight looks like for a board governing a mid-tier NGO. No Geneva advisory provider has developed this specific product for the NGO board context.

*Distinct from C218 Finding 4 (board personal fiduciary liability undefined): C218 identified the legal question of whether Swiss association law creates personal liability for board AI decisions. This finding addresses the specific practical mechanism through which that liability arises: not the legal question but the literacy deficit that makes it impossible for board members to exercise the oversight that would satisfy their duty of care — and describes the specific briefing product that would close that gap.*

---

**Finding 5: AI-Generated Proposal Assumptions Compounding Through the Commitment Chain — A Distinct Accountability Failure Mode That Has Not Been Named**

Donor proposals from Geneva NGOs increasingly contain AI-generated outcome projections, beneficiary statistics, and theory of change language. The specific accountability failure: AI-generated content inherits assumptions that program staff did not consciously select — assumptions about baseline conditions, causal logic, outcome trajectories, and intervention design that the AI selected from data without human deliberation. Those assumptions then become the basis for donor-approved program designs, implementation plans, and donor reporting frameworks. When the assumptions were wrong, the entire chain of commitments is built on unexamined AI outputs that the organization cannot always explain because it does not know what the AI assumed. Donor evaluators are beginning to perceive AI "sound" in proposals — a homogenized smoothness — which is creating a secondary reputational consequence: organizations whose proposals are perceived as AI-generated lose credibility in ways that compound across funding relationships. This failure mode has not been identified in any prior Geneva NGO AI research cycle. It requires a distinct advisory product: a proposal AI accountability review that audits AI-generated proposal content for assumptions, validates which assumptions require human deliberation, and develops documentation practices that allow organizations to defend their commitments.

*Distinct from C218 Finding 3 (AI program design entanglement): C218 identified the gradual embedding of AI into program methodology as a governance boundary problem. This finding identifies the specific downstream accountability problem: AI-generated proposal assumptions that bind organizations to commitments they cannot defend, and the compounding chain from proposal to implementation to reporting in which those unexamined assumptions propagate.*

---

**Finding 6: The Structural Incentive Misalignment in Geneva's AI Governance Knowledge Production — The Reason the Implementation Gap Persists Despite Abundant Guidance**

Geneva's AI governance knowledge producers (large institutions, academic centers, international organizations) create frameworks in an environment where they do not bear the implementation costs of what they produce. UNAIWG, ICRC AI ethics frameworks, OECD AI Principles — each produced by institutions with dedicated compliance infrastructure, for consumption by organizations that do not have that infrastructure. The structural result: knowledge production is calibrated to the production environment, not the implementation environment. The frameworks get more sophisticated over time, but the sophistication reflects the institutional capacity of the producers, not the implementation capacity of the consumers. The reputational reward for producing sophisticated frameworks exceeds the reputational reward for producing proportionate, actionable frameworks — because sophisticated frameworks are more visible in Geneva's academic and policy discourse. The gap between Geneva's AI knowledge production and the field realities of implementing NGOs is widening as a direct result. This is the systemic root cause of the Monday morning operationalization gap identified in C214, the UNAIWG downstream problem identified in C214, and the implementation gap identified in C218. No prior cycle has identified this structural cause. Closing the implementation gap requires someone to systematically invest in translation rather than new framework production — and the incentive structure of Geneva's knowledge production ecosystem does not reward that investment.

*Distinct from C218 Finding 6 (implementation gap as structural market failure): C218 identified the implementation gap as a market failure and described the mismatch between knowledge producers and consumers. This finding identifies the specific mechanism that causes the market failure to persist: the incentive structure of Geneva's knowledge production rewards sophisticated framework production, not proportionate framework translation. C218 described the symptom and the mismatch; this finding describes the specific incentive mechanism that ensures the gap continues to widen regardless of how much sophisticated guidance is produced.*
