## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Program wind-down data disposal as discrete regulatory exposure**
When programs close, the accumulated beneficiary data processed through AI systems has no Geneva-specific disposal framework. The question of what to do with AI-accumulated data at program closure is a discrete regulatory exposure that is not addressed by nFADP consent frameworks.

**Signal 2: Donor AI questionnaire asymmetry as structural documentation problem**
The donor asks questions in their AI governance questionnaire that the NGO cannot answer from its current documentation. The asymmetry is structural. The NGO would need a documentation architecture it does not have to close the gap.

**Signal 3: IT capacity constraint creating shadow AI governance failure**
Limited IT capacity means approved AI tools may be unavailable to staff. Staff use alternative tools outside official channels. Shadow AI creates accountability for ungoverned systems. The governance failure is a structural consequence of the IT constraint.

**Signal 4: Board-approved policy and staff AI use as parallel realities**
The board approved a policy. Staff are using AI tools the board does not know about. These two realities coexist without resolution. The parallel realities create latent exposure that only surfaces when something goes wrong.

**Signal 5: AI governance budget architecture failure as accounting problem**
The NGO cannot find AI governance costs in its budget. The problem is not priority; it is accounting architecture. No accounting category captures governance costs that do not map to existing line items.

**Signal 6: Routine AI derivation decay as continuous invisible exposure**
AI outputs that were approved become progressively less auditable as AI systems evolve. This is continuous invisible exposure. The degradation is normal operations, not a crisis event.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Program wind-down AI data disposal framework for Geneva NGOs**
A disposal framework specific to AI-accumulated beneficiary data at program closure, designed for Geneva NGO scale, does not exist. nFADP consent frameworks cover collection and processing. End-of-life disposal for AI-accumulated data is a distinct problem.

**Gap 2: Donor AI questionnaire response architecture for resource-constrained NGOs**
A response architecture that helps the resource-constrained NGO answer donor AI governance questionnaires credibly — without the documentation infrastructure the donor implicitly assumes — does not exist. The NGO needs a methodology for honest disclosure from an incomplete documentation base.

**Gap 3: IT-constrained governance design for small NGOs**
A governance design specifically for small NGOs with limited IT capacity — that works within the constraint rather than requiring infrastructure the NGO does not have — does not exist. The governance solution that requires IT infrastructure is not available to those who need it most.

**Gap 4: Geneva domestic NGO HQ-office governance split mapping**
The Geneva domestic NGO with a separate HQ and field office faces a governance split. Swiss cantonal law adds complexity that international org frameworks do not address. A specific mapping for this Swiss domestic variant does not exist.

**Gap 5: AI governance budget architecture for NGOs**
An accounting architecture that captures AI governance costs as a visible line item — so the NGO can budget for governance, not just absorb governance failures — does not exist. The cost visibility problem is an accounting problem, not a priority problem.

**Gap 6: Board-staff AI governance reconciliation mechanism**
A mechanism for reconciling board-approved AI policy with staff AI practice — without creating a compliance overhead that overwhelms small NGOs — does not exist. The parallel realities need a resolution mechanism.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Swiss Association Law Art. 75 ff. ZGB:**
Board members face personal liability under Art. 75 ZGB for decisions made in their function. AI governance decisions made without adequate documentation may implicate this personal liability. The personal exposure is structural and unmanaged.

**nFADP program wind-down and consent form gaps:**
nFADP consent frameworks cover the collection and processing of personal data. They do not cover the end-of-life disposition of data accumulated through AI-assisted program delivery. The disposal gap is structural.

**ZEWO AI certification uncertainty:**
ZEWO certification is beginning to include AI governance expectations. The expectations are not yet clearly defined. The NGO operating under ZEWO certification faces uncertainty about what adequate AI governance means for certification purposes.

**Institutional donor AI governance conditions:**
Institutional donors are including AI governance conditions in grant agreements. These conditions may require the NGO to have documentation capabilities that the NGO does not have. The grant agreement creates obligations the NGO cannot currently fulfill.

**Geneva cantonal data protection (CCPD) jurisdiction over AI inferences:**
The Geneva Cantonal Data Protection Authority (CCPD) jurisdiction may extend to AI inferences about beneficiaries. The question of when a data protection obligation attaches to an AI inference is not clearly resolved for the NGO context.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Program wind-down AI data disposal as discrete regulatory exposure**
When programs close, AI-accumulated beneficiary data has no Geneva-specific disposal framework. nFADP consent frameworks cover collection and processing. End-of-life disposition is structurally unaddressed. The regulatory exposure activates at program closure.

**Finding 2: Donor AI questionnaire asymmetry as structural documentation problem**
The donor's questionnaire assumes documentation the NGO does not have. The NGO would need a documentation architecture it does not have to answer the donor's questions credibly. The asymmetry is structural, not a documentation failure.

**Finding 3: IT capacity constraint creating shadow AI governance failure**
The governance failure is a structural consequence of the IT constraint, not a staff compliance failure. Solving it requires governance design that works within the IT constraint, not governance design that requires IT infrastructure the NGO does not have.

**Finding 4: Board-approved policy and staff AI use as parallel unresolving realities**
The parallel realities coexist without a reconciliation mechanism. The exposure is latent. The mechanism for aligning board-approved policy with staff AI practice is missing at the organizational level.

**Finding 5: AI governance budget architecture failure as accounting problem**
The cost visibility problem is an accounting architecture problem. Until governance costs map to a visible budget line item, the NGO cannot budget for governance. It can only absorb governance failures after they occur.

**Finding 6: Routine AI derivation decay as continuous invisible exposure**
The continuous degradation of AI auditable outputs is an invisible exposure. It accumulates silently in normal operations. The firm discovers the exposure only when it needs to reconstruct a decision or respond to an inquiry.
