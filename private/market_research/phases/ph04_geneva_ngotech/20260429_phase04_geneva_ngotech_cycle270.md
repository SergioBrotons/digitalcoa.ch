## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### English

**Executive director on what the AI tool actually does in the field:**
"We deployed the AI tool in three countries. Each deployment was different. Geneva did not review the deployments — we did not have the capacity. The outputs went to beneficiaries. We found out later that one of the outputs was wrong. We could not go back and correct it for everyone who had received it."

**On what the donor questionnaire revealed:**
"When the institutional donor sent the AI governance questionnaire, we had to stop and take stock. We had been using AI in our programs for two years. We had not made any governance decisions about it. The questionnaire asked us to document decisions that had never been made. We were producing a governance record for a process that did not exist."

**On board oversight and what the board actually sees:**
"We present AI governance to the board once a year. The board approves the strategy. Between presentations, the vendor updates the AI tools, staff depart and take institutional knowledge with them, and program configurations drift. The strategy the board approved is a document. It is not describing what is actually happening."

**On what the AI costs in the budget:**
"We cannot see what we spend on AI. The costs are absorbed into program budgets, into staff time, into general operations. The board cannot make decisions about AI investment because the information does not exist in a form the board can use."

**On beneficiary data and what the AI has inferred:**
"Beneficiaries have started asking questions about what data we have about them and what the AI has concluded. We did not design our AI systems with nFADP compliance in mind. The questions are arriving before we have any architecture for answering them."

## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Field AI deployment without Geneva review as structural quality gap**
Field deployments happen without Geneva-level review. Each deployment is different. The outputs go to beneficiaries. Errors discovered after distribution cannot be corrected retroactively across all recipients. The gap is structural.

**Signal 2: Donor AI governance questionnaire surfacing governance decisions that were never made**
Questionnaires arriving and surfacing that governance decisions were never made. The NGO is asked to produce a governance record for a process that did not exist. The discovery is costly because the gap has compounded silently over years.

**Signal 3: Board-approved AI strategy as document not reflecting current state**
Board-approved strategies become historical documents between annual presentations. Vendor updates, staff departures, and configuration drift accumulate without triggering review.

**Signal 4: AI cost invisibility across budget lines**
AI costs absorbed into program budgets, staff time, and general operations. The board cannot see total AI costs in a form that enables governance decisions.

**Signal 5: Beneficiary nFADP rights questions arriving before any compliance architecture**
AI-inferred beneficiary data activates nFADP rights that NGOs have no architecture for responding to. The questions are arriving from more sophisticated beneficiaries than expected.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Field AI deployment review protocol for Geneva HQ**
No protocol exists for Geneva HQ review of field AI deployments. The protocol would need to specify what HQ reviews, how deployments are documented, and what triggers HQ-level review.

**Gap 2: Donor AI governance questionnaire preparation service for NGOs**
No advisory service exists for preparing NGOs to respond to donor AI governance questionnaires before the deadline. The preparation would need to address what documentation exists, what is missing, and what to do about the gap.

**Gap 3: Beneficiary nFADP rights response architecture for AI-inferred data**
No architecture exists for responding to beneficiary nFADP rights questions about AI-inferred data. The architecture would need to address what data exists, what rights are activated, and how to discharge them.

**Gap 4: AI cost visibility architecture for NGO governance**
No architecture exists for making AI costs visible as a governance object. The architecture would need to address how to classify and aggregate costs across budget lines.

**Gap 5: Vendor AI update evaluation protocol for NGOs**
No protocol exists for evaluating vendor AI updates and their effect on program delivery. The protocol would need to specify what to assess when updates arrive.

**Gap 6: Field AI output quality assurance for multi-language delivery**
No protocol exists for quality assurance of AI outputs in languages the organization cannot review.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**ZEWO certification and AI governance documentation:**
ZEWO certification examines documentation completeness, not governance adequacy. NGOs with documentation satisfying ZEWO requirements may still have governance gaps that certification does not surface.

**Institutional donor AI conditions in grant agreements:**
Institutional donors are inserting AI governance conditions into grant agreements. The conditions create obligations NGOs sign without evaluating. The interpretation burden falls on the NGO with least capacity to assess it.

**nFADP Articles 13 and 21 for AI-inferred beneficiary data:**
Art. 13 requires explanations of automated decisions affecting data subjects. Art. 21 requires documentation of AI-assisted decision logic. AI-inferred beneficiary data activates these obligations without any architecture for discharge.

**Art. 75 ZGB board member personal liability for AI oversight:**
Board members approving AI strategies without adequate review mechanisms face personal liability under Art. 75 ZGB. The liability is active and specific.

**Data sovereignty in international NGO operations:**
NGO operations spanning multiple jurisdictions create data sovereignty questions that nFADP alone does not resolve. The intersection of Swiss data protection law with field country requirements creates compliance complexity no framework addresses.

**Vendor AI updates as missing trigger in governance frameworks:**
No Geneva NGO governance framework includes vendor AI updates as a governance trigger.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Field AI deployment without Geneva review as structural quality gap with no retroactive correction mechanism**
Field deployments happen without Geneva-level review. Each deployment is different. Errors discovered after distribution cannot be corrected retroactively across all recipients. The gap is structural and the correction mechanism does not exist.

**Finding 2: Donor AI governance questionnaire as discovery event for governance decisions that were never made**
The NGO is asked to produce a governance record for a process that did not exist. The gap has compounded silently over years of ungoverned AI adoption. The questionnaire surfaces the gap at the worst possible moment.

**Finding 3: Board-approved AI strategy as historical document with no between-presentations review trigger**
Vendor updates, staff departures, and configuration drift accumulate without triggering review. The structural gap is unnamed in all Geneva NGO governance frameworks.

**Finding 4: AI cost invisibility across budget lines as governance paralysis for board decision-making**
The board cannot see total AI costs in a form that enables governance decisions. The paralysis is organizational and structural.

**Finding 5: Beneficiary nFADP rights sophistication exceeding NGO response capability as structural exposure**
Beneficiaries arriving with more sophisticated questions about AI-inferred data than NGOs are equipped to answer. The gap will surface adversarially.

**Finding 6: Field AI output quality assurance structurally absent when language review is impossible**
No protocol exists for quality assurance of AI outputs in languages the organization cannot review. The gap is structural to how field operations work with resource constraints.
