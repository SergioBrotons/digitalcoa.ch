## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

1. **Board member personal AI use creating organizational governance exposure without any governance surface** — Board members are using AI tools personally — to draft board documents, prepare meeting materials, draft resolutions — without any organizational rules governing that use. When the output becomes an organizational document, the organization owns it without knowing it was AI-generated. No Geneva NGO has governance rules for board member personal AI use. The exposure is unacknowledged and unmanaged.

2. **AI-generated content authenticity emerging as a donor relationship variable without defined norms** — NGOs are using AI to generate impact reports, program proposals, and donor communications. The quality of output is high enough that donors cannot easily detect AI generation. As donor sophistication on AI grows, the question of whether content is AI-generated becomes a credibility signal. NGOs have no disclosure norm to follow and no internal policy governing when AI-generated content should or should not be identified as such.

3. **AI vendor wind-down exposure as program continuity risk structurally unaddressed** — AI tools critical to program delivery are at risk of vendor discontinuation through acquisition, strategic pivot, or market exit. The NGO has no contingency for tool loss because AI tools are embedded in workflows without documented fallback procedures. Unlike financial sector vendor concentration concerns, this is program delivery continuity — the mission stops when the tool stops.

4. **AI comprehension gap at beneficiary delivery level not resolved by legal disclosure adequacy** — Beneficiaries receiving AI-assisted decisions cannot understand what the AI decided or why. FADP-compliant disclosure documents are legally correct but operationally useless — they do not serve the beneficiary's actual need to comprehend what is happening to them. The gap is between legal disclosure adequacy and functional comprehension at the point of service delivery.

5. **ZEWO AI disclosure evolution creating a documentation imperative for smaller NGOs without compliance infrastructure** — ZEWO is tightening AI governance narrative requirements under Swiss GAAP FER 21. For smaller NGOs, this creates a documentation imperative with no internal capacity to fulfill it. The forcing function is real but the implementation pathway is absent.

6. **Efficiency paradox: AI saves time on program delivery but compliance overhead consumes the saved capacity** — NGOs adopt AI to increase program delivery efficiency. The saved time is then consumed by documentation requirements, donor reporting, and AI governance obligations that did not exist before the AI was adopted. Net capacity gain is zero or negative, but the governance obligations remain. The efficiency proposition does not account for the compliance cost it generates.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### Gaps — What No One Is Addressing for Geneva NGO/Tech

- **Board member personal AI use governance for NGOs** — No framework exists for governing board member personal AI use when that use produces organizational documents. The gap is distinct from board AI literacy (understanding AI) or board mandate staleness (approving AI) — it is governance of a specific behavior that creates organizational exposure without organizational awareness.

- **AI content authenticity disclosure framework for NGO donor relationships** — No norm exists for when and how NGOs should disclose that donor-facing content was AI-generated. This is distinct from AI-generated proposal authenticity (C222) — it applies to ongoing donor relationship content, not just proposal submissions, and the disclosure question is now operational rather than theoretical.

- **AI vendor wind-down contingency planning for program delivery organizations** — No advisory or planning methodology addresses AI tool discontinuation contingency for organizations where program delivery depends on the tool continuing to function. The distinction from financial sector vendor concentration: this is mission continuity, not financial risk.

- **FADP beneficiary comprehension gap at service delivery level** — No methodology exists for translating FADP-compliant legal disclosure into beneficiary-comprehensible explanation at the point of AI-assisted service delivery. This is distinct from consent form design (C214) — it is the comprehension problem after the consent form is signed.

- **AI efficiency compliance cost accounting for NGOs** — No analysis has been done on the net capacity impact of AI adoption for capacity-constrained NGOs, accounting for the governance and documentation obligations that AI adoption generates. The efficiency proposition is structurally incomplete.

- **Geneva AI knowledge economy gap: Geneva producers not Geneva consumers** — Geneva-based AI knowledge producers (EPFL, SDSC, UN agencies, Geneva think tanks) create AI governance frameworks primarily for external consumption — Global South organizations, implementing partners, UN field operations. The Geneva NGO that is itself based in Geneva is not the primary beneficiary of the knowledge economy it helps fund through institutional membership and participation. This is a structural market gap that creates a specific advisory opportunity.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Specific Obligations

1. **ZEWO AI disclosure evolution under Swiss GAAP FER 21** — The Swiss nonprofit certification body is increasingly expecting AI governance narrative in certification and annual reporting. For smaller NGOs with ZEWO certification, this is an emerging documentation requirement without a clear adequacy standard. The pressure is structural and growing.

2. **Board personal liability under Swiss association law Art. 69ff.** — Individual board members carry personal liability for organizational decisions. Board member use of personal AI tools to draft organizational documents creates a specific liability surface: the board member made an organizational decision using an AI tool with no governance rules, no documentation, and no organizational awareness. This is distinct from board-level AI mandate approval (which is acknowledged) — board member personal AI use creating organizational exposure is unacknowledged.

3. **FADP Art. 22 beneficiary rights: comprehension gap at service delivery level** — Beneficiaries have rights to explanation and human review of AI-assisted decisions. NGOs are technically capable of producing FADP-compliant disclosure documents. The compliance gap is functional, not legal: beneficiaries cannot comprehend the explanation even when it is legally correct. For vulnerable populations served by humanitarian NGOs, the comprehension gap is acute and creates practical protection failure even when legal obligations are technically met.

4. **AI-enhanced beneficiary due diligence and Swiss anti-terrorism financing** — When AI tools assist in beneficiary screening or prioritization, the organization is making decisions that may trigger enhanced Swiss anti-terrorism financing due diligence obligations. The intersection of AI-assisted decision-making and Swiss anti-terrorism financing compliance is unmapped for NGO operational contexts. Most NGOs do not know where the intersection begins.

5. **UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights + Swiss association law double exposure** — Geneva-based international NGOs implementing UN programs face obligations under UNGP BHR and Swiss association law simultaneously. The two frameworks are not reconciled in most organizational governance structures. The specific exposure: a board member of an international NGO organized under Swiss law faces personal liability under Swiss association law for decisions that the UN framework addresses at the institutional level. The double exposure is unacknowledged and unmanaged.

6. **FADP Art. 26 breach notification: 72-hour window vs. AI system monitoring capacity** — The 72-hour FADP breach notification requirement assumes the organization can detect a breach within 72 hours. AI systems processing beneficiary data through cloud-based APIs create breach surfaces that most NGOs cannot monitor. The compliance obligation is untethered from detection capacity at NGO scale.

7. **AI vendor wind-down and program continuity documentation** — No Geneva AI governance framework addresses what happens to an NGO's program delivery when the AI tool it depends on is discontinued, sold, or blocked by sanctions. The program continuity gap is structural: tools are embedded without documented fallback procedures, and no funder audit question yet addresses this gap, but the risk is real and growing.

8. **AI content authenticity in funder audit and due diligence contexts** — Major institutional donors are beginning to ask about AI usage in program delivery and reporting as part of standard audit and due diligence processes. NGOs using AI to generate impact reports and program documentation face an emerging documentation requirement: they must be able to answer what role AI played in producing what they submitted. This is distinct from donor AI governance conditions (which address organizational governance) — it is specifically about content authenticity in the materials submitted.

## 06 — Findings Summary

### 6 Net-New Findings (Distinct from C214–C225 Phase 04)

1. **Board member personal AI use is an unmanaged governance surface with direct personal liability consequences** — Board members are using personal AI tools to draft organizational documents — board resolutions, meeting materials, governance communications — without any organizational rules governing that use. When the AI-generated output becomes an organizational document, the organization owns it without knowing it was AI-generated. This is distinct from board AI literacy deficit (C222 — ability to understand AI briefings) and board mandate staleness (C225 — approved mandates becoming outdated) — it is governance of a specific personal behavior that creates organizational exposure without organizational awareness. The liability runs to the board member personally under Swiss association law Art. 69ff. when the organizational document carries consequences the board member did not meaningfully review.

2. **Geneva AI knowledge economy creates a structural market gap: Geneva producers are not Geneva consumers** — Geneva-based AI knowledge producers (EPFL AI Center, Swiss Data Science Center, UN agency working groups, Geneva think tanks) develop AI governance frameworks primarily for external consumption — Global South implementing partners, UN field operations, development sector clients. The Geneva NGO that funds this knowledge economy through institutional participation and membership is not the primary beneficiary of the frameworks it helps produce. This structural irony means the Geneva NGO segment — which has the most acute governance gaps — is systematically underserved by the knowledge infrastructure it participates in building. This is distinct from the framework proportionality gap (C222) — that addressed framework implementability; this addresses the specific market failure created by Geneva knowledge producers not designing for Geneva knowledge consumers.

3. **AI efficiency gains for NGOs are structurally offset by compliance overhead they generate — net capacity gain is zero or negative** — NGOs adopt AI to increase program delivery efficiency. The operational capacity saved is then consumed by documentation requirements, donor reporting obligations, and AI governance documentation that did not exist before AI adoption. The efficiency proposition at NGO scale is structurally incomplete: it does not account for the governance cost the AI adoption generates. This is distinct from the efficiency paradox in finance ICP cycles (C226 Phase 02 — verification time inflation) — the NGO manifestation is program efficiency creating compliance overhead, not financial service QC costs consuming saved time.

4. **AI content authenticity disclosure is an emerging donor relationship variable without established NGO-sector norms** — As institutional donors become more sophisticated about AI, the question of whether program reports, impact narratives, and funding proposals were AI-generated becomes a credibility signal in the donor relationship. NGOs have no disclosure norm to follow and no internal policy governing when AI-generated donor-facing content should be identified as such. This is distinct from AI-generated proposal authenticity (C222) — that addressed the initial proposal submission question; this addresses ongoing donor relationship content and the emerging expectation that organizations can account for the role AI played in producing submitted materials.

5. **AI vendor wind-down contingency for program delivery organizations is a distinct continuity risk from financial sector vendor concentration** — When AI tools critical to program delivery are discontinued through vendor acquisition, strategic pivot, or market exit, program delivery stops. Unlike financial sector vendor concentration concerns (where the risk is financial or data), this is mission continuity risk: the humanitarian or social program simply cannot be delivered without the tool. No advisory methodology in Geneva addresses AI vendor wind-down contingency for program delivery organizations. This is distinct from AI vendor lock-in (C225) — that addressed strategic vendor dependency; this addresses specifically the program continuity problem when the vendor ceases to exist as a going concern.

6. **FADP beneficiary rights compliance at NGO scale has a comprehension gap that legal disclosure adequacy does not resolve** — FADP-compliant disclosure documents are technically achievable by NGOs. The actual protection failure is at the service delivery level: beneficiaries who receive AI-assisted decisions cannot understand what was decided or why, even when the legal disclosure document is correct and available. For vulnerable populations served by humanitarian NGOs — those least likely to understand AI-assisted decisions and least likely to exercise legal rights without organizational support — the comprehension gap is acute. This is distinct from consent form participation drop (C214) and donor visibility vs. beneficiary autonomy conflict (C225) — those addressed consent and structural conflict respectively; this specifically addresses the functional comprehension failure after legal disclosure obligations are technically satisfied.
