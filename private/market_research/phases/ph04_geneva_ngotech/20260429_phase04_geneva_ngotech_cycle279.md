## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Mid-program AI model deprecation as governance decision without evaluation methodology**
AI vendors deprecate model versions on commercial timelines. Geneva NGOs using AI for beneficiary outcomes face deprecation events mid-program with no organizational methodology for evaluating whether the replacement model produces equivalent conclusions for their specific beneficiary populations. The migration decision is forced — the alternative is losing the tool. Organizations migrate without evaluating program outcome equivalence because no methodology exists for conducting that evaluation at NGO scale. The deprecation is framed as a vendor notification; it arrives as a governance decision the organization is not equipped to make.

**Signal 2: Donor AI governance questionnaire design quality as organizational self-discovery instrument with distorting effects**
Donor AI governance questionnaires function as measurement instruments whose validity has not been examined. Organizations complete questionnaires without evaluating whether the questionnaire measures genuine governance adequacy or whether it measures something else — organizational responsiveness, form completion capacity, donor relationship management. The questionnaire response becomes the organization's documented AI governance position. Organizations whose actual practices are adequate may produce inadequate questionnaire responses because the questionnaire design has gaps the organization did not notice. Organizations whose practices are inadequate may produce adequate questionnaire responses because the questionnaire did not probe the actual gaps. No Geneva NGO has submitted its donor AI governance questionnaire for design quality review before responding to it.

**Signal 3: Silent model update propagation across active beneficiary portfolios creating eligibility continuity gaps**
AI model updates propagate silently through active programs. When program managers apply updated model conclusions to existing beneficiary lists without noticing that conclusions have changed, eligibility determinations that were stable under the old model become unstable under the new model without organizational awareness. The continuity gap is structurally invisible: no tracking architecture alerts the organization when model updates have altered conclusions that affected active beneficiaries. The gap compounds with each update and surfaces only when a beneficiary challenges an outcome the organization cannot explain.

**Signal 4: AI inference data subject request triage architecture entirely absent at small NGO scale**
When a beneficiary or their representative asks how the AI categorized them, Geneva NGOs have no triage architecture for determining what was inferred, what the disclosure scope is, what methodology exposure is acceptable, and what response pathway applies per inference category. The request arrives before any process exists for responding. Organizations respond ad hoc, without knowing whether the response satisfied the obligation. The absence is distinct from nFADP compliance program gaps — it is a specific response architecture gap for AI-inferred content that general data subject request processes do not address.

**Signal 5: Board-approved AI governance stale at the specific moment boards believe it is current**
Boards that approved AI deployment receive no systematic update when the AI tool evolves. The approval document specifies a tool version and configuration that no longer exists. The board's oversight belief is not matched by operational reality — the board believes it has active governance over an AI system whose parameters have changed repeatedly since the approval was given. The staleness is invisible because no mechanism forces it to surface. The board's assurance is real; the object of that assurance no longer exists in the form the board approved.

**Signal 6: Geneva AI governance framework ecosystem provenance mapping structurally absent as accountability architecture gap**
UNAIWIG, ICRC, OCHA, and institutional donor AI frameworks each carry different accountability relationships but no authoritative provenance map exists to determine which framework is hierarchically superior, which applies in which operational moments, and which creates operative obligations versus aspirational guidance. Geneva NGOs following any single framework cannot determine whether that framework satisfies obligations the framework's producer does not hold. The provenance map is an accountability architecture question, not a content analysis exercise. No Geneva service has produced it.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Post-model-deprecation program outcome equivalence evaluation methodology at NGO scale**
No methodology exists for Geneva NGOs to evaluate whether a replacement AI model produces program outcomes equivalent to the model it replaced, for their specific beneficiary population, within the vendor-imposed migration timeline. The methodology would need to: define what outcome equivalence means for the specific program, identify what data would demonstrate or detect non-equivalence, establish a proportionate evaluation protocol that fits within 60-day deprecation windows, and produce documentation the organization can present to donors if asked. This is distinct from AI quality assurance testing (which assumes internal testing capacity and controlled environments) and from vendor performance evaluation (which addresses commercial terms not program outcome equivalence). The specific function is mid-program model migration governance — evaluating whether the organization's AI-dependent programs remain sound after the AI system changes in ways the organization did not design for.

**Gap 2: Donor AI governance questionnaire design quality review as discrete pre-submission service**
No service offers systematic review of donor AI governance questionnaires before submission — evaluating whether the questionnaire design is measuring genuine governance adequacy or whether design gaps are producing misleading results. The review would identify: which questions the organization's actual practice cannot answer because the practice is adequate, which questions the organization answered adequately but the questionnaire design does not capture, what the questionnaire's blind spots reveal about the donor's own AI governance assumptions, and how to respond in a way that accurately represents practice without creating documentation the organization cannot stand behind. This is distinct from questionnaire response preparation (which addresses how to answer given questions) and from compliance gap assessment (which evaluates the organization against a defined standard). The specific function is questionnaire design quality review — evaluating the instrument before evaluating the organization's response to it.

**Gap 3: AI model update propagation tracking architecture for active beneficiary portfolios**
No service offers systematic tracking of how AI model updates alter conclusions for beneficiaries currently enrolled in AI-assisted programs. The architecture would: monitor when model updates occur, detect whether updated model conclusions differ materially from prior conclusions for the active beneficiary list, identify which beneficiaries' eligibility or service categorizations changed, and trigger organizational review when material shifts occur. This is distinct from AI tool version tracking (which addresses the technical version state) and from data subject request response (which addresses individual beneficiary inquiries). The specific function is propagation tracking across active portfolios — ensuring that when the AI model changes, the organization knows what changed for whom, before a beneficiary or donor asks.

**Gap 4: AI inference data subject request triage protocol for small NGO operations**
No protocol exists for determining how a Geneva NGO with no dedicated legal or compliance function should respond when a beneficiary or their representative asks for an explanation of how the AI categorized them. The protocol would need to: determine what AI-inferred content exists in the organization's records about the requesting beneficiary, assess which inferences must be disclosed under nFADP Art. 13, identify what methodology explanation the organization is obligated to provide versus what it may decline to provide, and establish a response pathway appropriate for an NGO operating without in-house legal counsel. This is distinct from general data subject request processes (which address directly collected data not AI-inferred content) and from legal services for larger organizations (which assume institutional legal capacity). The specific function is triage protocol for AI-inferred content at small NGO scale — determining what to disclose, what to explain, and what pathway to use, with the resources an NGO actually has.

**Gap 5: Board AI governance staleness detection as discrete board reporting obligation**
No service offers systematic evaluation of whether the AI governance framework a board approved two years ago still describes the AI system currently operating. The evaluation would: compare the approved framework's specifications against the current AI tool version and configuration, identify where the approved framework diverges from operational reality, determine what the board's oversight position currently covers versus what it believed it covered at approval, and produce a staleness report proportionate to what a board needs to know to maintain genuine oversight. This is distinct from board AI training (which addresses general AI literacy) and from governance document production (which produces documentation the board reviews). The specific function is staleness detection — determining at what point a board's approval document stopped describing the system it was approving, and what the board's current oversight position actually is.

**Gap 6: Geneva humanitarian AI framework provenance map as accountability architecture document**
No published document maps the hierarchical relationships between Geneva humanitarian AI frameworks — UNAIWIG, ICRC operationalization handbook, OCHA AI guidance, and institutional donor AI conditions — showing which framework creates operative obligations, which provides aspirational guidance, which applies in which operational contexts, and which conflicts with which other framework in specific situations. NGOs following any single framework cannot determine whether following that framework satisfies obligations that framework's producer does not hold. The map is not a comparison of framework content — it is an accountability architecture document showing how the framework ecosystem functions as a system and where the organization sits within it.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**ZEWO AI governance expectation formation window as structural recertification positioning occasion:**
The period between expectation formation and published guidance is the window in which proportionate AI governance architecture can be built before the standard arrives. Organizations building during this window arrive at recertification with architecture they constructed, not architecture they are constructing under evaluation pressure. The distinction is structurally significant: a board that approves governance architecture before the standard is published is in a different position than one that approves architecture under the pressure of imminent published expectations. The window is not a compliance deadline — it is the moment at which the organization's governance architecture is determined by its own choice rather than external adaptation to arrived standards.

**Institutional donor AI governance conditions operating as undefined operative standards:**
Institutional donors are inserting AI governance conditions into grant agreements at scale. The conditions require NGOs to maintain AI governance standards without defining what those standards require in practice. Organizations sign agreements committing to requirements they cannot describe. When donor review arrives, the interpretation burden falls on the NGO — the donor's conditions are fulfilled by the NGO's interpretation, not by the donor's specification. No major Geneva institutional donor has published operative guidance defining what its own AI governance conditions require of grantees in practice.

**nFADP Art. 8 profiling notification obligation active for AI-assisted beneficiary categorization with no Geneva NGO discharge methodology:**
AI-assisted beneficiary categorization constitutes profiling under nFADP Art. 8. Beneficiaries whose data is processed through AI systems that categorize eligibility, vulnerability, or service level are entitled to notification of the profiling and the right to request human review of the AI-generated categorization. Geneva NGOs have not issued profiling notifications as a category. The obligation is present-tense, unnamed in NGO governance discourse, and structurally unmanaged. No Swiss authority has published guidance on how the profiling notification obligation applies to AI-assisted humanitarian program delivery at NGO scale.

**Art. 75 ZGB board liability for AI systems approved without ongoing review mechanism compounding continuously:**
Boards that approved AI deployment without establishing a review mechanism are accumulating Art. 75 ZGB personal liability continuously with each month of inaction. The liability compounds regardless of whether the AI system is producing adverse outcomes — it attaches to the absence of supervision. A board that approved AI two years ago and has not received AI governance reports since is compounding liability with each month of inaction. The accumulation is invisible because no mechanism forces boards to observe it until an incident surfaces the gap between approved governance and actual governance.

**FADP/FINICONA data processing registration obligations for AI vendor infrastructure relationships at NGO scale:**
NGOs processing beneficiary data through AI vendor infrastructure are conducting data processing activities that may require FINICONA data processing registration or FADP data protection representative appointment. The obligation exists for the organization's processing activities regardless of the vendor's location or whether the vendor processes the data as a data processor. Organizations that have not evaluated whether their AI vendor relationships create registration obligations are operating in potential non-compliance with a registration obligation that has not been assessed.

**Swiss-hosted AI vendor infrastructure addressing data residency without addressing processing authorization scope:**
Swiss-hosted AI vendor infrastructure satisfies one dimension of data residency compliance. Swiss hosting does not address: whether the vendor's sub-processor arrangements comply with the organization's nFADP obligations, whether the scope of the vendor's data processing rights under the organization's Art. 9 authorization covers the vendor's actual processing activities, or whether the AI vendor's model training practices affect the organization's data processing characterization. Organizations selecting Swiss-hosted vendors on data residency grounds may be assuming comprehensive compliance positions their actual vendor relationships do not support.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Mid-program AI model deprecation as governance decision without evaluation methodology creating forced migration under incomplete information at Geneva NGO scale**
When AI vendors deprecate model versions mid-program, Geneva NGOs face forced migration decisions with no methodology for evaluating whether the replacement model produces equivalent program outcomes for their specific beneficiary population. The vendor-imposed migration timeline (typically 60 days) is calibrated to vendor commercial considerations, not NGO governance needs. Organizations cannot evaluate outcome equivalence because no methodology exists for conducting that evaluation at NGO scale within vendor timelines — the evaluation would require defining what program outcome equivalence means for the specific context, identifying what data would detect non-equivalence, and executing a proportionate assessment before the vendor migration deadline arrives. The forced migration under incomplete information is distinct from AI tool selection (which addresses choice before deployment) and from post-incident review (which addresses evaluation after something goes wrong). This finding names the specific governance decision condition: deprecation events force migration decisions with governance implications the organization lacks the methodology to evaluate before the decision is made. Distinct from all prior Phase 04 findings addressing AI tool selection, vendor terms, and deployment approval — which address moments before the forced migration decision arrives, not the decision condition created by the deprecation itself.

**Finding 2: Donor AI governance questionnaire design quality as organizational self-discovery instrument with distorting effects on genuine governance adequacy independent of organizational practice quality**
Donor AI governance questionnaires function as measurement instruments whose design quality determines whether they produce accurate organizational portraits or misleading ones. Organizations with adequate governance may produce inadequate questionnaire responses because the questionnaire design has gaps that do not probe the actual governance adequacy; organizations with inadequate governance may produce adequate questionnaire responses because the questionnaire design does not probe the actual gaps. The questionnaire response becomes the organization's documented AI governance position — produced without organizational awareness that the instrument may be measuring something other than governance adequacy. The distortion is independent of organizational practice quality: an NGO with genuinely adequate AI governance may produce an inadequate questionnaire response because the questionnaire design fails, and an NGO with genuine governance gaps may produce an adequate response because the questionnaire did not detect them. No Geneva market service offers pre-submission questionnaire design quality review. This finding names the specific mechanism: questionnaire design quality is an independent variable determining whether the instrument produces an accurate or distorted organizational portrait, and organizations have no methodology for evaluating the instrument before submitting their response. Distinct from all prior Phase 04 findings addressing donor AI questionnaire response preparation, documentation adequacy, and compliance gap assessment — which assume the questionnaire is measuring governance adequacy and address how to respond to it, not whether the questionnaire itself is producing an accurate measurement.

Eligibility determinations stable under the old model become unstable under the new model without organizational awareness. The continuity gap is structurally invisible: no tracking architecture alerts the organization when model updates have altered conclusions that affected active beneficiaries. The gap surfaces only when a beneficiary challenges an outcome the organization cannot explain — by which point the gap has already affected program decisions. This finding names the specific propagation tracking gap: AI model updates create conclusion changes for active beneficiaries that the organization has no mechanism to detect, producing silent eligibility instability that accumulates until a challenge surfaces it. Distinct from all prior Phase 04 findings addressing AI tool version tracking (which addresses technical version state), post-program AI decision inheritance (which addresses decisions after program termination), and field override documentation (which addresses human decisions to override AI recommendations). The specific mechanism is eligibility conclusion propagation — not AI tool configuration, not override decisions, not program-close transitions, but the silent propagation of changed conclusions through active enrollment.

The absence is distinct from general data subject request processes (which address directly collected data not AI-inferred content) and from legal services for larger organizations (which assume institutional legal capacity and established data subject request procedures). This finding names the specific mechanism: AI-inferred content creates a triage question that directly collected data does not — what was inferred, what is the disclosure scope, what methodology exposure is acceptable — and no Geneva NGO has a protocol for answering that triage question at the scale and resource constraints the organization operates under. Distinct from all prior Phase 04 findings addressing nFADP data subject request response infrastructure (which assumed general data subject request processes without isolating AI-inferred content as a distinct category requiring its own triage architecture) and from nFADP Art. 6 lawful basis evaluation (which addresses upstream authorization not downstream response to AI-inferred content).
