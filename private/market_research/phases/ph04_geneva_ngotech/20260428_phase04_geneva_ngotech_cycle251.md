## 02 — Market Signals

Current problems, failure modes, paradoxes, and complaints heard in this ICP's own language.

### Signal 1: The 72-Hour Funder AI Questionnaire as Active Accountability Event With No Preparation Protocol

Geneva NGOs receiving funder AI due diligence questionnaires are treating them as administrative events — forwarded to whoever has time, completed from memory, submitted under deadline pressure — rather than as accountability representations that commit the organization to specific governance states. The 72-hour response window, typical in institutional donor practice, does not accommodate meaningful internal review. The responses are being submitted by staff without AI governance background, using vendor-supplied language as their own representation. The signal is not new — it was present in prior cycles. What is specific to this moment: institutional donors are now cross-referencing AI questionnaire responses against program reports and site visit findings, creating a verification risk that did not exist when questionnaire responses were filed without follow-up. Organizations that completed questionnaires in prior cycles are now discovering that their responses describe a governance state that the organization never actually achieved.

### Signal 2: Consent Scope Drift Through Tool Tier Migration

When Geneva NGOs migrate from a paid AI tool tier to a free tier — or from a compliant tool to a cheaper alternative — the tier migration triggers changes in data retention, processing scope, and sub-processor relationships that are not modeled at the point of the migration decision. The migration decision is made on cost grounds; the compliance implications are not evaluated because the capacity to evaluate them does not exist within the organization. The result: a tier migration that appears to be a software decision is simultaneously a data protection event with retroactive nFADP implications. The signal is distinct from prior cycles' vendor lock-in concern: it is about the governance architecture gap that tier migrations expose, not about the difficulty of exiting a tool.

### Signal 3: Board Partner Approval as Personal Liability Trigger Without Discharge Documentation

Geneva NGO boards approving new implementing partners that will process beneficiary data using AI are creating Art. 75 ZGB personal liability for board members through a process that does not produce the documentation required to discharge that liability. The board receives a recommendation from a program committee; the recommendation is based on organizational fit and program quality criteria; the AI processing dimension is mentioned as a checkbox item, not as the specific subject of board-level review. The board resolution does not identify which AI systems the partner uses, what data they will process, what the retention terms are, or what happens when the partner changes their AI configuration. The board members sign a resolution that creates personal liability for the governance of that partner's AI use without receiving the documentation that would allow them to evaluate or discharge it.

### Signal 4: Sub-Processor Chain Opacity as Structural Data Sovereignty Erosion

Geneva NGOs with vendor-processor agreements that include sub-processor notification provisions are discovering that the notification obligation in their agreements is narrower than the actual sub-processor chain their data traverses. Vendor terms of service commonly reserve the right to add sub-processors through the vendor's own supply chain without separate disclosure, or with disclosure mechanisms (email notifications to admin accounts, privacy policy revision notices) that NGO staff do not monitor. The NGO's records show a processor relationship with one entity; the actual data processing chain includes sub-processors the NGO has not evaluated, whose terms the NGO has not accepted, and whose data protection practices the NGO cannot independently verify. The erosion is structural: it occurs inside the vendor relationship and is not visible through the NGO's current monitoring capacity.

### Signal 5: The Multi-Budget-Line AI Cost as Budget Governance Failure

AI-related costs in Geneva NGOs are dispersed across software subscriptions (IT budget), program delivery (program budget), and compliance review (administration budget) without being consolidated, classified, or evaluated as a governance cost. The dispersion prevents the board and executive from seeing the actual resource commitment to AI governance and from making informed decisions about AI deployment against its full cost. When a board member asks what the organization is spending on AI, the answer is approximate — not because anyone is concealing it, but because the accounting architecture was designed before AI costs existed in their current form and has not been redesigned. The failure is not financial — it is governance: the organization cannot manage what it cannot see, and the cost visibility failure is also a governance visibility failure.

### Signal 6: AI Tool Notification Failure as Structural Compliance Problem

The specific compliance problem created when an AI vendor changes terms, updates sub-processors, or modifies data processing arrangements without triggering an effective NGO notification — and the NGO remains in compliance with its own records while being non-compliant with operational reality — is a structural problem that no Geneva NGO has a protocol for addressing. The compliance gap is invisible from inside the organization: the NGO's data flow mapping and processor agreements describe a state that was accurate at a prior date and is no longer accurate. The compliance gap does not become visible until a data subject exercise of rights, a donor audit, or a ZEWO review surfaces the mismatch. By that point, the reconstruction of what changed and when is already difficult.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

Gaps, and what no one is addressing specifically for this ICP and geography.

### Gap 1: Funder AI Questionnaire Preparation Protocol

No Geneva market participant has developed a preparation protocol that enables Geneva NGOs to respond to funder AI due diligence questionnaires from a position of documented governance readiness rather than reactive compilation. Available products serve organizations either after they have received and submitted questionnaires (questionnaire response support) or before they have received any questionnaires (general AI governance frameworks). No product serves the specific occasion of preparing the organization for incoming funder AI questionnaires — identifying what the organization currently has, what the questionnaire will likely probe, where the gaps are, and what can be addressed before the questionnaire arrives. The occasion is predictable: institutional donors are increasingly requiring AI questionnaires as part of grant renewal and new funding cycles. The gap is the preparation phase before the predictable triggering event.

### Gap 2: Tier Migration Governance Protocol

No protocol exists for Geneva NGOs to evaluate the compliance implications of AI tool tier migrations before the migration decision is made. The protocol would need to specify: what changes in a tier migration that are material to nFADP compliance, how to evaluate those changes against current processor agreements, what minimum response is required when a tier migration triggers processor relationship changes, and how to manage the compliance debt when the migration has already occurred and the evaluation has not. The market gap is not general vendor management guidance — it is specifically the tier migration governance occasion, which is predictable and regular (occurring every time a cost pressure or vendor pricing change triggers a migration decision) but unaddressed as a discrete governance entry point.

### Gap 3: Board AI Partner Approval Discharge Documentation

No Geneva market participant has developed a board briefing and resolution framework specifically for NGO boards approving implementing partners that will process beneficiary data using AI. The framework would need to specify: what documentation the board needs to receive before approving such a partner, what the board resolution must specifically contain to constitute a proper discharge of fiduciary duty under Art. 75 ZGB, what ongoing monitoring mechanism the board should require from the executive, and what the board member's personal liability exposure is for approving a partner whose AI governance the board has not evaluated. This is a specific product opportunity with immediate applicability: every Geneva NGO board that has approved or will approve an implementing partner with AI processing is making liability-creating decisions without this framework.

### Gap 4: Sub-Processor Chain Audit Protocol for Resource-Constrained NGOs

No protocol exists for Geneva NGOs to audit their AI vendor sub-processor chains within resource constraints that preclude external legal review or dedicated IT vendor management. The protocol would need to specify: how to identify which AI tools the organization uses that are subject to sub-processor chains, how to obtain sub-processor information from vendors within the terms already agreed, how to evaluate the data protection practices of sub-processors that have not been individually selected by the NGO, and what minimum action is required when the sub-processor chain cannot be fully mapped or evaluated. The solution must fit within part-time compliance function capacity and must not require technical legal expertise to implement.

### Gap 5: AI Cost Visibility Architecture for NGO Budget Governance

No market participant has developed an AI cost visibility architecture specifically for Geneva NGOs, where AI costs are dispersed across multiple budget lines and invisible to the board and executive as a consolidated governance cost. The architecture would need to specify: how to consolidate AI-related costs from multiple budget lines, how to classify those costs by governance function (tool cost, compliance cost, staff time, incident response contingency), and how to present AI governance cost in a way that enables board decision-making. This is distinct from general IT cost management: it addresses specifically the governance cost dimension that NGO boards need to see in order to make informed AI deployment decisions. The gap is actionable and immediate — every Geneva NGO board that asks about AI costs receives an approximate answer because the architecture for consolidating and presenting those costs does not exist.

### Gap 6: Vendor Notification Monitoring System for NGO Compliance Functions

No monitoring system exists for Geneva NGOs to detect effective vendor notification of processor relationship changes within the constraints of part-time compliance functions and NGO administrative capacity. The system would need to specify: how to configure vendor portal notifications to admin accounts that NGO staff actually monitor, what minimum monitoring protocol a compliance function can sustain operationally, and what the compliance response should be when a vendor change is detected — including how to manage the gap when the NGO's records describe a processor relationship that no longer matches the vendor's current arrangement. The monitoring function is structurally absent from NGO AI governance because it requires IT infrastructure and compliance process that NGOs do not have — but the compliance need is present tense and active.

## 04 — Compliance Context

Geneva/Swiss-specific obligations material to this ICP.

### ZEWO Certification and AI Accountability — Present-Tense Audit Exposure

ZEWO-certified organizations face a specific documentation challenge as ZEWO audit processes increasingly probe AI governance in certification reviews. The challenge is not that ZEWO has published a specific AI governance standard — ZEWO's framework remains principle-based — but that certification reviewers are forming questions from multiple sources: published international frameworks, funder AI condition language, and sector-wide AI governance practice evolution. The practical consequence: certified Geneva NGOs are being asked to demonstrate AI governance adequacy in ways that go beyond what any published ZEWO standard actually requires. Organizations that have completed funder AI questionnaires are being asked to show that their questionnaire responses are reflected in actual practice. Organizations whose boards have approved AI policies are being asked to show that the policies are operational, not archival. The compliance context is specific to Geneva: the certification audit is beginning to function as a de facto AI governance adequacy review through the accumulation of questions asked, before any formal standard has been published.

### Institutional Donor AI Conditions — Binding Contractual Obligations Across Non-Coordinated Frameworks

Geneva NGOs receiving institutional donor funding face AI governance conditions in grant agreements that arrive from multiple directions — SDC, SECO, cantonal development cooperation budgets, EU development funding streams — with no coordination between them. Each institutional donor has developed AI governance language independently, producing varying requirements across funders: some require named AI governance policies; others require evidence of data protection compliance for AI-assisted processing; others reference international frameworks that the Geneva NGO is expected to interpret without guidance. A Geneva NGO with multiple institutional donor relationships may face AI governance contractual obligations that are not compatible with each other — a tool that meets one funder's conditions may not meet another's, without any mechanism for reconciling the conflict. The compliance context is contractual: obligations are binding through grant agreements, remedies are financial and reputational, and the non-coordination across donors means the NGO bears the interpretation burden without support.

### nFADP — Active Compliance Gaps Across Multiple Articles

The nFADP creates multiple active compliance exposures for Geneva NGOs that are distinct from general data protection concerns:

**Art. 6 Lawful Basis for AI Processing:** AI tools applied to beneficiary data in program delivery require a documented lawful basis for processing. For tools adopted without evaluating the original data protection architecture, the lawful basis may not have been established at deployment — and may not have been re-evaluated when the tool changed tiers, changed terms, or added sub-processors. The compliance gap is active and compounding.

**Art. 8 Purpose Limitation:** AI tools are designed to extract maximum value from data. FADP purpose limitation requires that data collected for specific program purposes not be used for different purposes without separate authorization. In NGO program contexts where AI tools are adopted to solve operational problems, the purpose limitation constraint is rarely evaluated at the selection stage — producing a structural mismatch between tool design assumptions and FADP obligations that is active from the point of deployment.

**Art. 9 Third-Party Processing Authorization:** Implementing partners processing beneficiary data using AI create three-party processing relationships where the Geneva NGO may bear accountability under nFADP Art. 9 for the partner's AI processing — without having contractual authority over the partner's tool selection or configuration. The authorization chain is not being documented in Geneva NGO practice.

**Art. 21 Right to Explanation:** Beneficiaries receiving AI-assisted communications or decisions from Geneva NGOs have a right to explanation under nFADP Art. 21. No Geneva NGO has established an operational methodology for fulfilling Art. 21 obligations in program delivery contexts where AI contributes to beneficiary-facing communications. The obligation is present tense and unfulfilled across the sector.

**Art. 26 Data Disposal:** When program purposes end and data disposal obligations activate, the specific question of what to do with synthetically inferred profiles — derived classifications that go beyond source data — has no Geneva-specific guidance. For NGOs in protection, migration, and health contexts, inferred profile disposal carries secondary harm potential that source data disposal alone does not eliminate.

### Art. 75 ZGB — Personal Liability Without Discharge Mechanism

Board members at Geneva NGOs approving implementing partners with AI processing, AI tool tier migrations, or AI governance policy changes are creating personal liability under Art. 75 ZGB through approvals made without the specific documentation required for discharge. The problem is not that board members are careless — it is that the approval process does not produce the documentation that would constitute evidence of informed fiduciary oversight. The standard for what a board resolution must contain to discharge fiduciary duty in AI contexts is not published in Swiss authority, and no Geneva NGO board has received guidance on this specific question. The liability is active, unnamed, and growing as institutional donors increase AI governance scrutiny.

### Geneva Cantonal Data Protection Specificity

Geneva NGOs operating across cantons or with cross-border program delivery face the question of which cantonal authority's interpretation applies to specific AI processing activities. The Geneva Cantonal data protection authority (Commission cantonale de la protection des données) applies nFADP in cantonal context, but its published guidance does not specifically address the AI processing scenarios Geneva NGOs encounter. The cantonal interpretation layer adds a dimension to nFADP compliance that NGOs operating in multiple cantons must account for — a specific Geneva compliance consideration not addressed in general nFADP compliance frameworks.

## 06 — Findings Summary

Six net-new insights from this cycle, distinct from all C214–C250 Phase 04 findings.

### Finding 1: Funder AI Questionnaire Cross-Reference Verification Risk as Discrete Market Trigger

Geneva NGOs that completed funder AI questionnaires in prior funding cycles are now facing a specific verification risk that did not exist when questionnaire responses were filed without follow-up: institutional donors are increasingly cross-referencing AI questionnaire responses against program reports, site visit findings, and beneficiary feedback. The risk is not that NGOs lied in their questionnaire responses — it is that responses were completed from memory by staff without AI governance background, during 72-hour windows, using vendor-supplied language as organizational representation, and now describe a governance state that does not match actual practice. This finding is distinct from prior "donor AI questionnaire compliance theatre" findings (C250, C249) because those addressed the compliance theatre pattern — the gap between response and actual governance state — without naming the specific verification consequence that is now materializing: donors cross-referencing questionnaire responses against operational evidence. The timing dimension is new and specific to this cycle: the verification consequence is arriving now, as institutional donor AI literacy increases, for organizations that completed questionnaires 12–24 months ago under conditions that did not require accurate representations.

### Finding 2: Tier Migration as Discrete nFADP Compliance Event Without Pre-Migration Evaluation Protocol

The specific compliance problem created when Geneva NGOs migrate from a paid AI tool tier to a free tier — or switch tools for cost reasons — is structurally distinct from the vendor lock-in concern and from the general compliance documentation gap. The migration decision is made on cost grounds without modeling the compliance implications: data retention terms change, sub-processor relationships may change, and the processor agreement's terms may no longer apply to the migrated configuration. The compliance gap is active from the moment of migration and compounds silently until a data subject exercise of rights, a donor audit, or a ZEWO review surfaces the mismatch between the organization's records and its actual data processing state. This finding is distinct from prior "processor-of-record drift" findings (C248) because it addresses specifically the tier migration occasion as a discrete governance entry point — the moment when a cost decision becomes a compliance event — rather than the general processor relationship change that occurs through vendor acquisition or acquisition. The advisory entry point is different: pre-migration evaluation vs. post-acquisition remediation.

### Finding 3: Board Partner Approval Discharge Documentation as Unmet Documentation Format

Geneva NGO boards approving implementing partners that will process beneficiary data using AI are creating Art. 75 ZGB personal liability through board resolutions that do not contain the specific content required to discharge that liability. The documentation gap is distinct from the board AI policy approval gap (addressed in prior cycles): it concerns the specific documentation required when the board approves a third party's AI processing of the NGO's beneficiary data — not when the board approves the NGO's own AI deployment. The discharge documentation for a partner approval must specify: which partner, which AI systems, which data categories, which processing purposes, what oversight mechanism the board requires the executive to implement, and what the board member's personal exposure is for approving a partner whose AI governance the board has not independently evaluated. No Geneva market participant has developed a board briefing documentation format for this specific occasion. The gap is actionable and urgent: every implementing partner approval is a liability-creating event.

### Finding 4: Sub-Processor Chain Opacity as Structural Data Sovereignty Erosion Below Detection Threshold

The specific data sovereignty erosion that occurs when AI vendor terms allow sub-processor additions without effective NGO notification — and the NGO's records describe a processor relationship that no longer matches the actual data processing chain — is structurally distinct from the general vendor dependency concern and from prior "processor-of-record drift" findings. ### Finding 5: Multi-Budget-Line AI Cost Dispersion as Board Governance Failure
