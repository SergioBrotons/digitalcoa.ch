## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### English

**Executive director on the donor AI questionnaire they received:**
"We received a donor AI governance questionnaire. It was fourteen pages long. I spent a week trying to answer it. Some questions I could not answer because they assumed an IT infrastructure we do not have. Some questions I could not answer because I did not know what the AI tool was actually doing with the data. I submitted what I could. I am not sure what the donor will do with incomplete answers."

**On what happened when the AI tool changed:**
"We had configured our AI tool for a specific program. The vendor updated the model. The output changed. We did not notice for six weeks. During that time, we were making decisions based on outputs from a version we had not evaluated. When we discovered the change, we had to reconstruct what had happened. We could not."

**On the field staff and what they are doing:**
"Field staff are using AI tools in ways we did not approve. Some of them are using consumer AI tools on their personal devices for work purposes. The data flows are not governed. We do not have a way to see what they are doing. We found out when a problem arrived."

**On the grant close and what was left behind:**
"When the grant closed, we had AI-influenced decisions that had been made during the program. No framework addressed what those decisions meant after the funding ended. The obligations did not close when the grant closed. We are still managing them."

**On what the board knows:**
"The board approved our AI governance approach two years ago. The tools have changed since then. The board does not know what the tools are doing now. We have not told them. We do not have a clear way to tell them."

## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Donor AI governance questionnaires arriving with assumptions NGOs cannot meet**
Donor AI questionnaires are designed against organizational AI governance assumptions that do not match the capacity of small and mid-size NGOs. The questionnaire assumes infrastructure, documentation, and review processes that do not exist at the NGO scale.

**Signal 2: AI tool version updates changing output without organizational awareness**
AI model updates changing output quality and analytical framework without the NGO noticing. The version change creates a period of decision-making based on unevaluated AI output. No versioning protocol exists at NGO scale.

**Signal 3: Field staff AI usage outside approved channels**
Field staff using consumer AI tools for work purposes on personal devices. Data flows are ungoverned. The usage is invisible until a problem surfaces. No monitoring mechanism exists.

**Signal 4: Grant-close AI obligations persisting after funding ends**
AI-influenced decisions create obligations that outlast the grant relationship. No framework addresses what happens to AI accountability when the funding ends. The obligations persist without resources to manage them.

**Signal 5: Board-approved AI governance going stale without detection mechanism**
Board approved AI governance approaches that have since become stale as tools evolved. No mechanism exists for detecting when board-approved frameworks no longer reflect actual AI practice.

**Signal 6: nFADP data subject rights in displacement and conflict zone contexts**
No Swiss authority guidance exists for how nFADP data subject rights discharge in contexts where data subjects lack communications infrastructure, language capacity, or physical safety to exercise those rights.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Donor AI questionnaire response support for small NGOs**
No service exists for helping small NGOs respond to donor AI governance questionnaires in ways that are both accurate to actual practice and adequate for donor requirements.

**Gap 2: AI version tracking protocol for NGO program delivery**
No protocol exists for tracking AI model versions across active programs and detecting when output has changed due to vendor updates.

**Gap 3: Field AI usage governance for NGOs with limited IT capacity**
No governance framework exists for managing field staff AI usage in low-resource contexts where IT capacity is limited and the usage is largely invisible.

**Gap 4: Grant-close AI accountability framework for NGO obligations**
No framework addresses AI-influenced decision obligations persisting after grant close. The obligations persist without resources or clear ownership.

**Gap 5: Board AI governance staleness detection mechanism**
No mechanism exists for detecting when board-approved AI governance frameworks have become stale relative to actual AI tool usage and configuration.

**Gap 6: nFADP crisis-context discharge methodology for displacement operations**
No Swiss authority guidance or market service addresses how automated decision explanation and contest rights discharge in crisis contexts where data subjects cannot exercise those rights.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**ZEWO certification and AI governance:**
ZEWO standards do not yet include specific AI governance requirements. The certification framework is developing alongside NGO AI adoption. NGOs seeking ZEWO certification may face AI governance questions they cannot yet answer.

**Donor AI conditions as de facto standards:**
Institutional donors are imposing AI governance conditions that function as operational standards for NGOs. Non-harmonized donor conditions arrive simultaneously and require incompatible evidentiary formats. The cascade effect creates aggregate obligations no individual donor intended.

**nFADP Articles 6, 13, and 21:**
AI-inferred beneficiary profiles activate Article 6 lawful basis questions. Automated decision explanations required under Article 13 cannot be discharged in displacement contexts. Article 21 AI decision documentation obligations accumulate without methodology.

**FADP registration for AI-augmented data processing:**
NGOs processing beneficiary data through AI systems may have FADP registration obligations that have not been assessed. The registration obligation exists regardless of awareness.

**AI vendor contract indemnification asymmetry:**
NGO vendor contracts indemnifying the vendor for NGO use of the tool, with no reciprocal obligation on the vendor to indemnify the NGO for tool behavior. Downstream liability accepted without vendor obligation.

**Geneva humanitarian AI standards ecosystem:**
UNAIWG, ICRC, and OCHA frameworks developed in Geneva operate independently of Swiss regulatory review. Organizations following Geneva standards may be in unknown Swiss law compliance positions. No published mapping exists.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Donor AI questionnaire design assumptions exceeding small NGO capacity**
Donor AI governance questionnaires are designed against organizational AI governance assumptions that do not match the capacity of small and mid-size NGOs. Responding accurately reveals capacity gaps. Responding favorably requires claiming capacities that do not exist. No Geneva service has addressed this as a discrete response problem.

**Finding 2: AI version update propagation without organizational detection as program integrity failure**
AI model updates changing output quality and analytical framework without NGO awareness. The organization makes decisions on unevaluated output for the period between update and detection. No versioning protocol or detection mechanism exists at NGO scale.

**Finding 3: Field staff AI usage governance as structurally ungovernable shadow infrastructure**
Field staff using consumer AI tools on personal devices for work purposes creates shadow AI infrastructure that is invisible and structurally ungovernable with existing NGO governance capacity. The usage cannot be monitored and cannot be stopped without field staff compliance.

**Finding 4: Grant-close AI obligation orphaning as structural problem without resolution pathway**
AI-influenced decisions create obligations that outlast the grant relationship. The obligations persist without funding, without clear ownership, and without a framework that addresses what happens after the grant closes.

**Finding 5: Board-approved AI governance staleness as invisible architecture decay without detection trigger**
Board-approved frameworks becoming stale as tools evolve. No mechanism exists for detecting when approved frameworks no longer reflect actual practice. The staleness is invisible until a review is triggered by an external event.

**Finding 6: nFADP crisis-context discharge impossibility as discrete compliance gap in displacement zone operations**
No Swiss authority guidance or market service addresses how nFADP automated decision explanation and contest rights discharge in displacement and conflict zone contexts where data subjects lack the infrastructure, language capacity, or physical safety to exercise those rights. The gap is named and discrete.
