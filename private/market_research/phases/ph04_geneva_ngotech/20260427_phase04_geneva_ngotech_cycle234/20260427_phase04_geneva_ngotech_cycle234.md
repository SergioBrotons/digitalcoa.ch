## 01 — ICP Language

How this ICP actually describes AI problems.

### English

The executive director describes the problem as: *"We have AI tools running across the organisation and I can't tell you with certainty which decisions are being made by AI and which are not — or whether we can actually explain those decisions if a donor asks."*

The program manager frames it differently: *"We adopted AI tools because we had to — donors expect efficiency, staff expect help, and the alternatives were expensive. Now we have to figure out retrospectively what we committed to."*

The tension that surfaces most frequently: **"Our donor wants visibility into how we use AI. Our beneficiaries have a right to privacy under Swiss law. Those two things don't automatically resolve."**

Key language markers:
- "Accountability to our donors" / *Redevabilité envers nos bailleurs de fonds*
- "We didn't sign up for this level of complexity" / *Ce n'est pas ce à quoi nous nous étions engagés*
- "The tools changed — we didn't change our processes" / *Les outils ont changé — pas nos processus*
- "Who owns this decision?" / *Qui est responsable de cette décision ?*
- "Our HQ is in [city] — they made the AI decision, we live with it" / *Notre siège est à [ville] — c'est eux qui ont décidé pour l'IA, nous subissons*

What they do not say: "We need an AI governance framework." What they mean: "We need to survive the next donor audit without discovering we've been operating outside what we can actually defend."

## 02 — Market Signals

Current problems, failure modes, paradoxes, and recurring complaints.

### Signal 1: The Geneva Office Accountability Gap

Geneva-based offices of international NGOs are frequently the entity that hosts AI-assisted program delivery — but the entity that holds the legal relationship with the AI vendor, the entity that approved the AI use policy, and the entity that would face a regulatory inquiry are all different. No Geneva-specific guidance exists for this layered accountability problem. The executive director in Geneva is personally exposed under Swiss association law for decisions made by entities outside Switzerland.

### Signal 2: AI-Generated Evidence in Donor Reporting

NGOs producing donor reports that incorporate AI-generated beneficiary data, needs assessments, or programme outcome projections are discovering that donors — particularly institutional donors with their own compliance obligations — are beginning to request the underlying methodology. The NGO cannot always demonstrate what the AI actually processed or how it reached its conclusions. This is a qualitatively different problem from "donors want AI transparency." It is: "donors want defensible evidence, and AI has made the evidence-making process opaque."

### Signal 3: The Humanitarian Programme AI Evidence Standard Is Undefined

Geneva-based humanitarian organisations using AI for decisions in conflict zones — beneficiary targeting, resource allocation, risk assessment — face a documentation standard that does not exist in Swiss law, EU law, or any Geneva-originated humanitarian framework. The question of what an organisation must be able to demonstrate about an AI-assisted humanitarian decision, and to whom, has no published answer. This is not a gap in guidance. It is a gap in the entire concept of what adequate documentation means in this context.

### Signal 4: Shadow AI Under IT Constraints

Staff use AI tools on personal accounts or through unofficial channels not because governance is absent, but because the organisation's IT capacity cannot provide approved alternatives within a reasonable timeframe. The governance gap and the IT capacity gap are structurally linked — the solution to one cannot come from increasing governance burden without also resolving the IT constraint. Advisory approaches that ignore this sequencing produce documents that are technically correct and operationally irrelevant.

### Signal 5: The HQ Governance Transfer Problem

When an international NGO's headquarters imposes AI accountability requirements on its Geneva field office, the Geneva office is held accountable for compliance without having been given the authority to govern. This is not a communication failure. It is a structural feature of how many international NGOs are governed. The accountability requirement arrives with no accompanying authority transfer, no IT resource allocation, and no implementation timeline that accounts for the Geneva office's actual capacity.

### Signal 6: The AI Tool Succession Problem

NGOs that adopted AI tools during the 2022-2024 rapid adoption period are now confronting what happens when those tools change pricing, change terms, change model versions, or discontinue. The organisation has built programme processes — beneficiary communications, data analysis, reporting — around AI tool outputs, with no documented continuity plan for what happens when those tools change. This is distinct from the vendor wind-down problem. It is the chronic, small-scale version: tool updates that quietly reshape programme operations without any governance awareness.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

Gaps. What no one is addressing.

### Gap 1: Humanitarian AI Evidence Documentation

The humanitarian sector has produced extensive AI ethics frameworks. No one has produced a working documentation standard for what a Geneva-based humanitarian organisation must be able to demonstrate about an AI-assisted decision. The gap is not conceptual — it is operational and forensic. When an incident occurs, what must the organisation have on record? The answer does not exist as a usable operational standard. This gap is addressable by an advisor who understands both humanitarian operations and Swiss evidence standards.

### Gap 2: Geneva Office Authority Remediation

International NGOs with Geneva offices that are accountable without authority represent a specific, underserved market segment. The problem is not that the Geneva office needs a better AI policy. The problem is that the accountability framework imposed by headquarters is structurally impossible to satisfy with the authority the Geneva office actually holds. Advisors who understand Swiss association law governance structures, and who can translate that understanding into practical remediation steps for a Geneva office, are not visible in the market. This requires knowing how to escalate within an international organisation without disrupting donor relationships — a contextual skill, not a generic governance skill.

### Gap 3: IT-Constrained AI Governance

AI governance consulting universally assumes sufficient IT capacity to implement its recommendations. For the Geneva NGO segment, this assumption is systematically wrong. The gap is governance solutions that are designed from the outset to operate within part-time IT capacity, that can be maintained without dedicated technical staff, and that produce documentation that is legally adequate rather than technically optimal. This is not a compromise. It is a different design problem — one that no current market position is solving.

### Gap 4: AI Attribution in Donor Reporting

As donor AI scrutiny intensifies, NGOs need a methodology for documenting what portion of a report's conclusions derives from AI-processed data versus human professional judgment. This is a distinct skill from general AI governance documentation. It requires understanding how donors actually evaluate evidence, what audit rights donors actually exercise, and how to structure a methodology disclosure that protects the NGO without creating a liability admission. No Geneva advisory practice is visibly addressing this as a discrete service.

### Gap 5: FADP Art. 22 ADM in the Humanitarian Context

Article 22 of the Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection establishes rights for individuals subject to automated decision-making — including the right to obtain information, express their point of view, and challenge decisions. For humanitarian organisations operating in conflict zones or working with vulnerable populations, these rights exist in tension with operational necessity and security constraints. No Geneva guidance resolves this tension. Advisors who can navigate the intersection of FADP, humanitarian principles, and operational reality are not present in the market.

## 04 — Compliance Context

Geneva/Swiss-specific obligations.

### Swiss Regulatory Framework

**Federal Act on Data Protection (FADP / nFADP)**
The revised FADP entered force with a phased transition. NGOs deploying AI for beneficiary-facing decisions — targeting, prioritisation, resource allocation — are likely operating automated decision-making processes under Art. 22. Geneva offices of international organisations face a specific ambiguity: Swiss law applies to data processing occurring in Switzerland regardless of where the organisation's headquarters is located. The FDPIC has not published guidance specific to the humanitarian sector. NGOs that assume their international status resolves their FADP obligations are making an assumption that has not been tested.

**Swiss Association Law (Art. 60 ff. ZGB)**
Swiss associations are legal entities with governing bodies that have fiduciary duties. For Geneva-based NGOs, the question of whether board members have personal liability for AI decisions made under their governance — but outside their technical competence — is live and unresolved. The Swiss Association Law framework does not provide a safe harbour for "we didn't understand what we were approving."

**ZEWO Certification**
ZEWO certification imposes financial governance standards on Swiss NGOs that receive charitable donations. As ZEWO begins incorporating digital governance into its certification criteria, the question of what AI accountability obligations ZEWO-certified organisations must meet is becoming concrete. No published ZEWO standard for AI governance currently exists, but the direction of travel is clear.

### Institutional Donor Framework

**Institutional Donor AI Requirements**
Major institutional donors (Swiss Development Cooperation, EU funding mechanisms, multilateral development banks) are increasingly including AI governance provisions in grant agreements. These provisions typically require recipients to maintain AI use documentation, report on AI deployment, and in some cases submit to AI audits. The provisions are written at a level of generality that makes compliance requirements unclear. Geneva NGOs receiving institutional funding are being held to accountability standards that do not exist as operational specifications.

**Grant Agreement AI Audit Rights**
Grant agreements increasingly include clauses granting donors the right to audit AI systems and AI-related processes. These clauses are typically inserted without any corresponding evaluation of whether the NGO has the documentation infrastructure to respond to an audit. The practical exposure created by these clauses — what they actually require the NGO to produce — is not addressed by standard NGO compliance training.

### Geneva-Specific Considerations

**Geneva as Host State**
Geneva's role as host to major international organisations creates specific obligations under host state agreements. Swiss authorities' expectations for AI governance by Geneva-based international organisations are not publicly codified in a way that provides actionable guidance. The gap between what Geneva-based organisations believe is expected and what Swiss authorities actually require is wide and uncharted.

**Cantonal Jurisdiction**
AI-related matters touching on cantonal competencies — public order, education, health service delivery — fall under Geneva cantonal jurisdiction. For NGOs delivering services in these areas with AI assistance, the applicable governance standards may derive from cantonal regulations that have not been updated for AI deployment. The question of which cantonal authority has supervisory jurisdiction over AI-assisted service delivery is not clearly resolved.

## 06 — Findings Summary

Six net-new insights, distinct from C214–C233 Phase 04.

---

**Finding 1: The Geneva Office Accountability-Without-Authority Structure Is a Specific, Unaddressed Governance Failure Mode**

Geneva-based offices of international NGOs face a layered accountability problem that has not been identified as a discrete failure mode in previous cycles. The Geneva office is the entity that hosts AI-assisted programme delivery, that holds the beneficiary relationships, and that would face a Swiss regulatory inquiry — but the entity that controls AI procurement, approves AI policies, and holds the vendor relationship is headquartered elsewhere. No previous NGO/Tech Phase 04 cycle has mapped this specific accountability architecture. Advisors who understand how to remediate accountability structures inside international NGO governance hierarchies — without destabilising donor relationships or triggering institutional risk — are not visible in the Geneva market.

---

**Finding 2: AI-Generated Donor Report Evidence Is a Distinct, Emerging Liability Surface**

Previous cycles have addressed donor AI visibility requirements and FADP beneficiary rights. No cycle has identified the specific problem created when an NGO signs a donor report that incorporates AI-generated data — beneficiary analysis, needs assessments, programme outcome projections — that the NGO cannot independently verify. Institutional donors with their own compliance obligations are beginning to probe the methodology behind AI-derived conclusions in reports. The NGO's exposure is not "we used AI." It is "we signed a document whose evidentiary basis we cannot reconstruct." This is a distinct liability surface that requires a different documentation approach than general AI governance.

---

**Finding 3: The Humanitarian AI Evidence Standard Is a Genuine Conceptual Gap, Not a Guidance Gap**

Previous cycles have identified FADP Art. 22 ADM rights and their tension with programme operations. No cycle has observed that the humanitarian sector is operating in a documentation vacuum that is not merely a guidance gap but a conceptual gap: there is no working definition of what adequate documentation means for an AI-assisted humanitarian decision in Swiss law, EU law, or any Geneva-originated humanitarian framework. This creates a specific advisory opportunity for an advisor who can help an organisation establish its own evidence standard before an incident forces one upon it.

---

**Finding 4: Shadow AI Under IT Constraints Requires a Different Governance Design Logic, Not More Governance**

Previous cycles have identified limited IT capacity as a structural governance constraint and AI governance documentation as the primary documentation form. No cycle has addressed the specific failure mode that occurs when the IT constraint and the governance gap are structurally linked: staff use unofficial AI tools not because governance is absent, but because governance solutions require IT capacity that is not available. The implication is that improving AI governance without improving IT capacity does not close the governance gap — it closes it on paper. Genuine remediation requires governance solutions designed to operate without technical implementation capacity. This is a design problem that no current market position is solving.

---

**Finding 5: The HQ Governance Transfer Problem Produces Documents That Create Liability Without Creating Protection**

Previous cycles have addressed headquarters-imposed AI accountability without governance authority. No cycle has identified the specific liability mechanism this creates: when headquarters imposes AI compliance obligations on a Geneva office without transferring authority, the Geneva office produces documentation that acknowledges AI accountability on terms it cannot actually satisfy. This creates a paper trail that is, paradoxically, more damaging than silence — the organisation has documented its own obligations without documenting its capacity to meet them. The correct intervention is not better documentation; it is better sequencing of what the Geneva office commits to relative to what it can actually control.

---

**Finding 6: The AI Tool Succession Problem Is Operating Below the Vendor Wind-Down Radar**

Previous cycles have addressed AI vendor wind-down contingency as a mission continuity risk. No cycle has distinguished between the large-scale vendor discontinuation problem and the chronic, small-scale version: tool updates — model version changes, pricing modifications, terms revisions — that quietly reshape the AI outputs an NGO relies on, without any governance awareness or documented reassessment. The accumulation of undocumented tool updates creates a slow-moving AI environment drift that is structurally invisible because no single update triggers a review. The governance gap is not a discrete incident; it is an accumulation pattern. The remediation requires a reassessment trigger architecture, not a one-time AI inventory.
