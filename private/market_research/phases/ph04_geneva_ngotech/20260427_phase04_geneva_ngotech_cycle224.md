## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

1. **Donor AI representations as falsification risk, not documentation gap** — When NGOs sign donor AI representations without having the controls, the problem is not that documentation is missing. It is that the organization has made representations it cannot support. The accountability exposure shifts from a documentation problem to a potential misrepresentation problem.

2. **AI accountability without agency as structural NGO paradox** — Small NGOs are accountable for AI tool outcomes they cannot control. Vendor decisions — about data processing, model updates, term changes — are made by the vendor. The NGO absorbs the consequences. This accountability without agency is a structural feature of NGO AI use that no governance framework addresses.

3. **Donor transparency compliance creating program integrity failure** — Explicit AI disclosure in beneficiary consent forms causes participation rates to drop among vulnerable populations. NGOs stop disclosing. The compliance solution creates the program integrity failure it was designed to prevent. Neither obligation can be satisfied simultaneously.

4. **Implementing partner AI cascade as unowned accountability** — When implementing partners make AI tool decisions, the lead NGO absorbs accountability for those decisions when funders ask. The partner's AI choice becomes the lead NGO's governance exposure, with no contractual or oversight mechanism to manage it.

5. **Adoption-led governance debt as discrete failure mode** — NGOs adopt AI tools driven by staff convenience and program needs, without evaluating whether the adoption was warranted. The governance debt accumulates as a consequence of adoption pace, not as a governance design choice. This is a structurally distinct failure mode from the documentation gap.

6. **Dual accountability AI tension for Geneva international organizations** — International organizations in Geneva answer to both institutional governance structures and funder requirements. The two AI governance expectations are not aligned. Fulfilling funder conditions may require governance choices that conflict with institutional data sovereignty obligations.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### Gaps — What No One Is Addressing for Geneva NGO/Tech

- **AI misrepresentation risk from unsigned donor representations** — No one is addressing the specific exposure created when NGOs sign AI governance representations they cannot satisfy. The falsification dimension — making representations without supporting controls — is not being framed or remediated.

- **Accountability without agency as structural paradox** — The NGO AI paradox (accountable for outcomes, without control over causes) is not addressed in any Geneva governance framework. Solutions assume the organization can control what it is accountable for.

- **Consent form disclosure design for AI** — No one has designed an AI disclosure methodology for beneficiary consent forms that satisfies both donor transparency requirements and program integrity constraints. This is a genuine design gap.

- **Implementing partner AI governance cascade framework** — Lead NGO accountability for implementing partner AI decisions is not addressed as a discrete governance surface. No contractual or governance mechanism is being designed for this specific exposure.

- **Adoption-led governance debt measurement and remediation** — Organizations that adopted AI without governance do not have a pathway to close the gap. Existing frameworks assume governance preceded adoption.

- **Dual-loyalty AI governance for international organizations** — International organizations with dual accountability structures have no framework for resolving conflicts between funder AI conditions and institutional AI governance obligations.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Specific Obligations

1. **Donor AI governance representations and audit rights** — Institutional donors (SDC, EU DEVCO, UN agencies, private foundations) include AI representations in grant agreements with audit rights. Misrepresentation exposure is distinct from non-compliance.

2. **FADP Art. 21/22 automated decision rights for beneficiaries** — Individuals affected by AI-assisted decisions have rights to explanation, review, and human intervention. NGOs serving vulnerable populations are disproportionately affected.

3. **ZEWO AI reporting narrative expectations** — Swiss nonprofit oversight body (ZEWO) beginning to expect AI governance narrative in annual reports. The standard is developing without clear guidance.

4. **Swiss association law Art. 69ff. board fiduciary liability** — Individual board members carry personal exposure under Swiss association law for organizational AI decisions. The exposure is personal and not insured by standard NGO coverage.

5. **AI vendor data processing legal basis monitoring** — NGOs have no mechanism for tracking whether AI vendor data processing legal bases remain valid. Legal bases may have been invalidated without organizational awareness.

6. **Implementing partner AI accountability cascade** — Lead NGOs absorb downstream accountability from implementing partner AI decisions through funder contractual chains. No contractual AI governance mechanism exists for this cascade in most grant agreements.

## 06 — Findings Summary

### 6 Net-New Findings (Distinct from C214–C223 Phase 04)

1. **Donor AI representations create falsification exposure, not only documentation gaps** — When NGOs sign AI governance representations they cannot satisfy, the problem is not missing documentation. It is that the organization has made representations without supporting controls. The exposure shifts from a documentation compliance problem to a potential misrepresentation problem with legal and reputational consequences distinct from non-compliance.

2. **AI accountability without agency is a structural paradox no governance framework addresses** — Small NGOs are accountable for AI tool outcomes they cannot control. Vendor decisions — data processing, model updates, term changes — are made externally. The NGO absorbs consequences. Existing Geneva governance frameworks assume organizational control over what the organization is accountable for. This structural assumption is wrong for NGO-scale AI use.

3. **Donor transparency compliance and program integrity are in genuine conflict for vulnerable populations** — Explicit AI disclosure in beneficiary consent forms causes participation rates to drop among the populations NGOs serve. NGOs choose program integrity over compliance. Neither obligation can be satisfied simultaneously without a discrete design solution that no one has built for Geneva NGO contexts.

4. **Implementing partner AI cascade creates unowned accountability across the grant chain** — Lead NGOs absorb accountability for implementing partner AI decisions when funders audit. The partner's AI choice becomes the lead NGO's governance exposure, with no contractual mechanism in most grant agreements to manage this specific cascade. This is a structural gap in how AI accountability flows through the Geneva international development funding chain.

5. **Adoption-led governance debt is a discrete failure mode distinct from governance-led adoption** — When NGOs adopt AI tools driven by staff utility, governance debt accumulates as a consequence of adoption pace rather than as a governance design choice. This creates a structurally different remediation pathway than frameworks designed for organizations where governance preceded adoption. The remediation sequence is: measure adoption-led debt, then govern.

6. **Dual-loyalty AI governance tension is the specific structural problem for Geneva international organizations** — International organizations in Geneva answer to both institutional governance and funder AI conditions. When these expectations conflict — for example, when funder AI transparency requirements conflict with institutional data sovereignty obligations — the organization has no framework for resolution. This is distinct from the NGO accountability gap and requires a separate governance design.
