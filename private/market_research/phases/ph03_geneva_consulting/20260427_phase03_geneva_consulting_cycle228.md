## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Deliverable provenance opacity**
The boutique consultancy has accumulated AI-assisted deliverables across dozens of client engagements. The provenance of each deliverable — what AI contributed, what the professional contributed, under what version of which tool — is now opaque. This opacity is a quality control failure and a liability.

**Signal 2: Client methodology questions in competitive tendering**
Sophisticated clients are including AI methodology questions in tender documentation. The consultancy that cannot answer these questions credibly signals governance maturity below the threshold the client requires.

**Signal 3: AI tool proliferation without integration mapping**
Multiple AI tools have been adopted incrementally across the firm. No one has mapped how they interact or whether they create data flow complications. The cumulative environment is unmapped.

**Signal 4: Junior AI competency outpacing senior supervision capacity**
Juniors are more proficient with AI tools than the seniors who supervise them. The supervision standard cannot keep pace with the competence inversion. This is a structural quality control problem.

**Signal 5: Efficiency gain creating per-deliverable exposure**
The efficiency gain from AI-assisted production is real. It is also creating more exposed deliverables per unit time. Speed without quality architecture compounds exposure.

**Signal 6: AI methodology disclosure as a business development question**
The ability to credibly describe AI methodology to a prospective client is becoming a differentiator in competitive tendering. The consultancy that can articulate its methodology has a business development asset.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Deliverable provenance documentation service**
No one offers a service that helps the boutique consultancy document the provenance of its AI-assisted deliverables in a way that is operationally practical at boutique scale. The documentation systems designed for large organizations do not fit.

**Gap 2: Client methodology response template for consultancies**
A practical, credible response template that a small consultancy can use to answer AI methodology questions in tender processes does not exist. The gap between "we have a policy" and "here is our methodology" is unaddressed.

**Gap 3: AI tool interaction mapping for multi-tool environments**
A methodology for mapping how multiple AI tools interact within a boutique consultancy's environment — and identifying where the interactions create data flow complications — does not exist as a discrete service.

**Gap 4: Junior-senior competence inversion supervision framework**
A supervision framework designed for the reality that juniors are more AI-competent than the seniors reviewing their work does not exist. The existing supervision model assumes the supervisor is more capable than the supervised.

**Gap 5: Per-deliverable quality assurance for AI-assisted consulting work**
A quality assurance approach that is proportionate to boutique consultancy scale and practical to implement per deliverable does not exist. The large-firm QA approaches are not implementable.

**Gap 6: AI methodology as business development asset framework**
A framework for articulating AI methodology as a business development differentiator — rather than as a compliance burden — does not exist. The competitive upside of methodology clarity is unaddressed.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Swiss professional liability framework:**
Swiss consultancies operating under professional liability obligations face exposure when AI-assisted deliverables contain errors that a properly supervised professional would have caught. The supervision adequacy question is acute when the supervisor cannot evaluate the AI output.

**nFADP application to consultancy work product:**
Client work product processed through AI tools may constitute personal data processing under nFADP. The consultancy's obligations regarding client data that flows through AI systems have not been clearly mapped for the boutique context.

**FINMA-adjacent obligations for financial sector clients:**
Consultancies serving financial sector clients may have AI governance obligations that flow from their clients' regulatory requirements. A consultancy serving a Geneva wealth management firm may be part of that firm's AI governance accountability chain.

**Data protection obligations in multi-party AI tool environments:**
When multiple AI tools are in use simultaneously, each tool's data processing terms create obligations that the consultancy must track. The multi-party data environment is an unmanaged compliance surface.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Deliverable provenance opacity as structural quality control failure**
The consultancy cannot trace which deliverable had which AI input. This opacity means the quality control process cannot function as designed. The professional is reviewing something they cannot fully evaluate.

**Finding 2: Client methodology questions in competitive tendering as business development signal**
The question "what is your AI methodology?" in a tender is a proxy for governance maturity assessment. The consultancy that cannot answer credibly signals maturity below the threshold. The methodology question is a business development question in disguise.

**Finding 3: AI tool interaction mapping as prerequisite to governance**
Multiple AI tools adopted incrementally across the firm create an interaction environment that must be mapped before governance can be effective. The mapping is a prerequisite, not an optional exercise.

**Finding 4: Junior-senior competence inversion as supervision architecture failure**
The existing supervision model assumes the supervisor is more capable than the supervised. This assumption is false in AI-assisted consulting work. The architecture must be redesigned for the inverted competence reality.

**Finding 5: Efficiency gain compounding per-deliverable exposure**
AI-assisted production produces more exposed deliverables per unit time. The efficiency gain compounds the exposure per quarter. The firm is producing at speed while its quality architecture lags.

**Finding 6: AI methodology disclosure as competitive differentiator**
The ability to credibly describe AI methodology to a prospective client is becoming a differentiator in competitive tendering. The consultancy that has done this work has a business development asset that compounds over time.
