## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Internal AI use surface operating as ungoverned accountability domain distinct from client-facing AI governance**
Geneva consulting firms have built significant internal AI use patterns — analytical thinking, problem-framing, internal research, draft development before client delivery — that exist outside any client-facing governance framework. Internal AI use creates no direct client obligation but generates professional work product that enters the client delivery chain. No Geneva service addresses the internal AI governance surface as a discrete accountability domain with its own documentation and review requirements different from external AI governance disclosures.

**Signal 2: Billing model structural incompatibility with AI efficiency creating parallel exposure on two fronts**
The hourly billing model creates direct commercial contradiction when AI reduces production time: faster delivery reduces billable hours while client expectations about time investment may be calibrated to human-paced work. Simultaneously, if AI efficiency gains are not reflected in pricing, the firm absorbs cost improvements that should flow to clients, creating a different commercial exposure. Neither the client-expects-hours problem nor the pricing-absorption problem has been named as a structural billing model failure in the Geneva consulting market.

**Signal 3: Engagement letter obsolescence accelerating faster than review cycles**
Engagement letters governing active client relationships were written before current AI deployment levels. They do not reference AI tools, model versions, data handling protocols, or analytical framework version integrity. The gap between engagement letter content and actual AI-assisted delivery process is compounding faster than any firm is conducting formal reviews. The engagement letter is being superseded by actual practice without formal amendment.

**Signal 4: AI competency documentation as structural blind spot — what the MD cannot describe the MD cannot defend**
MDs at Geneva consulting firms cannot produce a coherent AI competency map for their own practice — what AI actually does in their process, where human judgment enters, where the tool operates independently, what would be lost if the AI were unavailable. The competency documentation gap is not a knowledge management problem. It is a professional liability problem: what cannot be documented cannot be defended in a carrier review, client question, or regulatory inquiry. No Geneva market service addresses AI competency documentation as a specific professional liability requirement for consulting firms.

**Signal 5: Internal AI governance vocabulary deficit creating organizational paralysis on documentation decisions**
Partner-level conversations about AI governance are being held without shared vocabulary, causing effective decision-making paralysis on basic documentation questions. Decisions that could be made with clarity become risk-avoidance defaults because no one wants to use the wrong term or commit to a formulation that might be incorrect. The vocabulary deficit is not about technical AI knowledge — it is about the absence of a professional-grade vocabulary for discussing AI accountability within the firm itself.

**Signal 6: Analytical framework continuity failure creating MD explanatory void at the accountability moment**
When former clients ask for explanations of AI-assisted recommendations from concluded engagements, MDs are discovering that the model has changed since the recommendation was produced, making the analytical basis unrecoverable through the current AI system. The MD's personal professional credibility is now dependent on a vendor-controlled system whose state they cannot verify. The explanatory void is not a communication failure — it is a structural condition where the MD cannot provide the explanation because the conditions that produced the original recommendation no longer exist in the current model state.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Art. 398 CO proper performance and internal AI surface accountability:**
The proper performance standard under Art. 398 CO requires that the consultant deliver the work product the client contracted for. When internal AI use shapes the analytical process before client delivery, the question of whether internal AI use is part of the delivery process — and whether it therefore falls under Art. 398 CO proper performance obligations — is unresolved. Internal AI use that influences client deliverables may be part of the professional performance obligation even when no AI tool appears in the engagement letter or client-facing documentation.

**Geneva consulting engagement letter adequacy for AI-assisted delivery relationships:**

The engagement letter adequacy standard is being superseded by AI-assisted delivery practice faster than formal review cycles operate. Active engagement letters governing client relationships in progress may not reflect the actual delivery process, creating a documentation gap with compliance consequences no Geneva authority has addressed specifically.

**Billing model professional obligation intersection with AI efficiency transparency:**
