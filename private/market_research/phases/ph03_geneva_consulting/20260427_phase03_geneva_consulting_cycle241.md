## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, and Complaints

**1. The Seventeen-Question Interrogation — AI Methodology Disclosure as Multi-Round Vendor Due Diligence**

The market signal: sophisticated clients — particularly those with EU AI Act deployer obligations or institutional governance requirements — are not asking single AI methodology questions. They are asking multi-round interrogation sequences in which the boutique's answer to each question becomes the basis for the next. The first answer is tested against technical follow-ups. The boutique that cannot sustain its position across a full question sequence is exposed not as uninformed but as practicing AI governance it does not actually have. The market consequence: the boutique is being evaluated against a vendor due diligence standard it did not know was the benchmark. The single-question response strategy — a prepared paragraph in a proposal — does not survive this level of scrutiny. The market signal is a shift from disclosure to substantiation: clients are not asking what you do, they are asking you to prove it.

**2. The Unreconstructable Reasoning Chain — AI Confidence Without Reconstructability**

The boutique is producing AI-assisted recommendations that are defensible in outcome but unexplainable in derivation. When clients — or carriers, or supervisory processes — ask the boutique to walk through the reasoning chain that produced a specific recommendation, the boutique cannot reproduce it. The AI generated the recommendation; the AI's derivation is not accessible to the professional who signed off on it. This is distinct from the quality control problem: the recommendation may be correct, but the boutique cannot demonstrate the professional judgment that produced it. The paradox: AI is enabling the boutique to make more defensible recommendations faster, while simultaneously making those recommendations less reconstructable — which is the opposite of what professional accountability requires. The market consequence: the boutique that cannot reconstruct its reasoning is accumulating an invisible liability that activates when a client or authority asks the question the boutique cannot answer.

**3. Knowledge Base Training Data Contamination — Three Years of Client Work as Undocumented Training Input**

The boutique's AI-assisted knowledge management systems have accumulated three years of client work product — proposals, analysis, deliverables, research — that is now embedded in the tool's operational context. The boutique does not know whether this accumulated work constitutes training data for model updates, whether it affects model outputs for subsequent clients, or whether it creates a client confidentiality breach when similar work is produced for different clients using shared context. The vendor's terms on training data use are often ambiguous — a non-committal answer to the direct question is not a clean no. The market consequence: this is not a future risk. It is a present-tense exposure from decisions already made, with no current resolution path, and no systematic methodology for assessing what the accumulated knowledge base means for confidentiality obligations.

**4. The Silent Model Update — Configuration Continuity Without Notification**

The AI tool updated itself without the boutique's knowledge. The recommendation made under the prior version — that the boutique documented, communicated to the client, and stands behind — is no longer reproducible under the new one. The model update changed the analytical conditions under which the recommendation was made. The boutique is now operating under changed conditions without having assessed whether the recommendation remains valid. This is distinct from the version documentation problem: it is not just that the boutique did not track the version; it is that the version changed the recommendation's evidential basis without anyone noticing. The market consequence: the boutique that does not have a model update notification protocol is accumulating silent recommendation exposure — recommendations made under conditions that no longer exist, documented as current positions.

**5. The AI Visible in the Output — Deliverable Stylometric Detection**

Clients are beginning to detect AI-generated content in boutique proposals and deliverables through stylistic analysis — stylometry — that identifies AI voice signatures independent of content quality. The boutique that believes it has successfully integrated AI-generated content into human-authored deliverables is discovering that sophisticated clients can identify the AI-generated sections. The market consequence: the boutique's assumption that AI content can be seamlessly embedded in human-authored deliverables is being tested, and in some cases disproven, by clients who have the analytical tools to detect the integration. This is not a content quality problem; it is a disclosure problem. The boutique that believed it was managing the visibility of its AI use is discovering that management is more difficult than assumed.

**6. The Managing Director Who Cannot Sign — Delegation Without Discharge Mechanism**

The managing director is personally asked to sign carrier renewal AI documentation that cannot be evaluated without AI governance expertise the managing director does not have and cannot practically acquire. The managing director cannot sign documents that represent AI governance diligence without either signing representations that may be inaccurate, or admitting to the carrier that the firm lacks the competence to assess its own AI governance. Neither option is acceptable. The paradox: the personal signature requirement that is designed to ensure accountability is creating a specific position in which the accountable person is structurally unable to discharge the accountability. The market consequence: managing directors are signing what they cannot evaluate, or avoiding the signature question by delaying or reducing AI governance documentation — both of which create different, worse exposures.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Specific Regulatory Obligations for Consultants

**4.1 OR Art. 398 Performance Standard and AI-Assisted Derivation — The Reconstructability Obligation**

Article 398 of the Swiss Code of Obligations requires that professional services be performed with competent care. For the AI-assisted boutique, competent performance now includes the ability to demonstrate that professional judgment — not just AI output — produced the recommendation. The boutique that cannot reconstruct the reasoning chain from input through AI processing to recommendation may be unable to demonstrate the competent performance that Art. 398 requires. The reconstructability obligation is emerging as a discrete dimension of the professional performance standard that Geneva consulting boutiques must now meet. The practical implication: the boutique needs a reasoning chain documentation architecture that allows it to demonstrate professional judgment participation in every AI-assisted recommendation, not just that the recommendation was made.

**4.2 nFADP Art. 6 Purpose Limitation and Knowledge Base Accumulation — The Training Data Question**

The nFADP purpose limitation principle requires that personal data processing remain within the scope authorized at the point of collection. When a Geneva consulting boutique's AI knowledge management system has accumulated client work product over multiple years, each client's data was collected for the specific purpose of serving that client. Accumulation in a shared AI system may constitute a purpose expansion that was not authorized by the data subject. The training data question — whether accumulated client work constitutes training data — is distinct from the purpose limitation question, but both are activated by the same accumulated knowledge base. The practical implication: the boutique needs a documented assessment of what the accumulated knowledge base means for purpose limitation obligations, with proportionate remediation where the original authorizations are insufficient for current system use.

**4.3 Carrier Renewal AI Representation — Personal Signature Under Structural Competence Constraint**

Swiss professional liability carriers are now requiring the managing director's personal signature on AI governance documentation at renewal. This representation must be made by a person who has the competence to evaluate the AI governance being represented. The managing director at a two-to-fifteen-person boutique is not an AI governance specialist, and cannot practically become one. The signature requirement creates an accountability for AI governance competence that the managing director cannot independently discharge without external qualified assistance. The practical implication: the managing director needs a documented basis for relying on qualified AI governance input when signing carrier renewal documentation — a discharge mechanism that is intellectually defensible and not merely a delegation of signature without substance.

**4.4 Client EU AI Act Deployer Obligations and Vendor Due Diligence Sequence — The Substantiation Standard**

When a boutique's clients have EU AI Act deployer obligations, those clients are required to conduct due diligence on their AI supply chain — including the AI tools used by consultants. The standard for this due diligence is substantiation: the client must be able to demonstrate that the AI tools used in their supply chain meet specific technical and governance criteria. The boutique that can supply only a prepared paragraph — and cannot sustain that paragraph across a multi-round technical substantiation sequence — may be causing the client to fail its own due diligence requirements. The practical implication: the boutique serving clients with EU AI Act obligations needs to understand what substantiation standard its clients must meet, and needs to be able to meet it.

**4.5 OR Art. 398 and the Silent Model Update — Recommendation Continuity Under Changed Conditions**

The competent performance standard under OR Art. 398 requires that recommendations be based on current analytical conditions. When a model update changes the analytical conditions under which an active client recommendation was made, the recommendation may no longer meet the current conditions requirement without anyone noticing. The boutique that made a recommendation under Version 3.4 of an AI tool, and now operates under Version 4.0, is making representations about a recommendation that was produced under conditions that no longer exist. The practical implication: the boutique needs a model update notification and continuity assessment protocol that triggers review of active recommendations when model versions change.

**4.6 Engagement Letter AI Warranty Clauses and the Detection Problem — Signed Before Known**

Engagement letters in the Geneva consulting market are increasingly containing AI-specific representations — warranties that no AI-generated content enters deliverables, disclosures of specific AI tools, commitments to client data not being used for model training. These clauses are being detected by boutiques after signature, when the engagement is already active and the representation has been made. The detection problem is distinct from the legal analysis problem: the boutique first must know the clause is present before it can analyze what it requires. The practical implication: the boutique needs a specific AI clause detection protocol that runs before engagement letter signature, not the standard contract review that does not know to look for AI-specific language.
