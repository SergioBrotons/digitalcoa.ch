## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Managing directors cannot attribute AI contribution in their own deliverables**
Managing directors reviewing AI-assisted deliverables cannot decompose what the AI contributed versus what the consultant contributed. The inability to attribute is creating a personal accountability problem for signing executives.

**Signal 2: Carrier questionnaires assuming a methodology that does not exist in documented form**
Carrier AI questionnaires assume a methodology that most Geneva consulting boutiques have not documented. The gap between what the questionnaire assumes and what exists is personal liability exposure.

**Signal 3: Client questions about cross-client AI data segregation arriving before any firm has a prepared answer**
Clients asking whether their strategic analysis was processed through the same AI infrastructure as other clients' work. No Geneva consulting firm has a prepared answer. The question is arriving before the answer exists.

**Signal 4: Proposal efficiency creating a billing paradox**
AI-assisted proposals are more efficient to produce. The efficiency creates a billing paradox: should AI-assisted proposals be billed at the same rate? The paradox is unsolved and is creating tension in client conversations.

**Signal 5: Sub-consultant AI delegation creating a three-party chain with no framework**
When consulting firms use sub-consultants who use AI systems, a three-party chain forms: client to firm to sub-consultant to sub-consultant's AI tool. No framework exists for managing the accountability in this chain.

**Signal 6: Professional competence visibility paradox**
The more effectively a consulting firm uses AI, the more visible AI use becomes. The paradox is that effective AI use is itself a signal of AI use that clients may evaluate as a negative.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: AI co-authorship ownership and liability framework for consulting**
No Geneva market service has produced an AI co-authorship ownership and liability framework specifically for consulting deliverables. The framework would need to address who is accountable for AI-assisted work product and what the professional liability implications are.

**Gap 2: Client AI questionnaire response protocol for consulting firms**
No protocol exists for responding to client AI questionnaires in the consulting context. The protocol would need to address what information to provide, what to withhold, and how to handle questions the firm cannot answer.

**Gap 3: Sub-consultant AI delegation accountability framework**
No framework exists for managing the three-party accountability chain when sub-consultants use AI systems. The framework would need to address what the originating firm is accountable for and what the sub-consultant's AI use means for client confidentiality.

**Gap 4: Proposal provenance reverse-engineering assessment**
No service exists for assessing how susceptible a consulting firm's proposals are to stylometric analysis. The assessment would need to determine whether AI contributions can be distinguished from firm contributions and what the implications are.

**Gap 5: Geneva consulting peer accountability mechanism**
No mechanism exists for Geneva consulting firms to hold each other accountable for AI governance. The absence is a structural market failure, not just a gap.

**Gap 6: Carrier attestation pre-signing review for consulting specific exposures**
No service offers pre-signing review of carrier AI attestations for consulting firms. The review would need to address consulting-specific exposures that the carrier's form does not anticipate.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Art. 398 CO warranty of proper performance:**
The Swiss Code of Obligations Article 398 establishes that amandatary must perform their mandate with the care of a orderly businessman. AI-assisted work product raises the question of what care standard applies to AI-assisted performance and whether the standard can be met when the MD cannot attribute the AI contribution.

**nFADP Articles 9, 21, and 8:**
Art. 9 requires a legal basis for processing sensitive personal data. Art. 21 requires documentation of AI-assisted decision logic. Art. 8 requires disclosure when profiling produces legal effects. Consulting firms processing client data through AI systems may be subject to all three obligations simultaneously without a coherent discharge methodology.

**Professional secrecy and AI data flows:**
Article 321 CP prohibits disclosure of client information to third parties. When consulting firms use AI systems, client information may be processed by the AI vendor. No Geneva service has published a compatibility analysis between professional secrecy obligations and vendor AI terms for consulting firms.

**Carrier AI attestation as personal liability instrument:**
The carrier AI attestation is a personal liability instrument signed by the MD. The attestation commits to an AI methodology that is not defined in most consulting firms' documentation. No Geneva service has produced a pre-signing review specifically for consulting firms.

**AI Convention obligations through existing client relationships:**
The AI Convention creates obligations through existing client relationships. Consulting firms with long-term client relationships may have AI Convention obligations that have already activated without the firm's awareness.

**EU AI Act downstream obligations for consulting firms:**
EU AI Act obligations may flow downstream to Swiss consulting firms through EU-connected client relationships. No mapping exists for which consulting services trigger Annex III obligations.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: AI co-authorship as Article 398 CO warranty of proper performance exposure**
The MD signing an AI-assisted deliverable is certifying that the work meets the Article 398 CO standard of care of an orderly businessman. When the MD cannot decompose what the AI contributed, the MD cannot confirm the standard has been met. The exposure is personal and active.

**Finding 2: Carrier attestation as personal liability instrument with undefined methodology standard**
The carrier attestation is a personal liability instrument that commits to an AI methodology. The methodology is not defined in the attestation form. No Geneva service has published what "AI methodology" means for a consulting firm. The undefined standard creates personal liability exposure for the signatory.

**Finding 3: Sub-consultant AI delegation three-party chain without accountability framework**
The three-party chain (client to firm to sub-consultant to sub-consultant's AI tool) has no governance framework. The originating firm may be accountable for AI-assisted work product it did not produce and cannot review. The chain is unnamed and unmanaged.

**Finding 4: Proposal provenance reverse-engineering as emerging competitive exposure**
Stylometric analysis can now distinguish AI-generated from human-generated text. Proposals that were AI-assisted may be identifiable as such. The competitive implications of stylometric detection are unnamed in the Geneva consulting market.

**Finding 5: Professional competence visibility paradox as AI adoption deterrent**
Effective AI use is a visible signal of AI use. The consulting firm that uses AI most effectively may be most exposed to client scrutiny of AI use. The paradox creates a disincentive for effective AI adoption.

**Finding 6: Geneva consulting peer accountability vacuum as structural market failure**
The absence of peer accountability mechanisms for AI governance among Geneva consulting firms is not a gap — it is a structural market failure. No informal or formal mechanism exists for firms to hold each other accountable. The failure means the market cannot self-correct.
