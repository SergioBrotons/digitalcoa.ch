### English

The managing director of a Geneva consulting boutique describes AI problems in the language of professional practice — not in the language of technology. What actually surfaces in conversation:

- "I can tell you exactly what went into the proposal we sent the client last week. I cannot tell you what the AI tool contributed and what I contributed. That is a problem when the client asks me how we produced it."
- "We have built something with our clients over fifteen years — a way of working, a methodology. Now that methodology is in a tool we did not design, and we do not fully understand how it uses what we gave it."
- "If I use AI to help structure a recommendation, and the client later makes a decision based on that recommendation and it goes wrong — what did I actually produce? Was it a recommendation from me, or was it a recommendation from the tool, attributed to me?"
- "My clients are beginning to ask me about AI. Not whether we use it — they assume we do. They want to know what that means for the work they are getting. I do not have a consistent answer for them."
- "The question I cannot answer is the one I have not been asked yet. The moment a client asks me directly how the deliverable was produced, I will have to decide in real time how much to say. That decision should not be made in real time."
- "Our competitive position is partly built on knowing this market — the patterns, the dynamics, the unspoken logic. If AI is absorbing that knowledge and returning it to clients in our name, I do not know what we are selling anymore."

The language is about professional authorship — who produced what, under what methodology, with what accountability — and about what happens to accumulated professional knowledge when it enters AI systems that the firm does not control.

## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Complaints, and Paradoxes

### 2.1 The Proposal-Deliverable Accountability Split — Where AI Risk Actually Lives

Geneva consulting boutiques have adopted a two-tier AI deployment model without naming it as such. AI is used freely for proposal production — research, structure, language refinement, competitive positioning — where it demonstrably improves win rates and reduces preparation time. For client deliverables, most boutiques maintain human control by default — either because the quality bar is higher or because the liability surface feels more acute. The problem: this split has created a documentation gap at precisely the wrong point. Proposals are generally non-binding representations of intended work; deliverables are the actual client-facing professional product. The boutique's AI usage is better documented — and better governed — at the proposal stage than at the deliverable stage. The liability exposure is concentrated in deliverables, where AI documentation is weakest. Market signal: boutiques are discovering this split when clients begin asking about methodology — the questions about proposals are answerable; the questions about deliverables are not.

### 2.2 The Methodology Contamination Problem — Accumulated Client Work Entering AI Systems Without Consent Architecture

The internal knowledge management use case — using AI to search, synthesise, and apply accumulated client work, sector expertise, and institutional methodology — has created a compounding structural problem that has not been addressed in boutique consulting governance frameworks. When a firm uses AI to query its own accumulated client work, the data enters the AI system's processing layer. For proprietary firm methodology developed over years of client engagements, this is not merely a data protection issue. It is an intellectual property erosion issue: the firm has built methodology by serving clients, and that methodology is now in tools the firm did not design, does not fully understand, and cannot fully retract. The firm is simultaneously the creator and the involuntary contributor to the AI system that is beginning to absorb its professional knowledge. Market signal: the firms that have recognised this problem are the ones whose clients have begun asking whether their confidential work appears in AI training data. No boutique has a clean answer.

### 2.3 The Competitive Reputation Contagion Risk — Geneva Peer Network as an Ungoverned AI Accountability Surface

Geneva's tight professional community — managing directors who know each other through industry associations, business school networks, and client overlaps — creates a reputation transmission mechanism that is structurally different from the regulatory accountability environment. An AI incident at one Geneva boutique does not stay at that boutique. The information moves through the professional community faster than any formal response can be deployed. The incident becomes a market signal: a cautionary example about AI governance failure that all peer firms must now address defensively. This peer-network accountability mechanism has no formal structure — it operates through informal conversation, referral patterns, and professional trust networks. The failure mode is not regulatory sanction; it is competitive repositioning: peer firms actively distancing themselves from the incident firm by emphasising their own AI governance credentials. Market signal: the Geneva peer network is functioning as an informal accountability mechanism, but no boutique is managing its position within it.

### 2.4 The AI Authorship Attribution Problem — Deliverable Production When the AI Cannot Be Separated from the Consultant

A structural problem has emerged as AI usage has deepened in client deliverable production: the question of whether a consultant can authentically claim authorship of a deliverable produced with significant AI contribution. For a law firm, the answer has professional licensing implications. For a consulting boutique, the question is more acute commercially: the client's engagement was with the consultant's judgment, methodology, and market knowledge — not with an AI system. When the AI contribution cannot be extracted from the deliverable, the consultant is attributing to themselves something they did not fully produce. The market signal: clients are not yet consistently asking this question, which is creating a window for boutiques that have not thought about it. When clients do begin asking — as they will — the boutiques without a documented answer will be in a difficult position.

### 2.5 The Client AI Expectation Calibration Gap — What Clients Assume vs. What Boutiques Have Decided

A market signal has been emerging from client conversations that managing directors are now reporting back: clients assume a level of AI involvement in their work that may be higher than what the boutique has consciously decided. The gap is not about whether AI is used — clients generally assume it is. The gap is about what that AI involvement means for the deliverable: whether the recommendation reflects the consultant's judgment or the AI's pattern-matching; whether the analysis was produced by a human with AI assistance or by an AI with human review; what the client should understand about AI's role to calibrate their reliance on the deliverable. Most boutiques have not made these decisions explicitly, which means they are defaulting into positions they have not evaluated. Market signal: the boutiques that have proactively calibrated client expectations — before being asked — have a significant relationship advantage over those waiting for the question.

### 2.6 The Firm-Level vs. Engagement-Level AI Governance Disconnect — Boutique Accountability Without Firm-Level Architecture

Geneva boutique consulting firms are making AI governance decisions at the engagement level — client by client, deliverable by deliverable — without a corresponding firm-level AI governance architecture. The engagement-level decisions are improvised: disclosure decisions made in real time, quality control protocols that vary by client and by consultant, AI usage that is not systematically tracked. The firm-level accountability — who is responsible for AI governance quality across all engagements, what the firm's methodology standard is, how AI decisions are reviewed — does not exist in most boutiques. The result is engagement-level AI governance that is inconsistent and unreviewable at the firm level. Market signal: this disconnect is becoming visible as client AI sophistication increases and counterparty AI governance expectations become more specific.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis: What Others Say, What Gaps Exist, What No One Is Addressing

### Current Competitive Landscape

Three established provider categories serve Geneva professional services firms on AI questions:

**Category 1: Management consultants** — positioning AI as a strategy and efficiency lever, offering frameworks designed for larger organisations. Pricing: CHF 150–250/hour for generalists, CHF 300–500/hour for strategy tier. Their frame is competitive advantage and operational improvement. They do not address the professional authorship question, the methodology contamination problem, or the firm-level governance architecture gap at the boutique scale.

Pricing: CHF 250–400/hour, or fixed-fee reviews. Their frame is regulatory compliance and data controller obligations. They do not address the professional practice dimensions — professional authorship, client expectation calibration, competitive reputation management, or the engagement-level vs. firm-level governance disconnect.

**Category 3: Technology vendors** — selling AI tool subscriptions, SaaS platforms, and implementation support with commercial incentives aligned to deployment, not governance constraint. Their frame is tool capability and workflow integration. They do not address accountability architecture, methodology contamination, or professional liability positioning.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Specific Regulatory and Professional Obligations

### 4.1 FINSO/SRO Supervisory Framework — AI Governance for Financial Advisory and Consulting Firms

Geneva consulting boutiques operating in the financial advisory space — wealth management advisory, family office services, investment consulting — are subject to FINSO oversight and the SRO framework under FinIA. The SRO-Guidelines on Digitalisation and AI advisory specify obligations that are beginning to be applied at the boutique scale. AI tool usage in client advisory mandates falls within the scope of SRO supervisory expectations on methodology documentation and client communication. The supervisory framework does not yet specify AI governance requirements in detail, but the direction of travel is toward explicit methodology documentation standards for AI-assisted client recommendations.

### 4.2 SVA Professional Body Membership — AI Competence as Emerging Membership Condition

The Swiss Association of Management Consultants has introduced AI competence guidance in its professional development framework. For Geneva consulting boutiques whose managing directors hold SVA membership, the AI competence signals carry professional membership implications analogous to continuing education requirements. The direction of travel: AI governance competence is becoming a condition of professional membership standing, not merely an optional enhancement. Firms should monitor SVA guidance for explicit AI practice standards and treat current signals as leading indicators of what membership obligations will require.

### 4.3 nFADP Purpose Limitation — AI Query of Accumulated Client Work as Data Processing

The revised Federal Act on Data Protection requires that processing of personal data meet purpose limitation standards and that data subjects receive adequate information about processing. When a Geneva consulting boutique uses AI to query its accumulated client work — which may include personal data of client employees, counterparties, or other identified individuals — this constitutes data processing under nFADP. The purpose limitation question is acute: the original client engagement purpose (specific professional advice) is different from the knowledge management purpose (methodology development and internal reference). The data may be processed for the latter purpose without a documented lawful basis. The practical implication: boutiques using AI on accumulated client work should conduct a purpose limitation assessment, distinguishing client work collected under a broad advisory mandate from client work collected under a specific engagement scope.

### 4.4 Professional Secrecy and AI Tool Data Flows — What Enters the Tool and What Returns

Swiss professional secrecy obligations under OR Art. 321a and professional body regulations require that client information be kept confidential. When a consulting boutique uses AI tools in client engagements — or to query client work product — the question of what data enters the tool and under what conditions is a professional secrecy question, not only a data protection question. The professional secrecy obligation does not have a general AI exception. The boutique's AI vendor agreements may not adequately protect professional secrecy obligations. The practical implication: boutiques should verify that their AI tool agreements include commercial confidentiality protections sufficient to support professional secrecy obligations — and should document this verification.

### 4.5 Client AI Governance Audits — Emerging as Tender and RFP Requirements

Geneva corporate clients and institutional buyers are beginning to include AI governance assessments in their tender and RFP processes for consulting services. The requests are not yet uniform, but the direction is clear: clients want to understand the consulting firm's AI methodology, tool inventory, quality control processes, and incident response capabilities before awarding mandates. There is no Geneva-specific standard for what a consulting firm's AI governance disclosure should cover in a client tender process. Firms that have developed a documented AI governance position will be better placed to respond to these requirements than firms improvising in the tender process.

### 4.6 ISO 42001 AI Management System Certification — Market Positioning and Client Confidence

ISO 42001 (Artificial Intelligence — Management System) certification provides a structured framework for AI governance that is beginning to appear in Geneva professional services client requirements. For consulting boutiques, ISO 42001 certification serves a dual function: it provides an internal AI governance architecture and it signals AI governance quality to clients and counterparties. Boutique consulting firms with ISO 42001 certification have a documented answer to client AI governance audit requests — and the certification is increasingly being requested by institutional clients in tender documentation. The investment in certification is recoverable through client confidence and competitive differentiation.

The risk is compounded by the methodology contamination problem: the consultant's methodology itself may be partially AI-absorbed. No advisory provider has designed an authorship attribution framework specifically for consulting boutiques — covering how to make AI authorship decisions, how to document them, and how to communicate them to clients.

**Product:** Professional Authorship Attribution Framework for AI-Assisted Consulting Deliverables — a structured engagement producing: (a) an authorship attribution policy for the firm covering different AI contribution levels, (b) a documentation methodology that allows the firm to describe AI involvement in a way that is honest and professionally defensible, (c) a client communication format for authorship disclosure that preserves client confidence while being accurate. Fee: CHF 1,600–2,200. Distinct from C216's Engagement Letter AI Clause Development — which addressed contractual disclosure, not the professional authorship attribution question at the level of the individual deliverable.

### Finding 5: The Firm-Level vs. Engagement-Level AI Governance Disconnect — Boutique Accountability Architecture Without a Firm-Level Owner

**Insight:** Geneva boutique consulting firms are making AI governance decisions engagement by engagement, without a corresponding firm-level AI governance architecture. The result is AI governance that is inconsistent across engagements, unreviewable at the firm level, and dependent on the AI awareness of whoever is managing each individual mandate. There is no person in the boutique who owns AI governance quality across all engagements, no methodology standard against which AI decisions are reviewed, and no mechanism for the managing director to have confidence that AI usage across the firm is consistent with the firm's accountability position. This is a boutique governance design gap — distinct from the engagement-level AI governance questions that boutiques have been improvising without a firm-level framework to govern them.

**Product:** Firm-Level AI Governance Architecture Design for Consulting Boutiques — a structured engagement producing: (a) a firm-level AI governance ownership assignment — who is accountable for AI governance quality across all mandates, (b) a methodology standard for AI involvement in client deliverables that can be applied consistently across engagements, (c) a periodic AI governance review mechanism that gives the managing director a verifiably accurate picture of AI usage across the firm. Fee: CHF 2,200–3,000. Distinct from C226's AI Competency Architecture Gap — which addressed individual consultant competency, not firm-level governance ownership and methodology standard design.

### Finding 6: The Client AI Expectation Calibration Gap — Boutique-Client AI Assumption Divergence Operating Ahead of Client Questions

**Insight:** Clients of Geneva consulting boutiques are operating with assumptions about AI involvement in their work that are more advanced than what the boutique has consciously decided. Most boutiques are managing client AI expectations engagement by engagement, without a firm-level framework. The firms that have proactively calibrated client expectations — before being asked — have a significant relationship advantage and a documented position. The firms that have not are at risk of the first client question exposing a position they did not know they were holding. No advisory provider has designed a client AI expectation calibration service specifically for consulting boutiques — covering how to establish a firm-level position on AI involvement disclosure and how to communicate it in a way that is relationship-preserving and commercially sound.

**Product:** Client AI Expectation Calibration Framework for Consulting Boutiques — a structured engagement producing: (a) an assessment of the gap between current client assumptions about AI involvement and the boutique's actual AI decisions, (b) a firm-level AI disclosure position that can be communicated consistently across client engagements, (c) a client communication format — for proposals, engagement terms, and ongoing client communication — that is honest, professional, and calibrated to client relationship stage. Fee: CHF 1,600–2,200. Distinct from C223's Counterparty AI Literacy Divergence — which addressed counterparty AI sophistication as a competitive variable, not the boutique's own client expectation calibration problem as a professional practice issue.
