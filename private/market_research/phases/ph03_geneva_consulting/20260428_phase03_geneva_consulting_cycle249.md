## 01 — ICP Language: How This ICP Describes AI Problems and Concerns

### English — Natural Language as Observed in Market

The managing director at a Geneva boutique consulting firm (2–15 employees) does not describe their situation in terms of "AI governance" or "compliance obligations." They describe it in specific moments — things that have happened, things their clients have asked, things they cannot explain in the room.

**On deliverable authorship when client's AI has been in the room:**
"We ran a three-day strategic workshop. The client's team used their own AI tool throughout. We used ours. The final recommendation document draws from both. When the client asks us to stand behind that document, I'm not sure what I'm actually standing behind — what was our contribution versus theirs versus the AI's."

**On what happens when client asks to see your AI tool:**
"More sophisticated clients are starting to ask: can we see how your AI works? What data does it use? Where does it run? I've had this conversation twice this quarter. I can't answer any of those questions with precision. I don't own that system."

**On the attestation language on the PI renewal:**
"The carrier form asks whether we have an AI review process. Yes or no. I've been checking yes. What I'm actually confirming when I sign that — I don't know. And I don't know if anyone at the carrier does either."

**On what the proposal version history reveals:**
"We went through nine proposal iterations for that mandate. The AI tool logged all of them. If the client ever reconstructed which directions we considered and dropped, and asked why — I don't have a clean account of what changed between versions and why. The AI knows. I don't."

**On the sub-consultant AI contribution no one mapped:**
"We brought in a specialist for part of that engagement. She used her own AI tool. The final deliverable has her contribution embedded in it. Our firm signed it. I've been thinking about what that means for our liability when we can't explain the specialist's AI methodology."

**On client AI expectation migration:**
"One of our clients hired a head of AI six months ago. The conversations we have with them now are completely different. They ask questions about our AI tool that I've only started to hear from more sophisticated clients — data residency, version control, what happens to their information when we close an engagement. They're ahead of most consulting firms on this."

**On the tool continuity nobody planned for:**
"We built our proposal process around one AI tool. We've been using it for two years. We don't have a contingency for what happens if that vendor changes pricing, has a prolonged outage, or is acquired. We also can't afford to run parallel systems. If the tool changes, we change. That's not a plan — I know that."

**On talking to peers:**
"The people I talk to casually — other managing directors at other boutiques — are dealing with exactly the same things. We compare notes in general terms. Nobody is going to say anything specific about their AI setup. The informal conversation exists, but it's not resolving anything."

## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

### Primary Market Signals

**Signal 1: Multi-party AI authorship in concurrent workshops creates undocumentable deliverable state.**
When boutique consultants run collaborative sessions with client teams using parallel AI tools (the client's tool and the firm's tool operating simultaneously), the final recommendation document cannot be attributed to a single source. The boutique signs the deliverable. The client contributed content via their own AI tool. The firm's AI shaped the structure and synthesis. No Geneva engagement letter or deliverable protocol addresses this configuration. The failure mode: when a client question about the recommendation's provenance arises, the boutique cannot explain the document's construction with precision. This is not a disclosure failure — it is an authorship reconstruction failure.

**Signal 2: Client AI sophistication is outpacing consulting firm AI transparency preparation.**
Clients who have hired AI leadership are beginning to ask the questions that consulting firms cannot yet answer: Where does our data go? What version of the model was used? What happens to our information at engagement close? How is our data separated from your training data? These questions are arriving from the most sophisticated clients first, then diffusing downward through the market. The boutique has no prepared response framework and is improvising in real time, often in client-facing contexts where uncertainty is professionally costly.

**Signal 3: PI renewal attestation is creating undocumented personal liability through unchecked boxes.**
The carrier AI questionnaire language during PI renewal is structurally miscalibrated for boutique scale. The managing director confirms AI governance adequacy with no reference standard, no methodology for assessment, and no Geneva professional community consensus on what "adequate" means at boutique consulting scale. The box is checked. The signature is personal. The exposure is unquantified.

**Signal 4: Proposal version archaeology creates retrospective accountability gaps.**
AI tools with full conversation and document version history allow clients (or their advisors) to reconstruct the evolution of recommendations — which directions were considered and abandoned, which framings were tested and revised. For a boutique that changed its recommendation mid-process (a normal consulting behavior), the version record now makes that evolution visible in a way it never was before. The client can ask why the firm moved from framing A to framing B. The boutique must answer for a decision process it may not have documented at the time.

**Signal 5: Sub-consultant AI delegation creates a three-party confidentiality chain the boutique cannot oversee.**
Specialist sub-consultants engaged on boutique engagements increasingly operate their own AI tools. Client information enters the sub-consultant's AI system under different vendor terms, different data residency, and different retention policies than the boutique's own tool. The boutique has contractual confidentiality obligations to the client that now extend implicitly to the sub-consultant's AI infrastructure — an infrastructure the boutique has not evaluated and cannot govern.

**Signal 6: AI tool vendor dependency without contingency is invisible business continuity exposure.**
A two-year accumulated dependency on a single AI tool — with process, templates, and proposal workflows built around that tool's specific interface and capabilities — creates a business continuity exposure that no Geneva consulting firm has formally named or planned for. Pricing changes, service interruptions, vendor acquisition, or model discontinuation would require immediate and costly process reconstruction. The exposure is real. The contingency planning is absent.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis: Gaps, What No One Is Addressing

### Identified Market Gaps

**Gap 1: No Geneva consulting-specific AI governance methodology designed for boutique scale.**
Existing AI governance frameworks are either enterprise-grade (designed for organizations with dedicated compliance functions) or generic (applicable across industries without sector-specific calibration). The Geneva boutique consulting firm — 2 to 15 employees, managing director as both commercial and operational lead, client work product as primary liability surface — has no governance methodology designed for its specific architecture. No Geneva advisory practice has published a boutique-scale consulting AI governance framework.

**Gap 2: No Geneva consulting peer accountability circuit (structural absence vs. reluctance).**
Legal professionals have the Geneva Bar (ORDRE des Avocats Genevois). Finance professionals have the SVA and SFAMA. Wealth management has AMAS. Geneva consulting professionals have no equivalent sector-specific governance community, guidance body, or informal accountability circuit. The gap is structural — the institution does not exist — not a gap of willingness or culture. This creates an information vacuum where AI methodology choices are made in isolation, without peer review or standard-formation feedback.

**Gap 3: No engagement letter standard addressing multi-party AI authorship.**
No standard Geneva consulting engagement letter template addresses the scenario where client AI tools and firm AI tools have operated in parallel during an engagement, contributing to the final deliverable. The question of authorship, accountability, and warranty is unaddressed in current professional templates. This is not a gap in existing documents — it is an absence of the document category entirely.

**Gap 4: No Geneva-specific AI vendor continuity advisory for professional services.**
No Geneva advisory practice offers AI vendor continuity planning as a professional services product. The business continuity dimension of AI tool dependency — operational, not technical — is treated as an IT question rather than a professional liability question. The advisory gap is distinct: the professional liability framing (what happens to client-facing deliverables and client confidentiality when your AI tool changes) has not been separated from the IT infrastructure framing.

**Gap 5: No client-facing AI transparency communication template for consulting firms.**
Sophisticated clients are beginning to ask detailed AI tool questions. No Geneva consulting firm has a prepared response template that communicates the firm's AI tool configuration, data handling, version control, and service level terms in a client-appropriate format. The response is improvised, inconsistent, and potentially creates additional liability through what it implies or omits.

**Gap 6: No sub-consultant AI delegation framework in professional services context.**
The three-party AI delegation chain (boutique as principal, sub-consultant as agent with own AI tool, client as beneficiary) has no Geneva professional services framework. The boutique absorbs accountability for sub-consultant AI output under the firm's engagement letter, while having no governance access to the sub-consultant's AI tool configuration, vendor terms, or data handling. This is not a contractual gap — it is a governance architecture gap that contracts cannot resolve.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Specific Regulatory Obligations for Consultants

### Geneva Professional Obligations Relevant to AI Deployment

**Swiss Code of Obligations, Art. 398 — Mandate (Beauftragter):**
The mandate provisions of the Swiss Code of Obligations impose a duty of diligent performance on commissioned agents. For Geneva consulting firms deploying AI, the question of whether AI-assisted recommendations meet the Art. 398 diligence standard is becoming operationally relevant. The standard requires the agent to act in the best interest of the principal, with the care and diligence expected of a professional in the field. When AI produces recommendations the consultant cannot fully explain, the discharge of the Art. 398 duty becomes difficult to demonstrate.

**nFADP Art. 8 — Lawfulness of Processing:**
Consulting firms processing client information in AI systems must establish a lawful basis for that processing. For Geneva consulting firms, the applicable lawful basis is typically contract performance (Art. 8(1)(a)) or legitimate interest (Art. 8(1)(c)). The critical compliance question is whether AI tool vendor terms (data processing, training data usage, data residency) are compatible with the firm's own nFADP lawful basis documentation. The consulting firm's liability under nFADP is not discharged by the vendor's compliance claims — the firm remains the data controller with primary responsibility.

**nFADP Art. 9 — Third-Party Processing Authorization:**
When client information is processed by a sub-consultant's AI tool (a third party's AI system, under the boutique's engagement), the boutique must have explicit authorization from the client for third-party processing of their information. Standard engagement letters in Geneva consulting practice do not currently contain AI third-party processing authorization clauses. This is a present-tense exposure — active client engagements where client information has entered sub-consultant AI systems without explicit authorization.

**nFADP Art. 21 — Rights of Data Subjects:**
Clients of Geneva consulting firms have Art. 21 rights regarding AI-processed information about them — access rights, correction rights, and the right to know the logic involved in automated processing. When the consulting firm's AI tool generates recommendations or analysis about a client, the client has the right to request an explanation of that processing. The boutique must be able to provide that explanation. The technical architecture to fulfill Art. 21 requests for AI-processed client information does not currently exist in most boutique consulting AI deployments.

**Professional Secrecy under Geneva Cantonal Law:**
Geneva attorneys are subject to Art. 321 CP (professional secrecy), which imposes strict confidentiality obligations on information acquired in professional practice. For consulting firms that serve clients in legally privileged or confidential contexts — strategic advice to executives, organizational consulting in regulated industries — the question of what client information enters AI systems and under what terms is a professional secrecy compliance question. No Geneva cantonal guidance exists for consulting firms on the intersection of AI tool deployment and professional secrecy obligations.

**Carrier AI Questionnaire as De Facto Standard:**
The PI insurance carrier AI questionnaire is functioning as the de facto operative standard for AI governance adequacy for Geneva consulting firms. The third-generation questionnaire (2026 edition) probes: tool version tracking, reassessment triggers, client communication records regarding AI, and AI output review evidence. Responding to this questionnaire with "yes" requires a documentation infrastructure the boutique must build. The carrier is not providing a methodology for building it — only probing for its existence.
