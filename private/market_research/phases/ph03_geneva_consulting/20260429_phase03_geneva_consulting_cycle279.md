"If a client challenges our proposal and we used AI tools to produce it, I do not know how to explain what the AI contributed. I can defend what I wrote. I cannot defend what the AI did."

## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Client-side AI analysis of consulting proposals arriving before firms have response frameworks**
Clients running consulting proposals through their own AI tools and returning marked-up versions. The consulting firm has no response framework for either the substance of the challenge or the question of what the client's AI found.

**Signal 2: Sub-consultant AI data flow creating three-party accountability void**
The consulting firm in the middle of client and sub-consultant has no framework for managing the AI data flow through the sub-consultant. No party has clear accountability for what happens to client data in the sub-consultant's AI tools.

**Signal 3: AI-assisted billing paradox remaining unresolved**
The billing paradox (faster work, better work, different price?) is not being resolved in client conversations. Firms are avoiding the conversation. The avoidance itself is creating risk.

**Signal 4: Carrier attestation signing without methodology definition**
Signing carrier attestations without a defined methodology is creating personal liability exposure. The attestation commits to something the signatory cannot describe.

**Signal 5: AI contribution opacity creating challenge-response asymmetry**
When a consulting proposal is challenged, the firm can defend what humans wrote but cannot defend what the AI contributed. The asymmetry creates exposure in any adversarial client situation.

**Signal 6: Proposal stylometric exposure emerging from client-side AI analysis**
Clients running proposals through AI tools may be building stylometric profiles of consulting firms. The profile reveals AI usage patterns, potentially before the firm has any awareness this is happening.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Art. 398 CO warranty of proper performance:**
AI-assisted work product raises the question of what care standard applies when the MD cannot decompose what the AI contributed versus what the consultant contributed.

**nFADP Articles 9, 12, and 21:**
Consulting firms processing client data through AI systems may face simultaneous obligations for automated decision processing, data subject rights, and AI decision documentation. No Geneva service has produced a consolidated framework.

**Professional liability and sub-consultant AI delegation:**
The consulting firm bears professional liability to the client for the sub-consultant's work. This applies regardless of the sub-consultant's AI tool usage. The firm cannot transfer the liability downstream.

**Carrier AI attestation as personal liability commitment:**
The carrier attestation commits the MD to a methodology that has not been defined. The personal liability exposure is structural and unaddressed.

**AI Convention obligations through existing consulting relationships:**
Consulting firms with long-term client relationships may have AI Convention obligations that have already activated through those relationships.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Three-party AI accountability void as structural consulting market failure**
The consulting firm occupies the middle position between client and sub-consultant, bearing professional liability for both, while having no framework for governing the AI data flows through both. No Geneva service has produced a three-party framework.

**Finding 2: AI billing conversation avoidance as accumulating exposure**
Firms are avoiding the AI billing conversation with clients. The avoidance is not neutral. It creates exposure that compounds silently. The conversation needs a protocol that has not been published.

**Finding 3: Client AI proposal annotation challenge as asymmetric response situation**
When clients return proposals with AI-generated annotations, the consulting firm faces an asymmetric challenge: they can defend what humans wrote but cannot explain what the AI contributed. The asymmetry creates exposure in adversarial situations.

**Finding 4: Stylometric profile building from client-side proposal analysis**
Clients running consulting proposals through their own AI tools are building stylometric profiles. The profile reveals AI usage patterns before the firm has any awareness this is happening. No Geneva service has addressed this as a discrete exposure.

**Finding 5: Sub-consultant AI data flow as non-transferable professional liability**
The consulting firm bears professional liability to the client regardless of sub-consultant AI tool usage. The liability cannot be transferred downstream by contract or otherwise. The firm needs a governance framework for sub-consultant AI data flows.

**Finding 6: Carrier attestation methodology definition gap as consulting-specific market opportunity**
No Geneva service offers methodology definition specifically for consulting firms completing carrier AI attestations. The gap is consulting-specific because consulting firms produce bespoke analysis that does not fit standard methodology templates.
