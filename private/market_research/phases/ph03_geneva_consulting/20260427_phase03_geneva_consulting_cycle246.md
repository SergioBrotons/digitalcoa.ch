## Deliverable 01 — ICP Language

### How This ICP Actually Describes AI Problems and Concerns

**English — In Their Words**

When the managing director of a small Geneva consulting firm talks about AI, the language is precise and operational. The problem is not abstract. It surfaces in specific, named moments:

*"I can't explain to a client how a recommendation in a proposal was derived — the AI generated it, but I'm signing it."*

*"The junior people are producing better-looking documents faster, and I can't tell if that's because they're better or because the AI is doing it — and I can't review what I can't explain."*

*"We won a pitch last year that we shouldn't have won. The client later asked a question about methodology and I didn't have a defensible answer. We're now more careful but more slow."*

*"I know our AI tool processes our client data. I don't know where that data goes. The vendor's terms are not reassuring."*

*"Three of my clients have started asking AI-specific questions in their RFPs. I either have to claim competence I haven't demonstrated or lose the mandate."*

*"My carrier renewal asks about AI. The questions are clear. My answers are not."*

**The core tension:** They are accountable for work product they did not fully produce, using tools they do not fully understand, in a regulatory environment that has not yet defined their obligations but is visibly moving toward definition.

## Deliverable 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, and Complaints

**1. The Unreviewable Deliverable Problem**
The boutique consulting firm's value is built on judgment. AI-assisted drafting accelerates output while simultaneously destroying the trail that would allow the senior reviewer to verify the quality of the judgment applied. The faster the output, the more opaque the review surface. This is not a training problem. It is a structural feature of AI-assisted professional output that becomes more acute as AI use matures.

**2. The Junior Competence Paradox**
Junior staff equipped with AI produce deliverables at senior quality — by appearance. The senior who must review and sign those deliverables lacks the AI literacy to detect errors of reasoning, not just errors of fact. The boutique ends up with senior liability exposure for junior-AI output they cannot meaningfully supervise.

**3. Client Data Drift Without Documentation**
The firm's AI tool accumulates client-specific information in its training context, in thread history, in plugin data. This is not a data breach. It is a silent contamination of the boundary between client engagements. When a client asks what happened to their data, the firm cannot give a coherent answer because the data has been distributed across systems in ways the vendor has not made legible.

**4. The Proposal Quality Inflation Spiral**
Competitive pressure to adopt AI produces a market-wide quality inflation. Proposals look better. The substance behind them is harder to verify. Sophisticated clients — the ones worth keeping — are beginning to detect the gap between appearance and methodology. They are asking harder questions. The boutique that cannot answer loses. The boutique that can answer has built the governance infrastructure it should have built before adopting AI.

**5. The Carrier Documentation Requirement Without Adequacy Standard**
The insurance carrier asks specific questions. The firm must produce documented answers. There is no external adequacy standard against which to measure the response. "We have a policy" does not mean "our policy would protect us." The documentation is created against no defined criterion, producing false conformance — the belief that having a document satisfies the underlying obligation.

**6. Competitive Pressure as Governance Substitute**
Geneva boutiques face AI adoption pressure from two directions simultaneously: clients who expect AI-assisted speed and efficiency, and competitors who are adopting AI with no visible governance failures. The market signal suggests adoption is low-cost. The actual liability exposure accumulates silently. The competitive pressure to adopt operates as a forcing function for governance decisions the firm has not made consciously.

**7. The RFP Methodology Trap**
Client RFPs now contain AI-specific questions. The boutique that cannot answer them loses mandates. The boutique that answers them based on ad hoc practice rather than documented methodology is making representations it cannot support. The methodology trap: to win the mandate, you must claim governance you have not built. To build the governance, you need the mandate.

## Deliverable 03 — Competitive Analysis

### Gaps — What No One Is Addressing

**Gap 1: Boutique-Scale AI Supervision Architecture**
The professional services AI governance market is oriented toward larger firms. Enterprise frameworks, ISO 42001 certification programs, and compliance tool vendors assume IT infrastructure, a compliance function, and governance staff the boutique does not have. The problem that actually exists — how a two-person Geneva boutique manages the accountability gap between AI output and senior signature — is unaddressed by current market offerings.

**Gap 2: Geneva Peer Accountability Forum for AI**
Legal and finance have professional communities that exchange information about AI governance informally. Consulting does not. There is no Geneva consulting equivalent to the SVA working groups or the bar association sessions where legal and finance practitioners compare carrier questionnaire answers and regulatory expectations. The consulting boutique operates in informational isolation. The market mechanism that resolves uncertainty in other professions does not operate here.

**Gap 3: Deliverable Provenance Documentation**
File note systems in consulting firms are designed for human-produced output. The provenance question — how much of this deliverable was AI-generated, under what tool configuration, with what client data context — has no documentary architecture. The firm's own records cannot answer the question it must answer when a client or carrier asks.

**Gap 4: Engagement Letter AI Terms as Governance Infrastructure**
No Geneva consulting firm has engagement letter language that addresses AI's role in the engagement. The absence is not neutral. When the engagement letter is silent on AI methodology, the firm accepts liability for whatever methodology was applied by default. The engagement letter could be a governance forcing function — a mechanism that requires the firm to document its AI methodology before it begins the work. No one is offering this.

**Gap 5: Proposal AI Quality as Client-Facing Governance Exposure**
A boutique's proposal is its most visible artifact. AI-generated proposals are becoming detectably AI-generated by sophisticated clients. The boutique with no AI methodology documentation is producing externally visible evidence of its methodology — to the clients who ask the most probing questions.

**Gap 6: Geneva Consulting AI Insurance Product Design**
Current PI insurance products do not have an AI-specific coverage tier with defined adequacy language. The coverage question — what does "AI-assisted professional services" coverage mean, what would trigger it, what documentation would support a claim — is unanswered. The insurance product has not caught up to the practice. No advisory firm has positioned itself at this intersection.

## Deliverable 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Specific Regulatory Obligations for Consultants

Geneva consulting firms are not a regulated profession in the same sense as law or finance, but they operate under layered obligations that create accountability exposure specific to their position.

**1. nFADP Data Processing Obligations**
The revised Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) applies to the consulting firm's AI tool processing. Client names, engagement details, project data, and deliverable content constitute personal data under nFADP. The firm must have a lawful basis for processing, must provide disclosure, and must maintain data processing records. AI tools that retain client data in model training context or thread history create processing obligations the firm may not have documented.

**2. Professional Secrecy and AI Tool Configuration**
Swiss professional codes do not have a Geneva consulting-specific secrecy statute equivalent to Art. 321 CP for attorneys, but contractual confidentiality obligations and client expectations create effective secrecy duties. The AI tool vendor's data handling — server location, model training, data retention — is a professional secrecy question the firm cannot answer without documented vendor assessment.

**3. Swiss Contractual Liability — OR Art. 398**
The Swiss Code of Obligations Art. 398 defines the mandate contract. The mandator (client) is entitled to faithful performance. The mandatory (consultant) must exercise the care and diligence of the profession. AI-assisted deliverables raise the question: what is the standard of care when the professional cannot fully explain the AI-generated output? The doctrine is emerging. The firm must be able to demonstrate it applied proportionate review.

**4. Carrier Insurance Disclosure Requirements**
Geneva PI insurance carriers are requiring AI-specific disclosures in renewal questionnaires. The questions probe tool inventory, version tracking, client data processing, review protocols, and AI output verification methodology. The firm's responses create representations that bind it. False or incomplete responses may void coverage precisely when coverage is needed.

**5. Client AI Governance Questionnaires**
EU-connected clients, multinational clients, and increasingly domestic clients are sending AI governance questionnaires to Geneva consulting firms. These questionnaires impose obligations on the firm that do not correspond to any Swiss domestic standard. The firm's responses create representations to clients who may share them with insurers, counterparties, or regulators. No Geneva consulting firm has a documented protocol for responding to AI governance questionnaires.

**6. Federal Council AI Deliberation — June 2026**
The Swiss Federal Council is expected to deliberate on AI regulatory framework direction in June 2026. The deliberation will set a frame — it will not fill in specifics for boutique consulting firms. The pre-deliberation window is the time to build documented positions. Post-deliberation, the market will move faster than the firm that has not prepared.

## Deliverable 06 — Findings Summary

### Six Net-New Insights — Distinct from C214–C245 Phase 03

**Insight 1: The Proposal Provenance Paradox**
A boutique consulting firm's proposal is simultaneously its most carefully reviewed output and its least governed AI artifact. Senior management reviews proposals obsessively for commercial and positioning reasons. No equivalent review exists for the AI methodology embedded in the proposal. The paradox: the output with the highest commercial scrutiny has the lowest governance attention. Sophisticated clients are beginning to evaluate the methodology behind the proposal, not just the proposal itself. This creates a specific, addressable exposure for the boutique that builds documented methodology before the RFP question arrives.

**Insight 2: Configuration Inheritance as Firm-Specific IP Exposure**
When a managing director configures an AI tool to optimize for the firm's specific practice areas and client types, that configuration constitutes implicit intellectual property. The configuration knowledge is embedded in individual practice, not in firm documentation. Staff departures, tool migrations, and vendor model changes all erode this embedded knowledge without the firm having a record of what was lost. The configuration itself — not just the outputs — is a firm asset that requires documentation architecture.

**Insight 3: Client-Generated AI Content as Discrete Three-Party Accountability Category**
Clients are beginning to send AI-generated content into the consulting engagement — project briefs, analysis frameworks, draft deliverables. The boutique that incorporates this content into its own deliverables inherits accountability for content it did not choose or produce. This is structurally different from the client-providing-data problem. The client is providing processed output, not raw data. No Geneva consulting engagement letter has provisions for this. The accountability chain runs from client AI → boutique incorporation → client consumption → third-party reliance, with no Geneva protocol for any link.

**Insight 4: Proposal Efficiency as Structural Differentiation Erosion**
AI-assisted proposal production is becoming commoditized across the Geneva boutique market. The efficiency gain from AI-assisted drafting is being competed away as all firms adopt AI simultaneously. The market is converging on a new equilibrium where all firms are equally fast and equally unable to explain their methodology. The boutique that previously differentiated on proposal quality must find a new differentiation basis. The efficiency gain that drove AI adoption is ceasing to be a competitive advantage — it is becoming a cost of market entry. The firms that will maintain premium positioning are those that build methodology documentation as the new differentiation signal.

**Insight 5: Engagement Letter AI Warranty Clauses as De Facto Governance Forcing Functions**
Most Geneva consulting engagement letters contain broad warranty language about the quality and authorship of deliverables. When the firm deploys AI-assisted production without updating the engagement letter, the existing warranty language implicitly covers the AI-assisted deliverables. The firm is making warranty representations about methodology it has not documented. The engagement letter is not a neutral document. It is a governance forcing function that requires the firm to document its AI methodology before the work begins — or accept liability for whatever methodology was applied by default. No advisory product in the Geneva market currently addresses engagement letter AI governance architecture.

**Insight 6: Geneva Consulting Peer Governance Vacuum as Structural Market Failure**
The legal and finance professions in Geneva have informal community mechanisms — bar association working groups, SVA sessions, FINMA informally circulating practitioners — where AI governance questions are resolved by peer exchange before doctrine arrives. Consulting has no equivalent. The Geneva consulting managing director operates in informational isolation, unable to verify whether their approach to AI governance is adequate, excessive, or deficient relative to peers. This vacuum is not a gap in advisory supply. It is a structural market failure: the mechanism by which practice norms form in other professions does not operate in consulting, producing a market where firms make decisions without the peer calibration that reduces collective exposure.
