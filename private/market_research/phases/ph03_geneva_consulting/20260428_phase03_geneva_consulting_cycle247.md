## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

### Structural Failure Modes

**Failure Mode 1: Proposal Approval Chain Opacity**
The managing director produces proposals with AI assistance. The proposal goes to the client. The client's procurement team now has AI detection tools. When a client's tools flag AI-generated content in a boutique proposal, the boutique has no documented AI methodology record to respond. This is not a hypothetical — it is a live operational risk that is not named in any Geneva consulting firm's documentation architecture.

**Failure Mode 2: Sub-Consultant AI Output Delegation Without Authority**
Boutique firms use specialist sub-consultants on engagements: sector experts, technical specialists, regional analysts. Some of these sub-consultants use AI tools. The boutique inherits their AI-generated output as part of the client deliverable, without:
- A documented sub-consultant AI tool inventory
- An AI output review protocol for sub-consultant contributions
- An engagement letter provision allocating AI accountability between boutique and sub-consultant

This is a three-party accountability gap structurally different from the client-supplied AI content problem, because here the boutique is the principal, not the recipient.

**Failure Mode 3: Client AI Disclosure Request — Unmanaged Exposure Surface**
Sophisticated clients — particularly EU-connected clients, private equity portfolio companies, and firms with internal AI governance functions — are beginning to send AI governance questionnaires to their consulting firms. The managing director at a boutique receiving such a questionnaire faces a structural problem: the firm cannot answer methodology questions about its AI tool configurations, training data provenance, model version tracking, or review protocols, because this documentation architecture does not exist at boutique scale.

The exposure is not that the firm uses AI. The exposure is that the firm cannot provide any evidentiary record of how AI is used in client work.

**Failure Mode 4: AI Co-Authorship Status in Client Deliverables**
When a boutique delivers a strategic recommendation, a market assessment, a regulatory analysis, or an implementation roadmap to a client, and that deliverable was produced with significant AI assistance — who is the author? The client engaged the boutique for professional judgment. The deliverable contains AI-generated analytical scaffolding and AI-synthesized research. The boutique's name is on the document.

This is not a disclosure problem. It is an authorship and ownership problem with downstream commercial and liability implications that engagement letters do not currently address.

**Failure Mode 5: Competitive AI Credentialism — Market Pressure Before Regulatory Requirement**
A growing number of RFPs and client due diligence processes in the Geneva market are beginning to require AI governance credentials from consulting firms. Not because Swiss regulation requires it — it does not yet — but because clients are applying market discipline. The boutique that cannot demonstrate AI governance documentation to a sophisticated client faces a competitive disadvantage against firms that have built this infrastructure.

This creates a structural paradox: the firms that most need AI governance documentation (small boutiques without dedicated compliance functions) are least equipped to produce it, while facing the most competitive pressure to demonstrate it.

**Failure Mode 6: Cross-Border AI Delivery Exposure**
Geneva consulting boutiques increasingly deliver work to clients with EU connections — EU-headquartered corporations, EU-regulated entities, organizations subject to EU AI Act obligations. When a Swiss boutique produces AI-assisted deliverables for an EU-connected client, the boutique's AI methodology becomes relevant to the client's own AI Act compliance. No framework exists for Swiss boutiques to understand this exposure or to document their AI methodology at a level that would satisfy EU AI Act downstream documentation requirements.

---

### The Paradox at the Core

The managing director of a 2–15 person Geneva boutique is simultaneously:
- A principal who personally signs client deliverables and engagement letters
- A professional who cannot independently verify the quality of AI-assisted work produced by their own team
- A business owner absorbing governance costs without a clear commercial return
- A competitive actor under pressure to adopt AI faster than their governance can support

The paradox: AI adoption is presented as an efficiency gain, but the governance requirements it creates at boutique scale produce costs that offset the efficiency gain and introduce personal liability exposure that the efficiency gain does not compensate.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva / Swiss Specific Regulatory Obligations for Consultants

### Regulatory Framework Applicable to Geneva Consulting Boutiques

**1. Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) — Present-Tense Obligations**
Geneva consulting firms processing client information in AI tools are subject to nFADP as of June 2023. Key obligations directly applicable:
- **Art. 6:** Lawfulness of processing — AI tool configuration requires documented lawful basis for data processing
- **Art. 8:** Data minimization — AI tools processing client strategy information must be configured to limit processing to what is necessary for the mandate
- **Art. 9:** Special categories of data — client information in consulting contexts may include sensitive business data; AI processing of such data requires explicit consent or specific legal basis
- **Art. 21:** Right to information — clients can request disclosure of AI systems used in processing their work; boutiques must be able to respond

**2. Professional Secrecy and Confidentiality — Art. 321 CP**
Swiss Criminal Code Art. 321 criminalizes disclosure of client information by professionals. AI tool adoption creates a structural tension: confidential client work product enters AI systems with terms of service that may not be compatible with professional secrecy obligations. No Geneva bar guidance resolves which AI tool configurations are compatible with Art. 321 CP obligations. This is a live, unaddressed structural exposure for every Geneva consulting firm using AI tools.

**3. Carrier Renewal — Annual Governance Attestation**
Professional liability insurance carriers are now including AI governance questions in renewal documentation. For Geneva boutiques, these questions require affirmative attestation on:
- AI tool inventory and configuration
- AI output review protocols
- Client communication records on AI methodology
- Reassessment triggers for AI tool adequacy

The problem: no Geneva carrier has published an adequacy reference standard for these attestations. The attestation is made against an undefined criterion. The managing director signs without knowing what standard they are confirming.

**4. EU AI Act Downstream Documentation Requirements**
Swiss boutiques with EU-connected clients face EU AI Act documentation obligations as a market-mechanism requirement, not a Swiss regulatory requirement. EU AI Act Art. 13 high-risk system documentation requirements create specific obligations for service providers whose outputs feed into EU-regulated activities. A Geneva boutique producing AI-assisted regulatory analysis for an EU financial services client creates documentation relevant to the client's own EU AI Act compliance. No Swiss advisory framework addresses this intersection.

**5. Swiss Code of Obligations — Art. 398 Professional Services Standard**
Swiss Code of Obligations Art. 398 establishes the professional services standard: a service provider must exercise the care required by the nature of the mandate. For AI-assisted consulting deliverables, the question of what "the care required by the nature of the mandate" means when AI contributes to the deliverable is undefined. No Swiss court has established what substantive review obligation a managing director has for AI-assisted client work under Art. 398. This uncertainty creates personal liability exposure that cannot be managed without a documented review protocol.

**6. Geneva Cantonal Business Registration — Professional Liability Insurance Requirements**
Geneva boutiques registered as providers of consulting services must maintain professional liability insurance. AI-related claims fall into a coverage ambiguity zone: policies written before AI tools existed do not explicitly cover AI-assisted deliverables, and carrier disclaimers for AI-generated content are increasingly appearing in renewal documentation. The boutique is caught between client expectations of coverage and carrier language designed to limit coverage for AI-related claims.

**7. Peer Governance Vacuum — No Sector-Specific AI Guidance**
Unlike Geneva finance (FINMA, SFAMA) and Geneva legal (SVA/ORDRE), the Geneva consulting sector has no professional body issuing AI governance guidance. There is no equivalent of FINMA Circular on AI deployment, no SVA working group on AI methodology documentation, no Geneva consulting association peer review mechanism for AI governance adequacy. The managing director of a Geneva boutique has no sector-specific reference for what adequate AI governance looks like for their professional context.
