## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### English

**Managing director on proposal authorship:**
"The proposal went out with our firm name on it. The client assumed we wrote it in the way we have always written proposals. We used AI to generate the first several drafts. The editing was more extensive than I expected. By the time we were done, I was not sure what was originally ours. The intellectual contribution is somewhere in the document. I cannot separate it from what the AI contributed."

**On competitive pressure changing the decision frame:**
"The pressure to adopt AI is no longer about whether it helps us. It is about whether we look like we are keeping up. That is a different decision. I am making adoption decisions for appearance reasons and I know it."

**On what happens when clients ask about AI governance:**
"One of our clients sent us their AI governance framework and asked if we aligned with it. We do not have an AI governance framework. We did not know how to answer the question. The conversation revealed a gap we did not know we had."

**On sub-consultant AI delegation:**
"We brought in a specialist for part of that engagement. She used AI tools extensively. The final deliverable has her contribution embedded in it. Our firm signed it. I have been thinking about what that means. I do not think it means what we assumed it means."

**On documentation:**
"I tried to document where we use AI for about three months. The documentation effort was taking more time than the AI was saving. I stopped. The calculation I was making was implicit. I am not sure I was making the right one."

**On what happens when something goes wrong:**
"If a client questions a recommendation we made, I need to be able to explain how we reached it. The AI was involved in ways I did not track. When the client asks me to explain, I am reconstructing the reasoning after the fact. That is harder than it sounds."

## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Client AI governance questionnaires arriving before response capability**
Client-sent frameworks are arriving before Geneva consulting firms have developed response capability. The questionnaire is a relationship qualification conversation being failed as a compliance exercise.

**Signal 2: Competitive adoption pressure decoupling from utility analysis**
Firms are making AI adoption decisions based on competitive appearance rather than utility analysis. The decision frame has shifted from "is this useful" to "does this make us look current." The decoupling is structural.

**Signal 3: Sub-consultant AI delegation chain accumulating without review**
Sub-consultant AI contributions are accumulating across active client relationships without a methodology for verifying confidentiality obligations or managing the liability chain.

**Signal 4: Documentation effort producing implicit negative ROI calculation**
The effort to document AI usage is being evaluated implicitly and found negative in some configurations. The calculation is made without a named framework.

**Signal 5: Reasoning reconstruction required when clients ask questions**
Client questions about how recommendations were reached require firms to reconstruct reasoning after the fact. The reconstruction work is different from producing the recommendation and firms are not equipped for it.

**Signal 6: Proposal authorship becoming an issue in client conversations**
Clients are beginning to ask about who wrote what in consulting deliverables. The question is not whether AI was used — it is whether the content is genuinely the firm's.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Client AI governance questionnaire response capability as relationship qualification product**
No Geneva consulting firm has developed a product for responding to client AI governance questionnaires that functions as a relationship qualification conversation. The capability converts the interaction from compliance exercise to sales qualification opportunity.

**Gap 2: Competitive adoption decision audit framework**
No framework exists for auditing whether AI adoption decisions are driven by utility or by competitive appearance pressure. The decoupling is structural and unrecognized.

**Gap 3: Sub-consultant AI delegation verification methodology**
No methodology exists for verifying confidentiality obligations through sub-consultant AI vendor infrastructure. The verification would need to be achievable at boutique scale.

**Gap 4: Reasoning reconstruction protocol for AI-assisted deliverables**
No protocol exists for reconstructing the reasoning that produced an AI-assisted recommendation. The protocol would need to address how to produce a reconstructable account of what the human evaluated versus what the AI contributed.

**Gap 5: Documentation ROI framework**
No framework exists for evaluating whether documentation effort produces a positive ROI relative to the AI usage it documents. The implicit calculation is being made without a named framework.

**Gap 6: Intellectual property attribution methodology for AI-assisted proposals**
No methodology exists for managing intellectual property attribution when AI has contributed to proposals. The methodology would need to address consent, documentation, and client communication.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Professional liability under Art. 28 CO for AI-assisted deliverables:**
Art. 28 CO liability for professional error applies to deliverables bearing the firm's signature regardless of AI contribution. The documentation of AI's contribution to errors is unresolved.

**Confidentiality obligations for client data in AI systems:**
Client data processed through AI tools creates confidentiality obligations under engagement letters and professional duty. The intersection with vendor terms of service is not addressed in standard engagement language.

**FINMA MiFID suitability documentation for AI-assisted recommendations:**
MiFID suitability obligations apply to recommendations made with AI contribution. The documentation requirements for AI-assisted suitability analysis are not defined at boutique scale.

**nFADP Article 6 legitimate interest for internal AI processing:**
Internal AI processing of client data requires a legal basis under nFADP Article 6. The legitimate interest assessment for AI processing of client work product has not been published.

**Proposal intellectual property under Swiss copyright law:**
Swiss copyright law vests authorship in the natural person who created the work. AI-generated content in proposals creates copyright attribution questions no court has addressed.

**EU AI Act downstream deployer obligations for consulting firms:**
Consulting firms using AI tools for client deliverables are downstream deployers under the EU AI Act. The Article 26 documentation requirements are not adapted for consulting firm use cases.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Client AI governance questionnaire response capability as undifferentiated sales qualification opportunity**
Firms are treating client AI governance questionnaires as compliance exercises. The same interaction, handled with response capability, functions as a sales qualification conversation. No Geneva consulting firm has named this conversion opportunity.

**Finding 2: Competitive adoption pressure has decoupled from utility analysis as structural recognition problem**
Firms are making AI adoption decisions based on competitive appearance pressure rather than utility analysis. The decoupling is structural and unrecognized. The decision frame being used is not the decision frame that should be used.

**Finding 3: Sub-consultant AI delegation chain creates bilateral liability without verification architecture**
The firm signs deliverables containing sub-consultant contributions made through AI tools. The delegation chain creates bilateral liability for both firm and client. No verification methodology exists at boutique scale.

**Finding 4: Documentation burden ROI calculation is implicit and negative for some boutique configurations**
The effort to document AI usage produces a negative ROI in some configurations. The calculation is implicit; the decision to stop documenting is made without a framework. The negative ROI configuration is unnamed.

**Finding 5: Reasoning reconstruction requirement as named capability gap for AI-assisted deliverables**
Clients asking about how recommendations were reached requires firms to reconstruct reasoning after the fact. The reconstruction is a different kind of work than producing the recommendation. No protocol exists for producing reconstructable reasoning records.

**Finding 6: Proposal authorship becoming an issue at the client relationship level**
Clients are beginning to ask about who wrote what in consulting deliverables. The question activates intellectual property, professional liability, and client relationship dimensions simultaneously. No Geneva firm has a protocol for managing this conversation proactively.
