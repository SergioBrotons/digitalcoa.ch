### English

**On the client expectation shift:**
"The conversation in the room has changed. Three years ago, the client asked if we used AI. We said yes or no and that was the end of it. Now the client has a point of view on what we should be using it for. They are forming opinions about how much human judgment should be in the product. We did not notice when it changed. We are now managing a conversation we did not prepare for."

**On deliverable confidence calibration:**
"We have a problem we cannot name. Our AI-assisted deliverables are very consistent. Very clean. Very well-structured. When a client reads them, they sometimes say the work is impressive. "We did not budget for what it costs to move from one AI tool to another. The content we built in the old tool is in the old tool's infrastructure. The new tool does not have it. It is not an AI governance problem in the way people describe governance problems — it is just a practical problem that nobody will acknowledge."

"Our engagement letters have a section on confidentiality and a section on work product ownership. Neither section mentions AI. Neither section was written when AI was a consideration. ## 02 — Market Signals

Current problems, failure modes, paradoxes, and complaints as observed from this ICP's expressed experience.

### Signal 01 — Deliverable Homogenization as Unnamed Competitive Differentiation Erosion

The MD observes that AI-assisted deliverables across the Geneva consulting market are becoming structurally indistinguishable at the output level. The formatting, structure, and argumentative flow of AI-assisted consulting deliverables is converging. The differentiation that used to come from individual analyst judgment is being replaced by a common AI output architecture. The MD experiences this as a specific competitive problem: the firm's deliverables are becoming difficult to distinguish from competitors' deliverables at a structural level, while the price points remain different. The erosion is not visible in any published competitive analysis. It is visible to the MD who reads both their own deliverables and competitors' proposals in close succession.

### Signal 02 — Client Confidence Calibration Problem as Unresolved Quality Category

Clients are responding to AI-assisted deliverables with a specific feedback pattern: high production quality creates uncertainty about the firm's professional contribution. The uncertainty does not resolve into a clear positive or negative judgment — it creates a residual question the client carries. The firm receives mixed feedback signals that do not map onto traditional quality categories. The MD does not have a framework for calibrating whether the mixed feedback represents a relationship problem, a positioning problem, a quality problem, or a market norm transition problem. No Geneva market participant has named this as a specific feedback category requiring its own diagnostic and response framework.

### Signal 03 — AI Tool Vendor Dependency as Unbudgeted Structural Cost

The boutique consulting firm's AI tool investment is exposed to vendor pricing changes, product discontinuations, and infrastructure transitions with no structural provision for migration cost. The content the firm has produced using its current AI tool — proposals, deliverable components, internal knowledge bases — is in the vendor's infrastructure. Switching costs are not calculated at tool selection. The unbudgeted migration cost is a present-tense structural exposure for firms that have been using AI for two or more years. The exposure is invisible as a governance problem because it presents as an IT budget question, not a professional liability question. The MD who has not budgeted for migration cannot afford to migrate even when migration becomes necessary.

### Signal 04 — Engagement Letter Coverage Gap as Active Unnamed Legal Exposure

Standard Geneva consulting engagement letters do not contain AI-specific provisions. The MD who uses standard engagement letters has been relying on general confidentiality and work product ownership clauses to cover AI-related questions for which those clauses were not designed. The gap is active: it surfaces when a client asks about AI methodology, when a deliverable质量问题 arises, when a sub-consultant delegation question arrives, or when a carrier renewal asks about AI practices. The gap has no name in the market and no standard remediation language. Each firm is discovering the specific shape of the gap through specific client moments, without reference to what other firms have learned.

### Signal 05 — Sub-Consultant AI Delegation as Three-Party Unmanaged Accountability Chain

The Geneva boutique consulting firm uses sub-consultants. Some sub-consultants use AI. The firm does not have a framework for evaluating what AI the sub-consultant uses, what the sub-consultant's AI does with the information the firm shares, or what the sub-consultant's AI governance practices are. The firm is accountable to the client for the sub-consultant's work product. The accountability chain — firm to sub-consultant to sub-consultant's AI vendor — has no documented governance architecture. The firm is managing the chain reactively, through individual relationship management, without any protocol for the aggregate exposure this creates across all active sub-consultant relationships.

### Signal 06 — Carrier Renewal Attestation as Unreviewed Personal Legal Instrument

The PI carrier renewal attestation is signed by the managing director. The attestation creates a documented personal legal representation about the firm's AI practices. The MD signs it without a methodology for determining what correct answers look like, without a review of whether the firm's actual practices match what was attested, and without legal counsel on what the attestation creates if a claim arises. The attestation functions as a legal instrument whose legal consequences are not reviewed before signing. The gap between attestation and actual practice is not actively managed because no one has named it as a specific liability-generating instrument requiring pre-signing review.

### Signal 07 — The Geneva Consulting Market Has No Informal Standard-Formation Circuit

The Geneva consulting sector has no equivalent to what the bar provides for attorneys or what SFAMA provides for wealth managers. AI governance standards for Geneva consulting firms are forming through client questionnaires, carrier renewal requirements, and RFP language — without any sector-specific informal circuit for peer calibration. The MD who wants to understand what adequate AI governance looks like for their firm has no Geneva-specific reference point. The absence is not experienced as a regulatory gap — it is experienced as the specific professional isolation of being the only person in the room who faces this problem without institutional support.

## 04 — Compliance Context

Geneva and Swiss specific regulatory obligations for consultants.

### Regulatory Landscape for Geneva Consulting Firms

**No Published Sector-Specific AI Governance Standard**

Geneva consulting firms operate without a published sector-specific AI governance standard. Unlike attorneys (Geneva bar/SVA guidance) and wealth managers (FINMA supervision, SFAMA guidance), the consulting sector has no institutional framework defining what adequate AI governance looks like for a Geneva consulting firm. The absence means each MD must independently determine what adequate practice is — without reference to published standards, without peer calibration mechanisms, and without any supervisory process that would reveal whether their determination was correct before a liability event occurs.

**Professional Liability as Primary Accountability Architecture**

The Geneva consulting MD's accountability structure operates through civil liability and PI insurance, not regulatory supervision. The Swiss Code of Obligations (Art. 398) warranty of proper performance attaches to consulting deliverables regardless of how the content was produced. When an AI-assisted deliverable produces suboptimal client outcomes, the warranty question is live and the firm cannot currently document what methodology produced the content. The warranty exposure is personal to the MD in boutique firm structures where the MD signature appears on all significant client deliverables.

**nFADP Article 9 Third-Party Processor Obligations**

Consulting firms using AI tools where the vendor is a non-Swiss third-party processor have active Article 9 nFADP obligations. The processor obligations include ensuring data processing agreements are in place, that processing is restricted to authorized purposes, and that processor security measures are documented. Boutique consulting firms have not systematically evaluated their processor obligations relative to their AI tool configurations. Client work product, analytical methodology, and client information processed through non-Swiss AI systems create ongoing nFADP compliance obligations that are not being actively managed.

**Contractual AI Representations Through Client Engagement Letters**

Consulting firms accepting client engagement letter AI addenda are making contractual representations about their AI methodology and practice. These representations are binding through the commercial contract independently of Swiss domestic regulatory requirements. Misrepresentation may constitute contractual breach with commercial consequences. The representations are accepted by firms that do not have the methodology documentation the representations assert. The contractual exposure compounds as the stock of AI-containing engagement letters accumulates with each new client engagement.

**EU AI Act Downstream Obligations Through EU Client Relationships**

Swiss consulting firms serving EU-connected clients face AI governance obligations imposed through commercial relationships, not through Swiss domestic regulation. As EU-based clients respond to their own EU AI Act obligations, they are imposing downstream documentation requirements on Swiss consulting firms that interact with their AI systems or incorporate their AI outputs. The EU AI Act Article 11 downstream deployer documentation obligations are operative for Geneva consulting firms with EU client exposure, regardless of Swiss domestic regulatory status. No Geneva advisory service has mapped this intersection for consulting firms.

**PI Insurance Coverage Terms for AI-Assisted Errors Remain Undefined**

Swiss PI insurance policies do not contain explicit coverage terms for AI-assisted professional errors in most current contracts. The carrier renewal questionnaire is probing AI practices without published clarity on what AI-assisted error coverage means under existing policy language. The MD's attestation creates documented evidence of AI use that may be relevant to coverage disputes if a claim arises. Coverage ambiguity for AI-assisted errors is a present-tense condition. The MD who signs the attestation without understanding what the policy covers is accepting coverage uncertainty as a personal liability risk.

**Federal Council AI Convention Implementation Timeline**

The Federal Council's ratification of the Council of Europe AI Convention creates implementation obligations that will flow through commercial relationships before domestic regulatory frameworks are finalized. Consulting firms serving EU-connected clients or international organizations in Geneva will encounter AI governance requirements imposed by counterparties responding to their own EU AI Act obligations. The implementation timeline creates documentation requirements for firms that have used AI without documenting methodology provenance. The consulting sector does not have a methodology provenance documentation standard and is not identified as a specific priority in current Swiss AI governance deliberation.
