The partner who negotiated the contract had not discussed them with the person who would be running the engagement. "We moved to AI-assisted work. The time to produce deliverables came down. Our billing looked better. What nobody measured is the time we are spending on governance, documentation, managing AI-related client questions, and reviewing things we would not have had to review before. It is real time. It does not appear as AI cost. It appears as management time."

## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Client stylometric detection of AI authorship operating below explicit feedback threshold**
Sophisticated clients are identifying AI authorship in deliverables through stylistic markers — structural patterns, language registers, analytical templates. When detected, it triggers credibility reassessment. The reassessment rarely surfaces as a direct client comment. It surfaces as relationship shift, reduced mandate scope, or silence where there was previously engagement. The firm experiences it as a lost client without understanding why.

**Signal 2: Client AI governance questionnaires as competitive intelligence instruments**
Client-sent AI governance frameworks are functioning as methodology due diligence. The response requires disclosing analytical approach, tool configuration, and review methodology. The disclosure is made to a party with commercial incentive to use the information. Firms are not treating questionnaire response as a competitive intelligence decision with commercial consequence — only as a compliance exercise.

**Signal 3: AI platform consolidation creating convergent methodology at market level**
The three to five dominant AI platforms among Geneva consulting boutiques are producing structural methodology convergence. Individual firms experience their analytical output as distinct. Competitive context reveals that peer firms using the same platforms are producing similarly structured recommendations. The convergence is competitively relevant and individually invisible without external reference.

**Signal 4: Contract AI governance commitments made outside operational review**
AI governance commitments embedded in negotiated client contracts are not being reviewed against operational delivery capacity before signature. The committing party and the implementing party are typically the same MD at boutique scale. No separation of commercial negotiation and operational governance review exists at the scale where this gap is most acute.

**Signal 5: AI-adjacent liability events accumulating as unexamined portfolio exposure**
Each AI-assisted engagement produces minor events — client questions, near-misses, documentation gaps, questionnaire responses. Individually, each event appears manageable. Across an active portfolio of engagements, the aggregate is a compounding undisclosed liability position. No Geneva consulting firm is managing AI-adjacent exposure as a portfolio problem.

**Signal 6: AI governance documentation as unbillable overhead structurally excluded from pricing model**
Time-based billing captures AI efficiency gains. AI governance documentation — the records, reviews, and protocols that AI governance requires — is unbillable overhead. Firms adopting AI under time-based pricing are absorbing governance costs into operational margins without visibility or allocation. The pricing model structurally mismeasures the true cost of AI-assisted delivery.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Stylometric AI authorship differentiation as boutique positioning category**
No Geneva consulting firm has developed a systematic approach to managing the stylometric signature of AI-assisted work. The competitive question is not whether to use AI but how to use it without the output reading as AI-generated to sophisticated clients. This is a differentiation category that has not been claimed.

**Gap 2: AI governance questionnaire response strategy as discrete competitive capability**
No Geneva advisory service addresses how a consulting firm should respond to client AI governance questionnaires — what to disclose, what to commit to, what to defer, and what the competitive consequences of the response are. This is a methodology protection question with commercial consequence, not a compliance documentation exercise.

**Gap 3: Portfolio-level AI exposure aggregation as risk management function**
No Geneva consulting firm is tracking AI-adjacent liability events across the active engagement portfolio as a cumulative exposure. The aggregation function — what the firm's total AI exposure looks like across all active mandates — is structurally absent from boutique-scale risk management.

**Gap 4: Analytical methodology convergence audit**
No service addresses how to assess whether a firm's analytical output is converging with peer firms' output through shared AI platform dependency. The competitive consequence — that distinct analytical methodology is being eroded without the firm's awareness — is unnamed as a discrete service occasion.

**Gap 5: Pre-signature AI governance commitment review for negotiated contracts**
No protocol exists for reviewing AI governance commitments embedded in negotiated client contracts against operational delivery capacity before signature. The protocol would prevent commercial teams from committing to standards the implementing team cannot meet, which is the primary failure mode for this specific gap.

**Gap 6: AI governance cost allocation methodology for time-based billing**
No methodology exists for allocating AI governance overhead costs back to engagements under time-based billing models. Without the allocation, firms are structurally mispricing AI-assisted engagements — capturing efficiency gains while excluding governance costs from the engagement economics.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Professional liability under Art. 28 CO for stylometric credibility erosion:**
Art. 28 CO protects personality rights including commercial reputation. When clients reassess a firm's analytical credibility based on perceived AI authorship, and the reassessment affects the commercial relationship, the question of what the firm represented about its analytical methodology becomes a pre-contractual misrepresentation question. The representation was implicit, not contractual — Swiss law has not addressed this specific mechanism.

**Geneva consulting accountability vacuum as structural liability amplifier:**
Geneva consulting firms have no equivalent to SVA professional standards, ORDRE guidance, or SFAMA oversight. The absence of a professional accountability framework means Geneva consulting firms are exposed to AI governance risks without the protective infrastructure that exists for legal and financial services. The vacuum is not a market opportunity — it is a structural liability amplifier.

**nFADP Article 6 legitimate interest for consulting firm internal AI analysis:**
Internal AI processing of client-related institutional knowledge requires a lawful basis under nFADP. The legitimate interest pathway under Article 6 is available in principle but has not been analyzed for the specific case where the processed information includes client-confidential analytical material processed for internal firm methodology development. The question is practically untested and unpublished.

**nFADP Article 8profiling and AI-assisted client relationship monitoring:**
AI tools processing client interaction data to build behavioral profiles of client decision-making patterns may constitute profiling under nFADP Article 8. Consulting firms that use AI to analyze client communication patterns, decision timing, or negotiation behavior may be conducting profiling operations without the required specific consent architecture.

**EU AI Act downstream deployer obligations for Geneva firms serving EU clients:**
Consulting firms using AI systems in client methodology development while serving EU-based clients are downstream deployers under EU AI Act Article 26. The documentation requirements are not adapted for methodology development use cases. The exposure exists independently of whether the firm is aware of the obligation.

**Swiss copyright and AI-generated analytical framework attribution:**
Swiss copyright vests authorship in natural persons. Where AI generates a substantial analytical framework that the firm then edits and delivers, the copyright status of the deliverable is legally ambiguous. If the client commissioned the deliverable and provided underlying data, the copyright question interacts with ownership questions in ways Swiss courts have not resolved.

**SQC disclosure requirements and consulting methodology confidentiality conflict:**
The Society of Quality Consultants' new AI disclosure standards require consultants to describe AI methodology used in client work. For a consulting firm, describing the methodology is equivalent to disclosing the proprietary analytical approach that constitutes the firm's commercial value. The disclosure requirement and the confidentiality obligation are structurally in conflict.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Stylometric AI authorship detection as a silent client credibility erosion mechanism operating below explicit feedback threshold — distinct from all prior client sophistication and communication quality findings**
Sophisticated clients are detecting AI authorship through stylistic markers in deliverables — structural patterns, language registers, analytical templates. The detection triggers credibility reassessment of the firm's analytical capability. The reassessment surfaces as client disengagement, not as a direct question. The firm cannot correct a dynamic it does not know is occurring. No Geneva market service has named stylometric detection as a discrete credibility erosion mechanism distinct from delivery quality, communication polish, or formal AI disclosure questions. This is distinct from C265 Finding 04 (deliverable polish calibration), C258 Finding 04 (deliverable boundary inversion), and C252 Finding 04 (deliverable polish calibration as client perception inversion) — all of which addressed deliverable quality perception, not stylometric authorship identification as a specific detection mechanism with its own failure mode.

**Finding 2: AI governance questionnaires as competitive intelligence instruments with undisclosed methodology disclosure consequences — distinct from all prior client questionnaire findings**
Client-sent AI governance frameworks function as methodology due diligence when the response requires describing analytical approach, tool configuration, and review protocols. The disclosure is made to a party with ongoing commercial incentive to use the information. Firms treat the response as a compliance exercise, not a competitive strategy decision. No Geneva advisory service has framed the questionnaire response as a competitive intelligence disclosure with commercial consequence. This is distinct from C265 Finding 01 (client AI governance questionnaires arriving before response capability), C264 Finding 04 (client AI questionnaire response as contractual accountability representation), and C263 Finding 04 (client AI questionnaire response as commercial representation creating contractual accountability outside Swiss regulatory framework) — all of which addressed the questionnaire as a compliance or contractual question, not as a competitive intelligence instrument with undisclosed methodology transfer consequences.

**Finding 3: AI-adjacent liability event accumulation as portfolio-level exposure management problem distinct from individual incident management — distinct from all prior exposure aggregation findings**
Each AI-assisted engagement produces minor events — client questions about recommendations, documentation gaps, near-misses, questionnaire responses. Individually, each appears manageable. Across an active engagement portfolio, the aggregate is a compounding undisclosed liability position. No Geneva consulting firm is tracking AI-adjacent events across the portfolio as a cumulative exposure. The aggregation function is structurally absent from boutique-scale risk management. This is distinct from C266 Finding 04 (engagement letter obsolescence as accumulating undisclosed liability) and C265 Finding 05 (proposal shelf-life collapse as active documented position exposure) — both of which addressed specific accumulating categories, not the general principle that AI-adjacent events across the portfolio constitute a cumulative exposure requiring portfolio-level management methodology.

**Finding 4: Contract AI governance commitment gap at boutique scale — the committing party and the implementing party are the same person — distinct from all prior engagement letter and sub-delegation findings**
Client contracts with embedded AI governance commitments are being negotiated and signed without operational review of delivery capacity. At boutique scale, the MD who negotiates the commercial terms is the same person who manages the engagement. There is no structural separation between commercial negotiation and governance delivery review. The gap is not about engagement letter language — it is about the organizational structure of small firms creating a specific failure mode where governance commitments are made by the person who cannot independently assess whether they are achievable. This is distinct from C266 Finding 04 (engagement letter obsolescence) and C265 Finding 06 (attestation without comprehension cascade) — which addressed documentation adequacy and comprehension gaps, not the specific structural failure of non-separated commercial and operational governance review at boutique scale.

**Finding 5: Time-based billing structurally mismeasures AI governance overhead cost, creating pricing model incentive to under-invest in governance — distinct from all prior billing model and efficiency findings**
Time-based billing captures AI efficiency gains as margin improvement. AI governance documentation is unbillable overhead. Firms adopting AI under time-based pricing are absorbing governance costs into margins without visibility, allocation, or recovery. The pricing model creates a structural incentive to minimize AI governance investment because the cost appears as overhead reduction, not as an accumulation of undisclosed liability. No Geneva advisory service has framed time-based billing as a structural cause of AI governance under-investment rather than an independent financial efficiency question. This is distinct from C265 Finding 05 (billing model structurally misaligned with AI governance documentation requirements — which addressed the structural incompatibility between billing incentives and documentation requirements, not the specific mechanism by which the pricing model creates incentive to under-invest in governance as a cost-avoidance strategy), and C257 Finding 01 (AI efficiency gain capture asymmetry which addressed client/competitive capture of dividends, not internal pricing model incentives).

**Finding 6: Geneva consulting accountability vacuum as structural liability amplifier with no professional body equivalent — distinct from all prior peer circuit and accountability vacuum findings**
Geneva consulting firms have no equivalent accountability infrastructure to what legal services have through the bar, or financial services through SFAMA/SBA. The absence means consulting firms are exposed to AI governance risks without the protective professional framework that creates accountability standards, publishes guidance, and provides safe-harbor mechanisms. The vacuum is not merely an absence of support — it is a structural amplifier of liability because firms are operating without a reference standard that defines adequate governance. No Geneva advisory or publication has framed the absence of a professional accountability framework as a liability amplifier rather than a market opportunity. This is distinct from C251 Finding 03 (Geneva consulting peer accountability vacuum as named structural market failure) and C252 Finding 06 (peer circuit performing reassurance without information content) — both of which named the peer circuit failure without isolating the specific mechanism by which the absence of professional infrastructure amplifies rather than merely fails to protect.
