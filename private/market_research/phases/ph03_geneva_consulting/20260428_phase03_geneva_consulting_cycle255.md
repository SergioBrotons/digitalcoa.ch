## 01 — ICP Language

**How This ICP Actually Describes AI Problems**

---

### English

The managing director of a small Geneva consulting firm (2–15 employees, or boutique up to 50–200) describes AI challenges in terms of client deliverables, proposal quality, and personal professional exposure. The framing is operational and commercial, not technical.

**How the ICP describes AI problems:**

- *"We're using AI to produce proposals faster, but clients are starting to ask questions we can't answer cleanly."*
- *"The quality of our deliverables has gone up, but I don't always know exactly what produced what."*
- *"My senior people are signing off on work they couldn't replicate if asked to explain the methodology."*
- *"If a client challenges a recommendation six months after delivery, I need to be able to show how it was built."*
- *"I'm personally on the line for what goes to clients. I need to know what's happening with AI in my firm."*
- *"We need AI to stay competitive on price and speed, but I need to be able to defend our work if something goes wrong."*
- *"The insurance renewal asks about AI. I sign it. But I don't know if what I'm signing actually covers what we're doing."*
- *"When clients send AI governance questionnaires, we don't have clean answers for many of the questions."*

**How the ICP describes what they need:**

- A way to maintain competitive proposal production without accumulating exposure
- Client deliverable documentation that would protect them in a challenge situation
- Internal quality control that produces defensible evidence, not just internal confidence
- A clear personal discharge mechanism for the MD's signature on AI-related attestations
- A methodology for knowing what AI contributed to any given deliverable at any given time

**What the ICP does NOT say:**

- Does not frame in terms of nFADP Articles or regulatory compliance
- Does not use data protection vocabulary
- Does not describe governance in organizational structure terms
- Does not reference FINMA or financial regulatory frameworks
- Describes the problem in commercial and liability terms, not regulatory terms

## 02 — Market Signals

**Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints**

---

### 1. The Bid Defense Documentation Gap

When a won proposal is later challenged by the client's procurement team or a subsequent audit, the consulting firm must reconstruct the analytical methodology including AI contribution. The problem is not that firms don't have records — it is that they don't have records that would survive reconstruction. AI-generated content enters proposals without methodology documentation at point of creation. When defense is required, firms discover the provenance trail doesn't exist. The failure mode is structural: the documentation that would protect them was never created because the problem wasn't named at the time the work was done.

This is distinct from the proposal version archaeology problem named in prior cycles (C251/C252). Version archaeology concerns what the firm said at prior moments. The bid defense problem concerns how to demonstrate what analytical methodology produced the work — the reasoning chain, not the version state.

### 2. Deliverable Version Attribution Failure with Multi-Tool Chains

When a single client deliverable passes through multiple AI tools across iterations — drafting tool, analysis engine, formatting layer, translation interface — no Geneva boutique has a methodology for tracking which model version produced which component of which version of the deliverable. The MD cannot represent the firm's methodology state at time of transmission because the version chain was never documented. This creates a specific gap: every client deliverable transmitted in the past 12–18 months exists in a state of unresolved provenance ambiguity. The problem becomes active when clients begin asking which AI tools were used in preparation — questions that are arriving now through AI governance questionnaires.

This is distinct from the proposal shelf-life problem (C251/C252) and the configuration inheritance problem (C245/C246/C251). It is specific to the multi-tool deliverable chain with version state uncertainty at each handoff.

### 3. Consulting Methodology Provenance as Emerging Client Demand

Sophisticated clients are moving from asking what the deliverable contains to asking how the analytical framework was constructed — training sources, search methodology, synthesis logic, version state at key decision points. The question is not whether AI was used, but how the firm's analytical methodology was constructed and what evidence exists for it. This represents a structural shift in client expectation: from output quality to process documentation. No Geneva consulting boutique has a methodology for responding to this type of methodology audit question. The failure mode is not poor quality — it is the absence of a documentation architecture that would allow the question to be answered.

This is distinct from client AI sophistication outpacing firm preparation (C249/C251). The specific new element is methodology provenance — the construction of the analytical framework itself, not just the output.

### 4. AI Quality Variance Creating Deliverable Vulnerability

More AI-dense proposals are producing higher-polish outputs that are more likely to pass initial client screening. But this same dynamic is creating deliverables that are more vulnerable to deep-dive client scrutiny. The quality signals that pass the initial review are precisely the signals that reveal AI-dominant authorship when examined closely. The paradox: efficiency gains produce proposals that look better and are structurally more fragile when clients probe the methodology. The boutique faces a structural problem where the visible quality improvement and the underlying vulnerability increase simultaneously.

This is distinct from the deliverable polish calibration problem named in C251/C252. That framing concerned client perception of AI-dominant authorship. This framing concerns the specific mechanism by which quality improvement creates vulnerability — a dynamic unnamed in the Geneva market.

### 5. Senior Review as Structural Formalization Without Substantive Capacity

The senior reviewer in a Geneva consulting boutique is asked to review AI-assisted work product. The reviewer has no AI evaluation methodology. The review produces a signature and a dated approval record. The formal record looks like adequate review. The substantive review cannot be demonstrated. The carrier's 2026 third-generation questionnaire probes four evidence categories including review substance — the specific gap is that formalization has outpaced substantive capacity. The MD signs what the senior reviewed, not what the senior understood.

This is distinct from the attestation without comprehension problem (C251/C252/C254). The specific new element is the carrier's systematic probing of review substance — the formal review infrastructure is being tested in a way that exposes the structural disconnect between formal review and substantive evaluation capacity.

### 6. Client AI Governance Questionnaires Creating Commercial Leverage Independently of Regulatory Obligation

When a sophisticated client sends an AI governance questionnaire as part of vendor due diligence, the firm must respond. The response creates contractual representations. The questionnaire is not a regulatory instrument. The representations are. A response that misrepresents the firm's AI governance practices creates commercial liability in the client relationship — liability that operates independently of Swiss data protection or regulatory compliance frameworks. The Geneva consulting boutique has no framework for evaluating what representations it can make, what it should document before responding, and what response commitments it is prepared to defend.

This is distinct from the client-initiated AI addendum problem (C251/C252). That problem concerned client contract terms imposing warranty obligations. This problem concerns the questionnaire response as a commercial representation creating contractual accountability outside any formal regulatory framework.

### 7. The Geneva Consulting Peer Accountability Vacuum — Structural Properties

The Geneva consulting market has no professional body equivalent to the SVA (law) or SFAMA/AMAS (finance). The peer circuit that exists operates under conditions that structurally prevent information exchange at the moment it would be most useful:

- MDs with active AI governance problems are most exposed and most discouraged from discussing them openly
- The informal circuit handles what-is-happening without resolution capacity for what-cannot-be-explained
- Information exchange creates liability exposure for the sharing party with no reciprocal protection
- Most exposed MDs have least access to information that would help them — the classic circuit adverse selection problem
- No Geneva publication or forum serves the function of confidential peer calibration on specific AI incidents

This is distinct from the peer circuit reassurance problem named in C251/C252 — the specific new element is that the structural properties of the vacuum are now named and mappable, not just described as an absence.

### 8. AI Configuration Ownership as Unresolved Commercial Question

When a consulting boutique configures AI tools to produce client-specific deliverables, the question of what the configuration constitutes is unresolved. Is the configured methodology a firm trade secret? A client asset? A hybrid? Does the client who paid for the deliverable have any claim on the AI configuration that produced it? Does the firm have any obligation to document the configuration in a way that would survive tool migration or vendor wind-down? These questions have no published resolution in Swiss commercial law or professional practice frameworks. They are becoming commercially active as tool consolidation forces configuration migration and as clients begin asking configuration-specific questions.

### 9. Proposal AI Contamination Through Prior Engagement Material Absorption

AI tools trained on or referencing prior client engagement materials may produce content in subsequent proposals that contains fragments of previous analytical frameworks, without the firm knowing or disclosing this. As client-side AI detection tools become standard in procurement, contamination becomes a procurement risk: clients can detect stylistic or analytical similarities between proposals from different engagements that should be independent. The contamination is not a data breach — it is a methodology contamination invisible to the producing firm.

### 10. Swiss Practice Management Systems Architecturally Incompatible with AI Contribution Records

The file note and document management systems used by Geneva consulting boutiques are designed to track human authorship and editorial revisions. They do not track AI tool state, AI model version, AI-assisted synthesis steps, or multi-tool attribution chains. The structural gap means that even well-managed boutiques cannot produce a complete record of what AI contributed to which deliverable — not because they failed to implement a governance system, but because the available tools were designed for a different production model. The documentation hygiene problem is architectural, not behavioral.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Geneva/Swiss Specific Regulatory Obligations for Consultants**

---

### 1. Professional Liability and Mandate Obligations

Swiss Code of Obligations Art. 404 mandates that a mandate holder performs the mandate with the care required under the circumstances. For Geneva consulting boutiques, the question of whether AI-assisted work meets this standard is unexamined in published Swiss doctrine. The specific gap: the care required standard was written for human performance. AI-assisted performance introduces a standard that is structurally different — the mandate holder must be able to demonstrate the reasoning chain that produced the deliverable, not merely that the deliverable was produced. When a client challenges a recommendation, the mandate holder must be able to show how it was constructed. AI involvement without documented methodology creates a gap between the deliverable and the reasoning that produced it — a gap that may constitute a breach of Art. 404 even where the output itself is sound.

This is distinct from legal sector Art. 321 CP analysis. The mandate framework and the professional secrecy framework create different liability surfaces.

### 2. Professional Liability Insurance — AI Coverage Architecture

Swiss professional liability insurance policies were not written to address AI-assisted consulting. Coverage ambiguity is structural, not a policy drafting failure. The specific unresolved questions for Geneva consulting boutiques:

- Does the policy cover an error in an AI-assisted recommendation where the AI tool produced the analytical error?
- Does the policy cover a methodology failure — an error in the firm's reasoning process, not the output?
- Does the policy cover a bid defense failure — the inability to reconstruct methodology when a client challenges a recommendation?
- Does the policy distinguish between human-authored and AI-assisted work product in coverage limits?
- What notification obligations does the firm have when AI tools are involved in mandate delivery?

Carriers have not published clarification on any of these questions. The market is operating with coverage ambiguity that creates personal liability exposure for the MD on every engagement.

### 3. Geneva Commercial Register and Mandate Documentation

Geneva consulting firms operating under cantonal commercial register requirements must maintain records of their mandate activities. The documentation standard was defined before AI-assisted production existed. AI co-authorship creates specific questions about what constitutes the firm's mandate record:

- Does the record need to identify AI tools used in mandate delivery?
- Does the record need to document the methodology that produced the deliverable?
- What constitutes the authoritative version of an AI-assisted deliverable for commercial register purposes?
- Can AI-generated content in a deliverable be authenticated to the firm's own production?

No Geneva cantonal guidance addresses these questions. The compliance gap is structural.

### 4. Client Counterparty Questionnaire as De Facto Commercial Standard

Sophisticated clients — particularly multinationals and EU-connected entities — are sending AI governance questionnaires to consulting vendors as part of vendor due diligence. These questionnaires are not regulated instruments. They create commercial representations through the firm's response. The representations are binding contractually. The specific Geneva consulting exposure: responses made in good faith that do not accurately reflect the firm's AI governance state create misrepresentation liability in the commercial relationship, independently of any Swiss regulatory framework.

The compliance dimension: the firm must be able to make accurate representations about its AI governance practices. The gap is that most Geneva consulting boutiques cannot accurately represent their AI governance state because the documentation doesn't exist to do so.

### 5. VVCh — Swiss Association of Consultants Membership Obligations

Firms that are members of the Swiss Association of Consultants (VVCh) operate under professional standards that include recommendation and mandate documentation requirements. These standards were not written for AI co-authorship. The specific structural collision: VVCh standards require the consultant to demonstrate reasoning and methodology. AI-assisted recommendations without documented methodology cannot be demonstrated in the reasoning chain the standard requires. VVCh guidance does not address AI co-authorship or how to document AI-assisted methodology within the association's professional standards framework.

### 6. Swiss Data Protection Act (nFADP) — Limited Direct Application but Emerging Indirect Exposure

Geneva consulting boutiques have limited direct nFADP exposure compared to law firms (client confidentiality) or finance firms (financial data). However, indirect exposure is accumulating:

- Client lists and engagement metadata processed through AI tools may trigger nFADP obligations
- AI tools processing client project information constitute data processing under nFADP Art. 5
- The authorization basis for AI processing of client project data may not be documented
- Client AI governance questionnaires increasingly ask about data protection practices as part of vendor due diligence — responses create representations about nFADP compliance that must be accurate

The specific Geneva consulting nFADP exposure: the firm may be making representations about data protection compliance that cannot be substantiated because the AI tool data flows have not been mapped.

### 7. Independent Consultant Status and AI Tool Use

Geneva boutiques operating as independent consultants (Einzelunternehmen or GmbH) face personal professional liability for all mandate outputs regardless of AI tool involvement. The MD's signature on deliverables and proposals constitutes a personal warranty of methodology and quality. AI tool use that the MD cannot fully explain creates a specific personal liability exposure: the MD is personally warranting a methodology they do not fully understand and cannot fully document. No Geneva market participant offers a methodology for discharging this personal warranty obligation at boutique scale.
