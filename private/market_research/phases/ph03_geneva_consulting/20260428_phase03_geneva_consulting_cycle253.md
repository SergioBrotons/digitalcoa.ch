## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Client-initiated AI contract terms as contractual exposure before evaluation framework exists**
Clients are sending AI contract terms that consulting firms are signing without evaluation. The terms create contractual obligations for data processing, liability, and compliance certifications. No evaluation framework exists for assessing these terms before signature.

**Signal 2: Client AI disclosure question as reputation management problem**
Clients asking whether proposals are AI-generated creates a reputation management problem that most firms have not anticipated. The question surfaces the firm's AI use in a client relationship context before the approach has been designed.

**Signal 3: Workshop AI output without editorial review as deliverable quality gap**
AI-generated output in workshops goes directly to clients without editorial review. The quality gap between what the AI generates and what the firm would produce through its own editorial process is invisible to the client, but creates exposure when clients evaluate work product quality.

**Signal 4: Junior consultant AI tool use without senior evaluation architecture**
Junior consultants using AI tools that senior staff have not evaluated create an authorization gap. The deliverable production chain does not include a review of whether the tools being used are appropriate for the work being done.

**Signal 5: AI tool inventory questions as discovery mechanism for authorization gaps**
Carrier and client questions about AI tools are functioning as discovery mechanisms. Firms discover that they have not authorized the tools in use, that access controls are unclear, and that the connection between tools and client work has not been evaluated.

**Signal 6: Peer circuit reassurance without resolution as structural vacuum**
Peer conversations provide reassurance without resolution. The structural vacuum — no institutional mechanism for Geneva consulting firms to exchange governance intelligence — means that the reassurance is the only function the peer circuit serves.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Client AI contract terms evaluation framework**
No framework exists for evaluating client-initiated AI contract terms before signature. The framework would need to address liability allocation, data processing obligations, compliance certifications, and the structural implications of signing terms designed by the client without negotiation.

**Gap 2: Workshop AI output editorial review protocol**
No protocol exists for editorial review of AI-generated workshop output. The protocol would need to address the timing problem — workshop output is needed immediately, editorial review takes time — and the quality standard problem — what the firm's review adds to the AI-generated output.

**Gap 3: Junior consultant AI tool authorization architecture**
No architecture exists for authorizing AI tool use by junior staff with senior oversight. The architecture would need to address what tools are authorized for what work, what review is required before client delivery, and how the authorization is documented.

**Gap 4: AI tool inventory as authorization system, not documentation exercise**
The AI tool inventory question is not a documentation exercise — it is an authorization system design problem. The inventory should reflect which tools are authorized for which work, who has access, and how the authorization is kept current as tools change.

**Gap 5: Geneva consulting peer accountability institutional mechanism**
No institutional mechanism exists for Geneva consulting firms to exchange governance intelligence. The vacuum is structural. The peer circuit serves a social function; it does not serve a calibration function. No alternative mechanism has been created.

**Gap 6: Proposal AI content dating and notification obligation**
No framework exists for managing AI-assisted proposal content that becomes dated over time. The proposal aging problem — content written with AI assistance that the client still has and may still reference — creates an undisclosed material change exposure. No notification obligation framework exists.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Art. 321 CP professional secrecy and AI tool authorization:**
AI tools processing client information through non-Swiss processors create professional secrecy authorization questions under Art. 321 CP. The authorization is not addressed by privacy policies or by general AI governance frameworks. Boutique firms using cloud-based AI tools are operating with unresolved authorization questions.

**OR Article 1 contractual obligations in client-initiated AI terms:**
Client-initiated AI contract terms create OR Article 1 contractual obligations. The obligation to read and understand what is being signed applies to AI terms as to any other contract. The exposure is present-tense for every firm that has signed client AI terms without evaluation.

**PI insurance attestation architecture for AI-assisted deliverables:**
PI insurance attestations require firms to certify that they have processes for ensuring deliverable quality. AI-assisted deliverables without editorial review architecture may not meet the attestation requirements. The gap between the attestation and the actual process is a present-tense exposure.

**nFADP third-party processor obligations for consulting work:**
Consulting deliverables that incorporate AI-generated content may involve third-party processors. The nFADP Art. 9 processor obligations apply to these configurations. Boutique firms have not systematically evaluated which processors their AI tools use and what the authorization obligations are.

**Federal Council AI Convention end-2026 timeline and consulting sector:**
The Federal Council AI Convention legislative timeline creates retrospective documentation requirements for AI configurations. Consulting firms that used AI in client work without documenting configurations face exposure when the legislation arrives.

**Art. 398 CO warranty liability for AI-assisted consulting deliverables:**
The warranty of proper performance under Art. 398 CO applies to AI-assisted consulting deliverables. When AI-generated content in a deliverable contains errors or produces suboptimal outcomes, the warranty question is live. No framework exists for managing this exposure.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Client-initiated AI contract terms as OR Article 1 contractual exposure without evaluation framework**
Client-sent AI terms create OR Article 1 obligations to evaluate before signing. The exposure is present-tense for every firm that has signed client AI terms without a framework for assessment. The structural implication is that firms are committing to liability, data processing, and compliance obligations without understanding what they are signing.

**Finding 2: Workshop AI output without editorial review as deliverable quality architecture gap with real-time constraint**
Workshop AI output going directly to clients without editorial review creates a quality architecture gap. The real-time constraint — output is needed immediately — is used as a justification for skipping review. The justification works until a quality problem surfaces in a client conversation or a dispute.

**Finding 3: Junior consultant AI tool authorization gap as organizational exposure distinct from training problem**
Junior consultants using AI tools that senior staff have not authorized creates an organizational exposure. The problem is not a training gap — it is an authorization architecture gap. The firm has not designed who can use which tools for which work, and what review is required before client delivery.

**Finding 4: AI tool inventory as authorization system design problem, not documentation exercise**
The AI tool inventory question is an authorization system design problem, not a documentation exercise. The inventory should reflect which tools are authorized for which work, who has access, and how the authorization is kept current. Most firms have a documentation list; none have an authorization system.

**Finding 5: Proposal AI content aging as undisclosed material change with notification obligation gap**
AI-assisted proposal content becomes dated without notification to the client. The client retains proposal content that may no longer reflect the firm's current position or capabilities. The undisclosed material change creates exposure that has no notification framework in current client relationship management practice.

**Finding 6: Geneva consulting peer accountability vacuum as structural condition with no market solution in development**
The peer accountability vacuum is a structural condition, not a market supply gap. No market participant is developing a solution because the silence is rational — exchanging governance intelligence has competitive implications that make open exchange costly. The condition persists without a solution in development.
