## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Deliverable provenance opacity as professional liability question**
The consultant cannot trace which parts of a deliverable were AI-generated versus their own reasoning. This is no longer a quality control concern. It is a professional liability question. The consultant is responsible for deliverables they cannot fully account for.

**Signal 2: Client methodology questions in competitive tendering as selection criterion**
Sophisticated clients are treating AI methodology answers as a selection criterion, not just a compliance question. The firm that cannot answer the methodology question credibly is being filtered out of competitive tendering at the shortlist stage.

**Signal 3: AI vendor sustainability as client matter continuity risk**
The AI vendor used to produce client deliverables may exit the market, change terms, or be acquired. The firm that has built client deliverables on a specific AI tool has a continuity risk that has not been named as such.

**Signal 4: Junior-senior competence inversion undermining review quality**
Juniors are more AI-proficient than the seniors who review their work. The seniors are supervising AI-assisted work they cannot evaluate. The review process is producing false assurance — it looks like supervision, it is not supervision.

**Signal 5: Competitive pressure to adopt AI creating governance shortcuts**
The competitive pressure to deploy AI quickly is creating governance shortcuts. Tools are being adopted without documentation of how they interact, without clear accountability assignment, without supervision protocol. The shortcuts are accumulating into structural exposure.

**Signal 6: Deliverable accountability chain from client to consultant to AI vendor undefined**
No one has mapped the accountability chain from client to consultant to AI vendor when a deliverable fails. The consultant is accountable to the client. The AI vendor is not accountable to the consultant in any structured way. The gap in the accountability chain is the structural liability.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Deliverable provenance documentation system for consulting**
A practical documentation system that creates a traceable record of what AI contributed to each deliverable, proportionate to boutique consulting scale, does not exist. The large-firm approaches are not implementable.

**Gap 2: Client methodology response template for competitive tendering**
A template the boutique consultancy can use to answer AI methodology questions in tendering — credibly, specifically, without overcommitting — does not exist. The gap between "we use AI" and "here is our methodology" is unaddressed.

**Gap 3: AI vendor sustainability assessment for client matter continuity**
A methodology for assessing AI vendor sustainability and its implications for client matter continuity does not exist. The continuity risk is unnamed and unmanaged.

**Gap 4: Junior-senior competence inversion supervision framework**
A supervision framework designed for the inverted reality — where the junior is more capable with AI than the senior who reviews them — does not exist. The existing supervision model assumes the opposite.

**Gap 5: Accountability chain mapping from client to AI vendor**
A mapping of the accountability chain from client to consultant to AI vendor, identifying where the chain has gaps and what those gaps mean for the consultant's exposure, does not exist.

**Gap 6: Methodology articulation as business development differentiator**
A framework for using AI methodology articulation as a business development differentiator — not as a compliance burden — does not exist. The competitive upside of methodology clarity is unaddressed.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Swiss professional liability framework:**
Swiss consultancies face professional liability exposure when AI-assisted deliverables contain errors that a properly supervised professional would have caught. The supervision adequacy question is acute when the supervisor cannot evaluate the AI component.

**nFADP application to consulting work product:**
Client work product processed through AI tools constitutes personal data processing under nFADP. The consultancy's obligations regarding client data flowing through AI systems have not been clearly mapped for the boutique context.

**FINMA-adjacent obligations for financial sector clients:**
Consultancies serving financial sector clients may be pulled into their clients' regulatory accountability chains. A consultancy serving a Geneva wealth management firm may be part of that firm's AI governance accountability structure.

**Multi-party AI tool data flow compliance:**
When multiple AI tools are in simultaneous use, each tool's data processing terms create obligations. The cumulative multi-party data environment is an unmanaged compliance surface at boutique consulting scale.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Deliverable provenance opacity as professional liability question**
The consultant cannot trace which parts of a deliverable were AI-generated versus their own reasoning. This is a professional liability question, not just a quality control concern. The consultant is responsible for deliverables they cannot fully account for.

**Finding 2: Client methodology questions in tendering as selection criterion**
The methodology question in competitive tendering is a proxy for governance maturity assessment. The firm that cannot answer credibly is being filtered out at the shortlist stage. This is a business development problem, not just a compliance problem.

**Finding 3: AI vendor sustainability as client matter continuity risk unnamed**
The AI vendor used for client deliverables may exit the market or change terms. The continuity risk is unnamed and unmanaged. When the vendor changes terms or becomes unavailable, the firm's client deliverables are affected without a contingency.

**Finding 4: Junior-senior competence inversion producing false supervision assurance**
The review process looks like supervision. It is not supervision because the senior cannot evaluate the AI component. False assurance is being produced at scale in every firm where this dynamic exists.

**Finding 5: Accountability chain from client to AI vendor has structural gaps**
The consultant is accountable to the client. The AI vendor is not accountable to the consultant in any structured way. The accountability chain has gaps that no one has mapped or addressed.

**Finding 6: Methodology articulation as both liability reduction and business development differentiator**
The firm that can credibly articulate its AI methodology has both a liability reduction (it can demonstrate adequate governance) and a business development asset (it can answer the methodology question in tendering credibly). The two benefits are simultaneous and compounding.
