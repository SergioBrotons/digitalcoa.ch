## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

### 2.1 The Undocumented Defensively Chain: Prompt to Client Signature

When a deliverable goes to a client, the MD cannot reconstruct the chain from initial prompt to final output. What was the prompt? What model version was active? What instructions were given about tone, scope, framing? What edits were made before sending? The chain is not documented, and in most cases was never consciously designed — it evolved through daily use without governance review. When a client asks how a recommendation was reached, the MD cannot answer with reference to a documented analytical process. When something in the deliverable is wrong, the firm cannot reconstruct what produced the error because the chain that led to it was never recorded. This is not a documentation failure — it is a structural absence: no one built the chain to be recorded.

Market signal: no Geneva consulting boutique has a documented defensibility chain architecture for AI-assisted deliverables. The prompt-to-delivery chain is the accountability infrastructure that doesn't exist.

### 2.2 Internal AI Governance Surface Operating Without Client Transparency

Geneva boutiques use AI for internal research, analytical support, and knowledge management — work that does not travel to clients in raw form but shapes the deliverables that do. The internal AI use is governed by no one: the same person who uses AI for client work uses it for internal analysis without a boundary between the two surfaces. The internal surface generates outputs the MD is accountable for through client deliverables, but those internal outputs have never been reviewed against professional accountability standards, never been checked against client confidentiality requirements, and never been documented as part of the firm's governance architecture. The MD is accountable for something they have not reviewed, in a process they have not observed.

Market signal: internal AI governance is not on the governance agenda at any Geneva boutique. The internal surface is treated as outside accountability because no client sees it, even though its outputs reach clients through deliverables.

### 2.3 Vendor Dependency as Operational Continuity Risk Structurally Unmanaged

A boutique that has been using AI tools for two to three years has built significant operational capability into the vendor's infrastructure: custom workflows, accumulated prompt libraries, firm-specific process configurations, institutional knowledge embedded in tool behavior. The vendor is not obligated to maintain continuity of service, to notify clients of ownership changes, or to preserve the boutique's configurations if the platform changes. The boutique has no contractual protection for the operational continuity of its AI-dependent processes. If the vendor fails, is acquired, changes terms, or discontinues the service, the boutique faces an operational disruption it has not planned for and cannot recover from quickly. No Geneva boutique has a vendor continuity plan.

Market signal: the vendor dependency is treated as a cost of doing business, not as a named operational continuity risk with a management plan.

### 2.4 Scope Creep Through AI Efficiency Structurally Invisible Because It Is Free

AI makes it possible to produce more than what was contracted without explicit decision. A proposal that was scoped at three months extends to six months because the tool identified additional considerations, deeper analysis paths, supplementary research. None of this was approved. The client receives more than they contracted for. The firm bills for additional time or delivers at the same price with higher margin — depending on billing model. The MD may not notice that scope has expanded because AI-assisted efficiency makes expansion free. The client may not notice because they received more quality than expected. But when something goes wrong — when the extended analytical direction produces a recommendation the client questions, or when the firm cannot reconstruct how the expanded scope was determined — the structural invisibility of AI-generated scope creep becomes an accountability problem.

Market signal: no Geneva boutique has a scope monitoring protocol for AI-assisted engagements. Scope creep is invisible because AI makes it costless to over-deliver.

### 2.5 Cross-Client Intelligence Segregation Anxiety as Emerging Client Question

Sophisticated clients are beginning to ask whether their strategic intelligence — shared with the firm for analytical purposes — flows through the same AI infrastructure as the firm's work for competing or adjacent clients. The question is not merely theoretical: AI tools that learn from all inputs they process may inadvertently incorporate one client's strategic intelligence into responses generated for another client. The vendor cannot provide contractual assurance that this has not occurred, because model improvement processes are not transparent. The firm's engagement letters do not address this scenario. The boutique that cannot answer the question credibly is carrying a client relationship risk that has no internal governance solution.

Market signal: the cross-client intelligence segregation question is arriving in client conversations before any Geneva boutique has a documented answer. The question surfaces in trust-sensitive relationships before formal contractual negotiation.

### 2.6 Carrier Attestation Pre-Signing Review as Structurally Absent Geneva Market Service

MDs are signing carrier attestation forms that commit them to personal liability for AI governance adequacy. The forms are written by carrier legal teams. The MD who signs has not had independent legal review of what the attestation commits them to personally, what coverage exists if the attested-to situation occurs, or what constitutes adequate discharge of the attested obligation. The signing decision is made under time pressure at policy renewal, reviewed by the MD alone without counsel. The attestation is functioning as a personal legal instrument with professional liability consequences, and no Geneva market service offers pre-signing review of carrier AI attestation forms as a discrete service.

Market signal: carrier attestation review is not available as a discrete service in the Geneva market. MDs are signing personal liability instruments without legal review of what they commit to.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Specific Regulatory Obligations for Consultants

### Professional Liability Under Art. 28 CO and the Defensively Chain Imperative

Art. 28 CO establishes professional liability for error in the performance of mandated services. The consulting firm's obligation runs through the deliverable to the client regardless of how the deliverable was produced. The MD who signs a deliverable is committing that the work met professional standards — that the analytical process was sound, the recommendations were reached through appropriate means, and the output reflects the firm's professional judgment. When the chain from prompt to delivery is undocumented, this commitment cannot be substantiated. The firm cannot demonstrate that professional standards were met because the process was not recorded. The MD who signs is exposed personally for a professional liability commitment that cannot be backed with evidence of what was done.

### Internal AI Use and Client Confidentiality Under nFADP

When AI tools are used for internal research and knowledge management, client-confidential information may enter the tool's processing infrastructure through internal workflow. The nFADP data controller obligations of the firm apply to any processing of client data, regardless of whether the processing produces client-visible outputs. Internal analytical work containing client-confidential information is still client data. The firm that has not assessed what client information enters its internal AI workflows, and whether those workflows comply with nFADP data controller obligations, is operating with an undocumented compliance exposure that surfaces as a client question or a data subject request before the firm knows it exists.

### Swiss AI Convention Accountability and the Internal Governance Surface

The Council of Europe AI Convention, signed by Switzerland, creates accountability obligations that operate through the firm's professional liability standards, client contracts, and carrier terms. The accountability applies to the firm's AI use in all contexts — not only to client-visible outputs. The boutique that has reviewed client-facing AI governance but not internal AI use is partially compliant with Convention obligations: the internal governance surface is still subject to the same accountability framework, and the firm remains responsible for it through the same private law mechanisms. The Convention does not distinguish between internal and external AI use surfaces for accountability purposes.

### Vendor Continuity and the Fiduciary Continuity Question

The boutique that has built operational capability into vendor infrastructure has a structural continuity risk it has not named. When the firm's analytical processes, institutional knowledge, and workflow configurations exist inside a vendor's system, the firm's operational continuity depends on the vendor's operational continuity. If the vendor fails, is acquired, or changes terms, the firm's delivery capacity is impaired. This is a fiduciary continuity question: the firm has an obligation to deliver against client commitments, and the vendor dependency creates a risk to that obligation that has not been addressed through contract, architecture, or planning. The engagement letter does not address vendor dependency as a delivery risk factor.

### Carrier Attestation as Personal Legal Instrument Without Pre-Signing Review Architecture

Carrier AI attestation forms are legal instruments signed by the MD personally. The forms commit the signatory to representations about the firm's AI governance adequacy. The representations have personal liability consequences if the attested-to situation occurs and the carrier determines the attestation was inaccurate. No Geneva market service offers pre-signing review of these forms as a discrete service. The MD who signs without review is committing to personal liability for representations written by the carrier's legal team, without independent counsel reviewing what the commitment actually covers.

### The Stacking Problem (Operative Across All Active Obligations)

The Geneva consulting boutique is simultaneously subject to: professional liability under Art. 28 CO with an undocumented defensibility chain, nFADP data controller obligations for client data in internal AI workflows, Swiss AI Convention accountability through private law operating on both internal and client-facing AI surfaces, vendor continuity risk in AI-dependent operational capability, and carrier attestation obligations with personal liability consequences. These obligations arrive simultaneously. None was designed for the boutique consulting firm's structure. No Geneva protocol exists for managing them coherently.

## 06 — Findings Summary: 6 Net-New Insights for C272 Phase 03

**Finding 01 — Undocumented prompt-to-delivery defensibility chain as primary accountability infrastructure absence at boutique consulting scale**
The chain from analytical prompt through model version to client-delivered output is not documented and was never designed to be documented. When a client questions a recommendation or an error surfaces in a deliverable, the MD cannot reconstruct what produced the output: the prompt instructions, model version, output parameters, and edits made before sending are not recorded. The chain is not a documentation failure — it is structurally absent because no protocol exists for building it. This is distinct from the deliverable version staleness finding (C271 Finding 02) which addressed the analytical framework staleness created by model updates over time. This finding addresses the specific structural absence of a documented accountability chain from prompt initiation to client delivery — the reconstructability problem exists in the present tense, not only at model version transitions, and applies to every AI-assisted deliverable the firm has produced.

**Finding 02 — Internal AI governance surface operating as unmanaged accountability domain with no client transparency but full professional accountability**
Geneva boutiques use AI for internal research and knowledge management — outputs that do not travel to clients in raw form but shape every client deliverable. This internal surface is not governed by anything: no review of what client data enters internal workflows, no assessment of whether internal AI outputs meet the accountability standards applicable to client-facing work, no mapping of nFADP obligations for internal processing of client information. The MD is professionally accountable for internal AI outputs through client deliverables, but those internal outputs have never been reviewed against any accountability standard. Distinct from the institutional knowledge contamination finding (C270 Finding 06) which addressed the capability dependency on vendor infrastructure. This finding addresses the specific accountability vacuum on the internal AI use surface — the governance surface that is invisible to clients but whose outputs reach clients through deliverables.

**Finding 03 — Vendor continuity risk as fiduciary-level operational exposure structurally unmanaged at boutique scale**
A boutique that has built two to three years of operational capability inside a vendor's infrastructure — custom workflows, prompt libraries, institutional process configurations — faces a fiduciary continuity problem distinct from general vendor risk. If the vendor fails, is acquired, or changes terms, the firm cannot meet client commitments at the level its delivery track record would suggest. Distinct from the AI tool lock-in finding (C270 Finding 04) which named the strategic liability of accumulated capability in vendor infrastructure. This finding specifically names the fiduciary continuity dimension: the firm's obligation to deliver against client commitments is at risk because the delivery infrastructure sits outside firm control, and no protocol exists for managing this risk.

**Finding 04 — AI-assisted scope creep structurally invisible because efficiency gains make over-delivery costless and therefore untracked**
When AI is used in client engagements, it produces additional analytical depth, supplementary research, and expanded consideration paths without an explicit scope decision. The additional output is delivered — either as additional billable time or at the same contracted price with higher margin — without anyone deciding that scope had expanded. The over-delivery is structurally invisible because AI makes it free: no additional senior time was consumed, no visible cost was incurred, no approval was sought or withheld. The result is that scope tracking — a fundamental commercial management function — has no mechanism for detecting AI-assisted expansion. When something surfaces the discrepancy between contracted and delivered scope, the firm cannot reconstruct who approved the expansion, whether it was intentional, or what it cost to produce. Distinct from the client expectation recalibration finding (C271 Finding 03) which addressed the irreversibility of elevated client expectations once calibrated. This finding addresses the specific mechanism of structurally invisible scope expansion through costless AI-assisted over-delivery, and the absence of any monitoring protocol for this phenomenon.

Distinct from the competitive intelligence confidentiality contamination finding (C270 Finding 01) which addressed client data flowing into vendor infrastructure through competitive intelligence analysis. This finding addresses the specific cross-client contamination concern: that model improvement processes may cause one client's intelligence to appear in outputs generated for another client, which is a structurally different exposure from the client-data-entering-vendor-infrastructure framing.

**Finding 06 — Carrier attestation pre-signing review as structurally absent Geneva market service creating personal liability exposure without discharge pathway**
Carrier AI attestation forms are legal instruments signed by the MD personally, committing to representations about the firm's AI governance adequacy with personal liability consequences. These forms are written by carrier legal teams and presented for signature at policy renewal under time pressure. No Geneva market service offers pre-signing review of carrier AI attestation forms as a discrete, time-bounded service. The MD who signs without review is committing to personal liability for representations written by the counterparty's legal team, without independent counsel assessing what the commitment covers, what coverage exists if the attested-to situation occurs, or what constitutes adequate discharge of the attested obligation. The structural absence of this service means the market has no mechanism for MDs to obtain this review before signing. Distinct from the carrier attestation as personal liability instrument naming (C260 Phase 03 Finding 06 and C266 Phase 01 Finding 06) which identified the instrument and its personal liability consequences. This finding specifically names the absence of pre-signing review as a service category — the problem is not merely that the attestation creates personal liability, but that no Geneva market service exists to help MDs understand what they are signing before they sign it.
