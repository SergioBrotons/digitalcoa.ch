## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### Real Language — How the ICP Describes AI Problems

- "We have been using AI for over a year. I cannot reconstruct which client work was AI-assisted and which was not. That is a problem when a client asks." — AI attribution failure across client work product.

- "Our team uses three AI tools. The juniors use them more than the seniors. The seniors do not know what the juniors are doing with AI on client matters." — Internal AI literacy disconnect between partners and staff.

- "We won business by saying we use AI. We are now being asked to demonstrate what that means in practice. We do not have a good answer." — AI governance as commercial claim without operational substance.

- "I have been using AI so long now that I cannot tell if my work product is still mine or if it has been shaped by the AI in ways I do not notice." — Professional judgment authorship anxiety. Continuous AI use changing the nature of professional judgment without awareness.

- "When the client sends us AI-generated analysis and asks us to comment on it, we are putting our name on something we did not produce." — Client-supplied AI content entering the firm's workflow without a verification layer. The firm's name on content the firm cannot vouch for.

- "Our engagement letters were written before AI. They do not say anything about AI. I do not know if that creates an exposure." — Engagement letter obsolescence as a legal exposure.

## 02 — Market Signals

1. **AI attribution failure is a present-tense client relationship exposure** — When clients ask which work was AI-assisted, the firm cannot answer with confidence. This is not a documentation problem — it is a client trust problem.

2. **Internal AI literacy disconnect between partners and staff creates accountability gaps** — Partners are accountable for work product they did not generate and cannot fully describe. Staff use AI tools the partners do not understand. The accountability and the knowledge are misaligned.

3. **AI governance as commercial claim is outpacing operational substance** — Firms that marketed AI capability to win business are now being asked to demonstrate what that means. The gap between commercial positioning and actual practice is a commercial and legal exposure.

4. **Professional judgment authorship anxiety is a genuine professional concern with no resolution** — The experienced consultant who has used AI for extended periods cannot confidently distinguish their judgment from AI-shaped judgment. This concern is real and unaddressed.

5. **Client-supplied AI content entering firm workflow creates liability without a verification layer** — When clients send AI-generated content for the firm to review and comment on, the firm is putting its name on work it did not produce and cannot verify. The exposure is structural.

6. **Engagement letter obsolescence creates legal exposure the firm does not know it carries** — Engagement letters written before AI deployment do not address AI use, liability for AI-assisted errors, or the firm's obligations when AI is used on client matters. This gap is present-tense and unaddressed.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### What No One Is Addressing for Geneva Boutique Consulting

1. **AI attribution documentation system for client work product** — A methodology for documenting which client work was AI-assisted, at what intensity, and with what human review. The client expects this information on request.

2. **Internal AI literacy bridging for partner accountability** — A framework for partners who do not use AI tools to maintain appropriate accountability for AI-assisted work product their staff produces.

3. **AI governance claim operationalization support** — Firms that have commercialized AI governance as a claim need a way to operationalize that claim before a client or counterparty asks them to demonstrate it.

4. **Client-supplied AI content verification intake layer** — A defined process for receiving AI-generated content from clients, reviewing it with appropriate verification, and putting the firm's name on it responsibly.

5. **Engagement letter AI clause architecture** — A methodology for reviewing and updating engagement letters to address AI use, liability allocation, and the firm's obligations on AI-assisted matters.

6. **Professional judgment authorship assessment for senior consultants** — A methodology for senior consultants to assess and document the relationship between their professional judgment and AI tools they have used over extended periods.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations for Boutique Consulting

1. **nFADP purpose limitation for AI tool client data processing** — Client data processed by AI tools must match the documented purpose. The purpose in the engagement letter and the actual AI use must be consistent.

2. **OR Art. 398 professional mandate standard** — The standard of care for AI-assisted work must meet the professional mandate standard. The question of what "reasonable AI-assisted professional work" looks like is unresolved.

3. **Client-supplied content supervisory obligation** — When clients supply AI-generated content for the firm to review and sign, the firm has a supervisory obligation that is not clearly defined by any professional standard.

4. **Engagement letter AI clause adequacy** — Current engagement letters do not address AI. The adequacy of silence on AI is an open legal question.

5. **Insurance PL coverage for AI-assisted professional work** — Professional liability insurance coverage for AI-assisted errors is not clearly defined by any insurer. The coverage question is active.

6. **AI tool vendor contract review for client data processing** — The vendor contract provisions on data retention, model training, and client data access create obligations the firm has to its clients that the engagement letter does not address.
