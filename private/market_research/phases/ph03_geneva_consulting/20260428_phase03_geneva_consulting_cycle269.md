- "I signed off on adopting AI tools. I did not sign off on how they were actually being used six months later."

- "We have no reference point for what 'good enough' looks like. There's no ORDERRE or SVA for consulting AI governance."

- "When someone leaves, the AI configuration they built leaves with them. And I can't always reconstruct what it was doing."

The language is about control, accountability, and the gap between what was decided at the top and what happens at the working level.

## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

### 2.1 The Implementation-Authorization Divergence

The managing director approved AI adoption. Six months later, the tools are being used in ways the MD did not authorize and may not be able to describe. This is not a documentation gap — it is an authorization gap: the approval covers a decision made at a point in time, while tool usage evolves continuously. The gap surfaces when a client question, a carrier inquiry, or a personnel departure forces a reconstruction of what the firm actually authorized versus what is actually happening. Geneva boutiques are discovering this gap reactively, at the worst moment.

Market signal: firms that deployed AI in 2023–2024 are now unable to produce an accurate description of their current AI usage scope. The gap between signed adoption and operational reality is unnamed and unmanaged.

### 2.2 The Recommendation Attribution Problem

A client questions a recommendation. The boutique cannot determine whether the concern pertains to the analytical framework, the underlying data, or the AI tool that contributed to the recommendation. This is not a communication failure — it is a structural attribution failure: the delivery mechanism (AI-assisted) has conflated multiple causation pathways that the firm is professionally accountable for distinguishing. When the MD cannot decompose a recommendation into its contributing components, client skepticism cannot be addressed with targeted evidence. The entire recommendation stands or falls together.

Market signal: as client sophistication with AI increases, the attribution question is arriving more frequently. Firms have no methodology for answering it.

### 2.3 The Billing-Governance Structural Misalignment

The firm bills by time. AI reduces hours on drafting. Efficiency gains accrue as margin. AI governance overhead — documentation, verification, supervision, review — is unbillable. The pricing model structurally incentivizes minimizing governance investment: every hour spent on AI governance is an hour not billed to a client, with no revenue offset. The firm that invests heavily in AI governance carries a net cost the firm that does not does not. The competitive market does not currently reward governance investment in the pricing signal it sends to clients.

Market signal: no Geneva boutique is building governance overhead into their pricing model. The firms investing most in governance are absorbing the cost without a mechanism to recover it. This is not a short-term problem — it is a structural disequilibrium that will determine which firms can sustain governance investment over time.

### 2.4 The Personal Authority-Operational Reality Gap at Junior Level

The people with the most current AI knowledge in the firm are junior staff. The people with the most professional accountability are the MD or partner. The MD cannot supervise what they do not understand; the junior staff are making consequential AI decisions — tool selection, prompt construction, output interpretation — with no formal authority to make them and no governance architecture capturing their decisions. The authority and the knowledge are structurally misaligned.

Market signal: Geneva boutiques are not building junior-level AI governance protocols. The MD's lack of technical comprehension is treated as a fixed constraint rather than a structural design problem requiring a governance solution.

### 2.5 The Accountability Vacuum Without Professional Body Equivalent

Legal has the bar. Finance has FINMA and SFAMA. Consulting has no equivalent. The absence of a professional accountability standard for Geneva consulting firms is not merely a gap — it is a liability amplifier. The firm that behaves as if no standard applies faces no immediate corrective mechanism, but bears full exposure when something goes wrong. The absence is structurally permanent: no Geneva professional body is developing consulting AI governance standards. Firms are operating in a vacuum with no reference point for what adequate governance looks like, and no external feedback mechanism until something fails.

Market signal: carrier questionnaires are the closest thing to a standard. Firms are discovering that what carriers require and what they assumed was adequate are not the same thing.

### 2.6 The Small Incident Accumulation Problem

The firm has had seven AI-adjacent events in the past twelve months: a client question about how a recommendation was produced, a deliverable that contained an undetected AI error, a staff departure where AI configurations left with the person, a client counterparty questionnaire asking about AI methodology, a carrier question the MD could not answer precisely, a tool update that changed output characteristics, and a proposal where the analytical position had drifted from what the firm would have produced without AI. Individually manageable. Aggregately undisclosed. No Geneva boutique is tracking AI-adjacent events across their portfolio as a cumulative exposure. Each event is handled in isolation. The aggregate is invisible.

Market signal: firms discover the pattern when a carrier renewal or a client counterparty request forces a retrospective review. By then, the documentation does not exist.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis: Gaps, What No One Is Addressing

The Geneva consulting market for AI governance advisory is thin. Large strategy consultancies offer AI governance frameworks designed for enterprise clients — too elaborate for a 5-to-15-person boutique, too expensive for the engagement scale Geneva firms work at. Small local IT providers offer tool setup and security reviews — technically competent on infrastructure, not on professional liability or client accountability. The professional bodies (SVA for commercial advisors, Ordre professionnel for certain consultancies) have not published AI governance guidance for their members. No Geneva publication or advisory service has specifically addressed the boutique consulting firm's AI accountability problem at the right scale and at the MD's level of concern.

**The accountability vacuum without a professional body equivalent is treated as background context, not as the specific structural problem it is.** The amplification mechanism — absence of standard → absence of correction → full exposure on failure — is not named in any published Geneva content.

**The authorization-evolution gap (what was approved vs. what's happening now) is not addressed.** Most AI governance advice assumes a static deployment. The Geneva boutique has a dynamic deployment that has drifted from its authorization point, and no protocol for identifying or closing that gap.

### What No One Is Addressing

The combination of: personal professional liability at MD level + operational AI decisions made by junior staff + no reference standard for adequate governance + billing model that structurally underprices governance investment + client sophistication arriving faster than firm preparation. No Geneva market participant has built an advisory product that addresses all five simultaneously for the boutique consulting MD.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Specific Regulatory Obligations for Consultants

### Professional Accountability Landscape

Geneva consulting firms operate without a dedicated AI governance regulatory framework specifically applicable to their sector. The obligations that apply come from multiple directions simultaneously, and none of them were designed for the boutique consulting firm's structural reality.

### FINMA and Professional Liability

FINMA does not directly regulate consulting firms. However, professional liability insurers increasingly require AI governance documentation as a condition of coverage. The carrier questionnaire — now in its third generation — has become the de facto operative standard for AI governance adequacy at boutique scale, not because any authority designated it as such, but because it is the only external party asking specific questions with direct coverage consequences. The MD who cannot answer the carrier's questions precisely carries undisclosed exposure on two fronts: the AI governance gap and the coverage adequacy gap.

### Contract Law and Client Accountability

The Swiss Code of Obligations (Art. 398 and following) governs the mandate relationship. When AI contributes to a recommendation, the firm's obligation to the client runs through the final deliverable regardless of the production chain. The firm cannot disaggregate its liability by pointing to the AI tool that contributed to a problematic recommendation. The MD is accountable for the output, not merely for the process. This is not a disclosure problem — it is a liability architecture problem: the firm bears full responsibility for AI-assisted work product in the same way as if the work were produced entirely by staff.

### Data Protection (nFADP)

When AI tools process client information — proposal content, market data, strategic analysis — the firm is responsible for ensuring that processing complies with nFADP. The Geneva boutique rarely has a data processing agreement with its AI vendors that was reviewed for nFADP compliance before the tool was deployed. The firm that uses AI to draft client proposals is processing personal and commercially sensitive data through vendor infrastructure it has not audited. This is present-tense, not future-risk.

### Professional Body Vacuum

Unlike attorneys (bar), wealth managers (FINMA), or pension fund advisors (BVG), Geneva consulting firms have no professional body that publishes AI governance standards or provides guidance on adequate governance practice. There is no SVA advisory opinion on AI governance for consulting firms. There is no equivalent of the Ordre professional's guidance for consultants. The vacuum is not transitional — it is structural. Firms that want to know whether their governance is adequate have no institution to ask.

### Client Counterparty Requirements

Geneva consulting firms working with institutional clients — banks, industrial companies, family offices — are beginning to receive AI governance questionnaires as part of vendor due diligence. These questionnaires probe methodology, tool stack, data handling, and accountability chains. Responses create commercial representations that bind the firm contractually. The firms receiving these questionnaires for the first time have no pre-existing documentation architecture to respond from. The questionnaire response itself becomes a liability document.

### The Stacking Problem

These obligations do not arrive in sequence — they arrive simultaneously. The same boutique firm is simultaneously subject to: carrier questionnaire requirements (coverage condition), client counterparty AI governance requests (commercial relationship condition), nFADP processing obligations (data protection condition), and mandate law accountability (contract law condition). No Geneva service has published a methodology for managing these simultaneously at boutique scale. The stacking creates a compound exposure that none of the individual frameworks was designed to address.
