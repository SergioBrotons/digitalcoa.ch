## 01 — ICP Language

### How This ICP Actually Describes AI Problems

**English**

The managing director or partner at a small Geneva consulting boutique describes AI problems in the language of what they cannot stand behind and what they cannot reconstruct:

- "I signed three proposals last week. I could not have told you in the moment what the AI contributed versus what the consultant contributed. I signed anyway."
- "A client asked me specifically whether their analysis ran through the same AI model as our other clients' work. I did not have that information. I did not know whether to look for it."
- "I am not sure what my staff are doing with AI when I am not in the room. I cannot supervise every proposal. I sign everything."
- "When the carrier's renewal questionnaire asks me to describe our AI methodology, I am describing something I hope exists rather than something I know exists."
- "If a key person leaves and they were the only one who really understood how we configured the AI tools, what happens to the client work?"
- "I have clients asking about AI now. Some want transparency. Some do not. I do not know which is which until I am already in the conversation."
- "The vendors are not Geneva firms. Their terms are not written with Geneva consulting in mind. Nobody has mapped that."

## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

**Problem 1: AI tool as institutional capital formation without ownership or compensation**
The boutique's use of AI tools over two to three years has produced something of value: accumulated prompt libraries, workflow configurations, analytical frameworks, and client-specific model adaptations that reflect the boutique's consulting methodology. This institutional capital lives in the vendor's infrastructure, not in the boutique's own systems. The vendor uses this accumulated configuration to improve its product for all users. The boutique bears the cost of producing it, carries the liability for outputs generated through it, and owns none of the value it creates. This is a structural economic distortion with no Geneva-specific name and no market service addressing it. The boutique cannot reconstruct how its own methodology was shaped by AI interaction because the vendor holds the only complete picture.

**Problem 2: MD analytical authority gap in mid-engagement AI disagreement**
When an AI tool produces a recommendation that conflicts with the MD's professional judgment during active client work, no Geneva consulting-specific protocol defines what the MD does. The disagreement could be the AI identifying something the MD missed, or it could be the AI producing confident incorrect output. The MD cannot determine which without reconstructed analytical reasoning. The protocol for resolving AI-MD disagreement in real-time — during a live engagement, not in a post-deliverable review — has no Geneva-specific reference. The absence is distinct from quality control inversion and from reasoning chain reconstruction: it addresses the specific moment when the MD must decide whether to override the AI tool in the middle of producing client work.

**Problem 3: Client-specific AI question inventory as discrete liability category**
Geneva consulting boutiques are receiving a specific set of client AI questions with enough frequency to constitute an identifiable category: questions about model version, cross-client data segregation, human review protocols, and what the firm knows about its own AI configuration. Different MDs are answering the same questions differently. No Geneva boutique has documented its own inventory of client AI questions or prepared responses calibrated to the firm's actual configuration. The inventory itself is a liability document: documenting what clients ask defines what the firm is accountable for having answered. Most boutiques have not built this inventory. The ones that have are uncertain whether their answers are defensible.

**Problem 4: Geneva consulting carrier questionnaire as de facto standard without Geneva-specific interpretation**
Carrier AI questionnaires for Geneva consulting boutiques function as the operative AI governance standard for the sector, despite being designed by insurers without Geneva consulting-specific input. No Geneva consulting body has published an interpretation of what adequate compliance means against these questionnaires. The questionnaires create two problems simultaneously: they define a standard the boutique must meet, and no Geneva guidance exists on what the standard requires in the consulting context. The standard is operative by virtue of signature, and undefined in Geneva-specific terms. The MD who signs the carrier questionnaire is committing to a standard that has not been defined for their sector. No market service has filled this gap.

**Problem 5: AI platform vendor terms structural incompatibility with MD professional accountability architecture**
AI platform vendor terms are designed for the vendor's commercial model, not for the professional liability structure of a Geneva consulting MD. The incompatibility is structural: vendor terms grant data processing and model training rights in exchange for platform access, while the MD's professional liability requires controlling what is done with client data and being able to explain any deliverable produced. The MD cannot satisfy both simultaneously. No Geneva market participant has produced a compatibility analysis mapping the specific points of incompatibility between standard AI platform vendor terms and Geneva consulting MD professional accountability obligations. The MD may be personally liable for conditions in the vendor contract that the MD has not read and cannot renegotiate.

**Problem 6: Multi-obligation documentation interaction mapping absent for Geneva consulting**
Geneva consulting boutiques face simultaneous documentation obligations from carrier questionnaires, client AI governance requests, engagement letter standards, AI Convention obligations, and nFADP processing requirements. These obligations do not operate independently — they interact, sometimes require incompatible evidentiary formats, and compound across engagements. No Geneva consulting-specific framework maps how these obligations interact at the boutique scale. The documentation burden is real, but the more specific gap is the absence of an interaction map: a document that shows how satisfying one obligation affects the firm's position relative to another, and what the compounding effects are across an active client portfolio. Boutique documentation programs address individual obligations without this interaction mapping.

### Failure Modes

**Institutional capital formation without ownership produces asymmetric value extraction.**
The boutique invests in configuring, adapting, and refining AI tool behavior through daily use. The vendor captures this investment as accumulated training data and workflow knowledge that benefits its entire user base. The boutique retains the liability for outputs generated through this configured system, but owns none of the value it contributed to creating. The failure is structural: there is no contract language in standard AI platform terms that addresses this asymmetry for professional services firms.

**MD analytical authority gap produces unreviewed AI decisions in live engagements.**
When the MD disagrees with AI output during active client work, there is no defined protocol for resolution. The MD may override without documented reasoning, may defer to the AI without understanding why, or may produce a compromise that satisfies neither the AI's logic nor the MD's professional judgment. None of these responses produces a documented analytical trail. The failure mode compounds: each mid-engagement AI decision made without protocol leaves a gap in the defensibility chain that only becomes visible when something goes wrong and reconstruction is required.

**Client AI question inventory absent produces inconsistent responses across portfolio.**
MDs answering the same client AI questions differently across different conversations create a disclosure record in clients' memories that the boutique cannot reconstruct. When a carrier asks what the firm's AI client disclosure policy is, the MD may not be able to describe it because the policy was applied inconsistently across the portfolio. The failure is not dishonesty — it is the absence of a systematic approach to a category of client questions that is now arriving with sufficient frequency to require one.

**Carrier questionnaire adequacy undefined creates personal liability against an un-defined standard.**
The MD signs the carrier attestation committing to AI methodology adequacy against a standard the carrier has not defined for the Geneva consulting context. The MD's personal liability attaches to a representation with no Geneva-specific content standard behind it. The failure is not that the MD signed — it is that no mechanism exists for the MD to obtain a Geneva-specific interpretation of what the carrier form actually requires before signing.

### Paradoxes

**The boutique that invests most in AI configuration creates the most value for the vendor at the boutique's own expense.**
Accumulated AI configuration refinement — prompt libraries, workflow adaptations, client-specific model tuning — represents the boutique's institutional knowledge made operational. This knowledge lives in vendor infrastructure the boutique does not own. The more precisely the boutique configures its AI tools, the more valuable the vendor's overall system becomes, and the more dependent the boutique becomes on a platform it cannot control. The investment that should make the boutique more valuable makes it more dependent.

**The MD who reviews AI-assisted work most carefully may have the least basis for confidence in the review.**
MDs who have developed sufficient understanding to evaluate AI outputs are the most aware of how much they do not know about the tool's actual contribution. MDs who have not developed this awareness review AI-assisted work with false confidence. The more the MD understands about AI limitations, the less certain they are that their review caught the relevant errors. The less they understand, the more confident their sign-off appears.

**Client AI transparency requests and client AI comfort requirements arrive simultaneously from the same client base.**
Some clients ask for AI methodology transparency as a sophistication signal. Other clients prefer not to think about AI involvement in their consulting deliverables. The boutique cannot determine which response each client prefers until after the response is given, and the wrong response may damage the relationship. The boutique that responds transparently to clients who wanted invisible AI assistance has created a disclosure record that may not have been necessary. The boutique that responds minimally to clients who expected transparency has created a different credibility gap.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss-Specific Regulatory Obligations for Consultants

**Swiss Code of Obligations, Art. 398 — Proper Performance and the AI Configuration Problem**
Article 398 requires the mandatary to perform with the care of a conscientious professional. For the Geneva consulting MD who cannot decompose what the AI contributed to a signed deliverable, the question of what "proper performance" requires in an AI-assisted context has no Geneva-specific published resolution. The obligation to demonstrate proper performance attaches to the signed deliverable. If the deliverable contains AI-generated analytical content the MD cannot reconstruct, the MD cannot demonstrate what standard of care was applied to the portions they did not independently verify. No Geneva court has addressed what Art. 398 proper performance requires for AI co-authored consulting deliverables. The obligation is present-tense. The operational content does not exist.

**nFADP Articles 6, 8, and 9 — Vendor Terms and the Boutique's Authorization Architecture**
Article 6 requires a lawful basis for processing personal data. Article 8 requires disclosure when profiling produces legal effects. Article 9 requires authorization for third-party processing. When a Geneva consulting boutique uses an AI platform, client data flows to the vendor under terms the boutique did not negotiate and may not have read. The lawful basis for that data flow depends on which AI platform terms the boutique agreed to — terms designed for the vendor's commercial model, not for Swiss professional liability standards. No Geneva consulting-specific compatibility analysis exists mapping the specific points where standard AI vendor terms conflict with nFADP authorization requirements for consulting client data. The boutique may be operating outside its authorized processing scope without awareness.

**AI Convention — Professional Services Accountability Under Geneva Law**
The AI Convention's obligations apply to Geneva consulting boutiques as providers of professional services. The Convention requires member states to ensure accountability for AI systems used in professional services. Swiss domestic implementation has not yet defined what this means for small Geneva consulting boutiques. The Convention's obligations flow through existing client contracts and professional liability standards. The boutique that has not evaluated its AI Convention compliance position is not non-compliant by choice — it is non-compliant without knowing it, because no Geneva-specific guidance on Convention compliance for consulting boutiques exists.

**Carrier Questionnaires as De Facto Standard Without Geneva-Specific Definition**
Carrier AI questionnaires are functioning as the operative AI governance standard for Geneva consulting boutiques. The questionnaires ask for methodology documentation, performance evidence, and MD attestations. None of these requirements has been defined against Geneva consulting-specific standards. The standard is operative by virtue of signature — the MD who signs commits to adequacy against a requirement that has not been defined for their context. The carrier is the only party with defined positions on what the standard means. No Geneva consulting body has engaged with carriers on what the standard requires for this specific population.

**Professional Liability and the Undefined AI Care Standard**
Geneva consulting MDs carry personal professional liability for deliverables they sign. AI-assisted deliverables contain analytical content the MD did not independently produce through their own process. No Geneva authority has established what care standard applies to AI-assisted consulting work. The MD who signs an AI-assisted deliverable is potentially personally liable for analytical errors that originated in the AI tool, not in the MD's own judgment. The gap between signed accountability and actual understanding is present-tense exposure, not a theoretical risk.
