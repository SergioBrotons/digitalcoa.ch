## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### English

**Managing director on the scope of what AI has absorbed into client work:**
"The proposal process used to be where we developed our thinking. Now the AI has absorbed that function. By the time we send a proposal, the thinking is done. The document looks like it came from us, but the intellectual development happened inside a tool we don't fully audit. I've started calling it ghost authorship — the work looks like ours but the intellectual process is somewhere else."

**On intellectual property attribution becoming an issue:**
"We've been in two situations where clients asked who wrote what in the proposal. Not whether we used AI — whether the content in the proposal is ours. One client asked because they wanted to share the proposal internally. The other asked because they had concerns about confidentiality of the methodology. Neither question has a clean answer."

**On what happens when you try to document AI's contribution:**
"We tried to document where AI was used in our last three deliverables. Within two weeks, the documentation was incomplete — new AI usage had happened that wasn't recorded. Within a month, we stopped updating it. The documentation burden was consuming more time than the AI was saving. We abandoned it."

**On the competitive parity problem:**
"Two years ago, using AI in proposals was a differentiator. Now it's assumed. The clients assume we use AI. Our competitors assume we use AI. The playing field has leveled in a way that doesn't help us — it just raises the floor for everyone. What used to be an advantage is now the minimum expectation."

**On client expectations having moved without the firm moving:**
"One client sent us their AI governance framework and asked if we aligned with it. We don't align with any framework — we don't have a framework. But the question was asked in a way that assumed we would have an answer. We did not. The conversation was uncomfortable."

**On proposal iteration tracking:**
"The AI tool logs every version. But the version history shows iterations of text, not iterations of thought. When a client asks why we considered and rejected a particular direction, the version history doesn't help. The tool knows. We don't."

## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Ghost authorship as emerging client conversation**
Clients are beginning to ask about intellectual property attribution in consulting deliverables. The question is not whether AI was used — it is whether the content is genuinely the firm's. The conversation is new and increasing.

**Signal 2: AI documentation abandoned within weeks of being instituted**
Firms that attempt AI usage documentation discover the burden compounds faster than anticipated. The documentation is abandoned, creating a gap in the audit trail exactly when it is most needed.

**Signal 3: Competitive parity has neutralized AI as a differentiator**
AI adoption in proposal writing has leveled the competitive field. The minimum expectation has risen without raising the value of any individual firm's positioning.

**Signal 4: Client-sent AI governance frameworks arriving without firm response capability**
Sophisticated clients are sending their AI governance frameworks to consulting firms and asking for alignment confirmation. Firms without frameworks cannot respond, and the conversation exposes a structural gap.

**Signal 5: Version history captures text iterations, not thought iterations**
AI tools log document iterations but not intellectual direction changes. When clients ask about considered alternatives, the firm has no reconstructable account.

**Signal 6: Proposal ghost authorship creating bilateral vulnerability**
The firm's proposals look like firm-authored work but the intellectual development happened inside an AI tool. Both firm and client are operating with misattributed deliverables.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Ghost authorship attribution methodology**
No Geneva consulting firm has developed a methodology for managing intellectual property attribution when AI has contributed to deliverables. The methodology would need to address consent, documentation, and client communication.

**Gap 2: AI usage documentation systems designed for boutique scale**
Existing documentation systems are designed for large organizations with compliance departments. No system exists for boutique consulting firms where the documentation burden would consume actual work time.

**Gap 3: AI governance framework response protocol for firms without frameworks**
No protocol exists for responding to client-sent AI governance frameworks when the firm has no framework. The response gap exposes the firm in client conversations that should be relationship-building.

**Gap 4: Competitive positioning after AI adoption parity**
No Geneva publication has addressed how boutique consulting firms should position themselves after AI adoption has become a minimum expectation rather than a differentiator.

**Gap 5: Intellectual direction tracking across AI-assisted proposal development**
No methodology exists for tracking which intellectual directions were considered and rejected during AI-assisted proposal development. The version history captures text, not thought.

**Gap 6: Client communication protocol for AI governance disclosure**
No protocol exists for communicating to clients about how AI is used in their deliverables before the client asks. The proactive disclosure question is unresolved.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Professional liability under Art. 28 CO for AI-assisted deliverables:**
Art. 28 CO liability for professional error applies to deliverables bearing the firm's signature regardless of AI contribution. The documentation of AI's role in errors is unresolved.

**Confidentiality obligations for client data in AI systems:**
Client data processed through AI tools creates confidentiality obligations under engagement letters and professional duty. The intersection with vendor terms of service is not addressed in standard engagement language.

**FINMA MiFID suitability documentation for AI-assisted recommendations:**
MiFID suitability obligations apply to recommendations made with AI contribution. The documentation requirements for AI-assisted suitability analysis are not defined at boutique scale.

**nFADP Article 6 legitimate interest for internal AI processing:**
Internal AI processing of client data requires a legal basis under nFADP Article 6. The legitimate interest assessment for AI processing of client work product has not been published.

**Proposal intellectual property under Swiss copyright law:**
Swiss copyright law vests authorship in the natural person who created the work. AI-generated content in proposals creates copyright attribution questions no court has addressed.

**EU AI Act downstream deployer obligations for consulting firms:**
Consulting firms using AI tools for client deliverables are downstream deployers under the EU AI Act. The Article 26 documentation requirements are not adapted for consulting firm use cases.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Ghost authorship as bilateral intellectual property attribution problem for boutique consulting firms**
Consulting firm proposals look firm-authored but the intellectual development happened inside an AI tool. The misattribution creates bilateral vulnerability — the firm cannot fully account for what it produced, and the client cannot fully rely on what they received. No Geneva market participant has named this specific configuration.

**Finding 2: AI usage documentation systems collapsing under their own burden at boutique scale within weeks**
Firms that institute AI usage documentation discover the administrative burden compounds faster than anticipated. Documentation is abandoned within weeks, creating an audit gap exactly when documentation is most needed. The failure is structural, not operational.

**Finding 3: Competitive parity neutralized AI adoption as a boutique consulting differentiator**
AI adoption has become a baseline expectation in Geneva consulting markets. The differentiator is no longer whether AI is used — it is how AI governance is managed, disclosed, and positioned. No Geneva boutique has repositioned around this shift.

**Finding 4: Client-sent AI governance framework exposure as the new client onboarding problem**
Sophisticated clients are sending AI governance frameworks to consulting firms and expecting alignment confirmation. Firms without frameworks cannot respond cleanly, exposing a structural gap in client relationship management. The conversation happens before any engagement begins.

**Finding 5: Version history as text iteration log, not thought iteration record, creating reconstructability failure**
AI proposal version histories capture text changes, not thought direction changes. When clients ask why the firm considered and rejected a particular direction, the firm has no reconstructable account. The intellectual decision trail is lost in the tool.

**Finding 6: Proactive AI governance disclosure protocol absent from boutique consulting client communication**
No protocol exists for proactively communicating to clients about how AI is used in their deliverables before the client asks. The absence means the disclosure conversation is always reactive and always uncomfortable. Proactive disclosure would transform the conversation from exposure management to trust signal.
