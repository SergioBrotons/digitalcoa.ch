## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

### 2.1 Analytical Authority Inversion as Primary Unnamed Governance Problem

When AI tools are used across proposals and deliverables, the tool's analytical logic progressively shapes the direction of the firm's recommendations. The managing director signs proposals containing analytical positions they did not independently reach. When the MD's professional judgment and the AI tool's recommendation are in tension — when experience suggests one direction while the tool produces another — there is no governance mechanism for resolving the inversion. The firm defaults to the AI's output not because it was consciously chosen but because no protocol exists for disagreeing with the tool at the boutique scale. The analytical direction of the firm is being set by tool logic without a governance decision to adopt that logic.

Market signal: no Geneva consulting boutique has a protocol for what happens when the MD's judgment and the AI tool's recommendation conflict. The absence is structural, not a matter of oversight or diligence.

### 2.2 Deliverable Version Staleness as Discrete Accountability Problem Distinct from Version Control

Traditional consulting deliverables are stable: what the client receives at signing is what was produced. AI-assisted deliverables have a different structural property — their analytical framework is a function of the model version active at the time of production. When the model updates, the same analytical request can produce a different framework. A boutique with a long-term client engagement may have produced deliverables under model versions that no longer exist. The client holds a documented position the firm can no longer reproduce or fully defend under the current model. The deliverable becomes stale in a way that has no traditional analog. No Geneva boutique has a versioning accountability protocol for AI-assisted deliverables.

Market signal: the model update event is not being tracked as an accountability occasion in any Geneva consulting firm's governance documentation. Deliverables are being treated as stable documents when their analytical foundation is model-version-dependent.

### 2.3 Client Expectation Recalibration as Irreversible Value Exchange Distortion

AI-polished deliverables — structurally coherent, grammatically precise, logically organized — raise the client's baseline for what the firm produces. Once clients experience this elevated quality standard, they calibrate future expectations against it. The firm that built this elevated baseline using AI tools cannot sustain it without those tools, or at a minimum cannot sustain it at the same cost structure. When AI assistance is removed or reduced — through tool unavailability, cost pressure, or a decision to reduce AI use — the firm's apparent quality drops from the client's perspective even though nothing about the underlying capability has changed. The expectation recalibration is irreversible through organic means.

Market signal: no Geneva consulting firm has modeled the expectation trajectory of its client portfolio against AI use intensity, or built a client communication strategy that accounts for the irreversibility of expectation elevation.

### 2.4 Liability Reconstruction Failure as Active Coverage Gap in AI-Assisted Work

When an AI-assisted deliverable is later found to contain a material error or omission, the firm must determine what caused the error and who is accountable. This is the liability attribution reconstruction question. AI tools are not designed to produce per-instance attribution traces that would allow this reconstruction. The firm cannot determine whether the error originated in the data provided, the analytical framework applied, the tool's generation, or the review process. Without this determination, the firm cannot demonstrate that the standard of care was met, cannot coordinate with its carrier on coverage, and cannot respond to a client question with a defensible account. The liability reconstruction failure is an active coverage gap: the engagement letter does not define the reconstruction obligation, and the carrier has not confirmed that coverage extends to errors whose origin cannot be reconstructed.

Market signal: no Geneva consulting firm has a liability attribution reconstruction protocol for AI-assisted deliverables. No carrier has confirmed coverage scope for errors where attribution cannot be established.

### 2.5 Junior Staff AI Tool Selection as Ungoverned Consequential Decision at Boutique Scale

At boutique scale, the most AI-literate personnel are typically junior staff, while the most professionally accountable are the MD. AI tool selection — which vendor, which model, which configuration — is a governance decision with downstream accountability consequences. At many boutiques, this decision was made by the person who was most comfortable with the tools, not by the person with the most professional accountability. The decision may never have been reviewed by the MD in governance terms. The tool selection shapes the full scope of what the firm can and cannot do with AI, what client data flows through whose infrastructure, and what the firm's documented AI governance position actually is. No Geneva boutique has a formal architecture for reviewing AI tool selection decisions against professional accountability standards.

Market signal: AI tool selection is not being reviewed as a governance decision in most Geneva consulting boutiques. The person most comfortable with AI is not the same person most accountable for its consequences.

### 2.6 EU AI Act Downstream Deployer Obligations Operating Unassessed Across Domestic and EU-Facing Engagements

Geneva consulting firms serving EU-based clients are downstream deployers under the EU AI Act. Article 26 requires downstream deployers to use AI systems in accordance with provider instructions and to maintain compliance documentation. The obligation applies to the firm's EU-facing engagements regardless of whether the firm has consciously assessed it. Most Geneva boutiques are not distinguishing between domestic and EU-facing engagements in their AI governance documentation, meaning EU-facing engagements are operating under an obligation the firm has not evaluated. The EU AI Act downstream deployer obligation is not on the firm's radar as a discrete compliance question with specific documentation requirements. The obligation exists regardless of awareness.

Market signal: no Geneva consulting firm has conducted a downstream deployer obligation assessment that maps which client engagements trigger EU AI Act obligations. No Geneva advisory has produced a boutique-scale methodology for this assessment.

**AI tool selection governance review.** No methodology exists for a boutique to review existing AI tool selection decisions against professional accountability standards — including vendor infrastructure, data processing terms, and model update policies — and to produce a documented governance position on whether current tool choices are consistent with the firm's accountability architecture.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Specific Regulatory Obligations for Consultants

### Professional Liability Under Art. 28 CO and the Attribution Reconstruction Imperative

Art. 28 CO establishes professional liability for professional error in the performance of mandated services. For AI-assisted deliverables, the error determination question is not straightforward: the professional error may originate in the AI tool's generation, the underlying data, the review process, or the prompt structure. The firm's ability to demonstrate that the standard of care was met depends on being able to reconstruct this chain. No Geneva court has defined what the standard of care requires for AI-assisted consulting work. The firm's carrier has not confirmed coverage scope for errors where attribution cannot be reconstructed. The MD who signs AI-assisted deliverables is personally exposed for errors that may be structurally impossible to attribute.

### nFADP Data Controller Obligations and AI Infrastructure for Competitive Intelligence Work Product

When a Geneva boutique uses AI tools to analyze client-competitive intelligence — strategic positions, market dynamics, competitive alternatives — that analysis contains client-confidential data. The nFADP obligations of the firm as data controller require that the legal basis for processing be established before processing begins. Most engagement letters do not address this scenario. Most vendor terms of service were not reviewed against the confidentiality implications of competitive intelligence analysis flowing through the vendor's infrastructure. The firm remains responsible for client data it sent through vendor systems regardless of vendor terms.

### Swiss AI Convention Accountability Through Client Contracts and Professional Liability

The Council of Europe AI Convention, signed by Switzerland, creates obligations that flow into private law through client contracts and professional liability standards. For consulting firms, the Convention means that accountability for AI-assisted decisions is not merely an internal governance question — it is a legal obligation with commercial consequences. The boutique that has not mapped how Convention obligations interact with its engagement terms and client deliverables is operating without a compliance architecture for obligations that already apply.

### EU AI Act Article 26 Downstream Deployer Obligations for Geneva Firms with EU Clients

Article 26 of the EU AI Act requires downstream deployers to use AI systems in accordance with provider instructions and to maintain documentation sufficient to demonstrate compliance. Geneva consulting firms serving EU-based clients are downstream deployers. The documentation requirements under Article 26 are specific and operational. Most boutiques are not distinguishing between domestic and EU-facing engagements in their AI governance documentation. The obligation applies regardless of whether the firm has assessed it.

### The Federal Council AI Regulation Bill and the Anticipatory Positioning Window

The Federal Council's AI regulation bill is expected before the end of 2026. The window between now and adoption is the period during which firms can document their AI governance position proactively. After adoption, documented practice becomes evidence of pre-regulation approach. The positioning window is time-bounded and will close. Firms that have not built documented positions on analytical authority, deliverable versioning, and liability attribution will have no pre-regulation baseline to contrast against the new requirements.

### The Stacking Problem (Operative Across All Active Obligations)

The Geneva consulting boutique is simultaneously subject to: professional liability under Art. 28 CO with undefined AI error coverage scope, nFADP data controller obligations for client data in AI vendor infrastructure, Swiss AI Convention obligations through private contract, EU AI Act downstream deployer obligations for EU-facing engagements, carrier AI governance questionnaire requirements with coverage consequences, and client confidentiality obligations for competitive intelligence analysis. These obligations arrive simultaneously. None was designed for the boutique consulting firm's structure. No Geneva protocol exists for managing them coherently.
