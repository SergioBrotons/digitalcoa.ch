- "A client asked me last month to explain the methodology behind a recommendation. The recommendation was partly AI-generated. I couldn't give a clean answer."

## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Complaints, and Paradoxes

### 2.1 The Knowledge Continuity Risk in Hiring — AI Configuration Leaves with People

A failure mode is emerging that is not about AI tools themselves but about the institutional knowledge encoded in them: when a senior consultant or analyst leaves a Geneva boutique, the AI configuration they built — their prompt library, their workflow structure, their approach to specific client types — leaves with them. The new hire starts from a different baseline. For firms where AI is embedded in the production methodology, this creates a knowledge continuity gap that is not covered by standard handover processes. The signal: firms are beginning to ask whether AI workflow configuration should be treated as a firm asset, documented independently of the person who built it. This is distinct from the prompt library asset problem (C215 Finding 4) — it is about the continuity risk when configuration is tied to individuals, not about the portability risk when configuration is tied to a vendor.

### 2.2 The Single-Vendor Concentration Problem — Boutique AI Stacks Are Not Diversified

Geneva consulting boutiques are standardizing on a single AI provider for all use cases — ChatGPT, Claude, or one other platform — across proposal writing, analysis, research synthesis, and internal knowledge management. This creates a vendor concentration risk that is not on the firm's risk register: if the provider changes pricing significantly, modifies terms of service, or experiences a disruption, the firm's operational methodology is exposed without a fallback. The market signal: firms that have grown dependent on a single AI vendor are beginning to ask what a diversification strategy would look like — and finding that there is no advisory framework for making this decision at the boutique scale.

### 2.3 The AI Transparency Expectation Gap — Clients Are Asking, Firms Are Uncertain

A qualitative shift is occurring: Geneva clients are not only asking "do you use AI?" but increasingly expecting a substantive answer about what that means for the work they receive. The expectation is moving from a yes/no question toward a methodology question — what did AI do, how was it supervised, what is the human contribution? Managing directors report that clients who ask this question are typically the more sophisticated ones, and the firm's inability to answer cleanly is creating credibility gaps in conversations where the client relationship is at stake. This is distinct from the disclosure decision framework (C216 Finding 1) — it is about the quality of the answer when disclosure is made, not about the decision to disclose.

### 2.4 The Hidden Cost of AI Productivity — Consultant Output Variance Is Now Visible

The productivity gain from AI is real but uneven across the firm. Some consultants using AI are producing substantially more output in the same time; others are producing AI-assisted work that clients or senior reviewers find below the firm's quality standard. The managing director is now managing a productivity range within the firm that was not present before AI — a range where two consultants with similar titles produce materially different volumes and quality of AI-assisted work. The market signal: firms are beginning to ask how to assess and govern the productivity dimension of AI usage, not just the quality dimension. This is a performance management question with professional liability implications.

### 2.5 The Proposal Quality Perception Problem — AI Speed vs. Boutique Positioning

Geneva boutiques compete partly on the quality and depth of their proposals. When AI is used to accelerate proposal production, a new tension emerges: faster proposals may look thinner to clients who expect the substantial, considered approach that characterized the firm's pre-AI proposals. Some managing directors report clients responding less well to proposals that arrived faster than expected — the speed created an impression of reduced depth. This is a market signal about the interaction between AI-assisted efficiency and client perception of boutique quality. The firm that uses AI to produce proposals faster but is perceived as producing less substantive work has solved the wrong problem.

### 2.6 The Succession and Institutional Knowledge Problem — AI Encoding Creates a New Transition Risk

For boutiques where senior partners are planning transitions — whether within the firm or to a new generation — the question of what happens to the firm's AI configuration is new. The institutional knowledge that used to live in personal relationships, experience, and judgment is now partially encoded in AI workflows. When a senior partner transitions, the question is not just who takes the client relationships but who understands the AI methodology the firm has developed. Some managing directors are finding that this question is harder to answer than the client succession question — because the AI configuration has been built incrementally and ad hoc, without documentation of what it contains or how it connects to the firm's sector expertise.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis: What Others Say, What Gaps Exist, What No One Is Addressing

### Current Competitive Landscape

The Geneva consulting AI advisory market is served by three categories:

**Category 1: Generalist management consultants** — framing AI adoption as an organizational efficiency project. Pricing: CHF 150–250/hour. Value proposition: productivity improvement, capability building. Gap: no professional liability framing, no Geneva-specific sector context, no acknowledgment that the boutique's output quality is the product — not a cost to be reduced.

**Category 2: IT and SaaS providers** — selling AI tool deployment and licensing. Pricing: CHF 5,000–40,000 for implementation and annual licensing. Value proposition: operational AI capability. Gap: the vendor benefits from deployment volume, not from the firm's governance quality. The firm receives the tool without the accountability framework, and without a continuity plan for when the vendor relationship changes.

**Category 3: Legal and compliance consultants** — offering nFADP reviews that include AI as a section. Pricing: CHF 250–400/hour. Value proposition: regulatory compliance. Gap: data protection compliance is not the same as professional practice governance. The frame is wrong for an advisory firm whose output carries professional liability — the compliance lens does not capture the practice quality dimension.

### What No One Is Addressing for This ICP

**The knowledge continuity risk from AI-embedded institutional knowledge is not on anyone's advisory menu.** When a senior consultant leaves a boutique that has built its methodology into AI configurations, the handover documentation does not cover what needs to be preserved. No advisory provider is offering a knowledge continuity review for AI-embedded firms — a structured assessment of what institutional knowledge is encoded in AI workflows, who holds it, and what happens to it during transitions. This is distinct from prompt library portability (C215 Finding 4) — it is about the people risk, not the vendor dependency risk.

**The single-vendor concentration risk for boutique AI stacks has no advisory framework.** The decision to standardize on a single AI provider is made for operational simplicity, but the concentration risk this creates — operational, financial, and knowledge dependency — is not reviewed against any framework. No advisory provider helps boutique managing directors assess whether their AI vendor concentration is proportionate to their firm's operational dependency on that vendor. This is not a technical question; it is a strategic risk management question.

**The AI productivity governance question — how to assess and manage consultant-level AI output variation — is not being addressed.** Firms are managing a productivity range in AI usage that they cannot see or govern through existing performance management frameworks. The gap is not quality control (addressed in C214 ACQS) but productivity governance — how to ensure AI usage is consistent with the firm's quality positioning and does not create client expectation mismatches. No advisory provider is offering this at the boutique level.

**The succession dimension of AI-embedded methodology is not being discussed.** As senior partners plan transitions, the question of what happens to the firm's AI methodology — who understands it, how it transfers, what the transition risks are — is a governance question that no advisory provider is raising. The gap is not about AI tool succession (covered by any IT provider) but about the intellectual methodology embedded in AI configurations — the institutional knowledge that is now partially outside the firm's personal knowledge base.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Specific Regulatory and Professional Obligations

### 4.1 Professional Competence and AI — OR Art. 398 and the Standard of Care

Swiss professional liability under OR Art. 398 requires the mandatary to perform with the care and skill expected of a professional in the field. For a Geneva consulting firm, this standard extends to the tools used in delivering advisory services. The question that is not yet resolved in Swiss professional guidance: at what point does AI-assisted work fall below the standard of competent professional practice? The answer depends partly on whether the consultant can explain how AI was used, what its limitations are, and how the final output was verified. As client sophistication about AI increases, the standard of care is implicitly rising — the client who asks about AI methodology is implicitly applying a higher standard to the firm's explanation. Managing directors should anticipate that "we used AI" without a defensible methodology explanation will not satisfy the competent performance standard in a dispute.

### 4.2 nFADP Accountability and AI Tool Configuration — Data Controller Obligations

The revised Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) places accountability obligations on the firm as data controller — the firm must be able to demonstrate that client data processed through AI tools was handled in compliance with data protection principles. The practical implication for a Geneva boutique: each AI tool in use must be assessed against the purpose limitation principle, the transparency obligation, and the data subject rights framework. The tool configuration question is not a one-time IT decision — it is an ongoing compliance obligation that must be reviewed when tools change terms or when new tools are added. The FDPIC's published enforcement range (CHF 500–25,000) applies to accountability failures regardless of whether a data breach occurred.

### 4.3 SVA Continuing Professional Education — AI Competence as an Emerging Requirement

The Swiss Association of Lawyers (SVA) and the Geneva Bar (ORDRE) have introduced AI competence requirements in their continuing education frameworks. For consulting firms whose professionals are members of a regulated body, this is a direct compliance obligation. For firms whose professionals are not bar members — management consultants, organizational advisors, strategy consultants — the equivalent obligation arises under the general duty to maintain professional competence. The direction of travel is clear: AI competence will be treated as a professional standard requirement, not a voluntary capability. Managing directors should ensure that AI tool usage by their staff falls within what a competent professional in their field would consider responsible AI practice.

### 4.4 Insurance Market — Professional Liability Underwriting Responses to AI

Swiss professional liability insurers are tightening coverage terms in response to AI-assisted advisory work. The market signal is that documented AI governance — including tool inventories, data flow assessments, and methodology documentation — is beginning to affect premium terms. Firms that cannot describe their AI usage to their insurer are likely under-priced on AI exposure, which means the coverage gap in a claim scenario is larger than the premium savings. The practical step: engage with the insurer's AI questionnaire with structured documentation, not informal responses. The documentation produced for insurance purposes serves the firm's professional liability position as well.

### 4.5 Employment Law — AI Tool Usage Policy as Employer Obligation

Swiss employment law (OR Art. 321) requires employers to provide staff with clear instructions about working conditions. As AI tools become embedded in consulting workflows, firms have an obligation to document the AI usage policy for staff — what tools can be used, for what purposes, what data cannot be entered into AI tools, and how AI-assisted work should be reviewed before client delivery. Firms that have not documented this policy face a distinct exposure from the client-facing liability: the internal policy gap creates an employer duty of care failure that is separate from the professional liability question.

### 4.6 Client Contractual Expectations — AI Disclosure as an Emerging Contractual Norm

The market is moving toward clients expecting AI disclosure as part of the advisory relationship. EU-based clients in particular are beginning to treat AI methodology transparency as a contractual expectation, not just a professional courtesy. For Geneva consulting firms serving international clients, the absence of AI disclosure provisions in engagement letters creates a contractual gap: if a client later claims they were not informed about AI involvement and suffered harm as a result, the firm's silence in the contract is a liability. The practical step is to include AI methodology provisions in engagement letters — specifying whether and how AI tools are used in the engagement, without needing to disclose proprietary methodology.

### Finding 1: AI Knowledge Continuity Risk — The Personnel Dependency of AI-Embedded Methodology

**Insight:** Geneva consulting boutiques that have integrated AI into their advisory methodology are discovering that a significant portion of their institutional knowledge is now encoded in AI configurations held by individuals — not documented at the firm level, not transferable during personnel transitions, and not recoverable without substantial rebuilding. When a senior consultant leaves, the AI workflow they built does not transfer with their replacement. The gap is not the tool vendor (covered by C215 Finding 4 on prompt portability) but the people dependency: the firm has an undocumented knowledge asset that is tied to individuals, not to the firm. The product: an AI Knowledge Continuity Assessment — a structured review of which AI configurations hold firm-critical knowledge, who holds them, and what documentation would be required to make that knowledge transferable and firm-owned. Fee: CHF 2,200–3,800 for the initial assessment. Deliverable: a Knowledge Continuity Map with specific transition risk mitigation recommendations.

**Why distinct:** C214 Finding 1 addressed AI confidentiality (tool data flows). C215 Finding 4 addressed prompt library portability (vendor dependency). C216 Finding 5 addressed the undefined Swiss professional practice standard. This finding addresses the specific personnel dependency of AI-embedded knowledge — a distinct gap that requires a different assessment scope (individual-held knowledge assets vs. vendor-portability or standard-definition) and a different product design.

### Finding 2: Boutique AI Vendor Concentration Risk — Single-Provider Dependency as Strategic Risk

**Insight:** Geneva consulting boutiques are standardizing on a single AI provider for all use cases — operational simplicity creating strategic concentration. The concentration risk is not on the firm's risk register because the financial exposure (subscription cost) looks manageable. The actual exposure — operational dependency, knowledge lock-in, and transition cost if the provider changes terms or becomes unavailable — is not being assessed. No advisory provider offers a boutique-appropriate vendor concentration assessment for AI tool stacks. The deliverable is not a recommendation to switch vendors — it is a framework for assessing whether the current concentration is proportionate to the firm's operational dependency.
