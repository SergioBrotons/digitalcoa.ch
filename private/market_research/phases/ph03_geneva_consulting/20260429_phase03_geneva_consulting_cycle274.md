## Market Signals (02)

### Current Problems

**Co-authored deliverables create shared legal authorship the MD has not evaluated.**
When an AI tool contributes substantively to a deliverable — not as a spell-checker but as an analytical co-author — the MD who signs the document is certifying professional authorship of content they did not produce through their own analytical process. Under Art. 28 CO professional liability, the certifying party is accountable for the soundness of the work, not merely for the accuracy of a tool's output. The legal authorship question is distinct from the attribution question: it is not about who wrote each word, but about who stands behind the analytical soundness of the document as a whole. Most Geneva boutiques have not evaluated whether their AI-assisted deliverables constitute co-authorship under professional liability standards, and no Geneva market service has offered this as a specific assessment.

**Carrier third-generation questionnaires shifted from methodology existence to performance evidence.**
Carriers are no longer asking whether the firm has an AI policy. They are asking whether the firm's AI governance produced documented outcomes: what the last three AI-assisted decisions were, what review process preceded them, what evidence the MD reviewed before signing off. The shift is from policy existence to performance documentation. Most boutiques are responding with policy documents — the artifact carriers no longer primarily request. The evidence gap is not that the policy is inadequate; it is that carriers are now asking for evidence of what the policy produced, and the boutique has not built the performance documentation architecture to answer.

**Proposal-level analytical influence accumulation as implicit firm position the MD has not authorized.**
Two to three years of AI-assisted proposals have systematically shaped the firm's analytical positions in ways that are no longer attributable to deliberate firm judgment. The framing, the preferred analytical angles, the standard response repertoire — all have been influenced by AI tool behavior without MD review of whether those accumulated framings reflect the firm's actual position. When a client or counterparty evaluates the firm's analytical stance, they are evaluating something the firm did not consciously author. The problem is not that the firm is misrepresenting its views — it is that the firm cannot be certain what views it holds independently of AI influence.

**Client disclosure conversation as moment-of-truth without preparation.**
The moment a client asks directly about AI use — in a meeting, in a call, in an email — the MD must produce an account that is consistent with every prior representation made about AI to that client and to other clients. This is a different skill from documentation. It is conversational accountability in real time, with a record being formed by the client in their own memory. No Geneva boutique has prepared structured responses for this moment, and the inconsistency risk is present-tense: the next client conversation may be the occasion where prior inconsistent disclosures surface.

**Vendor terms-of-service mutation as undisclosed contract modification event.**
AI platform vendors have been updating their terms of service with minimal notice. Each update potentially modifies the authorization the firm granted when it began using the platform — particularly around data processing, model training, and cross-client intelligence handling. The firm that adopted AI tools in 2023 or 2024 may be operating under terms that have materially changed since the original agreement. This is not a security question — it is a contract law question: the firm may be operating outside the authorization it thought it had granted, and may have clients whose data is being processed under terms they did not consent to.

### Failure Modes

**Co-authorship without assessment creates undifferentiated professional liability exposure.**
When a deliverable contains AI-generated analytical content, the MD cannot distinguish the portion of the work product they are professionally vouching for from the portion the AI contributed. Professional liability attaches to the document, not to the human-readable portions. The firm that has not assessed co-authorship exposure cannot manage it, cannot price it, and cannot explain it to a carrier if something goes wrong. The failure mode is undifferentiated liability: the MD is potentially liable for analytical errors that originated in the AI tool, not in their own judgment.

**Inconsistent client AI disclosure conversations creating contractual misrepresentation exposure.**
MDs who have answered client AI questions differently over time — in different conversations, to different clients — are carrying a disclosure inconsistency risk that is distinct from having a documentation policy. The client's memory of what the firm said is part of the commercial relationship. If a client can demonstrate that the firm described its AI use one way in a prior conversation and differently in a subsequent context, the inconsistency itself becomes a commercial representation the client may rely on. No Geneva boutique has audited its own disclosure history for consistency.

**Carrier performance evidence gap cannot be closed by documenting forward — it requires retrospective reconstruction.**
The carrier is asking for evidence of what the firm's AI governance produced. The boutique's response — building a policy going forward — does not address the retrospective evidence gap. The events that occurred before the policy was built are still undocumented. Building better documentation from today forward does not reconstruct what happened from 2023 to today. The boutique is presenting evidence of a future commitment in response to a question about past performance.

### Paradoxes

**More AI use produces more signable documents and fewer documents the MD can defend.**
The efficiency of AI-assisted production increases the volume of signed deliverables. Each additional signed deliverable adds to the MD's professional liability exposure without adding to their ability to explain or reconstruct the analytical process behind it. The firm's accountability position weakens as output volume increases.

**The vendor terms update notification goes to the firm's administrative email — not to the MD responsible for professional accountability.**
The person who manages the vendor relationship is rarely the same person who bears professional accountability for deliverables. Terms updates arrive in inboxes managed by operations or finance. The MD who is professionally accountable may learn of material terms changes only when a client question or a data subject request surfaces the implications. The accountability architecture and the administrative architecture are misaligned.

**The firm with the most comprehensive AI policy may have the least evidence of actual performance.**
Firms that invested early in AI governance documentation focused on policy existence — the document that demonstrates a framework exists. The firms now best positioned to satisfy carrier third-generation questions are those that built performance documentation alongside their policies. The firms with the most impressive policy documents may be precisely those least able to demonstrate what the policy produced in practice.

## Competitive Analysis (03)

### Gaps

**No Geneva service assesses co-authorship exposure for AI-assisted deliverables at boutique consulting scale.**
Co-authorship assessment — determining whether a deliverable constitutes joint professional work product under Art. 28 CO, what exposure that creates, and how to document the MD's review of AI-contributed analytical content — is not a disclosed service category in the Geneva market. It is architecturally different from policy drafting or tool selection advice. It requires evaluating specific deliverables against professional liability standards, which is a legal assessment function. No Geneva market participant has built this as a discrete boutique-scale service offering.

**No Geneva service offers carrier evidence package preparation specifically calibrated to third-generation performance requirements.**
The shift from policy-existence to performance-evidence questioning creates a specific advisory gap. Preparing a boutique to answer "what did your AI governance produce in your last three client engagements" requires a retrospective review methodology, a documentation reconstruction process, and an evidence packaging format — none of which exist as a discrete Geneva market service. Most boutiques are responding to carrier questionnaires with forward-looking policy documents, which do not satisfy the retrospective performance question.

**No Geneva service offers client AI disclosure conversation preparation — structured response scripts for the conversational moment-of-truth.**
Client AI disclosure conversations are not the same as client-facing documentation frameworks. The conversational moment has different stakes, different inconsistency risks, and different preparation requirements. No Geneva advisory has built structured conversation preparation as a discrete service — helping the MD map what they have said to which clients, identify inconsistencies, and prepare calibrated responses for the next disclosure conversation. This is different from policy work and different from documentation work.

**No Geneva service offers vendor terms mutation monitoring as a discrete advisory function.**
Vendor terms-of-service updates are not tracked systematically by any Geneva boutique. The monitoring function — comparing current terms against prior authorizations, assessing material changes, identifying exposure implications for client data — is structurally absent. This is not a legal review function (though it has legal consequences) and not an IT security function. It is a professional accountability monitoring function that no Geneva market participant has built.

**No Geneva service addresses multi-framework documentation coherence as an architectural requirement.**
Firms are accumulating documentation from multiple accountability frameworks simultaneously: carrier questionnaires, institutional client requirements, internal governance frameworks, and AI Convention obligations. These frameworks have different evidentiary formats, different audience registers, and different consequences for the same firm. No methodology exists for maintaining coherent documentation architecture across all frameworks simultaneously — producing documentation that satisfies each framework without creating internal contradictions that a carrier or client could identify.

### What No One Is Addressing

**The specific mechanism by which AI co-authorship converts professional liability exposure.** The problem is not that AI was used — it is that the MD's signature certifies professional authorship of analytical content without MD authorship of that content. This is not addressed by disclosure language in client communications. It is an Art. 28 CO professional liability question that requires specific deliverable-level assessment.

**The retrospective evidence gap that cannot be closed by going forward.** When carriers ask "what did your governance produce," building a better policy does not answer the question about what was already produced. The retrospective reconstruction requirement is a specific methodology problem — how to document past AI-assisted decisions when the documentation architecture for doing so was never built. No Geneva service has built a retrospective AI governance reconstruction methodology for boutique consulting firms.

**The disclosure consistency audit problem.** Most boutiques have had multiple client conversations about AI use over two to three years, with no record of what was said. The next client conversation is not the first opportunity to create an inconsistency — it is the first opportunity for an inconsistency to be noticed. No service exists to help firms audit their own disclosure history, identify inconsistencies, and prepare a coherent disclosure position going forward.

## Compliance Context (04)

### Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations for Consultants

**Art. 28 CO professional liability and the co-authorship exposure.**
Art. 28 CO establishes professional liability for error in the performance of mandated services. The consulting firm's obligation runs through the deliverable to the client, and the MD's signature on the deliverable certifies professional authorship. When AI tools contribute substantively to analytical content, the MD is certifying authorship of content produced without their own analytical process. The statute does not distinguish between human-authored and AI-authored content for liability purposes — it applies to the deliverable as a professional work product. Most Geneva consulting firms have not evaluated whether their AI-assisted deliverables constitute co-authorship, and no Swiss authority has published guidance specifically addressing co-authorship of professional work product.

**nFADP data processing authorization and vendor terms mutation.**
The firm's initial authorization for AI tool use — granted when terms were accepted — may have been materially modified by subsequent terms updates. Under nFADP Art. 6 lawful basis requirements, the processing must remain within the scope of the original authorization. When vendors update terms, the scope of the authorization may have changed without the firm's awareness or a renewed consent architecture. The firm may be processing client data under terms that differ materially from those initially agreed to. No Geneva authority has published guidance on the interaction between ongoing vendor terms updates and nFADP Art. 6 authorization scope.

**Client confidentiality and the internal AI surface.**
Client data entering internal AI workflows — internal research, methodology development, knowledge management — triggers nFADP data controller obligations. Internal AI outputs then travel into client deliverables without client visibility or consent to that internal processing. The firm that has not mapped what client information enters internal AI workflows, and what outputs those workflows produce that travel into deliverables, is carrying an undocumented confidentiality exposure that surfaces through client questions or data subject access requests before the firm knows it exists.

**Multi-framework documentation coherence as an operational requirement, not a documentation quality aspiration.**
The Geneva consulting boutique is simultaneously subject to: carrier AI attestation obligations (third-generation performance evidence requirements), institutional client AI methodology questionnaires, AI Convention obligations through professional liability standards, and nFADP Art. 6/12/13 accountability requirements. These frameworks do not share a documentation format. A single AI-assisted engagement may trigger documentation obligations under all of them simultaneously, with each framework requiring a different format, a different evidentiary standard, and a different audience register. No published Swiss methodology addresses coherent multi-framework documentation architecture at boutique consulting scale.

**Professional liability insurance coverage for AI co-authorship errors remains undefined.**
The carrier that issued the firm's professional liability policy has not published how AI co-authorship errors are classified within coverage terms. If an AI-generated analytical error in a deliverable causes client damage, the coverage question — whether the error is covered as a professional error, excluded as a tool error, or partially covered — has not been resolved. The MD who signed carrier AI attestation documents attested to AI governance adequacy without knowing how that attestation interacts with actual coverage for AI-assisted errors.

**Geneva consulting firms with attorney clients must consider Art. 321 CP intersection.**
Consulting firms working alongside attorneys or receiving attorney-referred client data must evaluate whether AI processing of that data implicates professional secrecy architecture. Client data flowing through AI systems in ways not contemplated by attorney-client privilege agreements creates an undisclosed intersection between consulting firm operations and professional secrecy obligations. No Swiss authority has published guidance specifically addressing this intersection for non-attorney consulting firms.

**Finding 06: Multi-framework documentation coherence as an architectural system property distinct from individual document quality — requiring coherence across all accountability frameworks simultaneously.**
Geneva consulting firms are accumulating documentation simultaneously under multiple accountability frameworks: carrier third-generation performance evidence, institutional client AI methodology questionnaires, AI Convention obligations through professional liability standards, and nFADP documentation requirements. These frameworks have different evidentiary formats, different audience registers, and different consequences. A single AI-assisted engagement may trigger documentation obligations under all of them. The specific failure mode is not that any individual document is inadequate — it is that the system of documents, built at different times against different requirements without a coherence requirement applied to the system as a whole, presents an incoherent picture when reviewed together. Individual document quality improvement cannot produce system coherence because the architectural problem is the relationship between documents, not the quality of any single document. This is distinct from all prior Phase 03 documentation findings (C269–C273) which addressed individual document quality, adequacy, or coherence in isolation without isolating the specific architectural distinction: documentation system coherence is a property of the relationship between documents across all frameworks simultaneously, not a property of any individual document, and cannot be achieved by improving the quality of individual parts.
