## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### English

**Managing director on the proposal authorship problem:**
"The proposal went out with our firm name on it. The client assumed we wrote it. We used AI to generate the first draft and then human-edited it. The editing took longer than I expected. By the time we were done, I was not sure what the proposal said that was originally ours. The intellectual contribution is somewhere in the document. I cannot separate it from what the AI contributed."

**On client AI governance questionnaire responses:**
"We have started receiving AI governance questionnaires from clients. The questions are more detailed than I expected. I do not have answers for most of them. The ones I can answer, I am not sure the answers are the ones they want. We have been treating these as compliance exercises. They are actually sales conversations we are failing."

**On what competitive pressure is doing to AI adoption decisions:**
"The pressure to adopt AI is no longer about whether it helps us. It is about whether we look like we are not falling behind. That is a different decision. The decision used to be: is this useful? Now it is: does this make us look current? I am making adoption decisions for appearance reasons and I know it."

**On the deliverable quality conversation:**
"Clients are starting to ask questions about our deliverables that I cannot answer with confidence. Not about the conclusions — about how we reached them. The AI was involved in ways I did not track. When the client asks me to explain a recommendation, I am reconstructing the reasoning after the fact. That is a different kind of work than producing the recommendation."

**On sub-consultant AI delegation:**
"We use sub-consultants for specialized work. One of them uses AI extensively. The final deliverable has her contribution embedded in it. Our firm signed it. I have been thinking about what that means. I do not think it means what we assumed it means."

**On the documentation burden:**
"I have been trying to document where we use AI. After three months, I stopped. The documentation was taking more time than the AI was saving. The effort was costing us more than the benefit we were getting from using AI. I am not sure that is the right calculation but I am not sure how to make a better one."

## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Client AI governance questionnaires are sales conversations being failed**
Firms are treating client AI governance questionnaires as compliance exercises. The questionnaire is a signal of client sophistication and a relationship qualification opportunity. Firms without response capability are losing the conversation before it starts.

**Signal 2: Competitive adoption pressure has decoupled from utility**
Firms are making AI adoption decisions based on appearance rather than utility. The decision frame has shifted from "is this useful" to "does this make us look current." The decoupling is structural and unrecognized.

**Signal 3: Deliverable quality conversations are reconstruction conversations**
Clients are asking questions about how recommendations were reached that require firms to reconstruct reasoning after the fact. The reconstruction is a different kind of work than producing the recommendation and firms are not equipped for it.

**Signal 4: Sub-consultant AI delegation chain creates unexamined liability**
The firm signs deliverables containing sub-consultant contributions made through AI tools. The liability question is unexamined and unresolved. The delegation chain is accumulating across all active relationships.

**Signal 5: Documentation burden is producing a negative ROI calculation**
The effort to document AI usage is costing more than the benefit from AI usage in some configurations. The calculation is being made implicitly; the decision to stop documenting is being made without a framework for evaluating it.

**Signal 6: Proposal ghost authorship creating bilateral vulnerability**
The firm's proposals look firm-authored but the intellectual development happened inside an AI tool. The misattribution creates bilateral vulnerability for both firm and client.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Client AI governance questionnaire response capability as sales qualification product**
No Geneva consulting firm has developed a product for responding to client AI governance questionnaires that functions as a sales qualification conversation rather than a compliance exercise. The capability would convert a failing compliance interaction into a relationship qualification opportunity.

**Gap 2: Competitive adoption decision audit framework**
No framework exists for auditing whether AI adoption decisions are driven by utility or by competitive appearance pressure. The decoupling is structural and unrecognized; the framework would surface the decision frame being used.

**Gap 3: Reasoning reconstruction protocol for AI-assisted deliverables**
No protocol exists for reconstructing the reasoning that produced an AI-assisted recommendation. The protocol would need to address how to produce a reconstructable account of what the human evaluated versus what the AI contributed.

**Gap 4: Sub-consultant AI delegation chain verification methodology**
No methodology exists for verifying that confidentiality obligations are maintained through the sub-consultant AI vendor infrastructure. The verification would need to be achievable at boutique scale.

**Gap 5: Documentation burden ROI framework**
No framework exists for evaluating whether documentation effort produces a positive ROI relative to the AI usage it documents. The implicit calculation is being made without a named framework.

**Gap 6: Proposal authorship attribution methodology for AI-assisted proposals**
No methodology exists for managing intellectual property attribution when AI has contributed to proposals. The methodology would need to address consent, documentation, and client communication.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Professional liability under Art. 28 CO for AI-assisted deliverables:**
Art. 28 CO liability applies to deliverables bearing the firm's signature regardless of AI contribution. The documentation of AI's contribution to errors is unresolved.

**Confidentiality obligations for client data in AI systems:**
Client data processed through AI tools creates confidentiality obligations under engagement letters and professional duty. The intersection with vendor terms of service is not addressed in standard engagement language.

**FINMA MiFID suitability documentation for AI-assisted recommendations:**
MiFID suitability obligations apply to recommendations made with AI contribution. The documentation requirements for AI-assisted suitability analysis are not defined at boutique scale.

**nFADP Article 6 legitimate interest for internal AI processing:**
Internal AI processing of client data requires a legal basis under nFADP Article 6. The legitimate interest assessment for AI processing of client work product has not been published.

**Proposal intellectual property under Swiss copyright law:**
Swiss copyright law vests authorship in the natural person who created the work. AI-generated content in proposals creates copyright attribution questions no court has addressed.

**EU AI Act downstream deployer obligations for consulting firms:**
Consulting firms using AI tools for client deliverables are downstream deployers under the EU AI Act. The Article 26 documentation requirements are not adapted for consulting firm use cases.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Client AI governance questionnaire response capability as undifferentiated sales qualification opportunity**
Firms are treating client AI governance questionnaires as compliance exercises. The same interaction, handled with response capability, functions as a sales qualification conversation. No Geneva consulting firm has named this conversion opportunity.

**Finding 2: Competitive adoption pressure has decoupled from utility analysis as structural recognition problem**
Firms are making AI adoption decisions based on competitive appearance pressure rather than utility analysis. The decoupling is structural and unrecognized. The decision frame being used is not the decision frame that should be used.

**Finding 3: Deliverable quality conversation requiring reasoning reconstruction is a named capability gap**
Clients asking about how recommendations were reached requires firms to reconstruct reasoning after the fact. The reconstruction is a different kind of work than producing the recommendation. No protocol exists for producing reconstructable reasoning records.

**Finding 4: Sub-consultant AI delegation chain creates bilateral liability without verification architecture**
The firm signs deliverables containing sub-consultant contributions made through AI tools. The delegation chain creates bilateral liability for both firm and client. No verification methodology exists at boutique scale.

**Finding 5: Documentation burden ROI calculation is implicit and negative for some boutique configurations**
The effort to document AI usage produces a negative ROI in some configurations. The calculation is implicit; the decision to stop documenting is made without a framework. The negative ROI configuration is unnamed.

**Finding 6: Proposal ghost authorship creating client expectation misalignment at the relationship level**
The firm's proposals look firm-authored but intellectual development happened inside an AI tool. Clients receiving proposals have expectations about firm reasoning that the proposal cannot satisfy. The misalignment operates at the relationship level before any formal engagement question arises.
