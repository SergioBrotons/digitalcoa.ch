## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Privilege and data residency convergence**
The attorney is beginning to understand that the privilege question and the data residency question are the same question. When client information is processed by an AI system outside the contracted data architecture, both questions are activated simultaneously. This convergence is new and unaddressed.

**Signal 2: Operational privilege documentation gap**
The gap is no longer conceptual. Attorneys understand in the abstract that AI use creates privilege questions. The operational question — what to document, where, in what format — has no answer. The documentation does not exist.

**Signal 3: Bar guidance principles-to-operations gap**
The bar guidance available is principles-based. Attorneys need operational answers: what exactly to do, what to document, how to answer the client question. The gap between principles and operations is preventing action even among attorneys who want to act.

**Signal 4: AI processing outside contracted architecture activating both privilege and residency**
The attorney who adopted AI tools incrementally now has AI processing happening in systems they did not explicitly contract for. Each of these systems represents a point where client information was processed outside the intended architecture, activating both privilege and data residency obligations simultaneously.

**Signal 5: Associate training creating unsupervised AI use patterns**
Associates trained on AI tools before they were trained on cases are starting from AI and working backward. Their AI use patterns are unsupervised by default because the supervising attorneys cannot evaluate what the AI is doing. The training has outpaced the supervision infrastructure.

**Signal 6: Client sophistication creating pressure to document**
Clients are becoming more sophisticated about asking how their matters are handled. The attorney's inability to answer with precision is creating pressure that is beginning to feel uncomfortable. The pressure to document is arriving from the client side, not just the regulatory side.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Operational AI privilege documentation system for small firms**
What would operational privilege documentation look like for a 2–5 attorney firm? Not a policy — a documentation system that produces the records the attorney needs when the question arises. No one has built this for the small Geneva firm.

**Gap 2: Data residency privilege mapping service**
A service that maps where client information actually flows through AI tools — including tools adopted incrementally — and identifies where the privilege and residency questions converge. The actual data flow, not the contracted data flow.

**Gap 3: Associate AI use supervision framework for small firms**
A supervision framework designed for the reality that associates are more AI-proficient than the partners supervising them. The framework has to work at small firm scale and has to be operationally implementable.

**Gap 4: Client privilege disclosure document for AI-assisted matters**
A disclosure document the attorney can provide to clients explaining how AI is used in their matter — in plain language, legally precise, operationally bounded. The document the attorney can actually give to the client.

**Gap 5: Bar guidance operational translation**
Someone who can translate bar principles-based guidance into operationally specific recommendations for the small Geneva firm. The translation layer between principles and operations is missing.

**Gap 6: AI vendor terms privilege audit**
An audit of AI vendor terms against privilege obligations — identifying which vendors create privilege transfers, what the exposure is, and what the remediation options are. A discrete, nameable service.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**SVA/ORDRE obligations:**
The supervision obligation under SVA/ORDRE requires that supervising attorneys be capable of evaluating the work they supervise. When the work is AI-assisted and the supervisor cannot evaluate the AI component, the supervision obligation is structurally unmet. The obligation exists; the capability to fulfill it does not.

**nFADP Articles 6, 12, and 21:**
Article 6 requires a lawful basis for personal data processing. AI processing of client files in systems not explicitly contracted for may lack a documented lawful basis. Article 12 requires transparency about automated decision-making. Article 21 creates rights that attorneys may not be equipped to fulfill for AI-assisted decisions affecting clients.

**FDPIC enforcement implications for attorneys:**
The FDPIC has not issued attorney-specific guidance. The practical risk is that general enforcement patterns apply to attorney contexts where privilege principles create a structural collision with data subject rights obligations. The enforcement pattern is observable and learnable.

**Geneva Cantonal Bar obligations:**
The cantonal bar rules require that attorneys maintain client file confidentiality and supervise matters adequately. AI tool use that is not systematically documented may create cantonal compliance gaps. The cantonal obligations are additional to, and partially distinct from, federal obligations.

**Cross-border privilege interaction:**
When a Swiss attorney handles a matter involving a client from a jurisdiction with different privilege standards, the AI processing of that client's information may implicate the foreign jurisdiction's privilege rules. The cross-border dimension adds a layer of complexity that the domestic-only attorney has not had to manage.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Privilege and data residency convergence as single diagnostic question**
The question "was this file processed by AI?" now implicates both privilege and data residency simultaneously. The two obligations have converged into a single diagnostic question. The attorney who cannot answer it faces exposure on both dimensions.

**Finding 2: Operational documentation gap preventing action despite willingness**
Attorneys understand the conceptual problem and want to act. The operational documentation system — what to produce, in what format, where to store it — does not exist at small firm scale. Willingness to act is not translating into action.

**Finding 3: Bar guidance principles-to-operations gap blocking implementation**
The bar guidance is principles-based. The questions attorneys bring are operational. The translation layer between principles and operations is missing. This gap is preventing systematic implementation across the Geneva bar.

**Finding 4: AI processing outside contracted architecture creating silent exposure**
AI tools adopted incrementally have created processing paths that were never explicitly contracted for. Client information is flowing through systems the attorney did not design or authorize. The exposure is silent because the attorney does not know it is happening.

**Finding 5: Associate AI training outpacing supervision infrastructure**
Associates trained on AI before cases are using AI as the primary work surface. The supervising partners cannot evaluate what the AI is doing. The training has outpaced the supervision infrastructure by design, not by accident.

**Finding 6: Client pressure arriving from client side, not just regulatory side**
The pressure to document is beginning to come from clients asking specific questions. The attorney's inability to answer credibly is becoming a client relationship risk. The regulatory pressure and the client relationship pressure are now simultaneous.
