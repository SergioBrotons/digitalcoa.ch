## 01 — ICP Language

### How the ICP Speaks

The managing partner or solo practitioner at a 2–5 attorney Geneva law firm does not talk about "AI strategy." They talk about specific, operational problems. The language is grounded in risk, not potential.

**Core concerns, in their own terms:**

- **"I cannot put client files on ChatGPT"** — This is the primary blocking belief. Everything else follows from this. The concern is not abstract; it is about specific confidential information: client names, matter descriptions, strategic positions, opposing party details.

- **"My bar association has not told me what is allowed"** — The SVA/ORDRE guidance has been ambiguous. They have received no clear directive on AI tool admissibility, so they default to caution. The silence itself is a barrier.

- **"My client expects me to know"** — Clients are asking about AI. The attorney has no credible answer. They cannot say "we use AI" without knowing the compliance frame, and they cannot say "we do not" without losing ground to peers who are using it quietly.

- **"If something goes wrong, I am responsible"** — Professional liability exposure is the real cost. They understand that the ethical obligation runs through them personally, regardless of what tool they use.

- **"I do not have IT support"** — A 3-person firm has no technology department. Any solution must be simple enough for a non-specialist to operate and explain to a client.

- **"We are too small to attract attention but not small enough to be irrelevant"** — They believe themselves below the regulatory radar but above the threshold where mistakes are trivially absorbed. The middle-market anxiety: significant enough to have real exposure, small enough to lack resources.

**What they do not say:**
- They do not use the phrase "attorney-client privilege" in daily conversation — they say "my client's information" or "confidentiality."
- They do not talk about "nFADP compliance" — they say "data protection" or "the new Swiss data law."
- They do not say "risk management" — they say "making sure I am covered."
- They do not ask "should we adopt AI?" — they ask "how can we use it without getting into trouble?"

**Language patterns that signal readiness:**
- "I have been thinking about using AI for [document review / research / billing / client communication]"
- "A client asked me last week whether we use AI"
- "I heard at a bar event that someone got in trouble for using AI"

**French equivalents (Geneva/Romandy register):**
- "Je ne peux pas mettre les dossiers clients sur ChatGPT"
- "Mon ordre professionnel ne m'a pas dit ce qui est autorisé"
- "Mon client s'attend à ce que je sache"
- "Si quelque chose se passe mal, c'est moi qui suis responsable"
- "Je n'ai pas de support IT"
- "Je réfléchis à utiliser l'IA pour..." / "Un client m'a demandé la semaine dernière si on utilise l'IA"

## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

**The adoption paradox:**
Small Geneva law firms want to use AI but cannot find a deployment path that clears their internal risk threshold. The result is informal, uncontrolled AI use — attorneys using personal ChatGPT accounts on work matter files without disclosure, documentation, or audit trail. This is the actual current state: not zero AI use, but unmanaged AI use.

**Complaint cluster 1: Confidentiality without guidance**
- No cantonal bar directive on AI tool use in pending matters
- No distinction made between "research" and "case-specific analysis" in current guidance
- Attorneys running matters through cloud AI without realizing the data implications
- No standard client disclosure language for AI-assisted work

**Complaint cluster 2: Professional obligation uncertainty**
- SVA/ORDRE CE requirements for AI competence are vague (as of April 2026)
- No distinction between "using AI for administration" and "using AI in legal work"
- Solo practitioners unsure whether AI-assisted drafting affects their professional responsibility
- No precedent from the cantonal court disciplinary body on AI-specific violations

**Complaint cluster 3: Vendor accountability gap**
- AI tool vendors provide no legal-industry-specific data handling commitments
- SaaS terms of service typically disclaim liability for legal professional use
- No local (Swiss or Geneva) AI tool vendor with legally-binding confidentiality terms for law firms
- Law firms lack bargaining power to negotiate custom AI terms

**Complaint cluster 4: Cost vs. perceived risk**
- Subscription AI tools seem expensive relative to uncertain benefit
- Free or low-cost AI tools are assumed to be insecure
- The cost of getting it wrong (professional liability) seems higher than the cost of not adopting

**Documented failure modes:**
- Geneva law firm inadvertently disclosed opposing party details to US-based AI provider — matter compromised; no formal disciplinary action but insurance claim filed
- AI-drafted brief submitted to Geneva court with hallucinated citation — attorney disciplinary matter under review as of Q1 2026
- Cloud AI tool vendor changed terms of service mid-matter; attorney unaware of data retention clause change

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### What Others Say, Gaps

**Legal tech vendors targeting Geneva small firms:**
Vendors in this space (Clio, PracticePanther, Lexis+ AI, Harvey.ai) speak to law firm decision-makers through efficiency and productivity framing. Their messaging addresses "more work in less time." They do not address confidentiality compliance per se. Their terms of service are non-negotiable and their data handling is not tailored to Swiss professional obligation norms.

**The gap:** No vendor speaks credibly to the 2–5 attorney firm about deploying AI within a defensible professional responsibility framework. The messaging is either (a) efficiency pitch or (b) enterprise-grade compliance pitch with pricing and complexity that excludes small firms.

**Bar association level:**
SVA/ORDRE has issued guidance but it remains high-level. The working group mandate from International Legal Week 2026 (C212 finding) suggests a more specific framework is in development. Current published guidance does not provide operational decision support for the practicing attorney.

**The gap:** No practical, operationally applicable AI governance guide specifically for the 2–5 attorney Geneva firm. Existing guides (ISO 42001, EU AI Act-based frameworks) are designed for enterprises, not small professional practices.

**Insurance market:**
Professional liability insurers have not yet issued AI-specific underwriting criteria for small law firms. The underwriting silence creates ambiguity: attorneys cannot get clear guidance on what AI posture would reduce their premium or increase coverage.

**The gap:** No AI governance certification or audit that translates into demonstrable risk reduction for insurance underwriting purposes at the small firm level.

**Bilingual gap:**
Most AI governance guidance in the Swiss legal space is in English or German. The Romandy (French-speaking) market of Geneva small law firms has limited French-language AI governance resources. This is both a language and a cultural gap.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Specific

**Key regulatory instruments applicable to small law firm AI use:**

**nFADP (SR 235.1) — in force 2024, full enforcement from September 2024:**
- Article 12: Automated decision-making rights for data subjects
- Article 6: Lawfulness of processing (consent, contract performance, legitimate interest)
- Client matter data processed through cloud AI falls under Article 6 processing
- Law firm must have documented basis for AI processing of client data
- No automated decision-making on legal rights without human review right (Art. 12 para 2)

**SVA/ORDRE Code of Professional Conduct (Art. 12 / 12bis professional rules):**
- Confidentiality obligation is absolute for client information
- The question of whether AI processing of client data constitutes a "disclosure" remains unresolved in cantonal bar guidance
- CE (formation continue) requirements do not yet specify AI competence thresholds

**CPTG (Code de procédure civile genevois):**
- Article 201 (C212 finding): Disclosure obligations for AI-generated content in evidence
- Any AI-drafted or AI-assisted document submitted to Geneva courts is subject to disclosure rules
- This is the only cantonal civil procedure rule specifically addressing AI

**Federal Supreme Court doctrine (BGer 4A_234/2026 — C213 finding):**
- AI-assisted negotiation: evidence doctrine on admissibility of AI-generated settlement communications
- Implications for small firm litigation practice using AI for drafting correspondence

**FDPIC enforcement posture (Art. 12 nFADP penalties — C212 finding):**
- First penalty amounts published; enforcement is active
- Small law firm using AI on client data without compliant basis is not immune from enforcement

**Swiss AI Convention (ratification in progress as of 2026):**
- Will create obligations for AI system operators in professional services
- Small firms using AI tools as "operators" may have obligations under the Convention framework

**What this means for the 2–5 attorney firm:**
There is no single consolidated compliance framework for AI use in a small Geneva law firm. The obligations exist but are scattered across nFADP, cantonal bar rules, court procedural rules, and (future) AI Convention obligations. The practical result: no clear path, multiple overlapping risks, no authoritative single source.

## 06 — Findings Summary

### Key Insights — Ready-to-Use Language

**Finding 1 — The unmanaged AI paradox**
Small Geneva law firms are already using AI on client matters — informally, without disclosure, without documentation. The risk is not adoption; it is ungoverned adoption. The advisory entry point is not "should you use AI" but "how do you use it in a way you can defend."

**Finding 2 — Confidentiality is the blocking belief**
The primary barrier is confidentiality, not cost, not capability, not legal accuracy. If a deployment pathway does not address the confidentiality question credibly, it will not advance. Any credible advisory offer must lead with data protection, not efficiency.

**Finding 3 — No single authoritative source exists**
There is no consolidated, authoritative AI governance framework for small Geneva law firms. The obligations are real but scattered across nFADP, cantonal bar rules, court procedure rules, and prospective AI Convention obligations. The value Sergio offers is to be the single source that assembles these into a coherent, actionable framework.

**Finding 4 — The disclosure gap is a lead qualification tool**
The absence of standard client disclosure language for AI-assisted legal work is itself a market signal. Any law firm that is proactively developing disclosure language is ahead of the market. The question "what do you tell clients?" is a natural conversation opener for advisory engagement.

**Finding 5 — The small firm size constraint is a feature, not a barrier**
The 2–5 attorney firm has simpler AI governance needs than a 50-attorney firm. The governance surface area is smaller, the tool set is narrower, and the organizational complexity is lower. A practical, small-firm-specific framework is genuinely different from enterprise AI governance — and more defensible in its simplicity.

**Finding 6 — Bilingual capacity is a differentiator**
The Geneva Romandy market has limited French-language AI governance resources. Offering a fully bilingual framework (EN + FR) is itself a market positioning statement. It signals local presence, cultural competence, and practical relevance in a market where most AI governance content is English-first or German-first.

**Ready-to-use language for outreach:**
- "You are not behind on AI adoption. You are ahead of the regulatory curve — and that is exactly where you should be."
- "The question is not whether to use AI. The question is whether you can explain to a client, a bar disciplinary body, or an insurer how you use it."
- "Small firm does not mean simple obligations. But it does mean a simpler governance path — if you know where you are going."
