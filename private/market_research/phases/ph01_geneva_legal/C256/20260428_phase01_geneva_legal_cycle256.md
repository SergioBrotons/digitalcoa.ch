## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Specific Regulatory Obligations

**SVA (Schweizerische Anwaltsverband / Swiss Lawyers Association) and ORDRE (Geneva Bar Association):**
- Neither the SVA nor the ORDRE has issued guidance on the retrospective AI practice history question — specifically, what a small firm is expected to be able to demonstrate about its AI-assisted practice history when asked by a carrier, a client, or a bar inquiry, and whether the absence of documentation from 2023–2025 creates any independent professional obligation or exposure
- Neither body has addressed the procedural status of informal opposing-party AI disclosure demands in litigation — whether such demands must be answered, whether they can be refused, and what professional consequences attach to various response choices
- The SVA working group on AI has not produced guidance on nFADP Art. 12 access requests for AI-processed client data — specifically, what the small firm is expected to produce when a client requests information about automated processing in a matter where AI was used without tracking
- Neither body has addressed whether the carrier third-generation AI questionnaire attestation creates any independent bar disciplinary exposure — specifically, whether the personal signature on the attestation is a certification that the bar would treat as a professional conduct question if the responses are later found to be inaccurate or incomplete
- The ORDRE has not clarified the privilege status of AI tool version histories and model configuration records — whether these records are protected work product or discoverable professional records in the context of a bar inquiry or civil procedure

**nFADP (revised Federal Act on Data Protection, in force 2024, ordinance phase):**
- Art. 12 data subject rights to information about automated processing create specific obligations for the firm that has used AI on client matters: the client who requests information about automated processing has a statutory right to specific information that the firm must produce from its own records
- Art. 6 lawful basis requirements apply to all client data processed through AI tools in use at the firm over the full period of AI deployment — including tools used before the current nFADP ordinance framework was in place
- The nFADP ordinance has not provided a discharge methodology for access requests where the AI processing activity was not tracked — leaving the small firm in the position of having a statutory obligation it cannot discharge from its own records because the records were never created
- Vendor term updates that changed data processing terms during the 2023–2025 deployment period may affect the lawful basis validity for data processed during those periods — and the firm has no systematic mechanism for assessing which vendor updates affected which processing periods

**FDPIC (Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner):**
- The FDPIC's investigative pattern — system-level audits, complaint-driven investigations — means that a client complaint about AI data processing at a Geneva law firm could trigger an investigation into the firm's AI processing records, AI disclosure practices, and data residency documentation
- The firm that cannot produce AI processing records in response to an FDPIC inquiry faces a specific investigative exposure: the absence of records looks like inadequate governance, regardless of whether the underlying AI use was appropriate
- The FDPIC has not issued guidance on the small firm's obligations with respect to AI model version history records — specifically, whether the firm is expected to maintain records of which model version processed which client data, and what the retention period for such records would be under nFADP Art. 8 retention limitation principles

**Bar Rules (Swiss Lawyers Act / BGFA; ORDRE disciplinary rules):**
- The confidentiality obligation (BGFA Art. 12 / ORDRE rules) applies to AI use, but has not been analyzed for the specific scenario where the firm's AI history is undocumented — the question of whether the inability to account for AI processing in a specific matter constitutes a different category of professional failure than the intentional disclosure of confidential information
- The professional obligation to maintain adequate supervision applies to AI use, but has not been analyzed for the scenario where the supervising attorney cannot account for what AI was used on which matters during the 2023–2025 deployment period — creating a supervision gap that predates the supervisory framework
- The ORDRE has not addressed whether the carrier third-generation AI questionnaire attestation creates any independent bar disciplinary exposure — the specific question of whether the personal signature on the carrier attestation is a certification that the bar would treat as a professional conduct question if the responses are later found to be inaccurate or incomplete
- Disciplinary precedent for the retrospective accountability dimension of undocumented AI history at Geneva small firms remains entirely absent — meaning the attorney navigating carrier renewals, access requests, and opposing-party demands is doing so without any published disciplinary reference point
