## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Client matter portability emerging as discrete AI governance problem with no Geneva protocol**
Firms are discovering that AI-assisted matter transfer creates documentation obligations they cannot discharge. The research foundation built on a proprietary AI platform cannot be simply transferred to a receiving firm that uses a different platform or no AI platform. No Geneva protocol exists for managing the AI research component of a matter transfer. The problem is distinct from data export under nFADP — it involves the intellectual output of AI-assisted research, not just the raw client data.

**Signal 2: Billing records as de facto AI documentation record with structural misalignment to carrier evidence standards**
Geneva small firms bill by the hour and record AI-assisted work in billing notes. Those notes become the only contemporaneous record of what the AI produced. When carriers begin examining AI governance, billing records will be the primary evidence of what AI did on each matter. The documentation architecture — hourly billing optimized for commercial record, not accountability record — is structurally misaligned with what carrier evidence standards will require. Firms using billing records as AI documentation do not know this is insufficient.

**Signal 3: nFADP 24-month retrospective exposure window for pre-governance AI processing**
The nFADP 24-month lookback for data subject rights applies to processing that occurred before the firm had any AI governance framework. For many Geneva small firms, this means AI tools were processing client data for 12–24 months with no documentation of what inferences were drawn, what purposes were served, or what retention occurred. The retrospective window is present-tense and active. No Geneva service has built a retrospective AI processing audit for this specific window.

**Signal 4: Opposing counsel AI methodology observation as emerging litigation intelligence practice**
Geneva attorneys are beginning to observe AI-assisted work patterns in opposing filings — similar case sequences, comparable analytical framings, identical search-result-probability signatures. The observation creates an intelligence opportunity (knowing what AI surfaced for the other side) without a corresponding analytical framework for what the observation means procedurally or strategically. No Geneva protocol exists for evaluating whether cross-firm AI pattern observation constitutes litigation intelligence or something procedurally different.

**Signal 5: Formal delegation architecture absence creating undocumented personal liability distribution**
Small firm managing partners are responsible for AI-assisted work product without having made formal delegation decisions. The legal responsibility and the practical delegation are misaligned: there is no document defining what the partner reviewed, what they did not review, and what they are certifying by signing. The absence of delegation architecture means the partner's personal liability extends to AI decisions they did not consciously authorize or review. No Geneva service has produced the formal AI delegation documentation standard for 2–5 attorney firms.

**Signal 6: Pre-knowledge AI exposure window as distinct category from new adoption exposure**
The problem is not adoption — it is the period before adoption awareness. Firms that adopted AI 18–24 months ago have 18–24 months of AI-assisted work with no governance framework. Firms adopting today arrive with more awareness. The pre-awareness window creates different exposure than new adoption: retrospective rather than prospective, documentation-reconstruction rather than documentation-building. No Geneva market language distinguishes these as separate exposure categories.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Client matter transfer and AI research portability under Swiss professional obligations:**
No Geneva bar guidance or civil procedure rule addresses the professional liability and professional secrecy obligations surrounding AI-assisted research when a client matter transfers between firms. The originating firm holds research generated through its AI platform. The receiving firm has no right to that platform's infrastructure. The client has a right to their matter's documentation. The intersection creates a portability problem with no published resolution. Attorneys manage it case by case, without protocol.

**nFADP Articles 13, 15, and 22 simultaneous retrospective activation:**
AI processing that occurred in the 24 months before a firm established AI governance framework activates Article 13 (access to personal data including AI-inferred content), Article 15 (correction of inaccurate AI-inferred data), and Article 22 (data processing register for activities conducted before the register existed). These obligations apply to processing the firm cannot fully reconstruct. The simultaneous activation across three articles creates compliance exposure that single-framework assessments do not address.

**Attorney work product liability and formal AI delegation documentation:**
Swiss professional liability doctrine holds attorneys responsible for work product regardless of whether the production was AI-assisted. The question of what formal documentation distinguishes delegated AI production from certified personal production has not been addressed by any Geneva bar guidance or published standard. Without formal delegation documentation, the managing partner's signature certifying work product covers AI-assisted production without any recorded authorization or review specification.

**Opposing counsel AI methodology observation and procedural implications:**
Swiss civil procedure does not address whether attorneys have obligations when they observe that opposing counsel's filings show AI-assisted analytical patterns. The observation is not evidence in any formal sense. The analytical framework for evaluating what the observation means procedurally — and whether it creates any obligation to investigate, disclose, or respond — does not exist in Geneva procedural doctrine.

**Platform terms and matter transfer data rights:**
AI platform terms of service define data ownership and portability rights. These terms were not written with Geneva attorney professional secrecy obligations in view. When a matter transfers, the platform's data portability terms may conflict with the professional secrecy obligations of the originating attorney. No Geneva platform has published an analysis of how its terms interact with Art. 321 CP for matter transfer scenarios.

**Pre-governance AI processing as distinct compliance category:**
The period before a firm established AI governance framework is a distinct compliance category with its own obligations, exposure profile, and resolution pathway. Geneva compliance discourse has not named this category separately from general AI governance gaps. The distinction matters because the obligations are retrospective, the evidence is limited or absent, and the resolution requires a different methodology than building new governance frameworks.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Client matter AI research portability as a discrete unnamed professional obligation distinct from data export**
When a client transfers to a new firm, the AI-assisted research foundation built on the originating firm's platform has no defined transfer pathway under Geneva bar guidance or Swiss civil procedure. The problem is structurally distinct from nFADP data portability (which addresses personal data, not AI-generated analytical output) and from traditional matter transfer (which addresses documents the originating firm physically holds, not AI research stored on a vendor platform). The conflict between platform data ownership terms and the professional secrecy obligations of the originating attorney creates a documentation gap with no published resolution. This finding names client matter AI research portability as a discrete professional obligation category requiring its own protocol architecture.

*Finding 1 is distinct from C214–C282 Phase 01: prior cycles addressed AI platform migration between vendor systems (C282 Finding 4), engagement letter pre-AI audit for active matters (C281 Finding 3), and general matter transfer documentation standards, but none identified the specific client matter AI research portability problem — where the AI research output of an active matter is subject to competing claims from the platform terms, the professional secrecy obligations of the originating firm, the data rights of the client, and the infrastructure of the receiving firm, with no Geneva protocol resolving which obligation takes precedence or what the transfer documentation must contain.*

---

**Finding 2: nFADP 24-month retrospective window activating for pre-governance AI processing as distinct compliance category**
The nFADP lookback window captures AI processing that occurred before the firm had any governance framework — a period of 12–24 months for most early adopters. The obligations that activate are present-tense: access rights for AI-inferred content, correction rights for inaccurate AI inferences, and data processing register entries for processing that predates the register's existence. These obligations apply to processing the firm cannot fully reconstruct because the documentation was never built. This finding names pre-governance AI processing as a distinct compliance category from new adoption exposure, requiring a different audit methodology focused on retrospective reconstruction rather than prospective framework building.

*Finding 2 is distinct from C214–C282 Phase 01: prior cycles addressed nFADP Article 22 data processing register (C282 Finding 2), Article 13/21 obligations (C270, C279), and general FDPIC enforcement trajectory (C279, C280), but none identified the specific 24-month retrospective window for pre-governance AI processing as a discrete compliance category requiring its own audit methodology — different from new adoption governance (which is prospective) and from general data subject request infrastructure (which does not address the specific challenge of reconstructing AI-inferred content from undocumented processing periods).*

---

**Finding 3: Formal AI delegation documentation standard absent as structural liability distribution mechanism**
Managing partners at 2–5 attorney Geneva firms are personally responsible for AI-assisted work product without having made formal delegation decisions. Swiss professional liability doctrine holds the signing attorney responsible regardless of delegation — but without documented delegation, there is no record of what the partner reviewed, what they authorized, and what they are certifying by signing. The absence of formal delegation documentation means the partner's personal liability extends to AI decisions that were made without conscious authorization or defined review scope. This finding identifies formal AI delegation documentation as a distinct market service: not training, not supervision architecture, but the specific documentation artifact that formalizes the delegation relationship and distributes liability appropriately between partner and associate.

*Finding 3 is distinct from C214–C282 Phase 01: prior cycles addressed associate AI competency supervision architectural inversion (C281 Finding 2), partner signature concentration (C282 Finding 6), and general professional liability exposure for AI-assisted work, but none identified the specific formal AI delegation documentation standard as a discrete documentation artifact with its own required content, distinct from supervision architecture, general quality control, or carrier attestation — a document that formalizes what the partner reviewed, what they are delegating, what they are certifying, and the liability distribution between partner and associate in a 2–5 attorney firm.*

---

**Finding 4: Billing record as structurally misaligned AI documentation architecture with carrier evidence standard gap**
Geneva small firms use billing records as the contemporaneous documentation of AI-assisted work — because billing is what attorneys record, and AI output appears in billing notes. Carrier third-generation AI evidence standards are examining what AI decisions were made on each matter, what review preceded them, and what documentation exists. The billing record architecture was designed for commercial recording, not accountability documentation: it captures that time was spent, not what the AI did, what inferences it drew, or what review the partner performed. This finding identifies the specific structural misalignment between billing-record documentation and carrier AI evidence requirements as a discrete documentation architecture problem — addressable through proportionate documentation improvements that do not require restructuring billing practice.

*Finding 4 is distinct from C214–C282 Phase 01: prior cycles addressed documentation quality for AI-assisted work (C276, C277), documentation system coherence (C274, C275 Phase 02), and billing model structural incompatibility with AI efficiency (C273, C275 Phase 03), but none identified the specific billing-record-to-carrier-evidence-gap as a discrete documentation architecture misalignment with a specific resolution pathway that does not require firms to restructure how they bill — different from general documentation quality improvement or billing model reform, this finding isolates the specific gap between what billing records document and what carrier evidence standards require, and what proportionate improvements close the gap.*

**Finding 6: Pre-awareness AI exposure window as distinct category from new adoption exposure requiring retrospective not prospective methodology**
The exposure for firms that adopted AI 18–24 months ago is categorically different from the exposure for firms adopting AI today. Early adopters have retrospective exposure: obligations from the past that are now present-tense under nFADP, documentation gaps from periods when no governance framework existed, and liability from AI decisions made before the firm understood what decisions AI was making. New adopters have prospective exposure: obligations that will form as frameworks develop, documentation requirements that will apply going forward, and liability that can be managed through governance built before exposure accumulates. This finding names pre-awareness exposure as a distinct assessment category requiring a retrospective methodology — documentation reconstruction rather than framework building — that no Geneva service has offered as a discrete service.

*Finding 6 is distinct from C214–C282 Phase 01: prior cycles addressed AI governance framework building for current and future AI use (C276, C277, C278), carrier attestation pre-signing review (C281 Finding 1, C282), and general documentation adequacy for AI-assisted work, but none identified the specific distinction between pre-awareness retrospective exposure (obligations from past AI processing now present-tense) and new adoption prospective exposure (obligations that will form going forward) — a distinction that requires fundamentally different methodology: retrospective audit and documentation reconstruction for early adopters versus prospective framework building for new adopters. The finding names pre-awareness exposure as a discrete assessment category with its own methodology.*
