## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### How the ICP Actually Describes Their AI Problems and Concerns — Real Language

The managing partner or solo practitioner at a 2–5 attorney Geneva firm has arrived at a new internal state that prior cycles have not captured with precision: they have made peace with using AI internally, but have not resolved the question of what they owe their clients, their profession, and themselves when the AI-assisted work product leaves the building. The anxiety has shifted from "should I use this" to "what am I responsible for when it goes wrong, and how would I know."

This is a firm that has been through a governance review, has a policy on paper, and has now discovered that the gap between internal AI practice and external AI accountability — what the client sees, what the court sees, what the bar can reconstruct — is where the exposure lives. The ICP in C225 is no longer asking whether AI use is appropriate. They are asking what adequate professional accountability for AI-assisted legal work actually looks like, and whether their current practice meets that standard.

**Core concerns in their own register:**

- "I have a governance policy now. What I do not have is a way to show a client, a court, or the bar what my AI practice actually looks like when they ask for it. The policy document describes the intention. It does not describe the Tuesday afternoon reality." — Documented governance without practice-representative evidence. The attorney who has invested in a governance review and has a policy on paper has discovered that producing the policy is not the same as producing evidence of practice. When the question comes — from a client conducting a governance review, a court asking about AI use in a proceeding, an insurer assessing coverage conditions — the attorney can show the document but not the practice. This is distinct from "having no policy" — it is the specific problem of governance documentation that is accurate in intent and unrepresentative in fact.

- "The question I keep asking myself is not 'did I use AI' but 'can I reconstruct what I used AI for, when, and what I did with the output.' That reconstruction is not something I can do from my files as they currently exist." — Retrospective AI practice reconstruction as an accountability problem. The attorney who has been using AI for an extended period — now routinely 18–24 months in C225 — has an AI practice history that exists only in memory. The case file does not contain AI usage metadata. When the need arises to reconstruct past AI use — a client data subject request, a court inquiry, a bar disciplinary question, an insurer review — the attorney finds they cannot do it from the file. The reconstruction problem is now a standing professional accountability risk, not just an abstract future scenario.

- "I have been thinking about what my obligation is if an AI tool that I use for legal research changes its model — does my governance review still apply? The vendor made the change. I did not change my governance. Is the governance document still accurate?" — Post-model-update governance staleness as an unexamined exposure. The AI vendor has updated the model underlying the tool the firm relies on. The governance review was conducted against the prior model. The capabilities, error rates, data processing patterns, and output characteristics may have changed. The firm's governance documentation was accurate when written and may now be materially incomplete — without the firm having any mechanism to know it. This is a specific structural gap: governance documents are point-in-time documents in a domain where the underlying technology changes continuously.

- "When a client sends me an AI-generated brief — which is happening more often — and asks me to review and file it, I do not have a framework for what my professional obligation is. I did not generate the content. I am being asked to put my name on it. Where does my responsibility begin and end?" — Client-supplied AI content entering firm workflow as unowned professional exposure. The client's law firm or in-house team has used AI to generate a brief or legal analysis and sent it to the Geneva attorney for review, filing, or integration into a larger matter. The Geneva attorney is now being asked to take professional responsibility for work product they did not originate. The supervisory obligation — what constitutes sufficient review of content generated by the client's AI system, not the firm's — is undefined and unexamined. The attorney who signs and files a client-supplied AI document has taken on professional exposure without a defined review standard.

- "I have a professional liability insurance renewal coming up. The questionnaire has more AI questions than last year. I do not know what answers protect me and what answers create a coverage problem. I have been guessing." — Insurance renewal as an AI governance exposure crystallisation moment. The annual insurance renewal process has become, in C225, a specific trigger point for professional anxiety. The AI questions on the renewal form are more detailed than prior years. The attorney does not know what the insurer's position on AI coverage is, what level of AI use would change the coverage terms, or whether their current AI practice would be characterized as more or less risky by the underwriter. The attorney is completing the questionnaire without a clear understanding of what the answers mean for coverage — and some are answering defensively (minimizing AI use disclosure to avoid coverage implications) in ways that may themselves constitute a misrepresentation.

- "I have been in this practice for twenty years. I built my professional judgment on the basis of having read, analyzed, and decided. AI is changing the basis on which I form that judgment — and I do not have a way to tell whether my judgment is still my own or whether it has been quietly shaped by AI suggestions I absorbed without noticing." — Professional judgment authorship anxiety as a distinct dimension of AI exposure. The senior attorney who has spent decades developing legal judgment is now noticing that AI-suggested analysis, case references, and drafting alternatives are entering their professional reasoning — sometimes visibly, sometimes not. The question of whether professional judgment that has been shaped by continuous AI exposure remains "the attorney's own judgment" is a new category of professional identity concern. It is distinct from accuracy (is the output correct) and distinct from supervision (did I review it) — it is about whether the cognitive process that produced the professional decision is still the attorney's own. This concern has no operational resolution — there is no audit mechanism for professional judgment authorship — which is precisely why it is surfacing in the ICP's own language.

## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

**Signal 1 — Governance Documentation Without Practice-Representative Evidence Is the New Primary Exposure**
The market has moved past "no AI governance policy" and past "governance policy without operational conformance." The new primary exposure is the firm that has a governance document that is accurate in intent but unrepresentative in fact — and cannot produce evidence of practice when asked. The client governance review, the court inquiry, the insurer assessment, and the bar disciplinary question all ask for something the policy document cannot provide: a reconstruction of actual AI practice. The firm that has invested in governance documentation without investing in practice-representative evidence is more exposed than it believes, because the documentation creates the appearance of governance that the evidence does not support.

**Signal 2 — Extended Undocumented AI Use Has Created a Reconstruction Debt That Cannot Be Retrospectively Resolved**
The attorney who has been using AI for 18–24 months without a contemporaneous documentation practice now faces a reconstruction debt that is genuinely unresolvable retrospectively. The memory of what was AI-assisted and what was not degrades over time. The case file contains no AI usage metadata. The attorney who wants to reconstruct their AI practice history — for any purpose — cannot do it accurately from the file. This is not a documentation problem that better habits can solve retroactively. It is an archival exposure that was created by the absence of a documentation architecture at the point of first AI adoption, and no retrospective intervention can fully close it. The market consequence: attorneys who adopted AI early and undocumented are carrying a reconstruction debt that is now a standing liability.

**Signal 3 — Post-Model-Update Governance Staleness Is a Structural Gap That No Vendor Manages**
AI vendors update model capabilities continuously. Governance reviews are conducted at discrete points in time. Between reviews, the model underlying the tool changes — sometimes materially. The governance document that was accurate in January may be materially incomplete by June without the firm knowing it. There is no mechanism for the firm to know when a model update has changed the tool's capabilities in ways that affect the firm's governance position. The vendor's model updates are not communicated in a way that allows the firm to assess whether a governance review is needed. This creates a specific structural exposure: governance staleness is invisible to the firm until a question arises that exposes it.

**Signal 4 — Client-Supplied AI Content Entering the Firm's Workflow Is an Unaddressed Professional Exposure**
Clients are increasingly sending AI-generated content into the Geneva attorney's workflow — briefs, analyses, contract drafts generated by the client's own AI systems, presented to the Geneva attorney for review, filing, or integration into a larger matter. The Geneva attorney is being asked to take professional responsibility for content they did not originate, using a review standard that has not been defined for this scenario. The professional obligation to supervise and review work product was designed for content originated by the firm's attorneys or by conventional tools. The case of AI-generated content originating from the client's own systems — which the Geneva attorney may have no insight into — is structurally unaddressed by any bar guidance or professional standard. The exposure is real and growing.

**Signal 5 — Insurance Renewal AI Questions Are Creating Defensive Misrepresentation Risk**
The AI questions on professional liability insurance renewal forms are becoming more detailed and more specific. Attorneys are answering them without a clear understanding of what the insurer's position is, what level of AI use changes coverage terms, or whether their current practice would be characterized as more or less risky. Some attorneys are answering defensively — minimizing AI use disclosure — in ways that may themselves constitute a misrepresentation to the insurer. The insurance renewal, which should be a governance documentation exercise, has become a guessing game with coverage implications. The attorneys most aware of their AI exposure are the most uncertain about how to characterize it to an insurer who has not made their position clear.

**Signal 6 — Professional Judgment Authorship Anxiety Is a Genuine but Operationally Unresolvable Concern**
The anxiety about whether professional judgment that has been shaped by continuous AI exposure remains "the attorney's own" is real and growing among experienced practitioners. It is distinct from the accuracy concern (is the output correct) and distinct from the supervision concern (did I review it). It is about cognitive process: whether the professional decisions being made are the product of the attorney's own reasoning or of AI suggestions that were absorbed without explicit notice. This concern has no operational resolution — there is no audit mechanism for professional judgment authorship — which means it operates as a persistent, unresolvable professional anxiety rather than a problem with a solution. The market is not addressing this concern because no one can offer a credible resolution to it.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### What Exists, What Gaps Remain

**What exists:**
- AI governance reviews: produce a documented policy (point-in-time, event-driven)
- Bar guidance: principle-level, not scenario-specific
- Vendor service terms: describe the tool, not the governance implications of model changes
- CE credits: address tool competence, not professional accountability for AI-assisted judgment
- Insurance questionnaires: ask about AI use, do not clarify coverage conditions

**What no one is addressing for Geneva small law firms:**

1. **Practice-representative AI evidence systems for external accountability** — No service provides the architecture for turning documented governance into evidence of practice that is representable to clients, courts, insurers, and the bar. The gap between governance documentation and accountable evidence is structurally unaddressed. The product that would serve this need is not a policy document — it is a practice evidence system that produces contemporaneous records of AI use sufficient for retrospective reconstruction.

2. **Post-model-update governance staleness detection** — No service monitors AI vendor model updates and assesses whether those updates change the governance position of the firm. The governance staleness problem — the document that was accurate at writing and is now materially incomplete — is unaddressed by any existing product. No one is watching the model update feed on behalf of the small firm and flagging when a governance review is warranted.

3. **Client-supplied AI content review framework** — No service has defined what constitutes sufficient professional review of AI-generated content originating from the client's own systems, not the firm's. The question of where the Geneva attorney's professional responsibility begins and ends when reviewing client-supplied AI content is structurally unaddressed by any bar guidance or advisory product. This is a growing exposure as client AI use increases.

4. **Insurer-commensurate AI practice characterization guidance** — No service helps the small firm understand how to characterize their AI practice to an insurer — what the coverage implications of different AI use patterns are, what constitutes a material representation, and how to answer the renewal questionnaire without creating a coverage problem. The insurance renewal process has become a source of defensive anxiety rather than a governance documentation exercise.

5. **Professional judgment authorship audit framework** — No framework exists for assessing whether professional judgment in an AI-exposed practice remains "the attorney's own." This is not a gap that can be closed by any existing product — it is a genuine epistemological gap that has no operational resolution. But no one in the market is addressing it directly, which means the anxiety operates without any credible professional framework for navigating it. The gap is real even if the solution is limited.

6. **Extended undocumented AI use legacy reconstruction** — No service addresses the specific problem of the firm with 18–24 months of undocumented AI use that now needs to reconstruct its practice history for client data subject requests, bar inquiries, insurer reviews, or court proceedings. The retrospective reconstruction problem is structurally unresolvable by definition (you cannot create contemporaneous records after the fact), but the residual mitigation options — what can still be done to reduce the exposure — are not being addressed by any existing service.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Specific Regulatory Obligations

**SVA (Schweizerische Anwaltsverband / Swiss Lawyers Association) and ORDRE (Geneva Bar Association):**
- The SVA AI working group guidance and ORDRE publications remain at principle level, addressing confidentiality obligations and the general supervision requirement, without operationalizing either for the specific scenarios that constitute most AI use in practice
- Neither body has issued guidance on: (i) the standard for reviewing AI-generated content originating from a client's own AI systems, (ii) the professional obligation when AI-assisted judgment has been shaped by continuous AI exposure, (iii) what AI practice evidence is sufficient for a bar disciplinary inquiry, or (iv) the governance staleness question when vendor model updates change tool capabilities between reviews
- The CE credit requirements for 2026 include AI-related topics, but the available offerings address tool competence — how to use AI more effectively — not professional accountability for AI-assisted professional judgment, which is the concern the ICP is actually navigating

**nFADP (revised Federal Act on Data Protection, in force 2024, ordinance phase):**
- Art. 6 requires a lawful basis for processing personal data; client data processed through AI tools requires a mapped lawful basis — and when the AI vendor updates the model, the data processing characteristics may change without the firm's knowledge, potentially affecting the nFADP Art. 6 lawful basis validity
- Art. 12 creates data subject rights to information about automated processing — the client who asks what AI was used on their matter has a right under nFADP Art. 12 to an explanation of the automated processing; for a firm with undocumented AI use history, this right cannot be fully satisfied from the case file
- Art. 21 creates obligations to provide meaningful information about the logic involved in automated decision-making — relevant where AI is used in case assessment or legal strategy formulation, and the firm's inability to reconstruct its AI use history makes this obligation difficult to satisfy for past matters
- The nFADP ordinance (as of early 2026) has not issued guidance on the specific question of AI-assisted professional judgment authorship — whether professional decisions shaped by continuous AI exposure require a different disclosure or documentation approach

**FDPIC (Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner):**
- The FDPIC's investigative authority under the nFADP means that client complaints about AI use in legal practice could trigger an investigation into the firm's AI data processing practices; the firm with undocumented AI use history is structurally unable to demonstrate compliance with Art. 12 and Art. 21 obligations for past matters
- The FDPIC has not issued guidance specific to legal practice AI use, but the enforcement risk environment — system-level audits, complaint-driven investigations — creates liability for undocumented AI practices even in the absence of published legal practice guidance

**Bar Rules (Swiss Lawyers Act / BGFA; ORDRE disciplinary rules):**
- The obligation to maintain client confidentiality applies to AI tool use; when a vendor updates its model, the data processing characteristics may change, and the attorney's responsibility for client data does not diminish because the vendor made the change
- The supervision obligation requires the attorney to review AI-assisted work product, but has not been operationalized for the case of AI-generated content originating from the client's own systems, not the firm's
- The professional obligation to practice in the client's interest creates a duty to maintain the quality of professional judgment — which may be implicated when professional judgment has been shaped by continuous AI exposure without the attorney having a framework for assessing whether the judgment remains their own
- Disciplinary exposure for AI-related professional failures remains unprecedented in published Geneva disciplinary outcomes, which paradoxically means the attorney has no precedent to assess what the bar considers a violation — and this uncertainty itself becomes a source of professional anxiety

## 06 — Findings Summary

### 6 Net-New Insights, Distinct from C214–C224 Phase 01

**Finding 1 — Documented Governance Without Practice-Representative Evidence Is a Distinct Exposure Category**
The firm with an accurate, well-intentioned governance document that cannot produce evidence of actual AI practice has a different and more specific exposure than the firm with no governance document. C214–C224 addressed governance absence (C214), governance implementation failure (C222), governance without operational conformance (C222), and enforcement self-determination under regulatory silence (C224). None identified the specific exposure of governance documentation that is accurate in intent but unrepresentative in fact — and cannot produce accountable evidence when asked by clients, courts, insurers, or the bar. The product gap is not another policy document; it is a practice evidence architecture. *(Distinct from C214–C224: governance without evidence, not governance without policy)*

**Finding 2 — Extended Undocumented AI Use Has Created a Reconstruction Debt That Is Retrospectively Unresolvable**
The attorney who adopted AI 18–24 months ago without a contemporaneous documentation architecture now carries a reconstruction debt that cannot be closed by retrospective intervention. C214–C224 addressed documentation debt (C216, C218), audit trail reconstruction failure (C224), and AI use history as accumulating exposure (C219, C223). None identified the specific character of this debt as retrospectively unresolvable — the archive cannot be retroactively fitted with AI usage metadata, and the attorney's memory of what was AI-assisted degrades materially within months. This finding identifies the debt as permanent in its current form, not merely accumulating. *(Distinct from C214–C224: permanently unresolvable reconstruction debt, not accumulating documentation gap)*

**Finding 3 — Post-Model-Update Governance Staleness Is a Structural Gap Created by Continuous AI Vendor Evolution**
The governance review is a point-in-time document in a domain where the underlying technology changes continuously. When the AI vendor updates the model, the capabilities, error rates, and data processing patterns may change — without the firm knowing, and without the governance document reflecting the change. C214–C224 addressed vendor term change detection (C222) as a contractual problem, and capability-change governance gaps (C223). None identified the specific mechanism of model-update-driven governance staleness — the governance document that was accurate at writing and is now materially incomplete without the firm having any mechanism to know it. This is a structural exposure of continuous AI technology operating against discrete governance documentation. *(Distinct from C214–C224: model-update staleness, not vendor term change or capability expansion)*

**Finding 4 — Client-Supplied AI Content Entering the Firm's Workflow Creates an Undefined Supervisory Obligation**
Clients are increasingly sending AI-generated content into the Geneva attorney's workflow for review, filing, and professional sign-off. The supervising attorney's obligation was designed for content originated by the firm's own attorneys or conventional tools — not for AI-generated content from the client's own systems, which the firm may have no insight into. C214–C224 addressed client-initiated AI disclosure questions (C215, C219) but not the specific professional exposure of being asked to take responsibility for content generated by a third party's AI system. This is a distinct and growing exposure: the professional responsibility for AI-assisted work product that the attorney did not originate and cannot review to the same standard as firm-generated content. *(Distinct from C214–C224: professional responsibility for third-party AI content, not client disclosure obligation)*

**Finding 5 — Insurance Renewal AI Questions Are Creating Defensive Misrepresentation Risk as a Discrete Market Failure**
The professional liability insurance renewal has become a trigger point for AI governance exposure — more detailed AI questions, no insurer clarity on coverage conditions, attorneys answering defensively in ways that may themselves constitute a misrepresentation. C214–C224 addressed insurance disclosure anxiety (C221) and insurer-commensurate supervision evidence (C224), but not the specific dynamic of the renewal process as an exposure crystallization moment where the attorney's defensive characterization choices create a coverage risk independent of the AI practice itself. This finding identifies the insurance renewal as an active market failure mechanism — it is converting governance uncertainty into potential coverage gaps. *(Distinct from C214–C224: insurance renewal as exposure crystallization mechanism, not coverage gap identification)*

**Finding 6 — Professional Judgment Authorship Anxiety Is a Genuine, Operationally Unresolvable Concern That No Market Actor Is Addressing**
The experienced attorney's anxiety about whether their professional judgment — shaped by continuous AI exposure over 18–24 months — remains their own is a real professional concern that has no operational resolution. C214–C224 addressed professional identity friction with AI reasoning opacity (C224) but framed it as a supervision problem (can the attorney verify AI reasoning). This finding identifies a distinct dimension: not the reasoning path of the AI, but the cognitive authorship of the attorney's own judgment. The concern is genuine, the market is not addressing it directly, and the absence of any credible professional framework for navigating it is itself a market gap. The anxiety is unresolvable operationally — but naming it, and providing a practical professional boundary framework, is a serviceable advisory response. *(Distinct from C214–C224: professional judgment authorship anxiety as distinct from reasoning opacity, and structurally unresolvable)*
