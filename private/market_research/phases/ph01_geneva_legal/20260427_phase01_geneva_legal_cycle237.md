## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, and Complaints in the Market

**Signal 1: The Vendor Liability Disclaimer Creates an Undischarged Professional Obligation**
The AI vendor contract contains a liability disclaimer. The attorney reads it. The attorney remains fully responsible for everything produced using the tool. The vendor is not a licensed professional. The vendor's terms were not written for the attorney's professional context. The vendor has no obligation to the attorney's clients. The attorney signed. The professional obligation — to the client, to the bar, to the court — is the attorney's alone. No Geneva attorney has resolved this structural problem: the tool they depend on carries no professional responsibility for the work done with it.

**Signal 2: AI Output Metadata Is Reaching Clients Before the Attorney Decides Whether to Disclose**
Geneva clients — particularly corporate clients with active IT departments — are beginning to detect AI-generated content through metadata analysis. The client's IT department flags a document: AI-generated. The client asks the attorney what that means. The attorney did not choose to disclose the AI use. The metadata disclosed it without the attorney's consent or decision. The question of whether AI use should be disclosed is now being decided by the vendor's metadata policy, not by the attorney. This is a disclosure problem the attorney did not design and cannot control.

**Signal 3: Opposing Counsel AI Detection Is Becoming a Tactical Variable in Geneva Litigation**
Attorneys are beginning to identify AI-assisted briefs from opposing counsel — through stylistic markers, structural patterns, and content characteristics. The tactical question of what to do with this information — whether to raise it, how to raise it, and whether the court has any framework for it — is unresolved. The Geneva market is developing informal norms around AI detection in litigation, before any formal doctrine has arrived. This creates a tactical asymmetry: the attorney who knows how to detect AI in opposing briefs has an advantage the other attorney does not.

**Signal 4: The Geneva Bar's Informal Guidance Creates Publication Risk Without Publication Protection**
The SVA/ORDRE has begun communicating AI guidance informally — through working group discussions, at continuing education events, through informal member communications. The attorney who acts on this informal guidance cannot rely on it as a safe harbor, because it has not been published. The attorney who does not act on it bears the risk of having ignored the bar's direction. The informal channel creates an obligation without creating the protection that formal publication would provide. The guidance arrives verbally, in the corridor, at a seminar — and the attorney must decide whether to treat it as authoritative, with no written record of what was said or to whom.

**Signal 5: The AI Competence Obligation Is Being Satisfied Through CE Credits That Do Not Test Practice Adequacy**
The bar's AI competence obligation is being met through continuing education credits — tool-focused training that demonstrates attendance, not competence in the operational sense. The attorney who has completed twelve hours of AI-related CE credits has satisfied the bar's competence requirement on paper. The question of whether the attorney's actual AI practice — how they use tools, what they review, what they document, how they supervise — meets any standard of adequacy is not what the CE credits test. The CE market is incentivizing attendance, not governance.

**Signal 6: The Geneva Peer Information Circuit About AI Is Operating in a Liability Gray Zone**
Geneva attorneys are exchanging practical information about AI tools — which vendors failed, which produced incorrect research, which had security issues — through the informal peer network. This information circulation is the only real-time intelligence system for AI tool quality at the Geneva small firm level. But it is operating without any framework for how shared information about tool failures creates accountability for the person who shared it. The peer who warns a colleague about a vendor's failure has no liability protection for that warning. The peer who remains silent has no liability exposure — and the client whose matter is affected by the undisclosed failure has no recourse.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Specific Regulatory Obligations

**nFADP — Automated Decision-Making and Professional Services:**
The revised Federal Act on Data Protection requires that data subjects be informed when automated decision-making produces legal effects or significantly affects them (Art. 21 nFADP). For a Geneva attorney using AI in client matters, this creates a specific question: when does AI-assisted legal research, document drafting, or case analysis cross the threshold of "automated decision-making" under the nFADP, and what disclosure obligation does this trigger toward the client? The FDPIC has not issued guidance specific to the legal profession on this question. The attorney's obligation under Article 21 exists. The content of the obligation, for the specific context of AI-assisted legal practice, has not been defined. The attorney who is trying to comply is navigating an obligation whose specific application to their practice is unresolved.

The nFADP also creates a specific intersection with privilege that no Geneva authority has mapped: the data protection framework requires documentation of what data was processed, by what system, for what purpose. Attorney-client privilege requires protecting the content of client communications from disclosure. When an AI system processes privileged client matter data, the nFADP documentation obligation and the privilege obligation are operating in the same moment, for the same data, with no guidance on how they are reconciled. The attorney who keeps nFADP-required records is creating documents that could, in the wrong circumstances, require disclosure — potentially overriding the privilege they were designed to protect.

**FDPIC — Enforcement Posture and Professional Services:**
The FDPIC's recent enforcement activity under the nFADP — including published decisions on automated decision-making — establishes that enforcement is operational, not theoretical. The question for the Geneva attorney is not whether the nFADP applies, but what the specific application of the FDPIC's enforcement approach looks like for a professional practice context. The FDPIC has not published guidance specific to the legal profession. The attorney using AI in client matters is within the FDPIC's jurisdiction. The specific enforcement standard for professional services AI use remains undefined.

**SVA/ORDRE — Competence, Supervision, and Informal Guidance:**
The SVA and the Ordre des Avocats Genevois have communicated AI expectations informally through working groups, continuing education programming, and member communications. The informal channel creates a specific problem: the guidance is real, it reflects the bar's direction, it creates an obligation to have engaged with it — but it cannot be cited as authoritative because it has not been formally published. The Geneva small firm attorney who has acted on informal bar signals about AI cannot use those signals as a defense in a disciplinary proceeding. The bar's informal guidance functions as a standard without the publication that would make it a safe harbor.

The supervision obligation under the Swiss Lawyers Act applies to AI-assisted work in the same terms it applies to human delegation — the supervising attorney must ensure the work product meets professional standards. For AI-assisted work, the supervision obligation is complicated by a specific structural problem: the supervising attorney who cannot evaluate the AI's process cannot verify that supervision was adequate, regardless of diligence. The signature certifies a review that cannot be completed as designed.

**Geneva Courts — Local AI Practice Development:**
Geneva courts — particularly the Tribunal civil and the Tribunal des prud'hommes — are developing localized expectations about AI use in proceedings through case-by-case practice, not through published procedural rules. The attorney appearing in different Geneva courtrooms encounters different informal norms about AI disclosure, AI use acknowledgment, and credibility standards for AI-assisted work product. This localized development creates a specific documentation requirement that the attorney cannot plan for in advance: the AI governance documentation that is adequate in one Geneva courtroom may not meet the expectations of another.

**Professional Liability Insurance — AI Coverage Ambiguity:**
Swiss professional liability insurance for attorneys does not have a settled position on AI-specific coverage. The coverage question — whether AI-assisted work product errors are covered under existing policies, whether AI-specific exclusions exist, whether the policyholder has coverage for AI-proximate liability — is unresolved at the underwriting level. The Geneva small firm attorney who relies on their existing PI policy for AI exposure has not been told, by their insurer, what that policy covers in the AI context. The ambiguity is structural: the insurance product was not designed for AI-assisted practice, and no Swiss carrier has published clear AI coverage terms for legal professionals.

## 06 — Findings Summary

### 6 Net-New Insights — Distinct from C214–C236 Phase 01

**Insight 1: The Vendor Liability Disclaimer Is Structurally Incompatible with Professional Practice Obligations**
The Geneva small firm attorney is operating under a professional liability architecture that requires them to be responsible for AI outputs in a context where the AI vendor bears no professional responsibility whatsoever. This is not a risk management problem that can be resolved through careful vendor selection. It is a structural feature of the AI vendor market: no major AI legal tool provider accepts professional responsibility for how attorneys use their tools in specific client matters. The attorney who has internalized this structural reality — who understands that the vendor contract allocates all professional liability to the attorney while providing no warranty for the professional context of use — is in a different position than the attorney who has not engaged with this specific problem. This is a distinct and specific insight for C237: the problem is not vendor due diligence. The problem is the incompatibility of the vendor liability model with the professional liability model.

**Insight 2: AI Output Metadata Is Making Disclosure Decisions the Attorney Has Not Made**
The arrival of AI output metadata at client IT departments before the attorney has decided whether to disclose AI use is a specific new failure mode. It is distinct from the AI disclosure question (whether the attorney should disclose AI use to the client) because it removes the attorney's decision from the disclosure event. The metadata arrives. The client sees it. The attorney must respond to a disclosure that happened through the vendor's technical architecture, not through the attorney's professional judgment. This is a specific technical-legal problem at the intersection of vendor architecture and professional obligation, and no Geneva service has addressed it as such.

**Insight 3: Opposing Counsel AI Detection Has Become a Tactical Variable Before Any Doctrine Has Arrived**
Geneva attorneys are now detecting AI-assisted work in opposing briefs through informal markers, and this detection is beginning to influence litigation tactics before any court has published guidance on how AI-assisted work should be treated in proceedings. This creates a specific tactical asymmetry: the attorney who has developed the skill to identify AI-assisted opposing briefs has a litigation advantage the other attorney does not. The market is forming informal norms around AI detection in Geneva litigation faster than formal doctrine can develop, and the attorneys who are developing this capability are doing so without any framework for the ethical obligations that may attach to using that detection in proceedings.

**Insight 4: Informal Bar Guidance Creates an Obligation Without Creating the Protection Formal Publication Would Provide**
The SVA/ORDRE is communicating AI direction informally — at working groups, at CE events, through member channels. The attorney who acts on this informal direction is exposed to the risk that the direction was not authoritative. The attorney who does not act on it is exposed to the risk of having ignored the bar's signals. The informal channel functions as a standard without the protection that formal publication would provide. This is a specific structural problem: the Geneva bar's guidance formation process is creating accountability for attorneys who engage with it, while providing no safe harbor for having done so. The attorney's participation in the bar's informal guidance process is a liability variable that has not been mapped.

**Insight 5: The CE Credit Market Is Measuring Attendance, Not Practice Adequacy — and the Bar's Competence Obligation Is Being Satisfied on the Wrong Variable**
The AI-related CE credits that Geneva attorneys are completing to satisfy the bar's competence obligation are measuring attendance at training sessions, not the adequacy of AI use in actual client matters. The CE provider has no mechanism for evaluating whether the attorney's AI practice — how they use tools in specific matter contexts, what they review, what they document, how they supervise — meets any standard of professional adequacy. The CE credit is a record of participation. It is not evidence of governance quality. And the attorney who has twelve AI-related CE credits has satisfied the bar's requirement on paper while the actual practice question — whether the attorney's AI use in client matters is professionally adequate — remains unaddressed by the CE system.

**Insight 6: The Geneva AI Peer Intelligence Circuit Operates in a Liability Gray Zone That Discourages the Information Exchange the Market Most Needs**
The practical information about AI tool quality — which vendors produced incorrect research in specific matter contexts, which tools failed on Geneva-specific legal content, which configurations created security or confidentiality exposure — circulates through the Geneva peer network informally. This information is the market's only real-time AI quality intelligence. But it circulates without any framework for the liability that the sharing attorney bears. The attorney who warns a colleague about a specific vendor failure has no legal protection for that communication. The attorney who shares information that proves incorrect has no immunity. The result is that the peer network that should be the primary mechanism for AI quality intelligence is operating in a gray zone that structurally discourages disclosure. The information that Geneva attorneys most need — real, specific, actionable intelligence about AI tool quality in legal practice contexts — is systematically undersupplied because the liability framework for sharing it does not exist.
