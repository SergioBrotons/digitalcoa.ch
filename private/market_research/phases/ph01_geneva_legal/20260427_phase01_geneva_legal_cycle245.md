## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems and Failure Modes

**Electronic seal certification scope for AI-assisted filings — undefined in Geneva civil procedure.**
Geneva attorneys filing electronically certify accuracy and authenticity under their professional seal. When the analytical content of the filing was generated or significantly shaped by an AI tool, the question of what the certification warrants — content, process, or both — is unresolved. No Geneva court has published procedural guidance defining the scope of electronic certification for AI-assisted filings. The seal is being used without a defined theory of what it covers. The question is distinct from citation fabrication: it concerns the attorney's own AI-assisted analytical process, not the integrity of AI-generated citations within a filing.

**Client-provided AI content creating a three-party certification situation the attorney did not choose.**
Clients are sending AI-generated legal documents to their Geneva attorneys for review and professional adoption. The attorney is asked to put their professional letterhead on content they did not produce, whose methodology they cannot fully verify, and for which they bear full personal professional liability. No Geneva bar guidance defines what "adequate review" means when adopting client-provided AI content. The question is structurally distinct from AI-assisted drafting (attorney uses own AI tool) and from citation fabrication (research integrity within attorney's own output). Three structurally different problems are being conflated in firm-level understanding.

**Standard mandate letter architecture absent AI data processing authorization for third-party vendor infrastructure.**
Standard Geneva mandate letter templates were written before AI research and drafting tools were used in active client matters. They do not authorize the attorney to send client matter content — including privileged legal analysis, case strategy, and client communications — to third-party AI vendor infrastructure for processing. nFADP Art. 9 requires documented authorization for third-party processing. The authorization does not exist in standard mandate letters. No Geneva bar guidance, no standard form, and no advisory service has addressed this specific gap. The exposure exists in every active matter where AI tools are in use.

**AI research query metadata as inadvertent privilege log exposure — discovered reactively in discovery disputes.**
AI legal research tools generate metadata logs recording query terms, retrieval patterns, and interaction sequences. These logs have entered discovery disputes and revealed not just the research output but the attorney's analytical approach and litigation theory before any filing was made. The metadata function creates a privilege-relevant data flow that has not been systematically addressed in privilege preservation protocols. No Geneva bar guidance addresses AI research metadata as a privilege surface. Attorneys are discovering this reactively — at the point of a discovery demand — not proactively.

**Geneva court AI disclosure procedural inconsistency varying by judge with no published map.**
Geneva courts have not published procedural expectations for AI-assisted document disclosure. What attorneys experience is inconsistent: some judges expect disclosure, others do not ask, no published rule defines the minimum standard. The inconsistency is not random — it reflects different judicial assessments of an unresolved doctrinal question. Attorneys are managing this uncertainty without a map, discovering judge-specific expectations at the point of proceedings. No Geneva publication has named this as a structural procedural phenomenon.

**Opposing counsel AI use as unresolved professional conduct and tactical question — growing in frequency.**
Geneva attorneys in commercial litigation are encountering AI-generated submissions from opposing counsel and have no framework for what to do with this knowledge. No published Geneva bar guidance addresses whether it creates a professional conduct obligation, a strategic option, or neither. The Geneva civil procedure framework has no doctrine that maps cleanly onto this situation. The frequency of this situation is growing as AI adoption increases across Geneva law firms.

### Paradoxes and Complaints

- The attorney most advanced in AI deployment has the most unrecoverable research trail — AI research metadata compounds with every session, and the exposure is invisible until demanded.
- The firm with the most AI tools has the least clarity on what the mandate letter actually authorizes — tool proliferation has outpaced engagement documentation architecture development.
- The generational competence inversion is producing a false sense of security: senior attorneys believe juniors are supervising AI adequately because juniors understand the tools better; but the supervision obligation runs the other direction.
- The carrier's AI questionnaire creates compliance-like documentation without compliance-like clarity — attorneys are completing documents that commit them to standards they cannot evaluate.
- Geneva court AI disclosure inconsistency means identical firms in identical positions face different procedural obligations depending on judicial assignment — a structural variable the firm cannot control or predict.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Specific Regulatory Obligations

**SVA/ORDRE — Professional Competence and Supervision Obligations**
Art. 321a Swiss Criminal Code requires attorneys to supervise the professional activities of their staff. The provision imposes a personal professional supervision obligation that runs to the managing partner. AI deployment does not suspend this obligation — it transforms it. The obligation is real; the discharge mechanism does not exist within the legal professional framework. Geneva bar AI-related continuing education credits measure attendance, not practice adequacy. The structural inversion between seniority of authority and AI competence (junior attorneys understand AI tools better, senior attorneys bear supervision liability) is not addressed in any SVA/ORDRE guidance. The SVA's AI working group circulates draft guidelines in informal sessions; firms not participating do not have access to emerging direction.

**nFADP Articles 6, 8, and 9 — Purpose Limitation, Lawful Basis, and Third-Party Processing**
Art. 6 nFADP requires a lawful basis for processing personal data. Art. 8 requires purpose limitation — processing limited to the specific purpose for which data was collected. Art. 9 requires that processing by third parties be authorized — explicitly or through the mandate agreement. Client matter content processed by AI tools on vendor infrastructure constitutes personal data processing under nFADP. The question of whether implied mandate authority extends to third-party AI vendor processing is unresolved. Standard Geneva mandate letter templates do not include AI data processing authorization. No published FDPIC guidance addresses whether AI vendor data processing on foreign servers constitutes permitted processing under Art. 9 in the professional services context. The FDPIC has not published enforcement decisions addressing legal practice AI data processing; their enforcement pattern suggests the standard will be established through enforcement action against another sector, not through proactive guidance.

**nFADP Article 21 — Automated Decision-Making Profiling**
Art. 21 triggers explainability obligations when automated decisions affect the data subject. AI-assisted legal analysis — document review, case assessment, strategy development — may constitute profiling under Art. 21 if it produces outputs that influence legal outcomes. The FDPIC has not published guidance on the applicability of Art. 21 profiling obligations to AI-assisted legal practice. No Geneva bar guidance has addressed this intersection. The exposure is present-tense and unremediated.

**Art. 321 Swiss Criminal Code — Professional Secrecy and Foreign Server Data Processing**
Swiss attorney-client privilege (Art. 321 CP) protects client communications and file contents from disclosure. When an AI tool processes client data on servers outside Switzerland, the question of whether privilege extends to that processing configuration is unresolved. The professional secrecy obligation requires that client information not be disclosed to third parties without authorization. The vendor's processing of client matter data involves disclosure to the vendor's infrastructure. Whether this falls within the "necessary for the mandate" exception is not addressed by published Geneva guidance. The question arises every time a firm adopts a new AI tool.

**Geneva Bar (ORDRE) — Electronic Filing Certification**
Geneva civil procedure requires electronic filings to be certified by the filing attorney. The certification is personal: the attorney warrants accuracy and authenticity. No published ORDRE guidance addresses what the certification warrants when the analytical content was generated by an AI tool. The absence of guidance means attorneys are certifying without a defined theory of what they are certifying — a specific and present-tense exposure.

**EU AI Act — Client-Imposed Obligations**
EU-connected clients subject to EU AI Act high-risk system requirements are beginning to impose AI governance documentation conditions on Swiss legal counsel. The obligation is created by EU client pressure, not by Swiss domestic law. Swiss regulatory frameworks do not provide a resolution pathway for this obligation. Geneva firms with EU-connected client portfolios are exposed to documentation requirements imposed from outside the Swiss regulatory system.
