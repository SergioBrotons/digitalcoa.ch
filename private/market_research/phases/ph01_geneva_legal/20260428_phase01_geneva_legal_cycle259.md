## 01 — ICP Language

How this ICP actually describes their AI problems and concerns, in their own terms.

### English

The managing partner at a two-to-five-attorney Geneva firm describes AI adoption in terms that reveal a specific structural position. The language is careful, often indirect, and reflects genuine uncertainty about what they have actually agreed to.

**On confidentiality:**
*"We were told the AI would help with research. Nobody explained that it meant client file data was going somewhere outside the firm."*

This is not a data residency question. It is an authorization question: the attorney assumed research assistance, not data processing. The gap between what was presented and what was activated is the structural exposure.

**On supervision:**
*"I sign off on briefs. I'm the senior reviewer. I can't tell you what the AI actually produced versus what the associate edited."*

The supervision structure works until it meets AI. The signature attests to a review that cannot be substantively performed. This is distinct from a training gap — it is a structural incompatibility between professional responsibility architecture and AI output characteristics.

**On bar guidance:**
*"The bar sent a reminder about AI competence. It was three pages. None of it told me what I'm actually supposed to do."*

The guidance circuit has produced communications that create awareness without providing operational content. The attorney is not asking for a decision — they are asking for a methodology. The gap between institutional communication and operational instruction is the actual problem.

**On privilege:**
*"I assume privilege covers AI research the same way it covers anything else in the file. I'm not sure what happens if someone compels disclosure of how the AI reached its conclusions."*

This is the query trail question appearing in the language of practical uncertainty, not technical analysis. The attorney knows the question exists but has no framework for answering it.

**On personal exposure:**
*"I signed the carrier form. I didn't know I was signing something that could be read as a personal attestation of AI governance adequacy."*

The carrier questionnaire has introduced a document with legal and commercial consequences that the attorney did not consciously evaluate. The signature creates personal exposure the signatory may not have intended.

**On what clients are beginning to ask:**
*"One of our clients sent a questionnaire. Eighteen questions about AI. I didn't have answers for most of them. It wasn't on our radar."*

Client-side AI governance expectations are arriving before Geneva firms have named them as a category. The questionnaire is not framed as a regulatory requirement — it arrives as a commercial document — but it creates accountability that functions like a regulatory standard.

## 02 — Market Signals

Current problems, failure modes, paradoxes, and complaints circulating in the Geneva legal market.

### The Billing Model–Governance Incompatibility

The small Geneva firm's hourly billing model creates structural pressure toward AI utilization without creating any corresponding documentation architecture. The incentive is to use AI to increase billable capacity; the documentation consequence is that AI's contribution to each billable hour becomes unreconstructable. This is not a behavioral problem — it is a structural one. The billing model and the governance model are operating in opposite directions, and no Geneva market participant has named this as a discrete category.

### The Jurisdiction-of-AI Question Nobody Is Asking

As Geneva firms handle increasing proportions of international matters, attorneys are fielding questions from clients about which AI jurisdiction governs their matter — a category distinct from data residency. A client whose matter touches the EU, the UK, and Switzerland may have expectations about which AI regulatory framework applies to the tools processing their case. The Geneva small firm has no framework for answering this question, and it is beginning to arrive as an explicit client expectation in commercial relationships. The gap is structural: the question exists before any answer infrastructure exists to address it.

### Practice Management AI — The Adoption Nobody Decided

Small Geneva firms operate on practice management software platforms that have integrated AI features into their existing infrastructure. This adoption occurred without a governance decision — the firm did not select an AI tool, it selected a practice management platform that subsequently incorporated AI. The accountability gap is structural: the firm is now operating with AI in its technology stack that was never evaluated as an AI governance decision. No Geneva market service addresses this specific category — the question of what administrative AI processing the firm has implicitly adopted through its software choices.

### The Contradictory Signal Problem from Geneva's Two Regulatory Bodies

The Geneva bar (SVA/ORDRE) and FINMA both issue guidance relevant to AI governance for Geneva firms. The signals are not always coherent. A practice that satisfies the bar's AI competence recommendations may not satisfy FINMA's expectations for a firm with financial regulatory exposure. A practice that satisfies FINMA's documentation expectations may not align with the bar's professional secrecy requirements. The small firm, with no compliance infrastructure to map these intersections, receives contradictory signals and has no methodology for resolving them. No Geneva service has named this as a discrete structural problem requiring its own reconciliation framework.

### The Procedural Accountability Vacuum in Geneva Courts

Geneva courts are receiving AI-assisted briefs and filings without any established authentication procedure. The attorney signature covers the filing; the question of what AI contributed to the procedural content — and who is accountable when AI-assisted procedural content is erroneous — has no Geneva framework. The accountability chain is undefined. This is not a documentation problem; it is an authority-and-responsibility problem at a moment when the court has not established what it expects and the firm has not established what it can defend.

### The Client nFADP Accountability Question Arriving as a Commercial Document

Geneva clients are beginning to send questionnaires asking about nFADP obligations — whether client data was used in AI training, whether clients can access AI-generated conclusions about them, whether they can request correction of AI-processed information. These questions arrive not as regulatory requirements but as commercial documents. The small firm's inability to answer them creates immediate accountability exposure in a commercial relationship, before any regulatory consequence has materialized. The compounding dynamic is active: each matter handled without AI data governance documentation adds to the exposure when the next client questionnaire arrives.

## 04 — Compliance Context

Geneva and Swiss-specific regulatory obligations relevant to this ICP.

### SVA/ORDRE — Geneva Bar Professional Obligations

The Geneva bar's AI guidance has proceeded through two parallel channels. The formal channel — published recommendations and continuing education requirements — creates awareness without operational content. The informal channel — working group discussions, session content at professional events, and OAGE informal guidance communications — circulates operationally relevant direction without the safe harbor of publication. Attorneys who rely on formal guidance alone are operating with incomplete information; attorneys who rely on informal guidance have no documented basis for their practices.

The continuing education system measures attendance, not practice adequacy. An attorney who has completed all required AI competence credits may have no better governance infrastructure than one who has not. The credential measures exposure to information, not integration of practice.

The OAGE informal guidance has created substantive accountability expectations — what a properly governed AI practice should look like — before any published document has articulated those expectations. Firms that adopted AI early are operating under informal standards that were not available when they made their adoption decisions.

### nFADP — Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection

**Article 9 — Third-party processing authorization.** The use of non-Swiss AI processors in attorney work creates an authorization chain that Article 9 requires clients to understand and approve. The typical law firm engagement letter does not contain AI processing authorizations. Every active AI-assisted matter represents a potential Article 9 gap that is compounding in real time as additional client data enters the processing chain.

**Article 21 — Automated decision explanation.** When AI contributes to case management decisions that affect clients, the client may have a present-tense right to explanation under Article 21. The structural problem is that most current AI tools cannot produce per-instance reasoning traces that would satisfy this obligation. The exposure is present-tense and technically unsolvable within current tool architecture.

**Article 13 — Data correction and access.** Clients are beginning to ask whether AI-processed conclusions about them can be accessed and corrected. This question has no established protocol in Geneva legal practice. The exposure compounds as AI systems accumulate inferences about clients that were never explicitly collected.

The small-business exemption under nFADP remains ambiguous for professional services firms. Law firms process client data as a primary activity, not an incidental one. Whether the exemption threshold applies — and what over- or under-compliance consequences follow from either interpretation — has no published resolution.

### FDPIC — Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner

The FDPIC has not issued enforcement guidance specific to attorney-client data processing through AI tools. Firms are operating without a published supervisory standard. The enforcement trajectory can be read from pattern — which complaint categories the FDPIC has pursued, which sectors have received inquiry letters — but the specific documentation that would constitute adequate AI governance for a small Geneva law firm has not been articulated by the Commissioner.

The FDPIC's pattern intelligence suggests a direction: data processing accountability, vendor chain documentation, and data subject rights fulfillment are the active enforcement categories. A Geneva firm cannot read its specific obligations from published doctrine because the doctrine does not yet exist at that level of specificity.

### Geneva Civil Procedure

Geneva courts have not published a standard for AI disclosure in filed documents. Procedural expectations are forming through case-by-case resolution — different judicial formations applying different expectations, no published standard, no formal protocol for authentication. Firms are filing AI-assisted documents without knowing what the court expects and without any mechanism for determining whether their current practice would satisfy a challenge.

The revised Swiss CCP, effective January 2025, introduced provisions on AI-assisted filing without defining the human responsibility standard. The question of what "human responsibility" for an AI-assisted filing means in operational terms — what review is required, what documentation is sufficient — remains open. Geneva courts have not resolved this for ordinary commercial litigation matters.

### PI Insurance

Professional liability policies do not explicitly exclude AI-assisted errors. The ambiguity is in the carrier's favor: vague exclusion language preserves post-hoc interpretation room. The practical consequence is that coverage for an AI-assisted professional error may depend on how the carrier reads the policy at the moment of claim — not on a clear contractual standard.

The carrier's AI questionnaire functions as a de facto governance adequacy standard. Third-generation 2026 questionnaires probe specific evidence categories: tool version tracking, reassessment triggers, client communication records, AI output review evidence. A firm that cannot provide evidence in these categories faces a documented coverage gap at renewal.

### Federal Council AI Deliberation — June 2026

The Federal Council's ongoing deliberation on AI governance will produce a framework that sets medium-term accountability expectations for Swiss firms. The Geneva small firm needs to build documentation infrastructure before this deliberation concludes — not in anticipation of what the deliberation will require, but because the documentation infrastructure itself takes time to build and the carrier questionnaire is already asking the questions today.

### EU AI Act — August 2, 2026

Swiss firms serving EU-connected clients face AI obligations imposed through commercial relationships independently of Swiss domestic regulatory determination. The EU AI Act's high-risk system obligations arrive through client contract language and counterparty requirements. A Geneva firm that has not mapped which of its matters involve EU-connected clients is already potentially in breach of obligations it has not identified.

**Finding 01: The Billing Model Creates Structural AI Governance Pressure in the Opposite Direction of Documentation Requirements**

The hourly billing model of a small Geneva firm creates economic incentive to use AI for billable work without creating any corresponding documentation architecture for AI's contribution to each billable unit. The billing structure and the governance structure operate in opposing directions by design. No Geneva market participant has named this as a discrete structural incompatibility. The consequence is that firms are being paid for AI-assisted work they cannot document — and when the carrier asks what AI contributed to billed hours, the billing record cannot answer. This is not a training problem or a policy problem; it is a structural contradiction in how small Geneva firms are compensated for professional services in an AI environment.

*Distinction from prior findings:* Prior Phase 01 cycles have addressed AI documentation gaps, carrier questionnaires, and supervision adequacy. None have identified the billing model itself as a structural force operating against AI governance documentation requirements — a problem that exists independent of the firm's intentions or competence.
