## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Associates using AI tools at highest volume with least supervision**
Associates are the highest-volume users of AI tools in small law firms. Supervising partners review outputs, not pipelines. The accountability for AI decisions sits with the signing attorney who did not make the AI decision.

**Signal 2: Carrier attestation signed without methodology confirmation**
Managing partners signing carrier attestations without being able to confirm what their actual AI methodology is. The gap between what the attestation asks and what the firm can confirm is a personal liability exposure.

**Signal 3: Bar providing no guidance eight months after specific inquiry**
Bar office still reviewing AI guidance eight months after a specific inquiry. The absence of guidance is not equivalent to permission. Firms are operating without any formal reference point.

**Signal 4: Client confidentiality questions arriving without prepared answers**
Clients beginning to ask directly about data security and AI vendor terms. Managing partners do not have prepared answers that are both accurate and do not create more exposure than they resolve.

**Signal 5: AI research reproducibility failure for filed briefs**
Platform version updates making past AI research irreproducible. Research cited in filed briefs cannot be reconstructed when needed. No retrieval obligation on any Swiss-hosted platform.

**Signal 6: Vendor terms and professional secrecy conflict unresolved**
No Geneva law firm has obtained legal confirmation that AI vendor terms are compatible with Article 321 CP professional secrecy obligations. The conflict is structural and affects every active matter.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Article 321 CP professional secrecy:**
Article 321 CP prohibits unauthorized disclosure of client information to third parties. AI vendor terms consistently contain data use provisions for model improvement. No compatibility analysis exists for any major platform.

**nFADP Articles 13 and 21:**
Art. 13 requires explanations of automated decisions. Art. 21 requires documentation of AI-assisted decision logic. Attorney work product generated with AI assistance creates derivation questions the nFADP does not resolve for the legal professional context.

**SBA AI guidelines June 2024:**
Useful as orientation but provide no operational discharge methodology for small firm obligations. The guidelines do not address the specific configuration of a 2–5 attorney firm using AI tools.

**FDPIC and AI guidance:**
No guidance specifically addressing attorney use of AI systems. Absence of guidance is not evidence of compliance.

**Geneva civil procedure:**
No court has established an AI authentication protocol for disputed filings. The procedural question of how to verify AI-assisted work product authenticity is formally undefined.

**EU AI Act and Swiss law firms:**
Swiss law firms serving EU-connected clients may qualify as deployers under EU AI Act Annex III. No published standard defines what compliance looks like for a Swiss law firm.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: AI reasoning chain integrity as distinct from hallucination**
AI produces defensible conclusions through pattern-synthesis, not principled legal derivation. The reasoning chain cannot be reconstructed. This is a distinct failure mode: correctness versus derivability. No Geneva authority has addressed reasoning chain integrity for legal work product.

**Finding 2: Query metadata as attorney work product outside Article 321 CP jurisdiction**
The AI research query trail (search patterns, strategic inquiry sequences, hypothesis-testing) reveals case strategy through vendor infrastructure. Traditional attorney research notes are protected under Article 321 CP. AI query metadata is not. No Geneva bar communication has addressed query metadata as work product.

**Finding 3: Security instinct gap for AI tools**
Geneva attorneys have disciplined information security instincts (Chinese walls, classified matter protocols, clean desk policies) that have not been extended to AI tools. Data flows, retention practices, and access controls of AI tools have never been evaluated against the same principles applied to everything else. The gap is unnamed and unaddressed.

**Finding 4: Calendar-event timing mismatch**
AI accountability problems surface through predetermined calendar events (carrier renewals, client audits, court challenges) before governance infrastructure exists. The accountability moment arrives regardless of when decisions were made. This is distinct from documentation adequacy findings — those address what documents should contain; this addresses when they must exist.

**Finding 5: Associate analytical competency degradation**
Junior attorneys produce defensible AI-assisted output while independent analytical capability declines. No bar continuing education framework distinguishes AI-assisted output quality from independent analytical competency. The degradation is unnamed and unaddressed.

**Finding 6: Three ungoverned parallel accountability tracks**
Bar ethics, nFADP data protection, and carrier underwriting operate as independent systems with different standards, languages, timing, and consequences. No coherence mapping exists between them. Firms manage three systems without any map. This is a structural market condition, not a temporary gap.
