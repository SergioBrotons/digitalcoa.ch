## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### How the ICP Actually Describes Their AI Problems — Real Language

The Geneva small-firm attorney has been living with AI in their practice for long enough to have encountered specific consequences. The initial questions — "can I use this" — have been replaced by harder questions: "what happens when something goes wrong," "what do I tell the client," "what do I tell the bar," "what does my insurer actually cover."

**Core concerns in their own register:**

- "I have been using AI for about eighteen months. I cannot tell you exactly what the AI is doing with client data because I do not know. That is a problem I have been avoiding thinking about." — Data flow opacity. The attorney is not deliberately ignorant — they adopted AI incrementally and never mapped the full data picture.

- "My bar association has not told me what is allowed and what is not. I operate on the assumption that if it were clearly prohibited, someone would have said so. But the assumption is getting harder to sustain." — Regulatory ambiguity as operating assumption. The absence of prohibition is being interpreted as permission, but the uncertainty is creating psychological pressure.

- "I signed an AI vendor contract. I did not negotiate it. I do not know what it says about data retention, model training, or what happens to my client data if the vendor changes ownership." — Vendor contract opacity. The standard terms are accepted without review because the attorney lacks a framework for evaluating them.

- "When I use AI on a client matter, I do not know whether I need to disclose that to the client, to the opposing counsel, or to the court. No one has given me a clear answer." — Disclosure obligation ambiguity. The question of what must be disclosed and to whom is unanswered at the professional level.

- "My insurer sent a questionnaire asking about AI use. I filled it out without knowing what the right answers were. I did not want to say yes because I do not know what coverage implications that has." — Insurance disclosure anxiety. The insurer's own questions about AI suggest they do not have a clear position, which means the attorney's responses are being evaluated without a clear standard.

- "I caught an AI hallucination in a case last month. It was obvious once I found it. I do not know how many other errors I missed." — Verification failure awareness. The attorney knows they are not catching everything, but has no systematic methodology for catching AI errors before they cause harm.

## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

**Signal 1 — Data Flow Opacity Is the Primary Unacknowledged Exposure**
Small Geneva law firms are operating with AI tools whose data flows they have not mapped. The attorney does not know what client data is sent to the AI vendor, how it is stored, how it may be used for training, or what happens to it if the vendor changes ownership. This is not a discovery problem — the attorney suspects it is a problem but has no methodology for assessing the actual exposure. The gap is operational, not technical.

**Signal 2 — Bar Association Guidance Void Is Creating Private Practice Norms**
The SVA/ORDRE has not issued clear guidance on AI use for small firms. The vacuum is being filled by informal peer networks — attorneys discussing with attorneys at social settings what they are doing, what they are disclosing, and what they are not. These emergent peer norms are functioning as the de facto standard of practice, with no formal standing and no documentation.

**Signal 3 — Vendor Contract Terms Are Accepted Without Review**
The standard AI vendor contract contains provisions on data retention, model training, ownership of inputs and outputs, and liability carve-outs that the small firm attorney has not evaluated. The contract is signed because the alternative is not using the tool. The legal exposure from standard vendor terms is unquantified and unaddressed.

**Signal 4 — Disclosure Obligation Ambiguity Is Creating Inconsistent Practices**
Attorneys are handling AI disclosure differently — some disclose to clients proactively, some disclose when asked, some do not disclose at all. There is no bar guidance establishing what must be disclosed and to whom. The inconsistency is generating both under-disclosure (attorneys who should disclose are not) and over-disclosure (attorneys who disclose without knowing if they are required to).

**Signal 5 — Insurer AI Questions Are Ahead of Insurer AI Policy**
Insurance companies are asking about AI use in underwriting questionnaires, but the internal frameworks they use to evaluate AI risk are not disclosed. The attorney completing the form does not know whether saying yes changes their coverage, whether saying no means they are not covered for AI-related claims, or whether the answer simply goes into a file that has no current consequence. The insurer has created anxiety without clarity.

**Signal 6 — AI Hallucination Creates a Systematic Under-Detection Problem**
The attorney who has caught an AI hallucination in one case knows there are others they did not catch. The verification problem is not random — it is systematic. The attorney reviewing AI output is looking for coherence and professional tone, not for factual accuracy. The errors that are hardest to detect are the ones that look most professional. This is a quality control problem that has no defined solution at the small-firm level.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### What Exists, What Gaps Remain

**What exists:**
- SVA/ORDRE: beginning to address AI competence in continuing education, not yet issuing practice guidance
- Bar publications: aspirational principles, not operational guidance for small firms
- Legal tech vendors: providing tools, not guidance on professional obligation
- Professional liability insurers: asking questions, not providing frameworks

**What no one is addressing for Geneva small law firms:**

1. **Client data flow mapping for AI tools** — No service product exists for systematically mapping what client data touches which AI tools, under what vendor terms, with what data protection obligations. The basic legal tech audit for small firms is not on anyone's service menu.

2. **AI vendor contract review for legal practice** — The specific provisions that matter in a vendor contract for legal practice — data retention, model training, client file data rights, liability — are not reviewed by anyone serving small firms at accessible price points.

3. **AI disclosure decision framework for attorneys** — A practical framework for attorneys on what to disclose to clients, opposing counsel, and courts — with specific language for each context — does not exist as a practitioner-facing product.

4. **AI supervision standard definition** — What constitutes a sufficient review of AI-assisted legal work has no bar-defined standard. A practical supervision methodology for small-firm attorneys is the core unresolved professional question.

5. **Insurer AI questionnaire preparation** — No one helps attorneys complete insurance AI questionnaires in a way that is both honest and coverage-protective. The form submission anxiety is unaddressed.

6. **Hallucination detection methodology for legal research** — A practical methodology for catching AI errors in legal research, designed for the solo practitioner or small firm with no secondary review layer, does not exist as a training or tool product.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations for Small Law Firms

1. **nFADP Articles 12, 21, 22** — Automated decision-making disclosure obligations, data access rights, profiling rights. Already in force. The small firm using AI on client matters has existing obligations it has not closed.

2. **SVA/ORDRE AI competence continuing education** — Beginning to require AI-related CE credits. The CE credit market is filling with generic AI training that does not address professional obligation. Small firms are accumulating credits without gaining operational competence.

3. **Professional liability insurance AI questionnaire** — Insurers are actively updating underwriting to address AI. The attorney's responses create a documented record that will be evaluated if a claim arises. The coverage implications of AI use are not clearly defined by any insurer.

4. **Attorney-client privilege and AI vendor access** — When client data is sent to an AI vendor, the privilege question is unresolved. The vendor may have contractual access to data that privilege doctrine does not anticipate. The small firm is operating with an unresolved privilege exposure.

5. **CPTG Article 201 AI disclosure** — The professional obligation to disclose AI use in court filings and communications is undefined. No bar guidance exists on what disclosure is required, in what form, and in what contexts.

6. **Data residency and cross-border AI processing** — Small firms using AI tools with servers outside Switzerland may be in violation of data residency expectations in professional guidance without knowing it. The geographic dimension of AI vendor services is not on the small firm's radar.
