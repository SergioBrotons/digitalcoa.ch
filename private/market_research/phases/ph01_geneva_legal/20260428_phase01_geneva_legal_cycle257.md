### English

**What the managing partner or individual attorney actually says, translated from the professional register:**

> "We started using AI for research and drafting two or three years ago. It helped us handle more work with the same team. But now there are questionnaires — from carriers, from clients, from opposing parties — asking us to explain what we did, when, with which tools, and what the AI contributed. > "The bar association has had sessions on AI. They're aware the question exists. But I can't point to anything published that tells me what I am actually required to do. There's informal conversation. There's the SVA working group. But formal published guidance that I could rely on — nothing I could hold in my hand."

> "Our professional liability insurance covers professional errors. If AI makes a mistake and I sign off on it, I'm not sure where that sits. Is it covered? Is it excluded? I haven't found language in the policy that answers the question."

> "Client confidentiality is the foundation. But if I'm using an AI tool to work on a privileged matter, where does that data go? What does the provider keep? What can they show a regulator if asked? These are questions I don't have answers to, and nobody in Geneva seems to have written them down."

> "The privilege question haunts me. If the AI retains my search queries and reasoning — even after the matter closes — is that privileged? Can an opposing party, through discovery or a data subject access request, reconstruct how I built my legal theory? The answer isn't published anywhere."

> "When an associate leaves, they take knowledge of how the firm set up its AI workflows. There's no protocol for capturing that. The next person starts over, and the firm's institutional memory of its own AI configuration is simply gone."

**Primary concern vocabulary:** reconstruction, documentation, accountability, personal signature, exposure, coverage, guidance.

## 02 — Market Signals

Current problems, failure modes, paradoxes, and complaints in the Geneva legal AI market as observed from position C257.

---

### The Guidance-Layering Paradox

Geneva attorneys are receiving AI governance signals from at least three distinct institutional sources simultaneously: the SVA working group process, OAGE published positions, and carrier questionnaires. The paradox is that these sources are not coordinated, may be inconsistent, and carry different levels of institutional protection. An informal steering-committee signal may be more operationally current than a published OAGE position — but the informal signal carries no safe harbor. Attorneys who follow the most current guidance expose themselves to the least institutional protection. The market has not named this structure.

### nFADP Exemption Ambiguity as Active Planning Blocker

The nFADP small-business exemption creates genuine uncertainty specifically for professional services firms. The exemption threshold applies to data processing volume, but law firms process client data as their core business — not as a secondary activity. A 2–5 attorney Geneva firm may exceed the exemption threshold simply by functioning normally as a law practice, regardless of AI deployment. This question is unresolved in OAGE guidance and has not been addressed by the SVA working group. Firms are making assumptions in both directions without a published resolution. The ambiguity is not a theoretical gap: it determines which obligations are operative.

### Data Residency as Privilege Architecture Question

The data residency question for law firms is distinct from other professional services because attorney-client privilege makes the research and reasoning process itself sensitive — not just the client data within it. AI research platforms retain query content, search logic, and reasoning traces. When a Swiss firm uses a US-based AI research platform on a privileged matter, the query trail may be retained by the provider under US law, accessible to US authorities, and outside the scope of Swiss professional secrecy protections. No Geneva bar guidance has addressed whether this constitutes a privilege waiver through third-party retention. The question is live for every active privileged matter using AI research tools.

### The Associate-Departure Knowledge Loss Problem Has No Protocol

The departure of an associate who understood the firm's AI configuration has been identified in prior cycles as a knowledge management problem. What has not been addressed is that there is no Geneva OAGE or SVA protocol for documenting AI workflow configuration as a firm asset subject to succession obligations. The firm's AI institutional knowledge is not covered by Art. 553 CO succession framework because the obligation applies to client files and contact relationships — not to tool configuration knowledge. This creates a structural gap where accumulated AI governance decisions evaporate with staff movement, compounding daily.

### Carrier Questionnaire as Retrospective Forcing Function, Not Adequacy Measurement

The third-generation carrier questionnaire probes the 2023–2025 period specifically. For Geneva small firms, this means: the documentation that would demonstrate adequacy was never created, the period it covers is now closed, and the questions being asked are more precise than any guidance available at the time. The carrier is functioning as an informal retrospective standard-setter. The market failure is not that firms lack guidance — it is that guidance arrived after the behavior it would have governed, and the carrier's retrospective questions cannot be answered by retroactive documentation because the documentation architecture did not exist.

### Geneva as International Arbitration Seat Creates Dual-Framework Exposure

Geneva is the seat of ICC, WIPO, and major ad hoc international arbitration. Attorneys practicing international arbitration face AI procedural requirements from tribunals that operate under their own procedural rules — not Geneva civil procedure, not OAGE guidance. ICC and WIPO have issued or are forming AI procedural guidelines for participating counsel. This creates a dual-framework accountability obligation: OAGE/SVA domestic guidance plus tribunal-specific AI requirements simultaneously. No Geneva market service has addressed the intersection of domestic bar obligations and arbitral tribunal AI requirements for the same matter.

### Professional Liability Insurance Boundary Is Operationally undefined

Managing partners at small Geneva firms cannot determine from their PI policy language whether AI-assisted professional errors fall within or outside coverage. Insurers have not published AI-specific coverage interpretations. The policy may contain vague exclusions designed to preserve post-hoc interpretation room — language that protects the insurer's right to decide after a claim, not the policyholder's right to know before. Managing partners are signing carrier AI questionnaires attesting to AI governance adequacy without knowing whether their PI coverage would respond to an AI-assisted error claim. These are two separate accountability instruments with no reconciliation.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

What others say, gaps, and what no one is addressing in the Geneva legal AI governance market.

---

### What the Market Is Currently Offering

Geneva market participants addressing small-firm AI governance include bar association CE programming (introductory, attendance-measured), general cybersecurity firms (focused on data breach, not professional accountability), IT vendors (selling tool configuration, not governance documentation), and management consultants (enterprise-scale frameworks inapplicable at 2–5 attorney scale). None of these address the specific accountability architecture a small Geneva law firm needs.

### What No One Is Addressing

**The OAGE formal-versus-informal guidance distinction.** No market participant has mapped OAGE published positions against informal steering-committee signals to identify where the gap lies — and what guidance attorneys are actually relying on versus what they should be relying on. The informal circuit carries operationally relevant guidance with no institutional protection. The published circuit carries institutional protection without operational content. No service has addressed this structural divergence.

**The nFADP exemption question for professional services firms.** No Geneva service has addressed the specific question of whether the nFADP small-business exemption applies to law firms given the nature of their data processing. This is a planning-level question that determines the entire compliance architecture. Getting it wrong in either direction — over-complying when exempt, under-complying when not — has consequences. No market participant has published a position on this.

**The dual-framework obligation for international arbitration attorneys.** The intersection of OAGE/SVA domestic guidance and ICC/WIPO/ad hoc tribunal AI requirements for the same matter is unaddressed. An attorney practicing international arbitration in Geneva may have domestic guidance, tribunal rules, and carrier requirements all pointing in different directions simultaneously. No Geneva advisory service has mapped this intersection.

**The PI coverage question for AI-assisted errors.** No insurer, broker, or advisory has published a framework for evaluating whether a specific PI policy would respond to an AI-assisted professional error. Managing partners are evaluating this in the dark. A service that could assess policy language against the specific AI error scenarios Geneva attorneys face would be addressing a gap no one has named operationally.

**The Geneva civil procedure AI evidence authentication standard.** C254 addressed notarial certification for AI-assisted instruments. But Geneva civil procedure authentication requirements for AI-generated evidence in court proceedings — what attorneys must be able to demonstrate about AI contribution, tool version, and human review — is unpublished. Attorneys filing AI-assisted briefs or presenting AI-compiled evidence in Geneva courts have no published standard to reference.

**Data residency as a privilege architecture question.** No Geneva service has addressed AI research platform data residency as a privilege architecture question — the question of whether query trail retention constitutes a privilege waiver for the research and reasoning process, not just the client data within it. This is distinct from the general data residency question because the privilege interest is in the attorney's analytical process, which AI platforms retain.

## 04 — Compliance Context

Geneva and Swiss-specific regulatory obligations relevant to the Geneva legal AI ICP.

---

### OAGE / SVA — Ordre des Avocats de Genève and Schweizerische Anwaltsverband

The Ordre des Avocats de Genève publishes standards governing attorney professional conduct under the Geneva Law on the Legal Profession (LPAav). The SVA operates at the federal level. Neither has published AI-specific binding guidance for small firms that would constitute a safe harbor. OAGE has held educational sessions on AI, but session attendance measures participation, not practice adequacy. The SVA working group on AI has produced discussion documents that circulate informally — creating professional responsibility signals without the institutional protection of published guidance.

The structural accountability gap: informal guidance may be more current but carries no safe harbor. Published guidance carries institutional protection but may lag operational reality by 12–18 months. Geneva attorneys have no framework for navigating this gap.

### nFADP — Federal Act on Data Protection (revised, in force)

The revised nFADP applies to law firms processing client personal data. Key provisions relevant to small Geneva firms:

- **Art. 6 / Art. 8:** Lawful basis and data minimization obligations apply to client data processing. AI tools processing client matter data for research, drafting, or case management constitute data processing under nFADP. The small-business exemption threshold may or may not apply to professional services firms depending on data processing volume and nature — a question with no published resolution.

- **Art. 9:** Third-party processor authorization. Using a non-Swiss AI provider for legal work requires documented processor authorization under Art. 9. For US-based providers, the standard contractual clauses may not resolve the professional secrecy intersection under Art. 321 CP.

- **Art. 12 / Art. 13:** Data subject access rights. Clients may submit access requests for matters involving AI processing. Firms must be able to identify what AI processed, what it contributed, and what data was retained. This requires documentation architecture most small firms do not have.

- **Art. 21:** Automated decision explanation. If AI contributes to substantive legal recommendations, clients may have the right to explanations of the AI's reasoning. Most AI legal tools do not produce per-instance reasoning traces. The explanation right may be structurally incompatible with the AI architecture in use.

### FDPIC — Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner

The FDPIC has not published law-firm-specific AI guidance. Enforcement patterns suggest law firms are an emerging focus as AI deployment in legal practice becomes more prevalent and more visible through carrier questionnaires and client questionnaires. The FDPIC's pattern of enforcement suggests it reads practical deployment rather than published policy — meaning firms are being measured against obligations they cannot read in published guidance.

The compliance context gap: the FDPIC is forming a position on professional services AI processing without publishing it in operational form. Geneva attorneys are operating under regulatory anticipation rather than regulatory guidance.

### Professional Liability Insurance

PI insurance for Geneva attorneys is governed by the Federal Act on the Free Movement of Lawyers and the Geneva bar admission requirements. Coverage applies to professional errors. The critical ambiguity: whether AI-assisted errors — where the attorney reviewed and approved AI output that proved incorrect — constitute covered professional errors or excluded technological errors is not resolved in published policy language. Insurers have not issued AI-specific coverage interpretations. Managing partners signing carrier AI governance attestations are doing so without knowing whether their PI coverage would respond to the exposure they are attesting to.

### Geneva Civil Procedure

The Swiss Code of Civil Procedure (CPC) and Geneva-specific procedural rules govern evidence authentication in Geneva courts. AI-generated evidence — briefs compiled with AI, AI-compiled case summaries, AI-generated reasoning chains presented to courts — has no published authentication standard under Geneva procedure. C254 addressed notarial certification for specific instruments, but the broader question of how attorneys authenticate AI contribution in court filings, and what opposing parties may demand in terms of AI methodology disclosure, remains unresolved.

### International Arbitration — Geneva as Seat

ICC and WIPO arbitration rules increasingly include AI procedural provisions for participating counsel. ICC has published guidance on AI use in arbitration proceedings. WIPO has issued administrative Circulars on AI tool use. These create specific procedural obligations for Geneva-based attorneys practicing international arbitration that operate in addition to — not instead of — OAGE/SVA domestic guidance. The dual-framework intersection is not addressed by any Geneva bar guidance or market service.

## 06 — Findings Summary

Six net-new insights distinct from C214–C256 Phase 01 Geneva Legal findings.

---

**Finding 01: OAGE formal-versus-informal guidance divergence creates a named structural accountability gap with no institutional resolution mechanism.**

The Geneva bar operates two simultaneous guidance channels: formal published positions carrying institutional protection without operational content, and informal steering-committee signals carrying operational relevance without safe harbor. No prior C214–C256 Phase 01 has identified this divergence as a discrete structural phenomenon. Managing partners navigating AI accountability are making daily decisions based on whichever channel is most accessible — not on which channel provides actual protection. This is distinct from prior findings about SVA guidance absence, bar CE false adequacy signals, and informal circuit adverse selection.

**Finding 02: The nFADP small-business exemption applicability to professional services firms is a planning-level ambiguity with no published resolution — operative in both directions simultaneously.**

The nFADP exemption threshold applies to data processing volume. Law firms process client personal data as their primary activity — not incidentally. A 2–5 attorney firm functioning normally as a legal practice may exceed the threshold regardless of AI deployment. This question has not been addressed by OAGE guidance, SVA working group output, or FDPIC interpretation. Firms are making assumptions in both directions. Getting it wrong means either over-complying with unnecessary obligations or under-complying with operative ones. No prior cycle has identified this as a specific planning-level question for the professional services exemption — distinct from the general nFADP application findings in C255/C256.

**Finding 03: Geneva civil procedure AI evidence authentication requirements are distinct from notarial seal certification — the former remains formally undefined while the latter was addressed in C254.**

C254 Phase 01 identified notarial electronic seal certification for AI-assisted instruments as unresolved under Swiss civil procedure. But notarial certification addresses a specific high-risk instrument category — not the broader question of how attorneys authenticate AI contribution in court filings generally, what opposing parties may demand in terms of AI methodology disclosure, or how Geneva civil procedure applies to AI-generated evidence in ordinary litigation. These are distinct procedural questions. No prior cycle has addressed the civil procedure authentication question separately from the notarial certification question.

**Finding 04: PI insurance coverage boundary for AI-assisted professional errors is undefined in published policy language — creating an undisclosed dual-accountability exposure when managing partners sign carrier AI attestations.**

Managing partners are signing carrier AI governance attestations creating documented personal exposure, while simultaneously holding PI policies whose coverage boundary for AI-assisted errors has not been published by any insurer. These two accountability instruments are not reconciled. If an AI-assisted error occurs and the PI insurer declines coverage based on ambiguous exclusion language, the managing partner's carrier attestation stands as a documented personal acknowledgment of governance adequacy that the error contradicted. No prior cycle has identified this specific dual-accountability exposure — the intersection of carrier attestation language and PI policy language — as a discrete finding.

**Finding 05: AI legal research platform query retention creates a privilege architecture problem distinct from client data residency — the attorney's analytical process itself may be retained by non-Swiss providers outside professional secrecy protection.**

The data residency question in prior cycles has focused on client personal data. For law firms, a distinct question is whether AI research platform query retention constitutes a privilege waiver for the attorney's analytical and reasoning process — not the client data within it. US-based AI research platforms retain query content and search logic under US law. Swiss professional secrecy (Art. 321 CP) protects communications and work product — but third-party retention by non-Swiss providers may fall outside this protection. When a client submits an access request, or an opposing party pursues discovery, the question of whether the attorney's reasoning trail in the AI platform constitutes discoverable material is unresolved. No prior cycle has identified this as a privilege architecture question distinct from the general data residency framing.

**Finding 06: Geneva as international arbitration seat creates a dual-framework accountability obligation for practicing attorneys that no market service has addressed — domestic bar guidance plus tribunal AI procedural requirements operating simultaneously for the same matter.**

ICC, WIPO, and ad hoc international arbitral tribunals seated in Geneva have issued or are forming AI procedural requirements for participating counsel. Attorneys practicing international arbitration in Geneva simultaneously face OAGE/SVA domestic guidance and tribunal-specific AI obligations. These frameworks have not been harmonized — and may be inconsistent — because they were developed by different institutions at different times for different purposes. An attorney practicing international arbitration cannot discharge both frameworks by following one. No Geneva advisory service has mapped this dual-framework intersection. No prior C214–C256 Phase 01 has identified international arbitration as creating a specifically Geneva-based dual-framework accountability obligation distinct from domestic court AI procedural questions.
