## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### How the ICP Actually Describes Their AI Problems — Real Language

The managing partner or solo practitioner at a 2–5 attorney Geneva firm has been operating with AI long enough to have passed through the early adoption anxieties (C214's confidentiality blocking, C215's liability framing, C216's documentation debt). C222 finds them in a new state: they have been through an AI governance review, they have attempted to implement a documentation structure, and they are now discovering that the gap between "having a governance policy on paper" and "operating in compliance with it in practice" is where the actual exposure lives.

The language has shifted. The frame is now about governance implementation failure — the experience of having tried to govern AI and discovering that the operational reality of a small practice does not sustain the governance structure they adopted.

**Core concerns in their own register:**

- "I paid for an AI governance review eight months ago. I have a document. What I do not have is a practice that actually operates according to that document. The gap between what I signed and what I do is where I live now." — Governance implementation gap. The attorney who invested in a governance assessment and has a documented policy has discovered that sustaining the governance structure in daily practice requires ongoing attention the small firm cannot allocate. The document exists; the practice does not conform to it. This is a different problem from "no governance policy."

- "The bar association published guidance on AI use. I read it. I still do not know what I am supposed to do on a Tuesday morning when a client sends me an urgent brief and I need to use AI to meet the deadline." — Guidance abstraction problem. Bar-level guidance addresses principles; it does not address the operational scenarios that constitute most of the attorney's AI use. The guidance is intellectually coherent but practically useless for the time-pressured Tuesday morning scenario.

- "I have three AI tools in the practice. One of them changed its data retention terms in January. I found out in March because I happened to look at the terms. No one notified me." — Vendor term change detection failure. The small firm that relies on ad hoc vendor monitoring misses vendor term changes that affect client data handling. There is no system for tracking vendor term changes. The attorney discovers material changes by accident.

- "My bar requires AI-related continuing education credits. The courses available are about how to use AI, not about what you are allowed to do with it. I am getting credit for learning things that do not address my actual professional obligation." — CE credit misalignment. The AI-related CE available to Geneva attorneys addresses tool competence — how to use AI more effectively — not professional obligation — what you are permitted and required to do with AI. The attorney is accumulating credits that do not serve their actual governance need.

- "The question I get from clients now is not 'do you use AI' — it is 'can you show me your AI governance documentation.' And I can show them the document, but I cannot show them a practice that operates according to it." — Client governance audit anxiety. Clients with their own board-level AI governance requirements are no longer asking whether AI is used — they are asking to review the governance documentation and, increasingly, to assess whether the governance is live or performative. The attorney who can produce a document but cannot demonstrate operational conformance faces a new kind of client relationship risk.

- "My associate uses AI differently than I do. I do not have a way to know whether their AI use is within the governance framework we nominally operate under. I signed off on the policy — that does not mean I know what is happening in the practice." — Delegation accountability gap for AI. The managing partner who adopted a governance framework cannot verify that the framework is being followed by other attorneys in the practice. The delegation of AI governance to associates is nominal — there is no verification mechanism. This is the supervision architecture problem in a new form: not "what is my review process" but "how do I know what my associate's review process is."

## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

**Signal 1 — Governance Implementation Failure Is the New Primary Problem**
The market has shifted from "no governance policy" to "governance policy without operational conformance." Attorneys who invested in governance reviews and have documented policies are discovering that the small firm's operational reality does not sustain the governance structure they adopted. The policy exists; the practice does not conform to it. This is a qualitatively different problem from no policy — it involves active misrepresentation risk (operating differently from documented policy) rather than just absence-of-governance risk.

**Signal 2 — Bar Guidance Addresses Principles, Not Tuesday Morning Scenarios**
The SVA/ORDRE guidance published to date addresses AI governance at the principle level — confidentiality obligations, supervision standards, disclosure language. It does not address the operational scenarios that constitute most AI use: the urgent brief, the client who specifically requests AI-assisted research, the associate who uses AI on a filing without the supervising partner's awareness. The gap between principled guidance and operational Tuesday morning practice is creating a new market failure: attorneys who have guidance and cannot apply it.

**Signal 3 — Vendor Term Change Detection Is an Unaddressed Small Firm Problem**
AI vendors change data retention terms, model training provisions, and service terms without active notification to small law firm customers. The attorney who discovered a January vendor term change in March — by accident — illustrates a systematic problem: there is no mechanism for a small firm to track vendor term changes that affect client data handling. The nFADP compliance position of the firm can change without the firm's knowledge when a vendor changes its terms.

**Signal 4 — CE Credit Alignment Failures Are Creating a Documentation Paradox**
The AI-related continuing education available to Geneva attorneys is calibrated to tool competence — how to use AI tools more effectively — not to professional obligation — what attorneys are permitted and required to do. The attorney accumulating AI-related CE credits is not necessarily advancing their governance position. This creates a documentation paradox: CE credit records show AI-related training that does not demonstrate professional obligation competence. The bar record may actively misrepresent the attorney's actual governance knowledge.

**Signal 5 — Client Governance Reviews Are Moving From Documentation to Operational Conformance**
Corporate clients and institutions are no longer satisfied with governance documentation — they are beginning to assess operational conformance. The question has shifted from "can you show me your AI policy" to "can you show me a practice that operates according to it." This is a materially higher standard. The attorney who can produce a document but cannot demonstrate operational conformance faces a new client relationship risk category.

**Signal 6 — Delegation Accountability for AI Is Nominal Without Verification Mechanisms**
The managing partner who adopted a governance framework cannot verify that the framework is being followed by associates. The delegation of AI governance is nominal — the policy exists, the signature is on the document — but there is no mechanism for the managing partner to know whether associates are operating within it. This creates a supervisory liability exposure that is invisible: the partner is responsible for the associate's AI use but has no verification path.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### What Exists, What Gaps Remain

**What exists:**
- AI governance review products (from multiple providers): produce a documented policy
- SVA/ORDRE CE credits: address tool competence, not professional obligation
- Bar publications: principled guidance, not operational scenario guidance
- Vendor DPA offerings: general-purpose, not client-specific legal practice documentation

**What no one is addressing for Geneva small law firms:**

1. **Governance operationalization support** — No service product helps attorneys move from documented governance policy to operational conformance. The gap between "having a document" and "operating according to it" has no service solution.

2. **Vendor term change monitoring for small law firms** — No service monitors AI vendor terms for changes that affect small law firm client data handling. The vendor term change detection problem is entirely unaddressed.

3. **CE credit alignment audit** — No service reviews an attorney's CE credit history against their actual governance need and identifies the misalignment. The attorney accumulating credits without advancing governance has no diagnostic tool.

4. **Operational AI governance conformance assessment** — No service assesses whether a firm's actual practice conforms to its documented governance policy. The client governance review standard (operational conformance vs. documentation) has no corresponding advisory product.

5. **Associate AI governance accountability framework** — No framework exists for the managing partner to verify associate AI governance conformance without intrusive monitoring. The delegation accountability problem has no structured solution.

6. **Tuesday morning scenario guidance** — No bar guidance or advisory product addresses the specific operational scenarios — urgent client matters, specific client AI requests, associate AI use without supervising partner awareness — that constitute most AI use in practice. The principled framework exists; the scenario-specific operational guidance does not.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations for Small Law Firms

1. **SVA/ORDRE CE credit requirements for AI** — The bar requires AI-related continuing education credits but the available curriculum addresses tool competence, not professional obligation. The attorney's CE record may actively misrepresent their governance knowledge. The bar has not defined what "appropriate AI CE" means for professional obligation purposes.

2. **nFADP Art. 6 legal basis documentation** — The firm with a documented governance policy but non-conforming practice has a specific compliance exposure: the documented policy does not reflect actual data processing practices. When a vendor changes terms (as in the January-to-March discovery), the nFADP Art. 6 legal basis may have changed without the firm's knowledge or documentation update.

3. **SVA/ORDRE supervision obligations for delegated AI use** — When a managing partner delegates AI use to an associate, the supervision obligation under SVA/ORDRE professional rules requires the partner to verify that AI use is within the governance framework. Without a verification mechanism, the partner cannot satisfy this obligation even if the policy document exists.

4. **CPTG Art. 201 AI disclosure in Geneva proceedings** — The court-level expectation for AI disclosure in proceedings has not been operationalized into a format that attorneys can use in their daily practice. The gap between the rule and the operational scenario remains unresolved.

5. **Client board-level AI governance review requests** — Corporate clients with their own AI governance requirements are conducting reviews of external counsel AI governance documentation. The standard for these reviews is not defined — it varies by client — but the expectation of operational conformance (not just documentation) is appearing in the more sophisticated clients.

6. **Professional liability insurance AI disclosure** — The documented governance policy creates a representation to the insurer. If the practice does not conform to the documented policy, the firm may have made a misrepresentation to the insurer about its AI governance practices. This is a new coverage exposure category.
