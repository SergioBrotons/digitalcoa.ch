## 02 — Market Signals

**Current problems in the Geneva small law firm AI market:**

1. **Professional secrecy vs. vendor terms structural conflict.** AI vendor terms of service consistently contain data use provisions for model improvement. Professional secrecy obligations under Art. 321 CP require that client information not be accessible to third parties. No Geneva law firm has obtained legal confirmation that these are compatible. Firms are operating under unresolved conflict.

2. **Associate-level AI use without supervisory architecture.** Associates are the highest-volume AI users in small firms. Supervising partners review outputs, not pipelines. The accountability for AI decisions made during research and drafting sits with the signing attorney who did not make the AI decision and cannot describe the system that produced the output.

3. **Client data entering vendor infrastructure without authorization architecture.** Uploading client files to AI systems occurs continuously, without systematic authorization review. Vendor privacy policies govern, not firm policy. Corporate acquisitions of AI vendors have already changed terms silently. Active matters are operating under restructured authorization chains without firm awareness.

4. **False security from FDPIC absence.** No enforcement action, no guidance, no advisory opinion from the Federal Data Protection Information Commissioner on AI use in legal practice. Absence is interpreted as permission. First enforcement action will arrive without warning — capacity constraint is not compliance evidence.

5. **Geneva bar advisory void as operating permission.** No formal ORDRE advisory channel for AI governance questions. No published guidance. No advisory opinion mechanism. Silence functions as de facto permission, which self-reinforces: firms do not ask because nothing has been published, bar publishes nothing because no one is asking.

6. **Client AI sophistication escalation creating conversational exposure.** Sophisticated clients — particularly international private clients and corporate counsel — are beginning to ask specific questions about AI methodology, data residency, and provenance. Responses are improvised. Improvised responses to client questions about AI create documentary evidence of firm practices without governance architecture behind them.

7. **International arbitration AI use entirely ungoverned.** Geneva is an ICC and WIPO seat. AI use in arbitral proceedings has no disclosed framework — not from arbitral institutions, not from the bar, not from any Geneva service. Counsel using AI in high-stakes proceedings with no protocol for what must be disclosed, what is privileged, and what happens to the AI system's outputs after proceedings close.

8. **Billing model structurally mismeasures AI governance cost.** Hourly billing captures AI efficiency gains as margin. Governance documentation — pipeline reviews, authorization checks, vendor terms assessment — is unbillable overhead. The pricing model creates a structural incentive to minimize governance investment. This is not a behavioral problem; it is an architectural one.

9. **Geneva courts have no established AI disclosure procedural framework.** AI-affected proceedings are handled on a case-by-case basis with no published Geneva court practice directive. First formally contested AI disclosure motion in a specific chamber creates operative precedent through the response — uneven exposure across chambers, no consolidated framework, no attorney knowing what standard applies.

10. **PI insurance coverage for AI error architecturally undefined.** Insurers have not published classification methodology for AI-assisted errors. The coverage question — whether it falls under professional liability, whether it requires separate rider, whether it is excluded — is unanswered. Managing partners are signing carrier attestations for coverage they cannot confirm exists.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Geneva and Swiss-specific regulatory obligations for small law firm AI use:**

### Federal Data Protection Act (nFADP, nFZO, in force 1 September 2023)

- **Art. 4–6:** Lawful basis, data minimization, purpose limitation — applicable to client file processing in AI systems
- **Art. 9:** Processing of sensitive personal data (health, financial, legal matters) requires explicit legal basis; client files in AI systems activate this obligation without systematic authorization architecture in place
- **Art. 13:** Information obligations at data collection; uploading client files to AI systems triggers disclosure obligations most firms are not discharging
- **Art. 21:** Data subject rights including derivation of conclusions — AI inference chains from client files subject to derivation requests clients have not yet systematically made
- **Small-business exemption applicability:** Unresolved. Law firm primary activity involves systematic client data processing. Exemption threshold — data processing "ancillary" to non-data-processing primary activity — likely not met. No published authority resolving this.
- **Cross-border transfer:** US-based AI vendor processing creates cross-border transfer under Art. 6(e) or adequate country basis; US does not have Swiss adequacy determination; standard contractual clauses required but not implemented by any Geneva law firm surveyed.

### Federal Data Protection Information Commissioner (FDPIC)

- No enforcement action against Geneva law firm for AI use as of April 2026
- No guidance specifically addressing attorney use of AI systems
- No advisory opinion on nFADP applicability to AI vendor terms for law firms
- **Paradox:** Absence creates false security; capacity constraint is not compliance evidence. First enforcement action will arrive without warning.

### SVA/ORDRE Professional Obligations

**Geneva Bar Association (ORDRE des Avocats de Genève):**
- Art. 321 CP (Swiss Criminal Code): Professional secrecy is statutory obligation; criminal sanction for unauthorized disclosure
- Professional code of conduct: Secrecy obligations apply to all client information, all communications, all work product
- No AI-specific guidance published as of April 2026
- No advisory opinion mechanism for AI governance questions
- No CE requirement adapted for AI content
- **Structural void:** Absence of published guidance functions as de facto operating permission, which is structurally permanent until formal guidance is published

### Civil Procedure — AI Disclosure

**Geneva Code of Civil Procedure (CPC):**
- Art. 162/171: Disclosure obligations; AI inference chains from privileged documents potentially constituting disclosure under different provision than the documents themselves
- Art. 229 CPP: Algorithmic evidence in criminal proceedings — creates Geneva law firm exposure for AI-assisted case preparation with no response protocol
- No Geneva court practice directive on AI-assisted filing certification under revised CCP Art. 142
- No established procedural status for opposing-party AI-related discovery requests

### Federal AI Coordination Office

- Contact point established February 1, 2026 within Federal Chancellery
- No published Geneva-specific protocol for professional services firms
- No guidance on how small law firms should interface with the contact point

### PI Insurance

- No Swiss PI insurer has published AI error classification methodology
- No underwriting standard for AI-assisted legal services exists in Geneva market
- Coverage architecture undefined in published policy language
- Carrier attestation requirements have been elevated without corresponding coverage definition

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Six net-new insights for C268 Phase 01, distinct from all C214–C267 Phase 01 findings:**

**Finding 01: Professional secrecy / AI vendor terms structural conflict as unnamed primary liability.**

The conflict between Art. 321 CP professional secrecy and AI vendor terms of service is not a compliance question with a published answer — it is an unresolved structural conflict operating simultaneously across all active matters at every Geneva small law firm using AI. No Geneva attorney has obtained legal confirmation that vendor data use provisions are compatible with professional secrecy obligations. No Geneva service has produced a compatibility analysis for any major AI platform. This conflict is not hypothetical: vendor terms consistently include model improvement provisions, professional secrecy requires client information not be accessible to third parties. The conflict is real, present-tense, and undisclosed. [Distinct from all prior Phase 01 findings — C267 Finding 01 addressed inference chain privilege exposure, not vendor terms incompatibility with professional secrecy as structural conflict.]

**Finding 02: International arbitration AI use as ungoverned highest-stakes accountability gap.**

Geneva is an ICC and WIPO seat. AI use in arbitral proceedings has no disclosed framework — not from arbitral institutions, not from the Geneva bar, not from any Geneva advisory service. Counsel using AI in proceedings where outcome consequences are significant, with no protocol for what must be disclosed, what constitutes privileged AI work product, and what happens to AI system outputs after proceedings close, is operating in an accountability gap no Geneva service has mapped. This gap has a specific structural characteristic: the stakes are highest in arbitration, the governance infrastructure is absent everywhere, and the exposure compounds with each proceeding. [Distinct from all prior Phase 01 findings — C262 Finding 03 addressed Geneva arbitration AI disclosure asymmetry as question, this finding names it as ungoverned operational gap requiring discrete protocol framework.]

**Finding 03: Engagement letter AI file access authorization as undisclosed present-tense liability across all active matters.**

Pre-AI engagement letters contain no authorization for client files to enter AI vendor infrastructure. The authorization question — what happens to the file inside the system, who can access it, whether data trains models, what happens to the file if the vendor changes terms or is acquired — is not addressed in any active engagement letter at any Geneva small law firm. When clients begin asking these questions directly, firms have no prepared response that is both accurate and does not create more exposure than it resolves. This is not a documentation quality problem — it is a documentation absence problem with specific accumulation dynamics across all active matters. [Distinct from C267 Finding 06 engagement letter AI file access authorization which named the category — this finding isolates the specific authorization architecture gap and the client question arrival as distinct mechanism.]

**Finding 04: Geneva bar structural advisory void as permanent operating permission condition without feedback path.**

The absence of published ORDRE guidance on AI governance is not a transitional state pending forthcoming guidance — it is a structurally permanent condition. The absence functions as de facto operating permission because no formal prohibition exists, which prevents attorneys from knowing whether they are within acceptable practice bounds. The void has no built-in feedback path: attorneys who encounter problems do not report them to any body that would incorporate the information into guidance development, because no such body exists or functions. The advisory void is not pending resolution; it is the permanent operating condition until the bar chooses to publish formal guidance. [Distinct from all prior Phase 01 bar void findings which described the absence without isolating the permanent feedback-path-free structural quality.]

**Finding 05: nFADP cross-border AI processing obligations for Geneva attorneys advising on foreign law create multi-jurisdictional compliance impossibility with no published resolution pathway.**

A Geneva attorney advising on French law using a US-based AI platform creates simultaneous obligations under Art. 321 CP (Swiss professional secrecy), nFADP (Swiss data protection), French legal professional obligations, and US platform terms of service — with no published framework resolving which obligation hierarchy applies or how conflicts are managed. The cross-border AI processing chain means client file data flows through a US-based system for advice on French law, from a Geneva law firm, with Swiss professional obligations attaching regardless of where processing occurs. No Geneva authority has addressed this specific multi-jurisdictional configuration. This is distinct from cross-border data residency in prior cycles because it involves the intersection of professional secrecy obligation and foreign law advice using AI infrastructure with no resolution pathway published anywhere. [Distinct from all prior Phase 01 cross-border findings.]

**Finding 06: Geneva-specific small-firm AI governance documentation taxonomy absent from all published frameworks creating structural operational confusion at the exact firm size most exposed.**

All published AI governance frameworks — FINMA guidance, carrier questionnaires, bar association materials — are designed for institutional-scale operations. No published framework addresses the specific documentation taxonomy a two-to-five attorney Geneva firm actually needs: which documents must exist, what they must contain, who must sign what, what the retention period is, what the review cadence is, and what the audit trail looks like at that operating scale. The documentation requirements exist in abstract form but not in operationalized small-firm form. This is not a translation problem — it is a structural gap where the documentation requirement exists at institutional scale but has no equivalent at the scale where Geneva attorneys actually operate. The effect is operational confusion: attorneys know they are supposed to govern AI use but have no framework telling them what "governed" means at their scale. [Distinct from all prior Phase 01 documentation findings which identified gaps in specific document categories without naming the structural absence of a small-firm-calibrated documentation taxonomy.]
