## 02 — Market Signals

Current problems, failure modes, paradoxes, and complaints in the market.

**AI vendor corporate restructuring as undisclosed present-tense client data event.**
When AI vendors undergo acquisition or restructuring, data processing terms change without attorney awareness. Non-Swiss vendors specifically have completed acquisitions in 2024–2025 where data handling terms shifted silently. Attorneys using those vendors have client data under terms that are no longer the terms they accepted. The event is not notified. The attorney cannot reconstruct what changed. The exposure compounds with each active matter using the vendor. No Geneva market service has identified this as a discrete present-tense risk category.

**AI model training data provenance as implied third-party processing authorization question.**
When an attorney uses AI for research, the model's training data includes documents from multiple jurisdictions and sources. The attorney is indirectly processing through the AI whatever was in that training data. This implies a processing relationship with entities whose data contributed to the model's outputs. The attorney has no visibility into this processing chain. Whether this constitutes a required third-party processing authorization under nFADP Art.9 has not been addressed by any Geneva authority. The structural problem is that the attorney is not aware this question exists.

**Practice management AI adoption operating below governance decision threshold.**
Small Geneva firms are receiving AI feature updates through routine practice management software updates — no decision to adopt, no governance review, no authorization documentation. Client data begins flowing through AI infrastructure before any attorney in the firm has made a conscious choice. The governance failure is architectural: the system is designed to add AI features by default, and the firm has no process for catching this before it happens.

**Confabulation as authenticity problem distinct from verification protocol design.**
The problem is not that attorneys lack a verification protocol. The problem is structural: the AI generates authoritative-sounding citations, the attorney lacks a reliable method to distinguish real from fabricated authority before filing, and filing AI-fabricated citations creates a distinct professional conduct problem that has no clear remediation procedure under Geneva civil procedure. The failure mode is not procedural — it is authentic: the attorney believes they have cited real authority.

**Attorney residence as AI governance documentation jurisdiction ambiguity.**
AI governance documentation at a small firm is often physically maintained on devices that travel between office and home. Attorney oath requires the oath-taker to be reachable under Art.551 CO. If governance documentation — carrier attestations, AI system descriptions, client data processing records — is maintained on a device that is legally at the attorney's residence, the question of which jurisdiction's rules govern that documentation is not defined. No Geneva bar guidance addresses this specific configuration.

**Carrier attestation interpretation running ahead of published doctrine and bar guidance simultaneously.**
Attorneys are interpreting carrier attestation requirements without formal bar endorsement of what adequate means. The carrier's third-generation questionnaire is more operationally specific than any published SVA or ORDRE guidance. Attorneys are functionally calibrating to commercial underwriting criteria as their primary operative governance standard — without any professional body confirming this calibration is appropriate. The gap between what carriers require and what bars publish is widening in real time.

**Client counterparty AI governance requirements arriving through commercial relationships before regulatory framework is operational.**
Sophisticated clients are requesting AI governance documentation — model version logs, data residency confirmation, explanation protocols — as commercial terms. This is happening before any Swiss regulatory framework is formally operational for these specific obligations. Attorneys are fielding these requests without knowing whether they constitute binding obligations or informal requests. The commercial requirement is already operative; the regulatory clarification is not.

## 04 — Compliance Context

Geneva/Swiss specific regulatory obligations.

**SVA/ORDRE — Professional Obligations**
The Swiss Lawyers Federation (SVA) and Geneva Bar (ORDRE) have published introductory AI guidance. The guidance does not provide:
- A small-firm-calibrated documentation architecture for AI-assisted client work
- An operational definition of adequate AI governance for a two-to-five attorney firm
- A safe harbor or minimum standard against which a small firm can measure itself

The bar has not issued an advisory opinion on any specific AI governance question. Informal guidance circulates through peer networks but carries no safe harbor. The structural advisory void persists: attorneys making decisions with personal liability consequences are operating without a published reference point.

**nFADP — Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection**
Art.9 requires a lawful basis for processing personal data. Client data processed through AI tools requires authorization documentation. For non-Swiss AI processors, the authorization question compounds: the attorney does not control the processor's data handling, the processor's corporate structure may change without notification, and the attorney cannot audit the processor's infrastructure.

Art.12 grants data subjects (including clients) rights over their personal data, including access rights. AI-generated content about a client may constitute the client's personal data. Data subject access requests arriving for AI-generated analytical content about a client create a structural collision with attorney work product protection: complying discloses potentially privileged material, not complying creates nFADP liability. No Geneva protocol exists for resolving this collision.

Art.21 requires meaningful information about automated decision-making. Client-facing AI outputs — AI-assisted research summaries, AI-generated case assessments — may constitute automated decisions requiring explanation. The obligation is present-tense, operational, and present across all AI-assisted client matters.

**FDPIC — Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner**
The FDPIC has not issued Geneva-specific guidance on AI governance for small law firms. Enforcement activity has not produced published precedent specifically addressing attorney AI use. The FDPIC's pattern of guidance-through-enforcement means attorneys cannot anticipate what standard will be applied until an enforcement action is taken.

**Art.321 CP — Professional Secrecy**
Attorneys are prohibited from disclosing client information. AI research query trails retained by non-Swiss providers may be discoverable through foreign legal process. The scope of this risk is undefined: no Geneva court has ruled on whether attorney research query metadata falls within Art.321 CP protection, and no FDPIC guidance addresses this intersection. The question is not whether the data should be protected but whether it can be protected once it has left Swiss infrastructure.

**Carrier Requirements**
Third-generation carrier questionnaires probe AI governance with five evidence categories: policy documentation, system architecture descriptions, review protocols, client communication records, and training evidence. These categories constitute the most operationally specific AI governance standard in the Geneva market. They are imposed through commercial insurance — not through regulatory process — and require personal signature. The operative standard is set through commercial underwriting.

**Federal Council AI Convention**
The Federal Council's end-2026 legislative draft may introduce retrospective documentation requirements for AI deployments during 2023–2026. Early-adopting firms face potentially retroactive obligations for which no reconstruction methodology exists. The legislation is still pending; the retrospective exposure is already operative.
