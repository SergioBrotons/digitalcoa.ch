## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: International arbitration AI procedural requirements converging independently of Geneva bar guidance**
Arbitral institutions seated in Geneva — ICC, WIPO, LCIA, and ad hoc tribunals — are developing AI procedural requirements for participating counsel independently of the SVA/ORDRE guidance. Counsel practicing before these tribunals face dual-framework compliance obligations with no Geneva-based advisory addressing the intersection. The signal is distinct from domestic bar guidance developments and is arriving through tribunal administrative communications and arbitration rules updates.

**Signal 2: AI citation fabrication in legal databases as compounding integrity failure entering second generation**
Hallucinated case citations generated by AI legal research tools are not random isolated errors. The fabricated citations are being absorbed into subsequent AI training data when attorneys share research files, into citation databases through improper reporting, and into filed briefs under attorneys' signatures. The contamination is entering a second generation where AI tools trained on AI-generated legal content produce increasingly confident fabrications that are structurally more difficult to detect because they exhibit the stylistic markers of legitimate Swiss or international authority. No Geneva bar communication has addressed this as a systemic integrity problem.

**Signal 3: Carrier attestation as adequacy measurement instrument creating false conformance without external reference**
The third-generation carrier AI attestation is functioning as an adequacy measurement instrument without defining adequacy externally. Firms completing the attestation are measuring themselves against themselves — prior attestation responses or internal inventory documents. The pattern produces documented governance without evidentially defensible governance. The market has not named this specific failure mode: the form creates the impression of adequacy while the question of actual adequacy remains unaddressed.

**Signal 4: Attorney AI expertise becoming discoverable liability under cross-examination**
Attorneys with demonstrable AI expertise — those who have adopted AI tools most thoroughly and can describe their usage most specifically — are discovering that their expertise is becoming a cross-examination surface in litigation. Opposing counsel is constructing arguments from the attorney's own description of AI tool behavior to challenge the attorney's testimony about non-AI matters. The mechanism is not well understood at small firm scale but the pattern is observable: the most AI-forward small firms are facing the most technically specific discovery requests.

**Signal 5: Geneva civil law notarial functions intersecting with AI document generation without published protocol**
Geneva attorneys exercising notarial functions under Swiss civil law are encountering AI document generation in contexts where the notarial electronic seal is applied to AI-assisted instruments. The certification function under Swiss civil procedure predates AI document generation and the question of what the notarial seal actually certifies when the document was co-created with AI is unresolved in published Geneva practice. The problem is distinct from filing authentication and affects the notarial instrument category specifically.

**Signal 6: SVA AI working group producing discussion documents without resolution creating obligation without guidance**
The SVA's AI working groups have produced discussion documents, guidance notes, and consultation papers that have not been formalized as binding professional guidance. The distinction matters: informal SVA communications create professional obligation awareness without the safe harbor of published rule. Attorneys who have read the informal materials and not acted face the same exposure as those who have not, while attorneys who rely on informal materials for their practice standard have no protection if those materials are later found incomplete. The working group output is creating obligation through circulation while providing neither protection nor resolution.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: International arbitration AI procedural compliance for Geneva counsel**
No Geneva-based advisory practice has positioned to address the specific problem of international arbitral tribunal AI procedural requirements for Geneva-admitted counsel. The gap requires knowledge of both Geneva bar obligations and specific arbitral institution requirements — a combination no current market position occupies. The advisory entry point is the tribunal notification that counsel must disclose AI tool usage, methodology, or output in specific proceedings, which is arriving now for 2026-2027 proceedings.

**Gap 2: AI citation integrity audit as discrete service for law firm practice**
No Geneva market participant offers a discrete service for AI citation integrity auditing — a structured review of filed briefs, research files, and court submissions for AI-fabricated citations before they create liability. The service requires access to legal database comparison tools, a methodology for Swiss and international citation verification, and a protocol for correction before filing or after discovery. The absence is measurable: attorneys are discovering hallucinated citations in filed documents without a service to detect them proactively.

**Gap 3: Cross-border bar AI guidance divergence response for multi-jurisdiction practitioners**
Geneva counsel practicing before French, Italian, or other EU bars alongside their Geneva admission face AI guidance that is diverging across jurisdictions. French bar guidance, Italian professional standards, and Geneva SVA guidance are not harmonized on AI disclosure, documentation, or client communication. Practitioners navigating multiple jurisdictions have no multi-jurisdiction advisory tool for identifying where the standards diverge and what simultaneous compliance looks like.

**Gap 4: Notarial AI instrument certification protocol as discrete deliverable**
No protocol exists for Geneva attorneys exercising notarial functions to apply electronic certification to AI-assisted instruments in a way that is defensible under current Swiss civil procedure. The protocol would address what the seal certifies, what disclosure is required, and what records must be maintained. The gap is distinct from general AI disclosure requirements and affects a specific and high-risk category of legal instrument.

**Gap 5: CE credit AI training adequacy verification as distinct from attendance compliance**
The market has not distinguished between CE credit AI training attendance compliance (satisfying the bar's CE credit requirement) and AI training adequacy verification (ensuring the training actually produces competent AI tool usage). The two are structurally different. Sophisticated firms are beginning to recognize that CE-compliant AI training does not equal substantively adequate AI governance, but no market service addresses the gap between attendance compliance and practice adequacy.

**Gap 6: Safe confidential peer forum for Geneva attorneys on AI governance questions**
The Geneva legal community has no safe, confidential forum for attorneys to discuss specific AI governance questions without creating liability exposure through the discussion itself. The informal peer conversations that would normally perform this function are chilled by the recognition that any attorney admitting to specific AI usage problems in a peer conversation is simultaneously creating a discoverable record of their exposure. The market failure is structural, not cultural, and no institution has provided a protected format for this exchange.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**SVA/ORDRE — Geneva bar AI practice guidance and working group output:**
The SVA's AI working groups have generated discussion documents and guidance notes that have not been formalized as binding professional guidance. The informal status creates a specific compliance situation: attorneys who have read and relied on informal materials have no safe harbor if those materials are later found incomplete, while attorneys who have not acted on informal materials face equivalent exposure. The distinction between SVA discussion documents and binding professional guidance has not been communicated clearly to the profession.

**nFADP Articles 9 and 21 — third-party AI processing authorization and automated decision explanation in legal work:**
Art. 9 requires authorization for third-party processors handling client matter data. Geneva attorneys using AI tools are transmitting privileged client matter data to non-Swiss AI processors under terms that have not been evaluated against Art. 9 authorization requirements. Art. 21 requires explanations of automated decisions in intelligible form. AI-assisted legal research and document generation have not been systematically analyzed under Art. 21 in Geneva published practice. The structural collision between professional secrecy obligations and nFADP processing authorization requirements remains unresolved.

**FDPIC — enforcement pattern intelligence methodology:**
FDPIC has 26 active inquiries and updated cookie guidance in March 2026. The enforcement pattern is being read by sophisticated practitioners as an intelligence methodology — not as regulatory guidance but as a signal of institutional priorities. The methodology for reading FDPIC operational behavior and translating it into practice-level documentation decisions is not published in any Geneva advisory context. Attorneys using AI tools in client matters are making nFADP compliance decisions without this intelligence input.

**Geneva civil procedure — electronic seal certification and AI-assisted instrument production:**
Geneva civil procedure requires electronic certification for filed instruments. The certification function was designed for instruments authored by attorneys, not instruments co-created with AI. The question of what the electronic seal certifies when the instrument was generated or substantially assisted by AI is unresolved in Geneva civil procedure. No published court guidance, SVA guidance, or cantonal directive addresses this specifically.

**Geneva cantonal court AI procedural expectations — judicial formation variation:**
Geneva court AI procedural expectations are forming unevenly across judicial formations without published standardization. The variation means that identical firm AI practices may satisfy different procedural requirements depending on judicial assignment. The variation creates a planning constraint: firms cannot establish a single adequate AI disclosure or documentation standard because the standard varies by judge. No mapping of the variation has been published.

**International arbitral tribunal AI procedural requirements — ICC, WIPO, and ad hoc tribunals:**
Arbitral institutions seated in Geneva are issuing AI procedural requirements for participating counsel independently of domestic bar guidance. ICC, WIPO, and other tribunal administrative communications are requiring disclosure of AI tool usage, methodology, and output in specific proceedings. The requirements create dual-framework compliance obligations for Geneva counsel practicing before these tribunals, with no Geneva-based advisory addressing the intersection of domestic bar guidance and international tribunal requirements.

**Federal Council AI Convention — end-2026 legislative timeline and retrospective documentation:**
The Federal Council legislative timeline creates retrospective documentation requirements for AI configurations from 2023 through 2025. The documentation window is present-tense and closing. The requirements will arrive before most Geneva small firms have addressed the retrospective documentation gap. The retrospective problem is distinct from present-tense governance: documentation adequate for current practice may not reconstruct what was done in the earlier period, and the earlier period is the one most exposed to retrospective scrutiny.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: International arbitral tribunal AI procedural requirements arriving independently of Geneva bar guidance creating dual-framework compliance obligation**
Arbitral institutions seated in Geneva — ICC, WIPO, and ad hoc tribunals — are issuing AI procedural requirements for participating counsel that are not coordinated with SVA/ORDRE guidance. Geneva counsel practicing before these tribunals face dual-framework compliance: domestic bar expectations and tribunal-specific requirements applied simultaneously, with no Geneva-based advisory addressing the intersection. The problem is distinct from domestic AI disclosure requirements and affects the specific population of Geneva attorneys engaged in international arbitration.

**Finding 2: AI citation fabrication contamination entering second generation through training data absorption**
Hallucinated case citations generated by AI legal research tools are not isolated errors. They are being absorbed into AI training data through shared research files, propagating into subsequent AI tool outputs with increasingly confident and stylistically accurate fabrications of Swiss and international authority. The compounding contamination mechanism has not been named in any Geneva bar communication or published advisory. The practical consequence — filed briefs containing fabricated authority that attorneys cannot explain — is an active liability surface for every Geneva firm using AI legal research.

**Finding 3: Carrier attestation as adequacy measurement instrument measuring internal consistency without external reference standard**
The third-generation carrier AI attestation is functioning as an adequacy measurement instrument for Geneva small firms while defining adequacy only internally — against the firm's own prior documentation. The form produces measured governance, not adequate governance. The distinction matters: firms completing the attestation believe they have established adequacy because they have documented their position, while the question of whether that position would be found adequate by any external standard — carrier examiner, court, or bar inquiry — remains unaddressed. The specific failure mode is the form creating the impression of adequacy where none has been established.

**Finding 4: Attorney AI expertise discoverability as inverse liability exposure — most knowledgeable attorneys facing most technically specific discovery**
Attorneys with the most thorough AI adoption and the most specific knowledge of their AI tools are discovering that this expertise creates cross-examination surface in litigation. Opposing counsel is constructing challenges to attorney testimony from the attorney's own descriptions of AI tool behavior, treating demonstrated AI knowledge as evidence of AI dependency. The mechanism produces an inverse proportionality at small firm scale: the most AI-forward firms face the most technically specific discovery requests, while firms with less developed AI usage face less targeted scrutiny. The market has not named this specific dynamic.

**Finding 5: Notarial electronic seal certification for AI-assisted instruments as unresolved Swiss civil procedure question affecting a specific high-risk instrument category**
Geneva attorneys exercising notarial functions under Swiss civil law apply electronic certification to filed instruments. The certification function predates AI document generation and was designed for attorney-authored instruments. When the instrument was co-created with AI, the question of what the electronic seal actually certifies — the human authorship, the AI assistance, or neither — is unresolved in published Geneva practice. The problem is distinct from general AI disclosure requirements and affects a specific instrument category where the certification function has direct evidentiary weight. No published court guidance, SVA guidance, or cantonal directive addresses this specifically.

**Finding 6: SVA working group output creating obligation without safe harbor — informal guidance circulation distributing professional responsibility without resolution**
The SVA's AI working groups have produced discussion documents, guidance notes, and consultation papers that circulate within the profession without formal adoption as binding professional guidance. The informal status creates a specific legal situation: attorneys who have read and relied on informal materials have no protection if those materials are later found incomplete, while attorneys who have not acted on informal materials face equivalent exposure. The working group output is functionally distributing professional obligation awareness while providing neither safe harbor nor resolution. No Geneva bar communication has clarified the distinction between discussion document and binding guidance, leaving attorneys who attempt to follow informal materials in a structurally unprotected position.
