## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### How the ICP Actually Describes Their AI Problems — Real Language

The managing partner or solo practitioner at a 2–5 attorney Geneva firm has crossed a threshold. They are not asking whether to use AI — they are asking what their AI footprint means for the practice they have built, the clients they serves, and the professional identity they are maintaining under conditions that have not been defined by any authority.

The language in C223 reflects a new internal state: **AI exception patterns** and the gap between documented governance and operational reality in a small firm context. The firm has been living with AI long enough to have developed informal exception patterns — situations where AI use deviates from documented governance, without those exceptions being formally acknowledged or reviewed.

**Core concerns in their own register:**

- "I know that on certain types of matters, I use AI differently than on others. The documented policy does not capture this. I have a mental model of when I apply more scrutiny and when I apply less — and that model is not written down anywhere." — AI exception pattern recognition. The attorney has internalized a variable scrutiny system for AI use — routine matters get more AI, sensitive matters get less — but the system is not documented, not reviewed, and not communicated to associates. The gap between the written policy and the operational pattern is where the exposure sits.

- "I have noticed that when I am under time pressure, I use AI on things I would not use it on if I had more time. That is a pattern. I am aware of it. I do not know what to do about it." — Time-pressure exception drift. The attorney has identified a specific behavioral pattern: AI use expands under time pressure, contracts when time is available. This is not a governance violation in the formal sense — the decisions are made case by case — but it constitutes an undocumented exception to any governance framework that does not account for the time-pressure variable. The firm has no mechanism for reviewing this pattern.

- "I am not sure what my clients actually know about how I use AI. I have been vague with some of them. That vagueness is starting to feel like a problem, not a feature." — Vague disclosure as accumulating professional risk. The attorney who described AI use to clients in general terms — "we use technology to support our work" — is now aware that the vagueness creates a disclosure gap. The client who later discovers the extent of AI use may have a grievance about incomplete disclosure that is distinct from any AI error. The vagueness was protective early; it is now a liability as client sophistication about AI increases.

- "When a client asks me specifically about AI, I feel like I need to say the right thing to protect the relationship without committing to something I cannot deliver. I am essentially managing two narratives — one for clients who want AI, one for clients who are nervous about it." — Client-specific AI narrative management. The attorney is maintaining different representations of AI use to different clients, calibrated to client expectations rather than to a consistent professional standard. The practice of calibrating AI descriptions to client preferences is itself an exposure — it means the firm's AI use description is not anchored to a fixed standard.

- "My AI tool added a new capability three months ago. I am not sure if that changes anything about how I should be using it. I have been using the new capability on client matters. I have not reviewed whether that changes my governance position." — Post-adoption capability governance gap. The AI tool has been updated with new capabilities that expand what it can do with client data — but the firm's governance review is event-driven (initial adoption) rather than capability-driven. The new capability is in production use without a corresponding governance update. This is a structural gap in any governance framework that does not have a capability-change trigger.

- "I have been thinking about what happens to my AI governance when I leave the practice. The person who inherits the practice inherits whatever AI footprint is there — and they may not know what it includes or what the exposure is." — Professional succession AI governance untransferability. The managing partner who built an AI-enabled practice has a governance structure that lives in their knowledge and informal practices, not in a transferable form. The succession scenario — retirement, departure, partnership transition — is creating a specific anxiety: the AI governance debt is not visible as an asset or liability in any standard practice valuation framework.

## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

**Signal 1 — AI Exception Patterns Are Operational Reality That Governance Documents Do Not Capture**
Small Geneva firms have developed informal variable-scrutiny systems for AI use — more AI on routine matters, less on sensitive ones, more under time pressure. These patterns are not documented. The written governance policy does not reflect the operational reality of how AI is actually applied across different situations. This creates a specific exposure: the firm is operating under an undocumented exception regime that has never been reviewed, approved, or acknowledged. Any audit, client review, bar inquiry, or insurer assessment that looks at actual practice will find deviation from the documented policy.

**Signal 2 — Time-Pressure Is a Governance Variable That No Framework Addresses**
The time-pressure exception drift — AI use expanding under time pressure — is a behavioral pattern that is recognized by attorneys but not addressed by any governance framework. The problem is not that attorneys make bad decisions under pressure; the problem is that the governance policy does not have a time-pressure variable, which means the policy is not describing actual practice and cannot be used to evaluate actual practice. The gap between the governance document and the operational reality under time pressure is where the exposure lives.

**Signal 3 — Client-Specific AI Narrative Management Is Creating Inconsistent Disclosure Records**
The attorney maintaining different AI descriptions for different clients — calibrated to client preferences rather than to a consistent professional standard — has created an exposure that is invisible to them. Each client interaction adds to a disclosure record that is internally inconsistent. If a client later challenges the attorney's AI use, the inconsistent descriptions become evidence of incomplete or misleading disclosure. The protection that vague disclosure provided early is now a liability as client AI sophistication increases.

**Signal 4 — Post-Adoption Capability Governance Gaps Are Structurally Unaddressed**
AI tools add capabilities between reviews. The firm's governance review is event-driven (adoption, bar guidance change, incident) rather than capability-driven. When a vendor adds a new capability — expanded data processing, new document analysis functions, new integration points — the governance framework does not have a trigger to initiate a review. The new capability enters production use without a corresponding governance assessment. This structural gap means that governance documentation can be accurate at the time of writing and materially incomplete by the next review cycle.

**Signal 5 — Professional Succession Is Revealing AI Governance as an Untransferable Asset**
The AI governance structure in a small firm lives in the managing partner's knowledge and informal practices — not in a transferable or auditable form. When the succession scenario arises — retirement, departure, partnership transition — the AI governance debt is not visible in any standard practice valuation. The successor inherits an AI footprint with undocumented exceptions, inconsistent client disclosures, and governance gaps that are not captured in any formal record. This is a new category of practice risk: AI governance succession liability.

**Signal 6 — Client AI Expectation Calibration Is Now Bidirectional**
Clients who initially demanded AI use are beginning to encounter the quality control consequences — AI-assisted work product that requires more review, errors that are caught late, delivery quality that varies. Some clients are expressing AI regret — wanting more human involvement, more transparency, more traditional legal product. This creates a new problem for attorneys who standardized AI-assisted workflows around the initial client expectation: the workflow is now misaligned with a client population that is beginning to want something different.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations for Small Law Firms

1. **nFADP Art. 6 lawful basis and capability expansion** — When an AI vendor adds a new capability that changes what data is processed, the nFADP Art. 6 lawful basis may need to be updated. The firm's governance framework does not have a capability-change trigger; the lawful basis documentation may be inaccurate relative to current vendor capability.

2. **SVA/ORDRE supervision obligations and exception pattern accountability** — The supervising attorney is responsible for the exception patterns in AI use that exist in their practice, even if those patterns are undocumented. The bar has not defined what constitutes sufficient supervision over variable-scrutiny AI use patterns, but the responsibility for those patterns sits with the supervising attorney.

3. **CPTG Art. 201 AI disclosure and client-specific narrative inconsistency** — The attorney who has provided inconsistent AI descriptions to different clients has a disclosure record that may not withstand scrutiny. If a matter goes to Geneva court and the attorney's AI disclosure to the client is found to be incomplete or inconsistent, the CPTG Art. 201 question becomes an adverse finding.

4. **Professional liability insurance and documented governance accuracy** — The documented governance policy represents a representation to the insurer. If the policy does not reflect actual operational exception patterns, the firm has made a misrepresentation about its governance practices. The exception pattern gap is an insurance coverage exposure.

5. **Client board-level AI governance review and exception pattern exposure** — Corporate clients conducting governance reviews are beginning to ask not just about policy but about how AI use varies across matter types and situations. The exception pattern question is beginning to appear in client governance reviews — a new escalation in what clients are asking for.

6. **Professional succession and AI governance debt transfer** — The AI governance debt in a small firm — undocumented exceptions, inconsistent disclosures, capability governance gaps — becomes a liability in any succession or partnership transition. There is no standard mechanism for quantifying or transferring AI governance obligations as part of a practice transition.

1. **AI exception patterns are operational reality that governance documents structurally do not capture** — C214-C222 focused on the absence of governance, the presence of governance without operational conformance, and the gap between documented policy and practice. C223 identifies a specific structural mechanism: the informal variable-scrutiny system — different AI use intensity across matter types, client categories, and time-pressure situations — is not captured in any governance framework because it was never documented. The exception patterns are not hidden; they are known to the attorney and not written down. This is distinct from the governance implementation failure in C222 (policy exists but practice does not conform) and the time-pressure variable is distinct from the verification cost paradox in C220.

2. **Time-pressure is a governance variable that creates a specific exception drift pattern no framework addresses** — C214-C222 identified multiple forms of supervision failure and verification problems. C223 identifies a specific behavioral mechanism: AI use intensity increases under time pressure in a way that is recognized by the attorney but not documented or reviewed. The governance document assumes a consistent application standard; actual practice applies a variable standard driven by time availability. The framework cannot evaluate what it does not describe. This is distinct from the verification cost paradox in C220 and the Tuesday morning scenario gap in C222.

3. **Client-specific AI narrative management has created an aggregate disclosure inconsistency exposure** — C214-C222 identified disclosure ambiguity as a single-interaction problem (what to say in one conversation). C223 identifies that the practice of calibrating AI descriptions to client preferences over time has created an aggregate disclosure record that is internally inconsistent. The attorney who described AI use differently to different clients has a disclosure record that does not anchor to a fixed standard — and that inconsistency is an independent exposure, distinct from any individual AI error. This is distinct from the client governance audit anxiety in C222 and the vague disclosure as accumulating risk signal in C221.

4. **Post-adoption capability governance gaps are structurally unaddressed by all existing governance frameworks** — C214-C222 identified vendor term changes as a detection problem (C222) and vendor contract gaps as a review problem (C218/C221). C223 identifies a specific structural gap: the governance review is event-driven rather than capability-driven, which means that when a vendor adds capability between reviews, the governance documentation may be materially incomplete without the firm knowing it. The new capability enters production use without a corresponding governance update. This is distinct from the vendor term change detection failure in C222 (terms changed, not capabilities added) and the capability-change governance gap is a new finding.

5. **Professional succession is revealing AI governance as an untransferable practice asset with no valuation mechanism** — C214-C222 identified attorney departure AI continuity as a knowledge loss problem (C217) and the succession question as an emerging concern (C218/C219). This is a market evolution finding distinct from the client AI expectation service gap in C217 and the client governance review escalation in C222.
