## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Geneva courts beginning to issue AI-specific procedural directions without established protocol**
Courts issuing directions requiring attorneys to confirm AI system usage in filings. The direction arrives before any established protocol exists for how attorneys should respond or what documentation they should maintain.

**Signal 2: Opposing counsel using AI to generate challenges against filed briefs**
Opposing counsel running AI analysis against filed briefs and generating challenges about reasoning gaps and unsupported citations. The attorney cannot evaluate whether the AI found a real problem or generated a plausible false positive.

**Signal 3: Bar communication providing principle-level guidance without operational content**
Bar issuing AI guidance that says attorneys should use AI responsibly without defining what responsible use means, what documentation is required, or what consequences attach to non-compliance.

**Signal 4: Attorney-client privilege and AI query metadata conflict unresolved**
No clear resolution exists for whether AI research queries maintain attorney-client privilege when they pass through vendor infrastructure. The technical possibility of vendor access is in tension with the legal protection of research trails.

**Signal 5: Clients beginning to ask about AI data segregation across their matter**
Clients asking whether their case information is processed through shared AI infrastructure alongside other clients' matters. No attorney has a prepared answer that is both accurate and does not create exposure.

**Signal 6: AI research citation stability problem for filed briefs**
AI systems version updates making research citations in filed briefs irreproducible. The attorney who cited AI research in a filed brief cannot reconstruct the analytical foundation when challenged.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Geneva court AI procedural response protocol**
No Geneva service has produced a response protocol for attorneys receiving AI-specific procedural directions from courts. The protocol would need to address what to confirm, what to decline, and what documentation to provide.

**Gap 2: AI-generated brief challenge evaluation framework**
No framework exists for evaluating whether an AI-generated challenge to filed briefs identifies a real substantive problem or a plausible false positive. The evaluation framework would need to be usable in real time.

**Gap 3: AI query metadata privilege status definitive assessment**
No Geneva attorney has obtained a definitive assessment of whether AI research query metadata falls within attorney-client privilege. The assessment requires understanding both the legal standard and the technical architecture.

**Gap 4: Client AI data segregation disclosure protocol**
No protocol exists for disclosing AI infrastructure configuration to clients in a way that is accurate, complete, and does not create more exposure than it resolves.

**Gap 5: AI research citation audit trail for filed briefs**
No service exists for reconstructing the AI research trail that produced citations in filed briefs. The audit trail would need to address version updates, model changes, and system configurations at the time of research.

**Gap 6: Bar AI practice standard formal interpretation request mechanism**
No mechanism exists for small firms to submit specific AI practice questions to the Geneva bar for formal interpretation. The guidance vacuum persists because there is no mechanism to trigger formal responses.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Article 321 CP and AI query metadata:**
The prohibition on unauthorized disclosure of client information applies to communications with third parties. Whether AI vendor infrastructure access constitutes disclosure to a third party is unresolved in Geneva legal practice.

**Geneva civil procedure and AI-specific procedural directions:**
No Geneva court has published a standing rule for AI-specific procedural directions. Each direction is issued ad hoc without established protocol. Attorneys receive them without knowing what response is expected.

**SBA/ORDRE guidance gap:**
The bar's guidance is principle-level only. It does not address specific operational questions: what documentation constitutes responsible AI use, what disclosure obligations exist, what happens when AI research cannot be reproduced.

**nFADP Articles 9, 12, and 21:**
AI-assisted legal work activates obligations for automated decision documentation, data subject rights, and AI system transparency. The simultaneous activation is not addressed in any Geneva bar guidance.

**FINMA and AI procedural expectations for regulated professions:**
Attorneys practicing in FINMA-regulated areas face additional procedural expectations that have not been defined for AI-assisted work. The gap between general bar guidance and sector-specific expectations creates compliance uncertainty.

**Federal Supreme Court and AI research tools:**
No Federal Supreme Court ruling addresses the status of AI research tool outputs as legal research authority. The absence means attorneys cannot cite AI research with confidence in appellate proceedings.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Court AI procedural direction as pre-established obligation with undefined discharge standard**
Geneva courts are issuing AI-specific procedural directions without published standards for what attorneys must provide. The direction asks questions the attorney cannot answer without undocumented infrastructure knowledge. The obligation exists before the discharge pathway is defined. This is distinct from carrier attestation and bar guidance gaps — those are market-generated obligations; this is court-generated.

**Finding 2: AI-generated brief challenge asymmetry as adversarial litigation exposure**
Opposing counsel using AI to challenge filed briefs creates an asymmetry: the challenging attorney can generate plausible objections without proving they are substantive. The responding attorney cannot evaluate the AI output's reliability in real time. No Geneva framework addresses this asymmetric challenge condition.

**Finding 3: AI query metadata privilege status as unresolved structural legal exposure**
The conflict between AI vendor infrastructure access and attorney-client privilege protection is unresolved. The technical possibility of vendor access may constitute disclosure to a third party under Article 321 CP. No Geneva bar opinion, court ruling, or legal scholarship has addressed this specifically. The exposure is structural and named.

**Finding 4: Client AI data segregation question arriving before any attorney has a prepared accurate answer**
Clients are asking whether their case information is processed through shared AI infrastructure alongside other clients' matters. The question requires technical knowledge of vendor architecture that attorneys do not have and cannot obtain from vendor terms. The honest answer creates exposure. The prepared answer does not exist.

**Finding 5: Bar AI practice standard formal interpretation request mechanism absence as structural guidance vacuum**
The bar guidance vacuum persists because no mechanism exists for small firms to submit specific AI practice questions for formal interpretation. The absence means the guidance vacuum is permanent until the mechanism is created. No Geneva service has proposed creating the mechanism.

**Finding 6: AI research citation version stability as filed brief integrity problem with no reconstruction obligation on any platform**
AI systems version updates making research citations in filed briefs irreproducible. No Swiss-hosted platform has a reconstruction obligation. The attorney who cited AI research cannot reconstruct the analytical foundation when challenged by opposing counsel or court. The problem is named and has no discharge pathway.
