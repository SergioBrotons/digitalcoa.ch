## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems

**The AI research platform credibility problem is being misidentified as a vendor selection problem.** Managing partners who experienced confabulation problems are switching platforms, assuming the new platform will be more reliable. The structural problem — that no AI research platform currently offers a verifiably authentic output guarantee — is not addressed by vendor switching. The credibility of the platform and the authenticity of the output are different questions. The market is responding to confabulation with platform migration, which does not resolve the underlying verification problem.

**Client questions about AI training data are arriving as direct questions, not as abstract privacy concerns.** Sophisticated clients are asking attorneys whether their data was used to train the model that processed their matter. This question is arriving in client conversations before attorneys have developed an answer, before carrier policies have been updated, and before any Geneva bar communication has addressed it. The training data provenance question has a specific client relationship dynamic: a vague or evasive answer reads as a governance failure signal. Attorneys who cannot answer are experiencing this as a credibility exposure, not primarily a compliance exposure.

**The managing partner's personal signature on carrier documentation is creating personal exposure that is not visible as such at the moment of signing.** The attestation language is general. The personal signature is specific. The gap between what the language commits the firm to and what the firm's actual AI configuration looks like is not visible at signing. When the managing partner signs what appears to be a routine insurance renewal form, they are creating a documented representation about AI governance adequacy that may not match reality and for which they bear personal responsibility.

### Failure Modes

**Firms are accumulating AI tool usage across attorneys without firm-level visibility into what any individual attorney is doing with client data.** When two or more attorneys in a small firm use different AI tools independently, the managing partner cannot describe the aggregate AI exposure the firm is carrying. The firm-level exposure is greater than what any individual attorney's usage would suggest, and there is no mechanism at small-firm scale for aggregating this visibility. The exposure is invisible from inside the firm because no one has built the aggregation tool.

**Bar CE credentialing is functioning as a false adequacy signal for AI competence.** The ORDRE and SVA measure attendance at AI-related events. The credentialing mechanism cannot measure practice adequacy because it was not designed to. An attorney with the maximum CE credits for AI could have attended introductory presentations without implementing any governance system. The most credentialed attorney may be the most exposed, because the credential signals preparedness while the practice signals none. No mechanism exists for the bar to distinguish between these cases.

**Matter closure AI state reconstruction is not being performed as a professional obligation, and the obligation itself is undefined.** When a matter closes, the attorney cannot reconstruct what AI state existed at closing — which tool version, which model, which configuration, which outputs were generated in the final phase. General file retention obligations do not address AI state reconstruction. No Geneva bar communication has defined what the matter-closure AI documentation obligation consists of. The failure mode is not that attorneys are doing it badly; it is that they do not know they are supposed to.

### Paradoxes

**The market is treating the confabulation problem as a platform quality problem when it is a structural property of generative AI.** Platform improvements reduce the frequency of confabulation. They do not eliminate it. The structural property of generative AI is that outputs can be generated that appear authoritative but do not correspond to any actual source. The market is waiting for a platform credible enough to eliminate the problem. No such platform exists. The paradox is that the expectation being held is not achievable by any current technology, and the market has not yet articulated this.

**The bar's CE requirement for AI competence is creating a credential that signals the opposite of what competence would require.** Competence in AI governance would require demonstrated practice adequacy — not attendance. The CE mechanism measures attendance, which is inversely correlated with practice adequacy at small-firm scale. The credential being generated is not a measure of competence. The paradox is that the bar's own credentialing system is functioning as a false adequacy signal that may make clients and counterparties more confident in firm AI governance than the actual practice warrants.

**AI efficiency gains in research are being absorbed by clients while the verification cost is being borne by the firm.** The efficiency dividend from AI research is captured by clients in the form of faster turnaround and lower cost. The verification cost required to ensure research output authenticity is borne by the firm and is not billable. The economic structure of AI research means the firm invests in verification to protect itself, while clients benefit from the efficiency the AI provides before verification is complete. The asymmetry is structural and not visible as a line item in firm finances.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### What Others Say

The Geneva legal AI governance market is currently offering three types of responses to the accountability problem: carrier questionnaires (commercial underwriting instruments), bar CE events (attendance-based credentialing), and legal technology vendor marketing (product feature frameworks). None of these modalities addresses the specific structural exposure categories identified in this cycle.

**Carrier questionnaires** are the most operationally concrete instrument in the market. They ask specific questions with specific consequences. But they are not designed as professional governance instruments. They do not provide a methodology for substantively adequate review. They do not address the research query trail, the AI state at matter closure, or the training data provenance question. They measure representations made against no external adequacy standard.

**Bar CE events** satisfy the credentialing mechanism. They do not establish AI governance adequacy. The gap between attendance-based credentialing and practice adequacy is not addressed by any bar communication, and no mechanism for measuring the latter has been proposed.

**Legal technology vendor marketing** addresses platform features and efficiency. It does not address personal liability, the carrier signature exposure, the nFADP Art.13/21 collision with professional secrecy, or the matter-closure AI state reconstruction obligation. The vendor frame — AI as a feature — cannot address the accountability problem because the accountability problem is not a feature problem.

### Gaps

No Geneva service has developed this as a specific product. The training data provenance question is arriving in client conversations now, and no response methodology exists in the market.

**No Geneva market participant has developed a firm-level AI usage aggregation methodology for small-firm scale.** The problem of seeing aggregate AI exposure across multiple attorneys using multiple tools is not addressed by any current tool or advisory product. The managing partner who wants to describe what AI the firm is using, and what the combined exposure is, has no methodology for doing so. No Geneva service has developed a firm-level aggregation methodology calibrated to two-to-five attorney operations.

**No Geneva market participant has developed a matter-closure AI state documentation protocol.** The question of what AI state documentation is required at matter closure — distinct from general file retention — has not been addressed by any Geneva service. The obligation, if one exists, has not been defined. The documentation format has not been specified. No protocol exists for managing what the AI state looked like at the point the retainer ended.

**No Geneva market participant has developed a carrier attestation pre-signing review methodology.** The managing partner who signs a carrier attestation needs a methodology for understanding what the language commits them to before signing, and what the gap between the language and actual practice looks like. No Geneva service has developed a pre-signing review product specifically designed for carrier AI attestation language.

### What No One Is Addressing

**The AI research platform credibility gap as a structural property of generative AI, not a vendor quality variable.** The market is operating on the assumption that some platforms are more credible than others, and that switching to a more credible platform resolves the confabulation problem. The structural property of generative AI is that outputs can be confidently inaccurate. No platform eliminates this property. The gap between platform credibility and output authenticity is not being addressed as a structural property — it is being treated as a vendor selection problem, which means the market is addressing the wrong variable.

**The training data provenance question as a client relationship credibility problem with professional liability dimensions.** When a client asks whether their data was used to train the model, the attorney who cannot answer faces a specific client relationship consequence: vague answers read as governance failures. The consistency question — whether all attorneys are using AI in ways that are consistent with the representations the firm has made — has not been named as a discrete exposure. No Geneva service has developed a methodology for assessing and managing AI usage consistency at small-firm scale.

**The matter-closure AI state reconstruction as an undefined professional obligation without a defined discharge protocol.** The question of what the managing partner owes to future accountability moments — in a dispute, in a carrier examination, in a bar complaint — about the AI state at a past matter's closure is not addressed by any framework. General file retention obligations do not cover AI state reconstruction. No Geneva bar communication has defined this obligation. The absence of definition does not eliminate the exposure; it makes it invisible.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva / Swiss Specific Obligations

### SVA / ORDRE — AI Competence Credentialing vs. Practice Adequacy

The Geneva bar measures AI competence through attendance-based CE credits. The credentialing mechanism was not designed to and cannot measure practice adequacy. An attorney who has attended the maximum CE hours for AI topics has satisfied the credentialing requirement without demonstrating any governance implementation. The bar has not published a mechanism for distinguishing between attendance and competence, and no proposal for doing so has been advanced. The consequence is that the bar's AI competence signal is structurally disconnected from the practice adequacy it purports to measure.

### nFADP — Client Training Data Provenance Questions

The nFADP does not currently require disclosure of whether client data was used to train the model that processed their matter. However, clients are asking this question directly, and the FDPIC has not issued guidance on whether training data provenance disclosure is required under nFADP's transparency obligations. For a law firm, the question has a compound dimension: disclosing training data use may reveal information about the firm's AI tool selection and matter processing patterns that constitutes privileged analytical information. The training data provenance question intersects with Article 321 CP in ways that are not resolved by any published FDPIC guidance. The question is arriving in client conversations before any regulatory or bar framework has addressed it.

### nFADP — Art.13/21 and Art.321 CP — AI State Documentation at Matter Closure

When a matter closes, the client may have ongoing data subject rights under Articles 13 and 21 regarding AI-generated content about them. The AI state at closing — what the model version was, what inferences were generated, what outputs were created — may be needed to respond to future data subject requests. Article 321 CP protects attorney work product. The question of whether AI state reconstruction at matter closure is required to discharge ongoing data subject rights obligations — and whether that reconstruction is covered by the privilege framework — has not been addressed by any FDPIC guidance or bar communication. The intersection of matter-closure documentation and ongoing nFADP obligations is a present-tense exposure for any firm that has used AI to process client data.

### Swiss Civil Procedure — AI State Documentation in Filing Accuracy

The revised Swiss Code of Civil Procedure (January 2025) introduced updated provisions for electronic filing. Geneva courts have not issued practice directives on what AI state documentation is required for AI-assisted filings — specifically, whether the model version and configuration at the time of filing must be recorded and retained. The question is distinct from the general AI disclosure question: it concerns the evidential state of the filing infrastructure at the moment of submission, not what was disclosed to the opposing party. No Geneva court practice directive has addressed this.

### ORDRE / SBA — AI Complaint Outcome Non-Publication

The Geneva bar's AI complaint mechanism is processing AI-related complaints without publishing outcomes. The de facto operative standard is forming through complaint resolutions that attorneys who have not faced a complaint cannot evaluate. The consequence is that the most operationally relevant information about what constitutes actionable AI conduct under bar rules is not accessible to attorneys who need it to calibrate their practice. The non-publication creates an asymmetric information environment: the bar knows what it is treating as actionable; the attorney population does not.

### Carrier Documentation — Personal Signature Exposure on General Language

The carrier attestation language is not calibrated to the individual firm's AI configuration. The managing partner who signs is attesting to representations about AI governance adequacy that may not correspond to what the firm is actually doing. The personal exposure created by the signature is not proportional to the specificity of the language signed. No carrier has published a methodology for evaluating whether the attestation language maps onto the firm's actual AI practice before signing, and no Geneva service offers a pre-signing review product for this specific scenario.

### Finding 01 — The Platform Credibility / Output Authenticity Gap as a Structural Property of Generative AI Misidentified as a Vendor Quality Problem

Geneva small-firm attorneys experiencing AI research confabulation are switching platforms, treating the problem as one of vendor quality. The market assumption is that a more credible platform will resolve the confabulation problem. The structural property of generative AI is that outputs can be generated that appear authoritative but do not correspond to any retrievable source. Platform improvements reduce the frequency of confabulation; they do not eliminate the structural property. The gap between platform credibility — which can be evaluated — and output authenticity — which cannot be guaranteed by any current platform — is not being addressed as a structural property. Attorneys who switch platforms are addressing the wrong variable. No Geneva market participant has distinguished between platform credibility evaluation and output authenticity verification as separate, structurally different requirements. This is distinct from C263 Finding 04 (confabulation verification protocol design) and C263 Finding 06 (authenticity crisis professional responsibility dimension) because both addressed the problem within the platform selection frame; this finding identifies that the platform selection frame is the wrong frame.

When a matter closes, the AI state at closing may be needed to respond to future data subject requests under Articles 13 and 21, carrier renewal questions about historical AI usage, or bar complaints about a closed matter. General file retention obligations do not address AI state reconstruction. No Geneva bar communication has defined what the matter-closure AI documentation obligation consists of. No FDPIC guidance has addressed the intersection of matter-closure documentation and ongoing nFADP obligations for a law firm. The obligation, if one exists, is undefined. The documentation format has not been specified. No discharge protocol exists. The exposure is present tense for every firm with a history of AI-assisted matters: the AI state at closing for matters that have already closed is now structurally unreconstructable, and the magnitude of that exposure is not visible from inside the firm because no one has calculated it. This is distinct from C258 Finding 03 (matter closure AI system state documentation obligation) which named the category without isolating the specific unreconstructability of past matters or the nFADP ongoing obligation intersection.

### Finding 06 — The Bar CE Credentialing Function and the Practice Adequacy Function Are Being Served by the Same Mechanism That Satisfies Neither, Creating a False Adequacy Signal With Compound Consequences

The Geneva bar measures AI competence through attendance-based CE credits. The credentialing mechanism cannot measure practice adequacy because it was not designed to. An attorney with maximum AI CE credits has satisfied the credentialing requirement without demonstrating any governance implementation. The bar's own credentialing system is functioning as a false adequacy signal that may make clients, counterparties, and carriers more confident in a firm's AI governance than the actual practice warrants. The compound consequence is that the most CE-credentialed attorneys may be the most exposed: they have the credential that suggests preparedness and the practice that provides none. No mechanism exists for the bar to distinguish between attendance and competence, and no proposal for doing so has been advanced. This is distinct from C260 Finding 04 (bar structural advisory void as de facto operating permission) which addressed the absence of published rules as interpreted as absence of obligations; this finding addresses the specific mechanism of CE credentialing as a false adequacy signal with compound consequences for the attorneys who have accumulated the most credits.
