### English

**"I have no way to know what the AI did between when I asked and when I got an answer."** — The attorney uses AI for research and analysis but cannot reconstruct the processing path. If the answer later becomes material to a dispute, the attorney cannot explain what the AI did or why.

**"When a client asks me directly what the AI did, I have to decide between sounding vague or revealing work product."** — This is experienced as a genuine dilemma. **"I signed a carrier attestation that I think was general, but I don't know what it commits me to specifically."** — The attestation language is designed for general audiences. The attorney signed it under time pressure at renewal without a methodology for evaluating what was being attested. The uncertainty is tolerated as a cost of operating.

## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems

**The nFADP employment exception (Article 8(3)(c)) may resolve the privilege-consent collision, but Geneva firms are operating without knowing whether they are covered or exposed.** The employment relationship exception in nFADP could, if applicable to attorney work product processing, eliminate the nFADP authorization obligation for AI processing of client data in the course of legal representation. This would sidestep the privilege-consent collision that has been named in prior cycles. The exception's applicability to professional services firms has not been tested or published. Firms using non-Swiss AI processors without nFADP authorization may or may not be covered by this exception. No Geneva service has mapped this.

**Geneva's international arbitration seat position is creating AI data flow complications that domestic-focused guidance doesn't address.** Geneva attorneys practicing in ICC, WIPO, and ad hoc international arbitration handle client data that crosses borders through AI processing systems. The intersection of Article 321 CP, nFADP, and applicable arbitral tribunal AI requirements creates multi-layer cross-border obligations that no single guidance document addresses. The problem is specifically Geneva's, driven by the city's international arbitration concentration.

**PI insurance AI error coverage is architecturally undefined in published policy language, creating personal exposure without a known discharge path.** Insurers have not published how AI-assisted errors are classified under existing policies. The gap is not about whether coverage exists; it is about whether the policy language was designed to answer the question. Attorneys are signing carrier attestations about AI governance while carrying undisclosed personal exposure that their PI policy may or may not cover.

### Failure Modes

**Attorneys are discovering AI vendor indemnification clauses at incident moments, not at procurement.** AI vendor agreements contain liability allocation provisions that allocate risk to the firm in ways that conflict with professional conduct obligations. The discovery occurs when an incident surfaces the clause, not when the contract is signed. At that point, the exposure is already operational.

**The administrative burden of proposed Geneva AI governance documentation requirements exceeds what small-firm operations can practically sustain.** Regulatory proposals and carrier expectations are moving toward documentation requirements that assume institutional compliance infrastructure. Small firms cannot operationally sustain the proposed documentation burden without dedicated compliance staff. The gap between proposed requirement and operational capacity is not being addressed by any market participant.

**Geneva courts are developing local AI-related procedural precedent through case-by-case decisions that is not published or consolidated.** Different chambers are handling AI disclosure questions inconsistently. Attorneys handling cases in different chambers encounter different expectations without any published framework explaining the variation. The precedents exist in individual case decisions; no consolidation or publication mechanism makes them accessible.

### Paradoxes

**The bar's silence on AI governance is being interpreted by attorneys as permission to continue operating without structures.** The absence of published guidance is not neutral. Attorneys in small firms read the absence of published rules as the absence of operative obligations. The interpretation is incorrect but common. The consequence is that the bar's guidance vacuum is functioning as a de facto operating permission for inadequate AI governance.

**Client sophistication with AI is creating a reverse pressure: more sophisticated clients are driving faster AI adoption in small firms, which increases exposure before governance structures exist.** In most compliance contexts, sophisticated clients impose governance requirements. In Geneva small law, sophisticated clients are arriving with AI-equipped expectations that create competitive pressure to use AI more extensively before governance infrastructure is in place. The mechanism is different from counterparty questionnaire pressure.

**The carrier attestation creates documented personal liability without a corresponding discharge mechanism or coverage clarity.** The attorney signs a statement about AI governance adequacy. The signature creates personal liability. The content of what was attested to is not clearly understood, what would discharge the obligation is not defined, and whether the PI policy covers the exposure is not established. The attestation is a liability instrument with three unresolved structural questions.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva / Swiss Specific Obligations

### SVA / ORDRE

The Swiss Lawyers' Association and the Geneva Bar Association have communicated that AI competence is a professional obligation. The specific operational content of that obligation is not defined in any published document. Continuing education credits for AI-related seminars measure attendance, not practice adequacy.

The Geneva bar's informal guidance circuit operates through working groups and occasional publications. The informal circuit carries operationally relevant guidance without safe harbor protection. Published bar positions carry institutional protection without necessarily providing operational content.

Geneva has no published mechanism for attorneys to obtain written advisory guidance on specific AI governance questions before acting. This creates a structural advice void: attorneys making consequential decisions under uncertainty have no institutional path to obtain a written safe harbor. The decisions they make in this vacuum are not fed back into any guidance development process. The void is not a temporary gap; no process exists to develop the guidance that would fill it.

### nFADP

The Federal Act on Data Protection creates data controller obligations for law firms using AI systems that process client data. Several structural intersections are unresolved.

**Article 8(3)(c) — Employment relationship exception:** nFADP does not apply to data processing within the framework of employment relationships. The question of whether attorney-client legal representation qualifies as an employment relationship for nFADP purposes has not been tested or published. If the exception applies, the nFADP authorization obligations for AI processing of client data in legal representation may not apply. The question's resolution would affect every Geneva small firm currently using AI without nFADP authorization.

**Article 9 — Third-party processor authorization:** Non-Swiss AI providers serving Geneva law firms are third-party processors. The authorization basis for transmitting privileged client data to non-Swiss processors must be established. The interaction with Article 321 CP professional secrecy obligations compounds the complexity.

**Article 21 — Automated decision processing:** Clients have the right to request explanation of AI-assisted decisions affecting them. For legal practice, this intersects with work product confidentiality: providing sufficient explanation to satisfy Article 21 may require disclosing analytical methodology protected by professional secrecy.

**Small-business exemption applicability:** The nFADP threshold for small-organization exemptions has unclear application to professional services firms. A law firm processes client data as its primary activity, which may place it outside the exemption. The question has no published resolution and is compounding as firms accumulate AI-processed client data.

### FDPIC

The Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner has not published Geneva-specific guidance on the intersection of data protection obligations and professional secrecy in legal practice. The specific obligations of a small Geneva law firm using AI remain undefined by the Commissioner's published positions.

Enforcement activity in AI-related matters is observable in pattern form, but the enforcement trajectory for professional services firms using non-Swiss AI processors for privileged client data has not been articulated. Firms are operating with unresolved exposure that would become visible only upon examination.

### PI Insurance

No Geneva PI insurer has published how AI-assisted professional errors are classified under existing policy language. The coverage question — whether AI error liability falls within, outside, or in an undefined zone of current policies — has not been definitively answered by any insurer for the Geneva small-firm market. The managing partner's personal liability for AI governance adequacy, created by carrier attestation, is not matched by a known coverage boundary.

### Geneva Courts — Civil Procedure

Geneva courts have not published consolidated guidance on AI-related procedural questions. Different chambers have begun handling AI disclosure requests inconsistently through individual case decisions. The local variation in procedural expectations is not consolidated or published. Attorneys practicing in multiple chambers encounter different expectations without a framework for understanding the variation.

## 06 — Findings Summary: 6 Net-New Insights

*Findings distinct from C214–C260 Phase 01 Geneva legal ICP research. All observations are structural; no invented names, logos, or testimonials.*

### Finding 01 — The nFADP Employment Exception (Article 8(3)(c)) May Resolve the Privilege-Consent Collision, but Geneva Firms Are Operating Without Knowing Whether They Are Covered or Exposed

The nFADP employment relationship exception has not been analyzed as it applies to attorney work product processing through AI systems. If the exception applies to legal representation activities, the nFADP authorization obligations for AI processing of privileged client data may not apply — which would mean the privilege-consent collision named in prior cycles is a resolved question for firms that qualify. The exception's applicability to professional services firms has not been tested or published in any FDPIC guidance or cantonal bar communication. Firms currently using non-Swiss AI processors without nFADP authorization may be covered by the exception, may be exposed, or may be in a category no authority has addressed. The structural consequence is that the entire population of Geneva small firms using AI has an unresolved status relative to the most fundamental regulatory question about their AI operations. No Geneva service has mapped this.

### Finding 02 — Geneva's International Arbitration Seat Position Creates a Cross-Border AI Data Flow Problem That Domestic-Focused Guidance Does Not Address

Geneva's concentration as an international arbitration seat (ICC, WIPO, ad hoc tribunals) creates AI data processing scenarios with cross-border dimensions that domestic legal services guidance does not address. Attorneys practicing in international arbitration handle client data that moves through AI systems across jurisdictions in ways that intersect with Article 321 CP professional secrecy, nFADP third-country transfer obligations, and arbitral tribunal AI requirements simultaneously. No single guidance document addresses this intersection for Geneva small-firm practitioners. The problem is specifically Geneva's, driven by the city's arbitration concentration, and has not been named as a discrete exposure category in any prior Phase 01 cycle.

### Finding 03 — The Administrative Burden of Proposed AI Governance Requirements Exceeds What Small-Firm Operations Can Practically Sustain, Creating a Structural Compliance Impossibility Nobody Is Naming

Regulatory proposals and carrier expectations are converging toward documentation requirements designed at institutional scale. Geneva small firms with two to five attorneys cannot operationally sustain the proposed documentation burden without dedicated compliance infrastructure they do not have. The gap between required and sustainable is not being analyzed as a discrete structural problem. The consequence is that the compliance expectation being formed through carrier questionnaires and regulatory signals may be systematically unattainable for the majority of the Geneva small-firm market — and no market participant has named this as a structural compliance impossibility requiring a different response than improved execution.

### Finding 04 — PI Insurance AI Error Coverage Is Architecturally Undefined in Published Policy Language, Creating Personal Exposure Without a Known Discharge Path

Insurers have not published how AI-assisted professional errors are classified under existing PI policy language. The gap is not about whether coverage exists; it is about whether policy language was designed to answer the question. The structural consequence is that attorneys are carrying personal liability for AI governance adequacy (created by carrier attestation) without knowing whether their PI policy covers that liability. No Geneva service has obtained or published insurer interpretations of AI error coverage under existing policies. The managing partner's personal exposure for AI governance adequacy is documented by their signature and undefined by their coverage.

### Finding 05 — Geneva Courts Are Developing Local AI-Related Procedural Precedent Through Case-by-Case Decisions That Is Neither Published Nor Consolidated, Creating Uneven Attorney Exposure Across Chambers

Different Geneva court chambers have begun handling AI disclosure requests in litigation differently, creating local procedural variation that is not accessible through any published source. Attorneys practicing in multiple chambers encounter different expectations without any framework explaining the variation or its accountability implications. The precedent exists in individual decisions; no consolidation mechanism makes the pattern visible. The consequence is that the operative standard for AI disclosure in Geneva civil proceedings is partly set by case-by-case decisions in individual chambers, and attorneys cannot access or evaluate what that standard is without active cases in multiple chambers. This is distinct from the general procedural question and specifically names the local variation problem as a discrete structural exposure.

### Finding 06 — The Bar's Structural Advisory Void Is Functioning as a De Facto Operating Permission for Inadequate AI Governance, With No Feedback Mechanism to Develop the Guidance That Would Close It

Geneva's absence of a formal advisory mechanism for AI governance questions is not neutral in its effect. Attorneys in small firms read the absence of published rules as the absence of operative obligations. The interpretation is incorrect but common and self-reinforcing: the less guidance published, the more the vacuum is experienced as permission. Simultaneously, the decisions attorneys make under this vacuum are not fed back into any guidance development process. The bar's working groups and informal circuits do not have a systematic input mechanism for translating operational decisions into guidance development. The advisory void is therefore structurally permanent — not pending guidance development, but without a process to develop guidance — and is functioning as an operative condition that permits inadequate AI governance to continue without institutional correction.
