## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Swiss-EU regulatory asymmetry creating structurally incompatible obligations for the same firm**
Small Geneva law firms serving both Swiss domestic clients and EU-connected clients face two different AI regulatory frameworks simultaneously. Switzerland's principles-based approach and the EU AI Act's prescriptive requirements create a compliance mapping problem that no Geneva service has addressed. The firm cannot be compliant with both frameworks by applying a single standard.

**Signal 2: nFADP Article 22 data processing register gap for AI-using law firms**
The Article 22 obligation to maintain a data processing register is in force. No Geneva data protection advisor or law firm has published what a law firm's AI data processing register should contain. The register gap is active and present-tense, with no operational guidance available.

**Signal 3: AI conflict-of-interest infrastructure question surfacing before any framework exists**
Small firms running the same AI platform across potentially adverse matters are encountering the conflict-of-interest question for AI infrastructure — not for the attorneys' judgment, but for the shared platform that processes research for both matters. No Geneva conflict-of-interest framework addresses AI infrastructure sharing.

**Signal 4: AI platform migration creating unrecoverable research liability**
Firms considering or completing AI platform migrations are discovering that research cited in filed briefs sits on a deprecated platform outside any retrieval obligation. No Geneva protocol exists for managing AI research continuity across platform migrations. The problem is distinct from in-platform version deprecation — it involves complete infrastructure discontinuity.

**Signal 5: PI insurance AI coverage language unreadable without proactive carrier disclosure**
Small Geneva law firms are renewing PI policies without clarity on whether AI-assisted errors are covered. Carriers are not proactively disclosing AI-specific policy language. The coverage determination will be made post-hoc, at the moment of a claim, with no pre-claim standard the firm can evaluate against.

**Signal 6: Partner signature concentration creating personal exposure disproportionate to governance control**
In 2–5 attorney firms, managing partners are personally signing carrier AI attestations, client AI disclosure documents, and bar AI practice declarations — often for AI governance documentation built by associates or external vendors. The personal signature exposure is disproportionate to the governance control the partner actually exercises over AI tool configuration and use.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Swiss principles-based AI governance vs. EU AI Act prescriptive requirements:**
Switzerland has adopted a principles-based approach to AI governance through the Federal Council's AI regulatory framework and the Council of Europe AI Convention ratification process. Simultaneously, Swiss law firms serving EU-connected clients face EU AI Act obligations that are structurally incompatible with principles-based compliance. The EU AI Act classifies law firms as deployers or providers depending on how their AI systems are operated, creating specific obligations — conformity assessment, technical documentation, human oversight — that have no Swiss-law equivalent. No published guidance maps the intersection for small Geneva firms.

**nFADP Article 22 — Data processing register:**
Article 22 requires controllers to maintain a written record of data processing activities. For a law firm using AI tools, this record must account for what client data is processed, through which AI systems, for what purposes, and with what retention. The Article 22 record for AI-assisted legal work has not been defined in FDPIC guidance. The obligation is present-tense. The compliance gap is active.

**Article 321 CP and AI infrastructure conflict of interest:**
Article 321 CP prohibits disclosure of client information to third parties. The question of whether AI platform infrastructure that processes research for potentially adverse matters constitutes disclosure to a shared system — and whether the shared system's processing of both matters' queries creates a functional conflict — has not been addressed by any Geneva bar opinion or court ruling. No formal conflict check system in use at Geneva small firms addresses AI infrastructure sharing.

**Geneva bar AI practice rules and enforcement trigger:**
The SVA/ORDRE AI practice rules (finalized Q2 2026) require AI tool disclosure, client consent frameworks, and audit trail maintenance. The enforcement mechanism — what triggers a bar disciplinary inquiry versus informal guidance — remains unpublished. Small firms do not know what the operational trigger is for enforcement action.

**Platform migration and professional liability continuity:**
No Geneva civil procedure rule or bar guidance addresses the professional liability implications of switching AI platforms mid-matter or after filing. When research cited in a filed brief was generated on a platform that has since been discontinued or replaced, no protocol exists for reconstructing or disclosing the research foundation.

**PI insurance AI coverage:**
PI policies written before 2024 do not contain explicit AI-assisted work coverage language. Carriers are not proactively disclosing whether AI-specific exclusions or sub-limits apply. The coverage determination will be made by carriers at the moment of claim, using policy language not designed for AI-assisted work.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Swiss-EU AI regulatory asymmetry as structurally incompatible dual-compliance obligation**
Small Geneva law firms serving both Swiss domestic clients and EU-connected clients face two fundamentally incompatible AI governance frameworks simultaneously. Switzerland's principles-based approach and the EU AI Act's prescriptive requirements produce different compliance standards, different documentation requirements, and different consequences. The firm cannot apply a single standard to satisfy both. No Geneva service has produced a dual-compliance mapping document for small firm use. The asymmetry is most acute for the firm serving Swiss-incorporated entities with EU operations — a growing market segment. The obligation is present-tense for any firm with one EU-connected client.

*Finding 1 is distinct from C214–C281 Phase 01: prior cycles addressed EU AI Act service provider compliance as an editorial content topic (C207), and FDPIC enforcement trajectory as a general phenomenon (C279, C280), but none identified the specific Swiss-EU regulatory asymmetry as a structurally incompatible dual-compliance obligation with distinct documentation requirements at boutique scale, requiring a non-trivial architectural reconciliation that does not exist as a published service.*

---

**Finding 2: nFADP Article 22 data processing register absence as active compliance exposure**
The Article 22 obligation to maintain a data processing register is in force. For a law firm deploying AI tools, the register must account for what client data each AI system processes, for what purposes, and with what retention and access controls. No FDPIC guidance, no Geneva bar opinion, and no law firm publication has defined what a Swiss law firm's AI data processing register should contain. The firm that is not maintaining the register — or maintaining it without a defensible standard — has a present-tense compliance exposure under nFADP. The exposure is distinct from the Article 9 consent gap: that gap involves client authorization for AI processing; this finding involves the administrative record of what processing is occurring, which is a separate and ongoing obligation.

*Finding 2 is distinct from C214–C281 Phase 01: prior cycles addressed nFADP Article 9 client consent (C235, C246, C250, C279), Article 21 automated decision explanation (C279, C280), and FDPIC enforcement trajectory (C279, C280), but none addressed Article 22 data processing register compliance for AI-using law firms as a discrete active exposure with a specific architectural resolution.*

---

**Finding 3: AI infrastructure conflict-of-interest disclosure gap as unnamed professional obligation**
When a small firm's AI platform processes research queries for Matter A and Matter B — where the parties are not directly adverse but have conflicting interests — the shared platform infrastructure raises a conflict-of-interest question that existing conflict check systems do not ask. No Geneva bar opinion has addressed whether AI infrastructure sharing constitutes a conflict of interest requiring disclosure or withdrawal. No Geneva service has produced a disclosure protocol. The problem is distinct from traditional conflict-of-interest doctrine, which addresses attorney judgment and positional conflicts — this addresses the technical infrastructure through which research is conducted. The exposure is present-tense for any small firm running a single AI platform across multiple matters.

*Finding 3 is distinct from C214–C281 Phase 01: prior cycles addressed traditional conflict-of-interest checks for AI use (C273, C278), client AI content conflict (C226, C233), and nFADP third-party processing authorization (C235, C246), but none identified the specific AI infrastructure conflict-of-interest gap — the question of whether shared AI platform processing across potentially adverse matters constitutes a conflict requiring disclosure or withdrawal under Geneva bar rules.*

---

**Finding 4: AI platform migration research continuity gap as distinct from in-platform deprecation**
Prior cycles identified AI model version deprecation as a research reproducibility problem — when the same platform updates its model, past research may become irreproducible. C282 surfaces a distinct problem: when a firm migrates from one AI platform to a different one, the research generated on the departing platform is subject to complete infrastructure discontinuity, not merely version change. No Geneva protocol exists for managing the disposition of that research, for notifying clients or courts of the discontinuity, or for reconstructing research foundations across platform migrations. The problem is named and has no resolution pathway.

*Finding 4 is distinct from C214–C281 Phase 01: prior cycles addressed AI model version deprecation and research reproducibility (C275, C278, C280), but none identified the specific platform migration research continuity gap — which is distinct from in-platform version deprecation because it involves complete infrastructure discontinuity between two different vendor systems with no continuity obligation on either platform, and no Geneva protocol for managing the disclosure and reconstruction obligations this creates.*

---

**Finding 5: Geneva PI insurance AI coverage language as structurally undisclosed post-hoc coverage determination**
Geneva PI insurance policies written before 2024 do not contain explicit AI-assisted work coverage language. Carriers are not proactively disclosing whether AI-specific exclusions or sub-limits apply. The coverage determination will be made at the moment of a claim, using policy language designed for traditional practice. Small firms cannot evaluate their coverage posture before a claim arises because carriers have not published what their policies cover or exclude for AI-assisted work. The gap is not that policies definitely do not cover AI — it is that the coverage determination is structurally deferred to a post-hoc moment when the firm cannot influence it. No Geneva broker or advisor has made AI-specific PI coverage language a pre-claim evaluation standard for small firms.

*Finding 5 is distinct from C214–C281 Phase 01: prior cycles addressed carrier AI questionnaire content (C278, C279, C280), carrier attestation personal liability (C278, C279), and PI policy coverage questions generally (C278), but none identified the specific structural problem of AI coverage language as a post-hoc coverage determination that carriers are not proactively disclosing, creating a coverage evaluation gap the small firm cannot close before a claim arises.*

**Finding 6: Partner signature concentration creating personal exposure disproportionate to governance control**
In a 2–5 attorney Geneva firm, the managing partner personally signs carrier AI attestations, client AI disclosure declarations, and bar AI practice attestations — often for AI governance documentation built by associates or external vendors without a defined partner review standard.
