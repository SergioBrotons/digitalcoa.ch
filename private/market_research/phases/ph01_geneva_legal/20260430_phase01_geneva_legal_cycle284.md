## Deliverable 01 — ICP Language

**English**

The operative concern is not efficiency. It is accountability layering — research that cannot be reconstructed, briefs that cannot be authenticated, client data that is in infrastructure the firm did not choose, carrier attestations signed without knowing what coverage they commit to, and bar guidance that arrives as principle without methodology.

The language they actually use:

*"The carrier form asks me to attest to a methodology I cannot describe."*

*"I'm signing something that commits me personally, and I don't know what it actually covers."*

*"My associate is more fluent with the AI than I am — I'm signing off on work I cannot evaluate."*

*"Client files are in a system I didn't choose — the platform came with the practice management software."*

*"If a client exercises their Article 21 rights three years from now, I don't know what we'd be handing over."*

*"The bar says AI is our responsibility — they haven't told us what responsible looks like."*

## Deliverable 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

**Structural accountability inversion at small-firm scale.** The attorney with the most AI knowledge in a 2–5 attorney firm is typically the most junior. The attorney with the most professional accountability is the managing partner who cannot independently evaluate what they are signing off on. This is not a training gap — it is a structural design problem with no proportionate supervision protocol available in the Geneva market.

**Attestation without operative standard.** Carrier third-generation questionnaires commit the signing managing partner personally to AI review methodology adequacy. No Geneva authority has published what adequate means for a 2–5 attorney firm. The signature creates personal liability against a standard nobody has defined. The market has not produced a pre-signing review service that maps the gap between what is being signed and what is personally committed.

**Post-error disclosure protocol vacuum.** When an AI-assisted error surfaces — a fabricated citation, an incorrect authority, a research gap that should have been caught — there is no Geneva-specific protocol defining what to disclose to the client, the court, and the carrier. The error response itself creates documentation. The response documentation will be read by the carrier against the attestation already signed. Most firms have not evaluated whether their natural post-error response is consistent with what the attestation commits them to.

**Discovery request arriving before authentication standard exists.** Opposing counsel in Geneva civil proceedings are beginning to request disclosure of AI use in case preparation. No Geneva court has published what adequate authentication looks like for an AI-assisted filing. The first attorney to respond to a formally contested AI authentication challenge in a specific TAC chamber creates operative precedent through their response — precedent that will govern subsequent cases in that chamber before any published standard exists.

**nFADP enforcement posture shift.** FDPIC enforcement is moving from guidance letters to operational consequences. Multi-firm audit patterns emerging in the Geneva context suggest enforcement focus on documentation completeness and processing register accuracy. Small firms are operating with compliance infrastructure designed for the prior posture — backward-facing, retrospective, focused on whether documentation exists rather than whether the documentation architecture is designed for current enforcement expectations.

**Research confirmation bias at the attorney-AI interface.** AI-assisted legal research produces defensible conclusions through pattern-synthesis rather than principled derivation. Attorneys using AI for research follow-up queries that confirm initial hypothesis — not because of AI confabulation alone, but because the query interface rewards hypothesis-confirming patterns. Over time, research outputs reflect attorney initial positioning rather than neutral legal analysis. The compounding effect goes undetected because the output is defensible — it simply may not be derived from the reasoning path the attorney believes they followed.

## Deliverable 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Specific Regulatory Obligations (SVA/ORDRE, nFADP, FDPIC, Bar Rules)

**1. nFADP Art.22 — Processing register obligation**
Art.22 requires controllers to maintain a processing register. For a Geneva law firm, the register must document every data processing operation involving personal data — including AI-assisted research, document drafting, and case management. FDPIC has not published guidance defining what a law firm AI processing register must contain. The obligation is present-tense and unnamed in the Geneva market. Non-compliance does not produce immediate sanction but creates compounding exposure when combined with FDPIC audit activity.

**2. Carrier third-generation attestation — personal liability instrument**
Third-generation carrier questionnaires now require evidence of specific AI review decisions — not existence of a policy. The attestation is signed personally by the managing partner. No Geneva market service has published what adequate compliance looks like at 2–5 attorney firm scale. The gap between what is signed and what is personally committed is unnamed and unmanaged.

**3. Geneva civil procedure — AI-assisted filing certification ( CCP Art.142)**
Revised CCP Art.142 requires certification of electronic filings. No Geneva court practice directive defines what certification covers for AI-assisted content. The attorney certifies compliance with undefined requirements. The first contested authentication challenge creates operative precedent for the specific chamber through the responding attorney's response — precedent that will govern before any published standard arrives.

**4. AI Convention — private law obligations through existing mandates**
The AI Convention ratification creates obligations flowing through existing client contracts and professional liability standard of care. These obligations are present-tense and operative before any implementing legislation. The specific obligations that flow through existing client mandates have not been published by any Geneva authority.

**5. Federal Supreme Court AI-authenticated filing doctrine**
The Federal Supreme Court is developing doctrine on what constitutes original AI-assisted work product under Swiss copyright. This doctrine will apply to briefs filed in Geneva civil procedure. No Geneva small-firm protocol exists for documenting analytical originality of AI-assisted work in terms the Court's doctrine will require. The doctrine is forming through case-by-case decisions — the first attorney to face a formally contested AI authorship challenge in a specific context creates operative precedent through their response.

**Regulatory interaction map:** These five obligations operate on different timelines, with different consequences, and with no Geneva service mapping their interactions. The specific sequencing question — which obligations activate first, which create compound exposure when addressed in wrong order — is not addressed by any published framework.

**Key FDPIC pattern:** FDPIC enforcement is shifting from guidance letters to operational audit activity. The compliance posture that was adequate under guidance-letter-era enforcement is no longer adequate under audit-era enforcement. Small firms with compliance infrastructure designed for the prior posture are structurally unprepared for current enforcement expectations.

**Finding 03: Research confirmation bias at the attorney-AI interface creates silent jurisprudence drift that is undetectable from inside the firm.**
AI-assisted legal research produces defensible conclusions through pattern-synthesis not principled derivation. The query interface rewards hypothesis-confirming follow-up searches. **Finding 04: The AI system state of older active matters is structurally less documented than new matters, creating inverse exposure asymmetry.**
Firms that adopted AI more recently have more documentation for their newer matters. **Finding 06: Client Article 21 rights activate at the moment of maximum information asymmetry about the matter's AI system state.**
nFADP Art.21 creates data subject rights that activate when clients request explanation of AI-assisted decisions affecting them. For a law firm matter, these rights activate precisely when the attorney-client relationship ends — matter closure.
