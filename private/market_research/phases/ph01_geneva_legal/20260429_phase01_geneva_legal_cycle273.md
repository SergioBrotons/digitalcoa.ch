## 02 — Market Signals

Current problems, failure modes, paradoxes, and complaints circulating in the Geneva legal market.

**Signal 01 — Associate-level AI use as shadow governance surface with no supervising partner visibility.**
The most active AI users in a small firm are typically junior associates. The supervising partner cannot review what they cannot see. AI-assisted work product arrives under the partner's name without the partner having a method for determining what the AI contributed. No Geneva bar guidance addresses this specific structural inversion: the person with least AI literacy is making the most consequential AI decisions, while the person with full professional accountability cannot observe the AI use surface. Market has no service offering addressing this specific supervision architecture failure at small-firm scale.

**Signal 02 — AI authorship and originality questions arriving in commercial disputes before Geneva courts rule.**
Geneva commercial courts are seeing AI-authorship questions in copyright and originality disputes. The Swiss Federal Supreme Court is developing doctrine on AI copyright. No Geneva small-firm practice has a protocol for what AI-authorship questions mean for their own work product, what constitutes original AI-assisted analysis versus derivative AI output, or how to document analytical originality in a world where AI-generated content is structurally indistinguishable from human-generated content. The question is not whether AI-assisted work is entitled to protection — it is whether the firm's brief represents the firm's analytical work or the AI's analytical work, and what that distinction means procedurally.

**Signal 03 — The billing model structural incentive toward AI over-dependence is没有人 named.**
The economic model of hourly billing creates a zero-marginal-cost incentive for AI use on every billable unit. Efficiency gains from AI flow directly to firm margin. The billing record cannot reconstruct AI's contribution to any billable unit. The same economic model that rewards maximum AI utilization is structurally incompatible with the documentation model that would allow attribution of AI contribution when something goes wrong. This is not a cost management problem. It is a structural incentive misalignment that nobody in the Geneva legal market has named as a discrete market problem.

**Signal 04 — nFADP automated decision explanation obligation has no Geneva-specific discharge protocol for legal services.**
nFADP Article 21 requires explanation of automated decision logic. The statute does not define what constitutes automated decision-making in the context of AI-assisted legal analysis. Geneva attorneys do not have a discharge protocol for this specific obligation: how to document AI contribution, what constitutes adequate explanation, whether AI-assisted analysis qualifies as automated decision-making requiring Article 21 response. FDPIC has not addressed this intersection. No Geneva bar communication exists. The obligation is present-tense and unnamed.

**Signal 05 — Multi-jurisdictional discovery in Geneva arbitration creates ungoverned cross-framework exposure.**
Geneva's concentration of international arbitration means Geneva attorneys regularly manage discovery requests calibrated to foreign procedural expectations. AI system disclosure requests from opposing counsel operating under US or UK procedural frameworks create specific exposure at the intersection of Swiss professional secrecy (Art.321 CP), arbitration confidentiality obligations, and foreign court AI disclosure orders. No Geneva protocol exists for managing the precedence question: which framework governs, what can be produced without violating Art.321 CP, and what the consequences of different choices are. The exposure compounds as AI use in international arbitration increases and as AI disclosure requests become more common in foreign proceedings.

**Signal 06 — Geneva civil procedure has no formal AI disclosure obligation — and that is itself the problem.**
Geneva civil procedure has not defined: what AI systems must be disclosed in filings, whether AI assistance in brief preparation requires disclosure to the court, what the standard of AI verification is before filing, or what happens procedurally when a brief is found to contain AI-confabulated citations. The absence of procedural rules is not neutral. Attorneys are making filing decisions without a procedural framework, and the first Geneva attorney to face a formally contested AI disclosure motion will create operative precedent through their response. The vacuum is itself the operative condition.

**Signal 07 — The documentation window on 2023–2026 AI adoption period is closing without firms knowing what they cannot reconstruct.**
The 2023–2026 period — during which most Geneva small firms adopted AI tools without governance frameworks — is becoming permanently non-reconstructable. Tool versions have changed. Vendor terms have changed. Staff who configured systems have departed. Firms have no inventory of what was configured when, under what terms, for which matters. The documentation window is closing as a structural fact, not a compliance deadline. The consequences of this window closing will surface as present-tense liability at carrier renewal, bar examination, or client dispute — whenever someone asks what the firm's AI governance looked like in 2023 and no documentation exists.

## 04 — Compliance Context

Geneva and Swiss-specific regulatory obligations relevant to this ICP.

**SVA/ORDRE (Swiss Bar Association / Geneva Bar):**
SBA published AI guidelines in June 2024. Key obligations: attorney remains fully responsible for AI-assisted work; professional secrecy under Art.13 FMLA must be maintained; AI tools must comply with data protection requirements; attorneys must understand AI system capabilities and limitations; independent professional judgment must be exercised; clients should be informed about AI use where confidential information is involved or billing is affected. ORDRE has not published Geneva-specific guidance distinct from SBA guidelines. No bar advisory opinion mechanism for specific AI governance questions exists. The supervision inversion at small-firm scale — where junior associates make consequential AI decisions outside supervising partner visibility — is not addressed in any published guidance.

**nFADP (Federal Act on Data Protection, revised September 1, 2023):**
FDPIC confirmed direct applicability of nFADP to AI-supported data processing (December 2024 guidance). Key obligations: transparency regarding AI system purpose, functionality, and data sources; data protection impact assessments for high-risk AI processing; data subject rights to object to automated processing and request human review of automated decisions significantly affecting them. nFADP applies to client data processing by law firms. The automated decision explanation obligation (Article 21) has no Geneva-specific discharge protocol for legal services: the statute does not define what constitutes automated decision-making in AI-assisted legal analysis, and FDPIC has not addressed this specific intersection. Art.321 CP and nFADP create structural tension when data subject access requests arrive for AI-generated content that constitutes attorney work product.

**FDPIC (Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner):**
FDPIC issued guidance on AI applicability (December 2024), confirming existing nFADP applies directly to AI systems. FDPIC has not published guidance specifically addressing the attorney-client privilege intersection with AI processing obligations. FDPIC pattern enforcement is observable through practice but not published as formal doctrine. The automated decision explanation obligation in legal services context — whether AI-assisted legal analysis triggers Article 21 — is unaddressed by FDPIC guidance.

**Professional Liability Insurance:**
Mandatory for all Swiss attorneys. Coverage for AI-assisted errors remains architecturally undefined in published policy language from Swiss carriers. Carrier third-generation questionnaires operate as de facto operative standard-setting through commercial underwriting. Carrier denial creates operative precedent without appeal mechanism. The billing model incentive misalignment — where hourly billing incentivizes maximum AI utilization while billing records cannot reconstruct AI's contribution — is also a coverage documentation problem: carriers seeking evidence of AI review methodology will find most small firms unable to produce it.

**Art.321 CP (Swiss Criminal Code — Professional Secrecy):**
Professional secrecy obligations apply to all client communications and work product. AI systems processing client data through non-Swiss vendor infrastructure create unresolved conflicts with Art.321 CP requirements. The multi-jurisdictional discovery bind in international arbitration creates specific tension: foreign court AI disclosure orders may conflict with Art.321 CP obligations, with no published Swiss protocol for which obligation takes precedence. Swiss Federal Supreme Court is developing AI copyright doctrine — relevant to what constitutes original AI-assisted work product under Swiss law.

**Geneva Civil Procedure:**
AI disclosure procedural status formally undefined under Geneva civil procedure. AI authentication, model version disclosure, and methodology explanation in discovery unpublished. Swiss Federal Supreme Court is developing AI authorship and originality doctrine relevant to AI-assisted work product. The first contested AI-authorship motion in a Geneva commercial court will create operative precedent without published framework for attorneys to anticipate. No Geneva court practice directive exists for what AI verification standard is required before filing.

**Federal Council AI Regulatory Approach (February 2025):**
Federal Council confirmed technology-neutral, sector-specific approach with targeted adjustments following AI Convention incorporation. No standalone comprehensive AI legislation. Federal AI Coordination Office established February 1, 2026 within Federal Chancellery as contact point. Geneva firms preparing for obligations where institutional architecture is still forming. The multi-jurisdictional discovery bind — where foreign procedural AI disclosure expectations conflict with Swiss professional secrecy — is unaddressed by Federal Council approach.

**EU AI Act (for Geneva firms serving EU clients):**
Annex III high-risk AI system obligations intersect with Art.321 CP professional secrecy and SVA/ORDRE rules in unresolved ways for small Geneva firms serving EU clients. No Geneva service has mapped this intersection for small-firm context. The EU AI Act high-risk system classification may apply to AI systems used in contexts affecting EU data subjects, creating simultaneous compliance obligations under incompatible frameworks with no published reconciliation methodology.
