## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Carrier AI attestation as documentation creation forcing function**
Carriers asking AI-specific questions are forcing firms to create documentation they did not previously have. The documentation is being created to complete the form, not from a governance decision. The forcing function creates documentation without creating governance.

**Signal 2: Client AI disclosure expectation as communication ambiguity**
Clients are beginning to ask whether work product is AI-generated. The question creates communication ambiguity for firms that have not decided what their disclosure policy is. The ambiguity is present-tense.

**Signal 3: 2023 AI documentation gap as retrospective exposure**
Documentation created in 2023 about AI use is being measured against current market-formed standards. The gap between 2023 documentation and current standards is a retrospective exposure that firms are only now recognizing.

**Signal 4: Bar AI guidance as necessary but insufficient operational reference**
Bar AI guidance provides necessary but insufficient operational reference. It addresses the regulatory frame, not the Tuesday problem. The gap between regulatory guidance and operational decision-making is where most of the actual exposure lives.

**Signal 5: AI tool inventory question as discovery moment**
The FINMA question about AI tool inventory surfaced that firms had not thought of their three-tool usage as a system requiring documentation. The question revealed a documentation gap that was not visible before the question was asked.

**Signal 6: AI reliability question as unresolved operational problem**
Associates facing AI-assisted research do not have a protocol for evaluating reliability. The bar guidance does not address the Tuesday question of whether a specific AI-assisted finding is reliable enough to use in client work.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: AI tool inventory documentation methodology for small firms**
No methodology exists for documenting an AI tool inventory at small firm scale in a way that satisfies carrier questions and FINMA inquiry. The methodology would need to be operationally practical and compliance-relevant.

**Gap 2: Client AI disclosure policy design for professional services firms**
No framework exists for designing a client disclosure policy about AI-assisted work. The policy design requires decisions about when to disclose, how to disclose, and what the firm's position on AI actually is.

**Gap 3: 2023–2024 AI documentation retrospective gap remediation protocol**
No protocol exists for addressing the retrospective gap between what was documented in 2023–2024 and what current standards require. The remediation would need to be done without creating new exposure through the remediation itself.

**Gap 4: AI reliability evaluation protocol for legal research**
No protocol exists for evaluating the reliability of AI-assisted legal research at the operational level. The protocol would need to address what to do when the AI finding cannot be independently verified.

**Gap 5: Carrier AI attestation adequacy evaluation**
No methodology exists for evaluating whether a carrier AI attestation is adequate. The adequacy question is distinct from the completeness question — a complete attestation may still be inadequate by carrier expectations.

**Gap 6: Geneva law firm AI governance baseline assessment format**
No assessment format exists for establishing what adequate AI governance looks like at small Geneva firm scale. The format would need to be usable by firms that do not have a dedicated compliance function.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**SVA/ORDRE professional obligations and AI:**
The Geneva bar has published AI guidance that addresses professional conduct obligations and AI use. The guidance is principle-based, not rule-based. The principle-based structure creates interpretation space that firms navigate without clear benchmarks.

**nFADP Article 21 automated decision explanation obligation:**
Art. 21 requires that data subjects receive explanations of automated decisions in intelligible form. Legal work that involves automated document processing or AI-assisted research has not been analyzed under this provision. The obligation applies to client-facing AI-assisted work.

**nFADP Article 9 third-party processor authorization:**
AI tools processing client matter data through non-Swiss processors create third-party processor authorization requirements under Art. 9. The authorization requirements are active for most firms using cloud-based AI tools. The scope of the professional secrecy exception for these processors is unresolved.

**FINMA AI governance examination progression:**
FINMA examination questions are becoming more specific about AI tool inventory, model version tracking, and use-case differentiation. The examination is forming the standard through question specificity without publishing adequacy benchmarks.

**Revised Swiss CCP (January 2025) human responsibility standard:**
The revised CCP requires that human responsibility remain paramount in AI-assisted proceedings. The standard has no published definition of what adequate human review of AI-assisted work requires. International commercial proceedings in Geneva are beginning to encounter this gap.

**Carrier third-generation AI questionnaire:**
The third-generation carrier questionnaire probes five evidence categories without publishing adequacy standards. The questions function as a de facto adequacy definition through specificity, not through disclosure.

**Federal Council AI Convention legislative timeline:**
The Federal Council AI Convention legislative draft is expected end-2026. Legislation designed for future AI use may retroactively create documentation requirements for 2023–2025 AI configurations that cannot be met retrospectively.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Carrier AI attestation as documentation forcing function creating governance-implicit response without governance-explicit decision**
Carrier AI attestation questions are forcing firms to create documentation they did not previously have. The documentation is created to complete the form, not from a governance decision. The forcing function creates documentation without governance, which may be worse than no documentation — documented governance positions become measurement standards.

**Finding 2: Client AI disclosure expectation as present-tense communication ambiguity without policy foundation**
Clients beginning to ask whether work product is AI-generated creates communication ambiguity for firms that have not designed disclosure policy. The ambiguity is present-tense for any firm that has not decided what its position is. The disclosure question surfaces the firm's AI use in a client relationship context before the firm has designed its approach.

**Finding 3: 2023–2024 AI documentation adequacy inversion as retrospective exposure distinct from compliance gap**
Documentation created in 2023–2024 is being measured against current market-formed standards, not against the standards of that time. The inversion means that firms that documented their AI governance in 2023–2024 are worse off than firms that documented nothing — documented positions are measured against current standards, undocumented positions are not. The retrospective exposure is distinct from a present-tense compliance gap.

**Finding 4: Bar AI guidance as necessary-but-insufficient operational reference creating interpretation space without benchmarks**
Bar AI guidance addresses professional conduct obligations and AI use in principle-based terms. The guidance is necessary but operationally insufficient. The interpretation space that principle-based guidance creates is where firms navigate without benchmarks. The gap between regulatory guidance and operational decision-making on a Tuesday is where most actual exposure lives.

**Finding 5: AI tool inventory question as discovery mechanism for documentation system gap firms did not know they had**
FINMA and carrier questions about AI tool inventory are functioning as discovery mechanisms. Firms discover through the question that they had not thought of their AI tool usage as a system requiring documentation. The discovery is not a compliance failure — it is a documentation system gap that was invisible before the question was asked. The gap exists for firms using AI, not only for firms that have failed to document AI.

**Finding 6: AI reliability evaluation for legal research as unresolvable operational problem at the associate level without protocol design**
Associates facing AI-assisted legal research findings do not have an operational protocol for evaluating reliability. The bar guidance does not address the Tuesday question. The problem is unresolvable at the associate level without protocol design that specifies what to do when AI-assisted findings cannot be independently verified. The unresolvability is structural, not a training gap.
