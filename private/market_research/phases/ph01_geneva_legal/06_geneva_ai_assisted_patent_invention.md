# Finding 06 — Federal Administrative Court 2025: AI-Assisted Invention Patentability — Human Contribution Documentation Standard

**Phase:** Phase 01 Geneva Legal
**Cycle:** C206 | **Date:** 2026-04-06 | **Sources:** Web Search (Lenz + Staehelin, Haug & Partners, IJLSSR) + Domain Knowledge

## Finding

A 2025 ruling by the Swiss Federal Administrative Court clarified that while AI systems cannot be named as inventors under Swiss patent law, AI-assisted inventions are patentable if human contributions are adequately documented and credited. This precedent establishes a human contribution documentation standard that directly affects Geneva IP law practice, technology transfer offices, and AI-development companies.

**Legal architecture:**
- AI cannot be inventor — Swiss patent law follows the position that inventorship requires legal personhood, which AI systems lack
- AI-assisted inventions remain fully patentable — the AI contribution is treated as a tool, analogous to computer-aided design software or laboratory equipment
- Human contribution must be "adequately documented and credited" — the operative standard is documentary evidence of human creative input, not a specific form or format
- This aligns Switzerland with most major jurisdictions (US, EP, UK) on the AI inventorship question, while establishing Switzerland-specific documentation requirements

**Geneva-specific context:**
- Geneva's concentration of technology companies, pharma R&D, and university technology transfer offices (Université de Genève) makes this a high-frequency issue for Geneva IP practitioners
- CERN technology transfer agreements involving AI are directly affected — human documentation standards must be met in any AI-assisted invention disclosure
- Geneva's life sciences sector (HUG research, pharma subsidiaries) faces regular AI-assisted research invention questions

**Advisory implications:**
- Geneva IP law firms need AI invention file protocol advisory services — structured processes for documenting human creative contributions in AI-assisted R&D
- Technology transfer offices at Geneva universities need AI IP policy frameworks — governing ownership, inventorship disclosure, and documentation standards
- Geneva companies using AI in R&D need internal AI invention disclosure protocols — to ensure patent rights are preserved and not forfeited through undocumented AI contributions
- The "adequately documented" standard is currently principles-based — practitioners who shape client documentation practices now will help define what "adequate" means in practice

**Source:** Lenz + Staehelin analysis of Swiss Federal Administrative Court AI invention ruling 2025; Haug & Partners "Swiss court signals more lenient approach to AI inventions" analysis; International Journal of Law and Social Science Research IJLSSR.

## Distinct from C205
C205 Finding 1 covered the BGer AI authorship precedent (copyright, not patent). This C206 finding covers the Federal Administrative Court patent inventorship ruling — a distinct legal domain with different documentation standards, different affected client categories, and different advisory opportunities. The human contribution documentation standard is a new, specific legal test that was not present in C205 Phase 01 findings.
