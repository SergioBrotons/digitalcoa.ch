## 02 — Market Signals

**Current problems, failure modes, paradoxes, and complaints circulating in the market.**

---

### English

**The double-authority vacuum.** The Geneva bar has not issued formal AI guidance. Informal guidance circulates through peer networks without safe harbor. The result: attorneys treat informal reassurance as if it were formal permission. The absence of published rules is being read as the absence of operative obligations. This is a self-reinforcing circuit. More attorneys operating on informal reassurance means more attorneys accumulating undocumented AI use. More undocumented AI use means more attorneys who would be harmed if formal guidance were issued — creating a growing constituency with a structural interest in maintaining the void.

**The billing paradox.** Small Geneva firms are under simultaneous pressure to adopt AI (competitiveness, margin) and to document AI use (carrier requirements, bar accountability). Hourly billing cannot reconstruct AI's contribution to billable time. The more efficiently AI is used, the less traceable the efficiency becomes in the billing record. Firms are generating AI efficiency gains that simultaneously strengthen their competitive position and weaken their accountability documentation. Neither problem is being named as a single structural paradox.

**The CE adequacy illusion.** The Geneva bar's AI continuing education tracks attendance, not competence. A managing partner who attended one introductory webinar and a managing partner who has built a governance system receive the same credential signal. Carrier third-generation questionnaires now probe substantive AI review adequacy — a different question entirely. The CE system cannot close this gap because the CE system is not designed to.

**The client sophistication inversion.** Geneva clients are becoming more technically specific in their questions about AI. "What model did you use?" "Was it reviewed by a human?" "Can you tell me what the configuration was?" These questions arrive before any Geneva bar guidance exists on what answers are appropriate, complete, or potentially self-incriminating. Attorneys are improvising responses in real time, not realizing they are creating precedent in how they describe their practices.

**The arbitration asymmetry.** Geneva's concentration of international arbitration creates an AI governance question with no published framework: what, if anything, must counsel disclose to the tribunal about AI use in preparing a submission? ICC Rules, WIPO Policy, and HKMC Procedures are silent on this specific question. Counsel are making individual decisions with no peer consensus, no tribunal guidance, and no safe harbor. The arbitration context — with its higher stakes, its international parties, and its confidentiality obligations — amplifies every one of these questions.

**The documentation archaeology trap.** Every month that passes adds to an undocumented AI practice history. Carrier questionnaires and potential bar guidance are both approaching from the same direction: they will ask what the practice looked like. The 2023–2026 period is closing as a reconstructable record. Most small Geneva firms are operating with the same awareness that something should have been documented, without any methodology for what to reconstruct or how.

**The nFADP privilege collision arriving silently.** Client nFADP data subject access requests (Art.13, Art.21) are beginning to arrive through legal services relationships. The request targets data about the client that may have been generated through AI inference, stored in AI platform infrastructure, or derived from queries the attorney made. The attorney faces a structural collision: satisfying the data subject's access rights may require disclosing attorney work product, analytical process, or query metadata that falls under Art.321 CP professional secrecy. No Geneva protocol exists for resolving this collision. The requests are arriving; the protocol is not.

### Français

**Le vide d'autorité double.** Le barreau de Genève n'a pas publié de directives formelles sur l'IA. Les orientations informelles circulent dans les réseaux professionnels sans harbor sûr. Résultat : les avocats traitent le rassurance informelle comme si elle constituait une autorisation formelle. L'absence de règles publiées est interprétée comme l'absence d'obligations opératives. Il s'agit d'un circuit auto-renforçant. Davantage d'avocats opérant sur la base de rassurance informelle signifie davantage d'avocats accumulant une utilisation de l'IA non documentée. Plus l'utilisation de l'IA est non documentée, plus le nombre d'avocats qui seraient prejudicés si des directives formelles étaient publiées augmente — créant une constituency croissante avec un intérêt structurel à maintenir le vide.

**Le paradoxe de la facturation.** Les petits étude genevoises sont sous pression simultanée pour adopter l'IA (compétitivité, marge) et pour documenter l'utilisation de l'IA (exigences de l'assureur, responsabilité du barreau). La facturation horaire ne peut pas reconstruire la contribution de l'IA au temps facturable. Plus l'IA est utilisée efficacement, moins l'efficacité devient traçable dans l'enregistrement de facturation. Les cabinets génèrent des gains d'efficacité en IA qui renforcent simultanément leur position concurrentielle et affaiblissent leur documentation de responsabilité. Aucun des deux problèmes n'est nommé comme un seul paradoxe structurel.

**L'illusion de l'adéquation de la formation continue.** La formation continue en IA du barreau de Genève suit la participation, pas la compétence. Un associé gérant qui a assisté à un webinar introductif et un associé gérant qui a construit un système de gouvernance reçoivent le même signal de credential. Les questionnaires de troisième génération des assureurs examinent désormais l'adéquation substantielle de la révision par IA — une question entièrement différente. Le système de FC ne peut pas combler cette lacune parce qu'il n'est pas conçu pour.

**L'inversion de la sophistication du client.** Les clients genevois deviennent plus techniquement spécifiques dans leurs questions sur l'IA. « Quel modèle avez-vous utilisé ? » « Est-ce qu'un humain l'a révisé ? » « Pouvez-vous me dire quelle était la configuration ? » Ces questions arrivent avant que toute directive du barreau de Genève n'existe sur les réponses appropriées, complètes ou potentiellement incriminantes. Les avocats improvisent des réponses en temps réel, sans réaliser qu'ils créent un précédent dans la façon dont ils décrivent leurs pratiques.

**L'asymétrie de l'arbitrage.** La concentration de Genève en arbitrage international crée une question de gouvernance de l'IA sans cadre publié : quoi, le cas échéant, le conseil doit-il divulguer au tribunal concernant l'utilisation de l'IA dans la préparation d'une soumission ? Le Règlement de la CCI, la Politique OMPI et les Procédures du CMH ne disent rien sur cette question spécifique. Les conseils prennent des décisions individuelles sans consensus professionnel, sans directive du tribunal et sans harbor sûr. Le contexte de l'arbitrage — avec ses enjeux plus élevés, ses parties internationales et ses obligations de confidentialité — amplifie chacune de ces questions.

**Le piège de l'archéologie de la documentation.** Chaque mois qui passe ajoute à une historique de pratique de l'IA non documentée. Les questionnaires des assureurs et les directives potentielles du barreau approchent tous deux de la même direction : ils demanderont à quoi ressemblait la pratique. La période 2023–2026 se ferme en tant qu'enregistrement reconstructable. La plupart des petits cabinets genevois opèrent avec la même conscience que quelque chose aurait dû être documenté, sans méthodologie pour ce qu'il faut reconstruire ni comment.

**La collision privilège nFADP arrivant silencieusement.** Les demandes d'accès des personnes concernées au sens des art. 13 et 21 nFADP commencent à arriver par le biais des relations de services juridiques. La demande cible des données sur le client qui peuvent avoir été générées par inférence IA, stockées dans l'infrastructure de la plateforme IA ou dérivées de requêtes que l'avocat a effectuées. L'avocat fait face à une collision structurelle : satisfaire aux droits d'accès de la personne concernée peut nécessiter la divulgation du travail préparatoire de l'avocat, du processus analytique ou des métadonnées de requêtes qui relèvent du secret professionnel de l'art. 321 CP. Aucun protocole genevois n'existe pour résoudre cette collision. Les demandes arrivent ; le protocole n'existe pas.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**What others say, gaps, and what no one is addressing.**

---

### English

**Where the market is operating.** Geneva legal AI governance advisory currently operates in three modes: bar-sponsored introductory webinars (attendance-only credentialing, no practice adequacy measurement), carrier questionnaires (setting operative standards through commercial underwriting, not regulatory process), and informal peer exchange (no safe harbor, no publication mechanism, no feedback loop to guidance development).

**What no one is addressing.** The following problems have been identified in market conversation but have not been claimed as discrete service offerings by any Geneva market participant:

The Geneva administrative court AI decisions problem. Geneva's Tribunal administratif cantonal (TAC) has been issuing decisions in 2025–2026 that touch on AI-assisted administrative decisions, algorithmic burden of proof questions, and explanation requirements in AI-affected proceedings. These decisions are not published with AI-specific indexing. They are not tracked by any Geneva legal publication as a consolidated body of operative precedent. An attorney with Swiss regulatory exposure, administrative law matters, or EU-connected clients subject to Swiss administrative decisions is operating without any map of how Geneva's administrative courts are treating algorithmic recommendations, human-in-the-loop adequacy, or AI explanation obligations. No Geneva publication has claimed this as a discrete editorial or advisory beat.

The Geneva legal aid AI integration problem. Geneva's state-funded legal aid program (aide juridique) is beginning to incorporate AI tools in delivering legal services to legally aided clients. Attorneys providing aided services operate under simultaneous obligations: the state legal aid contract requirements, Art.321 CP professional secrecy, and nFADP data processing obligations. The intersection of these three obligation sets for aided-client AI use is uncharted. There is no Geneva protocol for what a legally aided attorney must document about AI use, how client data subject rights are handled in the legal aid context, or how the three-layer obligation collision is resolved. This is distinct from general law firm nFADP questions because the state contract creates an additional accountability relationship — toward the canton — that private retainer agreements do not.

The Geneva arbitration AI disclosure asymmetry. Geneva's concentration of international commercial arbitration creates a specific AI governance gap: counsel's AI use in preparing arbitral submissions, witness briefs, or expert reports has no disclosed framework for whether, how, or when disclosure to the tribunal, opposing counsel, or the ICC/WIPO/HKMC secretariat is required. This gap is distinct from domestic court procedural questions because arbitral rules are contractually defined, confidentiality obligations are different, and the stakes of inadvertent disclosure are higher. No Geneva bar working group, no arbitral institution, and no Geneva advisory service has produced a framework for this specific scenario. It is the highest-stakes context in which Geneva attorneys operate with no AI governance guidance.

The Geneva SBA AI complaint precedent trajectory. The Geneva bar (SBA) has begun receiving AI-related professional conduct complaints. Complaint outcomes are not published. The pattern of what is being sustained, dismissed, or resolved informally is forming an implicit accountability standard without any publication mechanism. Attorneys who have not faced a complaint have no way to know what distinguishes a sustained from a dismissed AI-related complaint. The trajectory — what the SBA is treating as the operative standard in practice — is invisible to the population it governs. No Geneva publication has proposed tracking this as a discrete intelligence function.

The Geneva civil procedure AI authentication protocol under revised CCP Art.142. The revised Swiss Code of Civil Procedure (January 2025) introduced updated electronic filing requirements. The specific question of what certification is required when an attorney certifies a filing containing AI-generated content — particularly what the certification covers with respect to the AI-generated portion versus the human-authored portion — has not been addressed by any Geneva court practice directive. The question arises in two distinct contexts: (a) domestic Geneva civil proceedings and (b) proceedings before the Geneva courts in international commercial cases. Neither context has a published answer. Attorneys are certifying what they are not in a position to certify.

**What is being addressed inadequately.** The nFADP employment exception question. Geneva attorneys are generally aware that nFADP applies to them but have not examined the threshold question of whether a law firm's client data processing is "ancillary" (auxiliaire) to a primary activity that is not data processing. For a law firm, the primary activity is legal services — which is client data processing. The exemption threshold question is live, unresolved, and being navigated by assumption rather than analysis. This is distinct from the Art.9 authorization collision and from the privilege-consent architecture collision identified in prior cycles.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**Geneva and Swiss-specific regulatory obligations: SVA/ORDRE, nFADP, FDPIC, bar rules.**

---

### English

**Swiss Bar Association (SVA) / Ordre des Avocats Genevois (ORDRE).** The Geneva bar has not issued formal AI practice rules. The ODAGE working group produced introductory curriculum content that tracks attendance, not competence. No formal advisory opinion mechanism exists for specific AI governance questions. The absence of published guidance is being treated as an absence of operative obligations by attorneys who are simultaneously uncertain and exposed. The informal guidance circuit operates without publication, without safe harbor, and without any feedback path to formal guidance development. The structural advisory void identified in C258 and confirmed in C261 remains operative and has not been addressed by any bar initiative.

**nFADP (LPD/BGSA) — employment exception and professional services applicability.** The nFADP employment exception (Art.8(3)(c)) exempts data processing "inherent to the employment relationship" from certain obligations. Geneva attorneys are uncertain whether their use of AI tools for client matters falls under this exception, which would affect whether client data processing requires separate lawful basis under Art.6. The exemption's applicability to professional services firms — where client data processing is not incidental but constitutive of the primary activity — is a planning-level question with no published resolution. This is distinct from the Art.9/Art.321 CP collision identified in C251 and C258, and from the employment exception structural question identified in C261. The specific question is: does a law firm qualify for the employment exception at all, or does its primary activity disqualify it?

**nFADP Articles 13 and 21 — legal services context.** Client data subject access requests and automated decision explanation requests are arriving through legal services relationships. For a law firm, the data processed includes client communications, case materials, legal research, and AI-generated analytical content. These categories may trigger different response obligations under Art.13 (access) and Art.21 (automated decision explanation). The attorney faces the additional complication that responding to these requests may require disclosing attorney work product or analytical processes protected by Art.321 CP. No Geneva bar protocol exists for resolving this collision. No FDPIC guidance has addressed the specific intersection of data subject rights and professional secrecy in the legal services context.

**FDPIC oversight.** The Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner has not issued guidance specific to Geneva law firms' AI use. FDPIC pattern enforcement remains oriented toward sectors with published enforcement priorities. The specific question of whether FDPIC considers Geneva attorneys' AI research query metadata, AI-generated client file content, or AI-assisted brief drafting as activities within its supervisory scope — and what compliance posture it expects — remains unstated.

**Revised Swiss CCP (January 2025) — AI-assisted filing authentication.** The revised Code of Civil Procedure introduced updated provisions on electronic filing. The specific question of what an attorney certifies when certifying an AI-assisted filing — particularly the boundary between AI-generated content and human-authored content, and what the certification covers with respect to each — has not been addressed by any Geneva court practice directive. This leaves attorneys in the position of certifying compliance with requirements that have not been defined.

**Geneva TAC AI administrative decisions.** The Tribunal administratif cantonal has issued decisions touching on algorithmic administrative decisions, burden of proof questions in AI-affected proceedings, and explanation requirements. These decisions are not consolidated, not indexed by AI-specific issue, and not tracked by any Geneva legal publication. They represent the only body of published Geneva precedent on how a Geneva court treats algorithmic recommendation in substantive proceedings — yet they are invisible to the attorneys most affected by them.

**Geneva legal aid AI integration.** Attorneys providing state-funded legal aid services face additional accountability obligations under the cantonal legal aid contract, which operates alongside Art.321 CP and nFADP. The intersection of these three obligation sets for AI use in aided matters is unaddressed by any published protocol, guidance, or bar communication.

**International arbitration AI disclosure.** Geneva's position as the world's most concentrated arbitration seat creates AI governance questions governed by institutional rules (ICC, WIPO, HKMC), arbitral tribunal discretion, and international practice. No Geneva institution, bar body, or advisory service has produced guidance on AI disclosure obligations in arbitral proceedings seated in Geneva. Counsel are making individual decisions without framework, without peer consensus, and without institutional safe harbor.

L'avocat fait face à la complication supplémentaire que répondre à ces demandes peut nécessiter la divulgation du travail préparatoire de l'avocat ou des processus analytiques protégés par l'art. 321 CP. Aucun protocole du barreau de Genève n'existe pour résoudre cette collision. Aucune directive du PFPDT n'a traité l'intersection spécifique des droits des personnes concernées et du secret professionnel dans le contexte des services juridiques.

**Surveillance du PFPDT.** Le Préposé fédéral à la protection des données et à la transparence n'a pas publié de directive spécifique à l'utilisation de l'IA par les cabinets d'avocats genevois. L'application du PFPDT reste orientée vers les secteurs avec des priorités d'application publiées. La question spécifique de savoir si le PFPDT considère les métadonnées de requêtes de recherche IA des avocats genevois, le contenu de dossiers clients généré par l'IA ou la rédaction de mémoires assistée par l'IA comme des activités dans son champ de surveillance — et quelle posture de conformité il attend — reste non stated.

**CPC suisse révisé (janvier 2025) — authentification des dépôts assistés par IA.** Le Code de procédure civile révisé a introduit des dispositions mises à jour sur le dépôt électronique. La question spécifique de ce qu'un avocat certifie lors de la certification d'un dépôt assisté par l'IA — en particulier la frontière entre le contenu généré par l'IA et le contenu authored par l'humain, et ce que la certification couvre par rapport à chacun — n'a été traitée par aucune directive de pratique du tribunal de Genève. Cela laisse les avocats dans la position de certifier la conformité à des exigences qui n'ont pas été définies.

**Décisions administratives IA de la TAC de Genève.** Le Tribunal administratif cantonal a rendu des décisions portant sur des décisions administratives algorithmiques, des questions de charge de la preuve dans les procédures affectées par l'IA et des exigences d'explication. Ces décisions ne sont pas consolidées, pas indexées par problème spécifique à l'IA et pas suivies par aucune publication juridique genevoise. Elles représentent le seul corps de précédent genevois publié sur la façon dont un tribunal de Genève traite la recommandation algorithmique dans les procédures substantielles — pourtant elles sont invisibles pour les avocats les plus affected by them.

**Intégration de l'IA dans l'aide juridique genevoise.** Les avocats fournissant des services d'aide juridique financés par l'État font face à des obligations de responsabilité supplémentaires en vertu du contrat d'aide juridique cantonal, qui opère aux côtés de l'art. 321 CP et du nFADP. L'intersection de ces trois ensembles d'obligations pour l'utilisation de l'IA dans les affaires aidées n'est traitée par aucun protocole publié, directive ou communication du barreau.

**Divulgation de l'IA dans l'arbitrage international.** La position de Genève en tant que siege arbitral le plus concentré au monde crée des questions de gouvernance de l'IA gouvernées par les règles institutionnelles (CCI, OMPI, CMH), la discrétion du tribunal arbitral et la pratique internationale. Aucune institution genevoise, aucun organe du barreau ni aucun service-conseil genevois n'a produit de directive sur les obligations de divulgation de l'IA dans les procédures arbitrales siégeant à Genève. Les conseils prennent des décisions individuelles sans cadre, sans consensus professionnel et sans harbor institutionnel sûr.

### Finding 01: Geneva administrative court (TAC) AI decisions as untracked operative precedent

Geneva's Tribunal administratif cantonal has been issuing decisions in 2025–2026 that address algorithmic administrative recommendations, burden of proof questions in AI-affected proceedings, and AI explanation requirements. These decisions are the only published Geneva precedent on how a Geneva court substantively treats algorithmic recommendation in proceedings. They are not indexed by AI-specific issue, not consolidated, and not tracked by any Geneva legal publication. An attorney with Swiss regulatory exposure, administrative law matters, or EU-connected clients subject to Swiss administrative decisions is operating without any map of how Geneva's administrative courts are treating these questions. No Geneva publication has identified this as a discrete editorial or advisory beat. This is distinct from the Geneva civil procedure AI disclosure findings in C255 and C257, which addressed court procedural questions in civil litigation — this finding addresses a specific judicial formation (TAC) issuing substantive AI administrative decisions that are currently invisible as a body of precedent.

**Classification:** Net-new finding | **Source:** Geneva market signals, regulatory context review | **Distinct from:** C255 Finding 01 (civil procedure discovery-to-filing shift), C257 Finding 04 (civil procedure AI evidence authentication)

### Finding 02: Geneva legal aid AI integration as compounded three-layer professional secrecy obligation

Geneva's state-funded legal aid program is beginning to incorporate AI tools in delivering legal services to aided clients. Attorneys providing aided services face three simultaneous and non-harmonized obligation sets: the cantonal legal aid contract requirements, Art.321 CP professional secrecy obligations, and nFADP data processing obligations. The intersection of these three for AI use in aided matters is uncharted and unaddressed. There is no published Geneva protocol for what a legally aided attorney must document about AI use, how data subject rights are handled in the legal aid context, or how the three-layer obligation collision is resolved when a data subject request arrives for an aided client's matter. This is distinct from general law firm nFADP questions because the state legal aid contract creates an additional accountability relationship — toward the canton — that private retainer agreements do not. No Geneva bar working group, no legal aid administration communication, and no Geneva advisory service has addressed this specific intersection.

**Classification:** Net-new finding | **Source:** Geneva market signals, compliance context review | **Distinct from:** C259 Finding 06 (client nFADP data rights in commercial context), C257 Finding 02 (nFADP small-business exemption for professional services firms)

### Finding 03: Geneva arbitration AI disclosure asymmetry as unnamed highest-stakes governance gap

Geneva's concentration of international commercial arbitration creates a specific AI governance gap that is distinct from domestic court procedural questions in both stakes and structure: counsel's AI use in preparing arbitral submissions, witness briefs, or expert reports has no disclosed framework for whether, how, or when disclosure to the tribunal, opposing counsel, or the ICC/WIPO/HKMC secretariat is required. Arbitral rules are contractually defined, tribunal confidentiality expectations differ from domestic court rules, and inadvertent disclosure consequences are higher in an international commercial context. No Geneva bar working group, no arbitral institution seated in Geneva, and no Geneva advisory service has produced a framework for this specific scenario. Geneva attorneys operating in international arbitration are the highest-stakes context in which Geneva attorneys face the most significant AI governance gap — and no Geneva market participant has named it.

**Classification:** Net-new finding | **Source:** Geneva market signals, competitive analysis | **Distinct from:** C258 Finding 01 (international arbitral tribunal dual-framework compliance obligation), C258 Finding 06 (attorney research query trail privilege architecture)

### Finding 04: Geneva SBA AI complaint precedent trajectory as invisible operative standard-formation mechanism

The Geneva bar (SBA) has begun receiving and resolving AI-related professional conduct complaints. Complaint outcomes are not published. This is distinct from C258's finding on bar structural advisory void, which addressed the absence of formal guidance — this finding addresses the informal standard-formation mechanism operating through complaint resolution that is making that void more dangerous.

### Finding 05: Revised CCP Art.142 AI-assisted filing certification gap as procedurally defined but substantively undefined obligation

The revised Swiss Code of Civil Procedure (January 2025) introduced updated provisions on electronic filing and instrument certification. ### Finding 06: nFADP small-business exemption applicability to law firms as planning-level unresolved question distinct from employment exception collision

The nFADP small-business exemption (Art.6 / Federal Act provisions) exempts data processing that is "ancillary" (auxiliaire) to a primary activity that is not data processing. For a law firm, the primary activity is legal services — which is, substantively, client data processing. The exemption's threshold applicability to professional services firms where data processing is not incidental but constitutive of the primary activity is a planning-level question with no published resolution. This is distinct from: (a) the nFADP employment exception collision identified in C261 — which addressed whether the employment exception covers AI processing in attorney work product preparation; (b) the Art.9/Art.321 CP structural collision identified in C251 and C258 — which addressed third-party processor authorization; and (c) the nFADP small-business exemption ambiguity identified in C257 — which addressed the general question without isolating the law firm applicability specific. This finding isolates the specific planning question that determines whether Geneva law firms are outside nFADP scope entirely or fully subject to it, with all the consequent documentation and obligation implications that follow from each answer.

**Classification:** Net-new finding | **Source:** Compliance context review | **Distinct from:** C261 Finding 01 (employment exception collision), C257 Finding 02 (nFADP small-business exemption ambiguity — general), C251 Finding 02 (Art.9/Art.321 CP structural collision)
