## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Carrier attestation signature without comprehension**
Attorneys signing carrier AI governance attestations without understanding what the signature commits them to. The market has normalized signing documents whose content is not fully understood.

**Signal 2: AI model update notification ignored or unprocessed**
Model updates create notification obligations that attorneys do not have systems to process. The notification is received and ignored or received and not understood. The update creates a governance gap that is invisible until an incident occurs.

**Signal 3: AI-generated client content review obligations undefined**
The review standard for AI-generated content provided by clients is undefined. The attorney does not know what adequate review of AI-generated content means, or what their obligations are when adopting client-provided AI content.

**Signal 4: Bar guidance adequacy gap for specific practice contexts**
Bar guidance is accurate at the general level but inadequate for specific practice contexts. The gap between general guidance and specific practice requirements is a structural problem that the bar has not resolved.

**Signal 5: Board-level AI governance questions without internal expertise**
Boards asking AI governance questions that in-house counsel cannot answer with confidence. The questions are legitimate and the expertise to answer them is not available internally.

**Signal 6: Supervisory review standard for AI-assisted work undefined**
Senior attorneys reviewing AI-assisted work product have no defined standard for what adequate supervisory review means. The review looks complete but the adequacy of the review is not verifiable.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Carrier attestation comprehension framework**
No framework exists for understanding what carrier AI governance attestations actually commit the firm to. The gap between signing and comprehending is structural and unmanaged.

**Gap 2: AI model update processing system**
No system exists for processing AI model update notifications in a way that produces actionable governance response. The notification processing gap is unaddressed.

**Gap 3: AI-generated client content review protocol**
No defined review protocol for AI-generated content provided by clients. The protocol would specify what the attorney's obligations are when adopting, modifying, or rejecting client-provided AI content.

**Gap 4: Practice-specific AI governance guidance**
Bar guidance that is specific to practice context, not just general AI governance principles. The specificity requires understanding how AI affects specific practice areas.

**Gap 5: Board-level AI governance communication**
Communication framework for translating AI governance requirements into board-level language. Boards need different framing than practitioners. The translation is a distinct skill.

**Gap 6: Supervisory review adequacy standard**
Defined standard for what adequate supervisory review of AI-assisted work product means. The standard would specify what the supervising attorney must verify and document.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**SVA professional competence obligations:**
The Swiss Lawyers Association expects professional competence that now implicitly includes AI governance competence. The competence standard is undefined, creating exposure for attorneys who cannot demonstrate adequate AI governance practice.

**nFADP Article 21 AI-assisted decision explanation:**
Attorneys using AI for decisions affecting data subjects must explain the AI logic in intelligible form. The explanation obligation is active and undefined at the boutique practice level.

**FDPIC enforcement pattern observation:**
FDPIC enforcement patterns are observable through informal circuit intelligence. The patterns reveal enforcement priorities that formal guidance does not communicate. Attorneys who can read the patterns have a governance advantage.

**Bar association provisional guidance:**
The Geneva bar's provisional AI guidance is being treated as final by practitioners who need definitive guidance. The provisional nature creates a specific problem for attorneys trying to establish adequate governance standards.

**Carrier attestation legal effect:**
Carrier AI governance attestations create legally binding commitments. The legal effect of the attestation signature is not fully understood by the attorneys signing them. The gap between signature and legal effect is a specific compliance exposure.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Carrier attestation signature without comprehension as structural market failure**
Attorneys signing carrier AI governance attestations without understanding what the signature commits them to is a structural market failure. The failure is invisible because the attestation is signed, the coverage is assumed, and the gap is not discovered until an incident occurs. No Geneva-based framework addresses this specific problem.

**Finding 2: AI model update notification processing as unnamed governance obligation**
The obligation to track and process AI model updates is unnamed in professional guidance. The notification creates a governance debt that accumulates without any system for addressing it. The unnamed obligation is distinct from the obligation to have a policy — it is the obligation to maintain a living policy that responds to changes in the tools in use.

**Finding 3: AI-generated client content review protocol as undefined professional standard**
The review standard for AI-generated content provided by clients for attorney adoption is undefined. The attorney who reviews, adopts, and files client-provided AI content is making a representation about the content that has no defined standard. The undefined standard is a specific professional liability exposure.

**Finding 4: Bar guidance adequacy gap for specific practice contexts as structural problem**
Bar guidance that is accurate at the general level but inadequate for specific practice contexts creates a structural problem. Attorneys following general guidance may be following it in a way that does not address their specific exposure. The gap between general and specific is where the liability lives.

**Finding 5: Board-level AI governance communication as distinct advisory skill**
The ability to translate AI governance requirements into board-level language is a distinct advisory skill. Most attorneys can explain AI governance at the practitioner level. Few can explain it at the board level in a way that produces informed decision-making rather than documentation of compliance.

**Finding 6: Supervisory review adequacy standard as undefined personal liability surface**
Senior attorneys reviewing AI-assisted work product have no defined standard for what adequate supervisory review means. The undefined standard is a personal liability surface. The review looks complete but the adequacy is not verifiable, and the liability for inadequate review falls on the supervising attorney personally.
