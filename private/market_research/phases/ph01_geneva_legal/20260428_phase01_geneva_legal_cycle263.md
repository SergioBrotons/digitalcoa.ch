## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems

**The AI research tool confabulation problem is arriving in Geneva small-firm practice without any detection methodology.** Managing partners using AI research tools are encountering case law results that are plausible in format but may be substantively inaccurate or fabricated. The problem is not new in legal AI literature, but it is now a present-tense operational problem for small Geneva firms that do not have senior associate-level research verification infrastructure. The attorney cannot efficiently verify every AI research output, and no Geneva service has developed a specific verification protocol calibrated to small-firm practice constraints.

**The research query trail has left the firm's infrastructure and is subject to no audit obligation the firm is aware of.** When an attorney enters a privileged research query into a non-Swiss AI research tool, the query metadata — including what the attorney was looking for, when, and in what context — is retained by the provider. The query metadata may reveal analytical strategy, client matter substance, and case theory. Article 321 CP protects communications and work product; it does not protect query metadata stored in vendor infrastructure outside Swiss jurisdiction. The problem is compounded by the fact that the attorney did not make a conscious decision to share this information — it was operationally generated by the research workflow.

**Opposing counsel AI is beginning to reconstruct analytical strategy from filed AI-assisted briefs.** Commercial disputes in Geneva are becoming the first context where attorneys are aware that opposing parties are using AI systems to analyze their filed documents. The AI-assisted brief may contain stylistic, structural, and strategic markers that can be inferred by adversarial AI analysis. The problem is distinct from traditional legal strategy confidentiality: it involves machine inference at a granularity that human reading would not achieve. No Geneva attorney has a protocol for managing what their AI-assisted filings reveal about their analytical approach.

### Failure Modes

**Geneva attorneys are receiving nFADP Art.13/21 data subject access requests that, if complied with, would require disclosing AI-generated analytical content about the requester that falls under Article 321 CP.** The structural collision is not hypothetical. It is arriving through legal services relationships as clients become more technically aware. A former client's Art.13 request for "all data held about them by the firm" may cover AI-generated case assessments, inference content, and analytical positions that are attorney work product. Complying discloses privileged material. Not complying creates nFADP liability. No Geneva protocol exists for navigating this collision, and the requests are compounding.

**The carrier's third-generation questionnaire is setting the de facto operative standard for AI governance, not reflecting the bar's position.** The questionnaire asks specific questions about review protocols, configuration documentation, and output verification. The answers attorneys provide create documented representations about their AI practice. These representations are establishing what "adequate" means in practice — through insurance underwriting criteria, not through bar guidance. The consequence is that the operative standard for AI governance in Geneva small firms is partly set by commercial underwriting instruments, which are not designed as normative professional standards and which are not subject to the same procedural protections as bar guidance.

**Geneva attorneys using AI legal aid tools in state-funded matters are creating AI-processed records of legally aided client data without any published protocol for the three-layer obligation intersection.** The cantonal legal aid contract, Article 321 CP professional secrecy, and nFADP data processing obligations create three non-harmonized requirement sets. Attorneys are making individual decisions about AI use in aided matters without knowing whether their decisions satisfy any of the three frameworks simultaneously. The problem is specifically Geneva's because the cantonal legal aid program is active and the intersection has not been analyzed.

### Paradoxes

**The Geneva bar's guidance vacuum is creating an asymmetry where carrier questionnaires are more operationally specific than bar guidance, making insurance underwriting the de facto guidance mechanism.** Carriers are asking specific questions with specific meanings. The bar is communicating general principles without operational content. The managing partner reads the carrier questionnaire — because it is concrete and because the consequence of an inadequate answer is concrete — as the actual governance standard. The result is that the most specific AI governance guidance many Geneva small-firm attorneys have encountered is coming from a commercial insurance form, not from their professional association.

**Geneva attorneys are simultaneously concerned about AI confabulation and unable to verify AI research output at the scale required.** The problem is not that attorneys don't know AI can produce inaccurate results. The problem is that verifying every research output against primary sources is more time-intensive than the AI efficiency it was meant to provide. The economic structure that makes AI research attractive is the same structure that makes thorough verification economically impractical. The paradox is that the more efficiently the AI is used, the less verified the research output is likely to be.

**The EU AI Act creates compliance obligations for Geneva firms serving EU clients, but no Geneva advisory service has mapped the specific intersection of EU AI Act high-risk obligations and Swiss domestic professional obligations.** Geneva law firms serving EU-based clients may be deploying AI systems that qualify as high-risk AI systems under the EU AI Act. The EU AI Act conformity assessment, technical documentation, and human oversight obligations intersect with Swiss professional conduct rules in ways that are not simultaneously satisfiable through any published framework. Geneva firms serving EU clients are navigating this intersection without a map, and the problem is specifically Geneva's given the city's EU client concentration.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### What Others Say

The Geneva legal AI governance market is currently operating in three modalities: carrier questionnaires (setting operative standards through commercial underwriting), bar-sponsored introductory webinars (attendance-based credentialing), and legal technology vendor marketing (framing AI governance as a feature problem). None of these modalities addresses the specific structural exposures identified in this cycle.

**Carrier questionnaires** are the most operationally specific signal in the market. They are specific in a way that bar guidance is not. But they are designed as underwriting instruments, not as professional governance guidance. They set the operative standard for what "adequate" means for the firms that have completed them — but they do not explain what adequate substantively requires, and they are not designed to discharge professional conduct obligations.

**Bar-sponsored webinars** measure attendance, not competence or practice adequacy. They do not distinguish between an attorney who attended one introductory seminar and an attorney who has implemented a governance system. The CE credentialing function and the practice adequacy function are served by the same mechanism, which satisfies the former while leaving the latter unaddressed.

**Legal technology vendor marketing** frames AI governance as a product selection problem. Vendors address feature functionality, efficiency gains, and ease of adoption. They do not address personal liability exposure, the bar advisory void, the nFADP Art.13/21 collision with professional secrecy, or the carrier attestation coverage question.

### Gaps

The solution is not simply "use a Swiss provider" (which may not be feasible) but a protocol for managing query metadata, provider selection criteria, and disclosure risk mitigation. No Geneva service has developed this as a specific product.

**No Geneva market participant has developed a defensibility mapping methodology for the carrier questionnaire.** The questionnaire asks about review protocols, configuration documentation, and output verification. The gap between what attorneys write in their responses and what would constitute a substantively adequate practice is not visible. A methodology for mapping the gap between questionnaire representations and actual practice adequacy — specifically designed for two-to-five attorney operations — does not exist in the market.

**No Geneva market participant has addressed the EU AI Act intersection with Swiss professional obligations for Geneva small firms serving EU clients.** The specific question of how EU AI Act high-risk system obligations interact with Article 321 CP professional secrecy, SVA/ORDRE professional conduct rules, and nFADP obligations simultaneously has not been mapped for the small-firm context. This is distinct from general EU AI Act coverage because it requires specific analysis of the intersection rather than general awareness of the regulation.

**No Geneva market participant has developed a protocol for managing what AI-assisted filings reveal about analytical strategy when opposing parties use adversarial AI analysis.** The adversarial AI pattern-reconstruction problem — where opposing AI systems analyze filed briefs to infer analytical strategy — is not being addressed as a discrete governance concern. Traditional legal strategy confidentiality assumes human reading. The machine-inference dimension of what AI-assisted filings reveal is a different problem requiring different protocols. No Geneva market participant has named this or developed guidance for it.

### What No One Is Addressing

**The research query trail as attorney work product that has operationally left Swiss jurisdiction.** When a Geneva attorney uses a non-Swiss AI research platform, the query metadata is retained by the provider. This metadata may reveal what the attorney was investigating, in what sequence, with what priority ordering. For a contested matter, this is analytically sensitive. The Article 321 CP protection framework has no published answer for how to handle query metadata that has been operationally generated and retained outside Swiss infrastructure. The problem is structurally distinct from data residency because the issue is not where client data sits, but where privileged analytical activity metadata sits.

**The carrier questionnaire as normative instrument establishing the de facto operative standard.** The carrier's third-generation questionnaire is functioning as a governance standard-setting instrument without the procedural properties of a standard. It was not developed through professional consultation, does not carry bar endorsement, and is not subject to appeal or revision through professional channels. It is, however, the most specific and operationally consequential AI governance document most Geneva small-firm attorneys have encountered. No market participant has analyzed the carrier questionnaire as a normative instrument, which it effectively is for the firms that have completed it.

**The nFADP Art.13/21 and Art.321 CP structural collision as a present-tense compliance impossibility for specific Geneva practice scenarios.** The collision between data subject access rights and professional secrecy obligations is not a hypothetical future risk. It is a present-tense problem for attorneys who have used AI systems to process client data and who are beginning to receive Art.13/21 requests from current and former clients. The structural impossibility of simultaneously satisfying both obligations in certain scenarios requires a protocol that no Geneva market participant has developed.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva / Swiss Specific Obligations

### SVA / ORDRE — Research Query and AI Tool Disclosure

The Geneva bar has communicated that AI competence is a professional obligation, but has not addressed the specific question of what disclosure is required when using AI research tools in matter preparation. The question of whether research query metadata constitutes work product under Article 321 CP — and whether that work product is protected when retained by a non-Swiss AI provider — has not been addressed by any ORDRE communication. The ODAGE working group curriculum covers introductory AI concepts, not research query privilege management.

The Geneva bar's AI complaint mechanism (SBA) is receiving complaints but not publishing outcomes. The de facto operative standard is forming through complaint resolution without publication. Attorneys who have not faced a complaint cannot evaluate what the bar is treating as actionable.

### nFADP — Art.13/21 and Art.321 CP Structural Collision

Article 13 (data access) and Article 21 (automated decision explanation) create data subject rights that intersect with professional secrecy obligations in legal practice in ways that are not resolved by any published FDPIC guidance. For a law firm, the data processed includes client communications, matter files, AI-generated analytical content, and research query metadata. A data subject access request for AI-generated content about the requester may require producing AI-inferred assessments, analytical positions, and query context that constitute attorney work product under Article 321 CP.

The structural collision is compounded by the fact that the nFADP data controller obligations and the Art.321 CP professional secrecy obligations do not share a common resolution mechanism. Complying with one may breach the other. The Geneva bar has not published a protocol for navigating this collision. No FDPIC guidance has addressed the intersection for legal services specifically.

### FDPIC — AI Research Tool Enforcement Trajectory

The Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner has not issued guidance on the AI research query trail question — specifically, whether query metadata retained by non-Swiss AI providers constitutes personal data processing within FDPIC's supervisory scope, and what Swiss attorneys are required to do about it. FDPIC's enforcement trajectory for professional services firms remains oriented toward sectors with clearer published priorities. The specific exposure for attorneys whose research queries are retained by non-Swiss providers outside their knowledge or control has not been articulated.

### Swiss Civil Procedure — AI Authentication Protocol

The revised Swiss Code of Civil Procedure (January 2025) introduced updated electronic filing provisions. The specific question of what an attorney certifies when certifying an AI-assisted filing — particularly whether the certification covers the AI-generated content and what "human review" means as a certification element — remains unaddressed by any Geneva court practice directive. Geneva courts have not issued practice guidance on AI-assisted filing certification requirements. The question arises in both domestic Geneva proceedings and international commercial cases before Geneva courts.

### Geneva Legal Aid — Three-Layer Obligation Intersection

Geneva's state-funded legal aid program (aide juridique) operates under the cantonal legal aid contract alongside Article 321 CP professional secrecy and nFADP. The three-layer intersection for AI use in aided matters has not been analyzed in any published protocol. Attorneys providing aided services have no guidance on AI tool selection criteria, AI use documentation requirements, client data subject rights handling, or how the three frameworks interact when a data subject request arrives for an aided client's matter. The legal aid administration has not published AI-specific guidance for aided attorneys.

### EU AI Act — Intersection with Swiss Professional Obligations for Geneva Firms Serving EU Clients

Geneva law firms serving EU-based clients may be deploying AI systems that qualify as high-risk AI systems under EU AI Act Annex III. The conformity assessment, technical documentation, human oversight, and transparency obligations for high-risk AI systems intersect with Swiss professional conduct rules — Article 321 CP professional secrecy, SVA/ORDRE guidance — in ways that create potential conflicts. No published framework addresses how these simultaneous obligations are navigated in a Swiss domestic law firm context serving EU clients. The problem is specifically Geneva's given the city's concentration of EU commercial clients.

### Finding 01 — The Research Query Trail as Attorney Work Product That Has Operationally Left Swiss Jurisdiction

When a Geneva attorney enters a privileged research query into a non-Swiss AI research platform, the query metadata — including analytical priority ordering, case theory development sequence, and matter-specific research context — is retained by the provider outside Swiss infrastructure. Article 321 CP protects attorney work product and privileged communications. It does not have a published framework for query metadata that was operationally generated through a research workflow and retained outside the firm's infrastructure without a conscious disclosure decision. The protection gap is structurally distinct from data residency because the issue is not where client case data sits, but where privileged analytical activity metadata sits. No Geneva service has developed this as a discrete exposure category, and no Geneva bar communication has addressed the specific intersection of query metadata and professional secrecy. The managing partner may be unable to explain, in a dispute or examination, what happened to the research trail for every AI-assisted matter currently in progress.

### Finding 06 — The GenAI Confabulation Authenticity Crisis in Case Law Research Creating Unquantified Legal Accuracy Exposure
