## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Carrier attestation signature as ritual without comprehension**
Attorneys signing carrier AI governance attestations as a condition of coverage. The signature is a ritual. The comprehension of what the attestation commits the firm to is absent. The market has normalized the ritual.

**Signal 2: AI model update notification as unnamed obligation**
The obligation to track and process AI model updates is unnamed in professional guidance. The notification creates governance debt that accumulates without a system for addressing it. The obligation exists whether or not it is named.

**Signal 3: Supervisory review adequacy undefined at boutique scale**
Senior attorneys reviewing AI-assisted work product have no defined standard for what adequate supervisory review means. The standard does not exist at any scale, but it is especially absent at boutique scale where the senior reviewer may be the only reviewer.

**Signal 4: Bar guidance accurate but not applicable to specific practice contexts**
The bar's AI guidance is accurate at the general level. It is not applicable to specific practice contexts. The gap between accurate and applicable creates a situation where attorneys following guidance may still have exposure specific to their practice that the guidance does not address.

**Signal 5: Board-level AI governance questions without internal capacity to answer**
Boards asking AI governance questions that in-house counsel cannot answer with confidence. The questions are legitimate and the expertise to answer them is not available internally. The gap is structural at small organizations.

**Signal 6: Client-arriving-with-AI-tools as new demand pattern**
Clients arriving with their own AI tools and specific questions about the firm's AI methodology. The demand pattern is new and increasing. The firm's capacity to respond credibly has not developed at the same pace as client expectations.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Carrier attestation comprehension framework**
No framework exists for understanding what carrier AI governance attestations actually commit the firm to. The gap between signing and comprehending is structural and unaddressed.

**Gap 2: AI model update tracking system**
No system exists for processing AI model update notifications in a way that produces actionable governance response. The notification processing gap is unaddressed.

**Gap 3: Supervisory review adequacy standard**
Defined standard for what adequate supervisory review of AI-assisted work product means. The standard would specify what the supervising attorney must verify and document.

**Gap 4: Practice-specific AI governance guidance**
Bar guidance specific to practice context, not just general AI governance principles. The specificity requires understanding how AI affects specific practice areas.

**Gap 5: Board-level AI governance communication**
Framework for translating AI governance requirements into board-level language. The translation is a distinct skill that most attorneys do not have.

**Gap 6: Client AI methodology response protocol**
Protocol for responding to client questions about AI methodology in a way that is both accurate and credentialing. The protocol would specify what to say, what not to say, and how to present limitations credibly.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**SVA professional competence and AI:**
The Swiss Lawyers Association expects professional competence that implicitly includes AI governance competence. The competence standard is undefined, creating exposure for attorneys who cannot demonstrate adequate AI governance practice.

**nFADP Article 21 AI-assisted decision explanation:**
Attorneys using AI for decisions affecting data subjects must explain the AI logic in intelligible form. The explanation obligation is active and undefined at the boutique practice level.

**FDPIC enforcement pattern observation:**
FDPIC enforcement patterns are observable through informal circuit intelligence. The patterns reveal enforcement priorities that formal guidance does not communicate.

**Bar association provisional guidance limitation:**
The Geneva bar's provisional AI guidance is being treated as final by practitioners who need definitive guidance. The provisional nature creates a specific problem for attorneys trying to establish adequate governance standards.

**Carrier attestation legal effect:**
Carrier AI governance attestations create legally binding commitments. The legal effect of the attestation signature is not fully understood by the attorneys signing them.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Carrier attestation signature without comprehension as structural market failure**
Attorneys signing carrier AI governance attestations without understanding what the signature commits them to. The failure is invisible until an incident occurs. No Geneva-based framework addresses this specific problem.

**Finding 2: AI model update notification processing as unnamed governance obligation**
The obligation to track and process AI model updates is unnamed in professional guidance. The notification creates governance debt that accumulates without any system for addressing it. The obligation exists whether or not it is named.

**Finding 3: Supervisory review adequacy undefined at boutique scale**
Senior attorneys reviewing AI-assisted work product have no defined standard for what adequate supervisory review means. The undefined standard is a personal liability surface. The review looks complete but the adequacy is not verifiable.

**Finding 4: Bar guidance accurate but not applicable to specific practice contexts**
General guidance that does not address specific practice contexts creates a structural problem. The gap between general and specific is where the liability lives.

**Finding 5: Board-level AI governance communication as distinct advisory skill**
The ability to translate AI governance requirements into board-level language is a distinct skill. Most attorneys can explain at practitioner level. Few can explain at board level in a way that produces informed decision-making.

**Finding 6: Client-arriving-with-AI-tools as new demand pattern with no response protocol**
Clients arriving with their own AI tools and specific questions about the firm's AI methodology. The demand pattern is new and increasing. No response protocol exists. The protocol would specify what to say, what not to say, and how to present limitations credibly.
