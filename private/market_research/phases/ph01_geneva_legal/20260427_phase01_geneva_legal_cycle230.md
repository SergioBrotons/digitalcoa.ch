## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

**Signal 1 — The Governance Policy Investment Has Produced a Document, Not a Governed Practice**
The firms that invested most seriously in AI governance — that commissioned policies, that engaged with the problem — are discovering that the documented governance structure does not solve the operational problem. The policy is a point-in-time document describing intended practice. It does not capture real-time AI use decisions, it does not track actual practice against the standard it sets, and it does not provide a record of what was actually done versus what was intended. The investment in governance documentation has produced a document. The document has not produced governance. This is creating a specific new anxiety: the firm that did the work is not in a materially better position than the firm that did not, because the governance question was never really about the document.

**Signal 2 — Incremental AI Adoption Has Outpaced Governance Policy Coverage**
The AI tool inventory at small firms is growing through incremental adoption — junior attorneys introducing tools, practice needs driving new deployments, AI capabilities expanding faster than governance processes can track. The governance policy written six or twelve months ago covers the tools known at that time. The tools adopted since then are not covered. The policy and the actual AI inventory have drifted apart, and the firm with a governance policy may have less visibility into current AI use than a firm without one — because the policy creates the false impression that AI use is being systematically managed when it is actually proliferating outside the governance framework.

**Signal 3 — AI Disclosure Sequencing Is the Operational Question No Policy Resolves**
The question "was AI used on this matter" is not a single question. It is a sequence of questions with different answers depending on the type of AI use, the data processed, the client's reasonable expectations, the applicable obligations, and the disclosure trigger. The policy that says "AI use will be disclosed to clients" does not resolve this sequence. The attorney who wants to give the right answer — not over-disclose, not under-disclose, disclose appropriately — is making a sequence of judgment calls that no documented guidance can answer in real time. The disclosure question is the highest-stakes version of the problem the policy cannot solve.

**Signal 4 — Vendor Term Updates Are Professional Obligation Triggers With No Translation Layer**
AI vendors update terms continuously. Each update may change data processing characteristics, sub-processor relationships, jurisdiction, or feature behavior in ways that affect the attorney's professional obligations. The attorney receives a notification describing product changes. There is no translation layer between vendor product updates and professional obligation implications. The attorney who wants to stay current on how vendor updates affect privilege, confidentiality, and nFADP obligations must perform this translation independently, with no authoritative reference. The professional exposure from vendor updates accumulates silently until it surfaces in a specific inquiry.

**Signal 5 — Counterparty AI Governance Questionnaires Are a New Professional Practice Surface Without Standard Response Architecture**
The market is producing a new category of professional request: counterparty AI governance questionnaires sent as part of transaction due diligence, relationship management, or institutional accountability. These questionnaires are not formal discovery. They do not have a defined legal status. They feel significant. The attorney who receives one must decide what to answer, what to decline to answer, what to say in lieu of an answer, and what "no comment" communicates about the firm's AI governance posture. No Geneva guidance exists on how to navigate these requests, and the firm's response — or non-response — creates a record that may have consequences the firm has not considered.

**Signal 6 — Bar AI Guidance Authorship Gap Is Creating Guidance That Addresses the Wrong Layer**
The Geneva bar guidance on AI is written by attorneys who are not in day-to-day AI practice at the small firm level. The authorship profile — senior practitioners and committee members removed from operational AI decisions — produces guidance that is cautious, general, and operationally incomplete. The guidance addresses the questions that can be answered safely from a distance. It does not address the questions that arise in the Tuesday afternoon, at the specific matter, at the moment of decision. The gap between guidance authorship and practice reality is creating a class of decisions that the attorney must make without authoritative support, and the guidance that exists is not narrowing this gap.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Specific Regulatory Obligations

**SVA (Schweizerische Anwaltsverband / Swiss Lawyers Association) and ORDRE (Geneva Bar Association):**
- Neither the SVA nor the ORDRE has issued guidance on the governance policy-practice conformance monitoring question — specifically, what a small firm is expected to do to verify that actual AI practice conforms to documented governance in real time, and whether the existence of a documented policy creates any obligation to maintain conformance
- Neither body has addressed the AI tool inventory drift problem: when the governance policy does not reflect the actual tool inventory, whether the firm has any obligation to update the policy, and how frequently the policy is expected to track current AI use
- Neither body has provided operational guidance on the AI disclosure sequencing question — when disclosure is required versus voluntary, what constitutes adequate disclosure, and how disclosure should be documented in the matter file
- The SVA working group on AI continues to produce cautious, general guidance. The authorship profile of the working group does not include small firm practitioners in day-to-day AI use. The resulting guidance has the credibility of an institutional source without the operational specificity that small firm practice requires
- The ORDRE has not addressed whether the existence of a documented AI governance policy creates any inference of deeper AI use than the policy describes — a question that matters when the policy and the actual practice have diverged

**nFADP (revised Federal Act on Data Protection, in force 2024, ordinance phase):**
- Art. 6 lawful basis requirements apply to all client data processed through AI tools in use at the firm — including tools adopted incrementally that are not reflected in the governance policy's AI tool inventory
- Art. 12 data subject rights to information about automated processing create disclosure obligations that are particularly acute in the legal context: the client who asks about AI use on their matter has a statutory right to information that the nFADP has not mapped to the attorney-client privilege context, creating genuine uncertainty about how Art. 12 applies to privileged matters
- The nFADP ordinance has not addressed the specific question of whether attorney work product and client file materials processed through AI tools require a different lawful basis analysis than other personal data processing — leaving the attorney in genuine uncertainty about whether standard AI tool lawful basis documentation is adequate for privileged client information
- Vendor term updates that change sub-processor relationships or data flows may affect the nFADP Art. 6 lawful basis validity for data processed through those tools — and the attorney has no systematic mechanism for monitoring whether vendor updates have this effect

**FDPIC (Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner):**
- The FDPIC's investigative pattern — system-level audits, complaint-driven investigations, periodic enforcement signals — means that a client complaint about AI data processing at a Geneva law firm could trigger an investigation into the firm's AI tool inventory, AI disclosure practices, and data residency documentation
- The attorney whose governance policy does not reflect the actual tool inventory — because of incremental adoption since the policy was written — faces a specific FDPIC investigation exposure: the inventory gap looks like inadequate governance even when the underlying AI use is not materially different from what the policy contemplated
- The FDPIC has not issued guidance on AI vendor term updates as a professional obligation trigger — meaning the attorney who continues to use a vendor whose terms have changed, without assessing the professional implications, may be proceeding without recognizing a material change in their compliance posture

**Bar Rules (Swiss Lawyers Act / BGFA; ORDRE disciplinary rules):**
- The confidentiality obligation (BGFA Art. 12 / ORDRE rules) applies to AI use, but has not been analyzed for the specific scenario where AI tool inventory drift means that the AI tools actually in use are not the AI tools described in the governance policy — the question of whether undisclosed AI tools in use constitutes a breach of the confidentiality obligation, or whether it constitutes a different category of professional failure
- The professional obligation to maintain adequate supervision applies to AI use, but has not been analyzed for the scenario where the supervising attorney does not know the full inventory of AI tools in use at the firm — creating a supervision gap that is invisible to the supervisor
- The ORDRE has not addressed whether the existence of a documented AI governance policy creates any positive obligation to maintain it current — or whether the policy's staleness, in the face of incremental AI adoption, creates any independent professional exposure
- Disciplinary precedent for AI-related failures at Geneva small firms remains limited — meaning the attorney navigating policy-practice drift, disclosure sequencing, and vendor update obligations is doing so without any published disciplinary reference point

## 06 — Findings Summary

### 6 Net-New Insights, Distinct from C214–C229 Phase 01

**Finding 1 — The Governance Documentation Investment Has Produced a Document, Not a Governed Practice**
The firm that invested in AI governance documentation has a point-in-time document describing intended practice. It does not have a mechanism for tracking actual practice against the standard the document sets. C214–C229 addressed governance documentation debt (C216, C218), governance conformance gap (C227), and governance without feedback (C227) — framing governance as accumulating debt or policy-practice divergence without naming the specific mechanism: the governance investment produced a document. The document has not produced governance because it was never designed as a conformance tracking instrument. It was designed as a policy statement. The firm that has the most sophisticated governance document may be in the worst position, because the document creates the false impression that governance is being maintained when it is not. *(Distinct from C214–C229: governance documentation investment creating false conformance impression, not governance debt, implementation failure, or conformance gap)*

**Finding 2 — Incremental AI Tool Adoption Has Outpaced Governance Policy Coverage at the Small Firm Level**
The AI tool inventory at small firms is growing through incremental adoption that governance processes cannot track. C214–C229 addressed vendor proliferation as a problem for wealth management firms (C215 Phase 02) and tool diversity in NGO contexts (C218 Phase 04), but framed it as a scale or inventory management problem. This finding identifies the specific consequence for small Geneva law firms: the governance policy written six or twelve months ago covers the tools known at that time. Tools adopted since then are not covered. The firm with a current governance policy may have less visibility into actual AI use than a firm without one — because the policy creates a reference point that is no longer accurate while appearing to be authoritative. *(Distinct from C214–C229: incremental adoption creating policy coverage drift, not vendor proliferation, tool diversity, or inventory management)*

**Finding 3 — AI Disclosure Sequencing Is the Operational Question No Governance Document Can Answer in Real Time**
The client question "was AI used on my matter" is not answerable with a yes or no. AI disclosure is a sequence of context-dependent judgment calls. C214–C229 addressed disclosure obligations from regulatory (C214, C215, C224, C225), client expectation (C227, C229), and counterparty (C226, C229) perspectives — framing them as obligations to be met through documentation or communication. This finding identifies the specific operational problem: no governance document can answer the disclosure sequencing question because the answer depends on the specific AI use, the specific client context, and the specific disclosure trigger — all of which vary in real time and cannot be anticipated in a policy. The attorney who wants to give the right answer on each disclosure occasion must make a judgment that no document resolves. *(Distinct from C214–C229: disclosure sequencing as real-time judgment sequence, not disclosure obligation or documentation requirement)*

**Finding 4 — Vendor Term Updates Are Professional Obligation Triggers That Accumulate Unmonitored Between Governance Reviews**
AI vendors update terms continuously. Each update may change professional obligations — data processing characteristics, sub-processor relationships, jurisdiction, privilege implications. C214–C229 addressed vendor term change detection failure (C222) as a contractual problem, model update silence as compliance erosion (C225) in wealth management, and vendor compliance claims without evaluation (C224 Phase 03, C226 Phase 03). This finding identifies the specific accumulation mechanism: vendor updates arrive between governance reviews, accumulate silently, and the attorney has no mechanism for assessing whether any given update changes their professional obligations. The nFADP Art. 6 lawful basis validity may be affected by vendor updates that the attorney has not tracked, creating a compliance position that was changed without the attorney's knowledge. *(Distinct from C214–C229: vendor term updates as accumulating professional obligation exposure, not vendor term change detection or compliance erosion)*

**Finding 5 — Counterparty AI Governance Questionnaires Are a New Professional Practice Surface Without Standard Response Architecture**
The growth of counterparty AI governance questionnaires in transaction and institutional contexts is creating a professional practice challenge that has no defined status, no standard response format, and no Geneva guidance. C214–C229 addressed counterparty AI governance DD as a market signal (C226 Phase 03, C228 Phase 02) and as a secondary regulatory mechanism (C226 Phase 05) — framing it as an external demand on the firm. This finding identifies the specific internal professional practice problem: the firm's response to these questionnaires — comprehensive, incomplete, or declined — creates a record with potential professional implications, and no one in the Geneva market has provided a response protocol. The attorney is improvising under conditions where the response is a professional act with uncertain status. *(Distinct from C214–C229: response protocol gap as professional practice problem, not counterparty DD as external demand signal)*

**Finding 6 — Bar AI Guidance Authorship Gap Is Creating Guidance That Operates at the Wrong Layer of Small Firm Practice**
The Geneva bar AI guidance is written by senior practitioners and committee members who are not in day-to-day AI use at the small firm level. C214–C229 addressed bar guidance principles-to-operations gap (C229) without identifying why the gap persists despite growing awareness. This finding identifies the specific structural cause: the guidance authorship profile produces guidance that addresses the questions that can be answered safely from senior distance. It does not address the questions that arise in the Tuesday afternoon, at the specific matter, at the moment of AI use decision. The gap is not a content gap — the bar has said things about AI. It is an authorship gap: the people who have the authority to produce guidance are not the people who are in the operational decisions the guidance is meant to govern. The guidance is authoritative and operationally incomplete simultaneously. *(Distinct from C214–C229: guidance authorship profile as structural cause of principles-operations gap, not gap existence or guidance absence)*
