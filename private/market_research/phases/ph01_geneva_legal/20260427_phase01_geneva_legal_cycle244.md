## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems and Failure Modes

**Hallucinated citations entering Geneva court records.**
AI legal research tools are producing fabricated case citations that are being submitted to Geneva courts. The problem is systemic — shared databases propagate false citations — and secondary verification is consuming efficiency gains that AI research was supposed to create. No Geneva court has published a remediation protocol. No Geneva bar has issued guidance on AI-assisted citation verification.

**Vendor liability disclaimers structurally incompatible with attorney professional obligations.**
AI tool vendors contractually disclaim all professional liability for tool outputs. The attorney bears full professional liability under Swiss law, the vendor bears none. The structural incompatibility is present in every vendor contract and has not been resolved in any published forum. The attorney signs the vendor's terms as a condition of access, absorbing liability without a resolution mechanism.

**CE credit market creating false adequacy signal on AI competence.**
Bar-organized continuing education programs measure attendance, not practice adequacy. Attorneys accumulate CE credits by attending AI-related sessions. The credits are counted, the competence to supervise AI-assisted legal work is not assessed. The gap between attendance credit and professional adequacy is recognized by sophisticated practitioners and unaddressed by any published framework.

**Bar guidance circuit adverse selection operating at full force.**
The firms most exposed to AI governance risk are structurally the least able to access bar guidance — they lack the time, the personnel, and the capacity to participate in working groups, consultations, and CE programs. The firms least exposed are the ones most present in guidance circuits. The result is guidance that addresses the conditions of the firms that need it least.

**Attorney departure AI configuration knowledge loss compounding annually.**
AI tool configurations are embedded in individual attorney practice — custom instructions, selected databases, configured output parameters. When attorneys depart, the configuration knowledge departs with them. No Geneva law firm has a designed protocol for preserving this institutional knowledge. The gap compounds with each annual departure cycle and is not visible until the moment of departure.

**Court AI disclosure expectations vary by judge with no published consistency.**
Geneva courts have not published procedural expectations for AI-assisted document disclosure. What attorneys experience is inconsistent — some judges expect disclosure, others do not ask, no published rule defines the minimum standard. The inconsistency is not random; it reflects different judicial assessments of a question that has not been resolved at the doctrinal level. Attorneys are managing this uncertainty without a map.

**Client-driven AI disclosure demand arriving before market has named the categories.**
Clients equipped with their own AI tools are arriving at Geneva law firms with specific questions: which model, what data residency, whether human review occurs, what the methodology is. This is a new demand pattern that emerged in 2025–2026. The questions are specific, the firm's capacity to answer them is not developed, and no Geneva market standard defines what a compliant answer looks like.

**Opposing counsel AI use as unresolved tactical and professional conduct question.**
Geneva attorneys have no framework for assessing whether opposing counsel's AI-assisted work product meets professional conduct standards. The question has two dimensions: whether the attorney's own use of AI creates conduct exposure, and whether opposing counsel's AI use is a litigation variable that should be actively assessed. Neither dimension has published resolution. The vacuum is being filled by informal peer speculation, not doctrine.

---

### Paradoxes and Complaints

- The attorney who has deployed AI most extensively over 2023–2025 has the most to explain when bar guidance eventually arrives — early adoption creates retrospective exposure, not strategic advantage.
- The firm with the most AI tools has the least documented governance because tool proliferation outpaced policy development — more tools, less control, greater exposure.
- The firm that seeks bar guidance most actively is the firm with the least AI exposure — firms with genuine exposure are managing it silently.
- AI research promised efficiency gains that are being offset by the secondary verification burden that hallucinated citations create — net efficiency neutral or negative.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### What Others Say

**Vendors** say AI enhances legal practice, improves efficiency, and is the future of legal research. Their terms of service disclaim all professional liability. Their marketing materials do not disclose that disclaimer prominently. The positioning is aspirational; the contractual reality is a full transfer of liability to the attorney.

**Bar associations** (SVA/ORDRE) have published discussion papers, organised working groups, and held informational sessions. The substantive AI practice rules remain in draft. Informal guidance is circulating in working group sessions and bar events. Firms not participating in those circuits do not have access to that guidance and do not know what they do not know.

**PI insurers** say AI-related claims are covered unless specifically excluded. Exclusion language is vague by design — written to preserve post-hoc interpretation room, not to inform policyholders. No Geneva insurer has published a clear AI coverage position. The ambiguity benefits the insurer, not the attorney.

**Consulting firms** offering AI governance services to law firms are addressing compliance documentation and policy frameworks — important but insufficient. The specific professional conduct question — what constitutes adequate supervision of an AI tool the supervisor cannot evaluate — is not being addressed by generalist governance advisors.

**Peer firms** are largely silent on their AI practices. The silence is not confidence; it is uncertainty about personal exposure. Geneva peer circuits that were previously forums for professional exchange have become liability-conscious environments where AI practices are not discussed.

### Gaps — What No One Is Addressing

**The supervision adequacy standard that does not exist.**
The core problem — what substantive review of AI-assisted work requires when the reviewing attorney cannot reconstruct the AI's reasoning — has no published resolution. The obligation is real (Art. 321a CP, professional supervision). The discharge mechanism is absent. No Geneva bar publication, no court decision, no scholarly commentary has named the specific elements of adequate AI supervision for a small firm context.

**The privilege waiver surface that is invisible.**
AI research query metadata — the query itself, not just the output — reveals work product. The metadata surface is not addressed in vendor terms, not covered in bar guidance, and not visible to the attorney until an adverse party reconstructs it. No Geneva firm has a published protocol for managing AI research query metadata as a privilege preservation measure.

**The AI tool selection decision treated as procurement, not professional conduct.**
AI tool selection in small Geneva law firms is typically made by the managing partner or an administrative decision-maker based on cost, features, and vendor representation. It is not subject to the professional conduct assessment that other vendor selections in legal practice are subject to. The decision that most determines the firm's AI accountability exposure is the decision receiving the least professional scrutiny.

**The cross-border privilege collision with no resolution pathway.**
When a Geneva attorney uses an AI tool processing data on servers outside Switzerland, the question of whether attorney-client privilege extends to that processing configuration has not been resolved by any Swiss authority. The question is live in practice — it arises every time a firm adopts a new AI tool — and there is no published resolution, no bar guidance, and no court precedent.

**The AI output metadata disclosure sequence before the attorney makes a decision.**
AI tool output arrives with embedded metadata — query context, session information, configuration parameters — that the attorney did not consciously control. Some of this metadata is structured to be included in shared documents by default. The disclosure sequence — what the attorney transmits to the client before making an informed disclosure decision — is not governed by any published protocol. The attorney may be disclosing information about their own work process that they do not know they are disclosing.

**The Geneva attorney AI peer accountability forum that does not exist.**
Peer consultation on AI governance questions would reduce individual firm exposure and build market-wide adequate practice. The condition for that exchange — a confidential, liability-protected forum — does not exist in Geneva. The structural reason it does not exist is that the same liability exposure that makes guidance necessary makes peer consultation legally risky. No institution has established the forum; no bar or carrier has provided the liability protection that would make it safe.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations

**SVA/ORDRE — Swiss Lawyers and Notaries Association**
The SVA (Schweizerische Vereinigung der Advokaten) and ORDRE (Ordre des Avocats de Genève) have an active AI working group. The working group has been circulating draft AI practice guidelines since 2025. The guidelines remain in draft. Informal direction is being communicated in working group sessions and bar events, which creates an information asymmetry: firms participating in those circuits have access to emerging guidance, firms not participating do not. The structural effect is that the most exposed firms — those with the least capacity to participate — are the least informed.

**nFADP — Revised Federal Act on Data Protection (2023, in force 2024)**
nFADP Article 8 (purpose limitation) and Article 21 (profiling) create specific obligations for AI-assisted legal work. Attorney-client files are personal data under nFADP. AI processing of client matters for legal research, document drafting, or case analysis constitutes data processing under nFADP. Purpose limitation requires that processing be limited to the specific purpose for which data was collected — which is distinct from the AI tool vendor's commercial purpose. Profiling Article 21 triggers explainability obligations when automated decisions affect the data subject — directly applicable when AI assists in legal outcomes. The FDPIC has not published guidance specific to legal professional data processing under nFADP. No bar publication has addressed the nFADP implications for AI-assisted legal practice.

**FDPIC — Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner**
The FDPIC has published general nFADP guidance and has enforcement authority. The FDPIC has not published guidance specific to legal professionals' AI use. The enforcement pattern of the FDPIC is systematic — they read tool configurations against Art. 21 profiling obligations. The gap between general guidance and specific legal professional application leaves Geneva attorneys without a published compliance baseline from the FDPIC.

**Professional Liability Insurance**
Swiss professional liability insurance for attorneys operates under general policy language that has not been updated for AI-specific exposures. The standard exclusion language covers "gross negligence" and "intentional misconduct." AI-specific failures — hallucinated citations in filed documents, AI-generated content that the attorney cannot verify, metadata disclosures the attorney did not authorise — fall into an ambiguous zone. The ambiguity benefits the insurer, not the attorney. No Geneva PI insurer has published a clear AI coverage position or a defined AI endorsement. The silent ambiguity is a structural exposure for every Geneva law firm.

**Geneva Court Procedure — No AI-Specific Disclosure Protocol**
Geneva civil procedure has no published rule on AI-assisted document disclosure. The procedural environment is operating in a gap — judges applying informal expectations that are not consistent across chambers. The absence of published procedure creates two problems: attorneys do not know what to disclose, and adverse parties cannot rely on consistent standards when challenging opposing AI use.

**EU AI Act — Client-Imposed Obligations**
EU-connected clients — companies operating under EU AI Act requirements — are beginning to impose AI governance conditions on their Swiss legal counsel. The EU AI Act's high-risk system requirements create documentation obligations for firms that provide legal services to EU clients. Swiss domestic law does not require this; EU client pressure creates the obligation independently. The obligation is present-tense for Geneva firms with EU-connected client portfolios and is not addressed in any published Swiss guidance.

**Attorney-Client Privilege — Cross-Border Data Processing**
Swiss attorney-client privilege (Art. 321 CP) protects client communications and file contents from disclosure. When an AI tool processes client data on servers outside Switzerland, the privilege question — does privilege cover the processing configuration and the data in transit? — is not resolved by any published authority. The question arises every time a firm adopts a new AI tool. No bar guidance, no court decision, no published legal opinion has resolved it.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: The AI review adequacy standard is undefined and structurally necessary.**
The core supervision obligation — what substantive review requires when the supervisor cannot evaluate the tool's reasoning — has no published resolution at the Geneva bar level. This is not a knowledge gap. It is a structural gap: the obligation is real, the discharge mechanism is absent, and the gap is present in every small firm that has deployed AI without a defined review protocol. The market has not named this as a discrete service category. DigitalCoa.ch naming it creates the entry point.

**Finding 2: AI tool selection is categorised as procurement when it is actually professional conduct.**
The decision that most determines a law firm's AI accountability exposure — which AI tool to deploy, with what configuration, for which matter types — is being made as an administrative or IT procurement decision, not as a professional conduct decision. The categorisation error means the wrong people are making the decision, against the wrong criteria, with the wrong accountability framework. No Geneva market actor has named this distinction or positioned against it.

**Finding 3: Client-driven AI disclosure demand has reversed the burden of proof before a standard exists.**
Geneva law firms now face clients arriving with specific AI methodology questions — model identification, data residency, human review evidence — that the firms are not equipped to answer because no Geneva market standard defines what a compliant answer looks like. The demand arrived before the supply of knowledge. The firms most equipped to answer these questions are those with the most developed AI governance documentation. The firms most exposed to inadequate answers are those with the least.

**Finding 4: AI research query metadata is the privilege surface nobody has mapped.**
The privilege waiver risk from AI research is not primarily in the output — it is in the query metadata. Query logs, session context, configuration parameters embedded in AI research tool outputs reveal work product that the attorney did not consciously decide to disclose. The metadata surface is structurally invisible: the attorney does not see it in normal workflow, the vendor does not prominently disclose it, and no Geneva protocol governs the sequence of what the attorney transmits before making an informed disclosure decision. This is a discrete, unnamed exposure that compounds daily.

**Finding 5: Geneva court AI disclosure inconsistency is creating an unquantifiable litigation variable with no published map.**
Geneva courts have not published procedural AI disclosure expectations. The inconsistency across judges is experienced by practitioners, not mapped in any institutional record. What is forming is an informal, judge-specific set of expectations that attorneys are discovering reactively — at the point of proceedings, not before. The asymmetry — some judges expecting disclosure, others not — means firms in identical positions face different procedural obligations depending on assignment. No Geneva publication has named this as a structural procedural phenomenon.

**Finding 6: The Geneva AI peer accountability forum that the market needs does not exist and cannot be created without institutional liability protection.**
Peer exchange on AI governance questions would reduce firm-level exposure and build the market's adequate practice standard. The structural barrier is that the same liability exposure that makes guidance necessary makes peer consultation legally risky for the individual attorney. No Geneva institution — bar, carrier, professional association — has established the liability-protected confidential forum that would enable this exchange. The market failure is not ignorance; it is the absence of the institutional infrastructure required to act on available knowledge. DigitalCoa.ch positioning cannot create this forum — but the recognition that the gap exists is a named market condition that framing can address.
