## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Policy-practice drift as the defining second-generation problem**
The firm has a governance policy. The policy has drifted from actual practice. The drift is not a policy failure — it is a structural feature of governance documentation that was written for an earlier state of AI practice. The gap between documented governance and actual AI use is where exposure lives.

**Signal 2: Judgment calls not recorded creating permanent blind spot**
Every real-time AI decision involves a judgment call. These judgment calls are not recorded. The policy does not capture them because the policy was written for a different moment. The accumulation of unrecorded judgment calls is a permanent blind spot.

**Signal 3: Incremental tool adoption outpacing policy update cycles**
AI tools are added to the firm's practice faster than the governance policy can be updated. The policy update cycle cannot keep pace with the tool adoption pace. The gap is structural.

**Signal 4: Client disclosure questions surfacing the conformance gap**
Clients are beginning to ask specific questions about how their matters are handled. The firm's answers expose the gap between what the policy describes and what the practice actually is. The client question is the external event that surfaces the conformance gap.

**Signal 5: Counterparty AI questionnaires testing conformance**
Counterparties — carriers, counterparties in transactions, opposing counsel — are asking AI governance questions in their questionnaires. The firm's responses reveal how far the practice has drifted from the documented governance. The questionnaire is a conformance test, not just an information request.

**Signal 6: Bar guidance authorship does not address the operational layer**
The bar guidance available addresses the policy layer. It does not address the operational layer where attorneys are actually making judgment calls about AI use. The guidance gap is structural and continuous.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Policy-practice conformance assessment**
No service exists that specifically assesses the gap between a firm's documented governance and its actual AI practice. The comparison between document and practice is the core diagnostic that no one is offering.

**Gap 2: Incremental tool adoption tracking system**
A system for tracking incremental AI tool adoption at small firm scale — capturing what was added, when, by whom, and for what purpose — does not exist as a discrete service. The inventory drift is the underlying cause of the conformance gap.

**Gap 3: Real-time judgment recording methodology**
A methodology for recording real-time AI judgment calls in a way that is operationally practical for an attorney during a busy workday does not exist. The recording has to be lightweight enough to actually happen.

**Gap 4: Client disclosure sequencing decision architecture**
A framework for making disclosure sequencing decisions — what to disclose, when, in what form, to what client — does not exist for the small Geneva firm. The decision is currently made reactively, case by case.

**Gap 5: Vendor term update professional obligation translator**
A service that monitors AI vendor term updates and translates them into specific professional obligation actions for the Geneva attorney does not exist. The professional implications of vendor updates are not being tracked.

**Gap 6: Counterparty AI questionnaire response preparation**
A service that prepares the firm to respond to counterparty AI governance questionnaires from its actual practice position — not from a policy document — does not exist. The response is being produced reactively.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**SVA/ORDRE obligations:**
The Swiss Lawyers Association guidance requires supervision adequacy and delegation appropriateness. AI tools adopted incrementally may fall outside the supervision scope the original delegation contemplated. The conformance gap creates a structural supervision adequacy question.

**nFADP Articles 6, 12, and 21:**
Article 6 requires a documented lawful basis for personal data processing. AI processing that occurred through tools adopted incrementally may lack a documented lawful basis under current nFADP standards. The retrospective exposure is structural.

**FDPIC enforcement patterns:**
The FDPIC has not issued Geneva-specific attorney guidance. The enforcement pattern is learnable from general enforcement activity. The pattern suggests that documentation of actual practice, not policy documentation, is what survives examination.

**BGFA professional obligations:**
The Federal Lawyers Act requires attorneys to maintain client confidentiality and professional competence. AI-assisted work that the attorney cannot evaluate may implicate the competence obligation. The conformance gap between documented governance and actual practice is evidence of a potential competence gap.

**Geneva cantonal bar:**
The cantonal bar requirements add obligations specific to client file management and matter supervision. AI tool use that is not systematically documented creates cantonal compliance gaps that the federal framework does not address.
