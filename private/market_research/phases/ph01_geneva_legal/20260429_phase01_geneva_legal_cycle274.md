## 02 — Market Signals

Current problems, failure modes, paradoxes, and complaints circulating in the Geneva legal market.

**Signal 01 — AI model deprecation creating retroactive accountability gaps for past matters with no Geneva recovery protocol.**
AI platforms update model versions without advance notice to subscribers. Research produced under Version A becomes unrecoverable when Version A is deprecated. Geneva attorneys who cited AI research in briefs filed in 2024–2025 are now unable to reproduce what their AI system retrieved at the time of filing. When opposing counsel challenges the research basis, or when a client question arrives about the brief's analytical foundation, the attorney cannot reconstruct the AI's output. This is a structurally different problem from AI confabulation (which is about AI producing wrong content) — this is about AI producing correct content that subsequently becomes unrecoverable through no fault of the attorney. No Geneva protocol exists for managing the deprecation event after the fact.

**Signal 02 — Post-error disclosure obligation has no Geneva-specific response protocol for AI-assisted errors.**
When an attorney discovers an AI-assisted error in a filed brief or client communication, no Geneva-specific protocol exists for: what to disclose to the client, what to disclose to opposing counsel or the court, whether and when to notify the carrier, what documentation to create in the response process, and what the consequences of different disclosure decisions are. The error correction question — what the attorney owes the client and when — is not defined. The silence is not neutral: attorneys are making disclosure decisions with professional liability consequences without a framework for what disclosure is required, optional, or contraindicated.

**Signal 03 — EU AI Act Annex III obligations flowing back to Geneva attorneys as undisclosed downstream service providers.**
EU-based clients are beginning to send questionnaires to their Swiss attorneys asking about AI Act compliance. The EU AI Act does not stop at the EU border for firms serving EU-based clients. Geneva attorneys providing legal services to EU-connected entities are receiving compliance obligations they cannot answer, because no published framework defines what "EU AI Act compliant legal services" means for a Swiss law firm. The downstream deployer obligation flows from client to attorney without a published standard for what the attorney must demonstrate.

**Signal 04 — Continuing education AI credits satisfying credential requirements while the substantive knowledge gap remains.**
Geneva attorneys are completing bar AI training requirements and recording credits. The available curriculum does not match the operational AI governance knowledge required for practice. Credits are earned against introductory content while the exposure comes from operational complexity the curriculum does not address. The credential requirement is satisfied; the knowledge gap is not closed. This is not a curriculum quality problem — it is a structural timing mismatch between when the credential is awarded and when the substantive exposure exists.

**Signal 05 — Carrier policy language undefined for AI-assisted errors while attestation commits the signatory to methodology they cannot verify.**
The gap between what the carrier's attestation requires and what the policy's coverage terms define has not been resolved. Managing partners are signing attestations describing AI review methodology while the policy document does not contain language defining coverage scope for AI-assisted errors. The product being purchased — professional liability coverage for AI-assisted practice — is not defined in the document governing the coverage. No Geneva market participant has published an interpretation of carrier policy language relative to AI-assisted errors.

**Signal 06 — Geneva civil procedure has no AI authentication protocol for disputed filings.**
When a Geneva attorney challenges whether an opposing party's filing was AI-assisted — or when the authenticity of an AI-assisted filing is contested — no procedural mechanism exists for resolving the question. There is no standard for what evidence establishes AI authorship, no burden-of-proof framework, no process for how the court evaluates authenticity challenges, and no consequence framework for what happens when AI authorship is established. The Federal Supreme Court AI copyright doctrine addresses copyright ownership, not procedural authentication of filed briefs. This is a distinct procedural vacuum with operative consequences for every commercial dispute in Geneva courts.

**Signal 07 — The documentation window on 2023–mid-2026 AI adoption period compounding toward irreversibility without firms knowing what they cannot reconstruct.**
The 2023–mid-2026 period is becoming permanently non-reconstructable as a structural fact. Model version changes, vendor terms restructurings, staff departures, and tool configurations accumulated without documentation architecture designed to preserve reconstructability. The compounding is invisible: firms believe they have the situation in hand because nothing acute has surfaced yet. The acute surfacing will arrive as a single event — carrier renewal, bar examination, client dispute — asking for documentation of what the firm's AI governance looked like at a specific date, with no documentation existing for that date.

## 04 — Compliance Context

Geneva and Swiss-specific regulatory obligations relevant to this ICP.

**SVA/ORDRE (Swiss Bar Association / Geneva Bar):**
SBA published AI guidelines in June 2024. Key obligations: attorney remains fully responsible for AI-assisted work; professional secrecy under Art.13 FMLA must be maintained; AI tools must comply with data protection requirements; attorneys must understand AI system capabilities and limitations; independent professional judgment must be exercised; clients should be informed about AI use where confidential information is involved or billing is affected. ORDRE has not published Geneva-specific guidance distinct from SBA guidelines. No bar advisory opinion mechanism for specific AI governance questions exists. The bar CE AI curriculum is introductory, records credits without closing substantive knowledge gaps. The structural void means attorneys are making consequential AI governance decisions without a path to authoritative guidance from their professional body.

**nFADP (Federal Act on Data Protection, revised September 1, 2023):**
FDPIC confirmed direct applicability of nFADP to AI-supported data processing (December 2024 guidance). Key obligations: transparency regarding AI system purpose, functionality, and data sources; data protection impact assessments for high-risk AI processing; data subject rights to object to automated processing and request human review of automated decisions significantly affecting them. nFADP applies to client data processing by law firms. The automated decision explanation obligation (Article 21) has no Geneva-specific discharge protocol for legal services. The EU AI Act Annex III high-risk classification may intersect with nFADP obligations for attorneys serving EU-connected clients — no published Swiss-law mapping of this intersection exists for small-firm context.

**FDPIC (Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner):**
FDPIC issued guidance on AI applicability (December 2024), confirming existing nFADP applies directly to AI systems. FDPIC has not published guidance addressing: the post-error notification obligation for AI-assisted errors discovered after filing, the deprecation accountability gap when AI research becomes unrecoverable, or the EU AI Act downstream deployer obligations flowing to Swiss attorneys. The post-error disclosure protocol question has no FDPIC authority guidance.

**Professional Liability Insurance:**
Mandatory for all Swiss attorneys. Coverage for AI-assisted errors remains architecturally undefined in published policy language from Swiss carriers. The gap between what the third-generation attestation requires (AI review methodology attestation signed personally by the managing partner) and what the policy defines as covered AI-assisted error is unresolved. No Geneva market participant has published an interpretation of carrier policy language relative to AI-assisted errors. Carrier denial creates operative precedent without appeal mechanism.

**Art.321 CP (Swiss Criminal Code — Professional Secrecy):**
Professional secrecy obligations apply to all client communications and work product. Post-error disclosure to the client creates specific tension with Art.321 CP: the attorney must disclose an error in the work product while maintaining professional secrecy obligations regarding the analytical methodology that produced the error. No Geneva protocol exists for balancing error disclosure obligations against professional secrecy when the error arose from AI-assisted analysis of privileged client matter. Swiss Federal Supreme Court is developing AI copyright doctrine relevant to what constitutes original AI-assisted work product.

**Geneva Civil Procedure:**
AI disclosure procedural status formally undefined. AI authentication, model version disclosure, and methodology explanation in discovery unpublished. No procedural mechanism exists for challenging the authenticity of an AI-assisted filing. No burden-of-proof framework for AI authorship authentication exists in Geneva civil procedure. The first contested AI-authorship challenge in a Geneva commercial court will create operative precedent through the response — before any published framework for attorneys to anticipate. No Geneva court practice directive defines what AI verification standard is required before filing.

**Federal Council AI Regulatory Approach (February 2025):**
Federal Council confirmed technology-neutral, sector-specific approach with targeted adjustments following AI Convention incorporation. No standalone comprehensive AI legislation. Federal AI Coordination Office established February 1, 2026 within Federal Chancellery as contact point. The EU AI Act Annex III high-risk system obligations flowing to Swiss attorneys through EU-connected clients are not addressed by Federal Council approach — the cross-border compliance obligation arrives through commercial relationships Bern formal process does not control.

**EU AI Act (for Geneva firms serving EU clients):**
Annex III high-risk AI system classification may apply to AI systems used by Geneva attorneys in contexts affecting EU data subjects. EU-based clients are beginning to flow compliance obligations back to their Swiss attorneys through downstream deployer questionnaires. No Geneva service has mapped what "EU AI Act compliant legal services" means for a Swiss law firm. Simultaneous compliance with EU AI Act obligations, Swiss professional obligations (Art.321 CP), and nFADP obligations creates a multi-jurisdictional compliance intersection with no published reconciliation methodology for small-firm context.
