## Deliverable 01 — ICP Language

### How Geneva Executives Describe Their Need for AI Governance Advice

**Primary Trigger: Documentation Continuity Collapse**

The executive who calls does not use the language of compliance. The language that surfaces in the first conversation is different — more immediate, more personal.

They describe a situation that was handled two or three years ago. A tool was selected, a policy was written, a signature was applied. The matter was checked off.

What they now describe is the sensation of opening a drawer and finding documentation that no longer corresponds to what the AI system actually does. The model has changed. The tool configuration has drifted. The person who wrote the document is no longer with the organization. The client relationship has moved to a different stage. The regulatory window has shifted.

The language that follows is consistent across sectors:

> *"We have something in place. I don't think it holds anymore."*

> *"The documentation was correct at the time. I can't stand behind it now."*

> *"I don't know what I would need to show. But I know what I have is not enough."*

> *« On a des documents. Ils ne correspondent plus à ce que le système fait maintenant. »*

> *« La documentation était correcte à l'époque. Je ne peux plus la défendre telle quelle. »*

> *« Je ne sais pas exactement ce qu'il faudrait montrer. Mais je sais que ce qu'on a ne suffit plus. »*

**Secondary Trigger: Approaching Accountability Moment**

A date is named. A board meeting. A client renewal. A carrier questionnaire. A counterparty due diligence round. An supervisory examination. Something with a date creates the call more reliably than something without one.

The executive is not calling because they are uncertain about the future. They are calling because a specific moment is approaching and they need to know what their documentation should look like when that moment arrives.

> *"The board meets in six weeks. I need to know what a defensible position looks like."*

> *"The counterparty questionnaire arrived Friday. I have ten days."*

> *« Le conseil se réunit dans six semaines. J'ai besoin de savoir à quoi ressemble une position défendable. »*

> *« Le questionnaire du donneur d'ordre est arrivé vendredi. J'ai dix jours. »*

**Expectation on Arrival**

Executives arriving at DigitalCoa.ch have already done preliminary work. They have read Brotons.net. They have spoken with peers in Geneva informal circuits. They arrive with a named problem — not an abstract governance concern.

Their expectation is not a training session. Their expectation is a specific answer to a specific situation.

> *"I need someone who knows Geneva. Not a framework. An answer."*

> *"I need to know what I should have built — and whether what I built comes close."*

> *« J'ai besoin de quelqu'un qui connaît Genève. Pas un cadre théorique. Une réponse. »*

> *« J'ai besoin de savoir ce qu'on aurait dû construire — et si ce qu'on a construit s'en approche. »*

**Distrust Markers**

The executives who call already distrust three things before the first conversation begins:

1. **Generic AI governance frameworks.** They have read the international frameworks. They understand that framework adequacy is not the same as their specific situation's defensibility.

2. **Compliance-oriented advisors.** They are not looking for someone to build a policy document. They are looking for someone who can sit in a room and tell them what they would actually need to show, to whom, under which conditions.

3. **Consultancy reports.** They have received documents that mapped their AI environment, catalogued their tools, and recommended next steps. The document was produced. Nothing changed. They distrust the producing of documents.

**What They Are Actually Looking For**

The underlying need behind the named need is accountability continuity — the ability to maintain a defensible position as conditions change, without starting from zero each time a new condition arrives.

The secondary need is interpretation confidence — knowing that the answer they have constructed would hold up when challenged by a counterparty, a regulator, a board, a carrier underwriter.

The tertiary need is discretion. They want a conversation that does not become a document that becomes a record that becomes an exhibit. They want advisory counsel, not another compliance artifact.

## Deliverable 02 — Market Signals

### Where Geneva Executive Demand Is Emerging

**Signal 1: Carrier Underwriting Third-Generation Questionnaire as Market Standard Probe**

The third-generation carrier questionnaire — now being deployed across the Geneva professional services market — represents the first systematic market mechanism probing the 2023–2024 early adoption period documentation. This is not a regulatory instrument. It is a market instrument.

The questionnaire probes four documentation categories: tool-specificity, model version tracking, use-case differentiation, and reassessment protocol. Firms that answered the first-generation questionnaire with policy documents are now being asked to produce evidence of process. The gap between policy and evidence is where demand concentrates.

The carrier underwriter is functioning as an informal standard-setter. The questions being asked are the market's answer to the question "what does adequate look like." Geneva firms are discovering that answer in real time, through the carrier renewal process, at predictable annual intervals.

**Signal 2: Counterparty Questionnaire Concentration in H1 2026**

Client AI governance questionnaires are arriving in concentrated bursts tied to client procurement and renewal cycles. The questionnaires carry specific, technically detailed questions that presuppose an AI governance infrastructure the Geneva boutique firm typically does not have.

The gap between what the questionnaire presupposes and what the firm has built is not a compliance gap — it is an infrastructure gap. The firm cannot answer the questionnaire not because it is non-compliant but because the infrastructure to generate the requested evidence does not exist.

This creates a specific advisory entry point: questionnaire response readiness. The service is not "help me answer the questionnaire." The service is "build the infrastructure that makes the questionnaire answerable." The distinction matters. The first is a document service. The second is a governance architecture service.

**Signal 3: Federal Council June 2026 Deliberation as Anticipatory Demand Window**

The Federal Council deliberation on AI governance regulation in June 2026 is functioning as a forward positioning signal. Executives are calling now — not in July, when the deliberation concludes — because they understand that the documentation window opens before the regulatory frame closes.

The executives who call in the pre-deliberation window are not asking what the Federal Council will decide. They are asking what their documentation should look like before the deliberation produces a market-formed standard. They are positioning for a standard that has not yet been set, using anticipation as their method.

This is a more sophisticated demand profile than the post-event trigger caller. Pre-deliberation callers represent the most sophisticated segment of the Geneva AI governance advisory market.

**Signal 4: Post-Supervisory Examination Revelation Pattern**

Executives who have recently undergone supervisory examination — carrier, FINMA-adjacent, or bar — arrive with a specific demand shape: they have seen what the examination actually looked for, and they need to understand the delta between what they had and what the examination actually required.

The examination revelation pattern has been consistent: firms believed they were prepared for documentation adequacy. The examination tested for evidence continuity — the ability to demonstrate that the governance position held across a period of changing conditions, tool updates, and personnel transitions.

This is structurally different from documentation existence. It is documentation maintenance over time. The market is learning this distinction through direct experience, not through published guidance.

**What Is Not Working in Advisory Positioning**

Generic AI governance training is not converting. The Geneva executive who needs AI governance advice is not looking for a course. They are looking for a specific answer to a specific situation.

Framework certification programs are not converting. The ISO 42001 certification pathway is being offered by multiple providers. Executives who have looked at certification have concluded that certification demonstrates process completion, not accountability adequacy. The distinction matters to them.

Vendor-sponsored advisory is not trusted. The AI tool vendors have begun offering governance advisory as a customer retention mechanism. Geneva executives who have seen vendor advisory offerings describe them as product demonstrations — useful for demonstrating the tool's capabilities, not for building independent accountability positions.

The advisory positioning that works is the one that leads with specific situation analysis before framework language. The executive wants to know what their situation requires before they want to know what framework applies.

The Geneva executive arriving at DigitalCoa.ch has already read the frameworks. What they are looking for is not another framework introduction. They are evaluating whether the advisor can think in both languages — not translate content, but conduct analytical reasoning in the language appropriate to the subject matter.

**Process Clarity Signal: Named Service Architecture with Specific Deliverables**

The ICP does not want to discover what they are buying. They want the service to be named, the process to be outlined, the deliverables to be specified, and the price to be on the page before they call.

The executive who has to ask these questions before booking has a higher booking abandonment rate than the executive who finds the answers on the website.

**Discretion Signal: The Conversation Does Not Produce a Record**

Geneva executives in professional services and international organizations are acutely aware that every document produced in their AI governance work could become an exhibit in a subsequent examination, dispute, or regulatory review. Their preference is for advisory conversations that produce internal clarity without producing external records.

The trust signal is in what is not on the website. If the website describes a documentation product as the primary output — a written report, a compliance gap analysis, a policy template — the executive reads this as a record-production service, not an advisory clarity service. The website must signal that the primary output is the executive's judgment, not the advisor's document.

**Independence Signal: Behavioral Exclusions, Not Stated Policy**

The most credible independence signal is not a statement of independence. It is a named list of what the advisor does not do.

DigitalCoa.ch does not sell AI tools. DigitalCoa.ch does not provide AI implementation services. DigitalCoa.ch does not offer framework certification through affiliated bodies. DigitalCoa.ch does not provide services to AI vendors.

Each named exclusion performs independence more credibly than any statement of policy. The exclusion list is the independence architecture. It must be on the website, named specifically, not buried in boilerplate.

**Trust Signal Audit Summary**

The ICP values institutional depth over framework breadth. They want an advisor who has seen their specific situation, in their specific sector, in Geneva, and can describe what a defensible position looks like.

They distrust advisors who lead with framework language, produce documentation as the primary output, and have undisclosed vendor relationships. They trust advisors who lead with situation analysis, produce judgment as the primary output, and have named behavioral exclusions.

The website must perform these signals before the first call, not during it.

## Deliverable 06 — Findings Summary

### Six Net-New Insights on DigitalCoa.ch Positioning, Distinct from C214–C247 Phase 06

**Insight 1: Documentation Continuity Architecture as Discrete Named Advisory Product**

The problem of maintaining a defensible accountability position across changing conditions — tool updates, personnel transitions, regulatory shifts, client expectation evolution — is not addressed by any existing Geneva market participant. It is treated as a subset of general AI governance maintenance. It is not named as a discrete service requiring its own methodology.

The naming of documentation continuity architecture as a discrete advisory product creates a market category that did not previously exist. Executives can only seek what has been named. The name creates the demand.

The product architecture — 90-minute briefing leading to a written continuity architecture for the client's specific conditions — is distinct from audit services, compliance gap analyses, and framework certification. The distinction is in the primary output: the executive's judgment, not the advisor's document.

**Insight 2: Pre-Deliberation Anticipatory Positioning as Distinct Advisory Timing Category**

Executives who call before a regulatory deliberation — in this case, the Federal Council June 2026 AI governance deliberation — are not asking what the deliberation will decide. They are asking what their documentation should look like before the deliberation produces a market-formed standard. They are positioning for a standard that has not yet been set.

This is structurally different from post-event advisory (calling after a decision is made) and from regulatory anticipation advisory (calling based on published regulatory text). Pre-deliberation positioning is its own timing category, requiring a different conversational framing, a different analytical output, and a different pricing structure.

The pre-deliberation caller represents the most sophisticated demand segment in the Geneva AI governance market. Serving them well requires a different service design than serving the post-event caller.

**Insight 3: Reassessment Protocol as Missing Operational Infrastructure**

Geneva professional services firms and international organizations have built AI governance documentation. They have not built reassessment protocols — documented procedures for determining when existing documentation no longer holds and must be updated. The absence of a reassessment protocol means that documentation adequacy can only be assessed reactively: in response to an incident, a questionnaire, or a supervisory examination.

The reassessment protocol is the missing operational component that separates firms with documentation from firms with maintained documentation. It is a discrete deliverable — a named, bounded document that specifies the conditions under which a reassessment is triggered, the scope of what is reassessed, and the methodology for conducting the reassessment.

DigitalCoa.ch reassessment protocol development as a discrete service component positions the advisory not as a documentation producer but as a documentation system architect.

**Insight 4: Client-Communications Audit Trail as Under-Named Accountability Surface**

Geneva professional services firms generate client communications continuously. When AI tools contribute to those communications — AI-assisted drafting, AI-generated analysis, AI-enhanced reporting — the contribution is frequently invisible in the communication itself and undocumented in the firm's records.

The client-communications audit trail is the record of what AI contributed to client-facing communications, how that contribution was reviewed, and what the client was told about the AI's role. It is a discrete accountability surface distinct from internal governance documentation.

The gap between what clients receive and what firms can explain about how client communications were produced is a present-tense accountability exposure that activates on client dispute, counterparty questionnaire, or supervisory examination. The client-communications audit trail is a discrete named documentation requirement that has not been addressed in Geneva AI governance advisory positioning.

**Insight 5: Multi-Obligation Sequencing as Unmet Advisory Framing Need**

Geneva professional services firms and international organizations face multiple simultaneous AI governance obligations: carrier renewal questionnaire, counterparty AI governance questionnaire, FINMA regulatory requirements, nFADP documentation obligations, EU AI Act downstream obligations for EU-connected clients, and internal board reporting requirements.

Each obligation arrives on its own calendar, from its own source, asking for its own documentation. The firm manages them sequentially, as they arrive. The sequential management produces reactive documentation for each obligation separately, without an integrated accountability architecture underneath.

The multi-obligation sequencing problem — the inability to sequence multiple simultaneous obligations into a coherent governance architecture that satisfies all of them without producing redundant documentation — is not addressed by any existing Geneva advisory. The framing of "multi-obligation sequencing" names the problem in a way that executives recognize immediately. The naming is the demand-creation mechanism.

**Insight 6: Geneva French-Language Advisory Market as Distinct Positioning Opportunity**

The Brotons.net platform has established analytical authority in English. The Geneva professional community that operates in French — the Geneva cantonal professional community, Geneva international organizations with French-language institutional practice, Geneva life sciences boards conducting business in French — has no equivalent analytical reference point.

DigitalCoa.ch bilingual positioning has been primarily English-focused with French translation as localization. The distinct opportunity is French-language analytical authority that leads in French, not one that follows English content as a translated version.

The Geneva French-language advisory market is distinct from the Brotons.net audience in language preference, institutional affiliation, and professional community participation. The opportunity is not to translate DigitalCoa.ch content into French. The opportunity is to produce French-language analytical content in Geneva institutional voice — on Swiss regulatory text, Geneva cantonal procedure, Geneva bar informal guidance — that serves an audience that has no equivalent reference point.

This is a content architecture investment that would compound over time: each French-language piece addressing a Geneva-specific institutional question builds the foundation for French-language executive advisory relationships that cannot be replicated by English-focused advisors operating in Geneva.
