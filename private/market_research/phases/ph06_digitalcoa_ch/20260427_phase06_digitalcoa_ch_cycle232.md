### Triggers

- Receiving an AI-specific counterparty due diligence questionnaire with questions that cannot be answered from current documentation
- Board meeting agenda item on AI risk with no preparatory briefing or written position paper
- A peer organization announces an AI-related incident or regulatory outcome
- An internal audit, external audit, or funder review identifies AI governance documentation gaps
- Vendor contract renewal triggers first structured review of AI tool accountability provisions
- Regulatory publication or enforcement action makes abstract accountability exposure concrete

### Expectations

- A direct, honest assessment of their current AI governance position — not reassurance, not a framework
- Clarity on where personal accountability attaches, specifically and practically
- A written record that can be kept and produced when needed, not filed and forgotten
- A description of what adequate governance looks like for their specific situation
- A clear next step — what they can actually do, in the time they have
- Absolute discretion: the content of this conversation does not become a matter of public record or market knowledge

### Distrusts

- Advisors who present a methodology before understanding the specific situation
- "Frameworks" presented as answers when the question has not been defined
- Documentation exercises that produce policies disconnected from actual decisions made
- Advisors who cannot describe what happens after the engagement ends
- General training positioned as governance adequacy for senior accountability holders
- Any advisor who uses the engagement for business development purposes
- Pricing structures designed to maximize upsell rather than to serve the client cleanly

## 02 — Market Signals: Where Geneva Executive Demand Is Emerging

**Signal 1: The counterparty questionnaire as forcing function — now arriving with real relationship consequences**
AI-specific due diligence questionnaires from counterparties are arriving in Geneva executive inboxes with increasing specificity. These are no longer abstract regulatory concerns — they are specific relationship events with specific consequences: a deal moving forward depends on credible answers, a procurement process stalls because AI governance documentation is missing, a client relationship is conditioned on the ability to demonstrate governance adequacy. The questionnaire is the most actionable demand trigger because it embeds a real relationship consequence in the deadline. Executives who have been managing the question reactively are now discovering that the inability to answer is a business problem, not just a governance problem.

**Signal 2: Board AI literacy as structural adequacy gap, not training gap**
Boards in Geneva are being asked to oversee AI risk in a context where no board-level AI literacy standard exists. The gap is not a training gap — training addresses knowledge gaps. The Geneva board AI gap is an adequacy gap: there is no published reference point that defines what a board should understand, no standard process for board-level AI oversight, and no clear delineation of what the board is being asked to approve versus what it is being informed about. The executive who brings this question is bringing it because they have recognized something their board has not yet articulated. This structural adequacy gap creates a specific advisory opportunity distinct from "board training."

**Signal 3: The annual vendor contract renewal as predictable advisory demand window**
The annual vendor contract renewal is a recurring, predictable moment when executives confront accountability provisions signed without review and AI decisions made without documentation. This annual window is not currently served by any discretely scoped advisory product. The executive who reviews AI tool contracts once a year has a specific, recurring need that is distinct from the general governance gap engagement: a bounded review scoped to what the renewal reveals, with a specific output that enables better decision-making at renewal. The predictability of the window makes it a specific product occasion — it arrives on a calendar, it has specific scope, and it produces a specific deliverable.

**Signal 4: Post-incident peer discovery as organic demand generator**
Executives who discover — through incidental conversation or a counterparty exchange — that their peers are navigating the same AI governance question they have been managing privately experience a sudden demand for external validation. The organic peer discovery mechanism is more powerful than any marketing channel because it bypasses the trust-building work that cold outreach requires. The peer who has the same question and has not yet found a credible advisor provides a warm introduction or a direct referral. This mechanism is particularly strong in Geneva's concentrated professional community, where the network is dense and information circulates quickly — even about discreet matters.

**Signal 5: Funders and procurement as enforcement proxies for AI governance standards**
Funder AI governance requirements and procurement AI standards are functioning as de facto regulatory enforcement mechanisms for organizations that are not directly subject to Swiss AI regulation. International organizations, NGOs, and entities receiving grant funding are being asked to demonstrate AI governance adequacy as a condition of continued funding. This is creating a specific demand channel: the organization that has been managing AI governance as an internal matter is now being asked to produce documentation by an external party with leverage. The advisory entry point is the funder questionnaire or procurement requirement — a specific, dateable trigger with real consequences.

**Signal 6: Switzerland's regulatory timing creating a specific accountability window**
Switzerland's deliberate approach to AI regulation creates a specific advisory positioning advantage for the executive who is willing to act before prescriptive regulation arrives. The Geneva executive operating in a non-prescriptive regulatory environment has more agency but more personal accountability — there is no regulatory safe harbor to hide behind. Advisors who can help executives understand what they need to decide proactively versus what they can defer to regulatory development are particularly valuable in this context. The executives who are acting now — before the regulatory question becomes mandatory — are the ones who will be best positioned when the regulatory landscape clarifies.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis: How Other Advisors Are Positioning AI Governance in Geneva

### Credible claims currently in the market

**Regulatory interpretation from law firms:**
Law firms offer credible regulatory interpretation — nFADP obligations, FDPIC enforcement posture, AI liability under Swiss law. Their credibility rests on their professional qualifications and their access to regulatory developments. Their structural limitation with this ICP is that they address the legal question rather than the governance question: "here is what the law requires" differs from "here is whether what you have is defensible."

**Large firm methodology and scale:**
Big Four and major consulting firms offer credible methodology — structured audit processes, cross-industry benchmarking, implementation roadmaps. Their credibility rests on brand recognition and methodology sophistication. Their structural limitation with this ICP is that their output — standardized reports, implementation plans, certification support — does not match what the senior executive needs, which is a direct professional view delivered personally, at senior-appropriate fee level, in a single focused meeting.

**Technology platform tooling:**
Compliance platforms and SaaS tools offer credible tooling capability — automated monitoring, policy document generation, certification support. Their credibility rests on functional capability and ease of deployment. Their structural limitation with this ICP is that platform positioning signals the wrong organizational level — it reads as IT management rather than senior personal advisory, and it cannot address the executive's specific accountability question.

**Training and awareness programs:**
Training providers offer credible organizational capability building — structured learning, certification tracks, professional education credits. Their credibility rests on curriculum design and institutional recognition. Their structural limitation with this ICP is that they address the awareness layer, not the senior executive's personal accountability exposure.

### Gaps in the competitive landscape

**Gap 1: Personal executive AI accountability as a named discrete product**
No Geneva advisor has clearly named personal executive AI accountability as the primary product — not as a component of organizational governance, not as a legal opinion, not as a compliance project. The executive's question — "where does my accountability for AI end and where does someone else's begin?" — is the specific question this ICP carries, and it is not being addressed as the primary product by any named competitor.

**Gap 2: Forward-looking reasoning record design**
No advisor offers a service that designs the executive's reasoning record going forward — specifying what the record should capture, in what format, for which decisions, on what timeline. This is distinct from documenting past decisions (forensic) and from designing governance policies (organizational). It is a specific operational design service that does not exist as a named product in the Geneva market.

**Gap 3: Discretion as primary positioning element — operationalized, not claimed**
Most advisors mention confidentiality as a professional obligation. None have operationalized discretion as a specific service design choice that shapes every touchpoint of the engagement — from first contact through written summary delivery to follow-up protocol. Discretion as a lived practice, not a disclaimer, is a gap in how Geneva advisory positioning is constructed.

**Gap 4: The written summary as the primary product artifact**
Most advisors treat the verbal briefing as the primary deliverable and the written summary as a secondary record. The executive who values the written artifact — something they can keep, reference, and produce when needed — is underserved. The written summary that is designed as the primary output, with the conversation as the process that produces it, is a gap in how the advisory product is structured.

**Gap 5: Independent AI governance advisory as a named professional category**
No Geneva advisor has clearly named independent AI governance advisory as a specific professional category — distinct from legal advice, distinct from compliance consulting, distinct from technology implementation. The absence of a named category forces executives to fit their need into an inappropriate service category or to accept generic positioning that does not fit their specific situation.

**Gap 6: Geneva-specific institutional knowledge as a specific positioning element**
No advisor has positioned Geneva-specific institutional knowledge as a specific value component — the regulatory culture, the professional community dynamics, the event circuit, the peer accountability mechanisms, the way decisions actually get made in Geneva's institutional environment. Most advisors treat the Geneva context as incidental geography rather than structural context that shapes what the executive needs to understand about their accountability.

## 04 — Trust Signal Audit: What Credibility, Process Clarity, Discretion, and Independence Signals This ICP Values Most

### Credibility signals — in order of weight

**Pattern recognition without case studies:**
The executive evaluates the advisor's credibility by the precision of their understanding — can the advisor describe the specific dynamic the executive is facing before the executive finishes describing it? This is pattern recognition. It is not a client reference — it is the advisor's demonstrated precision in naming the executive's situation accurately. The executive who hears their own situation described back to them with precision recognizes an advisor who has worked at this level before. The executive who hears general language recognizes the opposite.

**Swiss institutional groundedness:**
The executive evaluates whether the advisor understands the specific Geneva institutional environment — the FDPIC's investigative pattern, FINMA's supervisory approach, how Geneva's professional community operates, the specific accountability questions that arise in this context. Advisors who demonstrate this understanding signal a credibility that cannot be replicated by offshore or generic advisors. The institutional knowledge is the credential.

**Independent publication record:**
The executive who has read the advisor's publications before the first meeting arrives with a pre-formed judgment about the advisor's independent thinking. Publications in the advisor's own voice — not syndicated content, not framework marketing — are the primary credibility surface before the first conversation. The publication record is the evidence that the advisor forms independent judgments and can articulate them without client attribution.

**Direct judgment expression:**
The executive expects the advisor to form a view in the first meeting and express it directly. Advisors who hedge consistently are perceived as unable or unwilling to provide the direct judgment this ICP requires. The executive is looking for someone who will say: this is what you have, this is where the gap is, this is what adequate looks like for your situation. Hedging signals that the advisor is either uncertain or unwilling to commit to a professional view.

### Process clarity signals

**Engagement end-state definition:**
The executive asks: what does a completed engagement look like? The advisor who can answer this precisely — what the client holds at the end, what they can do with it, what the engagement does not cover — signals a practice that has done this before and thinks in terms of outcomes. Vague engagement scope signals vague thinking.

**Sequencing transparency:**
The executive wants to know what happens next before they commit. The advisor who can describe the sequencing — briefing, assessment, written summary, next steps — without being asked signals a practice that has a structured approach. The absence of sequencing clarity signals a practice that is improvising.

**Scope boundary articulation:**
The advisor who can describe what they do not do — what falls outside the engagement, what requires a different kind of engagement — signals an integrity about scope that executives recognize as distinct. The ability to hold boundaries clearly is itself a trust signal.

### Discretion signals

**No commercial behavior during or after engagement:**
The advisor who uses the advisory relationship for business development — introducing other services, positioning for follow-on engagements, leveraging the relationship for referrals — signals a conflict of interest that the executive notes immediately. Discretion is demonstrated by the absence of commercial behavior. The executive expects to be left alone after the engagement unless they initiate contact.

**Practice information management:**
The advisor who demonstrates knowledge of when information should not be shared — about other clients, about the advisor's own situation — signals a practice where information management is a professional discipline, not a policy. The executive infers how their own information will be managed from how the advisor manages theirs.

**Contact protocol clarity:**
The advisor who specifies how and when they can be contacted after the engagement — and what happens when they cannot be reached — signals a practice that has thought about the client relationship as an ongoing operational reality, not just a transaction.

### Independence signals

**Named behavioral exclusion list:**
The advisor who articulates specific exclusions — what they do not do, who they do not work with — demonstrates independence through practice, not claim. The executive evaluates the exclusion list as the true definition of the practice. "I do not implement AI governance systems. I do not sell platforms or software. I do not take referral fees from AI vendors. My revenue comes exclusively from professional advisory." Named exclusions perform more credibility than claimed independence.

**No vendor or platform financial relationship:**
The advisor who has no financial relationship with AI vendors, platforms, or technology providers signals an independence the executive can verify. Vendor relationships introduce conflicts that the executive cannot fully assess but instinctively discounts.

**Clean pricing without upsell architecture:**
The advisor who prices the engagement clearly — with no upsell pathway, no tiered service ladder — signals a practice that is not optimizing for revenue expansion. The executive who sees a pricing structure designed to maximize engagement value notes it and adjusts their trust accordingly.
