## 01 — ICP Language: How Executives Describe Their Need for AI Governance Advice

### English

**Managing partner, professional services firm:**
"I have been operating with AI in my practice for two years. No one has ever asked me to document how I used it or whether my supervision was adequate. The first time I hear about this as a professional obligation will be when something goes wrong."

**Board member, financial services:**
"The board approved the AI framework. We did not know what questions to ask. The document we approved described what the AI does in general terms. It did not describe what the board needed to understand about the decisions it was approving."

**C-suite executive:**
"The compliance team has a framework. The framework was built to pass an audit, not to manage the actual risk. I can see the difference but I cannot explain it in a way that protects me."

**Executive director, international organization:**
"Our AI tools were selected before governance frameworks existed. We are operating with decisions made in a period when no one was asking the right questions. Those decisions have consequences I cannot fully map."

**Senior executive:**
"I need to be able to describe my reasoning at the time I made the decision, not the reasoning I can construct afterwards. That record does not exist in a form I can use."

**Chief risk officer:**
"The moment I realized the gap was when a counterparty asked specific questions about our AI governance. I could not answer them accurately without exposing the practice. The questions were legitimate. The inability to answer them was the exposure."

### Triggers

- Counterparty due diligence questionnaire arrives with AI-specific questions
- Board meeting agenda includes AI risk for the first time without preparatory briefing
- A peer firm announces an AI-related incident or regulatory outcome
- An employee raises a question about how AI decisions affecting clients are reviewed
- Vendor contract renewal prompts first review of AI tool accountability provisions
- Regulatory publication or enforcement action makes the abstract question concrete

### Expectations

- A clear answer to where their accountability for AI decisions ends
- A written record of their reasoning that they can produce when asked
- A description of what adequate governance looks like for their specific situation
- An honest assessment of their exposure, not reassurance
- A clear next step, not a framework to implement
- Discretion: this conversation does not become a matter of public record

### Distrust signals

- "Frameworks" presented as answers to governance questions they have not defined
- General training or awareness sessions positioned as governance adequacy
- Documentation exercises that produce policies disconnected from actual decisions
- Advisors who cannot describe what happens when the engagement ends
- Advisors who explain AI governance without being able to describe what the FDPIC actually looks for
- Advisory relationships that require organizational buy-in before the executive gets what they need

## 02 — Market Signals: Where Geneva Executive Demand Is Emerging

**Signal 1: Counterparty AI questionnaires as forcing function**
AI-specific due diligence questionnaires from counterparties — banks, institutional clients, procurement departments — are arriving in Geneva executive inboxes without warning. The questionnaire is a forcing function that makes the abstract governance question concrete and dateable. Executives who have been postponing the question now have a deadline they did not choose. This channel is underused as an advisory entry point because most advisors do not read the questionnaire as a demand signal.

**Signal 2: The board briefing gap is structural and acute**
Boards in Geneva are being asked to oversee AI risk in a context where no board-level AI literacy standard exists, no published reference point defines what a board should understand, and the executives presenting to the board cannot themselves articulate the governance question. The gap is not a training gap — it is a structural adequacy gap. The executive who brings this question is bringing it because they have recognized something their board has not.

**Signal 3: Vendor contract renewals as accountability reassessment moments**
Annual vendor contract renewals are emerging as discrete accountability reassessment moments. The executive who reviews their AI tool contracts once a year confronts the accountability provisions they signed without reading and the AI decisions they have delegated without documentation. This annual review cycle creates a predictable advisory demand window that is not currently served by any discretely scoped product.

**Signal 4: Post-event Geneva event information processing remains underserved**
Geneva's event density in AI governance creates post-event information processing demand that is real and systematic. Executives leave events with questions they cannot answer and no clear pathway to getting them answered. The information processing gap is distinct from the awareness gap — awareness is abundant, interpretive clarity is scarce. This gap is not being served by the event organizers themselves and is only incidentally served by general advisors.

**Signal 5: Peer discovery of shared exposure as organic demand generator**
Executives who discover — through a casual conversation or a counterparty exchange — that their peers have the same AI governance question they have been managing privately, experience a sudden demand for external validation. The peer comparison mechanism that was previously suppressed by the absence of safe forums for disclosure is now activating through incidental discovery. This organic mechanism is more powerful than any marketing channel.

**Signal 6: Switzerland's regulatory timing as a specific positioning advantage**
Switzerland's deliberate approach to AI regulation — monitoring EU developments, avoiding premature commitment — creates a specific advisory positioning advantage. The Geneva executive operating in a non-prescriptive regulatory environment has more agency but more accountability. Advisors who can help executives understand what they need to decide versus what they can defer are more valuable in this context than advisors who offer premature regulatory certainty.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis: How Other Advisors Position AI Governance in Geneva

### Credible claims currently in the market

**Generic AI governance frameworks:**
Large consulting firms and professional services networks offer AI governance frameworks. These are credible as organizational design products. They are not credible as personal accountability products for executives who need to demonstrate the quality of their specific decisions. The gap between organizational governance and executive accountability is where the credible claims end.

**AI compliance training and awareness:**
Training products address the awareness layer. They are credible as fulfilling organizational duty-of-care obligations. They do not address the executive's personal accountability exposure or the quality of their reasoning record. The training market is crowded. The accountability briefing market is not.

**Legal opinions on AI liability:**
Law firms offer legal opinions on AI liability exposure. These are credible as legal analysis. They are typically retrospective or hypothetical — they describe the law as it applies to a defined scenario. They do not address the ongoing operational question of what the executive's decision record should look like going forward.

**Technology vendor compliance certifications:**
AI vendors offer compliance certifications for their platforms. These are credible as technical assurances. They do not address the executive's personal accountability for decisions made using those platforms. The vendor's compliance does not discharge the executive's professional duty.

### Gaps in the competitive landscape

**Gap 1: Personal executive accountability as discrete advisory product**
No advisor has clearly named personal executive AI accountability as a discrete advisory product. The executive's question — where does my accountability end, what do I need to be able to demonstrate — is treated as a component of larger organizational governance engagements. It is not positioned as the primary product.

**Gap 2: Reasoning record design as forward-looking operational service**
No advisor offers a service that designs the executive's reasoning record going forward — specifying what the record should capture, in what format, for which decisions. This is distinct from documenting past decisions (which is forensic) and from designing governance policies (which is organizational). It is a specific operational design service that does not exist as a named product.

**Gap 3: Geneva-specific institutional translation**
No advisor has positioned Geneva-specific institutional knowledge as a specific value component. The regulatory culture, the professional community dynamics, the event circuit, the peer accountability mechanisms — these are not factored into advisory positioning as a distinct credibility element. Most advisors treat the Geneva context as incidental geography rather than structural context.

**Gap 4: Discretion as primary positioning element**
No advisor has made discretion — the explicit management of confidential information — a primary positioning element. Most advisors mention confidentiality as a matter of professional conduct. None have articulated discretion as a specific service design choice that shapes what the engagement is and what it is not.

**Gap 5: The written summary as primary product artifact**
Most advisors treat the briefing conversation as the primary deliverable and the written summary as a secondary record. The executive who books a briefing because of what they will receive in writing is underserved. The written summary that can be kept, referenced, and produced when needed is more valuable to the executive than the conversation itself.

**Gap 6: Independent advisory as named category**
No Geneva advisor has clearly named independent AI governance advisory as a specific professional category — distinct from legal advice, distinct from compliance consulting, distinct from technology implementation. The absence of a named category forces executives to fit their need into an inappropriate service category or to accept generic positioning that does not fit.

## 04 — Trust Signal Audit: What Credibility, Process Clarity, Discretion, and Independence Signals This ICP Values Most

### Credibility signals

**Pattern recognition without case studies:**
The executive does not trust case studies — they distrust advisors who use client names or situations as marketing. The credibility signal is the advisor's ability to describe the specific dynamic the executive is facing without having been told. This is pattern recognition, not reference. The advisor who can accurately describe the executive's situation before the executive finishes describing it is the advisor who gets trusted.

**Swiss institutional grounding:**
The executive evaluates whether the advisor understands the specific institutional environment — FINMA's supervisory approach, the FDPIC's investigative pattern, the Geneva professional community's norms. Advisors who demonstrate this understanding signal credibility that offshore or generic advisors cannot replicate. The institutional knowledge is the credential.

**Independent publication record:**
The executive who has read the advisor's publications before the first meeting arrives with a pre-formed judgment about the advisor's independent thinking. Publications in the advisor's own voice — not syndicated content, not framework marketing — are the primary credibility surface before the first conversation.

### Process clarity signals

**Engagement end-state definition:**
The executive asks: what does a completed engagement look like? The advisor who can answer this question clearly — specifying what the client holds at the end, what they can do with it, what the engagement does not cover — signals a precision that is itself a trust signal. Vague engagement scope signals vague thinking.

**Sequencing transparency:**
The executive wants to know what happens next before they commit. The advisor who can describe the sequencing — briefing, assessment, written summary, follow-up options — without being asked, signals a practice that has done this before. The absence of sequencing clarity signals a practice that is improvising.

**Scope boundary articulation:**
The advisor who can describe what they do not do — what falls outside the engagement, what requires a different kind of engagement, what requires a different kind of advisor — signals an integrity about scope that executives recognize as distinct. Boundary articulation performs trustworthiness more effectively than boundary claims.

### Discretion signals

**No business development behavior:**
The advisor who uses the advisory relationship for business development — introducing other services, positioning for follow-on engagements during the briefing, leveraging the relationship for referrals — signals a conflict of interest that the executive notes immediately. Discretion is demonstrated by the absence of commercial behavior during and after the engagement.

**Practice information management:**
The advisor who demonstrates knowledge of when information should not be shared — about other clients, about the advisor's own situation, about the substance of other engagements — signals a practice that has information management as a professional discipline, not a policy. The executive infers how their own information will be managed from how the advisor manages theirs.

**Contact protocol clarity:**
The advisor who specifies how and when they can be contacted after the engagement — and what happens when they cannot be reached — signals a practice that has thought about the client relationship as an ongoing operational reality, not just a transaction. The executive who knows what happens when they need something signals more trust than the executive who does not.

### Independence signals

**Behavioral exclusion list:**
The advisor who articulates specific exclusions — what they do not do, who they do not work with, what advice they will not give — demonstrates independence through practice, not claim. The exclusion list is more credible than the service list. The executive evaluates the exclusions as the true definition of the practice.

**No vendor relationship:**
The advisor who has no financial relationship with AI vendors, platforms, or technology providers signals an independence that the executive can verify. Vendor relationships introduce conflicts that the executive cannot fully assess but instinctively discounts.

**Pricing with no upsell architecture:**
The advisor who prices the engagement clearly — with no upsell pathway, no tiered service ladder, no premium version — signals a practice that is not optimizing for revenue expansion. The executive who sees a pricing structure designed to maximize engagement value rather than client value notes it.

## 06 — Findings Summary: Six Net-New Insights on DigitalCoa.ch Positioning

**Insight 1: The reasoning record as the executive's primary durably valuable artifact**
Previous Phase 06 cycles have addressed the reasoning record gap as an accountability exposure. The net-new insight for C231 is that the written reasoning record is not only an exposure mitigation — it is the primary durably valuable artifact the executive receives from the advisory engagement. The conversation is transient. The written summary that can be kept, referenced, and produced when needed is the artifact that justifies the investment and drives the referral. This reorders the product design priority: the written summary should be designed as the primary output, with the conversation as the process that produces it.

**Insight 2: The counterparty questionnaire as the most underleveraged advisory entry trigger**
Previous cycles have identified regulatory events, peer discovery, and board moments as demand triggers. The net-new insight for C231 is that the counterparty AI due diligence questionnaire is the most underleveraged entry trigger currently operating in the Geneva executive market. It is dateable, relationship-specific, and arrives without warning. It forces the executive to confront governance gaps they have been managing reactively. Advisors who do not read the questionnaire as a demand signal are missing a specific, high-intent entry point that requires no cold outreach to access.

**Insight 3: Annual vendor contract renewal as a discrete advisory product occasion**
The annual vendor contract renewal cycle creates a recurring, predictable advisory demand window that is not currently served by any discretely scoped product. The executive reviewing AI tool contracts annually confronts accountability provisions signed without review and AI decisions made without documentation. This occasion — scoped specifically to the annual review moment — is distinct from the general governance gap engagement and from the post-incident debrief. It is a specific product occasion with specific timing and specific scope.

**Insight 4: Independence through behavioral exclusion, not service claim**
Previous cycles have addressed independence as a positioning element through practice model description and scope boundary articulation. The net-new insight for C231 is that the most credible independence signal is the behavioral exclusion list — specific, named things the advisor does not do — rather than the service list. The executive evaluates what the advisor will not do as the true definition of the practice. The exclusion list should be more prominent than the inclusion list in all positioning materials.

**Insight 5: Peer silence as institutional infrastructure failure, not personal inadequacy**
Previous cycles have characterized peer silence on AI governance as a signal of personal exposure. The net-new insight for C231 is that peer silence is better understood as an institutional infrastructure failure: the Geneva professional community has no safe, confidential forum for executives to discuss AI accountability questions with peers who share their exposure. The absence of the forum, not the executive's reluctance, is the structural cause. Positioning a confidential peer accountability format as an advisory product addresses the infrastructure failure directly.

**Insight 6: The written briefing ending as product design variable**
Previous cycles have identified the briefing ending as a conversion mechanism and the written summary as a conversion moment. The net-new insight for C231 is that the ending of the verbal briefing — what is said at the close of the 90-minute conversation before the written summary arrives — is a distinct product design variable. The ending of the conversation shapes what the executive expects from the written summary, which shapes how they receive it. The verbal ending should be designed to prepare the executive for the written artifact, not simply to close the engagement. What the advisor says at the end of the briefing is the bridge to the primary deliverable.
