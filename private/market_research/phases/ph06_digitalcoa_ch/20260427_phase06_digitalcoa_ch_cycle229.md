## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### Real Language — How Senior Executives Describe Their Need for AI Governance Advice

**EN**

The Geneva executive who needs AI governance advice is not describing a compliance project. They are describing a personal professional situation. The language is direct and stripped of abstraction: they describe what is on their desk, what their name is on, what they cannot explain if asked today. They use the vocabulary of accountability, not the vocabulary of technology adoption. They do not talk about "AI strategy" or "digital transformation." They talk about decisions made quickly under their authority, about AI tools that entered the firm without a governance-level review, about documentation that does not exist, about a counterparty questionnaire that arrived and could not be answered.

The distinction between information and judgment is present in how they frame the request. They have attended briefings. They have read guidance. What they lack is someone who will sit in the room and say: this is what you have, this is where the gap is, this is what adequate looks like for your situation — not a framework, a view. The trigger is consistently the moment of realizing that accumulated AI decisions were made faster than governance could follow, and that there is now a specific exposure attached to their personal professional accountability.

**Triggers:**

- "We onboarded AI tools fast because the team needed them and I authorized it. Eighteen months later I realize there is no documentation anywhere that describes what was decided, by whom, on what basis."
- "I am joining a board. They have AI systems that I am being asked to oversee. I need to understand what I am looking at before I sign the relevant documents and it becomes my liability."
- "A counterparty sent a twelve-point questionnaire on our AI methodology. We had no coherent answers. I realized that if we cannot answer this credibly, we have a problem that exists whether or not this specific client asks."
- "My predecessor made decisions about AI infrastructure before I arrived. Some of those decisions are now mine. I need to know what exposure I inherited."
- "The FDPIC published something about AI systems last month. I read it and realized that what we have might be in that territory. I need to know whether it is, and what we would show if they came."
- "My team is using AI in client-facing work. I authorized it. But I have not sat with them to determine what adequate review means for AI-assisted output. I am carrying that."
- "There is a board meeting in three weeks where we need a written AI governance position. I have nothing written that I would stand behind."

**Expectations:**

The executive who reaches out expects a direct, specific professional opinion — not a presentation, not a framework document. They expect the advisor to form a view in the first meeting and express it directly. They expect someone who works in their world: Geneva professional services, senior level, personal liability exposure — someone who has specific pattern knowledge of what these situations look like in practice. They expect the first conversation to be a consultation, not a commercial qualification. And they expect absolute discretion: the fact that they are asking, and the specific content of their exposure, is not information they want circulating.

**Distrusts:**

Any advisor whose website could describe services to a hospital, a cantonal government, and a private bank without distinguishing any of them. Any first meeting that presents a methodology before understanding the situation. Platforms that promise governance automation. Certification badges with no stated jurisdiction or exposure type. The phrase "AI governance framework" as a product name. Any advisor who cannot explain in one sentence what they do that a law firm does not and what they do that a Big Four team does not. Communications that read as marketing: a first meeting that feels like a pitch, a follow-up sequence that resembles a sales process. Advisors whose website leads with regulatory landscape summaries rather than with a direct statement of the specific problem they solve for the specific person they serve.

**The unstated question:**

"I have been making decisions about AI systems under my own authority for two or three years. I need to know whether what accumulated is defensible — now, not eventually — and I need a specific, direct answer from someone who has been in this exact situation before."

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### How Other Advisors Are Positioning AI Governance in Geneva

**The Geneva Competitive Landscape**

The Geneva AI governance market contains several distinct competitor categories, each with recognizable positioning and each leaving a distinct gap at the senior executive level.

**Law Firms**

Geneva law firms have positioned AI governance as an extension of their data protection and technology practices. Their strength is regulatory interpretation — nFADP obligations, FDPIC enforcement posture, Swiss AI Convention implications, contract liability questions. Their structural weakness with this ICP is threefold: they treat AI governance as a legal compliance question rather than a governance and accountability question; they bill at rates that make detailed internal review prohibitively expensive; and their output is legal opinion rather than actionable governance documentation. The senior executive who needs a direct view of whether their AI documentation would hold up if asked is not well-served by a law firm's legal interpretation of what the regulations technically require. The law firm answer to "what do we have?" is "here is what the law requires." The executive's question is different: "would what we have be defensible if asked?"

**Big Four Firms**

Big Four AI governance practices are built for large organizations — banks, multinationals, cantonal institutions. They are structurally oriented toward scale engagements, standardized methodologies, and report-based output. The boutique managing director or the mid-sized board member who needs a discrete, senior-level consultation is not their target. The cultural fit is wrong: executives describe feeling like a line item on a project plan rather than a person with a specific professional problem. Big Four output — standardized audit reports, implementation roadmaps, certification support — does not match what the senior executive needs, which is a direct, specific professional view delivered in a single focused meeting.

**Compliance Platforms and SaaS**

AI governance platforms and SaaS tools have entered the market with automated compliance monitoring, policy document generators, and certification support. They are positioned at IT departments and compliance teams. They are not credible to the senior executive who understands that governance is about accountability chains, decision attribution, and the specific exposure that attaches to their role. Platform positioning actively signals the wrong level — it reads as "this is a tool for managing AI use" rather than "this is an advisor for navigating personal accountability."

**Training and Education Providers**

AI governance training programs, certification courses, and professional education modules exist in the Geneva market. They serve a real need for organizational capability building. They do not serve the individual executive who needs a specific, applicable assessment of their personal exposure. The executive who needs governance clarity is not looking for a course.

**The Specific Gap**

The gap that remains unfilled is the senior-level, Geneva-embedded, independent advisory practice that works specifically on personal AI governance exposure — not as a legal question, not as a compliance project, not as a training program, and not as a platform. The practice that fills this gap provides a discrete, professional consultation to the individual who is carrying personal accountability for AI decisions made under their authority. It does not sell frameworks, does not position through certification badges, does not offer platforms, and does not approach AI governance as an education product. It provides the direct, specific professional judgment that the senior executive is looking for — delivered in a single focused meeting, with a written summary that gives them something concrete to work with.

**Competitive Credible Claims vs. Gaps**

Law firms can credibly claim regulatory knowledge and legal interpretation authority. They cannot credibly claim senior-level personal accountability advisory in a single focused meeting at a senior-appropriate fee.

Big Four can credibly claim scale methodology and cross-industry pattern knowledge. They cannot credibly claim senior-level personal advisory for boutique firms at a senior-appropriate fee and cultural fit.

Platforms can credibly claim tooling capability and documentation automation. They cannot credibly claim senior-level personal judgment delivery.

Training providers can credibly claim capability-building methodology and curriculum design. They cannot credibly claim senior-level personal accountability advisory.

The gap — senior-level, independent, Geneva-embedded, discrete consultation with written output — is Sergio's to hold.

## 04 — Trust Signal Audit

### What Credibility, Process Clarity, Discretion, and Independence Signals This ICP Values Most

**Credibility Signals — In Order of Weight**

Pattern specificity is the primary credibility signal. The executive needs to know that the advisor has worked in situations that resemble theirs — not in theory, not from a framework document, but in practice, in Geneva, at the level of personal exposure they are carrying. The advisor demonstrates this not by describing a client (which would violate discretion) but by showing precision of understanding: the specific configuration of exposure, the specific triggers, the specific questions that arise in situations like theirs. Language that is too general signals that the advisor has not actually worked at this level. The executive who hears "I have worked with several managing directors who discovered undocumented AI decisions in their firms after the fact" recognizes the pattern. The executive who hears "we help firms with AI governance" does not.

Independent judgment expression is the second credibility signal. The executive wants an advisor who will form a view and express it directly during the first meeting — not take the matter under advisement, not promise to review materials and report back, not hedge every statement. Advisors who hedge consistently are perceived as unable or unwilling to provide the direct judgment this ICP needs. The executive is looking for someone who will say: this is what you have, this is where the gap is, this is what adequate looks like for your situation.

Geneva institutional embeddedness is the third credibility signal. The advisor must demonstrate genuine operational knowledge of the Geneva professional environment — not as a remote consultant with a Geneva address, but as someone who understands the specific institutional context: cantonal professional bodies, supervisory expectations at the Geneva level, how the Geneva peer community operates. This is not geography. It is the difference between an advisor who knows Geneva from reports and one who operates in it. The executive who works in Geneva's professional environment can detect the difference immediately.

**Process Clarity**

The executive wants to understand the advisory process before committing. They want to know: what happens in the first meeting, what outputs they receive, what the written summary contains and how it can be used, what the relationship looks like after the initial briefing. Process clarity reduces the executive's sense of entering an undefined commitment and signals professionalism.

The first meeting must be clearly framed as a professional consultation — not a sales qualification, not a product demonstration. The executive should understand that they are receiving an advisor's professional time and judgment, not evaluating a service package. The output of the first meeting should be a direct, specific professional view on the executive's situation — not a proposal for further work.

**Discretion**

Discretion is non-negotiable. The senior executive navigating an AI governance exposure does not want this fact known in the market. Any advisor behavior — a follow-up sequence that resembles marketing, a public reference to a client's situation, a communication style that could result in information circulating — disqualifies the advisor regardless of their credentials. The practice's entire conduct must signal absolute discretion at every touchpoint: how the first contact is handled, how the briefing is described, how follow-up communications are framed.

**Independence**

Independence from vendors, platforms, certification bodies, and software tools is a required trust signal. The executive wants an advisor with no commercial interest in recommending a specific AI tool, platform, or certification. The practice's independence is best demonstrated through specific behavioral exclusions — the questions it does not answer, the scope boundaries it holds precisely, the specific things it does not do. Named exclusions perform more credibility than claimed independence. The practice that says "I do not do implementation, I do not sell platforms, I do not take referral arrangements from AI vendors" — said specifically — demonstrates independence more credibly than any generic independence claim.

**Insight 6 — The Annual Advisory Relationship as Accountability Continuity Infrastructure**

The annual advisory relationship is not primarily a revenue model — it is an accountability continuity infrastructure that addresses the structural nature of AI governance as a compounding accountability problem. AI governance is not a project with a completion date. AI systems are adopted, used, updated, and new decisions are made continuously under the executive's authority. The continuity model positions the practice as accountability infrastructure: the advisor who remains engaged over time arrives at each conversation with accumulated context — they know the client's situation before the question arrives, which is a materially different quality of advisory input than cold-start engagement. The executive who has a trusted advisor they can call before a decision is made — not after — has a fundamentally different governance position than the executive who treats AI governance as a periodic audit exercise.
