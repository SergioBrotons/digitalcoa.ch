## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### Real Language — How Senior Executives Describe Their Need for AI Governance Advice

**EN**

The Geneva executive who needs AI governance advice is not describing an organizational compliance gap. They are describing a specific personal situation: a decision they made or inherited, a signature on something they cannot fully explain, a conversation at a board meeting where the question was asked and there was no adequate answer. The language is stripped of abstraction. They describe the thing itself, not the category it belongs to.

The trigger is consistently a moment of recognition — not a regulatory deadline, not a new framework publication, but the realization that what accumulated under their oversight is now a specific named exposure. The gap between the speed of AI adoption and the speed of governance documentation is no longer an abstract observation. It is something they can feel in the specific document, the specific meeting, the specific question they cannot answer with confidence.

**Triggers:**

- "I signed the approval for an AI tool at a board meeting eleven months ago. I could not tell you today what it does with our data or which client information it touches. My name is in the minutes."
- "A counterparty sent us a questionnaire with specific questions about AI methodology — not generic questions, questions that assumed you had documentation of tool versions and review processes. We did not have it. That gap is now live."
- "I inherited a practice with an AI setup that was already in place. The decisions were made before I arrived. I am carrying the accountability for what was decided."
- "My team is using AI in client-facing work. I authorized it. But I have not been in the room with them to determine what adequate review looks like for AI-generated content. That question is sitting open."
- "I had a peer go through a review process after something went wrong. What they described was not a lack of policies — they had policies. The problem was that the reasoning behind the decisions was never documented. The policy existed; the judgment record did not."
- "A board colleague asked me: what would we show if someone asked whether we have adequate AI governance? I realized I did not know how to answer that question."
- "The FDPIC has been active in this area. I read the patterns. I want to know whether what we have is in that territory and what we would actually present if they came."

**Expectations:**

The executive who reaches out expects the advisor to form a direct view during the first meeting — not to take the matter under advisement, not to promise a report after a review period. They expect someone who has worked in situations that resemble theirs and who can say specifically: this is what you have, this is where the gap is, this is what adequate looks like in your situation. They expect the first conversation to be a consultation, not a qualification process. And they expect discretion — the fact that they are asking and the specific nature of their exposure is not information they want in circulation.

**Distrusts:**

Any advisor whose website could describe services to a hospital, a cantonal government, and a private bank without distinguishing any of them. Any first meeting that leads with a methodology presentation before listening. Platforms that promise automated governance. Certification badges with no stated jurisdiction or exposure type. Advisors who cannot explain in one sentence what they do that a law firm does not and what they do that a Big Four team does not. Any follow-up sequence that resembles a commercial sales process. The phrase "AI governance framework" as a primary product name. Advisors who treat AI governance as a legal compliance question rather than a professional accountability question.

**The unstated question:**

"I have been the senior person in the room on AI decisions that were made faster than governance could follow. The question is not whether I made those decisions in bad faith — I did not. The question is whether what I can now show exists is something that demonstrates I was paying attention. I need to know whether the record demonstrates active judgment or just delegated decisions."

## 02 — Market Signals

### Where Geneva Executive Demand Is Emerging

**The Reasoning Record Gap — Beyond the Policy Record**

The most specific demand signal emerging from senior executives is not "we need an AI governance policy." It is: "we have the policy. What we do not have is the reasoning behind the decisions." The distinction is precise and consequential. A policy document demonstrates that the organization considered the question. A reasoning record demonstrates that a specific person, at a specific time, evaluated a specific tool for a specific use case, considered alternatives, and reached a documented conclusion. When a regulator, a counterparty, or a board colleague asks why a particular AI system was selected, why a particular use case was approved, why a particular review process was designed as it was — the policy does not answer that question. Only the reasoning record does.

The senior executive who has been through a review process, or who has observed one in their peer network, is arriving with a specific understanding of what the actual exposure is: not the absence of a policy, but the absence of the judgment record. This is a more precise and more actionable demand signal than "we need AI governance." The advisory that can name this distinction specifically — in the first meeting, in the first paragraph of its positioning — demonstrates the pattern knowledge that the triggered executive is looking for.

**The Senior Enough Filter — Who Is Actually the Buyer**

An emerging pattern in Geneva AI governance advisory demand is the difficulty of reaching the actual decision-maker through organizational channels. The compliance team initiates the inquiry. The managing director approves the budget. The board chair needs to be briefed separately. The advisory that positions at the organizational level finds itself navigating multiple stakeholders with different accountability structures, different information access, and different definitions of what "resolved" means.

The senior executive who reaches out directly — bypassing the compliance team and the administrative filtering — is identifying themselves as a specific buyer type. They are not the organization. They are the individual whose name, registration, and reputation are on the line. The advisory that can receive this direct contact, can respond to it without a procurement process, and can deliver a consultation that addresses the individual's accountability rather than the organization's compliance is serving a distinct and underserved segment. The filter "senior enough to carry this personally" is a qualification signal that organizational positioning cannot replicate.

**Governance Theater as Emerging Failure Mode**

A specific demand signal from the Geneva executive community is the recognition that organizations with formal AI governance policies are sometimes more exposed than organizations with no policy at all — because the policy creates a document that can be examined, compared against practice, and found wanting. The organization that has a policy but no operational conformance to it has produced a record of the gap, not a record of governance. This is distinct from "no governance" as a failure mode. It is a specific failure mode: governance theater, where the existence of the policy creates a higher standard of scrutiny than the absence of a policy would.

The executive who recognizes this failure mode in their own organization is not looking for a new policy. They are looking for a way to understand what actual conformance looks like — what the gap between the policy and practice actually is, and what it would take to close it. This is a distinct and increasingly common demand profile. The advisory that can name governance theater as a specific failure mode, and can explain why it is more dangerous than no governance at all, speaks directly to an executive who has been paying attention.

**The Peer-Silence Signal as Unspoken Demand**

Geneva's senior professional community operates through informal intelligence channels — the conversation after the meeting, the question asked quietly at a conference, the reference to a peer who went through something. An emerging pattern is the executive who has been trying to start a conversation about AI governance with a peer and finding that the peer will not discuss it openly. The peer silence is not disinterest — it is the recognition that the topic touches on something sensitive: what they do not have, what they cannot explain, what their name is on.

This peer silence functions as a negative signal that is as informative as a positive reference. The executive who recognizes the silence as significant — rather than as peer indifference — is further along in their understanding than the executive who is still researching frameworks. They are looking for a discreet, independent advisor they can speak to openly, because they cannot get the clarity they need from their peer network. The practice that is known in the Geneva senior community as the person you can speak to about this, with discretion, is receiving a specific type of lead that organizational marketing cannot generate.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### How Other Advisors Are Positioning AI Governance in Geneva

**Law Firms**

Geneva law firms have moved AI governance into their technology and data protection service lines, with positioning strongest on nFADP obligations, FDPIC enforcement posture, and Swiss AI Convention implications. Their structural limitation is the frame: they address AI governance as a legal compliance question, their output is legal opinion, and their billing structure makes detailed internal review prohibitively expensive for what the senior executive actually needs. The executive who needs to understand whether the reasoning behind their AI decisions is defensible — not what the law technically requires — is not well-served by a legal opinion on regulatory obligations.

**Big Four**

Big Four AI governance practices are built for organizational scale — banks, multinationals, cantonal institutions — and structured around audit-style reviews, standardized methodologies, and report-based output. The managing partner of a boutique firm or the board member of a mid-sized organization who needs a focused senior consultation is not their target. The cultural fit is wrong: the executive who has been through a Big Four engagement describes feeling like a line item, not a person with a specific problem. The perception of technology vendor relationships and implementation revenue interests is structural and well-established.

**Compliance Platforms and SaaS**

AI governance platforms have entered with automated compliance monitoring, policy document generators, and certification support — positioned at IT departments and compliance teams, not at senior executives with personal exposure. Platform positioning actively signals the wrong level. The executive who has reached a point where personal accountability is the concern does not want a dashboard.

**Training and Professional Education**

AI governance training programs and certification courses serve a genuine organizational capability need. They do not serve the individual executive who needs a specific, applicable assessment of their personal exposure. The executive who needs governance clarity is not looking for a course.

**The Specific Gap**

The gap no current market participant is filling is senior-level, Geneva-embedded, independent advisory work on personal AI governance accountability — not as a legal question, not as a compliance project, not as a training program, and not as a platform. The gap is specifically the reasoning record: helping the executive understand what the gap between their policy and their practice actually is, what the reasoning behind their AI decisions looks like when reconstructed, and what a credible reasoning record would contain. This is not a documentation template problem. It is a professional judgment documentation problem that no current competitor is positioned to address.

**Independence**

Independence from vendors, platforms, certification bodies, and software tools is a required trust signal — stated once as a factual account of how the practice is organized, then demonstrated through the specific exclusions it names and holds precisely. The practice that can say specifically what it does not do — and hold that boundary without commercial qualification — demonstrates independence more credibly than any claim of independence.
