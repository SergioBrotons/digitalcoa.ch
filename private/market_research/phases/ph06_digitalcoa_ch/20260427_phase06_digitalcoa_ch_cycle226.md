## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### Real Language — How Senior Executives Describe Their Need for AI Governance Advice

**EN**

The senior executive in Geneva who needs AI governance advice arrives in one of two distinct states. The first is active alarm: a client question, a board concern raised by a colleague, an incident that made the exposure concrete. The second is quieter — a growing awareness that decisions have been made in their name about AI systems they did not personally select, and that this situation has probably been accumulating longer than they realized. Both states produce the same underlying need: to understand whether what exists inside their organization is defensible if examined.

The language they use is specific and personal. They do not say "our firm has an AI governance gap." They describe a situation. The vocabulary of risk and compliance is secondary; the primary language is about accountability, about their name on decisions, about what they can actually show if asked.

Triggers:

- "I approved the use of an AI system at a board meeting eight months ago. I could not explain to you today what it actually does or what data it processes. My name is in the minutes."
- "I inherited a practice. The AI setup was already there. I am responsible for decisions made before I arrived."
- "I had a board colleague ask me directly: do we know what we have running, and do we know what it is doing? I realized I could not answer that question with confidence."
- "A counterparty sent us a questionnaire with twelve AI-specific questions. We had no coherent answer for most of them. I realized we had been claiming AI competence without any actual documentation."
- "I have three tool subscriptions I did not personally authorize. Two of them are processing client-adjacent information. I am not sure what the third one does."
- "I need to know what I should be able to show exists — not for a regulator who has not asked yet, but so that if a question comes, I can answer it without discovering a gap in that moment."
- "Our team started using AI in client deliverables last year. We have not had a conversation internally about what we should and should not be using it for. That conversation needs to happen before something forces it."

Expectations:

The executive who reaches out expects a direct assessment: is what we have done so far defensible, and if not, what specifically makes it vulnerable. They are not looking for a framework presentation or a training session. They are looking for an advisor who can demonstrate, in the first conversation, that they have worked in situations that look like this — not in theory, not from a textbook, but in practice, in Geneva, at the level of personal liability that this executive is carrying. They expect the advisor to form and express a view during the meeting, not to take the matter under advisement.

The executive also expects discretion. The fact that they are asking — the specific nature of their exposure — is not information they want circulated. Any signal that this conversation is a sales qualification process rather than a professional consultation is immediately disqualifying.

Distrust:

Any advisor whose website could describe services to a bank, a hospital, and a cantonal government equally. Any first meeting that begins with a presentation rather than a conversation. Any positioning language that leads with the regulatory environment rather than the executive's specific situation. Compliance platforms that promise automated governance. Certification badges that do not specify what they certify against and for what type of exposure. Any advisor who cannot explain in one direct sentence what they do that a law firm does not do, and what they do that a Big Four practice does not do. The phrase "AI governance framework" as a primary product name — which signals the advisor is selling a framework rather than providing governance advice.

The unstated question:

"I did not get here through inaction — I got here through delegation and pace. The question is whether what accumulated under my oversight is something I can defend, and I need to know that before someone else finds the gap first."

## 02 — Market Signals

### Where Geneva Executive Demand Is Emerging

**The Delegation Accumulation Problem**

The dominant demand signal is not new AI adoption — it is unexamined AI that accumulated under delegation. The executive who is now seeking advice did not implement the AI systems running in their firm. They approved them, inherited them, or oversaw teams that adopted them at speed during 2023-2025 without a structured governance conversation at board or management level. The problem they now face is documentation debt: decisions made, outputs produced, client deliverables completed with AI assistance, all without an auditable record of what was done, by which system, at what level of human review.

This is distinct from the regulatory urgency story. The regulatory urgency narrative is "the rules are changing and I need to get compliant." The delegation accumulation narrative is "I did not know how much had accumulated, and I need to understand whether what exists is defensible." The first is a forward-looking compliance problem. The second is a present-tense accountability problem.

**What Is Working in Advisory Positioning**

What is working in Geneva advisory positioning for AI governance:

The independent advisor with a named, specific practice has strong resonance. Senior executives in Geneva respond to precision of category — not "AI governance consultant" but "board-level AI governance advisor working with managing directors and audit committee chairs in Geneva professional services." The specificity performs credibility in a way that category claims cannot.

The annual advisory relationship model is resonant. Executives who have experienced the gap-filling pattern of one-time audits followed by renewed uncertainty are receptive to the argument that governance requires an ongoing relationship, not a project. The framing "I need someone I can call before the question comes, not after" appears consistently and is underserved by firms organized around one-time engagements.

Written documentation as a primary deliverable is valued. The executive who receives a written assessment — clear, specific, applicable to their situation — has something they can use internally: to brief a board colleague, to respond to a counterparty questionnaire, to establish a record of active governance review. The written artifact removes the "hearsay problem" of oral advice and provides a document that can be shown to exist.

**What Is Not Working**

Compliance-platform positioning is failing with this ICP. The executive who has reached a level where personal liability is a live concern does not want a platform. They want a person who has specific experience in their type of situation. Platform positioning signals — automated governance, dashboard-based monitoring, certification badges — read as "this is not someone who works at my level" to the senior executive who is the target client.

Framework-first positioning is also failing. Advisors who lead with "ISO 42001" or "EU AI Act compliance methodology" or "AI governance framework implementation" are being evaluated on the wrong dimension by this ICP. The question the executive is asking is not "do you know frameworks?" It is "do you know what I am facing, and have you seen this before?"

Generic AI governance training programs for executives are not converting. The executive who needs personal governance clarity is not looking for a course. They are looking for a professional consultation. The distinction matters: training implies a knowledge deficit; consultation implies a judgment need. This ICP has a judgment need.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### How Other Advisors Are Positioning AI Governance in Geneva

**The Geneva Competitive Landscape**

The Geneva AI governance advisory market contains several distinct competitor types, each with a recognizable positioning approach and each leaving a distinct gap that Sergio's practice can occupy.

**Law Firms**

Large Geneva law firms (including international firms with Geneva offices) have begun offering AI governance advice as an extension of their technology or data protection practice. Their positioning is strongest on regulatory interpretation — what does the nFADP require, what does the Swiss AI Convention imply, what is the FDPIC's enforcement posture. Their weakness with this ICP is threefold: they tend to treat AI governance as a legal compliance question rather than a governance question; they bill at rates that make detailed internal review prohibitively expensive; and their output tends toward legal opinion rather than actionable governance documentation. The senior executive who needs a practical assessment of what their AI documentation actually looks like and whether it would hold up is not well-served by a legal opinion on what the regulations technically require.

**Big Four Firms**

The Big Four have AI governance service lines positioned at large organizations — banks, multinationals, cantonal institutions. Their positioning is built around scale: audit-style AI governance reviews, ISO 42001 certification support, EU AI Act compliance programs. Their weakness with the target ICP is structural. They are set up for large, multi-month engagements. Their output is standardized and report-based. The managing partner of a boutique firm or the board member of a mid-sized organization who needs a focused, discrete advisory conversation is not their target client. The cultural fit is wrong — executives describe feeling like a line item on a project plan rather than a person with a specific professional problem.

**Technology Vendors and SaaS Platforms**

AI tool vendors and governance SaaS platforms have begun marketing "AI governance solutions" — automated compliance monitoring, AI usage dashboards, policy document generators. These are positioned at IT departments and compliance teams. They are not credible to the senior executive who understands that governance is about accountability chains, decision attribution, and the specific exposure that attaches to their role. Platform positioning actively signals the wrong level of analysis.

**Swiss Professional Associations**

The SVA (Geneva Bar Association) and ORDRE (Chambre genevoise de la propriété intellectuelle) have begun issuing guidance on AI use by members, and some CLE programming exists. This is institutionally credible but practically limited. Association guidance is generalized and cannot address the specific situation of an individual firm or executive. The senior executive who needs a specific assessment does not find what they need in association guidance.

**What Gaps Remain**

The gap that remains unfilled is an independent, senior-level, Geneva-based advisory practice that works specifically with executives on personal AI governance exposure — not as a legal question, not as a compliance project, but as a governance and accountability question with personal liability dimensions. The practice that occupies this gap does not sell frameworks, does not offer platforms, does not position through certification badges, and does not approach AI governance as a training or education product. It provides professional advisory consultation to senior individuals who need a direct, specific assessment of what they have, what they are exposed to, and what they should do about it before a question arrives in a context they cannot control.

## 04 — Trust Signal Audit

### What Credibility, Process Clarity, Discretion, and Independence Signals This ICP Values Most

**Credibility Signals**

The senior executive evaluates AI governance advisors on three primary credibility dimensions, in order of weight:

First: Pattern specificity. The advisor must demonstrate that they have worked in situations that resemble the executive's specific situation — not by describing a client (which would violate discretion), but by showing that they understand the precise configuration of exposure the executive is facing. "I have worked with several managing directors who discovered AI tools in their firms after the fact" is credible. "I help firms with AI governance" is not.

Second: Independent judgment. The executive wants an advisor who will form a view during the conversation, not afterward. They want someone who will say directly whether what they have done so far is sufficient or insufficient, and why. Advisors who hedge consistently, qualify every statement, or promise to "review the materials and come back with an assessment" are perceived as unable or unwilling to provide the direct judgment this ICP is seeking.

Third: Geneva embeddedness. The advisor must demonstrate that they are operating in the Geneva professional environment — not as a remote consultant or a global firm with a Geneva office, but as someone who understands the specific institutional context: the cantonal professional bodies, the specific supervisory expectations, the peer community dynamics. This is not about geography. It is about whether the advisor understands the environment the executive operates in.

**Process Clarity**

The executive wants to understand the advisory process before committing. They want to know: what happens in the first meeting, what outputs they receive, what the written summary contains, what the relationship looks like after the initial briefing. Process clarity signals professionalism and reduces the executive's sense that they are entering an undefined commitment.

The first meeting should be clearly framed as a professional consultation, not a sales qualification. The executive should understand that they are receiving an advisor's time and professional judgment, not evaluating a product. The output of the first meeting should be a direct, specific view from the advisor — not a proposal for further work, but an initial professional opinion on the executive's situation.

**Discretion**

Discretion is non-negotiable for this ICP. The senior executive who is navigating an AI governance exposure does not want this fact known in the market. The advisor's entire conduct — how they communicate, what they say when they take a call, whether they follow up with marketing — must signal absolute discretion. Any advisor behavior that could result in the executive's situation being discussed, referenced, or disclosed — even indirectly — disqualifies the advisor.

**Independence**

Independence from vendors, platforms, certification bodies, and software tools is a required trust signal. The executive wants an advisor who has no commercial interest in recommending a specific AI tool, platform, or certification. Their interest is in the executive's governance position, not in driving the executive toward a product or a vendor relationship. Independence is best signaled by the advisor's practice structure: no vendor partnerships, no platform relationships, no referral arrangements with technology companies.

## 06 — Findings Summary

### 6 Net-New Insights on DigitalCoa.ch Positioning, Distinct from C214–C225 Phase 06

**Insight 1 — The Delegation Accumulation Entry Point**

The primary demand entry point for C226 is not regulatory anxiety or new adoption planning — it is the realization that AI usage has been accumulating under delegation without an accountable review. The executive who approved an AI tool at a board meeting eight months ago without understanding its actual processing logic, or who inherited a practice with an AI setup already in place, is now carrying an exposure they cannot describe. This is distinct from the "undocumented accumulation" framing in C225 (which was about client deliverables entering AI workflows) and from the "managing partner personal liability" framing in C224. The delegation accumulation entry point is specifically about governance oversight failure through delegation — not through inaction, but through the pace and distribution of decisions made under the executive's authority. Positioning language should name this directly: "decisions made in your name about AI systems you did not personally evaluate."

**Insight 2 — The Professional Inheritance Dimension**

A distinct and underserved segment is the executive who inherited an AI setup they did not build — mid-career managing directors, newly appointed board members, principals who acquired or succeeded into practices where AI was already integrated at some level. This segment faces a specific governance problem: they are personally accountable for decisions made before they arrived, with no documentation of what was done or why. The standard advisory framing ("you need to understand your AI governance exposure") misses the specific emotional and professional dimension of this situation: the inherited exposure problem. This segment is distinct from the organic adoption segment and requires different entry language. The advisory framing for this segment leads with succession and accountability: "what you inherited and what you are now responsible for."

**Insight 3 — The Geneva Peer Accountability Information Gap**

A demand driver that is growing and largely unaddressed in the market is the executive's need to understand what similar firms in Geneva actually have in place — not what they claim to have, but what they actually have. The counterparty questionnaire phenomenon (organizations receiving detailed AI questions from counterparties and having no coherent response) has created a class of executives who know they are behind their peers but cannot determine how far behind or in what dimensions. This "peer accountability gap" — the lack of a credible, discreet mechanism for understanding what responsible AI governance actually looks like at the boutique Geneva professional services level — is a specific positioning opportunity. Sergio's practice, with accumulated pattern knowledge across multiple Geneva engagements, is positioned to provide this comparative intelligence in a way that no other actor in the market can: not as gossip, but as structured advisory insight.

**Insight 4 — The Advisory Continuity Model as Trust Signal**

The annual advisory relationship model is not only a revenue structure — it is a primary trust signal for the senior executive who has experienced the pattern of one-time audits followed by renewed uncertainty. The executive who engages a consultant for a single AI governance review, receives a report, and then has no ongoing mechanism for questions as their AI usage evolves finds themselves back at the same point of uncertainty within twelve months. The argument for an ongoing advisory relationship is not a commercial upsell — it is a logical response to the nature of the problem: AI governance is not a project with an end date; it is an ongoing accountability responsibility that compounds over time. This framing — presented as a matter of professional seriousness rather than commercial retention — is resonant with the target ICP. The continuity model positions the practice as a trusted advisor rather than a service vendor.

**Insight 5 — The Decision Attribution Problem as Distinct Advisory Surface**

A specific, technically grounded governance question that is emerging as a distinct advisory surface is the decision attribution problem: when AI assists a professional decision, the question of where AI ends and professional accountability begins is not resolved by Swiss law or professional standards in a way that provides clear guidance to the individual. The executive who has AI-assisted decisions in their record — client recommendations, internal assessments, communications sent under their authority — cannot currently point to a Swiss authority that tells them whether their liability exposure for an AI-assisted decision differs from a fully human decision, and if so, how. This creates a specific advisory demand: executives who want a reasoned, Geneva-specific view on what "reasonable professional reliance on AI assistance" means in their context, with reference to the supervisory expectations that would apply if their decisions were reviewed. This is distinct from the "where AI ends and accountability begins" framing in C221 (which was about boutique professional services segment positioning) — here it is specifically about the decision attribution problem as a discrete advisory product for senior executives who carry personal liability exposure.

**Insight 6 — The Board-Level AI Literacy Gap as Positioning Anchor**

The board-level AI literacy gap — boards that know they need governance oversight but cannot currently evaluate what good looks like — is a specific and growing positioning anchor. Unlike the "C-suite AI governance gap" framing (which is generic), the board literacy gap is specific: it describes a class of audit committee chairs and board members who are being asked to oversee AI risk without any structural basis for evaluating what they are being shown. They cannot assess whether the AI governance documentation their management has prepared is adequate because they do not have a reference point for what adequate looks like. This creates a specific advisory demand: not "help us implement AI governance" but "help us understand what we should be looking for and how we should be evaluating it." Sergio's practice, positioned at the board advisory level, is uniquely suited to address this demand: a board-level briefing on AI governance oversight responsibilities, framed not as training but as a professional consultation on what boards in Geneva should be able to ask, expect, and evaluate.
