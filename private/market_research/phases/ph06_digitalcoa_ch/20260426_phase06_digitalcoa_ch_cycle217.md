## 01 — ICP Language: How Senior Executives Describe Their Need for AI Governance Advice

### EN — English

**Trigger moments:**

The Geneva senior executive arrives at AI governance through a specific moment of personal exposure — not a general awareness of AI risk, but a situation where they realize they have signed something, approved something, or endorsed something without the understanding required to do so responsibly. The board chair who approved the AI vendor contract without knowing what the system actually does in production. The CFO who signed the risk committee charter two quarters ago and has since been asked to explain to a board member why AI models appear in the risk register with no board-level governance framework behind them. The independent professional who accepted a board nomination and realized, three weeks in, that AI oversight is on the agenda and they have no preparation for the question.

The moment is not "AI governance is a strategic priority." The moment is "I am accountable for something I did not understand when I agreed to be accountable for it."

**Language they use:**

The framing is personal, immediate, and carries a specific undertone of self-concern — not organizational concern, but personal exposure. "I approved this and I don't think I understood what I was approving." "My name is on the AI resolution and I could not explain it to the board if someone asked." "I have been asked to join a technology committee and I don't know what questions to ask." "Our board chair asked me to look into AI governance and I realized I don't know where to start and I don't know who to ask."

The language of "what good looks like" recurs frequently — not abstract governance quality, but specifically: what is the standard of care I am being held to, and does what we are doing meet it? The executive who asks this question is usually not asking for a legal opinion. They are asking for a practical judgment from someone who has navigated this before.

**What they expect:**

They expect to encounter someone who has sat in the same room. Not someone who has studied board AI governance in the abstract — someone who has been in the board meeting where the AI question was raised for the first time, who has helped a board member prepare an answer to a question they were not expecting, who has sat across from a FINMA supervisor and explained an AI model governance framework. The specificity of that experience is what they are evaluating.

They expect to leave the first conversation with a clearer picture of where they actually stand — not a framework, not a legal summary, but an honest assessment of their current position relative to where they need to be. They want the advisor to form a view during the meeting and be willing to say it.

**What they distrust:**

Anyone whose first meeting is a qualification call. Any advisor whose website uses the words "holistic," "ecosystem," or "cutting-edge." Vendors who arrive with ROI models. Law firms whose AI governance expertise appeared in the last twelve months. Any platform or tool presented as an AI governance solution. The advisory firm that cannot clearly articulate what they do that a compliance firm does not do.

**The unstated question:**

The executive who has reached out is often carrying a question they do not say directly: they want to know whether their instinct about their organization's AI situation is correct. They have a private judgment — about whether the AI risk is real, about whether their board is taking it seriously enough, about whether the advice they are receiving internally is sound — and they want a credible second opinion before they act on it. The advisory conversation that acknowledges this unstated question and addresses it directly converts.

## 02 — Market Signals: Where Geneva Executive Demand Is Emerging — What's Working, What's Not

**Where demand is appearing:**

Two distinct demand clusters are crystallizing in the Geneva executive AI governance advisory market in 2026.

**The board accountability cluster** is driven by the personal fiduciary exposure that individual board members are beginning to feel. This is not the same as organizational compliance demand. The individual board member who is carrying the question — "am I personally exposed, have I approved something I did not understand, what are my actual fiduciary obligations when AI appears in the risk register" — is a different buyer than the compliance team that is managing organizational obligations. This demand is appearing at the individual board member level, often without the organization's knowledge, which has direct implications for how outreach and intake should be structured.

**The international organization cluster** is driven by a different pressure: AI procurement governance and staff accountability for organizations that do not fall under a single national regulatory framework. UN agencies, international NGOs, and Geneva-domiciled multilaterals are navigating AI governance questions that no Swiss regulatory framework was designed to address, and for which there are no established advisory options calibrated to their governance structure.

**What is working:**

The independent advisory voice continues to find a warm reception precisely because the market is so thin at this level. The Geneva executive who has been through enough AI governance conversations to recognize genuine independence — versus vendor-adjacent positioning — immediately values the independent voice.

The publications-as-intelligence mechanism continues to work as a top-of-funnel signal. The executive who reads a Sergio publication and recognizes the situation described because they have encountered it — that recognition is itself a qualification event. They arrive at the introductory call partially qualified: they understand what the practice does, they are already in the register of relevant questions, and they come with a specific situation rather than a general awareness.

Referral from within the Geneva professional ecosystem remains the highest-converting channel. The personal trust network in Geneva — among board members, in the financial community, through professional society relationships — operates at a level that digital channels cannot replicate. The website functions as the credentialing surface that confirms what the referral has already established.

**What is not working:**

Compliance-positioned offerings pitched to board-level executives do not convert. The board member who needs help fulfilling personal fiduciary obligations does not want a compliance audit — they want someone who understands the judgment question they are facing.

Any first interaction that feels like a sales process. The executive who senses that the introductory call is designed to qualify them for a proposal — rather than designed to help them determine whether this is the right advisor for their situation — disengages. The Geneva senior executive has enough advisory relationships to recognize the difference between a genuine exploratory conversation and a qualification call wearing an exploratory conversation's clothing.

The advisor who cannot answer the question "what do you do that a law firm does not do" with precision. The executive who cannot draw a clear line between the advisory offering and what they could get from a legal firm — either a law firm's AI practice or a compliance consultancy — does not have a clear reason to engage the independent advisor.

**The demand signal that is not yet being served:**

The Geneva executive who needs a judgment resource they can draw on continuously, across a multi-year horizon — not a project, not a compliance review, but an ongoing advisory relationship they can use as AI governance questions arise across the regulatory calendar. No current market participant has clearly positioned the annual advisory relationship as a discrete offering with a defined structure.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis: How Other Advisors Are Positioning AI Governance in Geneva

**The four current positioning types:**

**Law firms:** Structurally credible on legal authority and privilege. Structurally limited for this ICP because their frame is legal — they provide legal opinions on obligations, not governance judgment on how to fulfill those obligations in a specific organizational context. The board member who needs to understand their fiduciary obligations does not need a legal opinion; they need someone who can help them think through what good governance looks like for their board specifically. Law firms are the right answer for specific legal questions; they are the wrong answer for the ongoing judgment support that the board-level executive is actually looking for. Their structural limitation is compounded by their fee structures, their conflict-check requirements, and the fact that they are not set up for the kind of open-ended exploratory relationship that the executive actually needs before a specific legal question has crystallized.

**Big Four consultancies:** Credible on scale, project delivery, and brand recognition. Not credible on independence — the perception that Big Four AI practices generate implementation revenue and maintain technology vendor relationships that create conflicts with pure advisory positioning is well-established among Geneva senior executives. The executive who has been pitched by a Big Four AI governance team recognizes the product presentation. The quality of the individual team member is irrelevant to this perception — it is structural.

**Boutique governance and risk firms:** More credible on independence than the Big Four, but most are positioned at the compliance team or technical risk management level — not at the board level. A boutique that specializes in AI model risk governance frameworks for internal risk teams is doing a different job than what the individual board member needs. The positioning gap between "AI governance for compliance teams" and "AI governance for board members" is significant and largely unaddressed.

**Independent advisors:** The positioning that is most naturally aligned with this ICP's needs, but most independent advisors have not clearly articulated the board-level positioning, the annual relationship architecture, or the specific scope boundaries that would make them the obvious choice over a boutique consultancy or a law firm. Independence is necessary; it is not sufficient without a clear statement of what the practice does, at what level, for whom.

**The specific gap no one is filling:**

The individual board member with personal fiduciary AI exposure — who needs a judgment resource they can access without triggering organizational compliance processes — is not a served buyer in the current Geneva market. This person is often operating without their organization's knowledge — they have a personal question about their own accountability exposure, and they need a discreet advisory relationship that does not involve their organization's legal team or compliance department. This is a buyer that no current market participant is designed to serve.

## 04 — Trust Signal Audit: What This ICP Values Most

**Expertise credibility — the form that matters:**

The senior executive ICP is not evaluating a credential. They are evaluating evidence of judgment. What creates trust is specific detail that can only come from direct experience: not "I have worked with Swiss boards on AI governance" but "I have been in the board meeting where the AI question was raised for the first time and the room went quiet because no one had prepared for it — here is how that conversation typically unfolds and what a defensible board-level response looks like." That level of specific description — the kind that only comes from having actually been in the situation — is the credibility signal that cannot be fabricated and that the executive uses to verify the claim of expertise.

The digital expression of this credibility is the quality of the thinking in the publications. When a Geneva board member reads a Sergio publication and recognizes the situation described because they have lived it — that is the trust signal working. The specificity of the regulatory analysis, the precision of the Swiss institutional framing, the recognition of how decisions are actually made in Swiss financial institutions and international organizations — that specificity is itself the evidence of pattern recognition.

**Process clarity — the type that removes the last barrier:**

The executive who is considering the introductory call has one remaining hesitation that process clarity addresses: what happens if we are not the right fit? The advisor who can answer that question directly — who can describe what the exit looks like, what the obligations are if the engagement does not proceed — removes the last hesitation that sophisticated executives carry into advisory relationships. Most executives have been in an advisory relationship where the exit was awkward or unclear. The practice that addresses this proactively demonstrates the kind of experience that makes the relationship safe to enter.

**Discretion — the practice, not the policy:**

The senior executive is not concerned about whether the advisor will sign an NDA. They are concerned about whether the advisor understands the practice of discretion in a professional context — what it means to hold a conversation in confidence, what it means to not reference the engagement in any form, what it means to behave as if the content of the relationship does not exist outside the relationship itself. The website copy that signals this understanding — without legal framing — is the discretion signal that matters. "I do not reference specific clients or engagements. What we discuss stays in the room."

**Independence — structural and behavioral:**

The structural dimension: the advisor who has no affiliate products, no implementation revenue stream, no certification program affiliation, and no technology vendor relationship. This structural independence needs to be stated once, clearly — not as a marketing claim but as a factual account of how the practice is organized. The executive who understands how the economics of the practice work — and sees that there is no revenue source other than advisory fees — will stop worrying about conflicts and focus on the substance of the conversation.

The behavioral dimension: the questions the advisor asks in the first meeting, the references they do not make to technology products, the precision with which they describe their scope boundaries. Independence is demonstrated in the meeting; it is not claimed on the website.

**The limited engagement signal:**

The practice that explicitly frames its selectivity — "I work with a small number of organizations each year" — signals several things simultaneously: that the advisor is selective, that the work is substantive rather than volume-based, that the client who engages this practice is not one of many. For the Geneva senior executive who has been a client of large practices and felt like a number, the explicit framing of selectivity aligns with the executive's own self-image. This framing is a trust signal, not a capacity constraint.
