**How executives describe their need for AI governance advice:**

*"I've had three briefings on AI governance in the last eighteen months. I have three documents. I don't think they tell the same story, and I don't have a way to know whether they're consistent with each other."*
The executive who has received multiple advisory inputs is experiencing briefing archaeology — accumulated advisory documents that may or may not cohere, without a methodology for evaluating consistency across providers. This is distinct from having no documentation. It is a problem of too many documents from too many sources with no integration framework.

*"I know what I should be building. I don't know whether what I've already built counts."*
The executive who has attempted to build AI governance documentation is now uncertain whether the effort they've already made would be recognized as adequate or would need to be rebuilt from scratch. The question is not whether to start, but whether existing work holds.

*"I need to make a decision about an AI tool and I don't have anyone inside the room who can tell me what I'm actually deciding."*
The procurement decision surface as a discrete advisory occasion. The executive is making a consequential decision about an AI tool without an internal advisor capable of explaining what the decision commits them to. The question is not which tool, but what the tool selection commits the organization to in terms of ongoing accountability.

*"The person who set this up left. I can see the gaps from here, but I can't see what a filled gap looks like."*
Staff departure as both operational gap and documentation revelation. The executive can perceive that the documentation is inadequate but lacks a reference point for what adequate documentation would include. They see the absence without seeing the target.

*"I need someone who will tell me what they actually think, knowing that I'm going to test it against what I already know."*
The executive wants a real opinion. Not a framework, not a template, not a risk register. An actual judgment about their specific situation, grounded in Geneva-specific context, that the executive can evaluate against their own knowledge. The advisory relationship they are describing is not a service relationship — it is a peer-quality intellectual relationship.

*"I want to know if what we're doing would look right to someone who already knows how this works in Geneva."*
The Geneva-specific question. Not "is this compliant" but "would this look right in Geneva." The executive is asking whether their position would be credible to someone who knows the informal standard, not just the published rule.

*"I don't want a document that becomes another document I have to manage. I want something that works."*
Documentation fatigue. The executive who has accumulated AI governance documents does not want more documentation. They want the documentation they have to function. The desire is for working documentation, not more documents.

- An actual opinion on their specific situation, not a framework applied generically
- Geneva-specific judgment from someone who knows how the informal standard actually forms

## Deliverable 02 — Market Signals

### Where Geneva executive demand is emerging

**1. Briefing archaeology as a discrete demand occasion.**
Executives who have received multiple AI governance advisory inputs — from different providers, at different times, in different formats — are arriving with accumulated documentation that they suspect may not cohere. The briefing archaeology problem is not about the quality of individual briefings; it is about the absence of an integration framework that would allow the executive to evaluate whether their accumulated advisory inputs form a coherent position. The market signal is executives with three or more prior AI governance advisory engagements who are now seeking a coherence evaluation, not another standalone briefing.

**2. Pre-procurement AI advisory as a distinct occasion from post-implementation review.**
AI governance advisory has been positioned primarily as a retrospective or compliance function — reviewing what exists, assessing what has been built, preparing for what is coming. The emerging demand signal is for pre-procurement advisory: an independent judgment on whether a proposed AI tool commitment is appropriate given the organization's existing accountability architecture, before the procurement decision is finalized. The executive making a tool selection does not want a compliance review; they want an independent view of what they're committing to.

**3. The Geneva first-call problem as a distinct conversion dynamic.**
Executives who face an AI governance question for the first time have a specific conversion behavior: they call someone they know before they search for someone they don't know. The first call determines the trajectory. The advisor who receives the first call is working with a qualitatively different demand — an executive who has not yet framed the problem, who is still forming the question, who has not yet decided what kind of help they need. The first-call positioning is a distinct market positioning category that has not been explicitly addressed in Geneva advisory market positioning.

**4. Executive time constraint as a product design variable, not an external constraint.**
Geneva executives are increasingly explicitly naming their time constraint as a product design requirement — not "I don't have time" as an objection, but "design the service around the time I actually have." The briefing format, the written summary format, the follow-up structure — all must be calibrated to executive decision-making windows, not to the advisor's preferred service architecture. The market signal is executives who are evaluating advisory services partly on whether the service format matches their actual availability.

**5. The "would this look right in Geneva" question as a distinct quality calibration.**
Geneva executives are increasingly using a specific evaluation criterion: not "is this compliant" but "would this look right to someone who already knows how this works in Geneva." The question reflects a sophisticated understanding that the Geneva informal standard is forming ahead of published guidance and that credibility with Geneva-specific professionals requires alignment with that informal standard, not just with published regulatory text. This is a demand signal for advisors who can demonstrate Geneva-specific judgment, not just regulatory knowledge.

**6. Documentation fatigue creating resistance to new advisory documents.**
The executive who has accumulated AI governance documents is not looking for more documents. They are looking for the documents they have to actually work — to be usable in the conditions they're actually in, not just accurate in theory. The resistance is not to advisory engagement; it is to document production. The market signal is executives who specifically request that the advisory output be designed for real-time usability, not for comprehensive reference.

### What is working or not in advisory positioning

**Working:**
- Named problem categories that the executive recognizes from their own experience before the first call
- Service format description that accounts for the executive's time constraint as a design variable
- Bilingual capability presented as structural advisory capacity, not as a localization feature
- Pricing that performs the qualification function — specific price for specific deliverable signals advisor has thought precisely about what they are selling
- Written summary designed for the executive's actual use conditions, not for comprehensive reference

**Not working:**
- Framework introduction as the first step in every engagement — executives who have already engaged with frameworks do not want another introduction
- Service descriptions that describe what the advisor does without describing what the client receives and how the output is designed to be used
- Bilingual positioning that treats French as translation from English, not as primary analytical mode
- Pricing that appears designed to create negotiation room rather than to perform qualification
- Any language that suggests the executive's existing work would need to be discarded rather than evaluated for what it already covers

## Deliverable 04 — Trust Signal Audit

### What credibility, process clarity, discretion, and independence signals this ICP values most

**1. The first-call occasion as the primary trust evaluation moment.**
The executive who calls with a nascent AI governance question — not yet a documented problem, not yet a formal trigger, still forming the question — is evaluating the advisor on different criteria than the executive who arrives with a defined problem. The first-call executive wants to know whether the advisor can help them think about the situation before they've committed to a framing. Advisors who can engage with the nascent question — who don't require the executive to have already defined the problem before they can contribute — are trusted in the first-call occasion. Advisors who respond to the nascent question by asking diagnostic questions before offering framework language demonstrate a different kind of credibility than advisors who offer immediate structure.

**2. Geneva institutional context as the credential that cannot be faked.**
The executive who asks the "would this look right in Geneva" question is testing for a specific form of knowledge — not regulatory text knowledge, but the knowledge of how the informal standard forms, who sets it, and what the Geneva professional community reads as credible versus merely compliant. This knowledge is demonstrated through references to Geneva-specific institutional dynamics: carrier underwriting behavior as a standard-setting mechanism, the informal circuit's role in forming working standards, the specific quality of question that a sophisticated Geneva counterparty would ask versus a formulaic one. Advisors who demonstrate Geneva institutional context in their published content and in their first conversation are read as having been inside the Geneva professional environment, not just advising about it from outside.

**3. The written summary as a peer conversation artifact, not just a client record.**
The executive who receives a written summary evaluates it not only as a record for their own use but as a document they would be comfortable showing to a peer. The quality of the written summary determines the quality of the peer conversation that follows. If the summary is precise, Geneva-specific, and demonstrates judgment about the specific situation, the executive will reference it in peer conversations. If it is generic, framework-applied, or contains language the executive would not use themselves, they will not reference it. The trust signal is embedded in whether the executive would use the summary as a reference point in their own peer conversations. The test is: would you show this to a colleague you trust? If yes, the document functions as demand generation. If no, it does not.

**4. The behavioral exclusion list as the primary independence signal.**
The executive reads the exclusion list before reading the service description. A named, specific list of what the practice does not do — does not work with AI tool vendors, does not implement governance systems, does not accept referral arrangements from technology companies, does not provide certification pathway services — performs independence more credibly than any statement of independence. The exclusion list must be specific: general exclusions are read as policy language; named exclusions are read as structural commitments. The difference is the executive's confidence that the commitment would hold under commercial pressure.

**5. Pricing specificity as a qualification and trust signal simultaneously.**
The executive who reads a specific price — not a range, not "starting at," not a variable quotation process — attached to a specifically described service with a specific output format evaluates the practice differently than the executive who reads vague pricing. Specific pricing signals that the practice has thought precisely about what it is selling and has committed to a defined deliverable. The executive reads this as a proxy for how the practice thinks about everything else. Vague pricing creates uncertainty that transfers to content quality perception. Specific pricing performs the qualification function — the executive who is right for the service recognizes the price as appropriate; the executive who is not reads it as a signal to look elsewhere.

**6. The "what I do not do and why" language as a trust amplifier.**
Advisors who explain not just what they do not do but why they have made those exclusions — because the excluded activity would create a conflict with the primary advisory function, because it would compromise the discretion guarantee, because the practice's methodology depends on independence from those relationships — are demonstrating the same quality of reasoning they are offering to apply to the executive's situation. The reasoning about their own exclusions demonstrates the same analytical rigor the executive needs. This is a trust signal that cannot be replicated by advisors who simply list exclusions without explaining the logic.

**7. Pre-procurement advisory capability as a distinct trust signal.**
The executive who is evaluating an AI tool selection and has access to pre-procurement advisory — someone who can explain what the tool commitment would require in terms of ongoing accountability before the selection is finalized — reads the availability of this service as evidence of the practice's structural independence from AI tool vendors. An advisor who would advise against a tool purchase in the pre-procurement moment is demonstrating a form of independence that post-procurement advisory cannot demonstrate. The trust signal is in the pre-procurement moment: the advisor who would tell the executive the tool is not appropriate is trusted more than the advisor who only reviews tools already selected.

**8. The practice's own documentation as the demonstration of the product.**
The written materials the practice produces — the website, the Brotons.net content, the written summaries produced for prior clients — are themselves the demonstration of what the executive would receive. The executive who reads the published content and evaluates it against their own knowledge of the subject is using the content as a work sample. If the content demonstrates Geneva-specific depth, precise problem naming, and language the executive recognizes from their own experience, the executive arrives at the first call pre-sold on the practice's quality. If the content is generic, framework-applied, or includes language the executive would not use, the first call becomes a qualification conversation rather than an engagement conversation. The published content is the primary trust artifact before any engagement occurs.

## Deliverable 06 — Findings Summary

### Six Net-New Insights on DigitalCoa.ch Positioning (Distinct from C214–C249 Phase 06)

**Insight 1: Briefing archaeology as a discrete demand occasion distinct from first-time advisory engagement.**
Executives who have received multiple AI governance advisory inputs — from different providers, in different formats, at different times — are arriving with accumulated documentation that they suspect may not cohere but have no framework for evaluating. The briefing archaeology problem is not about the quality of individual briefings; it is about the absence of an integration function that would allow the executive to understand whether their accumulated advisory inputs form a defensible position or three contradictory partial positions. The service design for briefing archaeology is distinct from first-time advisory: the executive already has content, and the question is whether it coheres, not whether it exists. DigitalCoa.ch positioning should include explicit language for the executive who has had multiple prior briefings and is seeking a coherence evaluation — naming the problem creates the demand occasion.

**Insight 2: Pre-procurement AI advisory as a distinct timing occasion from post-implementation review.**
AI governance advisory has been positioned primarily as retrospective or compliance-oriented — reviewing what exists, assessing what has been built, preparing for what is coming. The emerging demand signal is for pre-procurement advisory: independent judgment on whether a proposed AI tool commitment is appropriate given the organization's existing accountability architecture, before the procurement decision is finalized. The executive making a tool selection is not asking for a compliance review; they are asking what they're committing to in terms of ongoing accountability documentation before they sign. This is a more valuable advisory position — the advisor who advises against a tool before purchase is demonstrating a form of independence that post-procurement advisory cannot demonstrate. The pre-procurement occasion is distinct in timing, in conversational framing, and in the advisor's demonstrated independence.

**Insight 3: The written summary as a peer conversation artifact with demand-generation function.**
The executive who receives a written summary evaluates it not only as a personal record but as a document they would be comfortable referencing in peer conversation. If the summary is precise, Geneva-specific, and uses language the executive would use themselves, it becomes a reference point in the peer conversations that generate the next prospect. If it is generic, framework-applied, or contains language the executive would not use, they will not reference it — and the summary fails to perform its demand-generation function. This means the written summary is not just a deliverable; it is the mechanism by which the advisory product generates subsequent advisory products. The quality of the summary determines the quality of the peer conversation that generates the next call. This is a compounding mechanism: each well-designed summary increases the probability of the next engagement.

**Insight 4: Executive time constraint as a product design variable, not a service delivery constraint.**
Geneva executives are increasingly naming their time constraint explicitly as a product design requirement — not "I don't have time" as an objection to be overcome, but as a specification for how the service must be designed. The practice that designs its service around the executive's time constraint is differentiating on a variable that most advisors treat as an external obstacle.

**Insight 6: The first-call occasion as a distinct conversion dynamic requiring different positioning than the documented-problem occasion.**
Executives who face an AI governance question for the first time have a specific conversion behavior: they call someone they know before they search for someone they don't know.
