## 01 — ICP Language: How Senior Executives Describe Their Need for AI Governance Advice

### EN — English

**Trigger moments:**

The Geneva senior executive arrives at AI governance advice through a specific type of personal exposure moment — not abstract risk awareness, but the realization that their name, their signature, or their vote is attached to a decision involving AI that they cannot explain to someone who asks. The board member who approved the AI vendor contract because the technology committee recommended it, and who has since wondered whether that approval exposed them personally. The CFO who discovered that AI models appear in the risk committee charter and has no framework for what that means for their personal liability. The independent professional who accepted a board nomination and found, three weeks in, that AI oversight was on the agenda and they had no basis for evaluating it. The executive who attended a Geneva conference and recognized, for the first time, that the questions being raised in the room were the same ones they had been carrying privately — and realized that recognizing it and not acting on it had its own risk.

The trigger is never framed as a strategic priority. It is always framed as a personal accountability question: am I personally exposed, and do I have what I need to answer the question before it is asked in the room where it matters.

**Language they use:**

The framing is personal and carries a specific urgency that organizational risk language does not capture. "I signed off on the AI system and I don't think I understood what I was signing." "Our risk register has AI in it and the board has no framework for what that means at board level." "I was asked to join the technology committee and I don't know what questions I'm supposed to be asking." "A board member asked me what our AI governance framework looks like and I had nothing to say." "I realized I was the only person in the room without a technical background and no one seemed to notice." "Our legal team is giving us answers that don't address what the board actually needs to do."

The language of personal exposure recurs: not "our organization needs an AI governance framework" but "I need to understand what I am personally accountable for." The executive who uses this framing is not asking for a compliance product. They are asking for a judgment on their personal position.

**What they expect:**

They expect to encounter someone who has been in the same room — who has sat in the board meeting where AI was raised for the first time, who has helped a board member prepare an answer to a question they were not expecting, who has sat across from a regulator and explained an AI model governance framework. The specificity of that experience is what they are evaluating. They want to ask a precise question and receive a precise answer from someone who has navigated the question before.

They expect to leave the first conversation knowing where they actually stand — not a framework, not a legal summary, but an honest assessment of their current position relative to the standard they are being held to. They want the advisor to form a view during the meeting and be willing to say it.

**What they distrust:**

Anyone whose first meeting is a qualification call. Any advisor whose website uses the words "holistic," "ecosystem," or "cutting-edge." Vendors who arrive with ROI models. Law firms whose AI governance expertise appeared in the last twelve months. Any platform or tool presented as an AI governance solution. The advisory firm that cannot clearly articulate what it does that a law firm does not do and what it does that a compliance firm does not do.

**The unstated question:**

The executive who has reached out is often carrying a question they do not say directly: they want to know whether their private judgment about their organization's AI situation is correct. They have an instinct — about whether the AI risk is real, about whether their board is taking it seriously enough, about whether the advice they are receiving internally is sound — and they want a credible second opinion before they act. The advisory conversation that acknowledges this unstated question and addresses it directly is the conversation that converts.

## 02 — Market Signals: Where Geneva Executive Demand for AI Governance Advice Is Emerging

**Where demand is appearing right now:**

Two signals are converging to create acute executive-level demand for AI governance advice in Geneva that did not exist at this intensity six months ago.

**The personal board liability signal.** Swiss association law does not yet have a codified AI-specific fiduciary standard, but the direction of travel is becoming legible. Board members who approved AI vendor contracts without understanding what the system did in production are beginning to recognize that their organizational liability protections may not extend to AI-specific oversight failures. The Geneva executive who has read the Federal Council's AI liability legislative proposal — or who has been briefed on it by their general counsel — is now asking whether their current board practices would be defensible if an AI failure occurred and a court examined what the board knew, when it knew it, and what it did about it. This is a different question from organizational compliance. It is a question about personal fiduciary exposure, and it is reaching individual board members directly.

**The AI supply chain cascade signal.** Geneva organizations — particularly NGOs, international organizations, and professional services firms — are increasingly exposed to AI governance failures through their implementing partners, vendors, and platform providers. An international organization using an AI-enabled HR platform from a US vendor faces AI governance questions it cannot answer: what training data was used, what decisions does the system make autonomously, what happens when it fails, who is accountable. The executive who discovered this exposure after deployment is now looking for someone who can help them understand the scope of what they signed and what a defensible response looks like.

**What is escalating:**

Geneva executives are spending real time and money on AI governance concerns in three specific areas. Advisory spend on board-level AI governance briefings — individual sessions where a board member or senior executive gets a personal briefing on their accountability position — is increasing. This is distinct from organizational compliance spend; it is personal investment in understanding one's own exposure. Geneva law firms are fielding more questions from board members about their personal AI liability position, separate from any organizational engagement. And Geneva risk committees are beginning to request AI governance briefings as a standing agenda item, which means the executive who has to present to the risk committee needs to understand the AI governance question well enough to face it publicly.

**What is concrete and happening:**

FINMA's supervisory dialogue is increasingly touching on AI model risk at the board level — not as a technical question for the risk team, but as a governance question for the board itself. Geneva executives who have been through recent supervisory dialogues report that AI model governance is appearing as a line of inquiry without a corresponding board-level framework to demonstrate. The gap between what FINMA is asking and what the board has in place is creating immediate, concrete demand for advisory support that helps boards close that gap before the next dialogue.

**Where executives are actually spending money:**

The spend that is actually occurring is not on AI governance software platforms or compliance frameworks. It is on individual advisory relationships — board-level briefings, personal accountability assessments, and discrete decision-support engagements — that help executives understand their own position. This is a different economic model from compliance software or framework implementation, and it is the model that the Geneva senior executive is most willing to pay for: personal clarity on personal accountability, from an advisor they trust, at a fee that reflects the seniority of the service.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis: The Geneva AI Governance Advisory Competitive Landscape

**How other advisors are positioning:**

**Law firms:** Credible on legal authority and privilege. Structurally positioned to provide legal opinions on obligations — what the law says, what the regulatory framework requires, what liability exposure looks like under Swiss association law. Their limitation for this ICP is precise: they provide legal opinions, not governance judgments. The board member who needs to understand what their fiduciary obligations are in a general sense will find law firms useful. The board member who needs to understand what good governance looks like for their specific board, how to structure AI oversight that is operationally defensible, and how to make the specific decisions they are facing — law firms are not designed to provide that. Their frame is retrospective and legal, not forward-looking and governance-oriented. They answer "what are my legal obligations" rather than "what should I do given where this is heading." For the Geneva executive who is asking the second question, a law firm is the wrong instrument.

**Big Four:** Credible on scale, project delivery, and brand recognition. Structurally vendor-adjacent in a way that is visible to sophisticated Geneva executives. The Big Four AI governance practices maintain technology vendor relationships, generate implementation revenue, and operate practices that are not structured for independent judgment. The Geneva senior executive who has been through a Big Four AI governance pitch recognizes the product presentation — the slide deck, the use-case examples, the ROI framing — regardless of the individual quality of the team presenting. The perception is structural, not personal. The Big Four are not serving the individual board member as a buyer; they are serving the organizational compliance function. The board member who needs personal accountability clarity will not find it in a Big Four engagement.

**Boutique governance and risk firms:** More credible on independence than the Big Four, and more specialized. The structural limitation for this ICP is that most boutiques are positioned at the compliance team level or the technical AI model risk level — not at the board member as an individual buyer. A boutique that specializes in AI model risk governance frameworks for internal risk teams is doing work that is useful for risk committees, not for the board member who needs to understand their personal oversight obligations. The positioning gap between "AI governance for compliance teams" and "AI governance for individual board members" is significant. Most boutiques have not made the leap to the individual buyer level, and their positioning language reflects that.

**The specific gap no one is filling:**

The individual board member — or senior executive — who needs a judgment resource they can access as a person, not as an organizational function, is not served by any current market participant.

Law firms serve the organization's legal function. Big Four serve the organization's compliance and risk functions. Boutiques serve the organization's risk management function. No one is serving the individual who carries personal fiduciary accountability for AI decisions and needs a discreet, independent advisory resource to help them understand and fulfill that accountability.

This gap is compounded by a second structural problem: none of the current market participants are designed for the kind of open-ended, confidential, no-commitment exploratory relationship that the individual board member needs before a specific legal question has crystallized. The board member who is asking "am I personally exposed" is not yet in a legal dispute. They are in a governance uncertainty moment. They need judgment support, not legal representation. No current market participant is designed for that moment.

**What positioning gap Sergio can fill:**

The independent advisor who explicitly positions for the individual board member as a distinct buyer — with a discrete offering, a confidential intake pathway, and a format designed for personal accountability clarity — occupies a space that no current competitor has claimed. This is not a matter of being better at what others do. It is a matter of doing something that no one else is doing: serving the individual buyer with governance judgment, in a relationship structure that does not require organizational involvement or legal representation.

## 04 — Trust Signal Audit: What This ICP Values Most

**Expertise credibility — what works:**

The senior executive ICP for DigitalCoa.ch is not evaluating a credential. They are evaluating evidence that the advisor has operated at their level and navigated situations like theirs. The credibility signal that works is specific detail that can only come from direct experience: not "I advise boards on AI governance" but "I have been in the board meeting where AI model risk was raised by FINMA for the first time, and here is what that conversation typically looks like and what a defensible board-level response requires." That level of specificity — the kind that only exists because the advisor has actually been in the situation — is the credibility signal that cannot be fabricated and that the executive uses to verify the claim.

The digital expression of this credibility is publication quality. When a Sergio publication demonstrates precise understanding of how a Swiss regulatory development interacts with board-level accountability — that precision is the evidence of pattern recognition. The executive who recognizes the situation described because they have lived it reads that publication as the most credible trust signal available, because it allows them to evaluate the judgment directly.

**Process clarity — what works:**

The executive who is considering the introductory call has one remaining hesitation that process clarity removes: what happens if we are not the right fit? The advisor who can describe what the exit looks like, what the obligations are if the engagement does not proceed, and what the introductory conversation is actually designed to do — removes the last barrier. The Geneva senior executive has been in advisory relationships where the exit was awkward or unclear. The practice that addresses this proactively demonstrates the kind of experience that makes the relationship safe to enter. This signal works. The absence of this signal — vague language about "exploring how we might work together" — creates hesitation that is difficult to overcome.

**Discretion — what works and what doesn't:**

What works: a direct statement that the advisor does not reference specific clients or engagements, and that what is discussed stays in the room. This statement works because it demonstrates that the advisor understands what the executive needs in order to speak freely. The executive who cannot speak freely in the first meeting cannot work effectively with the advisor.

What does not move the needle: NDAs, legal confidentiality language, or formal data protection statements. The Geneva senior executive is not evaluating legal structures — they are evaluating whether the advisor understands the professional practice of discretion. Legal language signals that the advisor is thinking about the legal structure of confidentiality rather than the professional practice of it. The former is table stakes; the latter is what the executive is actually looking for.

**Independence — structural and behavioral:**

Structural independence — no vendor affiliation, no implementation revenue, no affiliate products — needs to be stated once, clearly, as a factual account of how the practice is organized. The executive who understands the economics of the practice — and sees that advisory fees are the only revenue source — stops worrying about conflicts and focuses on the substance. This works as a structural trust signal.

Behavioral independence — demonstrated in the first meeting through the questions asked, the references not made, the precision of scope boundaries — is equally important and cannot be replaced by a structural statement. Independence is confirmed in the meeting, not on the website.

**What does not move the needle with this ICP:**

Certification badges. Industry awards. Client logos. Testimonial quotes. The senior executive who operates at board level can identify credential padding immediately and discounts it accordingly. These signals are designed for procurement processes and compliance checkpoints; they do not speak to the judgment question that the senior executive is actually evaluating.

Generic AI governance content that does not demonstrate Swiss institutional specificity. Content that could have been written by any AI governance consultancy about any jurisdiction does not signal expertise to the Geneva executive who has to navigate Swiss regulatory complexity, FINMA supervision, and multi-jurisdictional obligations simultaneously.

**Finding 01: The Swiss association law board fiduciary question is becoming a personal liability trigger — and no current market participant is addressing it as a personal governance question for the individual board member.**

Under Swiss association law, board members carry personal fiduciary obligations that do not automatically dissolve when a third party — a technology vendor, a compliance team, a management committee — is responsible for AI system selection and deployment. The board member who signs off on AI adoption without understanding what the system does, what can go wrong, and what the board's oversight obligations are may have a personal liability exposure that organizational liability structures do not absorb. This is distinct from regulatory compliance exposure — it is personal fiduciary exposure under Swiss association law, and it is reaching individual board members directly. The current market is not addressing this as a personal governance question. Law firms address it as a legal question. Compliance firms address it as an organizational obligation. Neither is serving the individual board member who needs to understand their personal fiduciary position and what a defensible response looks like before a problem occurs. DigitalCoa.ch's positioning should address this personal fiduciary dimension explicitly — the board member's personal accountability for AI oversight under Swiss association law — as a distinct framing from organizational compliance.

*Net-new vs. C214/C215/C216/C217 Ph06: C214 identified the board-level demand gap. C217 identified the individual board member as a distinct buyer. Neither identified the specific Swiss association law fiduciary exposure — the legal mechanism through which personal board liability attaches to AI oversight failures — as a distinct positioning frame that no current market participant is addressing. This finding identifies the Swiss association law personal fiduciary angle as a distinct positioning vector that creates immediate urgency for the individual board member buyer.*

**Finding 02: The Geneva executive AI governance advisory market has a structural timing asymmetry — demand peaks after organizational AI failures, but the individual board member needs advice before one occurs.**

The current market for AI governance advisory is largely reactive: organizations engage advisors after an AI failure has occurred, a near-miss has been documented, or a regulatory inquiry has exposed a governance gap. This creates a timing asymmetry in the Geneva market. The executive who needs AI governance advice most urgently — the individual board member before an AI failure — is least likely to seek it, because the failure has not yet occurred. The executive who is most likely to engage an advisor is the one who has just experienced a failure or a close call. For DigitalCoa.ch, this timing asymmetry has a direct service design implication: the briefing format and the positioning language should be designed to reach the pre-failure executive, not the post-failure one. The language of personal accountability, fiduciary duty, and defensible board practice speaks to the executive who has not yet had the failure — and who is therefore in the position to act on advice rather than recover from it. The advisory who is known as the person to see before the failure is in a different market position than the advisory known as the person to call after one.

*Net-new vs. C214/C215/C216/C217 Ph06: C214 identified the board-level demand gap. C217 identified the regulatory calendar as an engagement timing driver. Neither identified the reactive-versus-preventive timing asymmetry as a structural market characteristic that has direct implications for how DigitalCoa.ch should position itself in the market — specifically as the advisory who is engaged before the failure, not after. This finding identifies the timing asymmetry as a positioning opportunity: being known as the pre-failure advisory resource is a different market position than being known as the post-failure recovery advisor.*

**Finding 03: The Geneva executive's AI governance question is increasingly a multi-party accountability question — involving the board, management, vendors, and external advisors — and no current advisory format addresses the board member's position within that accountability chain.**

Geneva organizations deploying AI are navigating a multi-party accountability chain: the board that approved the deployment, the management team that selected the vendor, the vendor that built and maintains the system, and the external advisors who provided oversight. When an AI failure occurs, the question of who is accountable — and in what sequence — is not straightforward. The board member who approved the deployment is exposed not only to their own oversight failure but to the vendor's failure and the management team's failure. The Geneva executive who understands this multi-party chain is beginning to ask a different question: not "does our organization have an AI governance framework" but "where do I sit in the accountability chain and what is my specific exposure relative to everyone else's." This is a more sophisticated question than the market is currently addressing. The advisory who can map the multi-party accountability chain for an individual board member — showing them where their exposure begins and ends relative to management, vendors, and external advisors — is providing a distinct service that no current market participant has clearly positioned. This has implications for how DigitalCoa.ch frames its offering: not as a standalone board advisory but as a component within a multi-party accountability architecture.

*Net-new vs. C214/C215/C216/C217 Ph06: C214 identified multi-jurisdictional framing as an executive decision requirement. C217 identified the individual board member as a distinct buyer.
