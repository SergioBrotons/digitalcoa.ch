## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Personal liability language as primary framing displacement**
The market's dominant AI governance framing has shifted from institutional compliance to personal liability. This shift is not reflected in most advisory positioning. Advisors still leading with institutional compliance language are speaking a language that practitioners have already moved away from.

**Signal 2: Sequencing problem as acute demand trigger**
Multiple simultaneous accountability obligations with no prioritization framework. The sequencing problem is creating acute demand for guidance that does not exist. Practitioners need to know which obligation to address first.

**Signal 3: First-conversation trust verification through Geneva network embeddedness**
Executives verifying trust through the first minutes of conversation by checking whether the advisor knows what the informal circuit knows. The verification is immediate and disqualifying if failed.

**Signal 4: Carrier renewal cycle as predictable advisory demand trigger**
Carrier renewal windows create predictable demand spikes. Advisors who position for the renewal cycle appear at the right moment. Advisors who do not are missed when the urgency is highest.

**Signal 5: Documentation adequacy inversion as specific market failure mode**
Internal documentation standards appear adequate until compared against market-formed standards. The inversion is not visible from inside. Advisors who can show the inversion create urgency that internal review cannot.

**Signal 6: Advisory continuity compounding as client retention mechanism**
Each subsequent engagement with the same advisor is more valuable than the previous one. The accumulated context compounds. New advisors start without context and cannot replicate the value.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Personal liability framing absent from most advisory positioning**
Most advisors lead with institutional compliance framing. The practitioners who need guidance most urgently have shifted to personal liability framing. The gap between advisory language and practitioner language creates immediate trust failure.

**Gap 2: Sequencing methodology for simultaneous obligations**
No advisor offers a methodology for sequencing multiple simultaneous accountability obligations. The sequencing problem is acute and unresolved. An advisor who can provide the sequencing framework occupies a distinct position.

**Gap 3: Geneva-specific advisory positioning**
Most AI governance advisors position nationally or internationally. Geneva-specific positioning requires knowledge of Geneva-specific mechanisms, institutions, and informal circuit dynamics. The specificity is rare and valued.

**Gap 4: Post-incident advisory protocol**
When something goes wrong, executives need immediate guidance on what to do and what to say. No Geneva advisor has a published post-incident protocol. The absence is a gap.

**Gap 5: Board-level advisory communication**
Board members need AI governance information in a specific form. Most advisors cannot communicate at board level. The communication gap is a specific advisory opportunity.

**Gap 6: Advisory continuity infrastructure**
Advisory continuity requires infrastructure that most advisors do not build. The infrastructure includes documentation systems, client context retention, and systematic follow-up. The absence of infrastructure means clients lose the compounding benefit.

## 04 — Trust Signal Audit

**Trust Signal 1: Geneva network embeddedness as immediately verifiable**
Community embeddedness in Geneva professional networks is verifiable in the first minutes of conversation. An advisor who knows the informal circuit and its dynamics passes the verification. An advisor who does not fails immediately.

**Trust Signal 2: Specificity of problem description**
Executives trust advisors who describe their specific problem accurately. Generic problem description signals that the advisor does not understand the situation. Specificity is a trust signal.

**Trust Signal 3: Process transparency before engagement**
The advisory process described before engagement begins. Clients need to understand what the engagement will involve, what they will receive, and what is expected of them. Process transparency is a trust signal that most advisors underinvest in.

**Trust Signal 4: Limits of expertise communicated explicitly**
Advisors who communicate what they do not know are trusted more than advisors who present as knowing everything. The explicit communication of limits signals confidence and honesty.

**Trust Signal 5: Documentation of advisory position as deliverable**
A written summary of the advisory position, accessible and portable, serves as both deliverable and trust signal. The document demonstrates rigor and allows the client to share it with stakeholders.

**Trust Signal 6: Follow-up systematic as infrastructure**
Follow-up that is systematic rather than opportunistic. Clients notice the difference between an advisor who follows up because they have a system and an advisor who follows up when they remember.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Sequencing urgency as the acute AI governance problem**
Multiple simultaneous accountability obligations with no prioritization methodology. The problem is not that obligations are unknown — it is that when multiple obligations arrive simultaneously, practitioners do not know which to address first. A sequencing framework under uncertainty is a distinct advisory product.

**Finding 2: Documentation shelf-life collapse as a specific market mechanism**
Documentation adequate when written becomes inadequate before conditions change. The document has not changed. The conditions have. Market standards are moving faster than documentation cycles. An advisor who can show the specific mechanism creates urgency that internal review cannot.

**Finding 3: Geneva accountability-chain fracture as a named structural condition**
Decision authority located elsewhere, accountability exposure located in Geneva. The specifically Geneva segment of international organization and multinational accountability chains. Named structural condition with specific resolution requirements.

**Finding 4: Career-moment accountability triggers as distinct from incident triggers**
Board appointment, succession, and departure create accountability urgency independent of any incident. The moment triggers a review that would not otherwise happen. Distinct from incident-triggered demand.

**Finding 5: Geneva peer-network trust verification as an immediate disqualifier**
Community embeddedness as a trust signal verifiable in the first minutes of conversation. The advisor who knows the informal circuit and its dynamics passes the verification. The advisor who does not fails immediately. Not about credentials — about whether the advisor operates in the same information environment as the client.

**Finding 6: Advisory continuity compounding as a long-term competitive moat**
Each subsequent engagement with the same advisor is more valuable than the previous one because accumulated context compounds. New advisors start without context and cannot replicate the value. The moat does not depend on switching costs — it depends on accumulated context that the client cannot transfer to a new advisor.
