## 01 — ICP Language: How Senior Executives Describe Their Need for AI Governance Advice

### EN — English

**How the need surfaces:**
The Geneva senior executive arrives at the AI governance question through a specific type of organizational moment — not an abstract concern, but a concrete event that has made the question unavoidable. A board agenda item that names AI risk for the first time and the board chair who does not know how to govern it. A board colleague who has read something alarming about AI liability and asks an unprepared question in a meeting. A regulatory team that has flagged that something in the EU AI Act may apply to their Swiss operations and does not know how to answer it. A peer firm that has been named in an enforcement matter and the question that follows: are we exposed in the same way?

What they do not do is Google "AI governance." They ask things like: "what are a board member's fiduciary obligations when the organization uses AI in material decisions," "who do we need in the room when FINMA asks us to explain our AI model governance," "how do we answer the question our EU counterparty is asking about our AI documentation," or "what does good look like — has anyone actually done this at board level in Switzerland."

**What they expect from the first conversation:**
They expect to learn something concrete. Not a framework, not a legal summary, not a presentation about services. They expect that the person across from them has worked with situations like theirs, understands how the Swiss institutional landscape actually functions, and can give them a direct answer to the specific question they have been carrying. They are evaluating the advisor's judgment in real time — whether this person has actually thought through the problem or is giving them prepared material.

They expect the advisor to have formed a view on their specific situation by the end of the first conversation. Not a proposal — a preliminary judgment about what the right question is, what information would sharpen that judgment, and what a credible path forward looks like. The executive who has reached out is not looking for a vendor; they are evaluating whether they have found the person they were hoping to find.

**What creates resistance:**
Anyone who arrives with a slide deck. Anyone whose first meeting is a consultation call designed to understand their budget and decision timeline. Anyone who cannot clearly describe what they do that a law firm does not do, or what they do that a technology vendor does not do. Anyone whose AI governance positioning appeared in the last twelve months. Anyone who uses the word "ecosystem," "cutting-edge," or "solution."

The executive who has been in the room for the full cycle of AI governance conversations — from vendor pitches to internal compliance presentations to board questions they could not answer — has developed a finely calibrated distrust for anything that feels like a product, a platform, or a generic framework. They are looking for a person who has thought carefully about the problem and who can demonstrate that thinking in real time.

**How they describe what they need:**
"We need someone in the room who has actually done this at board level" — not general knowledge, experience with the specific decision context.
"We need to know whether what we are doing is defensible" — not compliance certification, judgment about their specific situation.
"Our board needs to be able to answer the question before someone asks it in the room" — the board-level accountability frame.
"We need to understand what we are actually exposed to, not what the worst-case regulatory scenario is" — the executive preference for precision over worst-casing.
"Who has actually navigated this with a Swiss board and can tell us what they learned" — the peer experience expectation.

## 02 — Market Signals: Where Geneva Executive Demand Is Emerging — What's Working, What's Not

**Where demand is appearing:**

The advisory demand that is converting in Geneva is not coming through digital marketing funnels. It is moving through referral networks that have been built over years — peer introductions, board network connections, professional society relationships. The executive who has decided they need help does not search for an advisor; they ask a colleague who has been in a similar situation.

This has a direct implication: the lead generation question for DigitalCoa.ch is not about website conversion optimization or content marketing metrics. It is about being present in the right conversations, being visible to the right people, and being in a position where the peer referral happens naturally. The website functions as a credentialing surface — a place for the executive to confirm that this is a serious practice before they make the introduction call. It is not the lead source.

**What is failing:**

Generic AI governance advisory websites that lead with "AI strategy" and feature certification badges, client logos, andROI claims. The executive who has been through serious advisory relationships reads these surfaces and moves on. They can identify a vendor in seconds.

Any first-contact experience that feels like a sales process. The consultation call designed to qualify the lead, the follow-up proposal that arrives too quickly, the "next steps" framing in the first meeting — all of these create the impression that the advisor is managing a pipeline rather than working with clients. The Geneva executive who is evaluating an advisor is simultaneously evaluating whether the advisor will treat them as a person or as a lead.

Compliance-focused AI governance content that is positioned for legal departments or compliance teams. This content reaches the wrong audience and does not attract the executive who is looking for board-level advisory. When the executive finds this content, they do not see themselves in it.

**What is working:**

The executive briefing format — 90 minutes, no slides, no proposal at the end — is working because it removes the sales friction that sophisticated executives associate with consultancies. The format signals that the advisor is confident enough in their practice that they do not need to sell in the first meeting. This is itself a trust signal.

Independent positioning that is backed by behavioral evidence. Not just a claim of independence on the website, but behavior in the first meeting — the questions the advisor asks, the references they do not make to technology products, the clarity with which they describe their scope boundaries. Independence is demonstrated; it is not claimed.

Swiss institutional depth as a differentiator. When the executive understands that the advisor has worked with Swiss boards and financial institutions, understands the FINMA supervisory landscape, and has seen how multi-jurisdictional AI governance questions actually get resolved in Swiss practice — that depth becomes a reason to engage rather than a generic credential.

**The micro-conversion that matters:**

The actual conversion event for this practice is not a website form submission. It is the decision to make the introductory call. Everything that happens before that call — the publications, the referrals, the website visit — is working toward that decision. The website's primary function is to give the executive who has been referred the confirmation they need before they pick up the phone. The micro-conversion that matters is: referral → website verification → confidence to call. Not: content download → email nurture → proposal request.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis: How Other Advisors Are Positioning AI Governance in Geneva

**The four positioning types in the market:**

**Law firms:** Positioning AI governance as a legal compliance question. Their credibility comes from bar association affiliation, established client relationships, and legal privilege. Their limitation for this ICP is structural: they are lawyers, and their frame is legal. The executive who needs to help their board understand AI governance — not to receive a legal opinion on whether they are in compliance — is not looking for a law firm. Their fee structures and legal-conflict limitations also create friction that independent advisory does not have.

**Big Four consultancies:** Positioning AI governance as a service line within digital transformation or risk management. Their credibility is brand recognition and scale. Their limitation for this ICP is exactly the credibility problem that Geneva executives have identified: Big Four AI practices are seen as vendor-adjacent, generating implementation revenue that creates conflicts with pure advisory positioning. The executive who has been pitched by a Big Four AI governance team recognizes the product presentation dressed as advisory.

**Boutique governance and risk consultancies:** More credible on independence, but most are positioned at the compliance team level or at the technical AI governance level. A boutique that specializes in AI model risk governance for data science teams is not the right advisor for a board member who needs to understand what their oversight responsibilities are. The positioning alignment with the board-level ICP is rare.

**Independent advisors:** The positioning that is most credible for this ICP, but most independent advisors in this market have not clearly articulated what distinguishes their practice from a boutique consultancy or a law firm. The independence claim is necessary but not sufficient without a clear statement of what the practice actually does — at what level, for whom, around what decisions.

**The specific gap in the market:**

The gap is not AI governance expertise. The market has abundant AI governance content and frameworks. The gap is executive-grade advisory that is:

Explicitly positioned for board members and senior executives, not compliance teams.
Independent from technology vendors and implementation revenue.
Grounded in Swiss institutional reality — not a global framework applied to Switzerland, but a practice that understands how decisions are actually made in Swiss financial institutions, boards, and professional organizations.
Clear about scope — what the advisor does and what they do not do.
Clear about process — how an engagement actually works.

No current market participant is clearly occupying all four of these positions simultaneously. This is the DigitalCoa.ch positioning opportunity.

## 04 — Trust Signal Audit: What This ICP Values Most

**Expertise credibility — the type that matters:**

For the senior executive ICP, credentials are necessary but not sufficient. The credential that matters is not the certification or the university affiliation — it is the specific evidence of pattern recognition. Has this advisor worked with a situation like mine? Have they sat in a board meeting where the question my board is asking was actually raised? Do they know what FINMA's specific concerns are when they review AI model governance in a wealth management context?

The credibility signal is specificity: detail about the types of situations the advisor has navigated, the specific Swiss institutional contexts they have worked in, the nature of the questions they have helped boards and executives answer. Not testimonials — the executive cannot use testimonials to verify credibility in the way they can verify a credential. Evidence of pattern recognition — the ability to demonstrate that this is not the first time the advisor has encountered this configuration of questions.

**Process clarity — the type that removes friction:**

The executive who is considering engaging an advisor wants to understand the process before they commit. Not a detailed scope of work — a clear account of what the first conversation covers, how the advisory relationship is structured, how confidentiality operates in practice, and what they can expect if they decide to proceed.

The absence of this information reads as either inexperience or deliberate opacity. Geneva executives who have worked with advisors understand that the good ones are transparent about process. The process clarity signal is not about transparency for its own sake — it is about demonstrating that the advisor has enough experience with advisory relationships to know what the client needs to understand upfront.

**Discretion — the type that creates safety:**

This ICP values discretion above almost every other signal. They are not looking for an advisor who will sign an NDA — they are looking for evidence that the advisor understands the practice of discretion in a professional context. No client references in the conventional sense — no logos, no quotes, no case studies. Instead: a statement that makes clear that the nature of the work does not permit public reference to specific engagements. "I do not reference specific clients or engagements. The content of what we discuss stays in the room."

This signal works because it demonstrates that the advisor understands what the executive needs in order to speak freely. The executive who cannot speak freely in the first meeting cannot work effectively with the advisor. Discretion is not a legal structure — it is a practice, and the executive is evaluating whether the advisor understands that practice.

**Independence — the behavioral demonstration:**

The claim of independence needs to be backed by behavior in the first meeting. The advisor who asks questions about the client's specific situation, who does not reference any technology products or platforms, who describes what they do and do not do with precision — this advisor is demonstrating independence through behavior. The website copy that claims independence is necessary; it is not sufficient without the behavioral evidence in the meeting.
