## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### Real Language — How Senior Executives Describe Their Need for AI Governance Advice

**EN**

The Geneva executive who needs AI governance advice describes it in terms of personal exposure, not organizational compliance. The language is precise and stripped of abstraction. They do not talk about "AI strategy" or "governance frameworks." They talk about what is on their desk, what they signed, what their name is attached to, what they cannot explain if asked today.

The distinction between information and judgment is present in how they frame the request. They have attended briefings. They have read material. What they lack is someone who will sit in the room with them and say: this is what you have, this is where the gap is, this is what adequate looks like for your situation — not a framework, a view. The trigger is consistently the same: the moment of realizing that the accumulated AI decisions were made faster than governance could follow, and that there is now a specific, named exposure attached to their personal accountability.

**Triggers:**

- "I have been at this long enough to know that when something goes wrong, the first question is not what the policy said — it is what the senior person knew, when they knew it, and what they did about it. I need to know what I actually have."
- "We made the call on AI tools two years ago and moved quickly. I signed off on it at the time. Now I need to know whether what was put in place is something I can actually defend."
- "I am joining a board. They have AI systems in place that I am being asked to oversee. I need to understand what I am looking at before I am in the room where that question comes up."
- "I received a counterparty questionnaire with specific questions about AI methodology and tool versions. I realized I could not answer half of it with any confidence. That exposure is now live."
- "My team is using AI in client work. I authorized that. But I have not been in the room with them to determine what adequate review means for that output. I am carrying that."
- "There is a FDPIC investigation pattern in this area. I read about it. I want to know whether what we have is in that territory and what we would show if they came."
- "I had a conversation with a peer who went through this. They told me what they wish they had done eighteen months earlier. I am that person eighteen months earlier."

**Expectations:**

The executive who reaches out expects a direct, specific professional opinion — not a presentation, not a proposal, not a framework document. They expect the advisor to form a view in the first meeting and express it directly. They expect someone who works in their world — Geneva professional services, senior level, personal liability exposure — and who has specific pattern knowledge of what these situations actually look like. They expect the first conversation to be a consultation, not a commercial qualification. And they expect absolute discretion: the fact that they are asking and the specific content of their exposure is not information they want in circulation.

**Distrusts:**

Any advisor whose primary language is "regulatory environment" or "compliance landscape." Any first meeting that presents a methodology before listening to the situation. Platforms that promise governance automation. Certification badges with no stated jurisdiction or exposure type. The phrase "AI governance framework" as a product name. Advisors who cannot explain in one sentence what they do that a law firm does not and what they do that a Big Four team does not. Any communication that reads as marketing: a first meeting that feels like a pitch, a follow-up that resembles a sales sequence. Advisors whose website could describe services to a hospital, a cantonal government, and a private bank without distinguishing any of them.

**The unstated question:**

"I have been making decisions about AI systems for two or three years under my own authority. I need to know whether what accumulated is defensible — not eventually, not when a regulator asks, but now — and I need a specific, direct answer from someone who has been in this exact situation before."

## 02 — Market Signals

### Where Geneva Executive Demand Is Emerging

**Post-Event Information Processing Gap**

Geneva's concentration of professional events — finance sector briefings, legal technology conferences, board governance programs — generates a recognizable pattern: the executive who attends, absorbs information, exchanges contacts, and returns to their organization with no actionable next step. The event provides context and vocabulary. What it does not provide is a specific, applicable assessment of the executive's own situation. The resulting demand is not for more information — it is for a specific, discretely scoped professional consultation that processes the general context into a concrete view of the individual executive's exposure. Sergio's briefing format is specifically positioned to fill this gap: the executive arrives with event-level knowledge and leaves with a written, applicable assessment of their own situation. This is a structurally distinct demand that event participation creates but no current market actor is specifically positioned to capture.

**Counterparty Questionnaire Response as Active Demand Signal**

The counterparty AI governance questionnaire has become a primary trigger mechanism. When a Geneva professional services firm receives a twelve-point AI methodology questionnaire from a counterparty — a bank, an institutional client, a due diligence process — the receiving executive experiences a sudden, concrete realization of exposure. The gap between what they know they have and what the questionnaire asks for is visible and specific. Unlike abstract regulatory concerns, the counterparty questionnaire creates a dateable moment of demand: the questionnaire arrived, it cannot be ignored, a response is needed. This is a more actionable trigger than regulatory anticipation because it has a counterparty embedded in it — there is a real relationship at stake, not just a hypothetical future exposure.

**Written Summary as Primary Conversion Mechanism**

The written summary following the briefing is emerging as the primary conversion mechanism — not because executives prefer writing, but because the written summary removes the "hearsay problem" that prevents them from acting on oral advice. The executive who receives a direct, written view of their AI governance position can use it internally: to brief a board colleague, to respond to a counterparty, to establish a record of active review. The written artifact is what makes the advisory real to them — it is the thing they can show exists. The conversion moment is not the briefing itself; it is the delivery of the written summary. The executive who receives it recognizes whether the advisory is what they were looking for.

**Pricing as Qualification Mechanism**

The CHF 3,200 briefing price performs a qualification function that is not primarily about revenue — it is a self-selection mechanism that ensures the executive who books a briefing is the executive who needs the specific level of advisory provided. The investment signals seriousness and ensures that Sergio's time is engaged with executives for whom this is a live exposure, not a exploratory inquiry. Pricing language that explains why the investment is appropriate ("this is what the briefing is, this is what you receive, this is what it is designed to answer") functions as both a qualification mechanism and a trust signal — the executive who recognizes the value description is the executive the practice is designed to serve.

**The Geneva Institutional Depth Signal**

Executives in Geneva are particularly responsive to advisors who demonstrate genuine institutional depth — not just legal knowledge, not just regulatory knowledge, but the specific, practical understanding of how the Geneva professional community operates: the cantonal professional bodies, the peer dynamics, the specific supervisory expectations at the Geneva level. This is not geography; it is the difference between an advisor who knows Geneva from reports and one who operates in it. The institutional depth signal performs credibility in a way that generalist positioning cannot.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### How Other Advisors Are Positioning AI Governance in Geneva

**Law Firms**

Geneva law firms have positioned AI governance as an extension of their data protection and technology practices. Their strength is regulatory interpretation — nFADP obligations, FDPIC enforcement posture, Swiss AI Convention implications. Their structural weakness with this ICP is that they treat AI governance as a legal compliance question, they bill at rates that prohibit detailed internal review, and their output is legal opinion rather than actionable governance documentation. The senior executive who needs a view on whether their AI documentation would hold up is not well-served by a law firm's legal interpretation of what the regulations technically require.

**Big Four**

Big Four AI governance practices are built for large organizations — banks, multinationals, cantonal institutions. They are structurally oriented toward scale engagements, standardized methodologies, and report-based output. The boutique managing director or the mid-sized board member who needs a discrete, senior-level consultation is not their target. The cultural fit is wrong: executives describe feeling like a line item on a project plan, not a person with a specific professional problem.

**Compliance Platforms and SaaS**

AI governance platforms and SaaS tools have entered the market with automated compliance monitoring, policy document generators, and certification support. They are positioned at IT departments and compliance teams, not at senior executives with personal exposure. Platform positioning actively signals the wrong level — it reads as "this is a tool for managing AI use" rather than "this is an advisor for navigating personal accountability."

**Training and Education Providers**

AI governance training programs, certification courses, and professional education modules exist in the Geneva market. They serve a real need for organizational capability building. They do not serve the individual executive who needs a specific, applicable assessment of their personal exposure. The executive who needs governance clarity is not looking for a course.

**The Specific Gap**

The gap that remains unfilled is the senior-level, Geneva-embedded, independent advisory practice that works specifically on personal AI governance exposure — not as a legal question, not as a compliance project, not as a training program, and not as a platform. The practice that fills this gap provides a discrete, professional consultation to the individual who is carrying personal accountability for AI decisions made under their authority. It does not sell frameworks, does not position through certification badges, does not offer platforms, and does not approach AI governance as an education product. It provides the direct, specific professional judgment that the senior executive is looking for — delivered in a single focused meeting, with a written summary that gives them something concrete to work with.

## 04 — Trust Signal Audit

### What Credibility, Process Clarity, Discretion, and Independence Signals This ICP Values Most

**Credibility Signals — In Order of Weight**

Pattern specificity is the primary credibility signal. The executive needs to know that the advisor has worked in situations that resemble theirs — not in theory, not from a framework document, but in practice, in Geneva, at the level of personal exposure they are carrying. The advisor demonstrates this not by describing a client (which would violate discretion) but by showing precision of understanding: the specific configuration of exposure, the specific triggers, the specific questions that arise in situations like theirs. Language that is too general signals that the advisor has not actually worked at this level.

Independent judgment expression is the second credibility signal. The executive wants an advisor who will form a view and express it directly during the first meeting — not take the matter under advisement, not promise to review materials and report back, not hedge every statement. Advisors who hedge consistently are perceived as unable or unwilling to provide the direct judgment this ICP needs. The executive is looking for someone who will say: this is what you have, this is where the gap is, this is what adequate looks like for your situation.

Geneva institutional embeddedness is the third credibility signal. The advisor must demonstrate that they operate in the Geneva professional environment — not as a remote consultant with a Geneva address, but as someone who understands the specific institutional context: how the cantonal professional bodies work, what the supervisory expectations actually are, how the Geneva peer community operates. This is not about having a Geneva office; it is about demonstrating operational knowledge of the environment the executive works in.

**Process Clarity**

The executive wants to understand the advisory process before committing. They want to know what happens in the first meeting, what they will receive as output, what the relationship looks like after the initial briefing, and what the written summary will contain and how it can be used. Process clarity reduces the executive's sense of entering an undefined commitment and signals professionalism.

The first meeting must be clearly framed as a professional consultation — not a sales qualification, not a product demonstration. The executive should understand that they are receiving an advisor's professional time and judgment, not evaluating a service package. The output of the first meeting should be a direct, specific professional view on the executive's situation — not a proposal for further work.

**Discretion**

Discretion is non-negotiable. The senior executive navigating an AI governance exposure does not want this fact known in the market. Any advisor behavior — a follow-up sequence that resembles marketing, a public reference to a client's situation, a communication style that could result in information circulating — disqualifies the advisor regardless of their credentials. The practice's entire conduct must signal absolute discretion at every touchpoint.

**Independence**

Independence from vendors, platforms, certification bodies, and software tools is a required trust signal. The executive wants an advisor with no commercial interest in recommending a specific AI tool, platform, or certification. The practice's independence is best demonstrated through specific behavioral exclusions — the questions it does not answer, the boundaries it holds precisely, the specific things it does not do. Named exclusions perform more credibility than claimed independence.
