## Deliverable 01 — ICP Language

### English

**How Geneva executives describe their need for AI governance advice:**

*"We have AI decisions being made across the organization and I can't reconstruct who made which one, or why."*

*"I've started to ask myself whether I'm comfortable signing what the AI is producing. The honest answer is that I'm not sure I can anymore."*

*"The board wants a position on AI governance and I don't have one to give them — not because I haven't had time, but because I don't know what adequate looks like."*

*"My counterparties are asking questions I can't answer. Not because they're unreasonable, but because I genuinely don't know what my documentation would show."*

*"I've watched colleagues go first. I'd rather not be the case study that defines the problem for everyone else."*

**Triggers:**
- Counterparty AI governance questionnaire requiring documented response
- Board-level question asked without prior warning
- Regulatory examination or carrier underwriting query with AI components
- Post-incident discovery: realizing the documentation does not exist
- Peer disclosure: learning a colleague was asked the same question and had no better answer
- Vendor contract renewal: forced reassessment of tool's adequacy

**Expectations:**
- A clear personal position that can be stated, not a framework document
- Understanding of what "adequate" means in their specific context
- Written record of the advisory conclusion, durable beyond the conversation
- Honest assessment of exposure, not reassurance dressed as advice
- Discretion: this is not a conversation they want overheard

**Distrust signals:**
- Anyone who leads with a framework before understanding the problem
- Advisors who use AI terminology as credential rather than tool description
- Claims of certainty in a space where no authoritative standard exists
- Advisory relationships that begin with large retainer commitments
- Any suggestion that the problem can be solved, as opposed to understood

## Deliverable 02 — Market Signals

### Where Geneva executive demand is emerging:

**1. Post-enforcement disclosure intelligence**
Executives who have encountered regulatory scrutiny are no longer asking "what do I need?" but "what do they already know?" The value proposition has shifted from compliance preparation to post-discovery market intelligence — understanding what a regulatory examination or carrier questionnaire will surface, before it surfaces. This is a different product: not advice on what to do, but clarity on what exists.

**2. Board committee formation pressure**
AI governance questions are migrating from the CEO's desk to board committee agendas — audit committees, risk committees, newly formed technology committees. This creates a different entry point: not the executive who uses AI, but the board member who must evaluate AI governance adequacy without technical competence. The advisory question becomes how to formulate and assess AI governance adequacy when the person asking cannot evaluate the answer.

**3. Succession and departure as governance trigger**
The question of what happens to AI governance documentation and institutional knowledge when a senior professional departs has become concrete. Multiple executives have encountered this post-departure and are now asking the question in advance. This is distinct from "we need AI governance" — it is "I am about to leave and the question is not resolved."

**4. Vendor consolidation as accountability event**
When AI tool vendors are acquired, deprecated, or exit markets, clients are forced to confront the documentation they never built. This produces a specific demand moment: not "build AI governance" as a project, but "we just lost our AI vendor and we don't know what our exposure is." The advisory product here is rapid exposure assessment, not multi-year governance architecture.

**5. Geneva institutional context specificity requirement**
The demand is no longer satisfied by generic AI governance frameworks. Executives who have worked with general frameworks and still cannot answer specific questions are looking for advisors who understand Geneva-specific institutional context — international organization accountability structures, Swiss domestic regulatory layers, Geneva professional community norms. The specificity is not decorative; it is functional.

### What is not working in advisory positioning:

**Generic AI governance frameworks** — Executives who have encountered these in board presentations report that they produce a sense of coverage without a sense of understanding. The framework demonstrates effort; it does not demonstrate adequacy.

**Compliance-framed advisory** — Framing AI governance as a compliance obligation produces resistance rather than engagement. The executives who need the advice most are the ones who have the most sophisticated understanding of what compliance actually requires — and they know their problem is not a compliance gap.

**Large retainer advisory structures** — The advisory market in Geneva remains relationship-based and skeptical of commitment before understanding. Large retainer structures create commitment before qualification, which contradicts the self-selection pattern observed in this ICP.

## Deliverable 03 — Competitive Analysis

### How other advisors are positioning AI governance in Geneva:

**1. Big Four / Professional Services Firms**

*Positioning:* AI governance as a service line within broader digital transformation or risk management practices.

*Credible claims:* Established client relationships, regulatory relationships, global methodology, scale resources.

*Gaps:*
- Generic global frameworks applied to Geneva-specific problems without local institutional adaptation
- Team structures that do not match boutique-scale client needs — junior teams doing the work, senior partners for relationship
- Conflict exposure: same firm advises clients and AI vendors, creating independence questions the firm cannot resolve
- Pricing opacity and engagement structures designed for larger clients create friction for the boutique-scale executives who are the actual demand

**2. Law Firms Offering AI Governance Advisory**

*Positioning:* AI governance as an extension of technology law or data protection practice.

*Credible claims:* Legal privilege protection, regulatory understanding, client trust established through legal relationship.

*Gaps:*
- AI governance questions are not primarily legal questions — the governance problem exists even when the legal answer is clear
- Law firm AI governance products tend toward documentation production rather than judgment delivery
- Billing structures (hourly rate) create misaligned incentives — more work more revenue
- Legal privilege coverage does not extend to the operational judgment questions executives most need answered

**3. Technology Consulting Firms**

*Positioning:* AI governance as a technical implementation problem.

*Credible claims:* Technical depth, tool-specific knowledge, implementation capability.

*Gaps:*
- The AI governance problem in Geneva is not primarily technical — it is institutional and judgment-based
- Technical advisors tend to define the problem as "which controls" rather than "what does adequate look like"
- Technology firms lack the regulatory context and Geneva institutional understanding to frame the problem correctly
- Independence questions when the same firm may also be implementing AI systems

**4. Independent Advisory Practices (few, nascent)**

*Positioning:* Boutique independent AI governance advisory.

*Credible claims:* Independence, senior attention, Geneva focus, no conflicts.

*Gaps:*
- Market is early — trust signals not yet established through pattern
- No operational track record demonstrating pattern recognition capability
- Independent positioning often stated as claim rather than demonstrated through behavior

**5. The dominant gap across all competitors:**

No competitor is demonstrably operating from accumulated pattern knowledge across Geneva AI governance situations. Advisory positioning is service-description-based, not knowledge-evidence-based. The differentiation opportunity is to demonstrate that the advisory product is not advice about AI governance, but the accumulated judgment from having seen the pattern across multiple Geneva situations — and that this accumulated judgment is what the written summary delivers.

## Deliverable 04 — Trust Signal Audit

### What credibility, process clarity, discretion, and independence signals this ICP values most:

**Credibility signals:**

1. **Geneva institutional embeddedness as first credential**
The ICP arrives with contextual knowledge of Geneva's institutional landscape — international organizations, Swiss domestic regulatory layers, Geneva professional community dynamics. This is not a supporting credential; it is the primary credential. The executive does not want to explain Geneva context to their advisor.

2. **Pattern recognition without client identification**
The ability to describe patterns observed across situations without identifying clients or situations produces trust through demonstration rather than claim. "I've seen this pattern before" is credible when the pattern description is accurate and specific. Case studies are not needed; pattern precision is sufficient.

3. **Swiss law precision in the right domain**
Geneva executives distinguish between legal precision and regulatory navigation. The former is valued; the latter is a cost. Advisors who demonstrate precision in Swiss legal reasoning — where the question is actually legal — are trusted more than those who demonstrate regulatory process knowledge.

4. **Written summary as primary artifact**
The written summary of the advisory conclusion is the primary durable artifact. It is what executives take to boards, reference in regulatory conversations, and use as the basis for subsequent decisions. Trust is built through the quality of this artifact, not through the conversation that produces it.

**Process clarity signals:**

5. **Discreet intake process**
The ICP expects discretion before, during, and after the engagement. The intake process should not require explanation of the problem to administrative staff, and the advisory relationship should not be discussed externally. Quiet, direct scheduling — no discovery process.

6. **Scope precision before engagement**
The executive wants to understand what they are getting before committing — not a sales process, but a clear description of format, duration, language, and output. Pricing language that includes CHF communicates seriousness; pricing that is absent or variable communicates uncertainty.

7. **Explicit exclusions as independence signals**
The advisory practice should not do certain things — and these exclusions should be named, not implied. The ICP reads exclusions as evidence of independence more reliably than inclusions. What the practice does not do is part of the trust architecture.

**Discretion signals:**

8. **No case study references**
The ICP does not want to hear what was done for other clients. This is not a sign of lack of credibility; it is a condition of trust. Pattern recognition is demonstrated through precision of analysis, not through client references.

9. **No cross-client discussion**
The advisory relationship is confidential by default. The executive assumes — and this assumption must not be violated — that what is discussed stays confidential. Even the fact of the advisory relationship is not mentioned.

10. **No publication of engagement**
No testimonials, no "we advised X on Y" references, no conference case studies. The trust is built through the quality of the written output and the integrity of the process, not through reputation amplification.

**Independence signals:**

11. **No AI vendor relationships**
The advisory practice does not receive referral fees, commissions, or other compensation from AI tool vendors. This is structural, not claimed. The exclusion list is the demonstration.

12. **No regulatory agency conflicts**
The advisory practice does not simultaneously advise regulatory agencies on AI governance matters that would create conflicts with client advisory work. This is not a claim about appearance — it is a statement of behavioral exclusion.

13. **Behavioral consistency over stated independence**
The ICP is alert to the difference between a stated independence commitment and demonstrated independence behavior. The written summary should contain no language that serves the advisor's commercial interest; it should contain only analysis that serves the client's interest.

## Deliverable 06 — Findings Summary

### 6 Net-New Insights — DigitalCoa.ch Advisory Practice Positioning

*Distinct from C214–C234 Phase 06*

---

**Finding 01: Advisory knowledge as the primary product, not advisory opinion**

The Geneva executive who has worked through AI governance questions with multiple advisors is not looking for another opinion. They are looking for accumulated institutional knowledge — the pattern recognition that comes from having seen enough Geneva-specific situations to distinguish between what looks like adequate governance and what actually holds. The briefing is not a conversation that produces advice; it is a transfer of accumulated pattern knowledge. This requires the written summary to demonstrate analytical depth, not just conclusion. The product is the accumulated judgment, and the written summary is the evidence of it.

**Finding 02: Post-enforcement environment intelligence as a distinct offering**

The regulatory and carrier enforcement environment has moved from anticipation to occurrence. Executives who have been through an examination, responded to a carrier questionnaire with AI components, or seen a counterparty questionnaire surface unknown gaps are now seeking post-event intelligence rather than pre-event preparation. The advisory product here is not "what should I do" but "what have others encountered, what does my situation look like in that context, and what does a defensible response require?" This is market intelligence from a Geneva-specific pattern knowledge base, not regulatory guidance.

**Finding 03: Geneva-institutional specificity as functional requirement, not positioning**

Previous phases identified Geneva institutional embeddedness as a trust signal. This cycle identifies it as a functional requirement: executives who have tried generic AI governance frameworks have learned that the frameworks do not address Geneva-specific institutional structures — the intersection of Swiss domestic law, international organization immunity frameworks, Geneva professional community norms, and carrier underwriting expectations. The specificity of the advisory is not a branding element; it is the reason the advisory works. An advisor who does not understand the institutional context cannot produce a written summary that would hold in a Geneva regulatory conversation.

**Finding 04: Advisory relationship as institutional memory infrastructure**

The executives with the most acute need are those who have encountered AI governance gaps after a departure — discovering that the person who made AI decisions, configured tools, or maintained documentation is no longer present, and the institutional knowledge left with them. The advisory relationship, properly structured, functions as external institutional memory — not because the advisor is present in the organization, but because the accumulated advisory context across engagements provides the pattern knowledge that can be applied to reconstruct what the documentation would or should show. The written summary is the continuity artifact.

**Finding 05: Behavioral exclusion list as primary trust signal architecture**

Across the competitive landscape, independence is claimed. The differentiator is demonstrated independence through behavioral exclusions — specific things the advisory practice does not do. The exclusion list performs trust more credibly than any inclusion statement. The Geneva executive reads the exclusions as evidence of judgment: the advisor has decided what they will not do, which means the advisor has a position. Behavioral consistency over time — not a statement of independence but a record of what was declined — is the actual trust signal. This requires the exclusion list to be named, specific, and maintained.

**Finding 06: Written summary as the primary conversion mechanism, not the briefing**

The conversion moment in this advisory practice is not the decision to book the briefing. It is the receipt of the written summary. Executives who have received a written summary that demonstrates genuine pattern knowledge, institutional specificity, and judgment quality convert to ongoing advisory relationships not because they decided to trust the advisor, but because they received evidence of the advisor's accumulated knowledge in a form they can examine, take to a board, or use in a regulatory conversation. The written summary is the product. The briefing is the production process. The conversion happens when the written summary is delivered.
