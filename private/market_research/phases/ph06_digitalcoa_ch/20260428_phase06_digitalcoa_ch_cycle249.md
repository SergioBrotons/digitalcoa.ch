## Deliverable 01 — ICP Language

### English

**How executives describe their need for AI governance advice:**

*"I need to know what I would actually have to show if someone asked."*
The executive is not asking what the rule is. They are asking what their documentation would look like under scrutiny — a present-tense, defensible-position question. This is distinct from a compliance-question. The compliance question has an answer. The defensible-position question has a quality, and quality is variable.

*"I signed something on the AI tool approval and I'm not sure what that means."*
Personal liability language surfaces before organizational governance language. Managing directors and principals in Geneva arrive with a signature question, not a policy question. The signature is the entry point. The policy gap is the context.

*"We have documentation. I don't know if it's the right documentation."*
Documentation existence and documentation adequacy are experienced as the same problem by the executive who has it. The question "do we have enough?" is structurally different from "could we defend what we have?" — but in executive experience, they collapse. Advisory that addresses existence before addressing adequacy underserves the actual question.

*"The board asked me to explain how we use AI and I realized I couldn't answer properly."*
Board-level scrutiny as trigger. This is a career-moment accountability event — not an organizational compliance event. The executive is representing their own personal judgment, not their firm's process. This distinction maps to different service design.

*"A counterparty sent us an AI governance questionnaire. We filled it out. I don't know what we committed to."*
Counterparty questionnaire as discrete triggering event. The executive did not anticipate the questionnaire and did not evaluate the response before it was sent. The submission created a documented position that may not reflect actual practice. The gap between submitted response and actual practice is the live exposure.

**Triggers:**
- Personal signature on AI-related attestation or renewal
- Board or governing body asking for AI governance overview
- Counterparty AI governance questionnaire received or expected
- Regulatory examination announcement or precursor signal
- Staff departure carrying AI configuration knowledge
- Media or peer event where AI accountability question surfaced
- Regulatory calendar inflection (Federal Council deliberation, FINMA guidance, FDPIC enforcement pattern)

**Expectations:**
- Clarity on what defensible looks like, not just what compliant looks like
- Named risks that map to their specific situation, not general framework descriptions
- A written record that would protect them personally, not just their organization
- Discretion — they do not want their AI governance gaps discussed outside the room
- Independent judgment not influenced by vendor relationships or regulatory advocacy

**Distrust signals:**
- Framing AI governance as a technology question
- Offering a framework without discussing its limitations
- Suggesting that having a policy equals having adequate governance
- Equating AI tool vendor compliance with professional adequacy
- Framing the conversation toward adoption efficiency rather than accountability protection
- Presenting standard content as if it were specific to their situation

## Deliverable 02 — Market Signals

### Where Geneva executive demand is emerging

**1. Post-peer-examination debrief as discrete advisory occasion.**
Executives who have undergone supervisory examination — or observed a peer undergo one — are returning with specific questions that did not exist before the examination. The examination revealed documentation gaps the executive did not know they had. The debrief demand is more acute than the pre-examination preparation demand because the executive now has a concrete reference point. This is a leading indicator: peer examinations will accelerate through 2026.

**2. Counterparty questionnaire response architecture as unmet need.**
Counterparty AI governance questionnaires are arriving before Geneva firms have a response architecture. The response gap is not about individual questions — it is about the organizational process for evaluating what commitments a questionnaire response creates, who authorizes the submission, and how the response connects to actual practice. The market has named this problem insufficiently; executives recognize the gap but do not have vocabulary for it.

**3. Pre-decision documentation positioning ahead of Federal Council deliberation.**
The most sophisticated segment of the Geneva executive market is not waiting for the Federal Council decision. They are building documentation that would be adequate regardless of which direction the Council takes. This is a pre-anticipatory posture — different from both reactive compliance and regulatory anticipation. The service design required is different: the executive is not asking what the rule will be, but what their documentation must look like before the rule arrives.

**4. Personal liability discharge mechanism as primary executive concern.**
The managing director or principal who signed an AI governance attestation is experiencing personal exposure as distinct from organizational exposure. The attestation created a documented position without a corresponding discharge methodology. No Geneva market participant has named this as a discrete advisory product, but the demand is present and growing. The language of personal liability is displacing the language of organizational compliance in executive-level conversations.

**5. Geneva French-language advisory market underserved by English-language market positioning.**
The Geneva French-speaking executive community is accessing AI governance advisory in English, which does not match the operational linguistic environment of many Geneva institutions. This is not a translation gap — it is a positioning gap. Advisors who lead in French in Geneva are not following English-language market leaders; they are operating in a different competitive environment with lower saturation and higher institutional credibility in specific segments.

**6. Staff departure AI configuration knowledge loss as recurring trigger.**
The departure of a team member who configured or maintained an AI tool creates an immediate operational gap and a latent documentation gap. The executive knows the knowledge left with the person; they do not know what documentation would have been adequate to capture it. This trigger is recurring — each significant departure in a Geneva boutique creates a new instance of this problem. No advisory product names this specifically.

### What is working or not in advisory positioning

**Working:**
- Named accountability categories before generic governance frameworks
- Discretion emphasis — executives respond to implicit and explicit signals that their situation will not be discussed externally
- Pricing that signals seriousness without creating commitment threshold anxiety
- Written summary as primary output — executives value the artifact over the conversation
- Self-selection language that performs independence rather than claiming it

**Not working:**
- Framework-first positioning that requires the executive to map their situation to the framework
- Generic AI governance service descriptions that could apply to any geography
- Compliance framing that positions the advisor as an extension of the regulatory apparatus
- Lead magnet titles that use technical vocabulary the executive would not use about their own situation
- Any language that implies the executive's current documentation is likely adequate

## Deliverable 04 — Trust Signal Audit

### What credibility, process clarity, discretion, independence signals this ICP values most

**1. Geneva institutional embeddedness as first credential, not supporting claim.**
The executive evaluates advisors by their demonstrated presence in Geneva institutional life — not by their claimed expertise in Geneva. Publications, professional community membership, documented engagement with Geneva regulatory formations, and peer network visibility are the primary trust signals. Claims of Geneva expertise without visible institutional presence are discounted immediately. The credential is the presence, not the statement.

**2. Advisory methodology documentation as trust surface before analytical content.**
Executives in high-exposure situations are evaluating whether the advisor has a documented method for producing their analysis — not just whether the analysis is correct. The question is: if I challenge your output, what would you show me? Advisors who can describe their methodology documentation process — how they build the written record, what sources they draw from, what they do not include and why — signal credibility more effectively than advisors who present conclusions.

**3. Discretion as structural commitment, not stated policy.**
The executive assumes that any information shared in an advisory context could surface in market conversations, professional communities, or competitor intelligence. Discretion is not believed based on stated confidentiality policies. It is believed based on the advisor's demonstrated behavior: no references to unnamed clients, no case studies without explicit permission, no market positioning that would require sharing client information. The behavioral evidence is the trust signal. Statements about discretion are not trusted without behavioral evidence.

**4. Independence through named behavioral exclusions, not service inclusions.**
Executives evaluate independence by what the advisor does not do — not by what they claim in their positioning. Specific named exclusions perform independence more credibly than generic service claims. The advisor who explicitly states they do not work with AI tool vendors, do not provide AI implementation services, and do not accept referral arrangements from technology companies is performing independence in a way that advisors who simply claim independence cannot match.

**5. Written summary as primary trust artifact, not conversation summary.**
The executive who evaluates advisory quality over time is evaluating the written record the advisor produces — not the conversation quality. The written summary that survives the briefing, that the executive can re-read, annotate, and share selectively with a board colleague, is the durable trust artifact. Advisors who produce precise, defensible, clearly-scoped written summaries are trusted more than advisors who produce comprehensive conversation summaries.

**6. Self-selection language demonstrating genuine ICP understanding.**
Executives evaluate whether an advisor understands their specific situation by reading how the advisor describes it. Language that reflects the executive's actual experience — the signature question, the board moment, the counterparty questionnaire — is more trust-generating than language that describes the executive's category. Advisors who use the executive's own vocabulary, and who describe situations the executive recognizes from their own experience, are trusted before the briefing begins.

**7. Peer silence as intelligence signal (inverse trust mechanism).**
The executive reads peer silence about an advisor as a negative information signal, not a neutral one. When executives discuss an advisor positively in peer conversation, it is a strong positive signal. When executives do not mention an advisor at all in peer conversations, it is not neutral — it registers as absence of trust. The advisor's peer-network visibility and the quality of what is said in that network are primary trust evaluation inputs.

**8. Pricing language as qualification and trust signal simultaneously.**
Pricing that is specific, clearly scoped, and positioned as a qualification mechanism — not a commitment threshold — performs dual function. The executive who reads a specific price attached to a specific deliverable understands that the advisor has thought precisely about what they are selling. Vague pricing or range pricing without clear deliverable definitions signals uncertainty that transfers to content quality perception.

## Deliverable 06 — Findings Summary

### Six Net-New Insights on DigitalCoa.ch Positioning (Distinct from C214–C248 Phase 06)

**Insight 1: Pre-decision documentation positioning as primary advisory timing category.**
The most sophisticated Geneva executive segment is building documentation adequacy before the Federal Council decision arrives — not in anticipation of a specific outcome, but regardless of outcome. This is a pre-decision posture distinct from regulatory anticipation and distinct from reactive compliance. The advisory question the executive is asking is not "what will the rule be?" but "what must my documentation look like before the rule arrives, so that it is adequate regardless of direction?" This timing category has not been named in Geneva market positioning. The service design required is specific: the advisor works with the executive's current documentation state and builds toward a standard that would hold under any Federal Council direction. This is a more valuable advisory service than post-decision positioning because the executive who engages pre-decision has more time, more options, and a lower cost of adequate documentation than the executive who engages post-decision.

**Insight 2: Advisory methodology documentation as discrete trust signal distinct from analytical content.**
Executives in high-exposure situations are evaluating the advisor's method for producing analysis — not just the analysis itself. The question "if I challenged your output, what would you show me?" is answered by the advisor who can describe their methodology documentation process. This is distinct from analytical credibility: an advisor can be analytically credible without demonstrating methodology documentation process, and the executive in personal liability territory treats these as different questions. DigitalCoa.ch positioning should include explicit language about how the written assessment is built — what sources are drawn from, what the assessment does not include and why, what the executive would see if they reviewed the underlying methodology. This language performs credibility before the substantive content is evaluated.

**Insight 3: Peer-network trust architecture as primary conversion mechanism, not supporting evidence.**
The Geneva executive does not arrive at DigitalCoa.ch through digital discovery channels primarily. They arrive through the Geneva informal information circuit — peer conversations where an executive who has worked with Sergio describes what they found valuable, and the description includes enough specificity that the prospect recognizes their own situation. The peer circuit is not a reference list; it is the primary demand generation mechanism. This has conversion architecture implications: the quality of the written summary that ends each briefing determines the content of the peer conversation that generates the next prospect. The advisory product is the demand generation mechanism for the subsequent advisory product. This compounds with each cycle.

**Insight 4: Carrier renewal attestation as structural forcing function creating discrete advisory entry point.**
The third-generation carrier AI questionnaire has introduced present-tense attestation language that creates documented personal exposure for managing directors and principals. The attestation is not asking whether a policy exists; it is asking whether the signatory has evaluated the adequacy of their AI governance documentation against a standard that is not explicitly defined. This structural forcing function is operating on every Geneva professional services firm with active PI coverage. The executive who signed the attestation has a specific, dateable, personally-significant exposure. No Geneva market participant is positioning to address this specific triggering event with a discrete advisory product. The naming opportunity is the attestation review — a specific service designed for the executive who signed the carrier attestation and wants to understand what they actually committed to.

**Insight 5: Multi-obligation sequencing confusion as primary advisory surface, not documentation gap.**
The executive managing simultaneous carrier, counterparty, regulatory, and board obligations is not experiencing a documentation gap — they are experiencing a sequencing confusion. They know what their obligations are; they do not know which obligation to address first, how to ensure that addressing one does not create exposure under another, and what a coherent response to simultaneous obligations looks like. The advisory surface is prioritization and coherence, not documentation completeness. Advisors who offer documentation improvement without addressing sequencing are solving a different problem than the one the executive is experiencing. The naming opportunity is multi-obligation coherence — a specific advisory product that maps the executive's simultaneous obligations and produces a sequenced response architecture.

**Insight 6: Geneva French-language analytical authority as compounding positioning investment, not translation function.**
DigitalCoa.ch positioning in French is not translation from English content; it is primary analytical authority operating in French, leading with Geneva institutional context that English-language advisors do not have. The Geneva French-language professional community operates in a different information environment than the English-language Geneva professional community, and the two communities have different trust mechanisms, different reference standards, and different vocabulary for the same underlying accountability questions. Leading in French in Geneva is not following English-language leaders in translation — it is operating in a market with lower competitive density and higher institutional credibility in specific segments. The compounding logic: each piece of French-language analytical content that demonstrates Geneva-specific authority increases the credibility of subsequent content, and the accumulation creates a moat that English-language competitors cannot replicate without the same institutional investment.
