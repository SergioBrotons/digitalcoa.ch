## Deliverable 01 — ICP Language

### English

**How Geneva executives describe their need for AI governance advice:**

*"I approved the AI tool. I did not approve every decision that would be made with it. But the accountability trail leads back to my signature."*

*"The question I cannot answer is not 'do we have AI governance.' It is 'when someone asks who decided, what will the record show.' That's what I don't know."*

*"My organization made significant AI decisions during a period when nobody was asking these questions. Those decisions are now mine. I cannot explain them because they were never documented in a form that would survive examination."*

*"I've been through enough regulatory processes to know that the first question is never the real question. The first question is 'show me your documentation.' If your documentation doesn't answer the second question — 'show me why this decision was made' — you're already in difficulty."*

*"I have colleagues who are further along than I am. When I ask them what they did, they describe the same problem I have, with the same absence of record. We're all in the same position. None of us knows what the standard actually is."*

*"What I need is not a framework. What I need is to be able to sit in front of a board or a regulator and explain the decisions that were made, who made them, and why. I cannot do that right now."*

**Triggers:**
- A board question arrived without a documented position to stand behind
- A carrier or counterparty questionnaire requiring response in a domain where decisions were made without documentation
- A professional departure revealing that AI decision attribution knowledge left with the person
- A peer conversation in which someone described a regulatory question and the executive recognized their own situation in the description
- An AI vendor change, acquisition, or deprecation forcing reconstruction of decisions made under the previous tool
- A personal liability reflection: recognizing that a signature was placed on decisions that were not fully understood at the time

**Expectations:**
- An attribution map: who is accountable for which AI decisions, documented in a form that would survive examination
- A written position that can be stated under questioning — not a framework, a defensible record
- Honest assessment of what the documentation would show if produced tomorrow
- Language precise enough to be used in a board context without requiring translation
- Discretion: this analysis is not shared, not referenced, not used as illustrative material

**Distrusts:**
- Anyone who describes AI governance as "getting compliant"
- Advisors who need extensive discovery before they can describe what they would deliver
- Frameworks presented before the problem has been understood
- Pricing presented as a project scope rather than a specific bounded figure
- Any process that separates the analysis from the person who will use it — the executive should work directly with the senior advisor, not a team

## Deliverable 02 — Market Signals

### Where Geneva executive demand is emerging:

**1. The attribution architecture gap — from documentation to decision ownership**

The demand is not primarily for "AI governance documentation." It is for clarity on who owns the decisions that AI systems are making, under whose authority, and on what record. The executive who has AI tools deployed is not the same person who made the decision to deploy them — or is, but cannot reconstruct the reasoning. The advisory product is an attribution map: a documented record of decision ownership that would survive the question "who decided this, and why." This is distinct from a governance policy document. It is a specific accountability architecture that can be stated under examination.

**2. The mid-career professional departure intelligence gap**

Senior professionals in Geneva — managing partners, compliance officers, CFOs, executive directors — are leaving positions and carrying unresolved AI governance questions into new roles. The question they carry is personal: what is my exposure for decisions made under my authority that were not documented at the time? This demand is distinct from organizational governance demand. It is personal, portable, and time-sensitive. The advisory product is a personal attribution assessment: what decisions attached to you, what the record would show, and what a defensible position requires.

**3. The post-summary maintenance obligation — new territory**

Executives who have received a written AI governance position from an advisor are now encountering a problem that did not previously exist: they have a documented position, and conditions are changing. Tool versions update. Staff depart. New AI tools are adopted. The written summary they received is now incomplete — it does not reflect the current state. The question of when and how to update the documented position is arising without an existing answer. This creates a distinct advisory occasion: not the initial briefing, but the maintenance obligation that follows.

**4. Geneva multi-domain pattern compounding as competitive moat**

The advisor who has worked across Geneva legal, finance, consulting, and NGO professional services contexts accumulates a pattern knowledge base that is not available to single-domain advisors. The patterns of how accountability questions form, how carrier and counterparty questionnaires probe, and how regulatory expectations crystallize in each domain are related — and observing them across domains produces a systemic understanding that single-domain advisors cannot replicate. This multi-domain pattern knowledge is the specific moat: it compounds with each additional domain engagement and cannot be quickly acquired by a competitor.

**5. The "what have others encountered" intelligence demand**

Executives who have no Geneva-specific AI governance experience are seeking intelligence from those who do — not to understand frameworks, but to understand what the actual examination experience looks like. What questions were asked. What documentation was produced. What the examiner was looking for. This intelligence demand is specific, concrete, and not satisfiable through published guidance — because the standard has not been published. The advisory who can provide this intelligence, without identifying the parties involved, is providing something that cannot be obtained elsewhere.

### What is not working in advisory positioning:

**Framework-centric positioning** — The executive who has encountered a framework-based advisory product reports that frameworks produce documents, not positions. A framework tells you what a properly designed governance system looks like. It does not tell you what your documentation would show if produced tomorrow, or who is accountable for the decisions that were made.

**Large-firm brand reliance** — The Geneva executive who has worked with Big Four firms knows that the credibility at engagement sale is not the capacity at delivery. The brand provides initial trust; the work is done by others. For a problem that requires senior judgment and senior attention throughout, this structure is disqualifying.

**Process over substance** — Positioning that emphasizes the advisory process — discovery, methodology, framework application — fails with executives who want to understand what they are buying before committing. The description of the output should precede the description of the process.

**Compliance-framed language** — "AI compliance" is the wrong register for this audience. The executive who needs advisory help is not looking for compliance; they are looking for a defensible position. The distinction is meaningful: compliance implies a defined standard; defensibility implies a judgment about what would hold under questioning.

## Deliverable 03 — Competitive Analysis

### How other advisors are positioning AI governance in Geneva:

**1. Big Four / Global Professional Services Firms**

*Positioning:* AI governance as a workstream within broader digital risk, technology transformation, or regulatory compliance engagements.

*Credible claims:* Established client relationships at board level, global methodology libraries, regulatory network, ability to resource large-scale documentation projects.

*Gaps:*
- The Geneva institutional layer is not the primary analytical frame — Swiss domestic accountability and international organization accountability structures are treated as local implementations of global methodology
- The team that wins the engagement is senior; the team that does the work is junior — the executive who needs senior advisory attention throughout is underserved by this structure
- Structural independence questions: the same firm advises AI deployers and AI vendors, and consults for regulators on AI governance frameworks — the independence claim cannot be behavioral when the conflicts are structural
- Engagement economics require large engagements to be viable — the bounded, specific, 90-minute advisory product that Geneva executives are actually buying is not their model

**2. Swiss Law Firms**

*Positioning:* AI governance as a legal compliance and risk management question within existing legal mandates.

*Credible claims:* Legal privilege protection, established client trust, bar-regulated professional standards, regulatory understanding through mandate work.

*Gaps:*
- The accountability attribution problem is not primarily a legal question — the executive needs judgment about what the documentation would show and how to construct a defensible position, not a legal opinion on obligation
- Legal privilege does not protect operational governance judgment from regulatory examination — the privilege question is itself unresolved in Geneva AI contexts
- Hourly billing structures create misaligned incentives opposite to the executive's interest — more work, higher bills within the same engagement
- The product tends toward documentation production — a legal-grade document that satisfies legal review — rather than a judgment-grade position that would survive examination
- The Geneva-specific institutional pattern knowledge — how carrier questionnaires probe, what counterparty requirements look like, how informal regulatory signals form — is not the law firm's primary knowledge base

**3. Technology and Cybersecurity Advisory Firms**

*Positioning:* AI governance as a technical security, infrastructure control, and implementation problem.

*Credible claims:* Deep tool-specific knowledge, implementation capability, operational technology understanding, control framework expertise.

*Gaps:*
- The Geneva AI governance problem is institutional and accountability-based, not primarily technical — the question is not which controls are missing but who is accountable for which decisions and on what record
- Technical advisors define the problem as "gaps in controls or documentation" — the executive's problem is "what would hold when someone asks me to explain the decisions that were made"
- Geneva institutional context — international org accountability structures, Swiss domestic regulatory layering, carrier underwriting signals, Geneva professional community formation — is outside the technical frame
- Independence questions arise structurally when the same firm implements AI systems and advises on governance controls for those systems
- The deliverable is technical documentation, not a defensible executive position that can be stated under board or regulatory questioning

**4. Independent Boutique AI Governance Advisors (emerging, few in Geneva)**

*Positioning:* Independent boutique AI governance advisory, Geneva-focused, senior attention.

*Credible claims:* Independence from AI vendors and implementers, no conflicts, senior advisory attention, Geneva institutional focus.

*Gaps:*
- The market is nascent — accumulated pattern knowledge across multiple Geneva situations is still building, and the evidence of that knowledge is not yet publicly demonstrated through written outputs
- Trust signal architecture tends toward stated independence rather than demonstrated independence — "we are independent" is a claim; behavioral exclusions performed over time are evidence
- The written summary as primary product artifact is not yet established as a market-recognized standard — executives do not know to ask for it until they receive one
- Pricing and scope architecture is often undefined or variable, creating friction with executives who need specific bounded products
- Multi-domain pattern knowledge — legal + finance + consulting + NGO — is not yet demonstrated as a compounding moat because the multi-domain engagement base is still forming

**5. The dominant gap across all competitors:**

No competitor is demonstrably combining four things simultaneously: a specific bounded advisory product with a named deliverable, Geneva-specific institutional pattern knowledge accumulated across multiple domains, demonstrated behavioral independence through named exclusions maintained over time, and a written summary that contains no trace of the advisor's commercial interest. Each competitor addresses one or two of these dimensions. The advisor who demonstrates all four — through the quality of the written summary and the precision of the intake process — occupies a positioning space that is not currently filled.

## Deliverable 04 — Trust Signal Audit

### What credibility, process clarity, discretion, and independence signals this ICP values most:

**Credibility signals:**

1. **Attribution architecture precision**
The ability to describe the accountability attribution problem with precision — what it is, what it produces, what a defensible attribution map looks like — without reference to a specific client situation demonstrates the pattern knowledge that matters. Credibility is built through analytical precision, not through client references. The executive who hears the problem described correctly, for the first time, has the experience of recognition — this is the credibility signal.

2. **Multi-domain Geneva institutional knowledge**
An advisor who demonstrates understanding of how accountability questions form in Geneva legal, finance, consulting, and NGO contexts — and who can show how patterns across these domains inform the analysis — demonstrates the systemic understanding that distinguishes accumulated knowledge from framework application. The executive who operates in one domain benefits from pattern knowledge accumulated across others.

3. **Post-examination intelligence specificity**
The credibility of an advisor is demonstrated by the precision with which they can describe what an AI governance examination actually requires — not what published guidance says it requires, but what examiners probe and what documentation has held in practice. This intelligence is credible when it is specific and when no client is identified in the delivery.

4. **The written summary as evidence of knowledge, not marketing**
The written summary should read as if it could only have been produced by someone with Geneva-specific pattern knowledge — because it references institutional context, regulatory signals, and the specific accountability structures that apply. The executive should be able to read the summary and recognize that this knowledge could not have been obtained from a framework or a checklist.

**Process clarity signals:**

5. **Direct senior intake — no administrative gate**
The intake process should be direct: the executive sends a brief description of context, the advisor responds with a specific assessment of fit, and the briefing is scheduled. Any process that requires discovery forms, administrative staff interaction, or a sales process before speaking with the senior advisor signals the wrong organizational priority. The executive should work with the senior advisor from first contact.

6. **Scope defined before commitment, not during**
The executive wants to know what the written summary will contain, what it will not contain, and what the boundaries of the assessment are — before committing. The ability to define scope precisely before engagement is evidence of pattern knowledge: the advisor who has seen enough situations to know what the assessment can and cannot produce. An advisor who defines scope during engagement is managing an open-ended commercial situation.

7. **Specific pricing as qualification mechanism**
A specific CHF price — not a range, not "starting at" — signals that the advisor knows what they are selling, has defined the scope clearly, and is not managing the engagement toward a larger commercial outcome. Pricing specificity performs both qualification and trust.

8. **Bilingual capability as standard operating practice**
The Geneva executive operates in English and French across different contexts. The advisory that can conduct the briefing and produce the written summary in either language — without this being treated as an exception or an add-on — demonstrates the Geneva embeddedness that is the primary credential.

**Discretion signals:**

9. **No client reference in any form**
The executive assumes that what they describe will not be used as illustrative material, case study, testimonial, or reference. This assumption must not be violated under any circumstances. Pattern knowledge should be demonstrable through analytical precision, not through client-experience references. Even abstract references to "a Geneva professional services firm" or "a Geneva financial institution" are read as thin disguises for identifiable situations.

10. **Written summary ownership transfer**
The written summary is the executive's document. It is not retained by the advisor. It is not referenced in marketing. It is not used as a template or example. It is not discussed with other clients. The executive takes it to the board, to the regulator, or to a subsequent advisor — as their document. This is the fundamental trust mechanism.

11. **No post-engagement follow-up as commercial opportunity**
The period after the written summary is delivered should not be managed as a commercial opportunity. If follow-up contact occurs, it should be brief, specific to the client's stated situation, and not framed as an opportunity to expand the engagement. The executive who receives post-summary contact that reads as business development has received evidence of the advisor's actual priority.

**Independence signals:**

12. **Named behavioral exclusions as structural foundation**
The advisory practice does not implement AI systems, does not conduct AI governance audits as certification exercises, does not receive AI vendor referral compensation, does not advise regulatory agencies on AI governance matters where conflicts with client work could arise, and does not provide AI vendor recommendations. These exclusions are named on the website and in the briefing documentation — not as disclaimers, but as the structural foundation of the practice's positioning. They are performed, not just stated.

13. **The written summary contains no commercial interest**
The written summary should contain only analysis that serves the executive's interest. If any language in the summary could be read as managing the executive toward a larger engagement, a specific vendor outcome, or an extended advisory relationship, the executive will detect it. The discipline of producing a summary that contains only the executive's interest — demonstrated through behavioral consistency over time — is the structural independence mechanism.

14. **Exclusion maintenance record as long-term trust signal**
Over time, the most credible trust signal is the record of what the practice declined to do. The advisory practice that declined engagements that would have served commercial interest but violated named exclusions builds a record that no statement of independence can replicate. The maintenance of the exclusion list is the trust signal. This record accumulates with each declined engagement.

## Deliverable 06 — Findings Summary

### 6 Net-New Insights — DigitalCoa.ch Advisory Practice Positioning

*Distinct from C214–C236 Phase 06*

---

**Finding 01: Accountability attribution architecture as the specific advisory product — distinct from governance assessment**

Previous cycles identified AI governance adequacy as the assessment question. C237 identifies a more specific and more actionable problem: the executive cannot answer "who decided this and why" because the attribution record was never constructed. The advisory product is not an AI governance assessment — it is an accountability attribution architecture. The primary deliverable is a documented attribution map: who owns which AI decisions, on what authority, and on what record that accountability rests. This framing is more precise than "governance" and more useful than "compliance" — it tells the executive exactly what they will receive and exactly what question it answers. The conversion insight: an executive who recognizes the attribution problem cannot un-recognize it. This is the entry trigger that no other advisory positioning is capturing with this precision.

**Finding 02: Mid-career professional departure as a distinct and underserved demand occasion**

Senior professionals departing Geneva organizations — managing partners, compliance officers, CFOs, executive directors — are carrying unresolved personal AI governance exposure into new roles. The question is personal and portable: what decisions attached to me under my authority, what would the record show, and what is my exposure if those decisions are examined in my new position? This demand is distinct from organizational governance advisory because it is bounded by the individual's accountability history, not the organization's current tool inventory. The advisory product — a personal attribution assessment covering the prior role — is specific, bounded, and time-sensitive before the new role begins. No competitor is addressing this demand with a named, bounded product. The timing of the need (pre-departure or immediately post-departure) is predictable and therefore addressable through targeted positioning.

**Finding 03: The post-summary maintenance obligation as an emerging advisory occasion**

Executives who received a written AI governance position from an advisor are now encountering a new problem: conditions have changed, the documented position is incomplete, and there is no existing framework for when or how to update it. Tool versions update. Staff depart. New AI tools are adopted under existing approvals. The written summary they received is now a historical document, not a current position. This creates a distinct advisory occasion — not the initial briefing, but the maintenance obligation that follows. The advisory product here is a structured update protocol: a bounded mechanism for keeping the attribution architecture current without requiring a full reassessment. This is a new category of demand arising from the first generation of clients who received written summaries and are now living with changing conditions.

**Finding 04: Multi-domain Geneva pattern compounding as the structural competitive moat**

The advisory that has worked across Geneva legal, finance, consulting, and NGO professional services contexts accumulates a pattern knowledge base that multiplies in value with each additional domain. The patterns of how accountability questions form, how carrier and counterparty questionnaires probe, and how regulatory expectations crystallize are related across domains — and observing them systemically produces a judgment quality that single-domain advisors cannot replicate. This multi-domain pattern knowledge is the structural moat: it cannot be purchased, cannot be quickly built, and compounds with each additional engagement. The written summary produced for a Geneva consulting firm benefits from pattern knowledge accumulated from legal, finance, and NGO engagements. This compounding effect is not available to advisors who operate in a single domain. It becomes more defensible with each cycle.

**Finding 05: "What have others encountered" intelligence as distinct from regulatory guidance**

The executive who has no Geneva-specific AI governance examination experience is not asking for regulatory guidance — they know the guidance does not exist in usable form. They are asking for intelligence: what did a peer encounter when they were examined, what documentation held, what did not, and what was the examiner actually looking for. This intelligence is specific, concrete, and not available from any published source because the standard is forming through informal mechanisms before it reaches publication. The advisory who can provide this intelligence — without identifying the examined party — provides something that cannot be obtained elsewhere. The quality of this intelligence delivery is itself the trust signal: the precision of the description, the specificity of what held and what did not, and the absence of client identification demonstrate the accumulated pattern knowledge that makes the intelligence credible.

**Finding 06: Geneva independence arbitrage as the specific market gap — and the written summary as its evidence**

The Geneva AI governance advisory market has a specific structural gap: advisors who have Geneva-specific institutional knowledge typically have conflicts that limit their independence (vendor relationships, regulatory advisory work, implementation engagements), while advisors who are independent typically lack the Geneva-specific institutional knowledge to produce a useful written summary. The advisor who combines both — genuine independence through named behavioral exclusions, and accumulated Geneva-specific pattern knowledge from multi-domain engagements — occupies a market position that is not currently filled. This combination cannot be claimed; it must be demonstrated. The written summary is the demonstration: the executive who reads the summary should immediately recognize that it could only have been produced by someone with Geneva-specific pattern knowledge and no commercial interest in its conclusions. The moat is the combination. The written summary is the evidence of the combination. The exclusion list is what makes the independence credible.
