## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Board accountability without board AI literacy**
Boards are being asked to oversee AI risk without having the literacy to evaluate what they are approving. The approval happens without genuine evaluation. The governance gap is structural at the board level.

**Signal 2: Compliance team as proxy evaluator creating information asymmetry**
The compliance team evaluates AI risk and reports to the executive. The executive does not have direct access to the evaluation basis. The compliance team becomes a necessary intermediary who shapes what the executive knows about their own AI exposure.

**Signal 3: Post-incident positioning as the moment of truth**
The executive who must respond to an AI-assisted incident needs to be able to demonstrate that their decision process was sound. This need is not met by prior governance documentation — it is met by the quality of reasoning records at the time decisions were made. The moment of truth arrives after the fact.

**Signal 4: Peer silence on AI governance as information about personal exposure**
Executives who do not discuss AI governance with peers are not signaling collective adequacy. They are signaling that they have personal exposure they do not want to surface. The silence is private information, not social evidence.

**Signal 5: The Geneva event circuit as demand generator**
Geneva's concentration of AI governance events creates a systematic demand channel. Executives leave these events with questions they cannot answer and no clear next step. The post-event information processing gap is a discrete service opportunity.

**Signal 6: Direct executive access as qualification signal**
The executive who reaches out directly, without going through a compliance intermediary, is signaling a specific kind of urgency and a specific relationship to the problem. The compliance intermediary channel filters for a different kind of engagement.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Competitive gap 1: Decision authorship mapping as discrete advisory product**
No advisor offers a service that maps decision authorship for the executive's specific AI decisions — identifying where accountability attaches, what the exposure is at each decision point, and what the executive can do about it. This mapping is the core of the accountability question.

**Competitive gap 2: Post-incident reasoning record reconstruction**
The executive who faces an AI-assisted incident needs to be able to reconstruct the reasoning record that existed before the incident. No advisory service addresses this specific need. The reconstruction has to happen after the fact, under pressure.

**Competitive gap 3: Board AI literacy briefing**
A briefing designed for board members that gives them the reference point they need to evaluate AI risk — not technical depth, but the evaluative literacy to ask the right questions and understand the answers. This briefing does not exist in the Geneva market.

**Competitive gap 4: Executive peer accountability forum**
A confidential forum where Geneva executives can discuss AI governance with peers who have similar exposure and similar constraints. The competitive dynamics that prevent open discussion in professional networks make this format necessary. It does not exist.

**Competitive gap 5: FDPIC pattern translation into documentation practice**
The FDPIC's investigative pattern is observable and learnable. Translating that pattern into specific documentation practice guidance — what the record actually looks like when examined — is a specific advisory product that does not exist.

**Competitive gap 6: Geneva event post-processing advisory**
The post-event information processing gap is a specific demand channel. No advisor is positioned to capture it with a discretely scoped, time-limited advisory engagement specifically designed for the post-event context.

## 04 — Trust Signal Audit

**Pattern specificity:** The executive evaluates whether the advisor has seen their specific situation before. Generic AI governance framing is not credible. Specific pattern recognition — having seen the exact dynamic — is the primary trust signal.

**Independent judgment during the conversation:** The advisor who can demonstrate independent judgment in real time — not retreating to safe generalities when asked a specific question — signals competence that pre-prepared materials cannot.

**Geneva embeddedness:** The advisor who understands the Geneva institutional context — the regulatory culture, the professional networks, the event circuit, the peer dynamics — signals credibility that no generic advisor can replicate.

**Process exit clarity:** The advisor who can clearly describe what a completed engagement looks like, what the client has in hand at the end, and what they can do with it — this clarity is itself a trust signal.

**Discretion as professional practice:** The advisor who treats discretion not as a policy but as a professional practice — not discussing clients, not leveraging relationships for commercial purpose — signals trustworthiness that business development activity undermines.

**Operationalized independence through behavioral exclusions:** Independence is demonstrated by what the advisor does not do, not by what they claim. Named specific exclusions embedded in the practice's positioning are stronger trust signals than generic independence claims.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Decision authorship mapping as the gravitational center of executive AI governance**
The question "where does my accountability for this AI decision end?" is the gravitational center around which all executive AI governance questions orbit. Every other governance question is a derivative of this one. It should be the primary surface of the advisory practice.

**Finding 2: Reasoning record gap distinct from governance policy gap**
The executive needs reasoning records — documentation of how decisions were made, what was evaluated, what was chosen and why. This is different from having a governance policy. The policy does not substitute for the reasoning record.

**Finding 3: Board oversight adequacy as the structural governance gap**
Boards are accountable for AI risk without having the evaluative literacy to oversee it. The gap is structural. The board approval that is given without genuine evaluation is active exposure, not protection.

**Finding 4: Post-incident positioning as the moment of truth**
The executive needs to be able to demonstrate that their decision process was sound when something goes wrong. This need cannot be met after the fact — the reasoning record has to exist before the incident. The moment of truth arrives after the fact.

**Finding 5: Peer silence as signal about personal exposure**
The executive who does not discuss AI governance with peers is not signaling collective adequacy. They are signaling private knowledge about personal exposure. The silence is private information being protected.

**Finding 6: Direct executive access as qualification signal**
The executive who reaches out directly, without going through compliance, is signaling a specific relationship to the urgency. The direct access channel identifies the prospects who have the most acute need and the clearest authority to engage.
