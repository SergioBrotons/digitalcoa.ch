### Triggers

- Receiving a funder or procurement AI questionnaire with specific questions that cannot be answered from current documentation
- A peer organization's public AI-related incident or regulatory outcome surfacing personal accountability questions
- Annual vendor contract renewal arriving and forcing first structured review of AI accountability provisions signed without review
- A lateral hire arriving with AI practices formed at a prior firm with no documentation
- A board meeting where AI risk appears on the agenda without preparatory briefing or written position
- A carrier renewal questionnaire probing the 2023–mid-2024 early adoption period specifically

### Expectations

- A direct, honest assessment of their specific AI governance position — not reassurance, not a framework presentation
- Clarity on where personal accountability attaches in their role and situation
- A written artifact — specific to their firm, their role, their decisions — that can be kept and produced when needed
- A clear, specific next step — what they can actually do in the time they have
- Absolute discretion: the content of the engagement does not circulate

### Distrusts

- Advisors who present a methodology before understanding the specific situation
- Frameworks presented as answers when the question has not been defined
- Documentation exercises that produce policies disconnected from actual decisions made
- Advisors who cannot describe what the engagement produces at the end
- General training positioned as governance adequacy for senior accountability holders
- Any advisor who uses the engagement for business development purposes
- Pricing structures designed to maximize upsell rather than to serve the client cleanly

## 02 — Market Signals: Where Geneva Executive Demand Is Emerging

**Signal 1: Funder and procurement enforcement proxy as discrete demand channel**
Funder AI governance requirements and procurement AI standards are functioning as de facto enforcement mechanisms for organizations not directly subject to Swiss AI regulation. International organizations, NGOs, and grant-funded entities are being asked to demonstrate AI governance adequacy as a condition of continued funding. This creates a specific, dateable advisory entry point: the funder questionnaire or procurement requirement — a concrete trigger with real relationship consequences. This is distinct from counterparty questionnaires in mechanism (funding conditionality vs. commercial relationship) and in stakes (organizational continuity vs. deal progression).

**Signal 2: Third-generation carrier underwriting probing the early-adoption period specifically**
Carriers have evolved beyond framework documentation to methodology evidence. The third-generation questionnaire probes specifically what the firm did during the 2023 to mid-2024 early adoption period — when systematic documentation did not exist. The early adopters are being asked to document the undocumented past. This is a structural market pressure that will not recede and that no advisory product currently addresses directly.

**Signal 3: Lateral talent movement absorbing undocumented AI governance histories**
New hires arrive with AI practices formed at prior firms. Those practices were never documented. The hiring firm inherits undocumented AI governance without a protocol for managing it. This is a discrete, nameable exposure that is active now and not being addressed as a specific advisory product.

**Signal 4: Board AI accountability without evaluative literacy — structural adequacy gap**
Boards are accountable for AI risk oversight. They do not have the evaluative literacy to fulfill this oversight. The accountability and the capability are structurally disconnected. This is not a training gap — training addresses knowledge gaps. This is an adequacy gap: no published reference point defines what a board should understand, no standard process exists for board-level AI oversight, and no clear line delineates what the board is approving versus what it is being informed about.

**Signal 5: Vendor exit creating documentation debt for systems no longer in documented form**
AI vendors are being acquired, sunset, and consolidated. The documentation for systems that existed before exit is gone. The firm is accountable for outputs from systems it can no longer describe. This retrospective exposure has no forward solution and no advisor is currently offering it as a named discrete service.

**Signal 6: Geneva professional silence as institutional infrastructure failure**
The firms that most understand AI governance are most silent publicly. The market is forming standards without the input of the firms best positioned to contribute. The information failure is structural — not from competitive secrecy but from Geneva's professional culture of operating through discrete relationships rather than publication.

Publish forward-looking frameworks and design end-state governance architectures. Offer organizational capability building — structured learning, certification tracks, professional education credits. Addressing the awareness layer, not the senior executive's personal accountability exposure. Training signals the wrong organizational level for this ICP.

## 04 — Trust Signal Audit: What Credibility, Process Clarity, Discretion, and Independence Signals This ICP Values Most

### Credibility signals — in order of weight

**Pattern recognition without case studies:**
The executive evaluates credibility by the precision of the advisor's understanding — can the advisor describe the specific dynamic the executive is facing before the executive finishes describing it? This is pattern recognition. It is not a client reference. It is the advisor's demonstrated precision in naming the executive's situation accurately. The executive who hears their situation described back with precision recognizes an advisor who has worked at this level before.

**Swiss institutional groundedness:**
The executive evaluates whether the advisor understands the specific Geneva institutional environment — FDPIC investigative patterns, FINMA supervisory approach, how Geneva's professional community operates, the specific accountability questions that arise in this context. The institutional knowledge is the credential. An advisor who demonstrates genuine operational knowledge of this environment — not as a consultant with a Geneva address but as a practitioner embedded in it — is doing something that offshore advisors, large firm consultants, and generic AI governance specialists cannot replicate.

**Independent publication record:**
The executive who has read the advisor's publications before the first meeting arrives with a pre-formed judgment. Publications in the advisor's own voice — not syndicated content, not framework marketing — are the primary credibility surface before the first conversation. The publication record is the evidence that the advisor forms independent judgments and can articulate them without client attribution.

**Direct judgment expression:**
The executive expects the advisor to form a view in the first meeting and express it directly. Advisors who hedge consistently are perceived as unable or unwilling to provide the direct judgment this ICP requires. The executive is looking for someone who will say: this is what you have, this is where the gap is, this is what adequate looks like for your situation.

### Process clarity signals

**Engagement end-state definition:**
The executive asks: what does a completed engagement look like? The advisor who can answer this precisely — what the client holds at the end, what they can do with it, what the engagement does not cover — signals a practice that has done this before and thinks in terms of outcomes.

**Sequencing transparency:**
The executive wants to know what happens next before they commit. The advisor who can describe the sequencing — briefing, assessment, written summary, next steps — without being asked signals a structured approach. The absence of sequencing clarity signals improvisation.

**Scope boundary articulation:**
The advisor who can describe what they do not do — what falls outside the engagement, what requires a different kind of engagement — signals an integrity about scope that executives recognize as distinct. The ability to hold boundaries clearly is itself a trust signal.

### Discretion signals

**No commercial behavior during or after engagement:**
The advisor who uses the advisory relationship for business development signals a conflict of interest that the executive notes immediately. The executive expects to be left alone after the engagement unless they initiate contact. Discretion is demonstrated by the absence of commercial behavior.

**Practice information management:**
The advisor who demonstrates knowledge of when information should not be shared — about other clients, about the advisor's own situation — signals a practice where information management is a professional discipline, not a policy. The executive infers how their own information will be managed from how the advisor manages theirs.

**Contact protocol clarity:**
The advisor who specifies how and when they can be contacted after the engagement — and what happens when they cannot be reached — signals a practice that has thought about the client relationship as an ongoing operational reality, not just a transaction.

### Independence signals

**Named behavioral exclusion list:**
The advisor who articulates specific exclusions — what they do not do, who they do not work with — demonstrates independence through practice, not claim. The executive evaluates the exclusion list as the true definition of the practice. Named exclusions perform more credibility than claimed independence. "I do not implement AI governance systems. I do not sell platforms or software. I do not take referral arrangements from AI vendors. My revenue comes exclusively from professional advisory."

**No vendor or platform financial relationship:**
The advisor who has no financial relationship with AI vendors, platforms, or technology providers signals an independence the executive can verify. Vendor relationships introduce conflicts that the executive cannot fully assess but instinctively discounts.

**Clean pricing without upsell architecture:**
The advisor who prices the engagement clearly — with no upsell pathway, no tiered service ladder — signals a practice that is not optimizing for revenue expansion. The executive who sees a pricing structure designed to maximize engagement value notes it and adjusts their trust accordingly.
