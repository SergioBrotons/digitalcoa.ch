## 01 — ICP Language: How Senior Executives Describe Their Need for AI Governance Advice

### EN — English

**Trigger moments:**

The Geneva senior executive arrives at AI governance advice carrying a configuration of concerns that earlier cycles did not fully map. The managing director of a boutique professional services firm who has been using AI tools in client deliverables for eighteen months — and who has just realized, in a conversation with a peer, that the liability for what those tools produced followed the firm's name downstream, and that the engagement terms say nothing about it. The practice leader who discovered that a junior team member has been deploying an AI tool in client work without documentation, and who now carries personal professional exposure on top of organizational exposure. The board member who approved an AI system on a technology committee recommendation, and who has since heard a question asked in a board meeting they were not prepared to answer — and who has been sitting with the discomfort of not knowing whether what they approved is defensible.

The Geneva executive community is also entering a specific regulatory moment. July 2026 represents a convergence of Swiss AI regulatory milestones — not a single event, but a date around which the standard of care shifts. The executive who has been operating in the "before" period is beginning to recognize that the question is not just whether their current state is defensible, but whether it will remain defensible after the inflection point. This is a different psychology than urgency. It is the discomfort of knowing that the ground is about to move.

**Language they use:**

The language is personal and precise, carrying the specific register of someone who has realized they have been operating with an exposure they did not name. "I have been using AI in client work for a while now and I realized I don't know where my liability ends and the tool's begins." "My engagement terms were written before AI existed in our deliverables — I don't think they say anything about what happens when something goes wrong." "One of our juniors has been running client work through an AI tool and I only found out because something came back wrong." "I approved something on the technology committee's recommendation and I could not explain to you what it actually does in production." "I need to understand what I am personally exposed to — not what the organization is exposed to, what I am."

The frame that recurs most distinctly in C221 is the liability attribution question — not "do we have a governance problem" but "where does the AI end and where does my accountability begin." This is the precise language the executive uses to name their private concern. It is the question they have been carrying before they have the vocabulary to ask it.

**What they expect:**

They expect to encounter someone who has navigated this before — not at the level of theory, but at the level of practice. Someone who has sat in the board meeting where the AI question was raised unexpectedly, who has helped a managing partner understand what their professional registration actually covers when AI appears in client deliverables, who has reviewed engagement terms for a boutique firm and identified the AI liability gap that was invisible until it was named. The specificity of that experience is what they are evaluating. They want precision.

They expect to leave the first conversation knowing where they actually stand — not a framework, not a document, but an honest assessment of their current position. They want the advisor to form a view during the meeting and be willing to say it. They are evaluating judgment in real time.

**What they distrust:**

Anyone whose website uses generic AI governance language applicable to any jurisdiction, any organization, any size. Any advisor whose first meeting is visibly a qualification process. Law firms whose AI governance expertise arrived in the last twelve months. Vendors who arrive with platform demonstrations or ROI models. The advisory firm that cannot explain, in one sentence, what it does that a law firm does not do and what it does that a compliance firm does not do.

The Geneva executive has developed a specific sensitivity to advisors who position AI governance as a compliance obligation. Compliance framing signals that the advisor sees them as reactive, behind, and defensive. The executive who has been running AI in client work for eighteen months does not want to be positioned as someone who needs to catch up. They want to be positioned as someone who is taking a step that protects what they have built.

**The unstated question:**

The executive who has reached out is often carrying a question they do not say directly: they want to know whether the exposure they have identified is real, and whether the steps they have taken are sufficient. They have formed a private judgment about their situation — and they want a credible second opinion before they act on it. The advisory conversation that addresses this unstated question directly, and that is willing to form and deliver a view during the meeting, is the conversation that converts.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis: How Other Advisors Are Positioning AI Governance in Geneva

**The four current positioning types and their specific limitations for this ICP:**

**Law firms:** Credible on legal authority and privilege. Structurally limited for this ICP because their frame is legal — they provide legal opinions on obligations, not governance judgment on how to fulfill those obligations in a specific professional context. The managing director of a Geneva boutique who needs to understand what their professional registration covers when AI appears in client deliverables does not need a legal opinion. They need someone who can help them think through what defensible professional practice looks like in their specific situation. Law firms answer the question "what does the law require." The Geneva boutique professional is asking a different question: "what should I be doing given where this is heading, and will what I am doing protect me." Law firms are not designed to answer the second question at the personal professional level.

**Big Four consultancies:** Credible on scale, brand recognition, and project delivery. Not credible on independence — the perception that Big Four AI governance practices generate implementation revenue and maintain technology vendor relationships is structural and well-established in the Geneva professional community. The senior executive who has been through a Big Four AI governance pitch recognizes the product presentation regardless of the individual quality of the team. The Big Four serve organizational compliance functions; they are not designed to serve the individual professional's personal liability question.

**Boutique governance and risk firms:** More credible on independence than the Big Four, and more specialized. The structural limitation for this ICP is that most boutiques are positioned at the organizational compliance level — not at the level of the individual professional whose personal registration, name, and reputation are on the line. The boutique that specializes in AI model risk governance frameworks for internal risk teams is doing work useful for compliance committees, not for the managing partner who needs to understand what their engagement terms should say about AI liability.

**Independent advisors:** The positioning type most naturally aligned with this ICP's needs. The limitation is that most independent advisors have not made the individual professional — the managing director of a boutique firm, the practice leader, the board member — as a buyer explicit in their positioning language. They describe services at an organizational level rather than at the personal professional liability level. Independence is necessary but not sufficient without a clear statement of the specific buyer and the specific protection being offered.

**The specific gap no one is filling:**

The managing director of a Geneva boutique professional services firm who needs personal professional liability protection from AI decisions made in their practice — accessible without organizational involvement, without triggering a compliance review, and without requiring a specific legal question to have crystallized — is not served by any current market participant. The downstream liability creep problem identified in Phase 03 C221 is a boutique-scale problem that no current advisor is positioned to address. The advisory who explicitly fills this gap — serving the individual professional at the personal liability level, through a relationship structure designed for that moment — occupies a space that no current competitor has claimed.

## 04 — Trust Signal Audit: What This ICP Values Most

**Expertise credibility — the specific form it takes:**

The C221 senior executive ICP is not evaluating a credential. They are evaluating evidence of judgment — specific detail that can only come from direct experience with situations like theirs. The managing director of a Geneva boutique who has been running AI in client work wants to speak with someone who has reviewed engagement terms for firms like theirs, who understands what the Swiss professional registration framework covers and does not cover when AI appears in deliverables, who has helped a practice leader think through the liability attribution question before a problem occurred. That specificity of experience is the credibility signal that cannot be fabricated and that the executive uses to verify the claim of expertise.

The digital expression of this credibility is the quality of the thinking in the publications. When a Geneva professional reads a Sergio analysis and recognizes the specific situation described — because they have lived it — that recognition is itself a qualification event. The precision of the Swiss regulatory analysis, the understanding of how Swiss professional practice actually works, the recognition of the downstream liability geometry in boutique deliverables — that specificity is the evidence of pattern recognition.

**Process clarity — the type that removes the last barrier:**

The executive who is considering the introductory call has one remaining hesitation that process clarity removes: what happens if we are not the right fit? The advisor who can describe what the exit looks like — what the obligations are if the engagement does not proceed, what the introductory conversation is actually designed to do — removes the last hesitation. Most executives have been in advisory relationships where the exit was awkward or unclear. The practice that addresses this proactively demonstrates the kind of experience that makes the relationship safe to enter.

**Discretion — the practice, not the policy:**

The senior executive is not evaluating legal structures for confidentiality. They are evaluating whether the advisor understands the professional practice of discretion — what it means to hold a conversation in confidence, what it means to not reference the engagement in any form, what it means to behave as if the content of the relationship does not exist outside the relationship itself. The website copy that signals this understanding — without legal framing — is the discretion signal that matters. "I do not reference specific clients or engagements. What we discuss stays in the room." Legal language signals the wrong thing: that the advisor is thinking about the legal structure rather than the professional practice.

**Independence — structural and behavioral:**

Structural independence — no vendor affiliation, no implementation revenue, no affiliate products — needs to be stated once, clearly, as a factual account of how the practice is organized. The executive who understands the economics of the practice sees that advisory fees are the only revenue source, and stops worrying about conflicts.

Behavioral independence — demonstrated in the first meeting through the questions asked, the references not made, the precision of scope boundaries — is equally important and cannot be replaced by a structural statement. Independence is confirmed in the meeting, not on the website.

**What does not move the needle with this ICP:**

Certification badges. Industry awards. Client logos. Testimonial quotes. The senior executive who operates at board level or as a managing director can identify credential padding immediately and discounts it accordingly. These signals are designed for procurement processes; they do not speak to the judgment question the senior executive is actually evaluating.

Generic AI governance content that does not demonstrate Swiss professional practice specificity. Content that could have been written by any AI governance consultancy about any jurisdiction does not signal expertise to the Geneva professional who has to navigate Swiss professional registration obligations, multi-jurisdictional client relationships, and a specific regulatory calendar simultaneously.
