**Triggers:**

- A specific anticipated question — not a general governance concern, but a specific question the executive knows will arrive within a defined window, and for which the executive has no clean answer
- A colleague's question about a written summary that reveals a gap the summary was not designed to address — the gap in the document becomes the evidence of a gap in the governance architecture
- A peer event — watching another executive navigate a moment of AI governance exposure — that reframes the abstract into the concrete and makes action feel necessary rather than optional
- The recognition that the documentation was not designed to be read from outside — it was produced to satisfy internal requirements, and the perspective of the external questioner was never incorporated into the design
- A career occasion — joining a board, accepting a new role — that requires describing an AI governance position for decisions made before the executive's tenure
- The discovery that a peer has been navigating the same question for months without resolution, and that the question itself has evolved during that time — confirming that the problem is structural, not isolated

**Expectations:**

- An external perspective on what the documentation looks like to the person asking the question the executive cannot answer — not internal audit but external reception audit
- A named confirmation of what the executive already suspects but cannot verify independently — the condition described precisely enough to be recognized but not so precisely that it sounds like a diagnosis
- A service that addresses the coherence question with a written output designed to function in the executive's professional environment — board contexts, peer exchanges, counterparty conversations
- An advisor who demonstrates, in the first conversation, that they have encountered the specific question the executive is anticipating — not generic AI governance expertise, but the specific shape of the external question the executive is trying to prepare for
- A bounded engagement with a specific output — not a relationship that opens into an ongoing engagement, but a discrete question answered with a specific document

**Distrusts:**

- Advisors who describe the problem in terms the executive would need to already understand — the executive expects the advisor to demonstrate familiarity with the question from the outside, not translate the executive's own description
- Services framed as ongoing monitoring or continuous engagement — executives resist anything that feels like it creates dependency for the maintenance question
- Advisory content that reads as generic AI governance framework applied to Geneva, rather than Geneva-specific intelligence translated into advisory form
- Any impression that the advisor is using the first conversation to gather information rather than contribute it — the executive expects intelligence exchange, not information extraction
- Services where the written output is designed for internal reference but not for external circulation — executives who anticipate that the document will travel need to know it can hold up in contexts they do not control

## 02 — Market Signals: Where Geneva Executive Demand Is Emerging

**Signal 1: The external-question perspective as unmet advisory surface**
Geneva executives who have documentation are not primarily uncertain about their internal adequacy. They are uncertain about what their documentation looks like to the person asking the question they are not prepared for. The unmet surface is not documentation existence — it is external reception audit. The executive needs to know what the documentation would reveal to the counterparty, regulator, or board member who is asking from outside the documentation-building process. This perspective gap is distinct from any internal governance assessment.

**Signal 2: The anticipated question window as urgency generator**
Geneva executives are increasingly able to anticipate when a question will arrive — carrier renewal season, board meeting calendar, counterparty due diligence cycle — before the question arrives. The anticipatory window creates urgency that is qualitatively different from general governance anxiety. The executive who knows the question is coming in thirty days has a specific decision window that general awareness does not create. Market demand is accelerating not from increased uncertainty but from increased predictability of the triggering moment.

**Signal 3: Peer event observation as conversion mechanism more powerful than peer recommendation**
Geneva executives who have converted to seeking advisory help are more likely to have done so after observing a peer's experience — watching a colleague navigate an AI governance moment — than after receiving a peer recommendation for a specific advisor. The observation of a peer's experience creates conviction that abstract concern is concrete and time-bounded. The market signal is the executive who acts after witnessing, not after being referred.

**Signal 4: Written summary portability as primary demand generation mechanism**
The written summary is circulating in Geneva professional networks in ways that are not tracked by any referral mechanism. Executives share the document with board colleagues, with peers in other organizations, with professional contacts in adjacent sectors. The document's portability — designed to function in contexts the advisor does not control — is generating demand without the recipient having received a referral to the advisor. The demand is document-referral, not advisor-referral.

**Signal 5: Question evolution recognition as specific unmet need**
Executives who have been navigating AI governance questions in conversation with peers are discovering that the question itself evolves during the period of exploration — the question they thought they were asking is not the question they need to answer when the moment arrives. This question evolution phenomenon is creating demand for an advisor who can map the current shape of the question as well as its anticipated future shape, before the executive commits to a position based on a version of the question that has since changed.

**Signal 6: The "before it arrives" timing preference as market positioning signal**
More Geneva executives are seeking advisory help before the anticipated question arrives, not after it has been asked. The "before" timing preference reflects a growing understanding that the documentation coherence question is easier to resolve before it becomes an externally-triggered event. The market signal is a shift in timing preference from reactive to anticipatory — and a corresponding demand for services that can be accessed before the triggering event.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis: How Other Advisors Are Positioning AI Governance in Geneva

**Gap 1: External reception audit not offered as discrete service**
No Geneva advisor offers a service specifically designed to show the executive what their AI governance documentation looks like from outside — from the perspective of the counterparty question, the regulatory inquiry, the board briefing. The external reception perspective is not addressed by any current positioning. Advisors who offer internal governance assessments are not offering the external question mirror.

**Gap 2: Anticipated-question window positioning not used**
Advisors approach the market with problem-description language — what the executive is experiencing, what concerns they have. No advisor positions for the specific timing window — the executive who knows a question is coming in thirty days and needs to be prepared before it arrives. The anticipatory window framing converts urgency more precisely than problem description.

**Gap 3: Peer observation as conversion mechanism not leveraged**
Advisors seek peer referrals — a peer recommending the advisor to another executive. No advisor leverages the peer observation conversion — the executive who acts after watching a colleague navigate an AI governance moment. The observation conversion does not require the advisor to be known to the acting executive before the observation; it requires the advisor to have been present in the peer network as a known entity when the event occurred.

**Gap 4: Question evolution mapping not conceptualized as service**
No advisor offers a service that tracks the evolution of the executive's question over time — mapping how the question being asked has changed since the executive first encountered it, and what the current question actually requires. The question evolution phenomenon is unnamed in the market. Naming it creates a specific advisory surface that is distinct from general AI governance advisory.

**Gap 5: Written summary portability not designed as product element**
Advisors produce written summaries as deliverables — documents that record the briefing output. No advisor designs the written summary as a portable professional artifact that functions in peer exchanges and counterparty conversations without creating new exposure. The portability design standard changes the document's architecture: it must be quotable, defensible in contexts the advisor does not control, and neutral with respect to the reader's position.

**Gap 6: "Before it arrives" timing not positioned as primary entry point**
Advisors approach the market with services framed around problem resolution — the executive has a problem and needs it addressed. No advisor positions for the executive who wants to resolve the question before it arrives. The anticipatory framing is more powerful for a specific executive segment: the one who has sufficient pattern awareness to know the question is coming and sufficient urgency to act before the external event forces the issue.

## 06 — Findings Summary: 6 Net-New Insights on DigitalCoa.ch Positioning

**Finding 1: External Reception Audit as the Primary Unmet Need, Distinct from Internal Governance Assessment**
Geneva executives who have AI governance documentation are not primarily uncertain about their internal adequacy — whether their documentation meets their own standards. They are uncertain about external reception — what the documentation would reveal to the person asking the question they are not prepared for. The external reception question is distinct from and precedes the internal adequacy question. An executive can have documentation that meets internal standards and still be exposed when read from outside, because external questioners apply standards that were not incorporated into the documentation's design. DigitalCoa.ch's positioning should lead with the external reception framing — the question of what the documentation looks like to the person asking from outside — because it is the more actionable and more urgent framing for the executive who has documentation and suspects it may not hold up.

**Finding 2: The Anticipated-Question Window as Distinct Urgency Signal**
Geneva executives who are seeking advisory help are increasingly doing so not from general governance concern but from specific anticipatory urgency — they know a question is coming within a defined window (carrier renewal, board meeting, counterparty due diligence cycle) and they need to be prepared before it arrives. The anticipated-question window creates urgency that general awareness does not. The market is shifting from reactive to anticipatory demand: executives who have pattern awareness enough to know when the question is coming are acting before the external event forces the issue, not after. DigitalCoa.ch's positioning should explicitly address the anticipatory window — the executive who calls before the question arrives — as the primary conversion profile.

**Finding 3: Peer Observation as Conversion Mechanism More Powerful Than Peer Recommendation**
Geneva executives act on what they observe in their peer networks — watching a colleague navigate an AI governance moment — more than on direct peer recommendation. The observation creates conviction that abstract governance concern is concrete, time-bounded, and navigable. The acting executive does not need to have been referred to the advisor by the observing peer; they need to have observed the advisor's presence in the peer network when the relevant event occurred. This has implications for DigitalCoa.ch's positioning: the question is not how to generate peer referrals, but how to be visibly present in the peer circuit when observation-triggered conversions occur.

**Finding 4: Written Summary Portability as Primary Demand Generation Architecture**
The written summary is functioning as the primary demand generation mechanism in Geneva's AI governance advisory market — not through explicit referral but through document circulation. Executives share the summary with board colleagues, peers in other organizations, and professional contacts in adjacent sectors. The document generates demand where it travels without the recipient being told about the service. The portability design standard — quotable, defensible in contexts the advisor does not control, neutral with respect to the reader's position — is what makes this demand generation possible. DigitalCoa.ch should design the written summary as the primary demand generation artifact, with the same rigor applied to its portability as to its analytical content.

**Finding 5: Question Evolution Mapping as Discrete Advisory Surface Unnamed in the Market**
Executives who have been exploring AI governance questions in conversation with peers are discovering that the question itself evolves — the question they thought they were asking is not the question they need to answer when the moment arrives. The question evolution phenomenon creates a specific advisory surface: mapping the current shape of the question, tracking how it has evolved from its initial form, and identifying what the executive needs to answer now versus what they thought they needed to answer six months ago. This question evolution mapping is unnamed in the Geneva market. It is distinct from general AI governance advisory and creates a specific entry point for executives who have been circling a question without resolving it.

**Finding 6: "Before It Arrives" Timing Preference as Primary Positioning Category**
The most convertible Geneva executive is not the one who has a present-tense problem — it is the one who knows the question is coming and wants to resolve it before the external event forces the issue. This "before it arrives" timing preference is the primary positioning category for Geneva advisory demand. The executive who acts in anticipation is easier to reach, more motivated to engage, and less likely to be in a position where the documentation has already become the evidence of a gap. DigitalCoa.ch's positioning should make anticipatory action the normal entry point — not by suggesting the executive is behind, but by communicating that the service is designed for the executive who recognizes the window and acts within it.
