## 01 — ICP Language: How Senior Executives Describe Their Need for AI Governance Advice

### EN — English

**Trigger moments:**
Senior executives in Geneva arrive at the AI governance question through a small number of recognizable moments. Board-level risk reviews where AI systems appear in the risk register for the first time. Regulatory teams flagging that something has changed — the EU AI Act entering force, FINMA issuing guidance, or a peer firm being named in an enforcement action. A senior leader reads about an AI failure in their sector and wonders whether their own systems are in scope. Or a board colleague asks an uncomfortable question in a meeting and no one around the table has a prepared answer.

What they do not do is search for "AI governance." They search for things like "board AI oversight responsibilities," "what does FINMA expect from us on AI," "who can advise us on AI risk at board level," or "AI compliance for financial institutions Switzerland."

**What they expect:**
They expect a conversation with someone who has operated at their level — not a technology briefing from a vendor, not a legal update from a law firm, not a generic framework presentation from a consultancy. They expect someone who understands how decisions are actually made in Swiss financial institutions, how regulatory accountability works in practice, and what board-level AI governance looks like in a credible organization.

They expect to learn something in the first meeting — a clarification, a frame, an answer to a specific question they have been carrying. They are not looking for a proposal; they are looking for a signal that this person understands the problem.

**What they distrust:**
Generic AI governance frameworks presented as universal solutions. Anyone who leads with a tool, a platform, or a certification label rather than an understanding of their specific situation. Vendors who arrive with ROI figures that cannot be explained. Consultancies whose AI governance expertise appeared six months ago. Anyone who uses the phrase "AI strategy" without a clear definition of what decisions it encompasses. Any advisor who has not thought through the multi-jurisdictional picture — Swiss law, EU AI Act, Council of Europe Convention — and how it applies to their specific operating environment.

**How they describe the decision to seek help:**
It is rarely framed as "we need AI governance." It is framed as: "we need to be able to answer board questions on this," "we need to understand what our obligations actually are," "we need someone who has done this before to tell us what good looks like," "our legal team is not the right person to lead this conversation," or "we need to make a decision on this and we don't have the internal evidence to make it well."

The executive who reaches out has usually already decided they need external advice — they are trying to find the right person, not convince themselves to seek help.

## 02 — Market Signals: Geneva Executive AI Governance Advisory Market — April 2026

**Demand emergence points:**

The Geneva executive advisory market for AI governance is moving from latent to active, but unevenly. The most active demand signals are coming from three directions:

Board-level AI oversight is the primary demand driver. Geneva executives with board mandates are feeling the pressure of their fiduciary responsibility for AI risk and have no established internal resource to address it. This is distinct from operational AI compliance demand — it is about what the board itself needs to know, how AI risk should be presented to the board, and what the board's own AI governance responsibilities are. This demand is not yet well served by the market — most AI governance offerings target compliance teams or legal departments, not board-level decision-makers.

FINMA's evolving guidance on AI model risk has created a second demand cluster. Geneva financial institutions that have deployed AI in credit, risk, or customer-facing processes are now being asked by their boards and risk committees to demonstrate that AI model governance meets supervisory expectations. This is creating demand for advisory services that can bridge technical AI model governance and executive accountability — a gap most internal teams cannot fill without external support.

EU AI Act operationalization is creating a third demand cluster, particularly for Geneva firms with EU market exposure or EU-based subsidiaries. The question is shifting from "what is the EU AI Act" to "what does it mean for our specific operations and how do we sequence our response." This is where the Geneva executive's multi-jurisdictional awareness — Swiss law, EU AI Act, Council of Europe Convention — becomes a practical decision requirement rather than an abstract consideration.

**What is being communicated:**

The Swiss AI governance advisory market is predominantly communicating in three modes that are largely failing to address executive-level demand:

Consultancies and law firms are publishing AI governance frameworks, guides, and legal updates that address compliance teams and legal departments. The language is regulatory and technical. These communications are appropriate for their intended audience but do not reach senior executives who need a simpler, more decision-oriented framing.

Technology vendors and AI platforms are communicating AI governance through the lens of their product capabilities. The language is feature-focused and vendor-centric. This is precisely what sophisticated Geneva executives are trained to distrust — they can recognize a product presentation disguised as advisory content.

The few advisory voices who are communicating at executive level — board-level AI governance, decision-quality framing, independent advisory positioning — are doing so with credibility and attracting attention. But the market is thin at this level. The opportunity for an independent, executive-grade advisory voice is significant precisely because the supply is so limited.

**What is working:**

Executive briefings framed as intelligence sessions — not sales presentations — are converting. The format that is working in Geneva is a focused 60–90 minute executive briefing where a senior executive meets with an independent advisor to discuss their specific situation. No slide decks, no follow-up proposal pressure, no vendor material. Just a substantive conversation that leaves the executive with something useful regardless of whether they engage the advisor further.

Referral-based lead generation is strong. Senior executives in Geneva trust peer referrals above all other channels. An introduction from a Geneva colleague who has worked with the advisor is the single highest-converting lead source. The advisory challenge is building enough referral relationships to sustain pipeline without becoming dependent on a small number of intermediaries.

Independent positioning — explicitly not a vendor, not a tech platform, not a law firm — is resonating. The Geneva executive who has been pitched by AI governance platforms and generic consultancies is actively looking for an alternative. The independent advisor who can credibly claim no vendor affiliation and no implementation conflicts is finding a warm reception.

**What is not working:**

Generic "AI governance advisory" positioning without a clear executive framing. Compliance-focused AI governance services pitched to board-level executives. Any offering where the first interaction is a consultation call designed to qualify the lead for a sales process. Advisory firms that cannot clearly articulate what they do differently from a law firm or a technology vendor.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis: AI Governance Advisory Positioning in Geneva — April 2026

**What is being claimed:**

The Geneva AI governance advisory market breaks into four positioning categories:

**Law firms** (Geneva and Swiss): Claiming legal compliance expertise, regulatory navigation, and risk mitigation. Positioning as trusted legal advisors with AI governance as an extension of existing regulatory advisory relationships. Credibility strengths: bar association affiliation, privilege protections, established client relationships. Credibility weaknesses: fee structures positioning them as premium, technology expertise perceived as secondary to legal expertise, conflict concerns when advising on implementation vs. compliance.

**Management consultancies** (Big Four and Geneva boutique): Claiming broad AI governance framework expertise, implementation capability, and scale. Positioning AI governance as a service line within larger digital transformation or risk management practices. Credibility strengths: established brand, multi-disciplinary teams, project delivery infrastructure. Credibility weaknesses: generic frameworks, high staff turnover, perceived as vendor-adjacent, executive distrust of Big Four AI practices as "follow the technology" rather than independent judgment.

**AI governance platforms and tools vendors**: Claiming structured methodology, compliance automation, and certification support. Positioning AI governance as a product that can be implemented through software. Credibility strengths: structured output, clear deliverables, certification pathways. Credibility weaknesses: vendor-centric framing that sophisticated Geneva executives immediately identify, inability to address board-level strategic questions, technology-company credibility conflicts for independent advisory positioning.

**Independent advisors and boutique advisory firms**: Claiming independence, executive-level relationship quality, and Swiss institutional depth. Positioning AI governance as a decision-quality and governance question rather than a compliance exercise. Credibility strengths: no vendor conflicts, no implementation dependencies, executive-level trust. Credibility weaknesses: limited scale, no brand recognition beyond client referrals, difficult to demonstrate track record without client references.

**What is credible:**

For the Geneva senior executive ICP — board members, C-suite, independent professionals — the most credible positioning elements are:

Independence from technology vendors and implementation firms. Any perception of vendor affiliation or technology product interest destroys credibility with this ICP immediately.

Swiss institutional depth — the ability to discuss FINMA expectations, Swiss corporate law board responsibilities, and Swiss-specific regulatory landscape with precision.

Executive-level communication — not technical jargon, not compliance language, not frameworks for compliance teams. The executive wants the governance question framed at the level of decision quality, fiduciary duty, and organizational accountability.

Track record specificity — not testimonials, not certifications, not logos. Evidence that the advisor has worked with organizations like theirs, has answered questions like theirs, has helped boards and executives navigate decisions like theirs.

**What is missing:**

The Geneva market has a genuine gap at the intersection of three things that Sergio's DigitalCoa.ch is positioned to fill:

Board-level AI governance advisory that addresses the board's own governance responsibilities — not what the compliance team needs to do, but what the board needs to understand, decide, and oversee. This is distinct from legal compliance advisory and from technical AI governance implementation.

Independent advisory that explicitly positions against vendor conflicts — a clear, credible statement that the advisor has no technology product interest, no implementation revenue, and no platform dependency. This positioning is almost entirely absent from the Geneva market.

Multi-jurisdictional framing for Geneva executives — the ability to address Swiss law, EU AI Act obligations, and Council of Europe Convention implications as a coherent picture rather than three separate legal questions. Geneva executives operating in multi-jurisdictional environments need an advisor who can give them a unified view.

Calm, precise communication — no hype language, no exclamation marks, no "cutting-edge" or "revolutionary." The executive wants to see that the advisor communicates with the same precision and restraint that the executive values in their own professional environment.

Respect for the client's time — evidence that the advisory relationship is structured around the executive's calendar and decision timeline, not the advisor's billing cycle.
