The concern that surfaces most consistently is that the AI system has inserted itself into the advisory relationship in a way that creates exposure for the firm, without the firm having a clear record of what was AI-generated, what was human-reviewed, and what the client acted on.

## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

### Problem 1: The Advisory Relationship Liability Paradox

The most structurally underappreciated problem at boutique Geneva wealth management firms is the advisory relationship liability paradox: AI tools are deployed to make the firm more efficient and more responsive to clients, but the net effect of AI deployment — when measured in terms of what the firm can stand behind in a client conversation — may be negative. The AI produces outputs faster, the relationship manager reviews faster, the client receives faster recommendations. The efficiency gain is real. The liability amplification is also real, and largely unexamined. The firm has more client-facing AI output in circulation than at any prior point, with documentation that does not match the volume or the speed.

### Problem 2: The Private Supervisory Standard Gap

Boutique Geneva wealth management firms are preparing for what FINMA has published — public guidance, consultation documents, tier classification frameworks. What they are not prepared for is the private supervisory standard: the specific informal questions that FINMA examiners are asking during examinations, which are more advanced than the published framework, and which do not appear in any publicly available document. The managing director who prepares only for what has been published is preparing for a supervisory conversation that will not happen.

### Problem 3: The Documentation Theater Paradox

Boutique firms that have begun documenting AI governance have encountered a specific paradox: the documentation they produce does not reduce their actual exposure, but it creates the appearance of control that reduces the urgency of addressing real problems. The compliance team produces AI governance records, the managing director reviews them, the file looks complete. The underlying governance failures — which tools are in use, whether the human review is substantive, whether model updates are tracked — remain unaddressed. The documentation is real. The protection it provides is partial.

### Problem 4: The Managing Director Personal Liability Cloud

No managing director at a boutique Geneva wealth management firm has received clear advice on what their personal liability exposure is under Swiss law for AI governance failures at the firm. The question of whether FINMA supervisory findings against the firm create personal liability for the managing director is unresolved. The question of whether D&O insurance responds to AI governance failures — as opposed to operational failures or regulatory breaches — is unanswered by the insurer. The managing director inherits AI governance accountability without a clear map of what that accountability means personally.

### Problem 5: AI Performance Measurement Absent Industry Benchmarking

Boutique wealth management firms have no framework for evaluating whether AI is improving client outcomes. They measure AI by operational metrics: faster reports, more frequent client contact, more consistent formatting. The question of whether AI-assisted portfolio recommendations produce better or worse client outcomes compared to non-AI-assisted recommendations is not being asked — and cannot be asked without methodology that does not yet exist at boutique scale. The implication: firms are deploying AI into client relationships without any evidence that it is beneficial, and without any mechanism to detect if it is harmful.

### Problem 6: The Regulatory Timing Asymmetry

FINMA regulatory guidance and supervisory expectations are published after the problems they address have already evolved. The AI model risk governance framework scheduled for Q4 2026 will address AI systems that have already been in production for years. By the time a boutique firm reads the published guidance and maps it to their current AI tools, the guidance is already describing what has happened, not what is happening. This creates a specific planning paradox: acting on current guidance means acting on a retrospective framework. Waiting for updated guidance means falling further behind.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations

### FINMA — AI Model Risk Governance and Supervisory Examination Reality

FINMA's published AI model risk framework (expected Q4 2026) will establish tiered obligations: Tier 1 for AI in credit and lending decisions, Tier 2 for client-facing AI-assisted compliance and reporting tools, Tier 3 for administrative AI. Boutique Geneva firms using AI for portfolio analysis and client reporting fall primarily into Tier 2. The published obligations will include: AI model inventory, annual re-validation, continuous drift monitoring, 72-hour AI incident reporting, and human oversight documentation.

The critical gap for boutique firms is not the published framework — it is the private supervisory standard. FINMA examiners are asking questions in current examinations that are more specific than anything in the published guidance. The questions concern: how the firm detected the most recent AI model update from each vendor, what changed in the firm's outputs as a result, whether the managing director was informed of the change, and what human review occurred before and after. None of these questions appear as explicit requirements in the published framework. All of them are being asked.

For the boutique managing director: the practical obligation is not just to follow published guidance. It is to anticipate the informal questions that follow from a supervised AI system in production — and to have documentation that answers them.

### ASIP — Pension Fund AI Asset Allocation Accountability

ASIP guidance on AI decision-support systems in pension fund asset allocation creates direct obligations for Geneva wealth management firms managing occupational pension assets. The obligations include: documentation of AI systems used in pension fund portfolio management, duty to explain AI-generated allocation recommendations to pension fund trustees in language they can understand, audit trail requirements for AI allocation decisions, and accountability chain documentation from AI output to human decision.

The specific boutique-scale problem: pension fund trustees are increasingly asking AI governance questions during mandate reviews — questions about which AI tools are used, how they are supervised, and what documentation exists. The managing director must be prepared to answer these questions in a way that pension fund trustees (who are not technology specialists) can evaluate as reasonable. Standard AI governance documentation does not serve this audience.

### SFAMA — Fund Management AI Disclosure Obligations

SFAMA standards apply to Geneva firms with fund management authority using AI. Key obligations include: investor disclosure when AI generates investment decisions, model risk management documentation for AI-driven portfolio construction, validation records for valuation AI tools, and human oversight documentation equivalent to standards applied to human portfolio managers. The ASIP framework obligations and SFAMA obligations overlap in the asset allocation context, creating a combined documentation burden for boutique firms managing both private client and pension fund mandates.

### ISSB — Sustainability Disclosure and the AI Data Lineage Problem

ISSB S1 and S2 disclosure standards intersect with AI governance at the sustainability data layer. AI tools used to collect, verify, or generate sustainability data for client portfolios must document methodology and data lineage — specifically, what data sources the AI used, what processing occurred, and what sustainability claims the output supports. ISSB-aligned disclosure requirements become material from mid-2027, creating a forward compliance obligation for boutique firms already using AI-driven ESG analytics or sustainability screening tools.

The AI data lineage problem here is specific: AI systems generating ESG scores or sustainability ratings typically use multiple data sources, weighting methodologies, and third-party data providers. The boutique firm relying on an AI tool for sustainability data cannot independently verify the lineage of the sustainability claims being made — and cannot document what the AI tool's methodology actually does with client portfolio data.

### nFADP — Client Data Processing in AI Systems and the Third-Party Data Flow Problem

The Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection applies to all AI systems processing client financial data. Critical obligations include: documented legal basis for AI processing of client data, client notification when AI generates profiles or recommendations, purpose limitation documentation for AI data processing, cross-border data transfer restrictions where AI tools route data outside Switzerland, and Art. 21 nFADP right to explanation for automated decisions affecting clients.

For boutique firms using third-party AI SaaS tools: the cross-border data flow documentation is an active compliance surface. Client data processing agreements must accurately reflect where data actually flows — not where the firm believes it flows. AI vendor data processing addenda frequently contain provisions that the firm's standard agreements do not cover: training data usage clauses, model improvement provisions, third-party sub-processor lists that change without notification. The documentation gap between the firm's client agreements and the actual AI vendor infrastructure is a present-tense nFADP exposure.

## 06 — Findings Summary: 6 Net-New Insights (Distinct from C214–C225 Phase 02)

### Finding 1: The Advisory Relationship Liability Amplification Paradox

Boutique Geneva wealth management firms are deploying AI to improve client relationship efficiency — faster reports, more frequent contact, more consistent formatting. But the net effect of AI deployment, measured in terms of what the firm can stand behind in a client conversation, may be negative. AI produces outputs faster, human review compresses, clients receive faster recommendations they act on. The efficiency gain is real. The liability amplification — more AI-assisted output in circulation, with documentation that does not match the volume or the speed — is also real and structurally underappreciated. Prior research identified multi-system attribution failure (C225) as an AI tool constellation problem. This finding identifies the specific mechanism: liability amplification through efficiency compression, where speed gains in AI output production are not matched by speed gains in human review documentation, creating a net liability increase that is not visible in any compliance record. **Advisory relationship liability management as the primary relationship-level problem — not governance infrastructure, but the client-facing risk exposure that governance infrastructure is supposed to address.**
