The documentation shows a recommendation and an approval. It doesn't show the human override of an AI limitation."

## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: AI investment thesis derivation as the undocumented decision layer**
Boutique wealth management firms maintain documented investment theses for each client. When AI contributes to thesis evolution — suggesting changes, challenging assumptions, introducing factors the human advisor had not weighted as heavily — the documentation captures the current thesis, not the AI's contribution to it. The record does not show whether the human judgment preceded and endorsed the AI input, or whether the AI input preceded and shaped the human judgment. This matters for professional accountability: the question "what was the human investment decision and what was the AI's?" cannot be answered from a documentation record designed to show the outcome, not the derivation. This is distinct from the conformance documentation gap (C239): that concerned whether the manager followed, partially followed, or overrode the AI recommendation; this concerns how the investment thesis itself was formed when AI contributed to it — a different decision layer, not yet named as a distinct documentation category.

**Signal 2: AI-generated client communications as professional endorsement without endorsement protocol**
Boutique wealth management firms are signing and sending AI-generated client communications — performance summaries, attribution notes, risk updates. The signature is the firm's. The content was produced by the AI tool. The compliance officer reviews the communication before it is sent, but the standard of that review is not documented, the scope of the endorsement is not defined, and the professional liability the firm is assuming by sending the communication is not distinguished from the AI vendor's technical capability. The question "what does the firm's signature on an AI-generated communication actually endorse?" has no documented answer. This is distinct from the client-requested specific rationale problem (C239): that concerned post-hoc explanation of AI recommendations to clients; this concerns prospective endorsement of AI-produced content before it reaches the client, which is a different accountability surface.

**Signal 3: AI scope limitation unawareness as misrepresented advisory scope**
AI portfolio tools operate on the data they receive. When a client has material financial positions or circumstances that the AI cannot capture — a family business stake, a pension entitlement with complex conversion terms, real estate with held-period complexities — the AI processes an incomplete picture and produces recommendations based on it. The human advisors who know the full picture may override the AI's recommendation manually. The documentation records the recommendation and the approval. It does not record that the AI's scope was materially limited for this client, and that the human override corrected an AI limitation rather than a professional judgment. If the client later asks why the recommendation didn't account for a factor the AI should have known about, the record cannot explain that the AI was scope-limited. This is a distinct failure mode not yet named in Phase 02: the AI scope limitation that the firm manages manually but cannot document creates misrepresentation exposure.

**Signal 4: AI-advisor causal attribution ambiguity as reconstructability failure**
When a portfolio manager and the AI arrive at the same conclusion independently, the documentation cannot show this. When the AI's conclusion arrives first and shapes the manager's subsequent view, the documentation also cannot show this. The record is identical: recommendation, review, approval, implementation. The causal sequence — which produced confidence in which, whether the human review was substantively independent or confirmation of an AI conclusion — is not documentable with the current architecture. This creates a specific reconstructability failure that operates in both directions: it conceals both AI-shaped human judgments and genuine human-AI concurrence. The question "was this a human decision that the AI confirmed, or an AI decision that the human endorsed?" cannot be answered from the record.

**Signal 5: Risk limit calibration drift around AI recommendations**
Boutique firms maintain risk frameworks with defined limits: position sizes, sector concentrations, correlation thresholds. When an AI recommendation approaches or slightly exceeds a limit, the compliance review may approve something marginally within the limit. Over successive periods, the question becomes whether the limit is functioning as a constraint or whether it is being recalibrated incrementally around AI recommendations that are nearly but not quite compliant. The documentation shows approvals within the limit. It does not show cumulative calibration drift — whether the limit held because it was correct, or because it was adjusted to accommodate the recommendation. This is distinct from the AI recommendation conformance gap (C239 Finding 4): that concerned the manager's conformance decisions relative to AI recommendations; this concerns whether the risk framework itself is being recalibrated around AI behavior, a separate and less visible governance surface.

**Signal 6: Capital treatment sequencing relative to AI recommendations**
For Geneva boutiques subject to FINMA capital requirements, the sequencing of investment decisions and capital calculations creates an undocumented exposure. If an AI recommendation shaped a position, and the capital treatment of that position was determined after the AI's input was already embedded in the decision, the capital calculation was not independent of the AI's influence. The documentation shows that the position was taken and the capital was calculated. It does not show whether the capital treatment was determined before or after the AI contributed to the recommendation — whether the capital constraint was a genuine input to the decision or a post-hoc calculation applied to an AI-shaped outcome. This is distinct from the AI model correlation systemic risk (C239): that concerned correlated signals across peer firms; this concerns the internal sequencing of capital treatment and AI recommendation, which is a single-firm internal governance question.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: AI investment thesis derivation documentation architecture**
A documentation architecture for capturing how investment theses evolve when AI contributes to them — distinguishing AI input from human judgment, showing causal sequences, and demonstrating that the human thesis owner understood and reviewed the AI's contribution — does not exist at boutique scale. This is distinct from recommendation conformance documentation (C239 Finding 4): that concerned what the manager did relative to specific AI recommendations; this concerns the more foundational question of how the investment thesis — the document that underpins suitability, client understanding, and regulatory compliance — was formed when AI contributed to it. The investment thesis derivation record is a discrete documentation category with distinct accountability implications that has not been addressed in any Phase 02 prior.

**Gap 2: AI-generated client communication endorsement protocol**
A protocol defining what the firm's professional endorsement covers when signing an AI-generated client communication — what standard of review the firm must apply before sending, what the endorsement actually warrants, and how the firm's liability for the communication differs from the AI vendor's technical capability — does not exist. The endorsement protocol is distinct from the AI explanation relay protocol (C239 Gap 3): that concerned translating existing AI outputs for client comprehension; this concerns establishing what the firm is professionally warranting by sending the communication at all, before any specific explanation is given. No current boutique-scale advisory product addresses this gap.

**Gap 3: AI scope limitation documentation for client-specific contexts**
A methodology for documenting AI scope limitations that are client-specific — situations where the AI cannot process known material factors for a specific client, the firm manages around the limitation manually, and the documentation must be sufficient to explain why the AI's recommendation appeared complete when it was materially scoped — does not exist. This is distinct from general AI confidence interval disclosure (C238): that concerned the AI's uncertainty about its own outputs; this concerns the firm's obligation to disclose that the AI's information base was incomplete for this specific client, a different disclosure question with different professional liability implications.

**Gap 4: Human-AI causal attribution documentation methodology**
A documentation methodology that distinguishes genuine human-AI concurrence from AI-shaped human judgment — allowing reconstruction of whether a human advisor reached a conclusion independently before reviewing the AI's output, or whether the AI's output preceded and shaped the human's conclusion — does not exist. This is distinct from override documentation (C238): that concerned what happened when the manager disagreed with the AI; this concerns the more fundamental question of how to demonstrate that human review was substantively independent, not confirmation of an AI conclusion the human had already seen. The methodology is not about sequencing reviews artificially — it is about designing documentation that makes causal attribution reconstructable without requiring artificial process constraints.

**Gap 5: Risk framework AI calibration drift monitoring**
A monitoring methodology for detecting whether risk limits are being incrementally recalibrated around AI recommendation patterns — tracking whether approvals near-but-within limits represent genuine limit adequacy or cumulative calibration drift — does not exist at boutique scale. This is distinct from risk limit override documentation (C232): that concerned documented deviations from defined limits; this concerns the more subtle question of whether the limits themselves are functioning as constraints or being adjusted around AI behavior in ways that are individually defensible but cumulatively constitute a framework change. The documentation shows individual approvals. It does not show limit evolution.

**Gap 6: AI-recommendation capital sequencing documentation**
A documentation practice for establishing that capital treatment was determined independently of — or at least with documented awareness of — AI contributions to investment decisions does not exist. The question is not whether capital was calculated correctly after the decision; it is whether the sequencing of capital consideration relative to AI recommendation was documented, such that the independence of the capital constraint as a decision input can be demonstrated. This is a discrete boutique-scale gap at the intersection of AI governance and FINMA capital adequacy documentation, not addressed in any prior Phase 02 cycle.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations

**FINMA — Professional Endorsement Standard for AI-Generated Client Communications**
FINMA supervisory expectations for client communications require that the firm stands behind the content it sends. When the content is AI-generated, the professional endorsement standard that applies to the firm's own authored communications is not automatically satisfied — the endorsement must be based on a review that is proportionate to what the communication warrants. For AI-generated performance summaries, attribution notes, and risk updates, the question is what review the firm must conduct before the signature attaches. The supervisory expectation is not published as a methodology — it is inferred from examination findings and questionnaire responses. The gap between "we review before sending" and "our review meets the professional endorsement standard" is where the exposure concentrates. At boutique scale, the review process is typically informal and undocumented, which means the endorsement is also undocumented.

**FINMA — Circular 2016/1 Investment Thesis Documentation and AI Contribution**
FINMA Circular 2016/1 suitability documentation requires that the investment thesis — the documented rationale for the client's portfolio strategy — reflects the firm's professional judgment. When AI contributes to thesis evolution, the Circular requires that the human judgment remains the operative foundation. The documentation must show not just that a thesis exists, but that the human advisor understood the AI's contribution and incorporated it through professional judgment, not passive acceptance. The Circular does not specify a methodology for making this demonstration. At boutique scale, the documentation architecture was not designed for this requirement, and the gap between "thesis documented" and "AI contribution adequately documented" is present-tense and unaddressed.

**FINMA — Risk Framework Documentation and AI Influence on Limit Calibration**
FINMA risk framework expectations for wealth management firms require that risk limits represent genuine constraints on investment decisions. When AI recommendations influence whether limits are calibrated at particular levels, the documentation must be sufficient to show that the limit represents an independent risk judgment, not an accommodation of AI recommendation patterns. The supervisory question — is this limit a genuine constraint, or has it been calibrated around AI behavior? — requires documentation that most boutiques are not producing. The cumulative documentation pattern matters: individual approvals may be defensible while cumulative drift is not. Neither the methodology for detecting this drift nor the documentation architecture for demonstrating limit independence exists at boutique scale.

**ASIP — Pension Fund Trustee AI Scope Limitation Disclosure Under BVG**
Pension fund trustees who use AI-assisted analysis for investment decisions affecting beneficiary benefits face disclosure obligations under BVG Art. 51 regarding decision-making process. When an AI system operates with a known scope limitation — the tool cannot process certain pension-specific factors for this portfolio — and the trustee manages around the limitation manually, the question is whether the disclosure obligation requires documenting that the AI's recommendation was scope-limited for this specific client. The ASIP guidance does not provide a specific methodology for AI scope limitation documentation at the trustee level. The gap is present-tense: trustees are making AI-scoped decisions and managing them manually without a documentation practice that would demonstrate this to a beneficiary review.

**SFAMA — AI Portfolio Construction Professional Endorsement and Evidential Basis**
SFAMA fund management standards require professional endorsement of AI-assisted portfolio construction decisions. The endorsement must be based on an evidential record — what the AI recommended, what the fund manager reviewed, what the basis for the endorsement was. The evidential basis standard is where boutique documentation fails: endorsement records typically state that the endorsement occurred, not what evidence the endorsement was based on. For AI-assisted decisions, the evidence requirement extends to the AI's information base, its scope of analysis for this specific client, and the manager's basis for concluding that the AI's recommendation was appropriate within that scope. The SFAMA guidance describes the obligation. It does not describe the documentation architecture required to satisfy it.

**ISSB — AI Lineage in Sustainability Metrics and Scope Limitation Documentation**
The integration of ISSB standards into Swiss regulatory frameworks for listed entities creates derivative obligations for wealth management firms advising ISSB-obligated clients on sustainability-linked portfolios. When AI contributes to sustainability metrics in client portfolios — carbon intensity, transition risk exposure, alignment scores — the AI's lineage documentation must show not just that the metric was produced, but what the AI's information base was and whether it was materially complete. If the AI's scope was limited — relevant data was not available to the tool — and the human advisor corrected for this manually, the ISSB-aligned disclosure still requires documentation of the scope limitation. This is distinct from general AI explanation obligations: it concerns what the sustainability disclosure warrants about the AI's analytical completeness, which is a specific ISSB-aligned disclosure question.

**nFADP — Art. 21 Automated Decision Explanation and AI Scope Limitation Disclosure**
The revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection Art. 21 gives data subjects the right to meaningful information about automated decision-making logic when it produces significant effects. For boutique wealth management clients, the AI's recommendation is an automated decision with direct financial consequences. The right to explanation extends not just to the recommendation itself, but to what the AI's analytical scope was — whether it had access to material information, whether its scope was limited for this client, and whether the recommendation was adjusted manually to correct for AI scope limitations. The firm's obligation is not satisfied by disclosing that AI was used. It requires disclosing whether the AI's recommendation was based on a complete analytical picture for this specific client. This has a specific boutique-scale implementation challenge: documenting the AI's scope limitation requires knowing what the AI did not process, which requires human knowledge of what the AI cannot see.

**Emerging: Federal Council AI Ordinance Scope Documentation Expectations**
The Federal Council AI ordinance pipeline suggests documentation expectations that include AI system limitations, scope of analysis, and human oversight of AI outputs. The direction points toward documentation requirements that go beyond tool inventory and governance policy — toward documentation of what the AI actually analyzed, for which clients, and with what known limitations. For boutique wealth management firms, this creates a preparation requirement that is distinct from current documentation practice: the ordinance direction implies that firms must be able to demonstrate, for each significant AI recommendation, what the AI's scope was and whether the human review covered that scope. The window before the ordinance arrives is the window to build documentation architecture designed for this standard, not retroactive remediation of current records.
