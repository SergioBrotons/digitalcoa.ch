## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Cumulative AI environment exceeding intentional design**
The boutique firm's AI environment arrived incrementally over years. Its current features include things that would not have been designed if the firm had designed it intentionally. This cumulative environment exceeds the firm's mental model of itself. The first step is acknowledging the gap.

**Signal 2: Carrier questionnaire exposing documentation versus practice divergence**
The carrier's AI questionnaire asks the firm to attest to facts about its AI governance. The firm fills it in based on policy. The policy does not reflect practice. The divergence is known to the person filling in the questionnaire and is not disclosed to the carrier.

**Signal 3: Attribution reconstruction as the primary operational gap**
When a recommendation is challenged, the portfolio manager must be able to reconstruct what role the AI played. This is not currently possible. The attribution reconstruction gap is the primary operational liability in AI-assisted wealth management.

**Signal 4: Personal liability for managing directors remaining unmapped**
The managing director's personal liability exposure for AI-assisted decisions is a question that no advisor will answer definitively. The exposure exists and is material. The lack of a definitive answer does not eliminate the exposure.

**Signal 5: AI environment mapping as prerequisite to any governance action**
Before any other governance action, the firm must know what its AI environment actually is. The mapping is a prerequisite to everything else. The firm cannot govern what it has not mapped.

**Signal 6: Documentation debt as retrospective and prospective problem**
The firm has accumulated documentation debt — AI governance documentation that was never created. This debt has two dimensions: retrospective (exposure from past AI use that was undocumented) and prospective (ongoing AI use that continues to accumulate without documentation).

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: AI environment mapping for cumulative multi-vendor wealth management environments**
A service that maps the actual cumulative AI environment at boutique wealth management scale — including tools adopted incrementally, tools that have been updated or replaced, and data flows between tools — does not exist as a discrete service.

**Gap 2: Attribution reconstruction protocol for AI-assisted recommendations**
A protocol the portfolio manager can use to reconstruct the role of AI in a specific past recommendation. What documentation is needed, in what format, to reconstruct the attribution chain. The protocol designed for boutique scale.

**Gap 3: Documentation debt assessment and remediation architecture**
A methodology for assessing accumulated documentation debt and determining which gaps can be closed retrospectively and which require prospective architecture. The retrospective/prospective distinction is critical.

**Gap 4: Carrier questionnaire response preparation service**
Preparing the firm to complete the carrier's AI questionnaire in a way that reflects actual practice — not a policy document, but a practice-accurate response with supporting documentation. Bridging the policy/practice divergence before the carrier asks.

**Gap 5: Personal AI liability mapping for Geneva wealth management directors**
A precise mapping of what the managing director is personally exposed for under Swiss law and FINMA obligations when AI-assisted recommendations are made. The exposure mapped specifically, not principles-based generalities.

**Gap 6: Governance parity benchmarking for boutique wealth management**
The firm cannot determine how its AI governance compares to peer firms. No benchmarking mechanism exists at boutique scale. The firm operates without a reference point for adequacy.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA obligations:**
FINMA has published its expectations for AI use in supervised institutions. The expectations apply to the firm's actual practice, not its policy documentation. The firm whose policy documentation exceeds its actual practice is not in compliance — it is in a more dangerous position than the firm whose documentation is accurate but incomplete.

**ASIP obligations for pension fund trustees:**
Pension fund trustees using AI for investment decisions face specific obligations under ASIP guidelines. The trustees who adopted AI incrementally are now operating in a space where their obligations exceed their documentation.

**SFAMA documentation requirements:**
The Swiss Fund and Asset Management Association documentation requirements create specific obligations for AI-assisted portfolio management. The documentation requirements are multiple and cumulative.

**ISSB sustainability data and AI:**
ISSB requirements for sustainability data lineage intersect with AI tool use when AI is used to generate or process sustainability data. The intersection creates documentation obligations that are additional to the core AI governance obligations.

**nFADP third-party AI SaaS data flows:**
The firm's AI tools are predominantly third-party SaaS. Each tool creates data flows that the firm must be able to document under nFADP. The documentation of these data flows is a prerequisite to demonstrating lawful basis for processing.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Cumulative AI environment exceeding intentional design**
The firm's AI environment arrived incrementally over years and has features that would not have been designed intentionally. This cumulative environment exceeds the firm's mental model of itself. The first governance action is updating the mental model to match the actual environment.

**Finding 2: Carrier questionnaire policy/practice divergence as active liability**
The firm completes the carrier's AI questionnaire based on policy. The policy does not reflect Tuesday's practice. The person completing the questionnaire knows this and does not disclose it. The divergence is an active liability that could affect insurance coverage validity.

**Finding 3: Attribution reconstruction as primary operational gap**
When a recommendation is challenged, the portfolio manager must be able to reconstruct what role AI played. This is not currently possible. The attribution reconstruction gap is the primary operational liability in AI-assisted wealth management and no solution exists at boutique scale.

**Finding 4: Documentation debt with retrospective and prospective dimensions**
The accumulated documentation debt has two distinct dimensions: retrospective (past AI use that was never documented, creating permanent exposure) and prospective (ongoing AI use that continues to accumulate without documentation). The remediation logic for each dimension is different.

**Finding 5: Personal liability for managing directors unmapped and material**
The managing director's personal liability for AI-assisted decisions is a question no advisor will answer definitively. The exposure is material and structural. The firm is operating without a precise map of this exposure.

**Finding 6: Governance parity benchmarking absent at boutique scale**
The firm cannot determine whether its AI governance is adequate relative to peer firms. No benchmarking mechanism exists at boutique scale. The adequacy question cannot be answered without a reference point.
