## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: FINMA AI audit question progression without adequacy standard publication**
FINMA audits are asking more specific questions about AI governance. The questions do not come with a published adequacy standard. Firms are being examined on an undefined standard through the questions themselves.

**Signal 2: AI-generated report authorship ambiguity as client relationship risk**
AI-generated reports that go to clients have ambiguous authorship. The intelligence in the report is not fully the firm's. When clients ask about the reports, the ambiguity becomes a relationship risk.

**Signal 3: Model versioning opacity at boutique scale**
Model version tracking is not systematic at boutique scale. Portfolio managers cannot state which model version generated a specific flag or recommendation. The versioning opacity creates accountability gaps that are invisible until a specific question surfaces them.

**Signal 4: Vendor dashboard access as undocumented client data exposure**
Vendor maintenance access to client data dashboards is not audited systematically. The access arrangement creates exposure through the vendor's access pattern, not through the vendor's intent.

**Signal 5: Unexplained AI flag disappearance as risk management failure**
When AI flags are not followed up, they disappear from the dashboard. The asymmetry between what the AI recommended and what the firm acted on is untracked. The risk management function operates without a complete record.

**Signal 6: AI recommendation confidence language as liability-creating communication**
AI-generated explanations for recommendations sound confident and lack the firm's review credential. Client-facing explanations that are AI-generated without the firm's review credential create liability through communication, not through the recommendation itself.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: FINMA AI governance adequacy definition for boutique firms**
No market participant has defined what adequate AI governance looks like for boutique wealth management firms (50–200 employees). The gap exists because the standard is forming through audit questions, not through published doctrine.

**Gap 2: AI-generated report authorship documentation framework**
No framework exists for documenting the authorship of AI-generated client reports. The framework would need to address: what the firm's review credential is, what the AI generated versus what the firm approved, and what the client should understand about the report's provenance.

**Gap 3: Model version tracking protocol for boutique compliance**
No protocol exists for model version tracking at boutique scale. The protocol would need to be operationally practical (not enterprise-grade) and compliance-relevant (connected to the firm's audit trail obligations).

**Gap 4: Vendor access audit methodology for client dashboard environments**
No methodology exists for auditing vendor access to client data in dashboard environments. The methodology would need to address: what access exists, what access has been exercised, and what the firm's recourse is if the vendor's access creates an incident.

**Gap 5: Unexplained flag protocol for risk management documentation**
No protocol exists for documenting AI flags that were not followed up. The protocol would need to create a durable record of the AI's recommendation and the firm's decision not to act, without creating operational overhead that makes the protocol itself impractical.

**Gap 6: Client-facing AI explanation standard for boutique wealth management**
No standard exists for client-facing AI explanation in boutique wealth management. The standard would need to address: how the firm credentials AI-generated explanations, what clients should understand about AI involvement, and how the firm's review is documented.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA AI governance examination progression:**
FINMA is progressing through AI governance examination without publishing adequacy standards. The examination is forming the standard through questions, not through doctrine. Boutique firms are being evaluated against an undefined standard through the specificity of the questions asked.

**SFAMA asset management AI guidance gaps:**
SFAMA guidance on AI in asset management addresses large-scale operations more directly than boutique operations. The guidance leaves boutique-scale AI governance without a professional body reference standard.

**ASIP fiduciary obligation and AI recommendation accountability:**
Fiduciary obligations require that recommendations be attributable to the firm's judgment, not just to an AI system's output. The accountability for AI-generated recommendations that the firm presents as its own is a specific fiduciary exposure that ASIP has not addressed at boutique scale.

**nFADP Articles 6 and 8 behavioral profiling consent for portfolio analytics:**
Portfolio analytics that use AI to infer client behavioral patterns require specific consent architecture under Articles 6 and 8. The consent architecture is not generically satisfied by a privacy policy; it requires client-specific profiling consent for the specific analytical use.

**BVG (occupational pension) AI suitability assessment for retirement planning:**
BVG-mandated suitability assessments using AI for retirement planning have additional accountability requirements. The assessment must be attributable to the firm's judgment and must be documentable in a form that the BVG authority can review.

**FINMA Q1 2027 AI audit focus areas:**
FINMA Q1 2027 audit focus includes AI tool inventory, model version documentation, use-case differentiation, FADP audit trail substance, and reassessment protocols. The five categories are the carrier's probe points; no published adequacy standard exists for any of them.

**ISSB AI-related disclosure standards for sustainability reporting:**
ISSB standards for AI-related disclosures are arriving. The intersection of AI governance and sustainability disclosure is a new surface area that boutique wealth management firms serving sustainability-focused clients are beginning to encounter.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: FINMA audit question progression as de facto standard formation mechanism**
FINMA is forming the AI governance adequacy standard through the progression of audit questions, not through published doctrine. The mechanism means that the standard is knowable through the questions, but only retrospectively — the firm learns what the standard requires by being asked questions that reveal it.

**Finding 2: AI-generated report authorship ambiguity as boutique firm liability exposure**
AI-generated client reports have ambiguous authorship that creates liability exposure. The intelligence in the report is not fully attributable to the firm's judgment. When a client asks how a recommendation was made, the ambiguity becomes a present-tense problem, not a theoretical one.

**Finding 3: Model versioning opacity as specific accountability gap at boutique scale**
Model version tracking is absent at boutique scale in a specific and nameable way. Portfolio managers cannot state which model version generated a flag. The opacity creates accountability gaps that are invisible until a client or regulator asks the specific question that surfaces them.

**Finding 4: Vendor dashboard maintenance access as undocumented client data exposure category**
Vendor maintenance access to client data dashboards is a specific exposure category that is not addressed by privacy policies or by general information security frameworks. The exposure exists through access patterns, not through data breach events.

**Finding 5: Unexplained AI flag disappearance as risk management record failure**
When AI flags are not followed up, their disappearance from the dashboard means the risk management function loses the record of what the AI recommended. The asymmetry between AI recommendation and firm action is untracked, making the firm's risk management record incomplete in a specific and documentable way.

**Finding 6: Client-facing AI explanation confidence language as liability-creating communication independent of recommendation quality**
AI-generated explanations for recommendations are confident and sound credible. They lack the firm's review credential. The communication creates liability independent of whether the recommendation itself was correct. A firm that presents AI-generated explanations without documenting its review creates exposure through the explanation, not through the underlying recommendation.
