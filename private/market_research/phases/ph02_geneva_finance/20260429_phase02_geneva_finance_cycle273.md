Client portfolio data enters AI vendor infrastructure through report generation — not research queries. The data flow is through the delivery chain for client-facing documents. No Geneva market participant has documented this as a discrete data flow category with specific retention and access implications.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA and AI accountability for boutique wealth management:**
FINMA has not published AI-specific supervisory guidance for boutiques in the wealth management segment. The absence of published guidance does not reduce the accountability exposure — it means firms are making operational decisions without a regulatory reference point, and discovering the consequences through carrier examinations and client questions rather than through formal regulatory direction.

**FINMA and non-supervised staff AI use:**
No FINMA guidance addresses the specific accountability architecture for AI use by compliance staff whose outputs are signed by supervising MDs. The structural question — what the MD's signature covers when the underlying documentation was AI-assisted by non-supervised staff — is unaddressed in any published guidance.

**SFAMA and AI circular applicability to boutique advisory:**
The SFAMA AI circular was written for fund management operations. Its applicability to boutique advisory firms using AI for client recommendations is untested and assumed rather than confirmed. Firms operating under that assumption have not had the assumption challenged through examination.

**nFADP and client report data in AI vendor infrastructure:**
Client portfolio data processed through AI vendor infrastructure for report generation activates nFADP obligations. The data minimization principle (Art. 6) and purpose limitation principle apply to how vendor infrastructure processes and retains client data used in report generation. Most boutiques have not evaluated their AI vendor contracts against these specific obligations for this specific data flow category.

**FIDLEG Art. 26 and AI-assisted compliance documentation:**
FIDLEG Art. 26 requires disclosure of algorithmic routine basis and consequences in accessible language. AI-assisted compliance documentation generated by non-supervised staff without MD review may not satisfy this requirement in a form the MD can defend.

**ISSB S2 and AI-compiled Scope 2 data lineage:**
ISSB S2 requires entity-specific asset-level data for Scope 2. AI systems that estimate or assemble this data create a lineage question the standard does not resolve. The disclosure issue is not merely whether the data exists — it is what AI actually did to produce the data, and whether the firm's disclosure accurately represents that process.

**Carrier third-generation questionnaire and de facto audit function:**
Carrier third-generation questionnaires are functioning as de facto AI governance audits for boutiques without internal audit infrastructure. The carrier examination asks questions about operational reality that the firm's documentation was not designed to answer. The audit function is being performed by commercial underwriting instruments, not by regulatory examination.

**Institutional counterparty AI disclosure as de facto obligation:**
Institutional clients requesting AI model disclosure and protocol information are creating de facto obligations through commercial relationships. The obligations are not regulatory, but the commercial consequences of non-disclosure or inconsistent disclosure are material. The framing of these requests as non-regulatory does not reduce the exposure they create.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Client report data flow through AI vendor infrastructure as distinct data entry category creating nFADP exposure**
Client portfolio data enters AI vendor infrastructure through report generation — not through research queries. This specific data flow has been unnamed in Geneva market discourse. The exposure is distinct from research query data flows because it involves client-facing deliverable content, specific retention and access implications, and purpose limitation obligations under nFADP Art. 6 that most boutiques have not evaluated against their vendor contracts. No Geneva service has produced documentation architecture for this specific data flow category. Distinct from C262–C272 Phase 02 which addressed attribution documentation, carrier questionnaire pre-filing, and institutional questionnaire exposure — this finding names client report generation as a specific AI vendor infrastructure data entry point with its own compliance architecture requirements.

**Finding 2: Carrier questionnaire portfolio coherence gap as discrete service category distinct from pre-filing review**
Firms submitting carrier questionnaires to multiple carriers simultaneously are not reviewing whether their portfolio-wide AI use is consistently represented across all submissions. Each carrier receives one submission. No service has reviewed the aggregate coherence. This is not a pre-filing review of a single carrier questionnaire — it is a portfolio-level coherence review across all carrier relationships. The gap surfaces during carrier examination when inconsistencies in the aggregate become evidence. Distinct from C268 Finding 1 (institutional client AI questionnaire as secondary regulatory channel) and C266 Finding 2 (carrier third-generation questionnaire documentation obligations) — this finding names portfolio coherence across multiple carrier submissions as a discrete service category not offered anywhere in the Geneva market.

**Finding 3: Counterparty AI disclosure obligation operating outside formal FINMA framework as de facto obligation creating material exposure**
Institutional clients requesting AI model disclosure and model update protocol information before any regulatory requirement mandates it. The requests have commercial consequences independent of regulatory status. The exposure is identical whether or not a regulation exists. Most Geneva boutiques have no framework for what to disclose, to whom, in what form, and what consistency obligations apply across client relationships. This is not a FINMA compliance question — it is a commercial relationship obligation that has arrived before any formal regulatory framework defines it. Distinct from C272 Finding 5 (personal MD liability signing stack) and C269 Finding 1 (portfolio-wide AI model concentration) — this finding names institutional counterparty AI disclosure expectations as an ungoverned de facto obligation operating outside any formal regulatory framework.

**Finding 4: Non-supervised staff AI use for compliance documentation as structural MD signature exposure creating carrier examination risk**
Compliance staff using AI tools to draft regulatory submissions that the MD signs. The tools were not evaluated by the signing MD. The outputs reach regulatory bodies under the MD's signature. The carrier examination will ask what the MD knows about the AI tools used in compliance documentation production. The question is not hypothetical — it is the specific question carrier third-generation questionnaires are beginning to ask. The structural exposure is that the MD's signature covers AI-assisted work the MD did not review, and the carrier examination architecture is designed to surface exactly this configuration. Distinct from C272 Finding 4 (portfolio-level AI model concentration) and C269 Finding 4 (authority-knowledge misalignment at junior level) — this finding names the specific configuration of compliance documentation AI use by non-supervised staff producing MD-signed outputs the MD cannot defend.

**Finding 5: Historical AI recommendation chain reconstruction failure as present-tense compounding exposure distinct from attribution documentation gap**
Clients requesting historical AI-assisted recommendations. The system has been updated since the recommendation was generated. The original reasoning chain is not retrievable. The request arrives before the firm can determine what it holds and cannot produce. The exposure is present-tense — it exists now, before any formal audit request arrives. It compounds as more AI-assisted recommendations accumulate across mandates. The structural cause is that AI systems are not designed for historical reconstruction — the architecture that would make reconstruction possible was not part of the implementation decision. Distinct from C262 Finding 1 (AI attribution chain disaggregation) and C264 Finding 1 (approval-reality divergence) — this finding names the specific failure mode of multi-year AI recommendation chain reconstruction as a discrete unnamed category with its own documentation architecture requirements distinct from attribution documentation for current recommendations.

**Finding 6: Regulatory documentation integrity gap as architectural failure distinct from documentation quality failure**
Carrier examinations asking how the firm knows what its AI is doing, not what the firm's AI policy says. Firms discovering their documentation describes policy while carrier examination requires evidence of operational reality. The gap is not that documentation quality is insufficient — it is that the documentation architecture was built to describe process, not to evidence system behavior. These are different architectural requirements. Documentation designed to describe process cannot answer questions about operational reality without architectural redesign. The exposure is structural: the documentation architecture built to satisfy assumed compliance requirements does not satisfy the actual examination requirements. Distinct from C270 Finding 1 (documentation inversion as visibility/governance function distinction) and C264 Finding 1 (approval-reality divergence) — this finding isolates the specific architectural distinction between process documentation and operational reality evidence, naming the structural gap that process documentation cannot close regardless of quality.
