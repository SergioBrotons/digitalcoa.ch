## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Approval temporal architecture failure creating continuous documentation gap**
Point-in-time approval documents become historical by design the moment they are signed. The periodic review architecture is structurally incompatible with continuously operating AI. The gap is present-tense and unnamed.

**Signal 2: Client portfolio data training pipeline authorization under nFADP Article 9**
When portfolio data enters commercial AI vendor infrastructure, nFADP Article 9 third-party processing obligations are activated. The training pipeline use case is specifically unnamed in the Geneva market. The authorization chain is implied but never documented.

**Signal 3: Client review conversation attribution decomposition as pre-regulatory exposure**
Exposure activates at relationship review conversations before any regulatory inquiry. The timing is distinct from regulatory exposure — the conversation with the client is the triggering event, not the examination. No firm has a protocol for managing this timing.

**Signal 4: Regulatory documentation duality trap**
FINMA and carrier documents are accessible via nFADP Article 21 client data subject requests. Documentation designed for a regulatory audience acquires an unintended client-evidence function. Firms are producing regulatory documentation that clients can then use to interrogate the firm's AI governance.

**Signal 5: Client-facing AI output forming reputation independently from governance documentation**
Two structurally separate systems operating without integration. Governance documents describe internal state; client outputs form reputation independently. The disconnect means the governance documentation and the client-facing reputation are not aligned.

**Signal 6: Post-examination response architecture absence**
No methodology exists for documenting response to FINMA AI governance findings. The response is not itself regulated. The gap between finding and resolution is ungoverned. Firms develop responses without a standard for what the response documentation should contain.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Point-in-time approval documentation system for continuously operating AI**
No Geneva market participant has developed a documentation system for point-in-time approval of continuously operating AI. The architecture that would make the approval document reflect current state rather than historical state is unnamed.

**Gap 2: Client portfolio data training pipeline authorization documentation**
No methodology exists for documenting the authorization chain when client portfolio data enters commercial AI vendor infrastructure. The methodology would need to address what authorization exists, what authorization was obtained, and what to do when the vendor's terms have changed since the data entered.

**Gap 3: Client review conversation attribution decomposition protocol**
No protocol exists for managing the attribution decomposition question when clients ask about AI governance at review conversations. The protocol would need to address how to decompose what the AI contributed from what the human contributed, in real time, without prepared documentation.

**Gap 4: Regulatory documentation duality management framework**
No framework exists for managing the duality trap — the same document serving regulatory audience and client data subject request audience simultaneously. The framework would need to address how to design documentation that satisfies both audiences without creating exposure in either.

**Gap 5: Client-facing AI output and governance documentation integration methodology**
No methodology exists for integrating client-facing AI outputs with internal governance documentation. The methodology would need to address how to ensure the two systems produce aligned signals rather than independent reputational formations.

**Gap 6: Post-examination response documentation standard**
No standard exists for what response documentation should contain when FINMA issues AI governance findings. The standard would need to address how to demonstrate resolution, not just compliance, and how to document the response in a way that survives future examinations.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA suitability obligations for AI-assisted recommendations:**
FINMA requires that suitability assessments be documented and current. AI-assisted suitability recommendations that have drifted without detection create a documentation gap between what was approved and what the system is currently doing. The gap is structural.

**ASIP/BVG occupational pension AI obligations:**
BVG pension fund advisors using AI for retirement recommendations face ASIP documentation requirements. The intersection of occupational pension obligations with AI-assisted advice creates a layered compliance problem that no framework addresses at boutique scale.

**SFAMA fund governance and AI-assisted reporting:**
SFAMA compliance for AI-assisted fund reporting requires documentation of who reviewed AI-generated content. The documentation standard for review adequacy is not defined at boutique scale. The gap between requirement and implementation is unnamed.

**ISSB sustainability disclosure AI assembly documentation:**
ISSB-aligned sustainability reports assembled with AI require documentation of what the AI contributed versus what humans verified. The dual-evidence documentation requirement is active and unnamed at boutique scale.

**nFADP Article 21 derivation documentation:**
Article 21 requires documentation of AI-assisted decision logic. AI systems not designed to retain per-instance reasoning traces cannot discharge this obligation. The gap between obligation and system architecture is structural and present-tense.

**Behavioral profiling consent architecture:**
Behavioral profiling under nFADP Articles 6 and 8 requires consent that is structurally impossible to obtain correctly at boutique scale because per-instance consent is not technically feasible. The obligation cannot be discharged correctly; only approximately.
