## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

### Signal 1: The Counterparty AI Questionnaire Exposure — Internal Gaps Revealed by External Scrutiny

Geneva boutique wealth management firms receiving AI governance questionnaires from institutional counterparties are discovering that their internal AI accountability documentation is substantially weaker than the process documents they relied on suggest. The questionnaire asks specific questions: what model versions were active when recommendations were made, how model updates were managed and communicated, what the human review process documented, what the recomputation capability is for prior model versions. These are questions the firm did not anticipate because they were not generated by a regulatory examination — they were generated by a counterparty's own due diligence process. The failure mode: the firm has AI governance documentation that addressed the process questions internally. The counterparty's questions reveal that the documentation addresses process without producing evidence of substantive adequacy.

### Signal 2: The Boutique AI Correlation Monitor — Systemic Risk Without a Boutique-Scale Framework

Geneva boutique wealth management firms operating the same AI model vendors are generating portfolio recommendations that are correlated across the Geneva market, but no boutique-scale framework exists for monitoring or measuring this correlation. The individual firm cannot observe what peer firms are doing. The correlation builds silently as multiple boutiques recommend similar positions simultaneously, using the same AI model version, without any party measuring the aggregate effect. The systemic risk is real and growing. The monitoring framework does not exist at boutique scale because no regulator has addressed it and no advisor has built it. The firm is exposed to correlated position risk it cannot measure and cannot see.

### Signal 3: The Speculative Documentation Problem — Building for Requirements That Have Not Been Published

Geneva boutique wealth management firms preparing for FINMA's forthcoming AI governance circular are building documentation speculatively — guessing which requirements will be published and investing compliance resources in documentation that may not match the actual requirements when they arrive. The asymmetry: the firm that waits for publication has certainty about what is required but less preparation time. The firm that prepares speculatively has more preparation time but risks building the wrong documentation. The firms that can read the regulatory formation process — Basel Committee AI guidance development, IOSCO influence on FINMA through technical cooperation — have a material advantage in anticipating what the domestic framework will require before the requirement is published. This is not a compliance question. It is an intelligence question.

### Signal 4: The AI Configuration Inheritance Problem — Accountable for Decisions Made Before You Arrived

Boutique Geneva wealth management firms where managing directors have inherited AI configurations from predecessors are discovering that the AI environment they operate was built by someone else, with decisions that were not documented at the time, and whose rationale is no longer recoverable. The managing director is accountable for outputs produced by a system they did not design, using configurations they did not set, deployed under a philosophy they may not fully share. The configuration inheritance problem is distinct from the vendor update problem or the documentation problem — it is the problem of accountability for a state that was created in the past and that the current accountable party cannot fully reconstruct or verify.

### Signal 5: The Client AI Comprehension Illusion — Acknowledgment Without Comprehension

Geneva wealth management clients are signing AI acknowledgment forms and accepting AI-generated recommendations in a context where the acknowledgment records that disclosure was made, not that comprehension was achieved. The nFADP Article 21 right to explanation requires that explanations be provided in a form the client can use to understand what they are accepting. A client who signed an acknowledgment but cannot accurately describe what the AI did, what alternatives were considered, or what the model's limitations mean for their position — that client accepted without comprehension. The acknowledgment form is accurate. The comprehension was not verified. The firm is exposed to a challenge that the explanation obligation was not substantively met, even though the disclosure form was completed.

### Signal 6: The Efficiency Paradox — AI Frees Capacity But Increases Compliance Overhead

Geneva boutique wealth management firms adopting AI report genuine efficiency gains in portfolio analysis and client reporting. Those efficiency gains are being absorbed — and in many cases exceeded — by compliance overhead that did not exist before AI was deployed. The compliance overhead includes AI governance documentation, human review documentation, model change management records, client disclosure maintenance, and counterparty questionnaire responses. The net capacity effect of AI at boutique scale is frequently zero or negative when compliance overhead is properly accounted for. The paradox: the efficiency gain that was the rationale for AI adoption is not visible in the firm's actual capacity, because the compliance investment required to govern AI responsibly consumes the efficiency gain it was supposed to create.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations

### FINMA — Model Update Management as Documentation Requirement

FINMA's forthcoming AI model risk governance circular is expected to impose specific requirements on model change management documentation. The Basel Committee's AI model risk Technical Amendment addresses model change notification, model versioning, and human oversight documentation — the areas where FINMA domestic implementation will most directly reflect international guidance. For Geneva boutique wealth management firms: the model update documentation requirement is not simply about logging when updates occurred. It is about demonstrating that the firm assessed the impact of each update on active client recommendations, determined whether notification was required, and executed the appropriate response. The documentation standard is not a log of model versions. It is evidence of a process that assessed the substantive effect of model changes on client outcomes.

The practical implication: firms should be building the model update impact assessment methodology now, before FINMA requires it, so that when the circular is published, the firm's process is already in place. The methodology — determining when a model update materially changes the basis of an active recommendation, what recomputation is required, what client notification is appropriate — is a substantive process that takes time to implement correctly. The firm that builds it speculatively before publication has a material advantage over the firm that must build it under regulatory deadline after publication.

### ASIP — Trustee AI Oversight Competency Documentation for Pension Fund Clients

ASIP's developing trustee education curriculum is establishing a baseline expectation for what pension fund trustees must understand about AI-assisted investment decisions. Geneva boutique wealth management firms advising occupational pension funds: the ASIP competency expectation means the firm should be able to demonstrate that the trustees it advises have received adequate AI governance briefing for their oversight role — not just that the firm has AI governance documentation, but that the trustee's oversight function was substantively informed. The ASIP trustee AI governance module, once finalized, will define the knowledge baseline for what trustees should be able to demonstrate about AI-assisted decisions at pension fund level.

The documentation requirement is forward-facing: firms advising pension fund clients should be building AI oversight competency briefing documentation now, before ASIP's curriculum is finalized, so that the documentation demonstrates proactive oversight rather than reactive compliance with a newly published standard.

### SFAMA — AI Documentation as De Facto Institutional Market Access Requirement

SFAMA AI documentation standards are functioning as a de facto market access requirement for Geneva boutique wealth management firms seeking to serve institutional counterparties that are SFAMA members or apply SFAMA-equivalent standards as procurement conditions. The SFAMA AI guidance addresses human oversight documentation, model change management, and AI recommendation audit trails — the same areas FINMA will require, creating an opportunity for documentation that is simultaneously SFAMA-aligned and FINMA-ready. The practical implication for boutique firms: the documentation the firm builds for SFAMA institutional counterparties is the same documentation that will satisfy FINMA's forthcoming requirements, if the firm understands how the two frameworks converge on the same core accountability surfaces.

### ISSB — AI-Generated Sustainability Claims and the Disclosure Accuracy Standard

ISSB-aligned disclosures require issuers to account for the accuracy of sustainability claims in client-facing documents. Geneva boutique wealth management firms using AI tools to generate ESG ratings, climate risk scores, or sustainability portfolio characteristics are generating claims that enter client disclosures and for which the firm bears accountability — regardless of whether the AI tool's methodology is proprietary and undisclosed. The ISSB accuracy standard applies to the output claim, not to the AI tool's internal process. For boutique firms: the practical obligation is to audit every AI tool that touches sustainability-related portfolio analysis now, before ISSB-aligned disclosure requirements become mandatory, and determine whether each tool's outputs constitute claims that would need to be defensible under an accuracy standard. The audit should precede the first ISSB-aligned disclosure the firm prepares.

### nFADP — Explanation Quality as Client Communication Standard

The nFADP Article 21 right to explanation for automated decisions requires that explanations be provided in an intelligible form — meaning a form the client can use to understand what they are accepting. The Swiss Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner has indicated that technically accurate but client-incomprehensible explanations do not satisfy the substance of the obligation. For Geneva boutique wealth management firms: the explanation obligation is also a client communication quality obligation, requiring that explanations be both legally sufficient and practically comprehensible. The documentation should demonstrate that explanations produced comprehension, not just that they were provided in the required form.

The compliance planning implication: firms should evaluate every AI explanation currently provided to clients against a client comprehension standard, not just a legal accuracy standard, and build documentation that demonstrates comprehension was achieved, not just that disclosure form was completed. This is a documentation quality requirement that most current nFADP compliance processes do not measure.
