## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: AI model performance degradation in production unmonitored**
AI models degrade in production as the world they were trained on changes. At boutique wealth management scale, this degradation is not monitored. The AI is being used to generate client reports in production conditions it was not evaluated for.

**Signal 2: Client expectation evolution without explicit firm communication**
Client expectations about AI use have evolved since the firm adopted AI tools. The firm has not explicitly communicated its actual practice. The gap between client expectation and firm practice is widening silently.

**Signal 3: Portfolio recommendation chain custody gap**
The chain of contribution from AI model to portfolio recommendation to client record is not documented. When the recommendation is reviewed, the custody chain cannot be established. This is distinct from attribution reconstruction — it is about the trail from model output to client-facing recommendation.

**Signal 4: FINMA supervisory visit preparation gap**
The firm preparing for a FINMA supervisory visit can produce a policy document. It cannot produce evidence of practice. The preparation gap is active and unaddressed.

**Signal 5: Boutique AI governance maturity self-assessment absent**
The firm cannot self-assess its AI governance maturity. No methodology exists for boutique-scale self-assessment. The firm operates without a reference point for where it stands relative to where it needs to be.

**Signal 6: Cross-border client AI expectation harmonization**
Clients from different jurisdictions have different expectations about AI disclosure. The firm serving cross-border clients must harmonize these expectations without a methodology for doing so.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: AI model production performance monitoring for boutique finance**
A monitoring methodology designed for boutique wealth management scale — tracking model performance degradation in production without requiring the infrastructure of a large institution — does not exist.

**Gap 2: Client expectation evolution communication framework**
A framework for explicitly communicating AI practice evolution to clients — managing expectation gap growth without creating legal exposure through the communication itself — does not exist.

**Gap 3: Portfolio recommendation chain custody documentation**
A documentation system for establishing the chain of contribution from AI model output to client-facing portfolio recommendation does not exist at boutique scale. The custody chain is the evidential record.

**Gap 4: FINMA supervisory visit preparation specifically for AI governance**
A preparation methodology specifically designed to help the boutique wealth management firm present its AI governance practice to FINMA examiners does not exist. The gap between policy documentation and examination-ready practice evidence is not bridged.

**Gap 5: Boutique AI governance maturity self-assessment methodology**
A self-assessment methodology that the firm can apply to its own AI governance — establishing where it stands, what the gaps are, and what the remediation priorities are — does not exist at boutique scale.

**Gap 6: Cross-border AI expectation harmonization for boutique wealth management**
A framework for harmonizing client AI disclosure expectations across jurisdictions without creating legal exposure through inconsistency or over-disclosure does not exist.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA obligations:**
FINMA has published AI governance expectations for supervised institutions. The expectations apply to practice, not policy. The firm presenting only a policy document to FINMA examiners is not presenting what FINMA expects to see. The practice evidence is what is required.

**ASIP obligations for pension fund trustees:**
Pension fund trustees using AI for investment decisions face ASIP-specific obligations. The trustees who adopted AI incrementally have accumulated governance debt that the ASIP obligations make visible. The documentation debt and the regulatory obligation are converging.

**SFAMA documentation requirements:**
The Swiss Fund and Asset Management Association documentation requirements create specific obligations for AI-assisted portfolio management. The requirements are multiple and cumulative. The firm that has not systematized its documentation is structurally out of compliance even when the policy is in place.

**ISSB sustainability data and AI:**
ISSB requirements for sustainability data lineage intersect with AI tool use. When AI is used to generate sustainability data, the lineage documentation must meet ISSB standards. The intersection creates documentation obligations that the firm may not have mapped.

**nFADP cross-border client data:**
The firm serving cross-border clients must navigate nFADP obligations alongside the data protection regimes of the clients' jurisdictions. The cross-border dimension adds complexity that the domestic-only firm does not face.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: AI model production performance degradation unmonitored at boutique scale**
AI models degrade in production as the world changes. At boutique scale, this degradation is not monitored. The AI generating client reports may be operating under conditions it was not evaluated for. The monitoring infrastructure does not exist at the scale required.

**Finding 2: Client expectation evolution without explicit firm communication**
Client expectations have evolved since the firm adopted AI. The firm has not explicitly communicated its actual practice. The gap between what clients expect and what the firm actually does is widening silently. The communication gap is itself a risk.

**Finding 3: Portfolio recommendation chain custody as distinct from attribution reconstruction**
The trail from AI model output to client-facing recommendation is not documented. This is distinct from attribution reconstruction — it is about the custody chain, not the individual contribution. The custody chain is the evidential record the firm needs when a recommendation is reviewed.

**Finding 4: FINMA supervisory visit preparation gap between policy and practice evidence**
The firm can produce a policy document. It cannot produce practice evidence. FINMA examiners ask about practice. The preparation gap is active. The firm that shows up with a policy document is not showing what the examiners expect to see.

**Finding 5: Boutique AI governance maturity self-assessment methodology absent**
The firm cannot self-assess its AI governance maturity. No methodology exists for boutique-scale self-assessment. The firm operates without a reference point. Remediation without a baseline is unfocused.

**Finding 6: Cross-border client AI expectation harmonization without methodology**
Clients from different jurisdictions have different AI disclosure expectations. The firm serving cross-border clients must harmonize these expectations without a methodology. The inconsistency in disclosure across jurisdictions is itself a risk.
