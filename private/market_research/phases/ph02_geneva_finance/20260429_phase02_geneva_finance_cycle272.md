## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA and AI accountability:**
FINMA has not published AI-specific guidance for boutique wealth management. The absence of guidance is not equivalent to permission. Firms are making compliance decisions without a regulatory reference point.

**SFAMA and AI:**
SFAMA has not published AI-specific guidance for boutique member firms. The circular that exists was written for larger institutions and its applicability to boutiques is untested.

**nFADP and portfolio data:**
nFADP applies to client personal data in AI systems. The data minimization principle (Art. 6) and purpose limitation (Art. 6) apply to how AI systems process client portfolio data. Most boutiques have not conducted the required mapping.

**ISSB S2 and AI data lineage:**
ISSB S2 requires entity-specific asset-level data for Scope 2. AI systems that estimate or assemble this data create a lineage question the standard does not resolve for AI-compiled inputs.

**FIDLEG and AI recommendations:**
FIDLEG Art. 26 requires disclosure of algorithmic routine basis and consequences in accessible language. Most Geneva boutiques are not making this disclosure in a form that satisfies the requirement.

**Carrier third-generation questionnaires:**
Carriers are sending third-generation AI questionnaires that probe decision logic and reasoning chains. These questionnaires are operating as de facto audit instruments without published underwriting standards.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Institutional client AI questionnaire as attribution disclosure reveal, not just compliance event**
The institutional client AI questionnaire is revealing that the firm's documentation does not address attribution. The questionnaire asks about reasoning chains and AI contribution. The firm's policies address process. The gap between what is asked and what exists is the exposure.

**Finding 2: Carrier third-generation questionnaire as the de facto AI governance audit for boutiques without internal audit function**
The carrier third-generation questionnaire is functioning as the de facto AI governance audit for boutiques that have no internal audit function. The gaps discovered through the carrier questionnaire have been present for years. No other mechanism has surfaced them.

**Finding 3: ISSB S2 Scope 2 AI data lineage as the undocumentable disclosure**
ISSB S2 requires entity-specific asset-level data. AI systems estimate or assemble this data. The lineage of AI-compiled Scope 2 data is a disclosure requirement that cannot be satisfied at boutique scale with current vendor architecture.

**Finding 4: Portfolio-level AI model concentration as invisible systematic risk at boutique scale**
The same underlying model generating all AI-assisted recommendations creates concentrated model risk invisible at the individual mandate level. The concentration is a portfolio-level property that boutique documentation architecture does not detect.

**Finding 5: Personal MD liability signing stack as unmanaged aggregate with no separation analysis**
Managing directors sign across attestations, carrier questionnaires, and institutional responses without separating personal from firm exposure. The aggregate of the signing stack is an unmanaged exposure no Geneva service has addressed as a discrete product.

**Finding 6: Geneva cross-border mandate AI accountability as multi-jurisdictional obligation accumulation without mapping methodology**
Geneva cross-border mandates accumulate Swiss regulatory obligations, client-resident-abroad obligations, multi-jurisdiction asset obligations, and AI vendor infrastructure obligations simultaneously. No mapping methodology exists to manage the accumulation.
