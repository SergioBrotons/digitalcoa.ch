## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: AI confidence interval as unresolved client communication obligation**
When clients ask about the confidence or range behind an AI flag, portfolio managers have no protocol for what to disclose. AI systems produce classifications and signals — not confidence intervals calibrated for client communication. The gap between what the AI produces and what the client is asking for is becoming a recurring client conversation that the firm cannot answer adequately. This is distinct from the explanation deficit (C219): that problem concerned why an AI flagged something; this problem concerns how certain the AI is about it, which is a different disclosure dimension.

**Signal 2: AI recommendation override documentation as untracked decision layer**
When portfolio managers override AI recommendations, those override decisions are not being systematically documented. The firm accumulates a history of what the human decided, without any record of what the AI actually suggested. This creates two distinct problems: an inability to detect patterns in when the AI is being overridden (and whether the overrides are consistently right or wrong), and an absence of reconstructable evidence if the override decision is later questioned. The documentation gap is not about AI output — it is about the firm's own decision layer operating without a record.

**Signal 3: Compliance officer personal liability for AI content approval without evaluation methodology**
Compliance officers at boutique wealth management firms are formally approving AI-generated client communications with their signature, knowing they are evaluating text coherence rather than analytical methodology. The personal professional liability attaching to that approval is not proportional to the evaluation capacity the signature actually represents. This creates a specific compliance officer exposure: accountability for approving something the approver cannot fully assess. The market has not addressed the compliance officer's personal position as distinct from the firm's AI governance policy.

**Signal 4: Client AI preference management as inconsistent and unrecorded**
Boutique wealth management firms are receiving different AI preference signals from different clients — some explicitly opting out, some requesting AI-assisted reports, some remaining silent. These preferences are being handled inconsistently: sometimes documented, sometimes verbal, sometimes not recorded. The absence of a systematic AI preference management record creates an exposure at the next compliance review: the firm cannot demonstrate it has managed client AI preferences coherently across the client base.

**Signal 5: Cross-client AI signal correlation invisible at boutique scale**
When the same AI model is used across multiple client portfolios, correlated signals may be emerging across clients — positions being flagged as high-risk simultaneously, allocation recommendations pointing in the same direction across different portfolios. The boutique wealth manager has no cross-client aggregation tool to see whether the AI is producing correlated signals that would represent a concentration risk if those positions move together. This is distinct from individual portfolio AI aggregation blindness (C219): that concerned individual portfolio signals; this concerns cross-client correlation of AI-driven signals.

**Signal 6: Investment AI and compliance AI data use documentation requirements in structural conflict**
Boutique wealth management firms using AI in both investment management and compliance monitoring are processing the same client data through different AI systems with different documentation requirements. The compliance system requires clean access and audit trails; the investment system requires contextual continuity. These requirements are not always compatible. The data governance gap — how the same client data satisfies both documentation regimes simultaneously — is unaddressed at boutique scale.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: AI confidence disclosure client communication protocol**
A protocol for communicating AI confidence or uncertainty information to clients — translating AI classification outputs into client-comprehensible confidence disclosures — does not exist at boutique scale. The protocol is not about what the AI internally knows; it is about what the firm can responsibly state to clients about the reliability of an AI signal. The gap is distinct from explanation methodology (C219): that concerned the substantive reasoning behind a flag; this concerns the probability or confidence dimension the client is directly asking for.

**Gap 2: AI recommendation override audit trail as discrete documentation product**
A documentation methodology for systematically recording when and why portfolio managers override AI recommendations — preserving both the AI's actual suggestion and the human's override decision as separately auditable records — does not exist at boutique scale. This is not an AI governance policy question. It is a specific documentation architecture question: what the record must contain to support pattern detection in override decisions and to serve as reconstructable evidence if the override is later challenged. The override audit trail is distinct from the dual-evidence trail (C237 Gap 2): the dual-evidence trail documents AI recommendation and professional endorsement simultaneously at the moment of approval; the override audit trail documents the specific decision to depart from the AI's recommendation after the fact.

**Gap 3: Compliance officer personal AI approval liability framework**
A framework for compliance officers at boutique wealth management firms to assess and document their personal exposure when formally approving AI-generated client communications — establishing what evaluation is reasonably required, what can be documented as the basis for approval, and what personal liability attaches to the approval signature — does not exist. This is not a firm-level AI governance policy. It is a personal professional liability question specific to the compliance officer role, with no current advisory product addressing it.

**Gap 4: Client AI preference management record system**
A systematic record for managing client AI preferences across the client base — establishing which clients have opted in, which have opted out, what preferences have been communicated, and how those preferences are preserved for compliance review — does not exist at boutique scale. This is distinct from client data sovereignty (C228): that concerned where data is stored and processed; this concerns what clients have been told about AI use in their own reporting and whether the firm's AI preference management is coherently documented.

**Gap 5: Cross-client AI signal correlation monitoring methodology**
A methodology for boutique wealth management firms to monitor whether AI-driven signals are correlated across client portfolios — establishing what aggregation is possible at boutique scale, what concentration risk indicators would trigger action, and what documentation is required to demonstrate awareness of cross-client AI correlation — does not exist. The gap is distinct from individual portfolio aggregation blindness (C219): that was about seeing aggregate signals within one portfolio; this is about seeing correlated signals across multiple portfolios managed by the same firm using the same AI infrastructure.

**Gap 6: Investment/compliance AI data governance reconciliation documentation**
A documentation architecture for managing the structural conflict between investment AI and compliance AI data use requirements — establishing how the same client data satisfies audit trail requirements for compliance and continuity requirements for investment management simultaneously — does not exist at boutique scale. This is not a tool selection question. It is a data governance documentation question specific to firms running AI in both functions, with no current resolution framework.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations

**FINMA — Circular 2016/1 (Investor Segregation)**
FINMA Circular 2016/1 investor segregation and suitability documentation obligations apply to investment recommendations. The compliance officer's formal approval signature on AI-generated investment commentary must be supportable by evidence that the approver had a basis for approval. At boutique scale, the approver is evaluating text coherence, not analytical methodology. The documentation of the approval basis must reflect what the approver actually evaluated — not what the regulatory framework ideally requires. If a supervisory examination asks what the compliance officer did to evaluate the AI's analytical soundness, the documentation currently does not answer that question.

**FINMA — Supervisory Framework for AI/ML (2023)**
FINMA's AI/ML supervisory expectations require that AI-assisted decisions maintain appropriate human oversight. For compliance officers formally approving AI-generated content, the "appropriate oversight" standard requires the approver to have a documented basis for the approval decision. At boutique scale, that basis is currently the evaluation of text coherence — not a methodology for assessing AI analytical soundness. The supervisory framework does not provide a boutique-specific methodology for what human oversight of AI-generated content actually requires at the compliance officer level.

**FINMA — Q3–Q4 2026 Examination Intensification**
FINMA supervisory examinations are expected to intensify in Q3–Q4 2026, with specific attention to AI deployment in wealth management. The examination surface includes documentation of how AI-assisted decisions are reviewed, how override decisions are recorded, how client AI preferences are managed, and how compliance officers document their approval basis. Boutique wealth management firms that have not built documentation architecture for these specific questions will face examinations with records that do not answer the questions being asked.

**ASIP — Pension Fund AI Decision Documentation**
Pension funds using AI for investment decisions affecting beneficiary benefits face documentation requirements under ASIP digital governance guidelines. Override decisions — when the portfolio manager departs from the AI recommendation — require documentation of both the AI's suggestion and the override rationale. Pension fund documentation systems were not designed for this two-part record. The ASIP requirement for documented decision trails intersects with the AI override documentation gap at boutique pension advisory scale.

**SFAMA — Fund Management AI Documentation**
SFAMA fund management documentation standards require that AI-assisted portfolio construction decisions maintain methodology documentation, input documentation, and professional endorsement documentation. When a fund manager overrides an AI allocation recommendation, the documentation trail must capture what the AI recommended and why the professional departure was justified — not just the final decision. SFAMA members at boutique scale face this documentation requirement without an implementation methodology.

**nFADP — Client Preference Documentation and Data Use Accountability**
The revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection requires that data subjects — clients — receive clear information about AI processing of their data. Client AI preferences (opt-in, opt-out) constitute client choices about how their data is processed by AI systems. The nFADP Art. 12 right to be informed about automated decision-making intersects with client AI preference management: if a client opts out of AI-assisted reporting, the firm must have a record of that choice and a documented process for ensuring AI is not applied to that client's data. The documentation architecture for this preference record is not established at boutique scale.

**ISSB — AI Disclosure in Sustainability-Linked Analysis (Pipeline)**
The ISSB disclosure standards being integrated into Swiss regulatory frameworks for listed entities and large firms create derivative documentation obligations for wealth management firms advising ISSB-obligated clients. AI systems used in sustainability-linked investment analysis must be disclosed as part of the sustainability reporting value chain. The boutique firm using AI for sustainability analysis in client portfolios faces these obligations with documentation infrastructure designed for conventional portfolio analysis — not AI-assisted sustainability disclosure.

**Emerging: Federal Council AI Ordinance (H2 2026)**
The Federal Council AI ordinance pipeline will likely include client disclosure requirements for AI-assisted services. For boutique wealth management, the incoming requirements will probably include obligations to disclose AI use in client communications and to maintain documentation of client AI preference management. The preparation window is the period before the ordinance is finalized. Firms that build client AI preference management documentation architecture now will be positioned to satisfy requirements that are not yet formally published.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: AI confidence interval client disclosure as unaddressed boutique-scale communication obligation**
When a boutique wealth management firm's AI flags a position as high-risk and the client asks how certain the firm should be about that flag, the portfolio manager has no client-comprehensible answer. AI classification outputs do not translate directly into confidence intervals suitable for client communication. The gap between what the AI produces (classification) and what the client is asking for (confidence or range) is becoming a recurring present-tense problem. The communication protocol for translating AI confidence information into client disclosures — what can be stated, what cannot, and what documentation the firm maintains for its disclosure decisions — does not exist as a boutique-specific advisory product. This is distinct from the explanation deficit (C219) which concerned why the AI flagged something; this concerns how certain the AI is, which is a different disclosure dimension with different regulatory implications.

**Finding 2: AI recommendation override documentation as untracked decision layer creating reconstructability failure**
When portfolio managers override AI recommendations, those override decisions are not being systematically documented. The firm accumulates a record of human decisions without any record of what the AI actually suggested. This creates two specific problems: an inability to detect patterns in when the AI is being overridden (and whether overrides are collectively right or wrong), and an absence of reconstructable evidence if a specific override decision is later questioned by a client or a regulator. The override audit trail is distinct from the dual-evidence trail methodology (C237 Finding 2): the dual-evidence trail addresses simultaneous documentation of AI recommendation and professional endorsement at the moment of approval; the override audit trail addresses the specific decision to depart from the AI's recommendation after the fact, with a documentation architecture for preserving both the AI's suggestion and the override rationale as separately auditable records.

**Finding 3: Compliance officer personal liability for AI content approval without proportional evaluation methodology**
Compliance officers at boutique wealth management firms are formally approving AI-generated client communications with their personal professional signature — knowing that their evaluation covers text coherence, not analytical soundness. The personal liability attaching to that signature is not calibrated to the evaluation capacity the signature actually represents. This is a specific compliance officer personal exposure: accountability for approving analytical methodology they have no established way to evaluate. The market has not addressed the compliance officer's personal AI approval liability as distinct from the firm's AI governance policy. The advisory product here is not about the firm's governance — it is about what the compliance officer personally needs to document to sustain their approval decision against a supervisory examination question.

**Finding 4: Client AI preference management as inconsistent and unrecorded creating compliance exposure at review**
Boutique wealth management firms are receiving different AI preference signals from clients — explicit opt-outs, explicit opt-ins, silence — and managing those preferences inconsistently: sometimes in email, sometimes in a note, sometimes not recorded. The absence of a systematic client AI preference management record means the firm cannot demonstrate at a compliance review that it has managed client AI preferences coherently across the client base. The nFADP Art. 12 right to be informed about automated decision-making intersects with this: if clients have opted out of AI-assisted reporting, the firm must document that choice and demonstrate the opt-out has been implemented. The client AI preference management record architecture is a discrete boutique-scale gap with compliance review exposure implications.

**Finding 5: Cross-client AI signal correlation as invisible systemic risk with no boutique-scale monitoring methodology**
When the same AI model is used across multiple client portfolios, the AI may be producing correlated signals — the same types of positions flagged across different clients, allocation recommendations pointing in the same direction across different portfolios. The portfolio manager sees individual signals; there is no cross-client view of whether those signals are correlated. If those correlated positions move together, the concentration risk is invisible until it crystallizes. The cross-client AI signal correlation monitoring methodology — what aggregation is possible at boutique scale, what indicators trigger action, what documentation demonstrates awareness of correlation — does not exist. This is distinct from individual portfolio AI aggregation blindness (C219 Finding 6): that concerned seeing aggregate signals within a single portfolio; this concerns seeing correlated AI-driven signals across multiple client portfolios managed by the same firm using the same AI infrastructure.

**Finding 6: Investment/compliance AI data use structural conflict as unowned documentation gap with no reconciliation framework**
Boutique wealth management firms using AI in both investment management and compliance monitoring are processing the same client data through different AI systems with different documentation requirements. The compliance system requires clean access and audit trails (periodic, complete records accessible for examination). The investment system requires contextual continuity (preserving client context across reporting periods). These requirements point in different directions when documentation is being structured: compliance wants isolation and completeness; investment needs continuity and coherence across time. The data governance reconciliation — how the same client data simultaneously satisfies both documentation regimes — is unowned and unaddressed. No current boutique-scale advisory product resolves this structural conflict. The documentation gap is not about which system gets priority; it is about building a data architecture that can satisfy both requirements without forcing a choice that creates exposure in one direction or the other.
