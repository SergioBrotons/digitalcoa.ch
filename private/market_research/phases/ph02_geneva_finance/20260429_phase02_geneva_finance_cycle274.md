## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: AI-inferred content in client reports as a real-time client confidentiality obligation distinct from data-at-rest protection**
AI systems generating client-specific commentary are producing inferences about client financial behavior, risk tolerance, and future intentions in real time. These inferences are embedded in client-facing documents. The confidentiality obligation is not limited to raw portfolio data stored in the vendor infrastructure — it extends to what the AI generates about the client during the report assembly process. No Geneva boutique has evaluated whether AI-inferred content about the client constitutes client personal data under nFADP, whether it can be disclosed through a data subject access request, and whether it is protected under the confidentiality obligations of the advisory relationship. The real-time inference generation moment has not been evaluated as a discrete data protection occasion.

**Signal 2: Methodology performance evidence as the documentation gap carrier examinations are now probing**
Carriers are asking not just what the methodology says, but whether the methodology has performed as designed. Whether it has caught AI behavior that deviated from expectations. Whether the review process has a record of its own effectiveness. Firms discovering that methodology documentation describes the intended process; carriers are now asking for evidence that the process produced its intended result. This is a different accountability demand: evidence of performance, not description of design.

**Signal 3: AI-generated compliance text quality assurance as the unreadable dimension of MD-signed outputs**
Compliance documentation produced by AI systems reads as authoritative compliance language. The register, terminology, and structure are correct. The MD reviewing it assesses it as a document. The quality that cannot be assessed from reading the document is whether the legal positions it contains are accurate for the firm's specific situation. AI text generation is optimized for linguistic coherence and regulatory register, not for the legal accuracy of what it describes. The quality assurance gap — how an MD assesses whether AI-generated compliance text accurately represents the firm's compliance position — is unnamed in the Geneva market.

**Signal 4: Institutional client AI governance dialogue as an active ongoing channel distinct from questionnaire responses**
Institutional clients are asking AI governance questions in ongoing client conversations, not only through formal questionnaires. The questions arrive as follow-ups to questionnaire responses, as meeting agenda items, and as part of broader relationship reviews. The consistency problem is not limited to questionnaire answer consistency — it extends to what the firm communicates in dialogue across all institutional relationships. No Geneva boutique has a framework for maintaining a consistent AI governance communication posture in active client dialogue, distinct from documented questionnaire responses.

**Signal 5: Client-formed expectations about boutique AI documentation standards operating below firm awareness**
Sophisticated clients are forming expectations about what a Geneva boutique wealth management firm should document about its AI use — comparing firms against an implicit standard the firms themselves do not know exists. The expectation is not just about whether documentation exists; it is about whether the documentation reflects a coherent standard or reflects ad hoc responses to individual regulatory and commercial demands. The credibility dimension of AI governance documentation — what it signals about firm sophistication to clients who are paying attention — has not been named as a discrete market signal in Geneva.

**Signal 6: Documentation system coherence as the firm-level property carrier examinations assess, not individual document quality**
Carrier examinations evaluate whether the firm's AI documentation functions as a coherent system — whether individual documents connect to each other, whether they describe a consistent picture of what the AI system actually does, and whether they demonstrate that governance is active rather than documented. Individual document quality is necessary but not sufficient. A firm whose documents are each defensible individually can still present an incoherent picture when read together. The examination is not primarily about document quality — it is about documentation system coherence at the firm level.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA and documentation system coherence as the examination standard:**
FINMA has not published guidance on what constitutes adequate AI governance documentation for boutique wealth management firms. The absence of published guidance means firms are building documentation against assumptions about what will be evaluated. Carrier examinations are revealing that the evaluation standard is not individual document quality — it is documentation system coherence. Whether the firm's documents together demonstrate that it knows what its AI system is doing, that governance is active, and that the documentation reflects operational reality rather than intended design. This is a different standard from what most boutiques built their documentation programs against.

**FINMA and AI-inferred client content in reports:**
FINMA's suitability framework requires that recommendations be based on accurate client information. When AI systems generate inferences about client financial behavior and embed those inferences in recommendation-supporting reports, the question of whether those inferences are accurate — and what the firm's obligation is to verify them — is unaddressed in any published guidance. The AI-generated inference becomes part of the client profile the firm relies on for recommendations. The suitability of the recommendation may depend on the accuracy of the AI inference, not only on the quality of the underlying data.

**nFADP and AI-inferred personal data in client reports:**
AI-inferred content about client financial behavior, risk tolerance, and intentions may constitute personal data under nFADP, even if the inferences were generated by the AI system rather than provided directly by the client. The data minimization principle (Art. 6) and purpose limitation principle apply to AI-inferred data used in client reports. If AI-inferred content about the client is embedded in the client's own report, the question of whether that content is subject to data subject access requests under Article 13 is unaddressed in any Geneva authority guidance. The retention and access architecture of AI vendor infrastructure means that AI-inferred client content may be accessible to third parties through the vendor's systems.

**FIDLEG and AI-generated compliance documentation:**
FIDLEG requires that investment firms maintain documentation that allows clients to understand the basis of recommendations and the firm's compliance with regulatory requirements. AI-generated compliance documentation submitted under the MD's signature may not accurately represent the firm's compliance position. The MD's signature certifies compliance; the AI-generated text may describe a compliance posture that does not match the firm's actual situation. The FIDLEG documentation requirement is not satisfied by the presence of AI-generated text that reads as compliance language.

**Carrier third-generation questionnaire and methodology performance evidence:**
Carrier third-generation questionnaires are asking not just what the methodology is, but how it has performed. The shift from methodology description to methodology performance evidence is creating a documentation gap that most boutiques are only discovering during the examination. The gap is structural: the methodology was designed and documented; the performance of the methodology in catching AI behavior that deviated from expectations was not recorded. Building the evidence going forward requires a different documentation architecture than what most boutiques have in place.

**Institutional counterparty dialogue and the consistency obligation:**
Institutional clients asking AI governance questions in ongoing dialogue are creating consistency obligations that formal questionnaire responses do not. What the firm communicates in meetings and correspondence becomes part of the commercial relationship record. Inconsistent AI governance communication in dialogue — different messages from different firm personnel across different conversations — creates commercial representation exposure that inconsistent questionnaire answers do. The consistency obligation in dialogue is not addressed in any Geneva market guidance.

## 06 — Findings Summary: 6 Net-New Insights, Distinct from C214–C273 Phase 02

**Finding 1: AI-inferred content in client reports as a real-time nFADP confidentiality obligation distinct from data-at-rest protection**
Prior cycles addressed client data flows through AI vendor infrastructure, nFADP Art. 6 purpose limitation for AI-processed client data, and client portfolio data entering AI systems through research queries. This finding addresses a different moment and a different data category: the real-time generation of AI-inferred content about the client embedded in client-facing documents. When AI systems generate performance attribution narratives, risk tolerance inferences, and financial intention projections as part of report assembly, those inferences constitute AI-generated content about the client that travels to the client's address through the vendor infrastructure. The nFADP question is not whether the raw portfolio data is protected — it is whether AI-generated inferences about the client's financial behavior and intentions are client personal data subject to data subject access requests, purpose limitation, and confidentiality obligations. The real-time inference generation moment has not been evaluated as a discrete data protection occasion in any Geneva market service. The distinct mechanism: AI inference generation during report assembly produces client-specific content that is not raw data, is not the same as stored portfolio data, and has not been assessed against nFADP obligations in this specific document context. This is distinct from C273 Finding 1 (client report data flow as vendor infrastructure data entry point) which addressed data flow architecture; this finding addresses the specific nFADP obligation triggered by AI-inferred content generated during the report assembly process itself.

**Finding 2: Methodology performance evidence as the specific documentation artifact carrier third-generation examinations now require, distinct from methodology design documentation**
Prior cycles addressed carrier third-generation questionnaires as de facto audit instruments, methodology documentation adequacy, and the shift from process existence to substantive decision-logic review. This finding addresses a more specific demand: carriers are asking not what the methodology is, but whether it has performed as designed. Whether it has caught AI behavior that deviated from expectations. Whether the review process has a record of its own effectiveness. The documentation gap is not methodology design documentation — it is methodology performance evidence. The shift from design documentation to performance evidence requires a different documentation architecture: evidence that the methodology operated, not description of what it was designed to do. Most boutiques cannot produce this evidence because the documentation architecture was designed to describe methodology, not to record its performance. The distinct mechanism: carriers probing methodology performance in operation, not methodology design on paper, creates a documentation artifact gap that methodology documentation cannot fill regardless of quality. This is distinct from C272 Finding 2 (carrier questionnaire as de facto audit) and C273 Finding 6 (regulatory documentation integrity gap) — both addressed documentation quality; this finding identifies the specific performance evidence artifact that is structurally different from design documentation.
