## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, and Complaints

**Signal 1: AI-generated client communication authenticity — the review assumption gap**
Clients are beginning to receive AI-written communications from their advisors — reports, summaries, portfolio commentaries — and to ask whether the advisor actually read them before sending. The question is not about AI disclosure. It is about the client's assumption that what appears to be a professional communication from their advisor was actually reviewed by their advisor. When the answer is "approved but not read in that sense," the gap is not a disclosure failure — it is an operational one: the firm's workflow sends AI-generated text to clients under the advisor's name, and the advisor cannot honestly represent what they reviewed.

**Signal 2: AI tool vendor deprecation as documentation continuity event**
AI reporting tools and portfolio analytics platforms are being deprecated by vendors — vendor exit, product discontinuation, model sunset. When a tool is deprecated, the client reports generated by that tool remain in client files, may be referenced in ongoing advisory conversations, and may be subject to client request or regulatory examination. The deprecation creates a specific documentation event: the tool that generated the record no longer exists in its operating form. No Geneva boutique has a protocol for what vendor deprecation of an AI tool means for documentation continuity, for client file integrity, or for the ability to respond to questions about reports generated by a deprecated system.

**Signal 3: Correlated AI signals across Geneva boutique peer group**
Geneva boutiques using similar AI portfolio analytics platforms — concentrated vendor market — may be receiving correlated signals, producing similar allocation recommendations, and generating portfolios that are beginning to look alike without any intentional coordination. The problem is invisible at the individual firm level: each boutique sees only its own AI's output. The correlation becomes visible only at the market level, where it has not been measured. The boutique has no framework for determining whether its AI-generated signals are consistent with signals its peers are receiving, or whether its portfolio composition is drifting toward peer correlation through shared AI infrastructure.

**Signal 4: Counterparty AI questionnaire as informal standard formation event**
Counterparty AI governance due diligence questionnaires are functioning as an informal standard-formation mechanism. The questionnaire arrives with questions the boutique has not prepared for. The boutique must decide in real time what to disclose, what to decline to answer, and what to attempt to answer without adequate documentation. Each boutique's responses — and each refusal — contribute to a market understanding of what Geneva wealth management firms are prepared to disclose about their AI systems. The questionnaire is not only assessing the boutique; it is shaping the standard for what disclosure means in this context.

**Signal 5: AI recommendation finality language — the confidence problem**
AI-generated portfolio recommendations arrive with confidence language that human recommendations do not carry. The AI presents its recommendation with an implied confidence level that reflects the model's output certainty, not the advisor's professional judgment. This creates a specific operational problem: the advisor presents the recommendation to the client with an implicit weight that is determined by the AI, not by the human professional. The client's decision is being influenced by AI-generated confidence language that has not been validated against professional standards and that the advisor may not be able to defend.

**Signal 6: Operating timeline vs. compliance timeline mismatch at the documentation level**
AI portfolio tools generate continuous output — daily, weekly, in real time. Compliance review cycles are periodic — quarterly, annually. The gap between continuous AI operation and periodic compliance review creates a documentation surface that is entirely unaddressed: what the AI did between review cycles has no defined documentation architecture. The compliance officer signs a review that covers a point in time. The AI has been operating continuously between that point and the present. The review record does not capture what happened during the gap.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### Gaps: What No One Is Addressing

**Gap 1: AI tool deprecation documentation protocol**
No Geneva competitor offers a methodology for managing documentation continuity when an AI tool is deprecated — no protocol for what client files containing deprecated-tool output require, no framework for responding to client or counterparty questions about reports generated by a tool that no longer exists, no inventory methodology for identifying which client records were generated by which tool version under which vendor. This is distinct from general vendor management and from business continuity planning. The specific problem: the documentation record refers to a system that is no longer available for verification, and no one has defined what this requires in terms of file integrity management.

**Gap 2: Boutique AI signal correlation monitor**
No Geneva advisor offers a framework for boutique-scale monitoring of AI signal correlation across peer portfolios. The problem — that boutiques using similar AI platforms may be generating correlated recommendations without awareness — is not addressed by any current advisory service. The boutique has no methodology for determining whether its AI is producing signals that are also being produced to its peer firms, or what market-level concentration risk this creates. This is distinct from the portfolio concentration risk discussed in prior cycles; it addresses the AI infrastructure correlation that produces portfolio convergence across firms, not within a single firm's client portfolio.

**Gap 3: AI confidence language as a discrete communication obligation**
No Geneva competitor addresses the specific problem of AI-generated confidence language in client communications. The AI's confidence level is embedded in how recommendations are presented — not as an explicit communication choice made by the advisor, but as a property of the AI output that the advisor inherits without review. The boutique's client communications contain AI-generated confidence signals that have not been validated by professional judgment, that affect client decision-making, and that the advisor cannot currently audit. This is distinct from disclosure obligation (whether AI was used) and from attribution obligation (what the AI contributed): it is the question of what weight the AI's confidence carries in client communications and who is responsible for that weight.

**Gap 4: Documentation continuity architecture for operating/compliance timeline gap**
No competitor offers a documentation methodology for the gap between continuous AI operation and periodic compliance review. The operating timeline gap — what the AI did between review cycles — is not covered by existing compliance documentation, has no defined documentation architecture, and creates an exposure surface that is entirely unmanaged at boutique scale. The specific gap: the boutique's compliance record documents what was reviewed at a point in time, not what happened during the intervals between reviews.

**Gap 5: Portfolio attribution ownership — the accountability gap nobody owns**
When portfolio attribution questions arise — which signals contributed to this composition, how were they weighted, what changed between last period and this one — no Geneva boutique has a defined internal ownership structure for the accountability of that attribution. The compliance officer may own the tool approval. The portfolio advisor may own the recommendation. The managing director may own the client relationship. The attribution question crosses all three, and none of them has it as a defined responsibility. No competitor addresses the specific problem of attribution ownership as a discrete organizational role gap.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva / Swiss Regulatory Obligations

**FINMA — Financial Market Supervisory Authority**

The Q1 2027 AI advisory suitability audit program remains the operative enforcement horizon. FINMA has not published the specific scope of the audit, but the direction is established: supervisory scrutiny of AI-assisted advisory will include examination of documentation quality, not only documentation existence. The compliance officer's signature will be examined not only for whether review occurred, but for what the review covered and what standard was applied.

FINMA Circular 2016/1 (Asset Management) remains the primary suitability framework. AI-generated recommendations must satisfy the same documentation obligations as human-generated ones — but the AI's contribution to the recommendation must be traceable, which is not addressed by the circular's original framing. The practical enforcement trajectory: FINMA examiners are asking specific questions about AI model version state, human review substance, and recommendation attribution that the circular does not explicitly govern. Firms are being held to a standard that is forming through examination practice, not through published guidance.

The AI confidence language problem has a specific FINMA dimension: client communication obligations require that communications be accurate and not misleading. AI-generated confidence language in portfolio recommendations may create communication accuracy issues that have not been tested in Swiss supervisory practice.

**ASIP — Association Suisse des Institutions de Prévoyance**

ASIP guidance on AI in pension fund asset allocation continues to develop. For Geneva boutiques managing pension fund assets or advising pension fund trustees, the AI documentation obligations extend to demonstrating that fiduciary obligations were discharged for decisions where AI contributed. The signal-weighting problem (what signals contributed to this allocation, how were they weighted) is directly relevant to the pension fund trustee's fiduciary duty: the trustee must be able to demonstrate the reasoning basis for allocation decisions, not only that the AI produced an output within acceptable parameters.

**SFAMA — Swiss Funds and Asset Management Association**

SFAMA guidance on AI-assisted portfolio management does not prescribe specific documentation methodology, but SFAMA's role as an informal supervisory standard-setter means that what SFAMA publishes shapes examination expectations. The correlation concern (boutiques using similar AI platforms receiving correlated signals) has a SFAMA dimension: if portfolio convergence becomes visible at the market level, SFAMA member firms using the same AI infrastructure may face questions about systemic risk concentration that have not been posed at the boutique scale.

**ISSB — International Sustainability Standards Board**

Mid-2027 ISSB-aligned reporting creates the documentation requirement for AI-generated sustainability data. The AI tool deprecation problem has a specific ISSB dimension: client reports filed under ISSB-aligned frameworks may contain sustainability data generated by an AI tool that is subsequently deprecated. The ISSB framework requires that sustainability information be factual and verifiable — verifiable means the methodology must be documentably accessible. If the tool that generated the data no longer exists, the verification requirement becomes a documentation continuity problem.

**nFADP — Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (revised 2024/2025)**

nFADP enforcement is active. The FDPIC has investigative capacity and has signaled interest in automated decision-making systems. The specific compliance challenge for Geneva boutiques: AI portfolio analytics systems process client financial data for purposes that must satisfy Article 8 (purpose limitation) and Article 6 (lawful basis). The AI tool deprecation scenario creates a specific nFADP problem — data processed by a deprecated tool may be subject to data subject access requests that cannot be fully answered because the tool that processed the data no longer operates in its original form.

The confidence language problem has an nFADP dimension: automated decision-making that significantly affects clients (Article 21) requires the ability to provide explanation. If the AI's confidence level in a recommendation is part of what influenced the client's decision, and the confidence level cannot be explained or validated, the explanation obligation may be unmet.

## 06 — Findings Summary

### Six Net-New Insights — Distinct from C214–C248 Phase 02

---

**Finding 1: AI tool deprecation as a discrete documentation continuity event with no Geneva protocol**

AI tool deprecation — vendor exit, product discontinuation, model sunset — creates a documentation event that has not been named or addressed at Geneva boutiques. Client reports generated by the deprecated tool remain in client files and may be subject to client request, counterparty questionnaire, or regulatory examination. The documentation record refers to a system that no longer exists in its operating form, making verification impossible without a pre-deprecation protocol. The advisory gap is not general vendor management or business continuity; it is the specific documentation continuity problem for records generated by a system that has been discontinued. This is distinct from the model version tracking discussed in C248 (where the model updates continuously) — deprecation is a discrete terminal event with different documentation requirements. No Geneva competitor offers a deprecation documentation protocol as a discrete service.

**Finding 2: AI confidence language as an unexamined communication obligation distinct from disclosure and attribution**

AI-generated portfolio recommendations carry confidence language — certainty levels, probability expressions, strength indicators — that is produced by the AI model and embedded in client communications without being validated by the advisor's professional judgment. The client decision is influenced by AI-generated confidence signals that the advisor cannot currently audit, cannot defend against challenge, and may not be able to explain if asked. This is distinct from both the disclosure problem (whether the client knows AI was used) and the attribution problem (what the AI contributed): it is the question of what weight the AI's output certainty carries in the client's decision and who is accountable for that weight. The nFADP Article 21 right-to-explanation obligation may be implicated when AI-generated confidence language influences client decisions. No Geneva competitor addresses AI confidence language as a discrete communication accountability surface.

**Finding 3: Boutique AI signal correlation as an invisible market-level concentration risk not measured at boutique scale**

Geneva boutiques using similar AI portfolio analytics platforms — concentrated vendor market — may be receiving correlated signals and producing portfolio compositions that are converging without organizational awareness. This is distinct from the portfolio concentration risk addressed in prior cycles (which concerns concentration within a single firm's client portfolio). This is market-level concentration: correlated recommendations across peer firms using shared AI infrastructure. The problem is invisible from inside a single boutique, has no boutique-scale monitoring methodology, and creates systemic risk that has not been measured or named. The SFAMA dimension is specific: if portfolio convergence through shared AI infrastructure becomes visible at market level, boutique firms using the same platform may face questions about correlated exposure that the boutique itself cannot currently assess or answer.

**Finding 4: Operating/compliance timeline gap as a documentation design problem, not a documentation gap**

The gap between continuous AI operation and periodic compliance review creates a documentation surface — what the AI did between review cycles — that has no documentation architecture at boutique scale. This is not a documentation gap (which implies a missing document that could be produced) — it is a documentation design problem: the architecture for capturing continuous AI operation between compliance review points does not exist within current boutique compliance infrastructure. The compliance record documents what was reviewed at a point in time; it does not capture what happened during the intervals between reviews. The resolution is not a more complete periodic review — it is a continuous documentation architecture that maintains the connection between AI operation and compliance record in real time, not retrospectively.

**Finding 5: Portfolio attribution ownership as a discrete organizational role gap in Geneva boutiques**

When portfolio attribution questions arise — which signals contributed to this composition, how were they weighted, what changed — no defined ownership structure exists within the typical Geneva boutique. The compliance officer owns the tool approval. The portfolio advisor owns the recommendation. The managing director owns the client relationship. The attribution question crosses all three domains, and none of them holds attribution as a defined responsibility. This is distinct from the senior review protocol problem (which asks what substantive review requires) and from the signal-weighting attribution problem (which asks how weighting is documented): it is the organizational design problem of who is accountable for the firm's ability to answer attribution questions. No Geneva competitor addresses attribution ownership as a discrete organizational role question.

**Finding 6: Counterparty AI questionnaires as informal standard formation mechanism creating market-level disclosure norms**

Counterparty AI governance due diligence questionnaires are functioning as an informal standard formation mechanism: each questionnaire response — and each refusal to respond — contributes to market-level norms about what Geneva wealth management firms are prepared to disclose about their AI systems. The boutique must decide in real time how to respond to questions it has not prepared for, and those decisions shape the standard before it is formally defined. This is distinct from the EU counterparty pressure identified in C241 (which framed the questionnaire as a regulatory imposition). This framing sees the questionnaire as an active standard-formation event: the boutique's responses are contributing to an informal disclosure norm that will become the de facto standard before any regulatory guidance arrives. The competitive consequence: firms that respond substantively to questionnaires are defining the standard for those that do not.
