## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Single-model portfolio concentration as concentrated AI risk operating without a name at boutique scale**
Geneva boutique wealth managers typically deploy a single AI model architecture across their entire client book. The concentration is structural: it results from the economics of boutique-scale AI procurement, where one vendor relationship is manageable and multiple parallel AI systems are not. The concentration means that model behavior — directional positioning, risk assessment, rebalancing signals — propagates identically across all mandates simultaneously. The boutique is carrying a portfolio-wide exposure to a single analytical framework's failure mode. The exposure is not visible from inside any single client relationship. No Geneva market participant has named single-model concentration as a discrete risk category or produced a framework for managing portfolio-wide correlated AI risk at boutique scale. The assumption that consistent positioning is inherently safer has not been tested against the correlated failure scenario.

**Signal 2: AI vendor indemnification clause as the contractual mechanism no Geneva wealth manager evaluated at signature**
Vendor terms of service universally contain indemnification provisions drafted by the vendor's legal team. Wealth managers signing these agreements focus on data processing provisions and confidentiality terms. The indemnification clause — which determines who is liable for what when the vendor's tool produces adverse outcomes — is typically not reviewed by the wealth manager's counsel. The clause typically indemnifies the vendor against claims arising from how the client uses the tool, while remaining silent on indemnifying the client for consequences of the vendor's own tool behavior. The asymmetry is contractual and intentional. The wealth manager who signed the terms has committed to absorbing downstream consequences of AI behavior that the vendor designed the tool to produce. No Geneva market participant has surfaced this as a specific contractual exposure requiring review.

**Signal 3: AI-human simultaneous co-contribution as attribution problem distinct from reasoning chain reconstruction**
When a Geneva wealth manager delivers an AI-assisted recommendation, both the AI system and the human advisor are active simultaneously. The AI generates analytical output; the human integrates it with client objectives, market context, and professional judgment. The recommendation that emerges is the product of both. C268 established that the reasoning chain (how the system reached its conclusion) is distinct from the recommendation output. This finding addresses a different question: when a recommendation has both AI and human contributions operating simultaneously, what is the correct attribution? The law requires explanation of the reasoning, not attribution of causal contribution. But the MD's professional accountability requires knowing who or what produced the recommendation. These are different questions with different answers. No Geneva boutique has a methodology for making this attribution in real time, and the absence is not visible until a specific client question surfaces it.

**Signal 4: Institutional counterparty AI questionnaire answer inconsistency as cumulative undisclosed exposure**
Three or more institutional clients sending simultaneous AI governance questionnaires to the same Geneva boutique, answered by different personnel at different times, will produce inconsistent answers. The inconsistencies are not deliberate. They reflect natural variation in how different people interpret and respond to questions about the same AI system. The exposure is not that the firm answered incorrectly — it is that inconsistent answers across questionnaires addressing the same subject matter may constitute commercial representations that contradict each other. When an institutional client receives an answer that conflicts with what another institutional client received on the same question, the inconsistency may be discoverable through the commercial relationship or through regulatory review. No Geneva market participant has mapped the cumulative inconsistency exposure across multiple institutional counterparty questionnaires, and no response methodology exists for maintaining answer consistency across a client portfolio.

**Signal 5: MD professional liability for AI governance decisions as unnamed individual accountability category distinct from firm-level obligations**
Managing directors of Geneva boutique wealth management firms are personally named on every significant accountability instrument: FINMA attestations, carrier questionnaires, institutional client AI governance responses, ISSB sign-offs, and regulatory filings. Each instrument creates a different type of exposure. Some exposures attach to the firm as a corporate entity; some attach to the signing individual personally. The legal consequences differ: corporate liability is limited; personal liability is not. Professional liability exposure for the MD — the risk to the MD's personal professional standing, license, and assets — is distinct from the firm's corporate liability exposure. No Geneva market participant has separated these two exposure categories or identified which accountability instruments create personal MD liability versus firm liability. The signing decision is being made without this separation, and the MD is treating all signatures as equivalent risk events.

**Signal 6: nFADP data minimization and regulatory liability defense documentation as unresolved structural conflict with no Geneva protocol**
When a Geneva wealth management mandate terminates, two obligations come into direct tension. The nFADP requires data minimization: retain only what is strictly necessary, erase what is not. Liability defense documentation requires retaining records that document how the AI system operated during the mandate — what recommendations were made, what the AI contributed, what the human reviewed. These two obligations are structurally in conflict. Retaining what the nFADP requires you to erase may be necessary to defend against a future claim. Erasing what nFADP requires you to minimize may eliminate the record needed to respond to a regulatory inquiry. No Geneva market service has produced a protocol that resolves this tension. The existing approach — keep everything — satisfies neither framework and creates additional exposure. The conflict is real, unresolvable through a single framework, and present-tense for every mandate that has terminated or will terminate.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Single-model portfolio concentration risk framework for boutique wealth management**
No Geneva advisory has named single-model AI concentration as a discrete portfolio risk category at boutique scale. No framework exists for assessing correlated AI risk across a boutique wealth manager's full book, monitoring for concentrated directional exposure in a single analytical framework, or constructing portfolio-level oversight that accounts for the correlated failure properties of running one AI model across all mandates simultaneously. The competitive opportunity: a service that treats the boutique's entire AI model deployment as a single concentration risk to be managed, documented, and disclosed.

**Gap 2: AI vendor contract indemnification review and renegotiation methodology**
No Geneva market service offers a specific product for reviewing AI vendor terms of service through the indemnification lens — determining what the vendor is contractually obligated to absorb, what the wealth manager has committed to absorb, and where the contractual gap creates unallocated downstream liability. The renegotiation methodology — how to approach AI vendors about notification provisions, version documentation commitments, and liability allocation terms — does not exist in the market. The competitive opportunity: a vendor contract review focused specifically on the indemnification architecture of AI vendor agreements for wealth management use, with a negotiation methodology calibrated to vendor power dynamics.

**Gap 3: AI-human contribution attribution methodology for real-time recommendation delivery**
No Geneva service has produced a methodology that enables boutique wealth managers to attribute AI versus human contribution to specific recommendations at the moment of delivery. The methodology would need to work in real time, be defensible to clients and regulators, and account for the simultaneous co-contribution structure of AI-human collaboration in advisory. This is not the same as a reasoning chain reconstruction tool — it is a contribution attribution methodology for a different purpose: accountability assignment rather than explanation discharge. The competitive opportunity: a discrete product addressing the attribution question that arises at recommendation delivery, not after the fact.

**Gap 4: Institutional counterparty AI questionnaire consistency management across client portfolio**
No Geneva service has mapped the cumulative inconsistency exposure from multiple institutional counterparty questionnaires received by the same boutique. No methodology exists for maintaining answer consistency across questionnaires from different institutional clients, different timelines, and different personnel. The service would need to establish an internal AI governance answer standard, track which questions have been answered how across the client portfolio, flag inconsistencies before submission, and provide a methodology for updating prior answers when the AI system changes. The competitive opportunity: a discrete product addressing institutional questionnaire portfolio management as a distinct service category.

**Gap 5: Personal MD liability separation across simultaneous accountability instruments**
No Geneva service has separated the personal MD liability exposure from the firm-level corporate liability exposure across the cluster of accountability instruments that Geneva boutique wealth management MDs sign simultaneously. The separation requires understanding which instruments create personal exposure versus corporate exposure, what the magnitude of personal exposure is under each instrument, what professional liability coverage responds to each type of exposure, and what the MD's personal assets are exposed to in each scenario. This is a discrete product — not a legal opinion, but a liability separation framework specifically calibrated to the Geneva boutique wealth management MD role. The competitive opportunity: a named product addressing the MD's individual professional accountability for AI governance decisions as distinct from the firm's corporate accountability.

**Gap 6: Post-mandate nFADP-retention conflict resolution protocol**
No Geneva market service has produced a protocol for resolving the structural conflict between nFADP data minimization obligations and regulatory liability defense documentation retention requirements at mandate termination. The protocol would need to identify which records are subject to each obligation, where the obligations genuinely conflict, where they can be reconciled, and how to document the resolution in a way that satisfies both frameworks simultaneously. The competitive opportunity: a named protocol for a conflict that exists for every mandate that terminates, with no resolution methodology currently available.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA and the single-model concentration question:**
FINMA's suitability obligations are structured around individual mandate oversight. The question of whether a boutique's use of a single AI model architecture across its full book creates a portfolio-level supervisory concern has not been addressed in FINMA guidance. FINMA has not examined whether correlated AI-driven positioning across all client mandates simultaneously constitutes a separate suitability issue distinct from individual mandate suitability. The concentration question sits in a supervisory gap: individual suitability documentation does not capture book-wide concentration, and no framework exists for assessing the correlated failure property of a single-model architecture across a boutique portfolio.

**FIDLEG and the AI-human co-contribution attribution obligation:**
FIDLEG Article 26 requires disclosure when investment advice is based on automated processing. The disclosure obligation does not specify how to attribute causal contribution between the human advisor and the AI system when both have contributed simultaneously to the recommendation. The law asks for explanation of the algorithmic basis; it does not ask for attribution of which component produced which part of the recommendation. These are different questions. The compliance gap is that FIDLEG disclosure addresses the existence of automated processing, not the internal attribution of contribution within a human-AI collaborative recommendation. The MD's professional accountability — understanding what the recommendation actually is and being able to stand behind it — requires a different type of analysis than what FIDLEG discharge requires.

**ASIP/BVG and the AI governance documentation for pension-affected advisory:**
ASIP has not published guidance on what AI governance documentation standards apply to wealth management advisory that incorporates BVG pension entitlements. When AI-assisted recommendations affect the pension component of a client's overall wealth plan, ASIP's oversight reaches into the advisory process without a defined documentation standard. The documentation gap is acute for the intersection: the pension layer creates obligations under a framework that has not addressed AI documentation standards, while the advisory layer creates obligations under FINMA and FIDLEG frameworks that have. No framework resolves the documentation standard for the intersection.

**SFAMA and the boutique wealth management AI documentation standard:**
SFAMA's circular on AI in asset management specifies documentation expectations for fund managers. The application to boutique wealth management advisory is contested and unnamed. The documentation standard exists; whether it applies to the advisory segment, what it would require at boutique scale, and how to reconcile it with FINMA's advisory documentation expectations remain unresolved. Carriers and institutional counterparties are beginning to probe whether boutiques apply a documentation standard — the SFAMA circular functions as a reference point even for firms that believe it does not apply to them.

**ISSB and the post-mandate sustainability documentation retention question:**
ISSB-aligned client reports produced during a mandate create documentation obligations that survive mandate termination. The ISSB framework requires maintaining records that support the sustainability disclosures delivered to clients. When the mandate terminates, the sustainability documentation must be retained — but for how long, in what form, and under what standard, ISSB does not specify for the mandate termination context. The nFADP erasure obligations apply to the client personal data underlying those reports. The intersection — retaining ISSB documentation that necessarily contains client personal data, while erasing that data under nFADP — is unresolved. No Geneva authority has addressed what ISSB documentation retention means in the context of nFADP data minimization obligations for terminated mandates.

**nFADP Articles 6, 13, and 21 and the attribution/retention compound tension:**
nFADP Article 6 requires a lawful basis for processing client portfolio data in AI systems. Article 21 requires explanation of the logic underlying automated decisions affecting clients. Article 13 requires that data subjects can access and understand how automated decisions were made about them. These three provisions intersect at the attribution question: explaining what the AI contributed requires knowing what the AI contributed. When the AI and human produced the recommendation simultaneously, reconstructing the specific contribution of each requires documentation that was not built for that purpose. The retention obligation — keeping records long enough to discharge Article 13 and Article 21 obligations — collides with the minimization obligation: keeping records longer than necessary violates Article 13's principle that data should not be retained beyond its purpose. The three provisions create a compound tension for which no Geneva authority has provided resolution methodology.

## 06 — Findings Summary: 6 Net-New Insights, Distinct from C214–C269 Phase 02

**Finding 01: Single-model portfolio concentration as a discrete correlated AI risk category distinct from systemic accumulation framing**
C269 Finding 01 named portfolio-wide AI model concentration as an accumulating undisclosed systemic exposure. This finding sharpens the mechanism: the exposure is not merely accumulating — it is a specific property of single-model architecture that creates correlated directional positioning across the full client book simultaneously. The boutique is carrying a portfolio-wide concentration in one analytical framework's failure mode. The concentration is structural, resulting from the economics of boutique-scale AI procurement where maintaining multiple parallel AI systems is not viable. The correlated failure property — model error in one direction affecting all mandates simultaneously — is not visible from inside any single client relationship and is not captured by individual mandate suitability documentation. FINMA's supervisory framework does not address single-model concentration as a discrete risk category. No Geneva market participant has named single-model concentration as a correlated AI risk requiring its own assessment methodology. This is distinct from C269 Finding 01 in mechanism: C269 addressed accumulation over time; this finding addresses the specific structural property of single-architecture concentration that makes the accumulation a correlated risk, not merely a volume risk.

**Finding 02: AI vendor indemnification clause as the unnamed contractual mechanism creating unallocated downstream liability**
C269 Finding 02 named silent model updates and liability allocation as an unnamed commercial exposure with no contractual framework. This finding identifies the specific contractual mechanism: the indemnification clause in vendor terms of service, drafted by the vendor's legal team, which indemnifies the vendor against claims arising from how clients use the tool while remaining silent on indemnifying clients against consequences of the vendor's own tool behavior. The wealth manager signed the terms without evaluating the indemnification architecture. The contractual asymmetry is intentional and present-tense. Downstream liability for AI tool behavior falls on the party with the least information about and least leverage over the tool's design and behavior. No Geneva market participant has surfaced the indemnification clause as a specific contractual exposure requiring review, nor has any renegotiation methodology been produced for approaching AI vendors about notification provisions and liability allocation terms. The competitive opportunity — vendor contract indemnification review as a discrete product — is distinct from C269 Finding 02 in that it names the specific contractual mechanism (indemnification clause architecture) rather than the broader liability allocation gap.

**Finding 03: AI-human simultaneous co-contribution as attribution problem structurally distinct from reasoning chain reconstruction**
C268 Finding 02 established that the AI reasoning chain and the AI recommendation are structurally distinct accountability objects — the former is the logical path, the latter is the conclusion. This finding addresses a different question: when both AI and human judgment operate simultaneously to produce a recommendation, what is the correct causal attribution of contribution? This is not the reasoning chain question — it is not asking what logical steps the system took. It is asking what causal role each contributor played in the delivered recommendation. The law (FIDLEG Art.26, nFADP Art.21) requires explaining the algorithmic basis and the logic of automated decisions. It does not require attributing causal contribution between the AI system and the human advisor within a collaborative recommendation. The MD's professional accountability requires knowing what produced the recommendation in order to stand behind it. These are different questions with different answers. No Geneva boutique has a real-time attribution methodology for simultaneous co-contribution. The exposure surfaces when a client asks specifically how much of the recommendation was the AI versus the human advisor — a question that arrives before the boutique has a methodology to answer it accurately. This is distinct from C268 Finding 02 in object: C268 addressed the reasoning chain as a disclosure artifact; this finding addresses the attribution of causal contribution as an accountability assignment question.

**Finding 04: Institutional counterparty AI questionnaire answer inconsistency as cumulative undisclosed commercial representation exposure**
C268 Finding 01 identified institutional AI governance questionnaires as a secondary regulatory channel operating outside FINMA visibility and creating legal weight in commercial relationships. This finding addresses a specific structural consequence of that channel that C268 did not name: when the same boutique receives multiple institutional questionnaires from different clients, answered by different personnel at different times, the answers will not be perfectly consistent. The inconsistencies are not deliberate — they reflect natural variation in interpretation and response. But inconsistent answers on the same subject matter across multiple questionnaires may constitute commercial representations that contradict each other, creating exposure under commercial law principles of consistent representation. No Geneva market participant has mapped the cumulative inconsistency exposure across multiple institutional questionnaires, established an internal answer standard for consistency, or produced a methodology for tracking and flagging inconsistencies before submission. The service gap is specific: institutional questionnaire portfolio management as a distinct advisory product category. This is distinct from C268 Finding 01 in consequence: C268 identified the channel and its legal weight; this finding names the specific cumulative inconsistency consequence of that channel operating across a portfolio of institutional clients.

**Finding 05: MD professional liability for AI governance decisions as unnamed individual accountability category distinct from firm-level obligations**
C269 Finding 05 addressed personal MD liability across simultaneous accountability instruments, naming the absence of separation methodology across FINMA attestations, carrier questionnaires, institutional counterparty responses, and ISSB sign-offs. This finding takes a different angle: the unnamed category is not merely the absence of a separation methodology — it is the absence of a named individual accountability category for the MD's personal professional liability that is distinct from the firm's corporate liability. Corporate liability is limited by the corporate form; personal professional liability is not. The MD's professional license, personal assets, and professional standing are exposed by some instruments and not others, in magnitudes that differ across instruments, and with coverage that responds differently across each. No Geneva market participant has identified the MD's personal professional liability for AI governance decisions as a discrete accountability category requiring its own analysis separate from firm-level corporate liability. This is distinct from C269 Finding 05 in framing: C269 framed the finding as an absence of separation methodology; this finding identifies the specific unnamed individual accountability category that the separation methodology would need to separate from the corporate category.

**Finding 06: nFADP data minimization and regulatory liability defense documentation as unresolved structural conflict with no Geneva protocol**
C269 Finding 03 named post-mandate AI documentation as a multi-framework retention tension across FINMA, nFADP, and ISSB. This finding sharpens the specific nature of the tension: it is not merely that the frameworks point in different directions — it is that nFADP's minimization obligation and regulatory liability defense documentation retention are in direct structural conflict for the same records. Minimization requires erasing client personal data when it is no longer necessary. Liability defense requires retaining records documenting how the AI system operated during the mandate. These are not merely different evidentiarystandards — they are irreconcilable without a protocol that distinguishes between records that serve regulatory retention purposes and records that constitute client personal data. The nFADP provides no exception for regulatory documentation. No Geneva authority has addressed whether regulatory retention obligations override nFADP minimization obligations, or how to document the resolution when they do not. The nFADP Arts. 6, 13, and 21 compound the tension: Article 13 requires that data subjects can access automated decision records, but the data minimization principle limits how long those records can be retained. The three provisions create a compound obligation that no single framework can resolve. This is distinct from C269 Finding 03 in specificity: C269 identified the multi-framework retention tension; this finding names the specific structural conflict between nFADP data minimization and regulatory liability defense documentation as the irreconcilable core of that tension, with the additional compound obligation from nFADP Arts. 6, 13, and 21 that C269 did not address.
