## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Version-gated client accountability — the model version question arriving in real client conversations**
Managing directors in Geneva boutiques are now being asked by clients which AI model version generated their reports. This is not a hypothetical regulatory scenario — it is a present-tense client conversation. The boutique cannot answer it because internal version tracking does not exist. The problem is not that the information is unavailable from the vendor; the problem is that the firm has no internal record architecture to hold it, and no review process that would surface it before the client asks. The version question is the first point at which clients are testing the firm's actual AI governance depth, and boutiques are failing it in real time.

**Signal 2: Approval-document vs. operating-reality gap — the undisclosed structural exposure**
Compliance officers hold a documented approval for the AI system at a point in time. Between review cycles, the AI system continues operating and being updated by the vendor. The approval document covers a version that no longer exists in its original form. No Geneva boutique has explicitly mapped the gap between the scope of its approval and the actual operation of the system between review cycles. This is not a documentation gap — it is a structural exposure: the approval does not cover what the system is actually doing at any given moment.

**Signal 3: Portfolio composition reasoning opacity — weighting invisible at the client level**
When a portfolio advisor is asked to explain how portfolio composition was determined, the AI assembled the composition from multiple signals. The advisor can describe what each signal was supposed to do in general. The advisor cannot describe with precision how the AI weighted those signals for the specific client at the specific time. The difference between signal intent and signal weighting in a specific case is the difference between a correct answer and an explainable answer. Clients are beginning to notice this distinction. It is not a communications problem — it is a structural opacity in the AI's operation that cannot be resolved by better client communication.

**Signal 4: Alert reasoning inaccessibility at the moment of action**
When an AI produces a position limit breach alert, the reasoning chain that generated the alert lives in the model's internal processing and is not accessible at the moment of action. The firm acts on the alert but cannot access the specific reasoning that produced it. This creates a compound problem: the firm is operationally dependent on AI alerts it cannot explain at the moment it is relying on them, and there is no record of what the alert was based on that can be reconstructed later. The alert system is functioning correctly from a technical standpoint and failing from a governance standpoint simultaneously.

**Signal 5: Continuous update vs. periodic review — the silent mismatch creating ongoing undocumented exposure**
Geneva boutiques review AI tools annually. AI model vendors update continuously. Between annual reviews, the boutique operates with a version of the AI that has never been reviewed under the firm's approval framework. This mismatch is known at the compliance officer level but has not been addressed explicitly in any boutique's governance architecture. The annual review produces a compliance record that does not reflect the actual state of the AI during the year. The compliance record is accurate at the moment of signing and immediately begins diverging from operating reality.

**Signal 6: Signature adequacy as distinct from review substance — the invisible compliance surface**
When a compliance officer signs a review record, the signature certifies that review happened. It does not certify what was evaluated, at what depth, against what standard, with what conclusion. The compliance record shows compliance activity occurred. It does not show compliance was substantively meaningful. This distinction is beginning to matter in a specific way: when a boutique must demonstrate its review process to a counterparty, a regulator, or a client, the record shows a signature and a date. Whether the review that produced that signature was adequate is a question the record does not answer and the compliance officer may not be able to answer.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### What the Market Is Addressing

Current AI governance offerings for Geneva finance boutiques address:
- Initial AI tool selection and vendor due diligence
- Annual compliance review documentation
- nFADP data processing registers and lawful basis documentation
- FINMA compliance gap assessments
- Senior review protocol templates
- Model update notification workflows
- General AI governance policy documentation

These offerings treat AI governance as a discrete project — a tool is selected, a policy is written, an annual review is documented. This model fails to address the continuous operation problem: AI systems operate between review cycles, are updated continuously by vendors, and produce outputs that the firm's documentation architecture was not designed to capture.

### What No One Is Addressing

**Gap 1: Version-state documentation architecture**
No Geneva competitor offers a version-state documentation architecture — a system for maintaining a continuous record of which AI model version is active, which version was active during a specific client reporting period, and what changed between versions. This is distinct from model update notification (which notifies the firm of a change) and from the annual review (which evaluates a version). Version-state documentation is a continuous record that allows the firm to answer the client question ("which version generated my report") and the compliance question ("what were we operating under at any given point"). No boutique has this architecture, and no Geneva advisor is offering it as a specific service.

**Gap 2: Operating-scope approval mapping**
No competitor addresses the gap between the scope of a compliance officer's approval and the actual operating scope of the AI system between review cycles. The approval document covers a version; the system operates under a different version for most of its operational life. This structural mismatch creates a specific exposure: the compliance record documents approval of something that no longer exists in its approved form. Resolving this requires a framework that defines what continuous approval looks like — not a one-time approval with a periodic review, but an ongoing documentation architecture that maintains the connection between approval scope and operating reality.

**Gap 3: Signal-weighting attribution for client-level explanations**
No competitor offers a methodology for portfolio composition attribution that addresses the signal-weighting problem — the gap between what each signal was supposed to do and how the AI actually weighted those signals for a specific client at a specific time. This is not a communications challenge. It is a structural opacity in AI portfolio construction that boutique advisors cannot close by improving their explanations. The problem requires a technical documentation architecture that records how the AI weighted signals in each composition decision — not just what the signals were, but what weight each received in the specific case. No Geneva framework addresses this.

**Gap 4: Alert reasoning reconstruction architecture**
No Geneva competitor addresses the specific problem of alert reasoning inaccessibility — the fact that AI-generated alerts arrive without the reasoning chain that produced them, at exactly the moment when the firm needs to act on them. The firm is operationally dependent on AI alerts it cannot explain in real time, and has no record reconstruction methodology for later determining what the alert was based on. This is distinct from general AI explainability (a large research literature exists) and from the specific boutique need: a bounded, implementable system for documenting alert reasoning at the moment of generation, not in retrospect. No Geneva advisory offering addresses this as a discrete service.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### FINMA

**FINMA Circular 2016/1 — Supervisory Requirements for Financial Intermediaries**
The 2023 updated circular requires that financial intermediaries maintain adequate risk management frameworks for outsourced activities. AI system operation increasingly falls within this scope as AI is embedded in portfolio construction, risk monitoring, and client communication processes. The circular's outsourcing provisions require that firms maintain oversight of AI operations, not only of AI tool selection. The practical implication: a compliance officer's signature on an annual review is insufficient if the AI system operated continuously between reviews without oversight. The obligation is for continuous oversight, not periodic documentation.

**FINMA Guidance on Model Risk Management**
FINMA's supervisory approach to AI model risk does not prescribe a specific methodology but expects firms to have a documented framework for model validation, model inventory, and model performance monitoring. For Geneva boutiques, the practical requirement is a model inventory that tracks version state continuously, not just at annual review points. The gap between annual review documentation and continuous model operation is not addressed by current market offerings, but it is the specific area where FINMA supervisory scrutiny is moving.

**FINMA Enforcement Posture**
FINMA's enforcement actions in AI-related areas have focused on cases where AI system operation produced outcomes inconsistent with client disclosure obligations and suitability requirements. The enforcement pattern is not about whether the firm used AI — it is about whether the firm's documentation demonstrates that the AI's operation was understood, reviewed, and consistent with the firm's stated advisory approach. The documentation that protects a boutique in a FINMA examination is not the policy document; it is the record of continuous oversight and the evidence that review was substantive, not procedural.

### ASIP (Association Suisse des Institutions de Prévoyance)

**Pension Fund AI Asset Allocation — Documentation Obligations**
ASIP guidance requires that pension fund asset allocation decisions be documented with sufficient reasoning to demonstrate that fiduciary obligations were discharged. When AI tools contribute to asset allocation decisions, the documentation must capture the AI's contribution to the decision — not just the outcome. The signal-weighting opacity problem (Section 03, Gap 3) is directly relevant here: a pension fund trustee cannot demonstrate fiduciary compliance for a decision where the AI's weighting of factors is not recorded.

**ASIP Best Practice on Technological Innovation**
ASIP's position on AI adoption encourages innovation while maintaining that fiduciary responsibility cannot be delegated to technology. The practical implication: pension fund managers using AI must be able to demonstrate they understood the AI's reasoning, not only that the AI produced an acceptable outcome. This creates a specific documentation requirement that most pension fund AI governance frameworks do not address.

### SFAMA (Swiss Funds and Asset Management Association)

**AI in Portfolio Management — SFAMA Guidance**
SFAMA guidance on AI-assisted portfolio management addresses disclosure obligations and suitability documentation. For Geneva boutiques, the relevant requirement is that AI contribution to portfolio construction must be traceable — the firm must be able to demonstrate what the AI did, what the human advisor did, and how the final recommendation was constructed. This is the attribution problem that current market offerings do not resolve at the operational level.

**SFAMA on Model Governance**
SFAMA member surveys indicate that firms increasingly recognize AI model governance as an operational necessity, but report that practical implementation frameworks are lacking. The gap between recognized obligation and implementable methodology is where boutique-scale advisory is needed.

### ISSB (International Sustainability Standards Board)

**ISSB Standards and AI-Generated Sustainability Data**
The ISSB's IFRS S1 and S2 standards require disclosure of climate-related risks and opportunities, including the data sources and methodologies used to generate sustainability claims. When AI tools generate sustainability data that enters client reports and regulatory filings, the AI's methodology, version history, and data sources become relevant to ISSB compliance. The mid-2027 window for ISSB-aligned reporting creates a specific documentation requirement that most Geneva boutiques have not begun to address: AI-generated sustainability data must be traceable to verifiable sources and documented methodologies, not just to AI tool outputs.

**FINMA/ISSB Compound Exposure Window**
The Q1 2027 FINMA supervisory examination window and the mid-2027 ISSB reporting deadline create a compound exposure for boutiques that use AI tools for both investment decision support and sustainability disclosure generation. The same AI system may be generating outputs that must meet both FINMA suitability documentation standards and ISSB methodology disclosure requirements simultaneously. No current market offering addresses this specific intersection for boutique-scale wealth management.

### nFADP (Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection)

**Article 8 — Purpose Limitation and Data Minimization**
AI portfolio construction systems process client financial data to generate allocation recommendations and behavioral inferences. The nFADP purpose limitation principle requires that data processing be limited to the purpose for which it was collected. When AI systems infer behavioral risk profiles from trading patterns, the lawful basis for that inference — whether it constitutes profiling, whether it requires explicit consent, whether the purpose limitation is satisfied — is not addressed in most boutique documentation architectures. The gap between AI operational data processing and documented lawful basis is a present-tense nFADP exposure.

**Article 21 — Right to Explanation**
Clients have the right to request an explanation of automated decisions that affect them significantly. AI-generated portfolio composition decisions are likely to fall within this scope. The signal-weighting opacity problem (Section 03, Gap 3) directly affects the boutique's ability to comply with Article 21: if the AI's weighting of signals cannot be reconstructed for a specific client, the firm cannot provide the explanation the article requires. The compliance response cannot be a general policy statement; it requires a specific reconstruction capability that most boutiques do not have.

**Article 6 — Processing of Data Relating to Financial Position**
nFADP provisions on financial data processing create specific documentation requirements for AI systems that process client financial position data. Version-state documentation (Gap 1 in Section 03) is relevant here: when a client exercises Article 21 rights, the firm must be able to identify which version of the AI processed their data, what that version's processing methodology was, and how it affected the specific decision. Without version-state documentation, this cannot be answered.

## 06 — Findings Summary

*Six net-new insights, distinct from C214–C247 Phase 02*

**Finding 1: Version-state documentation as discrete boutique-scale service category**
The model version question ("which version generated my last quarterly report") has arrived in real client conversations at Geneva boutiques — not as a regulatory scenario, but as an actual question asked by actual clients. The firm's inability to answer it is not a communications failure; it is a structural documentation gap. The version-state documentation architecture required to answer this question is distinct from model update notification (which notifies the firm) and from annual review (which evaluates a version at a point in time). It is a continuous record architecture that no Geneva boutique has and no Geneva advisor is offering as a specific service. This is a specific, implementable advisory product with a clear deliverable and a clear client need driving demand.

**Finding 2: Operating-scope approval divergence as structural compliance exposure**
The compliance officer's approval document covers a version of the AI system that no longer exists in its original form. Between annual reviews, the AI system operates under vendor updates that have not been reviewed under the firm's approval framework. The compliance record documents approval of something that has changed since the approval was given. This is not a documentation gap — it is a structural exposure: the approval does not cover what the system is actually doing at most points during its operational life. The resolution is not a more detailed approval document; it is a continuous approval architecture that maintains the connection between approval scope and operating reality. This distinction matters for how compliance frameworks are designed and for what a compliance officer can defensibly represent to a regulator.

**Finding 3: Signal-weighting attribution as the specific form of AI portfolio explainability that clients are actually testing**
The AI portfolio explainability problem is not primarily about whether the AI was used — that question is settled. It is about how the AI weighted signals in a specific composition decision. The advisor can describe what each signal was supposed to do in general. The advisor cannot describe how the AI weighted those signals in the specific client's case. This is the specific point at which client trust is being tested in real conversations. The distinction between signal intent and signal weighting in a specific case is the gap that standard AI governance documentation does not close and that boutique clients are beginning to notice. The resolution requires a technical documentation architecture that records weighting decisions at the time of composition, not a better explanation strategy after the fact.

**Finding 4: Alert reasoning inaccessibility as compound operational and governance failure**
AI-generated alerts arrive without the reasoning chain that produced them, at the moment when the firm must act on them. The firm is operationally dependent on alerts it cannot explain in real time, and has no reconstruction methodology for later determining what the alert was based on. This creates two distinct failures simultaneously: operational dependency on an opaque system, and absence of any record that would allow reconstruction before the trail disappears. The problem is distinct from general AI explainability research (which addresses model interpretability in the abstract) and from the specific boutique need: a bounded, implementable system for documenting alert reasoning at the moment of generation. The 48-72 hour reconstruction window that most firms face after an alert-related incident is not an operational challenge — it is a governance failure that has operational consequences.

**Finding 5: Signature adequacy vs. review substance as the compliance documentation quality distinction**
The compliance record shows that a review happened — a signature and a date. It does not show what was evaluated, at what depth, against what standard, with what conclusion. The distinction between signature adequacy (the review occurred) and review substance (the review was meaningful and reached an defensible conclusion) is the quality distinction that compliance officers in Geneva boutiques are beginning to confront in counterparty questionnaires and client requests for documentation of the firm's AI governance process. A signature without a substantive record does not protect the firm in a dispute, a supervisory examination, or a client relationship challenge. The substance of what was reviewed is not visible in the documentation; it must be designed into the documentation architecture before the review occurs. This is a product design problem for compliance documentation, not a compliance officer competence problem.

**Finding 6: FINMA-nFADP compound documentation requirement at the AI-sustainability intersection**
The Q1 2027 FINMA supervisory examination window and the mid-2027 ISSB reporting deadline create a specific documentation requirement that most Geneva boutiques have not identified: AI tools used for both investment decision support and sustainability disclosure generation may be generating outputs that must simultaneously satisfy FINMA suitability documentation standards and ISSB methodology disclosure requirements. The AI system's version history, data sources, and processing methodology are relevant to both frameworks, but no documentation architecture currently exists that connects these requirements for boutique-scale AI operations. The compound exposure is not the sum of two separate compliance obligations — it is a specific documentation architecture problem: one AI system producing outputs that must be documented for two different regulatory purposes, with different but overlapping documentation requirements, at the same time. This is distinct from the ISSB documentation gap identified in prior cycles because it addresses the compound FINMA-ISSB window specifically, and because it identifies the documentation architecture gap (not the regulatory knowledge gap) as the primary operational problem.
