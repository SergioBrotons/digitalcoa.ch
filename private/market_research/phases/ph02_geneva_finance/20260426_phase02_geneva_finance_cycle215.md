## 01 — ICP Language

### How the ICP Actually Describes Their AI Problems — Real Language, EN + FR

The managing director at a boutique Geneva wealth management firm does not use the phrase "AI governance." They describe a practical, operational tension between what AI could do for the business and what they are permitted to do with it — under FINMA, under client confidentiality obligations, and under their own professional liability.

**Core concerns in their own register:**

- "The client expects the report by tomorrow. AI could draft it in twenty minutes. But I cannot put their portfolio data through a system I do not fully control." — The tension between speed and control is constant. They see the efficiency case clearly. The data sovereignty question stops them.

- "My client asked me directly whether we use AI in managing their account. I had no prepared answer. That is a problem." — Client-initiated AI questions are arriving faster than firms have prepared responses. The absence of a prepared answer is itself a risk signal — it implies informal, undisclosed AI use.

- "My seniors use AI for certain tasks. My juniors use AI for certain tasks. Nobody has documented what we are doing and why." — The informal diffusion of AI across the firm, particularly across seniority levels, creates a documentation problem that compounds silently. No one is responsible for what no one owns.

- "I know we are outsourcing our cognition when we use these tools. I just do not know where that becomes a regulatory problem." — This is the more precise way the sophisticated ICP frames it. They understand they are delegating not just processing but judgment to an external system. They do not know how FINMA categorizes that delegation.

- "We have a data processing agreement with one vendor. We have not reviewed the others. I suspect we are not fully covered." — Vendor proliferation within a single firm is creating a patchwork of data handling arrangements that no one has audited. The compliance gap is practical, not theoretical.

- "If something goes wrong with an AI recommendation, I need to be able to show what the system considered, what it discarded, and why I overrode or accepted its output. I do not have that." — The provenance trail requirement — not just the recommendation, but the reasoning path — is the missing documentation element. It is also what makes an AI-assisted recommendation defensible.

**What they do not say they need:**
- They do not say "we need an AI strategy"
- They do not say "we need a digital transformation roadmap"
- They do not say "we need to adopt AI to remain competitive"
- They say: "I need to know what I am allowed to do, and I need to be able to document why I did it"

## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

**Failure Mode 1 — The Vendor Concentration Problem**
A single Geneva boutique wealth manager is typically using three to five AI-adjacent tools: one for report drafting, one for portfolio data summarization, one for compliance document review, one for internal research. Each was adopted at a different time, by a different person, with different data handling terms. The firm has no consolidated view of what data is leaving the firm, to which vendor, under what terms. The compliance team — two people, if it exists as a dedicated function — cannot audit a vendor portfolio it did not know was assembled.

The specific failure here is not malicious vendor behavior. It is organizational: AI tool adoption has outpaced AI governance documentation. The firm's AI surface area is larger than its governance surface area.

**Failure Mode 2 — The Client-Asked-First Problem**
UHNW and HNW clients are arriving at meetings having read about AI in financial services. Some have asked their Geneva advisor directly: do you use AI in managing my account? The firm that cannot answer this question coherently has a client trust and a disclosure problem simultaneously. The disclosure problem is not just about what the AI does — it is about whether the client expects to be asked for consent before their data is processed through an AI system.

The failure mode: the firm has not thought through what disclosure is required, so it either says nothing (and later discovers the client assumed consent was implicit) or says something vague (and creates a record of incomplete disclosure that could become problematic if the client later questions the recommendation).

**Failure Mode 3 — The Cognitive Offshoring Paradox**
Geneva wealth managers who have adopted AI tools describe a specific cognitive shift: they are outsourcing not just data processing but judgment formation. The AI tool does not just compute — it suggests, frames, ranks, and implicitly recommends. The advisor who receives these outputs and endorses them is effectively operating as an AI ratifier.

The paradox: the more useful the AI tool, the more the advisor's role shifts from generating judgment to validating AI output. This creates an accountability inversion — the advisor is responsible for a recommendation they did not originate. Most firms have not mapped this shift in their internal documentation, risk frameworks, or compliance protocols.

**Failure Mode 4 — The Seniority Displacement Problem**
In many boutique Geneva firms, junior staff have adopted AI tools faster than senior advisors. The junior person uses AI for report drafting, market research, portfolio summarization. The senior person reviews the output and signs off, without knowing what AI was used or what inputs were processed. This creates a governance inversion: the person with the least organizational accountability is the person with the most direct AI interface.

The failure mode: when something goes wrong — a client complaint, a FINMA review, a data incident — the accountability chain runs backward through people who did not make the AI decision and did not document it.

**Failure Mode 5 — The Regulatory Classification Uncertainty Problem**
FINMA has not published explicit guidance on whether AI-assisted portfolio analysis constitutes "automated individual advice" under existing advisory rules. The classification question matters: if it is automated individual advice, the firm needs a specific license and compliance framework. If it is a productivity tool for the advisor, different rules apply. Firms are operating in a classification gap. Some have assumed the classification that requires less compliance work. None have a documented basis for that assumption.

**Failure Mode 6 — The Documentation Debt Spiral**
The documentation requirement for AI-assisted recommendations is not a one-time project. Every AI-assisted recommendation generates documentation obligations that compound over time: the input data, the AI output, the advisor's decision, the client's acceptance. Firms that have not built structured documentation workflows are generating documentation debt that grows faster than their capacity to service it. The spiral continues silently until a client dispute, a FINMA review, or a professional liability claim makes the debt visible.

**Market paradox — AI adoption accelerates while governance decelerates:**
The firms that have adopted AI most aggressively are, in many cases, the ones with the least governance infrastructure. The efficiency gain is real and immediate. The compliance debt is invisible and deferred. The moment the debt becomes visible — a regulatory trigger, a client incident, a liability claim — is the moment the firm discovers the gap between adoption speed and governance maturity.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations — FINMA, ASIP, SFAMA, ISSB, nFADP

**FINMA regulatory framework for AI in wealth management:**

**FINMA Circ. 2018/3 — Outsourcing (applied to AI tools):**
FINMA has not published a dedicated AI outsourcing circular. Existing Circ. 2018/3 on outsourcing is applied analogically to AI tools. Key implications for boutique Geneva wealth managers:
- AI tool vendors are de facto service providers under outsourcing rules
- Data processing agreements, audit rights, and business continuity provisions are required
- The firm remains accountable to FINMA for outsourced functions including AI-assisted advisory and reporting
- If AI tools support core investment decision processes, enhanced oversight requirements apply

**FINMA AI Model Risk Classification — Tier Structure (expected Q4 2026):**
FINMA is developing a three-tier AI model risk classification:
- **Tier 1 (high-impact):** AI directly produces or materially influences investment decisions — highest documentation and validation requirements
- **Tier 2 (medium-impact):** AI-assisted compliance, AML AI, customer-facing AI tools — moderate documentation requirements
- **Tier 3 (low-impact):** Internal productivity tools — minimal documentation requirements

Boutique Geneva wealth managers deploying AI for portfolio analysis and client reporting may fall into Tier 1 or Tier 2 depending on how the AI output is used. The classification uncertainty is itself a risk: firms deploying AI before the classification is published may face retrospective documentation requirements.

**Automated Individual Advice — Classification Question:**
FINMA has not explicitly classified whether AI-assisted portfolio analysis tools constitute "automated individual advice" under existing advisory licensing requirements. The classification determines whether the firm needs a specific automated advisory license or whether AI tools can operate as advisor productivity tools under existing advisory licenses. This is an open regulatory question with compliance implications.

**AI Advisory Suitability — Audit Program (expected Q1 2027):**
FINMA is developing an AI advisory suitability audit program. AI-driven portfolio recommendations must meet client suitability documentation standards, with AI explainability requirements. The audit program creates a concrete compliance deadline: Q1 2027. Firms without structured AI recommendation documentation in place by that date face audit exposure.

**ASIP — Occupational Pension Fund Governance (BVG/BVV2):**
Geneva wealth managers managing occupational pension fund mandates face ASIP documentation requirements:
- AI-assisted investment decisions on pension fund mandates require documented decision trails
- AI tools used in pension fund portfolio analysis must meet ASIP governance standards
- ASIP implementation guidance (Q2 2026): specific timelines for AI governance documentation for pension fund assets under management
- Pension fund client confidentiality and data processing requirements add an additional layer to AI tool vendor assessment

**SFAMA — Swiss Fund and Asset Management Association:**
Fund management companies affiliated with SFAMA face:
- SFAMA guidelines on AI in fund management: documented model governance for AI-assisted portfolio management
- AI model validation expectations for AI-driven asset allocation
- Client disclosure requirements for AI-assisted fund management recommendations
- Documentation requirements for AI tools used in fund performance reporting

**ISSB-aligned Disclosure Standards — Federal Council Endorsement March 2026:**
Federal Council endorsed ISSB-aligned disclosure standards. FY2026 Swiss annual reports must incorporate AI-enabled data collection for sustainability disclosures. Geneva wealth managers with fund management mandates and AUM reporting obligations need AI-enabled data collection infrastructure for mid-2027 disclosure deadlines.

**nFADP — Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection:**
- Art. 12: automated decision-making disclosure obligations — clients have the right to know when AI systems make or materially influence decisions about them
- Data processing agreement requirements for AI tool vendors: client portfolio data processed through AI tools requires documented lawful basis
- Client consent requirements: "automated decision with legal effect" disclosure, client right to human review
- FDPIC enforcement powers: FDPIC can investigate AI systems under nFADP Art. 12 obligations

**What this means in practice:**
The boutique Geneva wealth manager faces a layered compliance environment that requires simultaneous navigation of: FINMA outsourcing rules as applied to AI, the forthcoming FINMA AI model risk classification, AI advisory suitability requirements, ISSB disclosure deadlines, ASIP pension fund documentation, SFAMA fund management guidelines, and nFADP automated decision disclosure obligations. These obligations interact and overlap. There is no single authoritative Geneva-specific translation of these obligations into a practical deployment guide for a 50–200 person wealth management firm.

## 06 — Findings Summary

### 6 Net-New Findings — Distinct from C214 Phase 02

**Finding 1 — AI tool proliferation is outpacing AI governance documentation within individual firms**
The boutique Geneva wealth manager is not facing an adoption gap — they are facing a governance inventory problem. Most firms already have three to five AI-adjacent tools operating across their team, adopted informally at different times by different people, with non-standardized data handling terms. The compliance risk is not that no AI is in use; it is that the AI in use is not coherently documented. The advisory entry point: not "adopt AI governance" but "document what you already have." The inventory question is the first compliance question.

*(Distinct from C214 Finding 1: C214 identified the accountability gap as the psychological center — the emotional worry. C215 identifies the specific mechanism: tool proliferation without documentation is the operational reality that creates the accountability gap.)*

**Finding 2 — The cognitive offshoring accountability inversion is not recognized as a governance event**
When an advisor receives an AI-generated portfolio recommendation and endorses it, the advisor becomes an AI ratifier — not an AI originator. This role inversion (the person with the least direct AI interface has the most formal accountability; the person with the most direct AI interface has the least formal accountability) creates an undocumented accountability gap that is invisible until a client disputes a recommendation or FINMA requests documentation. The gap is structural, not individual. It requires a firm-level documentation protocol, not an individual-level reminder.

*(Distinct from C214 Finding 2: C214 identified informal AI use as the deployment pattern. C215 identifies the specific structural inversion that informal adoption creates: seniority displacement, where junior staff have the most AI interface and senior advisors have the most formal accountability — without documentation transfer.)*

**Finding 3 — Client-initiated AI questions are arriving before firms have prepared responses**
UHNW and HNW clients are asking their Geneva advisors whether AI is used in managing their account. This is not a speculative concern — it is a present interaction. The firm that has not prepared a response is implicitly disclosing through silence: it is revealing that its AI use is informal, undocumented, and not part of a coherent client communication strategy. The nFADP Art. 12 automated decision disclosure obligation and the client trust question converge here. The failure to prepare a response is itself a compliance and client relations risk.

*(Distinct from C214 Finding 5: C214 identified ISSB disclosure deadline as the external regulatory trigger. C215 identifies client-initiated AI questions as the interpersonal trigger — the client is asking before the regulator requires disclosure.)*

**Finding 4 — Vendor concentration in boutique AI tool usage creates correlated risk that is not assessed**
Most boutique Geneva wealth managers are using the same three or four AI tool categories: a large language model for drafting, a portfolio data tool, a compliance document review tool, and a research tool. The concentration means that a regulatory action, a data incident, or a terms-of-service change by one of these vendor categories creates correlated compliance exposure across the entire boutique segment. The firm-level risk is not just its own AI governance — it is its dependency on vendor categories that the firm has not assessed for systemic concentration risk.

*(Distinct from C214 Finding 3: C214 identified the forthcoming FINMA AI model risk classification as the regulatory timeline. C215 identifies vendor category concentration as a specific risk that the classification framework does not address.)*

**Finding 5 — The classification question (automated advisory vs. productivity tool) is the unresolved legal question for most deployed AI**
FINMA has not explicitly classified whether AI-assisted portfolio analysis constitutes "automated individual advice" under existing advisory licensing rules. Boutique Geneva wealth managers are operating in this classification gap — some assuming they fall under productivity tool rules, some assuming advisory rules apply, none having documented the basis for their assumption. The classification determines the license requirement, the documentation standard, and the client disclosure obligation. Until FINMA publishes explicit guidance, this gap creates asymmetric risk: firms that have assumed the less demanding classification face greater retrospective exposure if the classification resolves differently.

*(Distinct from C214 Finding 4: C214 identified the compliance documentation layer as the specific advisory gap. C215 identifies the classification uncertainty as the legal foundation under the documentation layer — without a classification, the documentation requirement itself is undefined.)*

**Finding 6 — Documentation debt compounds silently and becomes visible only at the moment of maximum damage**
The documentation requirement for AI-assisted recommendations is not a one-time deliverable — it is a continuous obligation. Every AI-assisted recommendation generates documentation debt: input data, AI output, advisor decision, client acceptance. Firms that have not built structured workflows are generating documentation debt that grows faster than their remediation capacity. The debt becomes visible only at the moment of maximum damage: a client dispute, a FINMA review, a professional liability claim. At that point, remediation costs are highest and the firm's ability to produce a clean record is most constrained.

*(Distinct from C214 Finding 5: C214 identified the mid-2027 ISSB deadline as the external trigger. C215 identifies the internal triggering event — documentation debt becomes visible precisely when the firm can least afford the exposure.)*
