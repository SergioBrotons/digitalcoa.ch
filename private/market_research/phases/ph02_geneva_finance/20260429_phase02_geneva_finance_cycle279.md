## 01 — ICP Language: How This ICP Actually Describes AI Problems

### English

The managing director or senior advisor at a boutique Geneva wealth management firm describes AI problems in terms of accountability they cannot yet pin down precisely — not in terms of technology capability gaps.

Where an external observer might frame the issue as "AI governance framework weakness," the Geneva wealth manager describes the situation as: *"I know the clients with more sophisticated compliance functions are going to start asking questions. I can feel the questions changing. I don't have a document that answers what they'll actually ask."*

The problem is expressed as a **coming accountability moment** — carrier renewal, institutional client audit, board presentation, FINMA supervisory examination — not as an abstract governance deficiency. The MD thinks in terms of specific future occasions where they will need to account for AI-influenced decisions, and knows the documentation currently in place was not built to survive those occasions.

Language that surfaces in this ICP's own framing:

- *"We told them we use AI in the process — that feels like disclosure"* — the gap between felt disclosure (a general statement) and accountable disclosure (evidence at the moment it will be examined)
- *"The model changed and nobody told us"* — silent AI model update as a reliability question, not an IT notification problem
- *"If something goes wrong in a client relationship and they ask how that recommendation was constructed — do I actually know?"* — recommendation attribution as personal liability question
- *"The carrier questionnaire now asks questions I've never been asked before — and I signed the last one personally"* — attestation as personal liability instrument, now with new content the signatory has not evaluated
- *"My clients who are also wealth managers are asking each other the same questions we couldn't answer a year ago"* — peer question convergence as signal that standards are forming

The ICP does not describe this as an "AI strategy" problem. They describe it as a **record problem**: something happened, decisions were made, and when accountability is demanded, there is no structured record of what happened and why. The distinction matters because "strategy" implies a forward-looking optimization choice. "Record" implies an existing exposure that may already be present.

## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

### The Documentation That Cannot Be Retroactively Reconstructed

The most consistent failure mode across boutique Geneva wealth managers is the inability to reconstruct the reasoning chain behind AI-assisted decisions made twelve to eighteen months ago. This is not a documentation quality problem — it is a fundamental architectural problem. AI tools do not produce contemporaneous reasoning records that survive the departure of the person who initiated the query. The documentation that would allow an MD to answer the question *"how was this recommendation constructed"* for a decision made fourteen months ago does not exist, and cannot be created retrospectively.

The paradox is that the firms most committed to rigorous documentation are discovering this exposure most acutely — because they have more documentation, they have more visibility into how much of their actual AI-assisted decision history cannot be reconstructed from their records.

### The Carrier Attestation Iteration Without Geneva-Specific Interpretation

Carriers are iterating their AI governance questionnaires at a pace that has outrun the Geneva market's ability to provide interpretive guidance. Each generation of questionnaire introduces new commitments — what the MD personally warrants — without a corresponding Geneva-specific analysis of what each commitment means in the context of Swiss professional liability, FINMA supervisory expectations, and the specific AI tools in use at boutique firms.

The complaint is not that carriers are asking harder questions. The complaint is that the questions have changed character: from "does your firm have a policy?" to "describe the specific decisions your AI system made in the last quarter and what human review preceded each." The first question is answerable with a document. The second requires an operational evidence architecture that does not yet exist at boutique scale.

### The Institutional Client Convergent Inquiry Problem

Institutional clients — family offices, pension funds, corporate treasury functions — are sending AI governance questionnaires that are non-identical but converging on the same categories of questions. A boutique wealth manager receiving three such questionnaires in quick succession faces three slightly different versions of the same underlying questions, each requiring a response that is simultaneously accurate relative to firm practice and calibrated to the specific questionnaire language.

The market signal embedded in this convergence is that the institutional accountability community is forming a shared understanding of what adequate AI governance disclosure looks like — and that shared understanding is arriving before any Geneva-specific guidance on what adequate compliance means for boutique wealth managers.

### The Silent Model Update Continuity Problem

The problem most commonly described as *"the model changed and nobody told us"* is not an IT notification problem. It is an investment decision continuity problem. Active client mandates have recommendations embedded in them that were produced under model conditions that no longer exist. The firm has no mechanism for detecting when model updates have changed the analytical basis of active client positions. The question the MD cannot answer is: *"Do I have any client positions right now that are based on a model version that has since been updated?"*

### The nFADP Inference Accuracy Exposure Arriving Silently

AI-generated inferences about client financial behavior — risk tolerance assumptions, financial intention projections, behavioral pattern categorizations — are embedded in client reports without any evaluation of whether those inferences are accurate personal data subject to Article 15 correction and erasure rights. The exposure is present-tense and unnamed. The firm has no process for identifying which AI-inferred conclusions about specific clients are embedded in client-facing documents, no mechanism for responding to an Article 15 request for those inferences, and no Geneva market guidance on how Article 15 applies to AI-generated inferences embedded in advisory reports.

### The Override Documentation Void as Three-Party Convergence Failure

When a portfolio manager overrides an AI recommendation, the override event is undocumented at three levels simultaneously: the client receives an explanation of the recommendation logic that was not applied, the carrier's AI governance documentation reflects AI-influenced recommendations that do not reflect actual practice, and the firm has no record of the override event itself. The three-audience convergence makes a single undocumented override categorically different from a single-party documentation failure. No Geneva market service has named this specific convergence mechanism.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations

### FINMA

FINMA has not issued dedicated AI governance guidance for wealth management firms. Supervisory expectations are forming through operational interactions — examination findings, response letters, and iterative carrier questionnaire iterations that reflect FINMA's supervisory priorities without formal publication. The Federal Council deliberation of June 2026 is expected to produce formal guidance that will formalize expectations currently observable only through carrier iteration patterns and informal circuit signals.

FINMA's existing framework for model risk management (FINMA Circular 2018/3, model risk governance) applies to AI systems used in investment decisions, with particular emphasis on:
- The three-tier model classification system (standard, elevated, high risk), determining validation and documentation requirements
- The distinction between AI used for portfolio construction vs. AI used for client reporting — each with different evidence standards
- Human review obligations preceding AI-influenced recommendations delivered to clients

The accountability exposure for boutique firms is the gap between the validation standards designed for large institutions and the proportionate documentation obligations appropriate at boutique scale. FINMA has not issued proportionate guidance for 50–200 employee wealth managers.

### ASIP (Association Suisse des Institutions de Prévoyance)

ASIP governs pension fund AI asset allocation advisory obligations. Pension funds receiving AI-assisted allocation recommendations from Geneva wealth managers have their own AI governance documentation requirements under ASIP guidance. The wealth manager's AI system is effectively operating within a two-layer accountability structure: its own FINMA/nFADP obligations and its pension fund clients' ASIP governance requirements flowing backward through the advisory relationship. No Geneva service has mapped this specific two-layer accountability architecture for boutique wealth managers.

### SFAMA (Swiss Fund and Asset Management Association)

SFAMA has issued guidance on AI use in asset management that is developing alongside FINMA's supervisory expectations. The key obligation for Geneva wealth managers advising on SFAMA-regulated mandates: algorithmic routine disclosure under FIDLEG Article 26, distinguishing between standard algorithmic execution and AI-assisted portfolio construction with different disclosure architecture for each category. The boutique-specific gap is the absence of a proportionate methodology for applying Article 26 disclosure requirements to mandates where the AI contribution is partial, continuous, and integrated into ongoing advisory relationships rather than discrete and identifiable.

### ISSB Standards (S1/S2)

ISSB S2 climate-related financial disclosure requirements are entering Swiss regulatory implementation. The specific exposure for Geneva wealth management firms: Scope 2 emissions reporting requires entity-specific electricity consumption data. AI systems used in portfolio analysis do not produce entity-specific asset-level electricity data — they produce estimates. The firm presenting AI-generated estimates as ISSB-aligned Scope 2 data has a disclosure accuracy problem distinct from general ESG reporting compliance. No Geneva market service has addressed the specific intersection of AI-generated estimates and ISSB disclosure accuracy obligations for boutique wealth managers.

### nFADP (Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection, revised)

The revised nFADP creates specific obligations that Geneva wealth managers have not yet evaluated for AI-adjacent operations:

- **Article 6**: Lawful basis for processing client financial data through AI vendor infrastructure — purpose limitation requires that data collected for portfolio management is not subsequently used for AI model improvement or other purposes beyond the original mandate scope
- **Article 8**: Profiling notification — AI-assisted portfolio construction constitutes automated profiling under Article 8, triggering a specific obligation to notify data subjects and offer human review. Geneva wealth management boutiques have not evaluated this specific obligation.
- **Article 15**: Accuracy correction rights for AI-inferred conclusions about clients embedded in client-facing documents — different from model accuracy, applies to the specific AI-generated inferences about a particular client's financial behavior that appear in reports
- **Article 21**: Automated decision explanation — when a client requests explanation of how AI affected a specific recommendation, the obligation to provide this explanation in understandable terms is operative regardless of firm readiness to discharge it

### The Documentation Window Close

The mid-2026 documentation window close creates a specific burden-of-proof reversal: before the close, firms building documentation are evaluated against forming standards; after the close, firms explaining absence are evaluated against arrived standards. New documentation built after the window closes cannot retroactively reconstruct evidence for the period before the window. This structural timing property is not addressed in any Geneva market service offering — most services treat documentation as an ongoing compliance improvement exercise rather than as a window-specific evidence positioning exercise.
