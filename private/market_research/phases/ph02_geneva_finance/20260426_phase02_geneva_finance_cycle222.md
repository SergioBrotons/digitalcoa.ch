## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### Real Language — How the ICP Describes AI Problems

- "We have an AI policy. We passed it six months ago. I could not tell you with confidence whether our actual workflow conforms to it." — Implementation gap. Policy exists; practice does not conform.

- "The vendor changed its data retention terms. We found out when a staff member noticed three months later." — Vendor term monitoring failure. No process for tracking when AI vendor terms change.

- "A client asked us to confirm our AI governance practice. We had to admit we did not have a recent conformance assessment." — Client governance review. External pressure now arriving.

- "The regulator is asking questions we did not expect. They are more specific than they were a year ago." — FINMA supervisory escalation. Questions outpacing documentation.

- "I cannot verify that the AI is producing accurate output. I can only verify that it looks professional." — Output quality calibration. Review methodology not designed for AI errors.

- "We use three AI tools. I have no combined view of what data touches all three." — Multi-tool data architecture blindness.

## 02 — Market Signals

1. **Implementation gap is now the primary boutique wealth management AI problem** — The firms that adopted AI governance policies now face the harder problem of making practice conform to policy.

2. **Client governance review requests are becoming a standard commercial expectation** — Institutional clients and family offices are beginning to request AI governance conformance confirmation as part of due diligence.

3. **AI vendor term monitoring is an unaddressed systematic risk** — No small firm has a process for detecting when AI vendor terms change. Vendor changes are happening without firm knowledge.

4. **FINMA supervisory questions are escalating in specificity faster than documentation is improving** — The gap between informal examiner questions and formal compliance is widening.

5. **Multi-tool data architecture remains unmapped at the boutique level** — Combined data exposure across multiple AI tools has no owner and no documentation.

6. **Output quality calibration remains the unresolved verification problem** — Senior advisors reviewing AI output without a methodology for assessing factual accuracy.

## 04 — Compliance Context

1. **FINMA Circ. 2018/3 model risk** — Supervisory expectations for AI model risk management being applied informally; formalization approaching.
2. **nFADP Articles 12, 21, 22** — Existing obligations for automated decision-making documentation that most boutique firms have not closed.
3. **FINMA Q4 2026 tier classification** — Classification framework will formalize documentation requirements. Firms unprepared will face adverse categorization.
4. **Client AI governance review requests** — Commercial expectations now requiring demonstration of practice, not just documentation of policy.
5. **AI vendor term changes** — Data retention and model training provisions changing without notification; existing legal bases may have been invalidated by vendor changes.
6. **Output quality documentation** — No framework for documenting how AI output quality is assessed and verified in the compliance review process.

## 06 — Findings Summary

### 6 Net-New Findings (Distinct from C214–C221 Phase 02)

1. **Policy-practice conformance gap is now the primary boutique AI governance problem** — Having a policy is no longer sufficient; operational conformance is what the regulator and clients are beginning to assess.

2. **AI vendor term monitoring is a systematic unaddressed failure** — No small firm has a monitoring process for vendor terms changes. Vendor changes create legal base invalidation risk that accumulates silently.

3. **Client governance review requests are a new commercial entry expectation** — Institutional clients and family offices are beginning to require AI governance conformance confirmation as a due diligence condition.

4. **FINMA supervisory specificity is outpacing boutique documentation maturity** — The informal questions being asked now are more advanced than the documentation most boutiques have prepared.

5. **Multi-tool data architecture blindness creates compounding exposure** — Each additional AI tool increases the combined data exposure with no proportionate increase in documentation or governance.

6. **Output quality calibration methodology remains the unresolvable verification problem** — The senior reviewer's methodology is structurally unsuited to detecting AI-generated factual errors in portfolio analysis and compliance documentation.
