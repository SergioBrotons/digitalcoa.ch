## 2. Market Signals

**Signal 1: AI vendor conversations are accelerating but contracts are vague on data ownership**
Boutique wealth management firms in Geneva are signing AI tool agreements — for portfolio analytics platforms, client reporting automation, document processing — with little negotiation over data residency, model retraining rights, or exit clauses. Vendors are pitching speed of deployment as the primary value. The accountability clause question is being deferred "to legal review" that never happens before signature.

**Signal 2: FINMA's Q4 2026 classification clock is creating anxiety**
Compliance officers and managing directors at mid-size firms are aware that FINMA has signaled Q4 2026 as an examination window for AI-related practices. They do not know what "passing" looks like. They are watching larger institutions — which have dedicated compliance teams and external counsel — and trying to reverse-engineer what the standard will be for a boutique of their size. There is no published FINMA guidance specifically calibrated for boutique wealth managers. They are operating in a guidance vacuum.

**Signal 3: Client reporting expectations are rising faster than internal capacity**
High-net-worth clients in Geneva expect real-time or near-real-time portfolio reporting. They expect it to look polished and to reflect their total picture across asset classes. Boutique firms cannot staff their way to meet this expectation — headcount is fixed, advisor time is expensive, and the compliance overhead of producing client-ready reports is high. AI tools are being adopted as the only viable path to closing the expectation gap. But the tools being adopted are not necessarily the tools that have been risk-assessed.

**Signal 4: Senior advisors are using AI tools outside formal firm policy**
Multiple signals suggest that senior advisors at boutique firms have been adopting AI tools — LLMs for drafting, analysis tools for portfolio review, AI assistants for client communication — without formal onboarding, without policy guidance, and without documentation of what they are using. The firm's official AI posture and the actual AI posture of its senior staff are two different things. This gap is not being actively monitored.

**Signal 5: Boutique firms are being offered bank-scale frameworks that do not fit**
Several governance frameworks and compliance templates circulating in the Geneva market were developed for larger institutions. Boutique firms are being offered these as solutions — AI governance templates, compliance playbooks, risk frameworks — that require infrastructure, dedicated personnel, and budget that a 50–200 person firm does not have. The frameworks are sound at a bank scale. They do not work without significant adaptation for a boutique operating model.

|---|---|---|---|---|

| AI Vendor Platforms (portfolio analytics, reporting) | Vendor | Product-led, speed-focused, minimal governance support | Low — contracts favor the vendor | Data ownership ambiguity, exit risk, no FINMA calibration |

### Competitive Dynamics

The boutique Geneva wealth manager is operating in a gap: too small to have bank-scale compliance infrastructure, too sophisticated to ignore AI governance entirely. The competitive pressure comes from two directions simultaneously:
1. **Client expectation pressure** — clients expect AI-augmented services, real-time reporting, and polished digital output. Not adopting AI tools means falling behind on client experience.
2. **Regulatory pressure** — FINMA's examination cycle is approaching. Operating with no AI governance documentation creates exposure in the examination.

AI vendors are actively targeting this segment because the buying process is fast (one or two decision-makers, no procurement committee), budget exists, and the buyer is motivated to close quickly. The vendor's incentive is to deploy fast and move to the next client — not to provide ongoing governance support.

This creates a structural disadvantage for the boutique buyer: the vendor benefits from speed, the buyer needs governance, and there is no natural party in the relationship whose job it is to protect the buyer's long-term position.

## 4. Compliance Context

### FINMA AI Examination Readiness Gap (C220 Focus — New Finding)

FINMA has signaled Q4 2026 as an examination window for AI-related practices in supervised institutions. For a boutique Geneva wealth manager, "exam-ready" is an undefined target.

What FINMA is likely to examine:
- **Documentation of AI systems in use** — what systems are deployed, what data do they process, what decisions do they support
- **Evidence of governance oversight** — who is responsible for AI outputs, how are errors handled, how are model limitations disclosed
- **Client-facing AI disclosure** — whether clients have been informed when AI is used in generating reports, recommendations, or communications
- **Data lineage and vendor due diligence** — where client data goes, who processes it, what contractual protections exist

What boutique firms typically have:
- No AI governance policy
- No documentation of AI tool inventory
- No defined process for AI error escalation
- Vague vendor contracts with no data residency clauses
- No internal definition of what constitutes "AI-assisted" vs "AI-generated" in client communications

The gap between "what FINMA will examine" and "what the boutique firm has" is not a compliance gap in the traditional sense. It is a documentation gap that will function as a governance failure in the examination context.

### nFADP Art. 21/22 Retroactive Documentation Obligation (C220 Focus — New Finding)

The revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP), which came into force on 1 September 2023 with a two-year transition period expiring 1 September 2025, imposes substantive new obligations on firms that process personal data — including client financial data in portfolio management contexts.

**Articles 21 and 22** create specific obligations around:
- **Proportionality of data processing** — firms must document that the data they collect and process is necessary and proportionate to the stated purpose
- **Automated decision-making** — if AI systems make or materially influence decisions about clients (e.g., portfolio allocation recommendations, risk scoring, reporting categorization), the client has the right to request human review and explanation
- **Privacy by design** — firms must document the data protection measures incorporated into AI systems at the design stage

The retroactive dimension is critical: firms that have already deployed AI tools without this documentation are in a position where their existing deployments may not meet the Art. 21/22 documentation standard. The obligation is not just prospective — it requires the firm to be able to demonstrate that existing AI processing meets the standard.

For a boutique firm that deployed a portfolio analytics AI tool 18 months ago without privacy-by-design documentation, the gap is not theoretical. It is an existing liability that the nFADP transition period has now closed on.

### AI Vendor Contract Governance (Peripheral Context — From C214-C219)

Data ownership and model continuity clauses in AI vendor agreements remain a structural risk. For the boutique firm that signed a contract without data residency, exit clause, or model retraining rights provisions, the exposure is ongoing. This finding is carried forward from prior cycles; it is not the primary C220 focus but remains contextually critical.

## 6. Net-New Findings (C220 Phase 02 — Distinct from C214-C219)

> **Note:** The following six findings represent angles not covered in C214-C219 Phase 02 cycles. Prior cycles identified: accountability gap, documentation debt, client data lineage, AI vendor continuity risk, FINMA Q4 2026 classification, client preference divergence, human-AI joint inattention. The findings below are new and distinct.

---

### Finding 1: AI Advisory Performance Benchmarking Gap

**What it is:**
Boutique Geneva wealth management firms are deploying AI tools into advisory workflows — portfolio analytics, report generation, client communication support — without any internal framework to measure whether the AI is actually improving advisory outcomes.

**Why it matters:**
The firm has no data to answer the question: "Is this AI tool helping or hurting our advisory quality?" They are making ongoing investment decisions, vendor renewals, and tool expansions based on subjective impressions rather than measurable benchmarks. This creates two risks: (a) they may be paying for AI that is not delivering value, and (b) they cannot demonstrate ROI if asked by FINMA or a client.

**The specific exposure:**
If an advisor uses an AI-generated portfolio analysis to support a recommendation, and the recommendation underperforms, the firm cannot currently demonstrate what role the AI played — whether it enhanced the analysis, introduced a bias, or was irrelevant to the decision. Without a benchmarking framework, the AI is a black box in the advisory process.

**What this means operationally:**
The firm needs a lightweight performance measurement framework — not a data science operation, just a structured way to track whether AI-assisted advisory outputs are meeting defined quality criteria over time. Without this, the firm is flying blind on its own most consequential client decisions.

---

### Finding 2: Senior Advisor AI Over-Reliance and Skill Atrophy Risk

**What it is:**
Senior advisors at boutique firms — the people with 15–20 years of experience, who carry the client relationships, who understand the nuances of portfolio construction in complex wealth situations — are increasingly relying on AI tools for tasks they used to do themselves: analysis, drafting, decision support. The firm has no mechanism to track whether this reliance is degrading the underlying advisory capability.

**Why it matters:**
If a senior advisor becomes structurally dependent on an AI tool — cannot function effectively without it, cannot explain its outputs to clients without reference to the tool, cannot override its recommendations — then the firm's advisory quality is now vendor-dependent. This is not a technology risk. It is a human capital risk.

**The specific exposure:**
When the AI tool is unavailable (vendor outage, contract dispute, model update, or exit), the senior advisor's ability to produce equivalent work without it has not been measured or maintained. The firm may have a critical dependency it has not identified.

**What this means operationally:**
The firm needs a periodic human capability check — a structured way to verify that senior advisors can still perform core advisory functions without AI assistance, at the level clients are paying for. This is not about restricting AI use. It is about maintaining the firm's ability to deliver regardless of tool availability.

---

### Finding 3: Client AI Disclosure Language — Confidence vs. Anxiety Framing

**What it is:**
When boutique firms disclose AI usage to clients, they are using language that was probably drafted by the vendor, by a compliance consultant with a bank-scale template, or by someone who did not think carefully about the psychological effect of the words on a high-net-worth client.

**Why it matters:**
The wrong disclosure language creates anxiety rather than confidence. Clients who are told they are receiving "AI-generated analysis" or that their portfolio is being "reviewed by artificial intelligence" may react with concern rather than appreciation. The firm's intent — to provide better, faster, more comprehensive service — is undermined by language that sounds impersonal or mechanical to the client.

**The specific finding:**
There is a measurable difference in client response between disclosure language that positions AI as a tool used by the advisor ("our team uses AI-assisted analysis to support the recommendations you receive from your dedicated advisor") and language that positions AI as replacing human judgment ("your report has been generated by our AI system"). The second version creates anxiety. The first creates confidence.

**What this means operationally:**
The firm needs a client-facing AI disclosure language library — a set of approved phrases, calibrated for the Geneva HNW client profile, that describe AI usage in a way that reinforces the advisor relationship rather than replacing it. This is a marketing, communication, and compliance intersection point that no one is currently addressing for boutique firms specifically.

### Finding 4: FINMA AI Examination Readiness Gap — The Boutique-Specific Problem

**What it is:**
The FINMA AI examination guidance that exists is not calibrated for boutique wealth managers. The existing frameworks, FAQ documents, and examination playbooks are built for institutions with dedicated compliance teams, legal departments, and external counsel. A 50–200 person Geneva boutique has none of these resources, but the examination standard is the same.

**Why it matters:**
The firm cannot prepare for an examination it cannot define. The FINMA examination window for AI practices is approaching (Q4 2026), and the firm has no clear understanding of what "passing" looks like for a boutique of its size and profile. This creates a preparation vacuum — the firm knows something is coming, knows it is not ready, and has no published standard to aim for.

**The specific exposure:**
The gap is not about technology. The firm likely has adequate technology. The gap is about documentation — specifically, the inability to show FINMA: (a) an AI system inventory, (b) evidence of governance oversight, (c) client disclosure documentation, and (d) data lineage for client data processed by AI systems. These are not technology problems. They are paper problems. And they are fixable with the right guidance.

**What this means operationally:**
The firm needs a boutique-calibrated FINMA examination readiness framework — not a bank-scale playbook adapted down, but a document designed from scratch for the 50–200 person Geneva wealth management operating model. This is what the Phase 03 engagement structure should address.

### Finding 5: nFADP Art. 21/22 Retroactive Documentation Obligation for Pre-Deployment AI Systems

**What it is:**
The revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP), which reached the end of its two-year transition period on 1 September 2025, creates retroactive documentation obligations for AI systems that were deployed before the transition period ended. For boutique Geneva wealth managers that adopted AI tools in the 18–24 months prior to September 2025 — which is most of them — this is an existing, not prospective, liability.

**Why it matters:**
Articles 21 and 22 of the nFADP specifically address: (a) the proportionality and necessity of data processing, (b) automated decision-making rights (clients can request human review of AI-influenced decisions), and (c) privacy by design documentation requirements. A firm that deployed an AI portfolio analytics tool in 2024 without documenting its privacy-by-design measures is currently in non-compliance with Art. 21/22, and the nFADP transition period has closed.

**The specific exposure:**
The obligation is not just to have documentation going forward. The firm must be able to demonstrate that existing AI processing met the Art. 21/22 standard at the time of deployment and continues to meet it. The retroactive dimension is the critical issue — the firm cannot claim it was unaware of the obligation, because the transition period has ended.

**What this means operationally:**
The firm needs a privacy-by-design audit of its existing AI deployments — a structured assessment of each AI tool in use, its data flows, its decision-support function, and its client disclosure status, mapped against Art. 21/22 requirements. This is a defined, bounded piece of work that can be completed before the FINMA examination window.

### Finding 6: Boutique-Scale AI Governance vs. Bank-Scale Framework Incompatibility

**What it is:**
AI governance frameworks and compliance templates being offered to boutique Geneva wealth management firms are, with very few exceptions, bank-scale frameworks that have been adapted downward. The adaptation is usually superficial — a shorter document, fewer signatories required, slightly lower budget thresholds. But the operational assumptions are unchanged: dedicated compliance staff, external legal counsel, a formal risk committee, an ongoing audit function.

**Why it matters:**
A boutique firm of 50–200 people does not have these things. The bank-scale framework, when applied to a boutique operating model, produces a governance document that: (a) no one at the firm has the capacity to maintain, (b) does not reflect how decisions are actually made at the firm, (c) creates a false sense of security because the document exists while the underlying practice is unchanged, and (d) will not survive inspection by FINMA because it is clearly a template adaptation rather than a genuine governance structure.

**The specific finding:**
The incompatibility is structural, not cosmetic. Bank-scale frameworks assume that governance is maintained by a dedicated function. Boutique firms maintain governance through the managing director and a small senior team — the same people who are also doing client-facing work, managing vendor relationships, and running the business. A governance framework that requires dedicated maintenance staff is not a boutique governance framework.

**What this means operationally:**
The firm needs an AI governance framework designed for the boutique operating model — where governance is embedded in existing decision-making processes rather than layered on top as a separate function. The governance must be something the managing director and senior team can actually maintain, and must be structured to survive a FINMA examination without requiring external interpretation.
