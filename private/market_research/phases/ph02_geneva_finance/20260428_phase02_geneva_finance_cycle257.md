## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Swiss retail client AI explanation expectations exceeding published FINMA standards**
Swiss retail clients are arriving with AI explanation expectations that may exceed what FINMA has formally codified. The expectation gap is creating an informal adequacy standard that Geneva boutique firms are held to even though it is not published anywhere.

**Signal 2: AI-generated portfolio risk scores with no standardized disclosure format**
AI systems generate risk scores for portfolio positions. No standardized disclosure format exists for communicating what the score means, how it was generated, and what limitations it has. Firms are making subjective disclosure decisions without a reference standard.

**Signal 3: AI system rejection reasoning as nFADP Art. 21 trigger**
When AI systems reject proposed trades or rebalancing decisions, the rejection reasoning itself can become data that clients have rights over under nFADP Art. 21. The trigger is unnamed in any compliance framework.

**Signal 4: AI vendor model updates creating undocumented compliance surface shifts**
Each AI vendor model update shifts the firm's compliance surface. The compliance surface shift is undocumented because no mechanism exists for tracking how vendor updates change the firm's compliance obligations.

**Signal 5: Client relationship data predating current nFADP compliance architecture**
Client relationship data that predates current nFADP compliance architecture creates a specific disclosure obligation. The data was collected before the compliance architecture existed; the obligation to disclose what the AI has inferred from it was not anticipated when the data was collected.

**Signal 6: Geneva private banking AI portfolio construction with embedded instruction generation**
AI systems used in Geneva private banking for portfolio construction can generate embedded instructions that are not visible in standard reporting. The embedded instructions can affect portfolio decisions in ways that clients have rights to know about.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Client-facing AI explanation format for portfolio recommendations**
No format exists for client-facing AI explanation that addresses what the AI system does, how it generates recommendations, what its limitations are, and what the firm's review process adds. The format would need to be readable by clients who are not technical.

**Gap 2: AI model update compliance surface tracking system**
No system exists for tracking how AI vendor model updates change the firm's compliance surface. The tracking would need to capture what obligations changed, when they changed, and what the firm's response obligation is.

**Gap 3: nFADP Art. 21 response methodology for AI rejection reasoning**
No methodology exists for responding to nFADP Art. 21 requests that ask for explanation of AI rejection reasoning. The methodology would need to address what data the firm has, what it can produce, and what the client is entitled to receive.

**Gap 4: Pre-nFADP client data AI inference inventory**
No protocol exists for creating an inventory of AI inferences generated from data that predates current nFADP compliance architecture. The inventory would need to address what was inferred, from what data, and what the current obligations are.

**Gap 5: Geneva private banking AI embedded instruction disclosure framework**
No framework exists for disclosing embedded instructions in AI-generated portfolio recommendations. The framework would need to address what instructions are embedded, how they affect portfolio decisions, and what disclosure is required.

**Gap 6: FINMA AI governance adequacy decoding for boutique wealth management**
No methodology exists for decoding FINMA's adequacy standard from question patterns at boutique wealth management scale. The decoding would need to capture what questions are being asked, what responses are being accepted, and what the standard is becoming.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA AI governance examination progression:**
FINMA examination questions are becoming more specific. The standard is forming through question specificity. Boutique firms are being measured against a standard they cannot read, only infer from the questions being asked.

**nFADP Articles 6, 8, and 21 obligations for portfolio AI:**
Art. 6 requires consent for profiling. Art. 8 requires transparency about automated decision-making. Art. 21 requires explanations in intelligible form. AI-assisted portfolio management activates all three provisions simultaneously.

**ISSB AI-related disclosure standards for wealth management:**
ISSB standards require documentation of how AI systems are used in sustainability-related disclosures. Wealth management firms serving sustainability-focused clients face this obligation without methodology for compliance.

**ASIP fiduciary obligations and AI recommendation accountability:**
Fiduciary obligations require that recommendations be attributable to the firm's judgment. AI-generated recommendations that the firm presents as its own create accountability exposure that ASIP has not addressed at boutique scale.

**FINMA circular on AML and AI systems:**
FINMA Circular 2016/1 on AML creates obligations for AI systems used in client onboarding and transaction monitoring. The obligations include documentation of how the AI system works and how decisions are reviewed.

**SFAMA member code and AI documentation at boutique scale:**
SFAMA member code does not include AI-specific documentation standards for boutique-scale members. The gap creates ambiguity about what adequate documentation looks like for firms that are too small to have dedicated compliance functions.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Swiss retail client AI explanation expectations exceeding published FINMA standards as operative adequacy gap**
Swiss retail clients are arriving with AI explanation expectations that exceed what FINMA has formally codified. The informal adequacy standard is operative in client relationships before it is published in any regulatory document. Geneva boutique firms are being held to this standard without knowing it exists.

**Finding 2: AI-generated portfolio risk score disclosure format as subjective firm decision without reference standard**
AI risk scores have no standardized disclosure format. Firms are making subjective decisions about how much to disclose about score methodology, limitations, and review process. The subjective decisions create exposure that no compliance framework addresses.

**Finding 3: AI system rejection reasoning as nFADP Art. 21 trigger creating client rights over firm decision logic**
When AI systems reject proposed trades, the rejection reasoning is data that clients have rights over under nFADP Art. 21. The trigger is unnamed in any compliance framework. The firm's rejection documentation may be incomplete relative to what Art. 21 requires.

**Finding 4: AI vendor model updates creating undocumented compliance surface shifts with no tracking mechanism**
Each AI vendor model update shifts the firm's compliance surface. The compliance surface shift is undocumented. No mechanism exists for tracking how vendor updates change compliance obligations. The firm may be out of compliance with obligations it does not know it has assumed.

**Finding 5: Pre-nFADP client data AI inference inventory as present-tense disclosure obligation without methodology**
Client data collected before current nFADP compliance architecture may have been processed by AI systems that generated inferences about clients. The inferences create present-tense disclosure obligations that the firm cannot meet because it has not inventoried what was inferred and from what data.

**Finding 6: Geneva private banking AI embedded instruction generation as undisclosed portfolio decision influence**
AI systems used in Geneva private banking portfolio construction can generate embedded instructions that influence portfolio decisions without appearing in client-facing reports. The undisclosed influence creates a fiduciary question about what the client is actually agreeing to when they authorize the portfolio management.
