## 01 — ICP Language (EN + FR)

### How the ICP Actually Describes AI Problems — Real Language

The managing director at a boutique Geneva wealth management firm is past the adoption question. AI tools are in their workflow. The question now is whether the governance keeps pace with the deployment — and whether the documentation will be there when the regulator or the client asks.

**Core concerns in their own register:**

- "I have three AI tools in use. I could not tell you, from memory, what data each one touches or where the data goes when the tool processes it. That is a gap I have been managing by not thinking about it too hard." — Multi-tool data flow opacity. The firm has AI in portfolio analysis, client reporting, and compliance documentation. No one has mapped the combined data exposure across these three tools.

- "The FINMA examiner has started asking about AI in supervisory meetings. Not in a formal way — in conversation. The questions are more specific than they were six months ago. I do not think this is hypothetical anymore." — FINMA supervisory dialogue escalation. The regulator is asking about AI in a way that is beginning to create documentation pressure that is not yet formal but is real.

- "I can tell when an AI-generated portfolio report looks right. I cannot tell you whether it is accurate. The presentation quality and the content accuracy are different things, and I do not have a method for distinguishing them." — Output quality calibration gap. The managing director's review is calibrated for presentation, not for factual accuracy.

- "I have clients who want to know exactly what AI does in their portfolio. I have clients who do not want to know. I do not have a policy for handling these two conversations differently." — Client AI communication divergence. The firm has not developed a client segmentation approach for AI-related transparency.

- "When the AI tool we use changes its model — which happens — the outputs change. The compliance documentation we generated with the old model looks different from the compliance documentation we are generating now. I do not know how to explain this to a regulator." — Model version documentation gap. No process exists for documenting when and how AI model changes alter firm outputs.

- "Our insurer asked about AI in the renewal questionnaire. I answered, but I do not know whether my answer affects our coverage. I do not think the insurer knows either." — Insurance AI questionnaire without coverage clarity. Both parties are operating with uncertainty that neither has resolved.

## 02 — Market Signals

### Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

**Signal 1 — Multi-Tool Data Flow Opacity Is the Primary Structural Risk**
Boutique wealth management firms are running multiple AI tools simultaneously — for portfolio analysis, client reporting, and compliance documentation — without a combined data flow map. Each tool has its own data handling architecture, vendor terms, and potential data residency implications. The managing director cannot give a coherent answer to the question "what data touches which AI tools and where does it go." This is not an IT problem — it is a governance problem that has no current owner in the typical boutique firm.

**Signal 2 — FINMA Supervisory Dialogue Is Escalating in Specificity**
FINMA examiners are asking about AI in supervisory conversations with increasing specificity. The questions are no longer general — they are beginning to address specific tools, specific documentation practices, and specific risk management approaches. The boutique firm with undocumented AI practices is entering a zone of regulatory risk that is real but not yet formally defined. The gap between informal examiner questions and formal compliance requirements is narrowing.

**Signal 3 — Output Quality Calibration Is Not Keeping Pace with AI Deployment**
The managing director's review methodology for AI-generated outputs is calibrated for professional presentation, not for factual accuracy. AI-generated portfolio analysis that looks authoritative may contain material errors that a non-expert reviewer would not catch. The quality calibration gap is most acute in portfolio analysis and compliance documentation — the two highest-stakes output categories.

**Signal 4 — Client AI Communication Divergence Is Creating Relationship Risk**
Clients are dividing into two distinct groups on AI: those who want transparency and those who prefer not to know. The boutique firm is managing both conversations with the same approach, which means both groups are being underserved. The client AI communication divergence is not just a service quality issue — it is a relationship risk with retention and trust implications at the individual client level.

**Signal 5 — Model Version Documentation Gap Creates Compliance Inconsistency**
AI tools change their models without notice. When a model changes, the outputs change. The compliance documentation generated before and after a model change may be inconsistent in substance, not just presentation. The boutique firm has no process for documenting model changes or assessing whether the outputs have changed in ways that affect regulatory compliance. When an examiner asks about this, there is no answer.

**Signal 6 — Insurance AI Questionnaire Creates Adverse Admission Risk**
The insurer's AI questionnaire is creating a documented record of the firm's AI use that will be evaluated in the event of a claim. The managing director completing the form without understanding coverage implications is creating an adverse admission. The insurer's own uncertainty about AI coverage is not protective — the documented AI use will be reviewed against whatever framework the insurer applies at the time of claim.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

### What Exists, What Gaps Remain

**What exists:**
- FINMA supervisory expectations: emerging and informal, not yet formalized into guidance for boutiques
- AI vendors: providing tools, not governance frameworks for wealth management practice
- Big Four: bank-scale frameworks that assume infrastructure the boutique firm does not have
- Insurers: asking AI questions, not providing AI governance guidance

**What no one is addressing for Geneva boutique wealth management:**

1. **Multi-tool data flow mapping for wealth management** — No service product maps the combined data exposure across multiple AI tools in a boutique wealth management context. The combined exposure is greater than any single tool's exposure because the tools interact.

2. **FINMA examination readiness preparation** — No one helps the boutique firm prepare for the informal AI questions that FINMA examiners are now asking in supervisory meetings. The preparation gap is between general AI governance and specific FINMA supervisory readiness.

3. **Output quality calibration methodology** — A practical methodology for non-technical senior advisors to assess AI output quality — calibrated for the specific types of errors AI is most likely to produce in portfolio analysis and compliance documentation — does not exist as a training or tool product.

4. **Client AI communication segmentation** — A practical framework for differentiating client AI transparency preferences and designing communication approaches for each segment does not exist at the boutique wealth management level.

5. **Model version documentation protocol** — A lightweight process for documenting AI model changes and assessing their impact on firm outputs, designed for the boutique compliance function with no dedicated staff, is missing from the market.

6. **Insurer AI questionnaire preparation** — No one helps the boutique firm complete the insurance AI questionnaire in a way that is both honest and coverage-protective, with an understanding of what the answers actually mean for coverage in the event of a claim.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations for Boutique Wealth Management

1. **FINMA Circ. 2018/3 and AI model risk** — The existing supervisory circular on model risk management is being applied by FINMA examiners as an analogical reference for AI governance. The boutique firm using AI without a documented model risk framework is exposed to examiner questions it cannot answer.

2. **FINMA Q4 2026 AI model risk classification** — The tier classification framework will categorize boutique firms' AI uses. The classification will determine the documentation requirements that apply. The firm that has not begun preparing for classification is leaving its categorization to chance.

3. **nFADP Articles 12, 21, 22** — Automated decision-making disclosure obligations for client-facing AI applications. Already in force. The boutique firm with AI in client reporting has existing obligations it has not operationalized.

4. **ASIP pension fund mandate implications** — For firms managing pension fund assets, the AI documentation requirements under BVG/BVV2 are creating specific obligations that are distinct from general wealth management compliance.

5. **SFAMA fund management AI expectations** — The fund management industry's professional standards body has begun to address AI documentation expectations in ways that are beginning to affect institutional client expectations of boutique managers.

6. **ISSB AI-related disclosure intersection** — The ISSB's disclosure framework is creating language that is entering client expectations for AI-related sustainability and governance disclosures. Boutique firms with institutional clients may face disclosure requirements they are not yet aware of.
