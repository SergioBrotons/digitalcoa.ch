## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Compliance function approving AI outputs without evaluation methodology**
The compliance function at boutique wealth management firms is approving AI outputs without a methodology for evaluating what it is approving. The approval exists. The evaluation does not. This is structurally different from having an inadequate review process — it is having a review label without the underlying methodology.

**Signal 2: Client-directed AI system interrogation as emerging behavior**
Clients are beginning to interact with the AI systems their wealth managers use directly — asking the AI system questions the client would previously have asked the advisor. The firm has no protocol for this. Client-initiated AI interrogation is not addressed in any current governance framework at boutique scale.

**Signal 3: Model discontinuity accountability retroactive gap**
When an AI vendor discontinues a model or makes a significant version change, the firm is left holding accountability for outputs produced under a system that no longer exists. The retroactive accountability gap — clients potentially requesting reports and demanding explanation of how they were produced — is structurally unaddressed.

**Signal 4: AI monitoring tool generates its own accountability record**
The monitoring tools being deployed to address AI governance gaps are themselves AI systems that generate outputs. Those outputs become part of the accountability record. The monitoring system's own adequacy is not monitored. The firm is building accountability infrastructure on tools whose own accountability is assumed, not demonstrated.

**Signal 5: Boutique AI infrastructure dependency on individual knowledge**
The boutique's AI infrastructure — configurations, workflow integrations, vendor relationships — is knowledge embedded in individual practitioners. When a senior advisor who configured the system leaves, the firm's ability to independently evaluate and govern the AI system departs with them. The institutional knowledge deficit is structural and silent.

**Signal 6: Pricing-tier AI risk distribution misaligned with client exposure**
The firm's AI tool pricing tier determines the capability of the AI used in client reporting. Lower-priced AI tiers generate outputs with less robust methodology documentation. The client exposure is highest for the lowest-priced AI tier. The pricing-to-risk distribution is not visible to clients and not actively managed by the firm.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Compliance evaluation methodology for AI outputs at boutique scale**
A methodology designed for the compliance function to evaluate AI outputs — not to replace professional judgment, but to establish what adequate review looks like when the reviewer cannot independently reproduce the AI's reasoning — does not exist at boutique scale.

**Gap 2: Client-directed AI system interrogation protocol**
A protocol for managing the situation where clients directly interrogate the AI systems used in their own advisory relationship — with the firm having no visibility into and no control over that interaction — does not exist. This is not a communications gap. It is a governance surface with no current framework.

**Gap 3: AI model discontinuity reconstruction methodology**
A reconstruction methodology for the scenario where a model is discontinued, a version changes significantly, or a vendor exits — and the firm must account for outputs produced under the prior system — does not exist. The retroactive accountability gap is not on the advisory market.

**Gap 4: AI monitoring tool evaluation framework**
A framework for evaluating whether the AI monitoring tools the firm uses are themselves adequate — not whether the monitoring is happening, but whether what it produces is reliable and interpretable — does not exist.

**Gap 5: Boutique AI infrastructure knowledge continuity protocol**
A protocol for managing AI infrastructure knowledge continuity when key individuals depart — ensuring the firm retains the ability to independently evaluate and govern its AI systems without relying on the continued presence of the person who configured them — does not exist.

**Gap 6: AI pricing-tier risk documentation for client disclosure**
A documentation practice for communicating to clients which AI tier is used in their advisory relationship, what the capability differences mean for their reporting, and what the associated documentation differences are — does not exist as a structured offering.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA obligations:**
FINMA has begun circulating structured AI governance questionnaires in supervisory reviews. The questionnaire approach represents a shift from informal consultation to formal documentation requests. The firm that responds to the questionnaire with policy language, rather than practice evidence, will be identified. The questionnaires are designed to distinguish between governance theater and governance practice. The gap between what the firm has documented and what it can demonstrate is the exposure.

**ASIP obligations for pension fund trustees:**
Pension fund trustees using AI-assisted investment decisions face ASIP obligations that include specific documentation requirements. Trustees who adopted AI incrementally — configuring systems without systematic documentation — face accumulated documentation debt. The debt is not visible until a supervisory review activates it. Personal liability for trustees under ASIP obligations is not theoretical.

**SFAMA documentation requirements:**
SFAMA requirements for AI-assisted portfolio management documentation are accumulating. The requirements include model version tracking, use-case differentiation, and reassessment protocols. The firm that has not implemented version-aware documentation architecture is structurally non-compliant regardless of the quality of its investment decisions. The documentation requirements are not discretionary.

**ISSB sustainability data and AI lineage:**
ISSB-aligned sustainability disclosure requirements create AI lineage documentation obligations. When AI tools contribute to sustainability data used in client reporting, the lineage from source data through AI processing to disclosed metric must be documentable. The ISSB requirements are not yet in force, but the preparation required is not short-term. The line between ISSB preparation and current compliance is not sharp.

**nFADP cross-border client data and AI-generated content:**
The firm serving cross-border clients must manage nFADP obligations alongside the data protection regimes of other jurisdictions. When AI generates content that becomes part of client records, the processing documentation must be producible for any client's jurisdiction. The cross-border dimension means that the highest standard among the jurisdictions in the client portfolio determines the firm's minimum documentation obligation.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: Compliance function AI evaluation without methodology is structurally distinct from inadequate review**
The compliance function approving AI outputs without an evaluation methodology is not a process failure. It is a structural condition: the review label exists without the underlying methodology. The compliance officer who approves outputs cannot produce the evaluation criteria they applied. The approval is a trust-based act, not an evaluation-based one.

**Finding 2: Client-directed AI system interrogation as governance surface without current framework**
Clients beginning to interrogate AI systems directly — outside the advisory relationship, without firm visibility — represents an emerging behavior with no current governance framework. This is not a client communications problem. It is a governance surface that exists independently of any policy the firm has written.

**Finding 3: AI model discontinuity retroactive accountability gap structurally unaddressed**
When a model is discontinued or significantly changed, the firm holds accountability for outputs produced under a system that no longer exists. The retroactive gap — the inability to explain to a client how a report they hold was produced under a version of the AI that no longer operates — is not on the advisory market. It is a live exposure for any firm that has changed AI systems or experienced vendor-side model updates.

**Finding 4: AI monitoring tool adequacy not evaluated creates second-order accountability gap**
The monitoring tools deployed to address AI governance gaps are themselves AI systems producing outputs that become part of the accountability record. The monitoring system's own adequacy is not evaluated. The firm is building accountability infrastructure on tools whose reliability is assumed, not demonstrated.

**Finding 5: Boutique AI infrastructure dependency on individual knowledge creates institutional evaluation deficit**
The firm's AI infrastructure — configurations, integrations, vendor understanding — is knowledge embedded in individuals. When those individuals depart, the firm's ability to independently evaluate and govern its AI systems departs with them. The institutional evaluation deficit is not visible until a personnel change occurs. The structural dependency on individual knowledge is a governance gap with no current framework for remediation.

**Finding 6: AI pricing-tier risk distribution creates client exposure inversion at the lowest tier**
The firm's AI pricing tier determines the methodology robustness of outputs used in client reporting. Lower-priced tiers carry higher client exposure with less documentation. The inversion — highest exposure at the lowest cost tier — is not visible to clients and not actively managed by the firm. The pricing-to-risk distribution is itself a governance gap.
