## 02 — Market Signals

1. **Output quality review methodology not designed for AI factual accuracy** — Senior reviewers are calibrated for professional presentation, not factual verification. AI-generated errors that look authoritative are the hardest to detect.

2. **Multi-tool data flow mapping remains a gap at boutique scale** — Combined data exposure across multiple AI tools has no documentation and no owner at the boutique wealth management level.

3. **FINMA supervisory specificity escalating faster than documentation maturity** — The informal questions being asked are now more advanced than the documentation most boutiques have prepared.

4. **Client governance review requests arriving before internal readiness** — External pressure for AI governance conformance confirmation is now arriving as a commercial expectation before the firm has done an internal assessment.

5. **Model version documentation gap with compliance consequence** — When AI model changes alter compliance output, there is no record of what changed or whether prior output remains valid.

6. **Insurance AI disclosure creating coverage uncertainty without resolution** — The documented AI use in insurance files will be evaluated against whatever framework the insurer applies at claim time. The current uncertainty is not protective.

## 04 — Compliance Context

1. **FINMA Circ. 2018/3 model risk management** — Supervisory expectations being applied informally; formalization through Q4 2026 tier classification approaching.

2. **nFADP Articles 12, 21, 22** — Existing obligations for automated decision-making that most boutique firms have not operationalized into compliance workflow.

3. **Client governance review requests** — Commercial expectations requiring demonstration of practice, not just policy documentation. Standard shifting from "do you have a policy" to "can you demonstrate conformance."

4. **AI vendor term changes** — Data retention and model training provisions changing without notification; existing legal bases potentially invalidated by vendor changes without firm awareness.

5. **Multi-tool data architecture** — Each additional AI tool increases combined data exposure across the portfolio of tools without proportionate documentation increase.

6. **Output quality documentation for compliance review** — No framework exists for documenting how AI output quality is assessed in the compliance review process at boutique scale.

## 06 — Findings Summary

### 6 Net-New Findings (Distinct from C214–C222 Phase 02)

1. **Output quality review methodology structurally unsuited to AI factual verification** — Senior reviewer calibration for professional presentation creates a systematic detection gap for AI-generated factual errors in portfolio analysis.

2. **Multi-tool data flow mapping gap is compounding at boutique scale** — Each additional AI tool increases the aggregate data exposure without the firm having a process for tracking what touches which tool.

3. **FINMA supervisory specificity outpacing boutique documentation by an widening margin** — The informal examination questions are now addressing topics the firm's documentation does not cover, creating an adverse gap in every supervisory conversation.

4. **Client governance reviews arriving before internal readiness creates bilateral exposure** — The firm forced to admit it has not done a recent conformance assessment is creating a commercial impression and a regulatory impression simultaneously.

5. **Model version documentation gap means compliance output validity cannot be reconstructed** — When regulators or clients ask about prior compliance documentation, the firm cannot distinguish which outputs were generated under which model version.

6. **Insurance AI disclosure without coverage interpretation framework is an adverse admission** — The documented AI use will be read against whatever framework the insurer applies at claim time. Completing the form without interpretation guidance creates coverage exposure that compounds silently.
