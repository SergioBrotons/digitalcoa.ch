## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Peer AI model correlation as unexamined concentration risk question**
Clients at Geneva boutique wealth management firms are beginning to ask whether their firm uses the same AI model as other firms — and whether that matters. The question is arriving without a firm-level answer. The underlying issue is real: if multiple boutiques serving similar client profiles use the same AI model, and that model produces correlated signals, positions flagged as over-weight or under-weight across different client portfolios at different firms may be moving in the same direction simultaneously. The boutique manager has no framework for answering whether this is a risk, whether it creates client disclosure obligations, or whether it changes what adequate monitoring requires. The question will not stop being asked.

**Signal 2: Compliance documentation versus evidentiary adequacy as distinct documentation quality failure**
Compliance officers are producing documentation that answers the question "what did we do?" without producing documentation that would answer the question "was what we did defensible?" The gap between these two records is structural. The documentation produced under time pressure — the quarterly review, the questionnaire response, the approval record — is internally accurate and evidentially thin. When a supervisory examination moves from "what did you do" to "how do you know that was enough," the documentation does not travel. This is distinct from the documentation frequency gap (C238): that concerned whether documentation was produced at the right cadence; this concerns whether the documentation produced has the right evidential character.

**Signal 3: Client-requested AI explanation as present-tense communication gap without adequate response**
Clients are asking advisors to explain why the AI made a specific recommendation for their portfolio — not the general methodology, but the specific rationale for their specific situation. The AI system does not produce this explanation in a form that is directly relayable. The advisor gives an approximate answer. The approximation is presented as a professional explanation. The gap between the approximation and what the AI actually processed is invisible to the client and unmanaged by the firm. This is distinct from the confidence interval gap (C238): that concerned how certain the AI is; this concerns why the AI made the specific recommendation it did, which is a different question with different disclosure implications.

**Signal 4: AI recommendation partial documentation as reconstructability failure in the wrong direction**
When portfolio managers agree with an AI recommendation and act on it, the resulting documentation shows a correct decision — but it does not show whether the manager followed the AI exactly, went further than the AI suggested, or declined to act on additional AI recommendations in the same period. The documentation is accurate in a misleading direction: it shows a right outcome without showing the full decision context. If the question is ever "did you adequately review what the AI suggested versus what you actually did?", the record cannot answer it. This is distinct from the override documentation gap (C238): that concerned documented overrides; this concerns undocumented partial conformance — the decisions where the manager agreed with the AI and did something, but not necessarily what the AI most fully recommended.

**Signal 5: AI liability question surfacing before firm-level position exists**
Clients are beginning to ask managing directors directly what the firm's legal liability is if the AI recommended something wrong and the firm followed it. The managing director is giving a personal answer in the moment. The firm's position — what liability the firm actually bears, what client disclosures exist, what the engagement terms say — has not been documented. The gap between personal managing director response and firm-level documented position creates an exposure: the personal answer may be more or less protective than what the firm's documents actually provide, and there is no record of which answer was given. This is distinct from the compliance officer personal liability gap (C238): that concerned the compliance officer's signature; this concerns the managing director's verbal representations to clients about AI liability before any documented firm position exists.

**Signal 6: AI tool version update as undocumented report format discontinuity**
When boutique AI reporting tools are updated — by vendors, by configuration changes, or by workflow modifications — the output format and content may change. The previous reports are in a format that is no longer produced. The compliance review that examines the AI reporting history across periods encounters different document types with no explicit explanation of the discontinuity. The version change happened. The documentation of what changed and why does not exist. This is distinct from the model update silence gap (C236): that concerned active recommendations under changed conditions; this concerns the documentary artifact itself — the report format change creating an unexplained historical discontinuity that surfaces during compliance review.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Peer AI model correlation disclosure and monitoring framework**
A framework for boutique wealth management firms to assess whether they face correlated AI-driven signals from using the same model as peer firms — establishing what disclosure obligations arise, what monitoring would detect correlation, and what documentation would demonstrate awareness — does not exist at boutique scale. The question clients are asking has no firm-level answer because the analytical framework has not been built. This is distinct from cross-client signal correlation within a single firm (C238 Finding 5): that concerned correlated signals across one firm's own clients; this concerns correlated signals across different firms using the same AI infrastructure.

**Gap 2: Evidentiary adequacy documentation architecture**
A documentation architecture that produces records designed for evidentiary adequacy — not just process completion — is absent from boutique-scale advisory. The distinction matters: process documentation answers "what did we do"; evidentially adequate documentation answers "how do we know that was enough and what would be required to defend it?" Building the second type requires knowing what the defense standard is, which requires understanding how FINMA supervisory examinations actually operate at the detail level. No current boutique-scale advisory product combines regulatory examination methodology knowledge with documentation architecture design.

**Gap 3: Client-requested specific AI rationale response protocol**
A protocol for translating AI system outputs into client-comprehensible specific rationales — structured to distinguish what the AI actually processed for this client's specific portfolio from what the advisor can accurately relay — does not exist. The protocol is not about simplifying technical explanations. It is about managing the boundary between what the AI produced and what the firm can state with professional confidence. This is distinct from general AI explanation methodology (C219): that concerned why the AI flagged something generally; this concerns the specific question a client asks about a specific recommendation for their specific portfolio, where the relay accuracy matters for professional liability purposes.

**Gap 4: AI recommendation conformance documentation beyond override records**
A documentation approach for recording not just when the manager overrides the AI, but the full range of conformance decisions — when the manager followed exactly, went further, did less, or declined additional AI suggestions — does not exist. The conformance spectrum beyond simple override is uncharted. The documentation that would allow reconstruction of the full AI-advisor interaction pattern is not being produced. This is distinct from override documentation (C238): that concerned the specific departure decision; this concerns the broader conformance pattern across all AI-advisor interactions.

**Gap 5: AI liability position documentation as discrete firm-level deliverable**
A documented firm-level position on AI-generated recommendation liability — covering what the engagement terms provide, what disclosures exist, what the firm's actual liability exposure is, and how that position is communicated to clients — does not exist as a discrete deliverable at boutique scale. The managing director giving personal answers to client liability questions is a real risk management failure, not a communications quality problem. The deliverable is a written AI liability position document, specific to the firm's client agreements and Swiss legal obligations, produced before the question arrives rather than answered in the moment.

**Gap 6: AI tool version change documentation protocol for reporting continuity**
A protocol for documenting what changes in AI reporting tool output when a version update occurs — establishing what the compliance record must contain to explain format discontinuities across reporting periods — does not exist. This is distinct from model update notification (C232): that concerned client notification of changes to AI recommendations; this concerns documentation of changes to the reporting artifact itself, ensuring the compliance review can reconstruct why historical reports look different from current ones.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations

**FINMA — Supervisory Examination Methodology: What the Questionnaire Actually Tests**
FINMA supervisory examinations of AI deployment at wealth management firms are moving from general governance questions to specific evidential questions. The questionnaire format is designed to distinguish firms that have documentation from firms that have evidence. The distinction matters: documentation can state what was done; evidence demonstrates that what was done was adequate. At boutique wealth management scale, the compliance function has produced documentation. It has not typically produced evidence-calibrated documentation. The examination methodology difference is not published in a form that allows firms to understand what calibrates the second category. This gap — between knowing FINMA examines AI governance and knowing what specific evidential standard the examination applies — is the preparation gap.

**FINMA — Circular 2016/1 Documentation Standard: Compliance-Adequate vs. Evidentially Defensible**
FINMA Circular 2016/1 investor segregation and suitability documentation obligations specify what documentation must exist. They do not specify what evidentiary character that documentation must have. The practical difference between a compliance-adequate record and an evidentially defensible record is the difference between a document that exists and a document that would sustain scrutiny when the examination moves from "do you have documentation?" to "is this documentation sufficient to demonstrate the review was adequate?" At boutique scale, compliance functions are producing the first without a methodology for producing the second.

**ASIP — Pension Fund Trustee AI Decision Documentation Under Art. 51 BVG**
Pension fund trustees who use AI-assisted analysis for investment decisions affecting beneficiary benefits face personal liability under Art. 51 BVG. The documentation obligation is not simply that a decision was made — it is that the decision-making process was adequate. For AI-assisted decisions, demonstrating process adequacy requires documentation of how the AI was reviewed, what the review covered, and what the basis for endorsement was. The ASIP digital governance guidelines do not provide a methodology for producing this documentation at the trustee level. The gap between the obligation and the available implementation guidance is a specific trustee exposure.

**SFAMA — AI Portfolio Construction Documentation and the Professional Endorsement Standard**
SFAMA fund management documentation standards require that AI-assisted portfolio construction decisions maintain methodology documentation, input documentation, and professional endorsement documentation. The professional endorsement standard is where the evidentiary gap concentrates: endorsing an AI-generated allocation requires a basis for the endorsement, not just a record that endorsement occurred. The documentation architecture that produces an endorsement record with an evidentially adequate basis is not described in SFAMA guidance. Boutique firms using AI for portfolio construction are producing endorsement records that are incomplete as evidence.

**ISSB — AI Lineage in Sustainability Disclosure and the Evidence Standard Problem**
The ISSB standards being integrated into Swiss regulatory frameworks for listed entities and large firms create AI lineage documentation obligations for sustainability-linked analysis. When AI contributes to sustainability metrics in client portfolios, the lineage from source data through AI processing to disclosed metric must be documentable. For boutique wealth management firms advising ISSB-obligated clients, this creates a derivative documentation requirement. The preparation required is not just that lineage is documented — it is that the lineage documentation would be evidentially adequate under the standard the client's disclosures must meet. This distinction is not typically addressed in boutique-scale ISSB preparation.

**nFADP — AI Explanation Obligation Under Art. 21 and the Relay Accuracy Problem**
The revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection Art. 21 gives data subjects the right to know when automated decision-making produced a significant outcome affecting them, and to receive meaningful information about the logic involved. For boutique wealth management clients receiving AI-assisted portfolio analysis, this right is active. The obligation is not simply that the firm can state "AI was used" — it is that the firm can provide meaningful information about the AI's logic as it applied to the specific client. Producing this in a form that is technically accurate, comprehensible to the specific client, and consistent with what the client has been told about the AI's role creates a documentation challenge that generic AI disclosure language does not resolve. The relay accuracy problem — translating what the AI processed into what the client can understand — is the specific nFADP Art. 21 implementation challenge at boutique wealth management scale.

**Emerging: Federal Council AI Ordinance and the Documentation Evidence Standard**
The Federal Council AI ordinance pipeline is moving toward requirements that will include documentation standards for AI-assisted client services. The direction suggests requirements calibrated to evidentiary adequacy — documentation that demonstrates the process was sound, not just that a process existed. For boutique wealth management firms, the preparation question is not just what documentation to produce — it is what evidential character that documentation must have to sustain scrutiny under requirements that are still forming. The window before the ordinance arrives is the preparation window. What firms build now, built correctly, will not need to be rebuilt.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**6 Net-New Insights, Distinct from C214–C238 Phase 02**

**Finding 1: Peer AI model correlation as an unexamined concentration risk creating disclosure obligations that have not been identified**
When multiple Geneva boutique wealth management firms use the same AI model, and those firms serve clients with similar portfolio profiles, the AI's signals may be correlated across firms — the same types of positions flagged simultaneously across different clients at different firms, moving in the same direction when those positions move. The question clients are beginning to ask — "are you using the same model as other boutiques?" — points at a real underlying risk that has not been examined. The disclosure obligations that arise if correlated AI signals create correlated client outcomes have not been mapped. The monitoring that would detect whether correlation is occurring is not in place. The framework for assessing whether this is a concentration risk, a client disclosure issue, or both is absent. This is distinct from cross-client signal correlation within a single firm (C238 Finding 5): that concerned one firm's visibility into its own client portfolio correlations; this concerns cross-firm correlation from shared AI infrastructure, which creates different obligations and different risks.

**Finding 2: The distinction between compliance-adequate documentation and evidentially defensible documentation is the primary documentation quality gap at boutique wealth management firms**
Boutique wealth management firms are producing documentation that meets the compliance function's understanding of what is required — documentation that exists, that records what was done. They are not systematically producing documentation that would be evidentially adequate if the compliance review moved from "do you have documentation?" to "is this documentation sufficient to demonstrate the process was sound?" The gap between these two records is where supervisory examination findings concentrate. It is not a documentation quantity problem. It is a documentation quality calibration problem: the compliance function does not have a methodology for knowing what evidentiary standard the documentation it produces must meet, because that standard is not published in a form that enables documentation design. This distinction — compliance adequacy versus evidentiary defensibility — has not appeared as a standalone finding in any prior C214–C238 Phase 02.

**Finding 3: Client-requested specific AI rationale as a present-tense professional liability exposure distinct from general explanation adequacy**
When a client asks an advisor to explain why the AI made a specific recommendation for their specific portfolio, the advisor gives an approximate answer. The approximation is presented as a professional explanation. The gap between the approximation and what the AI actually processed for that client's specific situation is unmanaged. This is not a communication quality problem — it is a professional liability problem: the firm is providing explanations of AI recommendations without a protocol for ensuring those explanations are accurate. If a client acts on an explanation that was materially inaccurate, the firm's exposure is not mitigated by the fact that the explanation sounded reasonable. The relay accuracy protocol — what the firm must verify before relaying an AI explanation to a client — is a distinct boutique-scale exposure that has not appeared as a standalone finding in any prior C214–C238 Phase 02.

**Finding 4: AI recommendation conformance documentation as a reconstructability failure operating in the direction of apparent accuracy**
The documentation failure in AI-assisted decision-making is not only about overrides. It is also about conformance: when the portfolio manager follows the AI's recommendation, the record shows a correct decision. It does not show whether the manager followed the AI exactly, went further than the AI suggested, reduced the AI's recommendation, or declined to act on other AI suggestions in the same period. The record is accurate in a misleading direction — it looks complete because the outcome was correct, but it cannot support reconstruction of what the full AI-advisor interaction actually was. This creates an exposure that operates in the direction of apparent accuracy: the documentation looks fine because the decision was right, until the question is "can you show us the full picture of what the AI suggested and what you actually did?" This conformance documentation gap — distinct from override documentation — has not appeared in any prior C214–C238 Phase 02.

**Finding 5: Managing director AI liability representations to clients made before a firm-level documented position exists**
Managing directors are fielding direct questions from clients about what the firm's legal liability is if the AI recommended something wrong. The managing director's answer is personal — not documented as the firm's position, not reviewed against the firm's actual engagement terms, not calibrated to what the firm's insurance actually covers. The answer is given in the moment, in conversation, without a record. This is a distinct risk management failure: the firm's exposure is being managed through informal verbal representations by an individual, not through documented firm-level positions that have been reviewed against legal obligations and insurance coverage. The AI liability position document — written, firm-specific, reviewed — is a discrete deliverable that has not been produced at most boutiques. This has not appeared as a standalone finding in any prior C214–C238 Phase 02.

**Finding 6: AI reporting tool version updates creating undocumented format discontinuities across compliance review periods**
When boutique AI reporting tools are updated — vendor-driven, configuration-driven, or workflow-driven — the output format changes. Historical reports and current reports are in different formats. The compliance review that examines AI reporting history encounters an unexplained discontinuity. The version update happened. The documentation of what changed and why — sufficient to prevent the discontinuity from being read as an anomaly — does not exist. This is distinct from model update notification obligations (C232): that concerned whether clients were notified when AI recommendations changed; this concerns the documentary artifact itself — the format integrity of the reporting history, which is a precondition for the compliance review being able to read that history coherently. The AI tool version change documentation protocol is a discrete boutique-scale gap that has not appeared in any prior C214–C238 Phase 02.
