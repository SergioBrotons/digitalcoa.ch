## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

### Signal 1: The Model Update Notification Gap — Silent Recommendation Condition Change

Geneva boutique wealth management firms are discovering that AI model updates change the analytical basis on which client portfolio recommendations were made — and that notification of these updates from AI vendors is inconsistent, delayed, or absent. A client holds a position recommended under Model Version 3.2. The vendor updates to Version 3.3. The model update may change how the AI evaluates the same underlying data. The position the client holds was recommended under conditions that the model update has altered. The firm has no notification obligation protocol from the AI vendor, no internal process for determining whether a model update materially changes the basis of a recommendation, and no client communication requirement that would trigger a disclosure about the changed analytical context.

The failure mode: the client portfolio contains positions whose recommendation basis has been altered by model updates that the firm did not surface to the client. When something goes wrong with a position, the question of whether the original recommendation is still valid under the updated model is unanswerable without a recomputation protocol and a model update notification system that the firm does not have.

### Signal 2: The Documentation Adequacy Gap — Process Records vs. Evidentiary Value

Boutique Geneva wealth management firms have AI governance documentation that records process — that AI was used, that a human reviewed the output, that a recommendation was made. In a regulatory examination or client dispute, the documentation is produced as evidence of adequate oversight. The documentation does not, however, demonstrate what it purports to prove: that the human review was substantive, that the recommendation was sound, or that the client understood what they were accepting. The documentation records the occurrence of steps. It does not record the quality of those steps.

The paradox: the firm that has invested in AI governance documentation may have documentation that is comprehensive in volume and weak in evidentiary value — it shows what was done without demonstrating that what was done was adequate. The documentation creates an appearance of governance that is not substantively confirmed.

### Signal 3: The Client Explanation Expectation — AI Accountability Demanded at a Level the Firm Cannot Meet

Geneva wealth management clients are increasingly asking questions about AI-generated recommendations that the firm's documentation cannot answer: why did the AI recommend this specific action, what alternatives were considered, what would the model recommend under different conditions, what are the model's known limitations, how confident should the client be? These questions represent genuine client comprehension expectations — the client wants to understand what they are invested in. The firm's documentation cannot provide answers in client-comprehensible terms because the AI's analytical process is not designed to produce explanations at the level of detail a non-technical client requires.

The failure mode: the firm discloses AI involvement, the client accepts the disclosure, the client then asks a substantive question about the AI's reasoning, and the firm must either admit it cannot explain the AI's reasoning in client-comprehensible terms or provide an explanation that is accurate but too technical to be useful. Neither option produces genuine client comprehension, and neither protects the firm if the client's position deteriorates and they challenge the recommendation.

### Signal 4: The Regulatory Anticipation Problem — Preparing for Questions That Have Not Been Published

Geneva boutique wealth management firms understand that FINMA's forthcoming AI governance framework will impose requirements they need to prepare for. The preparation challenge is that the specific questions the framework will require answers to have not been published — so the firm is preparing by guessing which documentation will be required and building that documentation retrospectively. The risk is investing documentation resources in the wrong areas while leaving the actual requirements unaddressed.

The structural problem: the firm cannot know with certainty what FINMA's framework will require until it is published. The firm that waits for publication will have less preparation time than the firm that correctly anticipates the requirements. The firm that prepares speculatively may build documentation that does not match the actual requirements. The asymmetry between anticipation risk and preparation certainty is producing a specific advisory opportunity for firms that can read the regulatory formation process accurately.

### Signal 5: The nFADP Explanation Obligation — Compliant in Form, Incomplete in Substance

Geneva wealth management firms are subject to nFADP Article 21 explanation obligations for AI-generated recommendations. The practical compliance approach is to provide a disclosure that AI was used, what type of AI was used, and what the recommendation was. This satisfies the regulatory form requirement. It does not satisfy the substance of the explanation obligation if the client cannot use the explanation to understand what they are accepting — which is the definition of meaningful explanation under the law.

The failure mode: the firm provides technically compliant nFADP explanations that are client-incomprehensible, creating documentation that shows an explanation was provided when the client could not meaningfully evaluate the recommendation. The firm is exposed to a challenge that the explanation obligation was not substantively met, even though the form of the requirement was satisfied.

### Signal 6: The Accountability Attribution Chain — Where the Firm's Responsibility Begins and Ends

Boutique Geneva wealth management firms are using AI tools in client reporting, portfolio analysis, and compliance documentation — generating outputs the firm presents as its own professional work. The accountability attribution chain is unclear: the firm is accountable for the recommendation, the AI generated the recommendation, the firm configured the AI, the vendor built the AI. Where does the firm's responsibility begin? Where does it end? No Geneva professional standard or FINMA guidance has defined the attribution boundary.

The consequence: the firm is accountable for AI outputs it cannot fully explain, cannot fully reproduce, and cannot fully attribute to a specific analytical process — because the AI's reasoning, even when accurate, is not transparent in terms the firm can document and defend. The accountability chain has gaps that no one has defined how to close.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations

### FINMA — Model Update Notification Requirements and the Domestic Implementation Pipeline

FINMA's forthcoming AI model risk circular is expected to address model change management as a specific documentation requirement. The Basel Committee's Technical Amendment on AI model risk management provides the international foundation for what domestic implementation will require. For Geneva boutique wealth management firms, the key question is whether FINMA's framework will impose affirmative notification obligations on AI model updates — specifically, whether the firm must notify clients when an AI model update has materially altered the analytical basis on which an active recommendation was made, and what the notification timeline and content requirements are.

The practical compliance planning challenge: model updates from AI vendors are not always communicated with sufficient lead time for the firm to assess impact before the update takes effect. The firm that waits to assess impact after a model update is already behind. The firm that proactively assesses every model update for client position impact is investing compliance resources speculatively — the correct approach, but resource-intensive. The advisory opportunity is to build the model update impact assessment methodology before FINMA requires it, so that the firm's process is already in place when the requirement arrives.

### ASIP — Pension Fund Trustee Duty of Care and AI Oversight Competency Documentation

ASIP's developing trustee education curriculum will create a specific expectation for what pension fund trustees must know about AI-assisted investment decisions — not just that AI was used, but what the AI does, what its limitations are, and what the trustee's oversight responsibilities are with respect to AI-assisted decisions. For Geneva boutique wealth management firms advising occupational pension funds: the ASIP competency expectation means the firm should be able to demonstrate that the trustees it advises have received adequate AI governance briefing for their oversight role.

The documentation requirement is forward-facing: firms should be building AI oversight competency documentation for the trustees they advise now, before ASIP's curriculum is finalized, so that the documentation demonstrates proactive oversight rather than reactive compliance with a newly published standard.

### SFAMA — AI Documentation Expectations Through Institutional Counterparty Requirements

SFAMA member institutions increasingly apply SFAMA AI documentation standards as a procurement condition for wealth management service relationships. Geneva boutique firms that serve as sub-advisors or service providers to SFAMA members face a specific documentation expectation: evidence of human oversight, model change management processes, and AI recommendation audit trails. The SFAMA standard does not apply directly to non-members, but functions as a de facto market access requirement through institutional counterparty pressure.

The practical implication: boutique firms that anticipate serving SFAMA-member institutions should build SFAMA-aligned AI documentation now, before a procurement relationship creates the requirement, rather than building it retrospectively under procurement deadline pressure.

### ISSB — AI-Generated Sustainability Claims and the Disclosure Accuracy Standard

ISSB-aligned disclosures require issuers to account for the accuracy of sustainability claims in client-facing documents. Geneva boutique wealth management firms that use AI tools to generate ESG ratings, climate risk scores, or sustainability portfolio characteristics are generating claims that enter disclosures the firm is accountable for — regardless of the AI vendor's proprietary methodology. The ISSB accuracy standard applies to the output claim, not to the AI tool's internal process.

The compliance planning implication: the firm should audit every AI tool that touches sustainability-related portfolio analysis now, before ISSB-aligned disclosure requirements become mandatory for the firm's client base, and determine whether each tool's outputs constitute claims that would need to be defensible under an accuracy standard. This audit should precede, not follow, the first ISSB-aligned disclosure the firm prepares.

### nFADP — Explanation Quality as Distinct from Disclosure Form

The nFADP Article 21 right to explanation for automated decisions requires that explanations be provided in an intelligible form — meaning a form the client can use to understand what they are accepting. The Swiss Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner has indicated that technically accurate but client-incomprehensible explanations do not satisfy the substance of the obligation, even if the disclosure form is complete. For Geneva boutique wealth management firms: the explanation obligation is also a client communication quality obligation, requiring that the explanation be both legally sufficient and practically comprehensible.

The compliance planning implication: the firm should evaluate every AI explanation it currently provides to clients against a client comprehension standard — not just a legal accuracy standard. The documentation should demonstrate that explanations produced comprehension, not just that they were provided in the required form. This is a documentation quality requirement that the firm's current nFADP compliance process may not measure.
