## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

### Signal 1: The Liability Tail — Accountability for Recommendations Under Discontinued Model Versions

Boutique Geneva wealth management firms are discovering that AI-generated recommendations made under earlier model versions create a liability tail that extends beyond the life of the model. A recommendation produced under a 2023 model version informed a client portfolio decision. That model has since been updated, discontinued, or replaced. The client holds the resulting position. The firm cannot reproduce the recommendation from the original model. It cannot fully explain why the model at the time made the recommendation it did. It is accountable for a recommendation produced under conditions it cannot reconstruct.

The failure mode is distinct from current-model attribution failure: the problem is not only that the current AI recommendation cannot be traced. It is that historical recommendations cannot be reconstructed because the model that produced them no longer exists in its original form. The documentation needed to close this gap — a recomputation protocol, a model version history with decision records — was never created when the model was active. The liability tail grows with every AI-assisted recommendation that ages under an updated or discontinued model.

### Signal 2: The AI Sophistication Paradox — More Capable AI Produces Less Documentable Recommendations

Boutique Geneva wealth management firms using more sophisticated AI tools are encountering a counterintuitive problem: as AI analytical capability increases, the AI's output becomes harder to document, explain, and defend in human reviewable terms. A simple AI tool produces a straightforward output that the advisor can verify. A sophisticated AI tool produces multi-factor analysis, contextual inferences, and conditional projections that exceed what the advisor can evaluate from first principles. The human review that is supposed to serve as the accountability check is limited by the reviewer's capacity to understand what they are reviewing.

The structural paradox: the firms deploying the most advanced AI, which are often the most sophisticated and well-run boutiques, may have the weakest human-reviewable documentation trails for their AI-generated recommendations. The quality of the AI and the quality of the documentation are moving in opposite directions — and no boutique firm has yet developed a methodology for managing this inversion.

### Signal 3: Model Version Correlation — The Systemic Risk No Boutique Is Measuring

Multiple Geneva boutique wealth management firms are using the same AI model vendors for similar portfolio analysis functions. When these firms generate portfolio recommendations for clients simultaneously, using the same AI model version, the resulting client positions may be correlated in ways that none of the individual firms are measuring. The correlation is invisible at the individual firm level. It becomes visible only at the market level — when a model update triggers simultaneous repositioning recommendations across multiple boutiques, or when a model failure or limitation affects correlated positions across multiple client portfolios simultaneously.

The failure mode is not a single firm's AI governance failure. It is a systemic risk that has no current measurement or monitoring framework at boutique scale. FINMA has not published guidance on AI systemic risk monitoring for wealth management firms. No self-regulatory framework exists. The firms using the same AI models are not exchanging information about their collective correlated exposure. The correlation is building silently.

### Signal 4: The Client Comprehension Gap — Acceptance Without Understanding

Geneva wealth management clients receive AI-generated recommendations and sign acknowledgments that they understand and accept the recommendation. In many cases, the acceptance is genuine but the understanding is incomplete. The client does not have the analytical framework to evaluate what the AI actually did, what alternatives were considered, or what the model's limitations mean for the recommendation. The firm discloses AI involvement. The disclosure satisfies a regulatory or contractual requirement. It does not produce genuine client comprehension.

The paradox: client acceptance of an AI-generated recommendation without genuine comprehension provides incomplete liability protection. If the recommendation proves incorrect and the client challenges it, the fact that they accepted it is weakened by evidence that they could not have evaluated the recommendation on its merits. The acknowledgment they signed was accurate in form but incomplete in substance — and this gap is becoming more visible as AI-generated recommendations become more complex and harder to explain in client-comprehensible terms.

### Signal 5: The Human-AI Performance Boundary — No Methodology for Adequate Review

Boutique Geneva wealth management firms have implemented human review processes for AI-generated recommendations without a methodology for determining what adequate human review actually requires. The human reviewer confirms that the recommendation looks reasonable. They compare it to prior recommendations. They check that the AI did not produce an obvious error. In many cases, this is the full extent of the review — not because the reviewer is inadequate, but because no methodology exists for determining what human review of a multi-factor AI recommendation must cover to be considered substantive.

The consequence: the human review process is documented as having occurred. It is not documented as having been adequate. When a recommendation is challenged, the firm can show that a human reviewed it. It may not be able to show that the human review met any specific standard of substantive evaluation, because no such standard has been defined for AI-assisted recommendations at boutique scale. The documentation records process without demonstrating quality.

### Signal 6: The International Standard Pipeline — Obligations Arriving Through Channels the Boutique Cannot See

The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision and IOSCO are actively developing guidance on AI use in financial services that will influence FINMA's AI governance framework. Geneva boutique wealth management firms are subject to FINMA supervision. They are not party to Basel Committee or IOSCO deliberations. Yet the obligations they will face from FINMA are being shaped by international conversations in which they have no direct participation and limited visibility.

The practical consequence: a Geneva boutique that prepares for FINMA's published requirements based on current domestic guidance may find that its preparation is incomplete when the next FINMA circular reflects the international pipeline's latest conclusions. The standard is moving. The boutique is preparing for where it was, not where it is going. The firms that understand the international standard formation process — Basel Committee AI guidance, IOSCO retail AI recommendations, ongoing BCBS-FINMA technical cooperation — have a material advantage in anticipating what domestic implementation will require before the requirement is published.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis: Gaps, What No One Is Addressing

### Gap 1: AI Correlation Risk Assessment — Are Your Peers' AI Portfolios Moving With Yours?

No advisory service exists for Geneva boutique wealth management firms to assess whether their AI-driven portfolio recommendations are correlated with competitor recommendations — specifically, whether multiple boutiques using the same AI model vendor and version are generating simultaneously similar client positions that create undisclosed aggregate correlation risk. This is distinct from portfolio concentration analysis (which examines individual client portfolios) and from systemic risk monitoring (which is a regulatory function, not a boutique advisory service). This specifically addresses the invisible systemic risk at boutique scale: the boutique cannot measure its correlation exposure because the information about what other boutiques are doing is not available to it, and no advisor has built the methodology for estimating aggregate AI-driven correlation from publicly observable model vendor patterns.

### Gap 2: AI Sophistication-Documentation Calibration — Managing the Inverse Relationship

No advisory service exists to help boutique Geneva wealth management firms manage the inverse relationship between AI analytical sophistication and human-reviewable documentation quality — specifically, a methodology for determining what additional documentation, explanation, or client communication is required as AI analytical capability increases beyond what human reviewers can evaluate from first principles. This is distinct from AI governance documentation frameworks (which address documentation existence) and from human review process design (which assumes the review is adequate without measuring the inverse relationship with AI sophistication). This specifically addresses the paradox that the most sophisticated AI deployments may produce the weakest human-reviewable accountability records, requiring a deliberate documentation compensation strategy that no framework currently provides.

### Gap 3: Client AI Comprehension Audit — Measuring What Acceptance Actually Covers

This is distinct from nFADP disclosure compliance review (which examines whether the disclosure was made in the required form) and from client satisfaction surveys (which measure whether clients were happy, not whether they understood). This specifically addresses the liability gap between formal acceptance and genuine comprehension: a client who accepted an AI-generated recommendation they did not understand has limited legal protection, and the firm that approved the recommendation without ensuring comprehension has an incomplete accountability record.

### Gap 4: Model Version Liability Mapping — Documenting the Tail for Discontinued Models

No advisory service addresses the specific problem of mapping liability exposure for recommendations made under AI model versions that have since been updated, discontinued, or replaced — the documentation architecture for reconstructing what a discontinued model recommended, whether recomputation is possible, whether clients need to be notified, and what the firm's accountability position is for a recommendation it cannot reproduce. This is distinct from model versioning (which tracks what version was active when) and from attribution reconstruction for current-model outputs (which is the simpler problem of tracing a current recommendation). This specifically addresses the liability tail problem: recommendations made under discontinued models create ongoing accountability that cannot be discharged without a recomputation protocol and a client communication strategy for each affected position.

### Gap 5: Human-AI Performance Boundary Methodology — What Adequate Review Actually Requires

No advisory provider has developed a practical methodology for boutique Geneva wealth management firms to determine what constitutes adequate human review of AI-generated recommendations at different levels of AI analytical complexity — a tiered framework that specifies what human review must cover, what the reviewer must be able to evaluate independently, and what documentation demonstrates substantive review rather than procedural sign-off. This is distinct from human review policy development (which produces a process document) and from AI literacy training (which addresses general competency). This specifically addresses the gap between "a human reviewed it" and "the human review was adequate" — the documentation quality problem that exists when the review process is documented but its adequacy cannot be demonstrated.

### Gap 6: International Standard Pipeline Intelligence — Basel, IOSCO, and the Domestic Implementation Timeline

No advisory service exists to help Geneva boutique wealth management firms understand how international standard-setting processes — specifically Basel Committee AI guidance development, IOSCO retail AI recommendations, and ongoing BCBS-FINMA technical cooperation — translate into domestic FINMA obligations that the firm will face in the next 12 to 24 months. ## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations

### FINMA — The International Pipeline Problem and Domestic Implementation Lag

FINMA's forthcoming AI model risk governance circular is being developed in a context where Basel Committee on Banking Supervision guidance on AI in financial services is actively evolving. The Basel Committee's Technical Amendment on AI model risk, expected in final form by end-2026, will influence the direction of FINMA's AI framework. For Geneva boutique wealth management firms, the practical implication is that domestic implementation guidance may lag international developments by 12 to 18 months — meaning the obligations the firm faces from FINMA in 2027 may reflect Basel positions reached in 2025-2026, while the Basel Committee is simultaneously moving toward additional guidance that will shape FINMA's next circular.

The compliance planning problem: a boutique firm that calibrates its AI governance to FINMA's current published requirements is preparing for where the framework was, not where it is going. The Basel Committee's AI guidance addresses model inventory, model validation, model change management, and human AI oversight — all areas where FINMA's forthcoming circular will reflect Basel influence. Firms that understand the international pipeline have a material preparation advantage. The specific Basel AI guidance areas that will most directly affect FINMA implementation at boutique wealth management scale: model change notification requirements, human oversight documentation standards, and model concentration risk assessment.

### ASIP — Pension Fund Trustee AI Competency and the Beneficiary Welfare Standard

ASIP's engagement with AI governance at pension fund level is accelerating through trustee education initiatives and the ASIP specialist commission on digital transformation. The BVG duty of care standard applies fully to AI-assisted investment decisions — pension fund trustees who approve AI-assisted asset allocation recommendations are accountable for the quality of that oversight, including understanding what the AI does, what its limitations are, and whether the trustees' oversight process is adequate for the AI system in use.

For Geneva boutique wealth management firms advising occupational pension funds: the ASIP accountability surface creates a specific documentation requirement for demonstrating that pension fund trustees have received adequate information to exercise oversight over AI-assisted decisions. This goes beyond the firm having AI governance documentation — it requires documentation that the trustee's oversight role was substantively informed, not merely procedurally notified. The ASIP trustee education curriculum is developing a specific AI governance module for pension fund trustees, which will raise the baseline expectation for what trustees should know about AI-assisted investment decisions.

### SFAMA — De Facto Market Access Standard Operating Through Institutional Counterparties

SFAMA guidance on AI-assisted portfolio management is functioning increasingly as a de facto market access standard through institutional counterparty requirements. Geneva boutique wealth management firms that interact with SFAMA-member institutions — as sub-advisors, as service providers, as counterparty relationship managers — are finding that SFAMA-aligned AI governance documentation is becoming a practical requirement for maintaining these relationships. Non-SFAMA-member firms are not formally subject to SFAMA requirements. In practice, they face SFAMA-aligned documentation demands from institutional counterparties who apply SFAMA standards as a procurement condition.

For boutique firms: the practical compliance obligation is to understand which counterparties are applying SFAMA standards as a de facto requirement, what specific documentation those counterparties expect, and whether the firm's current AI governance documentation meets the expectation. The SFAMA AI guidance addresses human oversight, model change management, and documentation — areas that overlap substantially with what FINMA will require, creating an opportunity for documentation efficiency if the firm understands how the two frameworks converge.

### ISSB — AI-Generated Sustainability Claims Entering Client Disclosures

ISSB-aligned sustainability disclosure requirements are creating a specific AI governance documentation obligation for Geneva boutique wealth management firms that use AI tools to generate ESG ratings, climate risk scores, or sustainability screening outputs. The accountability structure is direct: if an AI-generated sustainability claim enters a client disclosure, the issuer of that disclosure bears accountability for its accuracy — regardless of whether the AI tool's methodology is proprietary and undisclosed.

For boutique firms: the practical obligation is to audit every AI tool that touches sustainability-related portfolio analysis and determine whether its outputs constitute claims that would need to be defensible under ISSB-aligned disclosure standards. This is a specific documentation requirement at the claim level — not the AI tool's technical documentation, but documentation of what the tool is asserting about each portfolio's sustainability characteristics, and whether that assertion can be defended if challenged. The FSB's ISSB implementation monitoring report notes that AI-generated ESG data quality is a primary concern for disclosure accuracy, specifically naming the lack of documented AI methodology as the core accountability gap.

### nFADP — Client AI Explanation Quality and the Comprehension Standard

The nFADP Article 21 right to explanation for automated decision-making creates a specific quality obligation for AI-generated recommendations at Geneva wealth management firms — the firm must be able to provide a meaningful explanation of how the AI reached its recommendation, not merely a statement that AI was used. The explanation must be in a form the client can understand.

For boutique firms: the operational challenge is that sophisticated AI tools generate recommendations through processes that are difficult to explain in client-comprehensible terms. An explanation that satisfies the regulatory requirement may not satisfy the comprehension standard if the client cannot use it to understand what they are accepting. A firm that provides technically compliant explanations without ensuring client comprehension has satisfied the form of the obligation while leaving the substance unaddressed — and creating documentation that shows an explanation was provided when the client could not actually use it. The nFADP explanation obligation is therefore also a client communication quality obligation: the explanation must be both legally sufficient and genuinely comprehensible, which requires different documentation than a technically accurate but client-incomprehensible description of the AI's analytical process.
