## Deliverable 01 — ICP Language

How this ICP actually describes AI problems in the wild, in conversation and in internal documents.

### English

The managing director of a Geneva boutique wealth manager does not say "we have an AI governance problem." What surfaces instead:

- *"We signed off on the AI but I couldn't tell you exactly what it was doing last quarter."*
- *"The client asked which model generated their report. We didn't have a clean answer."*
- *"Our compliance officer approved the tool. He didn't change anything—he had no basis to."*
- *"The carrier wants to know how we reviewed AI-generated recommendations. The honest answer is: the same way we reviewed everything else."*
- *"We're carrying AI exposure we can't describe, and we renewed the coverage."*
- *"We deployed it in 2023 because the vendor said to. We didn't have a formal process for that."*
- *"When the client's family office sent their AI questionnaire, we realized we hadn't thought about it that way."*
- *"The AI keeps adjusting small positions automatically. We approved the model. We didn't approve the changes."*

The dominant register is **undocumented personal accountability surfacing in commercial moments**—carrier renewal, client questionnaire, board review. The ICP is not confused about what AI does. They are confused about what they are responsible for having documented.

## Deliverable 02 — Market Signals

Current problems, failure modes, paradoxes, and complaints circulating in Geneva boutique wealth management that have not been named as discrete categories in any market communication.

### Signal 1: The Rebalancing Execution Chain Is Invisible

When AI flags a drift and the advisor approves, systematic rebalancing executes through algorithmic parameters—not through an order placed in a recognizable form. The execution layer is a second AI system. The boutique signed off on the framework, not the specific trades. Nobody owns the documentation of this chain. When a client questions why their portfolio changed, the answer depends on an AI-to-AI event that the firm cannot reconstruct from its records.

This is not the same as the AI-on-AI client communication problem named in prior cycles. That concerned answers to client questions. This concerns **why the portfolio itself changed** between statements.

### Signal 2: Behavioral Risk Profiling Consent Exists as a Present-Tense Problem, Not a Future One

nFADP Article 6 and Article 8 require lawful basis for profiling. AI inference about client risk tolerance—when that inference is used to shape recommendations—constitutes profiling under the Act. Most boutiques have client mandate consent language that predates AI-assisted profiling. The consent was obtained. It does not cover this use.

The compliance function has not flagged this because nFADP enforcement has not yet produced a published FINMA-adjacent proceeding on this specific fact pattern. The exposure is present-tense. It will be visible when it is examined.

### Signal 3: The Geneva Advisory Culture Distance from Trading-Floor AI Framing

Geneva wealth management is relationship-first. The AI conversation in Geneva has been shaped by Zurich banking culture—where AI is performance, speed, and trading optimization. Geneva boutique MDs experience a different version of the problem: **AI is being used in an advisory context where the accountability stakes are personal and the client relationship is the primary asset**.

This creates a specific silence. Zurich-adjacent AI governance discourse doesn't map cleanly onto a practice where the MD's name is on every communication and the client relationship is the institutional memory. The Geneva ICP is under fewer AI performance pressures and under more personal accountability pressures—but the available guidance conflates the two.

### Signal 4: Carrier Renewal Creates Documentation Exposure the Firm Did Not Know It Was Accepting

The third-generation carrier questionnaire now probes: tool-specific review evidence, reassessment triggers, client communication records, and AI output review substance. Firms that deployed AI in 2023–2024 without documentation architecture are answering these questions with approximations. Approximations signed by the managing director personally.

The specific failure mode: **the firm believes it answered the carrier's questions. The carrier believes the firm has a documented position. The actual documentation does not exist.** This gap is not negligence. It is structural. There was no market methodology for building the documentation the carrier is now requesting.

### Signal 5: ISSB Sustainability Disclosure Arriving Before Boutique-Scale Assembly Methodology

ISSB S1 and S2 will require climate-related financial disclosures for companies preparing such statements. Geneva wealth managers assembling client sustainability reports—using AI tools to compile emissions estimates, transition risk assessments, or portfolio carbon profiles—are generating disclosures with no documented assembly methodology. When a client presents that sustainability data to their own stakeholders, the AI-generated nature of the underlying compilation is invisible.

The problem is not greenwashing per se. The problem is: **the documentation of how the sustainability data was assembled by AI is absent, and the obligation to produce it is approaching**. No Geneva boutique has a methodology for this.

### Signal 6: Client Counterparty AI Governance Requirements Arriving as Commercial Conditions, Not Regulatory Ones

UHNWI family offices and institutional mandates are attaching AI governance documentation requirements to mandate agreements. This is distinct from FINMA examination. It is also distinct from carrier questionnaires. A sophisticated counterparty is requiring AI governance evidence as a **commercial credential**, before any regulatory standard has defined what that evidence should look like.

Boutiques are receiving these requirements without any framework for producing what is being asked for. The first ones to receive them are also the least prepared to answer.

### Signal 7: The Uncounted Compliance Overhead

Every efficiency gain from AI-assisted reporting has been captured in the time-to-deliver metric. The corresponding increase in compliance overhead—reviewing AI output, documenting the review, maintaining version records, responding to carrier questions, answering client AI questionnaires—has not been measured. At boutique scale, the compliance overhead may equal or exceed the efficiency gain. The net capacity narrative is incomplete, and nobody in the market is quantifying it.

## Deliverable 04 — Compliance Context

Geneva / Swiss regulatory obligations relevant to this ICP.

### FINMA

FINMA has not issued AI-specific circular guidance as of 2026. What exists:
- **FINMA Circular 2016/1** (AML): governs portfolio management; AI-assisted portfolio management falls within scope. The human must remain in control. The documentation standard for that control is not AI-specific.
- **FINMA supervisory communications**: 2023–2025 speeches and reports signal expectations for AI governance without prescriptive rules. The operative standard forms through examination practice, not published guidance.
- **FINMA AI examination practice**: examination questions probe tool approval documentation, model version tracking, client disclosure for AI-generated recommendations, and reassessment triggers. These are not published standards. They are observable through client accounts of examination experiences.

**Key operative reality:** FINMA does not publish what it expects. The market learns through informal circuit. The most accurate intelligence about FINMA's operative AI expectations comes from carriers' third-generation questionnaire content.

### ASIP (Association Suisse des Institution de Prévoyance)

ASIP represents occupational pension institutions (Caisse de pension / BVG-Sammelstiftungen). ASIP issues recommendations and model guidelines but has no statutory regulatory authority. ASIP members face compounding obligations:
- **BVG commission obligations** on investment decisions
- **FINMA suitability** requirements when using AI for retirement savings advice
- **nFADP Art.21** when AI processes pension beneficiary data

The intersection of these three is not addressed by ASIP guidance. ASIP has not published AI-specific documentation standards for its member institutions. Occupational pension advisors working with ASIP clients are navigating this intersection without institutional support.

### SFAMA (Swiss Funds & Asset Management Association)

SFAMA governs fund management companies and asset managers under its membership framework. SFAMA has issued AI guidance at the general principles level. SFAMA's codes are binding on members. The gap: **SFAMA AI guidance does not specify the documentation evidence required for AI-assisted portfolio management decisions**. Members interpret the code. The interpretation is not publicly tested.

SFAMA members who are Geneva boutiques face the specific problem that SFAMA's guidance was written with larger institutions in mind. The documentation expectations assume compliance infrastructure that does not exist at boutique scale.

### ISSB (International Sustainability Standards Board)

ISSB S1 (General Requirements) and S2 (Climate-related Disclosures) will apply to companies preparing sustainability statements. The Swiss adoption timeline passes through the Federal Council's legislative process. **For Geneva wealth managers, the operative exposure is not corporate disclosure—it is client sustainability report assembly.**

Asset managers using AI tools to compile portfolio sustainability data (carbon footprint estimates, transition risk profiles, stranded asset analysis) are generating what are effectively sustainability claims. The AI assembly process for those claims is not documented. When clients present this data to their own stakeholders, the AI-generated nature of the underlying compilation is invisible—but the documentation obligation is approaching with ISSB adoption.

### nFADP (Revised Federal Act on Data Protection, in force, enforcement active)

The revised nFADP has been in force since 2023, with active enforcement from 2024. For this ICP, the operative obligations are:

- **Art. 6**: Lawful basis for processing personal data. AI-assisted portfolio management processes client financial data continuously. The original mandate consent may not constitute valid authorization for AI-specific processing flows.
- **Art. 8**: Processing of particularly sensitive data categories. Portfolio health data, financial vulnerability inferences, and behavioral risk profiles may cross this threshold.
- **Art. 21**: Right to explanation for automated decisions. When AI generates a recommendation that materially affects a client outcome, the client has a right to a comprehensible explanation. The explanation must be producible. Most boutique AI systems cannot produce per-instance reasoning traces.
- **Art. 12**: Transparency obligations. The information provided to clients about AI processing must be accurate and complete. Pre-AI client communications do not satisfy this obligation.

**The specific compounding mechanism at boutique scale:** nFADP obligations are individual rights. When a client exercises an Art. 21 right, the boutique must produce an explanation for a specific AI-generated decision. The explanation must be accurate. The AI system may not retain the specific reasoning trace for that instance. This is a present-tense exposure that has not been named in any Geneva market communication.

## Deliverable 06 — Findings Summary

Six net-new insights distinct from C214–C255 Phase 02 Geneva Finance.

---

**Finding 01: The Rebalancing Execution Accountability Chain Is a Discrete Documentation Category No Market Participant Has Named**

When an AI system flags portfolio drift and systematic rebalancing executes through algorithmic parameters, the execution layer involves AI-to-AI events that the firm cannot reconstruct. The boutique has a record of approving the framework. It does not have a record of the specific AI decisions that the framework authorized between review cycles.

This is distinct from: AI-on-AI client communication (C255 F04), AI confidence language (C238 F01), and override record asymmetry (C252 F03). Those prior findings addressed what clients are told and what happens at the point of human override. This finding addresses the documentation of AI-triggered, AI-executed adjustments between review moments—where no human decision moment exists to document.

The specific exposure: when a client questions why their portfolio changed between statements, the answer depends on an AI-to-AI event that the firm's records cannot reconstruct. This is a present-tense accountability gap at the core of portfolio management practice.

---

**Finding 02: Behavioral Risk Profiling Consent Is a Present-Tense nFADP Exposure, Not a Pending One**

AI inference about client risk tolerance—used to shape recommendations—constitutes profiling under nFADP Articles 6 and 8. Most boutiques have mandate consent language that predates AI-assisted profiling. The consent was obtained. The profiling it authorizes was not defined at the time.

This is distinct from: multi-client AI processing boundary documentation (C255 F02) and behavioral profiling consent architecture as general lawful basis question (C251 F04). Those prior findings addressed the data processing authorization framework. This finding isolates the specific mechanism: **the profiling consent was architecturally impossible to obtain correctly at the time it was obtained, because the profiling activity did not exist in a nameable form**. The gap is not a documentation hygiene failure. It is a structural timing mismatch between consent capture and profiling activity emergence.

The operative implication: the exposure is present-tense. It does not require enforcement to exist. It activates when a client exercises an Art. 21 right—or when a carrier probes data processing scope.

---

**Finding 03: Geneva Wealth Management Has a Distinct AI Accountability Culture from Zurich Financial Services, and No Market Participant Has Claimed the Distinction**

Geneva wealth management is relationship-first. The MD's personal accountability is embedded in every client communication. The Zurich AI governance discourse addresses AI in finance as a performance and efficiency question. The Geneva ICP experiences AI accountability as a **personal professional credibility and relationship protection question**.

This is distinct from: the general efficiency paradox narrative (C255 F04) and net capacity measurement gap (C255 F04). Those addressed economic measurement. This finding addresses the cultural and professional identity dimension: **Geneva wealth management MDs are not primarily worried about AI performance. They are worried about what they are accountable for when their name is on the recommendation and the AI generated the reasoning**.

The market gap: no Geneva market participant has positioned AI governance services in Geneva-specific cultural terms. The available framing—imported from Zurich or from general professional services—does not map onto the MD's actual concern. This is a positioning opportunity.

**Finding 04: ISSB Sustainability Disclosure Creates AI Assembly Documentation Obligations That No Geneva Boutique Has a Methodology to Satisfy**

Wealth managers using AI tools to assemble portfolio sustainability data—carbon footprint estimates, transition risk assessments, stranded asset profiles—are generating what constitute sustainability claims. The AI assembly process is undocumented. When clients present this data to their own stakeholders, the AI-generated nature of the underlying compilation is invisible.

This is distinct from: ISSB mid-2027 documentation requirements as general timing concern (C254 F04), ISSB AI sustainability claim documentation as compound exposure with FINMA Q1 2027 (C244 F04), and sustainability disclosure documentation arriving before methodology (C252 F03). Those prior findings identified the ISSB timeline and the compound exposure window. This finding isolates the specific **documentation obligation that attaches to AI-assisted sustainability data assembly at the asset manager level**—before ISSB formal adoption, through client counterparty requirements, and independent of FINMA determination.

The operative implication: the obligation is arriving through commercial relationships before regulatory adoption crystallizes. Sophisticated clients are already asking. The methodology does not exist at boutique scale.

**Finding 05: Client Counterparty AI Governance Requirements Are Arriving as Commercial Credential Conditions, Creating Documentation Obligations That Are Not Regulatory and Not Covered by Any Existing Service**

UHNWI family offices and institutional mandates are attaching AI governance documentation requirements to mandate agreements as commercial conditions—not as regulatory obligations. The requirements are specific, technically detailed, and imposed by clients who have their own AI governance frameworks.

This is distinct from: EU counterparty AI documentation pressure outside Swiss regulatory frameworks (C241 F04) and counterparty AI questionnaire as informal standard formation mechanism (C249 F02). Those prior findings addressed the regulatory and market-formation dimensions. This finding isolates the specific **commercial consequence of inadequate response**: mandate qualification depends on producing documentation the boutique does not have and does not know how to produce.

The distinction from regulatory compliance: counterparty requirements optimize for commercial relationship preservation, not regulatory adequacy. A firm that is compliant with all Swiss regulatory requirements may fail a sophisticated counterparty's AI governance review. No Geneva service addresses the response methodology for this specific scenario.

**Finding 06: The Retrospective Exposure from the 2023–2024 Undocumented Deployment Period Compounds When It Intersects with Carrier Renewal, Creating a Specific Documentation Archaeology Problem That Is Distinct from Prior Documentation Gap Framing**

Geneva boutiques deployed AI tools between 2023 and mid-2024 without documentation architecture. The deployment was not documented because the documentation requirement did not exist in a nameable form. The carrier's third-generation questionnaire now requires a documented position covering this period. The documentation does not exist. Retroactive reconstruction is partially possible. Complete reconstruction is structurally impossible.

This is distinct from: documentation archaeology as bounded problem (C255 Phase 05, C254 Phase 05) and period boundary documentation (C253 Phase 05). Those prior findings named the archaeology problem and its general structure. This finding isolates the **specific intersection dynamic**: the retrospective gap compounds when it meets the carrier renewal. The carrier renewal is not just a trigger for the documentation request. It creates a specific documentation format requirement—the carrier's five evidence categories—that is not the same as "document what you did."

The five carrier evidence categories (tool-specificity, model version tracking, use-case differentiation, FADP audit trail substance, reassessment protocol) constitute a **specific documentation format requirement that did not exist when the activity occurred**. The gap is not that the firm failed to document. The gap is that the documentation standard being applied was not defined at the time of the activity—and the carrier is applying it anyway.

The operative implication for this ICP: the carrier renewal creates a dateable, specific documentation requirement that maps imperfectly onto what actually occurred. This is not a documentation gap. It is a documentation archaeology problem with a specific carrier-imposed format overlay that makes standard retrospective reconstruction insufficient.
