## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: FINMA Q1 2027 audit adequacy standard formation through question progression**
FINMA is forming the adequacy standard through question specificity, not through published benchmarks. The standard is knowable through the questions, but only retrospectively — firms learn what compliance requires by being asked specific questions in audits.

**Signal 2: Model version tracking as undiscovered documentation gap at boutique scale**
Model version tracking is not systematic at boutique wealth management scale. The gap is undiscovered until a specific question surfaces it. The discovery moment is happening now as FINMA and carrier questions become more specific.

**Signal 3: AI recommendation explanation confidence language as client trust problem**
AI-generated explanations for recommendations sound confident but are not verifiable by the portfolio manager. The gap between confidence and verifiability creates a client trust problem when clients ask about recommendations.

**Signal 4: Unexplained AI flag disappearance as risk management record failure**
When AI flags are not followed up, they disappear from the dashboard. The record of what the AI recommended versus what the firm decided is lost. The risk management function operates without a complete record.

**Signal 5: Vendor model update notifications as unmanaged compliance events**
Vendor model update notifications arrive and are logged without a protocol for evaluation. The evaluation question — did this update change how the AI generates recommendations — is not answered. The unmanaged event creates potential untracked change in recommendation behavior.

**Signal 6: Client AI disclosure expectation as client relationship question**
Clients are beginning to ask whether reports and recommendations are AI-generated. The question arrives before firms have designed their disclosure approach. The response affects client trust directly.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: FINMA Q1 2027 adequacy standard decoding methodology**
No methodology exists for decoding the FINMA Q1 2027 adequacy standard from question patterns. The standard is forming through questions; the decoding requires systematic tracking of question progression across multiple firms and audit cycles.

**Gap 2: Model version tracking protocol for boutique wealth management**
No protocol exists for model version tracking at boutique scale that is both operationally practical and compliance-relevant. The gap is not a documentation problem — it is a documentation design problem that requires understanding what compliance actually requires.

**Gap 3: AI recommendation explanation documentation framework**
No framework exists for documenting the relationship between AI-generated recommendations and the firm's review and approval. The framework would need to address what the firm can say about its recommendations when clients ask.

**Gap 4: Unexplained flag protocol for risk management documentation**
No protocol exists for documenting AI recommendations that were not followed up. The protocol would need to create a durable record without creating operational overhead that makes the protocol impractical.

**Gap 5: Vendor model update evaluation protocol**
No protocol exists for evaluating vendor model updates and their effect on recommendation behavior. The protocol would need to specify what to assess when a notification arrives, and what the firm's documentation obligation is.

**Gap 6: Client AI disclosure conversation design**
No framework exists for designing the client conversation about AI-assisted work. The design requires deciding what to say, when to say it, and what the firm's position on AI actually is in client delivery.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA Q1 2027 AI audit focus areas:**
FINMA Q1 2027 audit focus includes: tool inventory, model version documentation, use-case differentiation, FADP audit trail substance, reassessment protocols. The five evidence categories form the adequacy standard through question specificity. No published benchmarks exist.

**ISSB AI-related disclosure standards for sustainability reporting (mid-2027):**
ISSB standards require documentation of AI-related risks and opportunities in sustainability reporting. The intersection of AI governance and sustainability disclosure is creating a new documentation surface for wealth management firms serving sustainability-focused clients.

**ASIP fiduciary obligations and AI recommendation accountability:**
Fiduciary obligations require that recommendations be attributable to the firm's judgment. AI-generated recommendations that the firm presents as its own create accountability exposure that ASIP has not addressed at boutique scale.

**nFADP Articles 6 and 8 behavioral profiling consent for portfolio analytics:**
Portfolio analytics using AI to infer client behavioral patterns require specific consent architecture under Articles 6 and 8. The consent requirement is not satisfied by a general privacy policy.

**FINMA Circ. 2017/1 continuous authorization analogy for AI systems:**
The principle that systems requiring continuous updates cannot be authorized through periodic review is applicable to AI systems. The analogy creates an authorization architecture problem for AI systems that update continuously.

**Triple convergence timeline:**
FINMA Q1 2027, ISSB mid-2027, and nFADP Art.21 all create documentation requirements on overlapping timelines. No methodology exists for addressing them simultaneously. The convergence is a specific boutique-scale problem.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: FINMA Q1 2027 adequacy standard formation through question progression as retrospective decoding requirement**
The FINMA adequacy standard is forming through question specificity, not through published benchmarks. The standard becomes knowable only retrospectively — through the pattern of questions asked across multiple firms and audit cycles. The decoding requirement creates an intelligence function that no advisory practice has claimed as a service offering.

**Finding 2: Model version tracking as undiscovered documentation gap at boutique scale with discovery asymmetry**
Model version tracking is absent at boutique scale in a specific way: the gap is undiscovered until a question surfaces it. The discovery asymmetry means firms learn about the gap through the examination itself, not through proactive assessment. The gap is not a compliance failure — it is a documentation system design problem.

**Finding 3: AI recommendation explanation confidence language as verifiability gap creating client trust liability**
AI-generated explanations are confident but not verifiable by the portfolio manager who presents them to clients. The verifiability gap creates client trust liability that operates independently of whether the recommendation itself was correct. The liability surfaces when a client asks a question the manager cannot answer with the actual reasoning chain.

**Finding 4: Unexplained flag disappearance as permanent risk management record asymmetry with no remediation mechanism**
When AI recommendations are not followed up, the record of what the AI recommended disappears from the dashboard. The asymmetry is permanent — it cannot be reconstructed retroactively. The risk management function operates with an incomplete record by design, with no mechanism for creating a durable record of the AI's recommendation versus the firm's decision.

**Finding 5: Vendor model update notification as unmanaged compliance event with untracked effect on recommendation behavior**
Vendor model update notifications arrive and are logged without a protocol for evaluating their effect on recommendation behavior. The unmanaged event creates potential untracked change in how recommendations are generated. The firm cannot state whether a specific recommendation was generated before or after a model update, or whether the update changed recommendation behavior.

**Finding 6: Client AI disclosure expectation as client relationship question with no prepared response design**
Clients asking whether reports and recommendations are AI-generated arrives before the firm has designed its disclosure approach. The response affects client trust in the firm's competence and in the work product delivered. The question is a client relationship question that requires a prepared response design — the response cannot be improvised without creating inconsistency that itself becomes a trust problem.
