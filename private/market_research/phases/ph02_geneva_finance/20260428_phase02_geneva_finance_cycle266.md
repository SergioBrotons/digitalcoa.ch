## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Approval document temporal architecture failure as the primary structural exposure**
The approved suitability profile is a point-in-time document. The AI system operates continuously. The document describes the system state at the moment of signature. Every day after signature, the system operates, and the document becomes less accurate. By the time of the next periodic review, the document is a historical record of a system state that no longer exists. The architecture was designed for human decision-making intervals. AI does not observe those intervals. The gap between approval document and system behavior is structural, not procedural.

**Signal 2: Client portfolio data in vendor AI infrastructure as an unnamed authorization exposure**
Client portfolio data flows into AI tool infrastructure. The infrastructure may train on that data, retain it, or route it to other systems. The authorization question under nFADP Article 9 — whether third-party processing with unknown downstream use requires explicit client authorization — has not been mapped for Geneva wealth management firms. The exposure is present-tense and compounding. It has not yet manifested as a client question. It will.

**Signal 3: Client review conversation attribution decomposition as a pre-regulatory exposure point**
Clients request attribution-level decomposition of recommendations at client review conversations — not at regulatory examinations, not in formal questionnaire responses. The request arrives at a conversation point where no documentation preparation has occurred. The firm cannot answer, not because the question is unreasonable, but because the documentation architecture does not capture per-instance AI contribution. The exposure manifests in relationship conversations before it appears in compliance processes.

**Signal 4: Regulatory documentation duality trap creating unintended client-facing admissions**
Documentation prepared for regulatory purposes — FINMA responses, carrier attestations, ISSB sustainability disclosures — becomes accessible through nFADP Article 21 client data subject requests. The documentation may contain admissions, system descriptions, and risk acknowledgments created for a regulatory audience that the firm did not intend to make available to clients. The duality trap is not addressed by any Geneva market service: documentation designed for one purpose acquires a second evidentiary function the author did not design for.

**Signal 5: Client-facing AI output functioning as reputational infrastructure independently of governance documentation**
The client's perception of the firm's AI quality is formed through client-facing outputs — reports, commentary, recommendations — not through internal governance documentation. A firm with comprehensive internal governance documentation can produce client-facing outputs that create a negative impression of AI quality. The reputational infrastructure and the governance infrastructure are operating independently. No Geneva market participant has addressed the integration between them.

**Signal 6: Post-examination response architecture absence creating a secondary accountability gap**
FINMA examinations identify AI governance findings. No methodology exists for how a boutique firm should document its response to those findings. The response is not itself regulated. A firm can respond inadequately to an examination finding and face no specific consequence for inadequate response — only for the underlying condition. The post-examination response is where examination findings are translated into documented accountability positions. That translation step has no defined standard and no market service addressing it.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Approval document temporal reconstruction methodology**
No market service addresses the specific problem of how to maintain an approval document that remains accurate for a continuously operating AI system. The gap is not about documentation quality; it is about the temporal architecture of the approval mechanism itself. The documentation that results from a periodic review is structurally a historical document the moment it is signed. No Geneva service has named this as a discrete product category or built a methodology to address it.

**Gap 2: Client portfolio data authorization mapping for AI tool infrastructure**
No Geneva advisory has mapped nFADP Article 9 authorization requirements for the specific configuration of client portfolio data flowing into commercial AI tool infrastructure. The mapping requires understanding what each vendor does with training data, what contractual provisions exist, and what client authorization language would be required. The absence is structural, not a gap in available information — no one has built the analysis.

**Gap 3: Client conversation attribution response protocol**
No protocol exists for responding to client attribution decomposition requests at the relationship conversation level — before the request becomes a formal questionnaire response or a regulatory inquiry. The protocol would need to be executable at boutique scale by relationship managers who are not AI governance specialists. The gap is specific to conversational context, not to formal compliance documentation.

**Gap 4: Regulatory documentation dual-use design service**
No Geneva market service addresses the design of regulatory documentation with awareness of its potential function as client-facing evidentiary record. The service would require understanding both regulatory documentation requirements and nFADP Article 21 implications simultaneously. No participant has offered this as a discrete product.

**Gap 5: Client-facing AI output governance integration**
No service integrates client-facing AI output quality control with internal AI governance documentation. The two systems are separate, managed by different functions, with no mechanism connecting what the client sees to what the governance documents describe. The gap compounds as client AI sophistication increases and clients evaluate firm AI quality directly through outputs.

**Gap 6: Post-examination response methodology for AI governance findings**
No methodology exists for structuring a boutique firm's written response to AI governance findings identified in a FINMA examination. The response is not itself a regulated deliverable, but it shapes how examination outcomes are documented and whether subsequent examinations find the response adequate. No Geneva service has built a post-examination response protocol as a discrete product.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA suitability and AI obligations:**
FINMA requires that suitability assessments be documented, current, and attributable. The suitability obligation applies to recommendations made with AI assistance. AI systems operating continuously between periodic reviews create a structural gap: the documented suitability profile describes a historical system state. The approved profile may not reflect current AI behavior. The periodic review architecture was designed for human decision intervals; it does not accommodate continuous AI operation. FINMA has not issued guidance specifying how firms should maintain approval documents for continuously operating AI systems. The obligation exists; the methodology for discharging it at boutique scale does not.

**ASIP and BVG occupational pension AI obligations:**
ASIP (Association Suisse des Institutions de Prévoyance) oversees Swiss occupational pension funds. AI-assisted investment decisions affecting BVG pension assets create specific documentation obligations. The BVG legal framework requires that investment decisions be attributable and explicable. AI systems that operate continuously between review cycles create a attribution gap: the decision was made by the system within an approved range, but no one can reconstruct what the system decided and why at any specific moment within that range. ASIP has not issued guidance specific to AI system documentation for institutional asset managers serving BVG clients. The obligation framework is present; the implementation methodology for boutique wealth managers is absent.

**SFAMA fund management AI documentation:**
SFAMA (Swiss Fund and Asset Management Association) governs fund documentation standards. AI-assisted portfolio construction for managed funds creates documentation requirements that SFAMA guidance does not specifically address. The fund documentation must accurately describe portfolio construction methodology. AI-generated contributions to portfolio decisions are not systematically captured in fund reporting. The investor disclosure requirements under Swiss fund law do not have a specified format for AI contribution disclosure. SFAMA has not issued guidance on how AI contribution should be documented in fund reports.

**ISSB sustainability disclosure and AI assembly:**
ISSB (International Sustainability Standards Board) disclosure standards require that sustainability-related data in client reports be accurate and attributable. AI-assembled sustainability data — carbon footprint estimates, transition risk assessments, portfolio climate exposure — is generated by AI systems without systematic documentation of the assembly process. ISSB-aligned client reports incorporating AI-assembled sustainability data inherit the AI assembly documentation gap. The ISSB framework requires data accuracy; the accuracy of AI-assembled data is not independently verified by most boutique wealth managers. The obligation is being activated through client counterparty requirements before formal ISSB adoption in Switzerland.

**nFADP (revised, in force September 2024, with September 2026 transition for SMEs):**
nFADP Article 9 governs third-party data processing authorization. Client portfolio data sent to commercial AI tool infrastructure may constitute processing by a third party with downstream uses the firm cannot trace. Authorization requirements under Article 9 are not discharged by standard service agreements that do not specify training data use. Article 21 governs client rights to explanation of automated decisions. AI-generated recommendations that cannot be decomposed into AI and human contribution may be impossible to explain in the required detail. The revised nFADP (in force September 2024) applies to all firms processing client data; the September 2026 SME transition period does not exempt professional services firms from Article 9 authorization requirements. Geneva wealth management firms are operating under the full framework now.

## 06 — Findings Summary: 6 Net-New Insights, Distinct from C214–C265 Phase 02

**Finding 01: Periodic review temporal architecture failure as primary structural accountability exposure**
The periodic review approval document becomes a historical record the moment it is signed. The AI system continues operating between review cycles. The approved profile describes a system state that no longer exists. This is architecturally distinct from:
- C264 Finding 01 (approval-reality divergence) — which identified the divergence without isolating the temporal architecture failure as the specific structural mechanism creating the divergence
- C262 Finding 04 (compliance committee comprehension gap) — which addressed review comprehension, not the temporal validity of the approval document itself
- All prior findings addressing review adequacy and periodic review timing — none isolated the structural incompatibility between point-in-time approval documents and continuously operating AI as the primary mechanism
*New angle: The approval document is not merely inadequate; it is architecturally a historical document by design. The temporal architecture of periodic review is the problem, not the review interval.*

**Finding 02: Client portfolio data training pipeline authorization as a specifically unnamed nFADP Article 9 exposure**
Client portfolio data flowing into commercial AI tool infrastructure creates a third-party processing authorization requirement under nFADP Article 9 that has not been mapped for Geneva wealth management firms. The exposure is present-tense and compounding, distinguishable from:
- C258 Finding 03 (non-Swiss AI processor Art.9 authorization) — which addressed processor authorization generally without isolating the training pipeline use case as a distinct operational trigger
- C260 Finding 04 (client AI audit requests) — which addressed audit requests, not the underlying data processing authorization question that makes some audit requests unavoidable
- C263 Finding 01 (client-side AI capability asymmetry) — which addressed the client's ability to evaluate firm outputs, not the data processing question underlying the firm's own AI infrastructure
*New angle: The training pipeline authorization question is a specific operational trigger. When portfolio data enters vendor infrastructure, a statutory authorization obligation may be activated that most Geneva wealth management firms have not identified.*

**Finding 03: Client review conversation attribution decomposition as a pre-regulatory exposure point distinct from formal review adequacy**
Clients requesting attribution-level decomposition of recommendations arrive at relationship review conversations, not at regulatory examination points. The exposure manifests in client conversations before it appears in compliance processes. This is distinct from:
- C262 Finding 01 (AI attribution chain disaggregation) — which addressed the documentation architecture problem in aggregate, not the specific timing of client conversations as the exposure activation point
- C265 Finding 06 (post-departure AI configuration knowledge loss) — which addressed staff departure knowledge loss, not client-conversation-initiated attribution requests
- C259 Finding 06 (client-initiated nFADP Art.21 activation) — which addressed formal Article 21 requests, not informal attribution questions at review conversations
*New angle: The client conversation is a specific, named exposure point that is earlier than and distinct from formal regulatory inquiry. The preparation required is different in kind, not degree, from compliance documentation.*

**Finding 04: Regulatory documentation duality trap creating unintended client-facing admissions through nFADP Article 21**
Documentation prepared for FINMA and carrier purposes becomes accessible through nFADP Article 21 client data subject requests. Documents created for regulatory audiences acquire a second function as client-accessible evidentiary records. This is distinct from:
- C261 Finding 02 (nFADP-FINMA collision) — which addressed the theoretical intersection of the two frameworks, not the specific mechanism by which regulatory documents become client evidence
- C265 Finding 04 (carrier attestation as personal legal instrument) — which addressed the attestation as a liability instrument, not the dual-use consequence of documentation designed for one audience and accessible to another
- C260 Finding 05 (documentation architecture engineering) — which addressed the architectural problem of reactive documentation accumulation, not the specific duality consequence of regulatory documentation designed without client-access awareness
*New angle: The duality trap is a design failure, not a documentation quality failure. Documents created for regulatory purposes can be made with awareness of their potential Article 21 function. That design step is absent from current market practice.*

**Finding 05: Client-facing AI output as reputational infrastructure operating independently of governance documentation**
The client's perception of the firm's AI quality is formed through client-facing outputs — reports, commentary, recommendation language — not through internal governance documentation. This is distinct from:
- C265 Finding 02 (client-side AI capability asymmetry) — which addressed the client's ability to interrogate outputs, not the structural independence of the reputational output system from the governance documentation system
- C258 Finding 05 (client recommendation communication framing) — which addressed language framing choices as professional liability questions, not the structural separation of the two systems
- C259 Finding 05 (client confidence calibration) — which addressed client perception management, not the structural architecture of how reputational signals are generated independently of governance quality
*New angle: Two separate systems are operating: the governance documentation system (internal, compliance-oriented) and the client-facing output system (relationship-forming, reputation-generating). They are not currently integrated. The gap is structural, not a quality problem within either system.*

**Finding 06: Post-examination response architecture absence creating a secondary accountability gap between findings and resolution**
FINMA examinations identify AI governance findings. No methodology exists for how a boutique firm should document its response to those findings. The response is not itself regulated, but it shapes how examination outcomes are documented and whether subsequent examinations find the response adequate. This is distinct from:
- C264 Finding 06 (post-departure AI configuration knowledge loss) — which addressed knowledge loss as an operational continuity problem, not the post-examination response documentation gap
- C263 Finding 06 (compliance officer attestation discharge methodology) — which addressed the attestation signing question, not the post-examination written response question
- C265 Finding 01 (approval-reality divergence) — which addressed the primary structural exposure, not the secondary gap in how the response to that exposure is documented
*New angle: The post-examination response is a discrete named accountability step between "finding identified" and "finding resolved" that has no defined methodology at boutique scale. A firm can respond inadequately to an examination finding without specific consequence for inadequate response — only for the underlying condition. The response documentation gap is itself ungoverned.*
