## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Institutional client AI questionnaires probing specific decision accountability — not policy existence**
The character of institutional AI governance questionnaires has shifted from "describe your AI policy" to "for these three specific client recommendations from the last quarter, describe what role AI played, what human review occurred, and what evidence you have that the review happened." The question is now operational — not about policy architecture but about decision-level accountability. Boutique firms cannot answer the new category of questions because they were never asked to build the evidence architecture that the questions require.

**Signal 2: nFADP data processing authorization scope evaluation as an exposure arriving post-signature**
Wealth managers who signed AI vendor data processing authorizations are arriving at the realization that the scope of what they authorized was never evaluated against what the vendor actually does with the data. Model improvement uses client financial data. Cross-client data pooling may occur. Third-party access may be granted. The authorization was signed. The authorization scope was never reviewed. The exposure is present-tense and unnamed — it exists because the authorization was signed, not because the firm made a governance decision to accept the risk.

**Signal 3: Portfolio construction AI non-attribution as a structural property of continuous AI integration**
The most consequential AI use in wealth management — portfolio construction — is also the least attributable to human oversight. AI contributes continuously across allocation decisions, risk adjustments, rebalancing recommendations. The human review that precedes client delivery cannot be documented in a way that distinguishes what the AI contributed from what the human reviewed. The recommendation represents itself as professional judgment. The proportion that is AI-generated analytical contribution cannot be established with evidence. This is not a documentation quality problem — it is a structural property of continuous AI integration in construction processes.

**Signal 4: AI model version provenance as an accountability gap that institutional clients are beginning to ask about**
Institutional clients with sophisticated compliance functions are starting to ask about model version consistency across their mandates. The question — "was this recommendation produced under the same analytical conditions as last quarter's?" — has no answer at firms without version tracking. The exposure is not about whether the model changed recently. It is about whether the firm can demonstrate continuity of analytical conditions across time for any given mandate. Most cannot.

**Signal 5: Carrier questionnaire evolution as the primary supervisory signal — not published FINMA guidance**
The most reliable indicator of what FINMA supervisory expectations actually are for Geneva wealth management firms is carrier questionnaire iteration patterns. Each carrier iteration introduces questions that reflect what FINMA communicated to carriers privately. The published guidance lags the operative expectations by twelve to eighteen months. Firms that wait for published guidance are building documentation against yesterday's standards while tomorrow's questions are already arriving through carrier iterations.

**Signal 6: Carrier attestation personal liability crystallization through renewal cycle accumulation**
The carrier attestation signed eighteen months ago committed the MD to evidence standards that were not yet defined. Each renewal cycle adds new content to what the attestation warrants. The personal liability exposure crystallizes incrementally through the renewal process — each new attestation cycle adding new commitments the signatory evaluates for the first time at the moment of signature. The exposure is not the original signature. It is the accumulated effect of successive renewal cycles adding new commitments without corresponding pre-signing review.

## 04 — Compliance Context

### FINMA

FINMA supervisory expectations for AI in wealth management continue to form through operational interactions rather than published guidance. The key structural property of this formation process:

- Carrier questionnaire iterations are the primary mechanism through which FINMA supervisory priorities are communicated to the market — not published circulars or guidance documents
- The personal accountability of the MD who signs AI-influenced recommendations remains the primary supervisory concern — not the AI system architecture, but who is professionally responsible for what the AI system produces in signed client documents
- The documentation standard that supervisory examinations apply is being set by what carrier third-generation questionnaires require — evidence of specific decisions, specific human review events, specific documentation of AI contribution — not by what published FINMA guidance describes as adequate
- The documentation window close in mid-2026 creates a specific burden-of-proof reversal: the question after the close will be why documentation that should have existed before the close was not built during the window, not whether current documentation is improving

**Boutique-specific proportionality gap:** FINMA guidance and carrier standards are calibrated for institutions with dedicated compliance infrastructure. No proportionate methodology exists for applying third-generation evidence requirements to a firm where the same three to five people hold investment authority, manage client relationships, and are responsible for compliance documentation. The documentation standard is forming without a corresponding Geneva-specific methodology for boutique-scale compliance.

### ASIP

ASIP obligations apply to pension fund relationships where the wealth manager's AI system provides allocation recommendations to ASIP-governed pension funds. The specific accountability architecture:

- Pension funds have their own AI governance documentation requirements under ASIP guidance — obligations that flow backward through the advisory relationship to the wealth manager's own AI governance documentation
- The wealth manager's AI system is operating within a two-layer accountability structure: its own FINMA/nFADP obligations and its pension fund clients' ASIP requirements simultaneously, with no Geneva methodology for mapping the two-layer intersection
- ASIP has not issued proportionate guidance for the specific documentation obligations that apply when a boutique wealth manager's AI system provides allocation recommendations to a pension fund governed by ASIP standards

### SFAMA / FIDLEG

FIDLEG Article 26 requires disclosure of algorithmic execution versus AI-assisted portfolio construction. The specific gap at boutique scale:

- The disclosure obligation applies differently depending on whether AI is used in the construction process itself versus the reporting of results — and at boutique firms with continuous AI integration, this distinction is architecturally difficult to draw
- No Geneva service has produced proportionate Article 26 disclosure language for boutique firms where AI contributes continuously across both portfolio construction and reporting, rather than in discrete identifiable events
- SFAMA's evolving guidance creates documentation obligations that are ahead of the proportionate methodology for discharge at boutique scale

### ISSB Standards

ISSB S1 and S2 climate-related financial disclosure requirements are entering Swiss regulatory implementation. The specific exposure for this ICP:

- AI systems used in portfolio analysis generate estimates that may appear in ISSB-aligned client reports — particularly Scope 2 emissions estimates for portfolio holdings
- AI-generated estimates are entity-specific in appearance but model-derived in production — presenting them as ISSB-aligned data creates a disclosure accuracy question that is distinct from general ESG reporting compliance
- No Geneva market guidance exists on the specific intersection of AI-generated estimates and ISSB disclosure accuracy obligations for boutique wealth managers advising on ISSB-aligned mandates

### nFADP (revised)

The revised nFADP creates obligations that remain largely unevaluated at Geneva wealth management boutiques:

- **Article 6**: Lawful basis for processing client financial data through AI vendor infrastructure — purpose limitation requires evaluation of whether data used for AI model improvement or cross-client analytics is beyond the scope of the original mandate. Most firms have not conducted this evaluation against their current vendor agreements.
- **Article 8**: Profiling notification — AI-assisted portfolio construction constitutes automated profiling. Notification obligations and human review rights under Article 8 are operative at most Geneva wealth management boutiques without any discharge methodology.
- **Article 9**: Third-party processing authorization — when client mandate data is processed through AI vendor infrastructure, the nFADP Article 9 authorization requirements apply. The scope of what has been authorized versus what is actually occurring in vendor infrastructure is unknown at most boutiques.
- **Article 15**: Accuracy rights for AI-inferred conclusions about specific clients embedded in client-facing documents — a specific response infrastructure requirement that does not exist at boutique scale: identification of AI-generated inferences in client documents, accuracy evaluation methodology, explanation architecture, and documentation of the response.
- **Article 21**: Explanation of automated decision logic — when clients request explanation of how AI affected a specific recommendation, the obligation to provide this in understandable terms is operative. No Geneva methodology for proportionate discharge exists.
- **Article 26**: Data protection impact assessment — required for high-risk processing activities. AI-assisted portfolio construction with cross-client data flows through vendor infrastructure is likely high-risk processing under nFADP Article 26 criteria. Most Geneva wealth management boutiques have not conducted this assessment.

### CoE AI Convention

The Convention is in force and creates present-tense obligations flowing through existing client mandate contracts. The specific accountability question for this ICP:

- The Convention's accountability requirements for AI systems used in client advisory relationships are operative through existing mandate contract framework, regardless of whether the firm has evaluated how those requirements apply
- The present-tense operative obligations under the Convention are creating accountability exposure that existing client contracts do not adequately address — because those contracts were written before the Convention entered into force
- No Geneva market participant has published guidance on how the Convention's obligations flow through existing mandate contracts at Geneva wealth management boutiques

**The finding:** When AI contributes continuously across portfolio construction, the AI analytical contribution is architecturally inseparable from human oversight throughout the construction process. The recommendation represents itself as professional judgment. The structural property of continuous AI integration makes the attribution question fundamentally unanswerable with evidence.

**Distinct from C214–C282 Phase 02 findings:** This is not about AI authorship attribution in client-facing recommendations (C282 Finding 01 — which addressed the structural non-separability of AI and human contribution in signed documents, the document-level attribution problem). This finding addresses the specific mechanism in portfolio construction: continuous AI integration where the AI contribution is not a discrete document event but a continuous process throughout the construction workflow, making the human oversight accountability question fundamentally different from document-level attribution. It is not about what the MD signs — it is about what human oversight documentation the construction process requires when AI is architecturally present throughout the entire construction workflow, not as a discrete input but as the continuous analytical medium. Different mechanism, different documentation requirement, different solution architecture. This finding isolates the specific structural property of continuous AI integration in construction processes as the mechanism creating the non-attribution problem distinct from the document-level attribution problem.
