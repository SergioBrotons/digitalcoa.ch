## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Compliance function structurally redefined without design choice**
The compliance officer's function at boutique wealth management firms is being redefined by AI deployment, not by a management decision. The redefinition is happening because AI outputs must be approved, and approval without evaluation methodology is what the current situation requires. The compliance officer is now an AI output evaluator by necessity. The competency implications of that redefinition have not been addressed. The gap between the role as designed and the role as currently functioning is not on anyone's agenda until a supervisory review makes it visible.

**Signal 2: AI text-to-client with compliance-approver-in-the-middle as unexamined arrangement**
The direct transmission of AI-generated text to clients, with a compliance approval step in between, creates an accountability structure that has not been interrogated. The compliance officer is accountable for approving text they did not write, on methodology they cannot reproduce, for transmission to a client who may not understand the AI's role. This is not a communications problem. It is an accountability architecture problem with three parties and no governing framework.

**Signal 3: Board-level AI risk reporting without adequate methodology**
The board is beginning to request quarterly AI risk summaries. Firms cannot produce them with evidence. The summaries that exist document tool existence, not review adequacy. The board is receiving a compliance artifact — a report that something was done — rather than an assessment that the process was sound. The gap between tool documentation and process adequacy evidence is structural.

**Signal 4: AI configuration knowledge as unrecoverable departure loss**
When the person who configured the AI reporting system departs, the firm's ability to independently describe what the system does departs with them. Successor configuration is based on predecessor training, not on documented system state. The documentation of what the AI system does is not held in documentation — it is held in the heads of the individuals who configured it. This creates a permanent reconstruction problem for any historical AI output.

**Signal 5: Client expectation gap on AI role in their reporting**
Clients receiving AI-assisted portfolio reports are forming expectations about the AI's role that may not match what the firm is actually doing. The gap between client expectation and actual AI contribution is not being managed. It surfaces when a client asks a direct question about methodology — a question the firm cannot answer with evidence, because the documentation does not exist at the level the question requires.

**Signal 6: Regulatory formation anticipation without preparation infrastructure**
The managing directors know FINMA guidance is coming. They cannot prepare because they do not know the standard. The firms that are preparing are doing so generically — building policy documents that will need to be remade when the standard arrives. The firms that are waiting are accumulating documentation debt. Neither approach is efficient. The anticipation gap is itself a risk.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Compliance officer AI output evaluation methodology at boutique scale**
A methodology that the compliance function can apply to evaluate AI outputs — not to reproduce the AI's reasoning, but to establish whether the process that produced the output was adequate — does not exist at boutique scale. This is not a training gap. Training addresses knowledge. This is a methodology gap: the compliance officer needs a framework for evaluating adequacy when they cannot evaluate correctness independently. That framework has not been built for this context.

**Gap 2: Board-reportable AI risk summary architecture**
A structure for producing board-level AI risk summaries that present evidence of process adequacy — not just tool inventory — does not exist as a packaged advisory offering for boutique wealth management firms. The board needs to see something it can engage with substantively, not a list of tools deployed. The documentation architecture that produces evidence-level board reporting is a distinct gap from general AI governance documentation.

**Gap 3: AI-to-client text accountability framework**
A framework for managing the accountability structure when AI-generated text goes directly to clients, with compliance approval, does not exist. The three-party accountability relationship — firm, compliance officer, client — with no governing standard for how it should operate is not addressed by any current compliance or advisory product.

**Gap 4: AI configuration continuity protocol for key-personnel dependency**
A protocol for managing AI configuration continuity when the individual who configured the system departs — ensuring the firm retains the ability to independently describe and govern its AI system — does not exist as a discrete service offering at boutique scale. The problem is not data backup. It is institutional knowledge continuity for a technical configuration that governs client-facing outputs.

**Gap 5: Client expectation calibration for AI-assisted reporting**
A structured approach for managing client expectations about what AI does in their portfolio reporting — before the client asks a question the firm cannot answer — does not exist as a discrete service offering. The firms currently handling this through ad hoc responses are building inconsistent disclosure records that themselves create liability.

**Gap 6: Regulatory formation intelligence translated to preparation roadmap**
A service that reads the FINMA formation process — not the published output, but the direction of formation — and translates that intelligence into a specific preparation roadmap calibrated to boutique wealth management firm scale does not exist. Firms are preparing generically or waiting. Neither is efficient. The gap between regulatory anticipation intelligence and actionable boutique-scale preparation is unoccupied.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA obligations:**
FINMA has moved from informal consultation to structured AI governance questionnaires in supervisory reviews. The questionnaires are designed to distinguish between governance theater and governance practice. A firm that responds with policy language, rather than practice evidence, will be identified. The critical gap is not whether the firm has an AI governance policy — it is whether the firm can demonstrate with evidence that the policy is operational. For the compliance officer who is now an AI output evaluator without a methodology, the questionnaire arrives at the moment the accountability exposure is most acute. The board-level AI risk summary, requested by governing bodies who themselves lack evaluation methodology, compounds the exposure.

**ASIP obligations for pension fund trustees:**
Pension fund trustees using AI-assisted investment decisions face ASIP obligations including documentation requirements under Art. 51 BVG. Trustees who adopted AI incrementally, without systematic documentation of what the system was doing and how it was reviewed, face accumulated documentation debt. Personal liability under ASIP is not theoretical — it is the frame within which trustee accountability operates. The compliance officer evaluating AI outputs at a boutique that serves pension fund clients faces a compounding accountability: the outputs they approve feed into decisions that trustees must document and for which trustees have personal liability.

**SFAMA documentation requirements:**
SFAMA requirements for AI-assisted portfolio management documentation include model version tracking, use-case differentiation, and reassessment protocols. The documentation requirements are not discretionary. A firm that has not implemented version-aware documentation architecture is structurally non-compliant regardless of the quality of its investment decisions. For the operations lead who cannot document what the AI system does because the configuration knowledge departed with the individual who configured it, the SFAMA documentation requirement is not a future problem — it is a present-tense reconstruction problem.

**ISSB sustainability data and AI lineage:**
ISSB-aligned sustainability disclosure requirements create AI lineage documentation obligations. When AI tools contribute to sustainability data used in client reporting, the lineage from source data through AI processing to disclosed metric must be documentable. The preparation required is not short-term. For boutique wealth management firms serving clients with cross-border portfolios, the ISSB requirements interact with nFADP obligations to create a compound documentation problem: the firm must document AI processing lineage while simultaneously ensuring that documentation does not itself become a data protection compliance issue.

**nFADP cross-border client data and AI-generated content:**
The firm serving cross-border clients must manage nFADP obligations alongside the data protection regimes of other jurisdictions. When AI generates content that becomes part of client records, the processing documentation must be producible for any client's jurisdiction. The cross-border dimension means the highest standard among the jurisdictions in the client portfolio determines the firm's minimum documentation obligation. For AI-generated text transmitted directly to clients, the nFADP Art. 21 explanation obligation intersects with client expectation management: the explanation the firm provides must be technically accurate, comprehensible to the specific client, and consistent with what the client was told about the AI's role.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**6 Net-New Insights, Distinct from C214–C235 Phase 02**

**Insight 1: The compliance function is being structurally redefined by AI deployment, not by management decision.**
The compliance officer's role is shifting from outcome approval to AI output evaluation — a redefinition that is happening by necessity, not by design. At the exact moment this redefinition is occurring, no competency standard exists to define what that evaluation requires. The structural mismatch between the role as designed and the role as currently functioning is the most acute operational gap at boutique wealth management firms deploying AI. This has not appeared in any prior C214–C235 Phase 02 as a standalone structural finding.

**Insight 2: The compliance officer approving AI-generated client text is a qualitatively distinct accountability problem.**
When a compliance officer approves AI-generated text for direct transmission to clients, they are accountable for content they did not write, using a methodology they cannot reproduce, for a client who may not understand the AI's role. This three-party accountability structure — firm, compliance officer, client — with no governing standard is structurally distinct from traditional compliance review. It has not been examined as a discrete accountability architecture problem in any prior C214–C235 Phase 02 cycle.

**Insight 3: Client expectation gap on AI role in reporting is a present-tense liability surface, not a future communications problem.**
Clients receiving AI-assisted portfolio reports are forming beliefs about the AI's contribution that may not match what the firm is actually doing. The gap surfaces when a client asks a direct question — a question the firm cannot answer with evidence because the documentation does not exist. This is not a communications gap that better disclosure language resolves. It is an evidentiary gap: the firm cannot demonstrate what the AI did because that documentation was never created. This has not appeared as a distinct liability surface in any prior C214–C235 Phase 02.

**Insight 4: AI trained on the firm's own historical data creates a self-reinforcing recommendation loop that is undetectable without documentation architecture.**
When AI used in portfolio analysis was trained on or learns from the firm's own historical recommendations and decisions, the AI's output may reflect the firm's pre-existing positions rather than independent analysis. This feedback loop is structurally undetectable without documented evidence of what the AI was trained on and how its outputs differ from baseline firm positions. The reconstruction problem — proving the loop does not exist — requires documentation architecture that has not been built at boutique scale. This specific problem has not appeared in any prior C214–C235 Phase 02.

**Insight 5: AI system ownership and AI system use are structurally separated at 50–200 employee firms, creating a delegation accountability gap no current framework addresses.**
At firms of this size, the person who configured the AI reporting system is typically not the person who uses it daily. This creates a delegation accountability structure: the user is accountable for outputs from a system they did not configure, and the configurer is no longer present to describe what the system does. Neither the compliance function nor the managing director has a framework for managing this separation. The gap is structurally distinct from the key-personnel knowledge loss problem because it exists even when the configurer has not departed — the separation is built into the organizational structure. This has not appeared in any prior C214–C235 Phase 02.

**Insight 6: The documentation burden created by AI continuous reporting and quarterly compliance review cycles is structurally incompatible, and the incompatibility is not visible until a supervisory examination.**
AI-assisted reporting is moving toward continuous output. Compliance review operates on quarterly cycles. At boutique scale, the documentation burden of maintaining a compliance review trail for continuous AI outputs, within a quarterly review structure designed for human-produced work, is not feasible without a documented protocol for what the trail must capture. No such protocol exists. The structural incompatibility becomes visible only when a supervisory review asks for evidence of the review process. This specific interaction between AI reporting velocity and compliance review cadence has not appeared in any prior C214–C235 Phase 02.
