## 01 — ICP Language | How This ICP Actually Describes AI Problems

### English

The managing director or senior advisor at a boutique Geneva wealth management firm (50–200 employees) describes AI problems in terms of **accountability ownership** — who is on record for what, and whether the documentation reflects what actually happened.

Typical language when describing AI adoption to a peer or regulator:

- *"We're using AI in client reporting — but I can't tell you exactly what the system contributed versus what the PM adjusted manually."*
- *"The client asked what methodology was used for their allocation. I had to reconstruct it."*
- *"We signed the carrier questionnaire. I'm not sure we answered it correctly."*
- *"A model update happened. I don't know when. I don't know if it affected anything in the book."*
- *"Our IPS was written before we had AI. It doesn't say anything about this."*
- *"When something goes wrong with an AI-assisted decision, I need to be able to explain it. I can't today."*

The vocabulary is: **reconstruction, accountability, documentation, disclosure, control**. Not AI capability, efficiency gains, or automation. The boutique wealth manager is thinking about what they are personally responsible for and whether they can demonstrate that responsibility if asked.

Language used in institutional client questionnaires is different — more structured, more specific: *"Describe your AI governance framework for portfolio construction decisions."* The MD recognizes this as a materially different question from anything in the firm's existing documentation.

Langage typique lors de discussions avec un pair ou un régulateur :

- *« Nous utilisons l'IA dans les rapports clients — mais je ne peux pas vous dire exactement ce que le système a contribué par rapport à ce que le gérant a ajusté manuellement. »*
- *« Le client a demandé quelle méthodologie a été utilisée pour son allocation. J'ai dû la reconstruire. »*

- *« Notre IPS a été rédigée avant l'IA. Elle ne dit rien à ce sujet. »*

Le vocabulaire est : **reconstruction, reddition de comptes, documentation, disclosure, contrôle**. Pas les capacités de l'IA, les gains d'efficacité ou l'automatisation. Le gérant de fortune genevois pense à ce dont il est personnellement responsable et s'il peut démontrer cette responsabilité si on le lui demande.

## 02 — Market Signals | Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

### Problem 1: The Documentation Moment Nobody Designed For

Boutique Geneva wealth managers have AI-assisted client reporting. They do not have a documented methodology for what the AI system contributed to any specific report. When an institutional client asks a methodology question — not a policy question — the MD cannot answer it from documentation. The response has to be reconstructed from memory. This is not a documentation quality problem. It is an architectural problem: the documentation moment was never designed into the process.

The paradox: firms producing more AI-assisted reports with greater sophistication have the most acute version of this problem. More output, more capability, less documented evidence of methodology. The wealth manager who has invested most in AI quality may be most exposed to an institutional methodology question they cannot answer.

### Problem 2: The Carrier Questionnaire Pre-Signing Moment

Third-generation carrier questionnaires are signed by the MD personally. The form is written by the carrier's legal team. Before signing, no independent review has been conducted from the MD's perspective — not whether the form is adequate to satisfy the carrier, but whether what the MD is personally committing to is consistent with what the firm actually does. A significant proportion of Geneva boutique wealth managers have signed these forms without that review. The failure mode is not that the form was wrong. The failure mode is that the MD committed personally to something they have not evaluated against their own practice.

### Problem 3: The Investment Policy Statement Structural Gap

Boutique Geneva wealth managers built investment policy statements before adopting AI. The IPS describes the firm's investment philosophy, decision-making process, and risk framework. It does not describe AI-assisted decision-making. When the IPS was written, AI-assisted portfolio construction did not exist. The IPS therefore does not address whether AI has a role in construction decisions, under what conditions AI recommendations are accepted or overridden, and what documentation the MD reviews before an AI-assisted recommendation goes to a client.

The gap is structural, not additive. Adding AI language to an existing IPS does not resolve the structural gap because the IPS is designed as a static document describing a relatively stable decision process, not as a living framework for a continuously updating AI system. No Geneva boutique has published an IPS supplement specifically addressing AI-assisted portfolio construction as a discrete governance architecture.

### Problem 4: The Periodic Report AI Disclosure Moment

Client reports are produced periodically. AI assists in their production. The question of whether and how the client should be informed that AI was used in constructing their report — and what that means — is not addressed in any Geneva boutique's reporting framework. Some firms disclose AI use in general terms in engagement letters. None have a specific disclosure framework for what AI contributed to any specific report.

The paradox: disclosure in general terms creates a reference point that clients use to evaluate specific reports. If a client receives a report and asks how the AI was used in this specific report, a general engagement letter disclosure may actually increase the firm's exposure rather than reduce it — because it acknowledges AI use without providing the specific methodology detail the question implies the client expects.

### Problem 5: The Institutional Questionnaire as Competitive Intelligence

Institutional clients are sending AI due diligence questionnaires to Geneva boutiques they are evaluating or reappointing. These questionnaires ask about portfolio construction methodology, model validation, documentation standards, and MD oversight of AI-assisted decisions. The response requires disclosing analytical methodology to a commercial counterparty that is simultaneously evaluating whether to maintain or award a mandate. No Geneva boutique has a clear framework for determining what to disclose in this context versus what to preserve as proprietary methodology. The questionnaires are treated as compliance exercises. They are also competitive intelligence instruments. These two functions are in structural tension.

### Problem 6: The AI Profiling Notification Obligation Nobody Has Activated

Under Swiss law, clients have rights regarding automated decisions, including profiling. AI-assisted portfolio construction involves behavioral profiling: risk tolerance inference, financial objective projection, investment horizon assessment. The nFADP gives data subjects the right to know when they are subject to profiling and to request human review of profiling-based decisions. Geneva boutique wealth managers have not, as a category, issued profiling notifications to their clients. The legal basis for this notification has not been evaluated in the Geneva boutique wealth management context. The obligation is present-tense and unnamed.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis | Gaps, What No One Is Addressing

### Gap 1: AI Usage Methodology Documentation vs. AI Tool Documentation

Every AI vendor provides documentation of the AI tool — what the system does, its capabilities, its limitations, its update history. No service in the Geneva market produces AI usage documentation for boutique wealth managers: the documentation of what the firm does with the AI tool, how the tool is integrated into the firm's specific decision process, what conditions govern AI recommendations versus human override, and what evidence the MD reviews to maintain accountability for AI-assisted decisions.

The two documentation types are structurally different. AI tool documentation is vendor-supplied. AI usage documentation is firm-specific. The market has not built the latter as a discrete service category. Every boutique wealth manager using AI is producing AI tool documentation without producing AI usage documentation.

### Gap 2: Institutional Questionnaire Response Strategy

No Geneva advisory offers a service that helps boutique wealth managers respond to institutional AI due diligence questionnaires as a strategic exercise — determining what to disclose, what to document as a precondition for disclosure, and what to preserve. This is distinct from a compliance review of whether the response is adequate. The strategic dimension — managing the competitive intelligence exposure embedded in the questionnaire response while maximizing disclosure credibility — is unnamed in the Geneva market. The service that exists is compliance review of individual questionnaire answers. The service that does not exist is questionnaire response strategy.

### Gap 3: MD-PM Accountability Line Documentation

Boutique wealth management firms (50–200 employees) have portfolio managers who use AI tools, and managing directors who are personally accountable for client outcomes and regulatory compliance. The accountability line between MD and PM for AI-assisted decisions is not documented at the boutique scale. The question is not who touched the AI tool — it is: at what threshold does the MD need to document personal review of an AI-assisted recommendation before it goes to a client? What documentation does the MD produce to evidence that review? What happens when the PM's AI-assisted recommendation is different from what the MD would have recommended independently?

No Geneva service has built a framework for this specific organizational accountability question at boutique scale.

### Gap 4: AI Convention Impact Assessment for Private Sector Wealth Managers

The Council of Europe AI Convention creates accountability obligations for parties to the convention. Switzerland has signed but not yet ratified. The Convention requires impact assessments for AI systems used in consequential decisions. At the boutique wealth management level, the question of what impact assessment means, when it is required, and how it operates at a 50–200 person firm has not been addressed by any Geneva service. This is distinct from FINMA guidance, which applies to supervised institutions, and from ISSB/ESG frameworks, which apply to disclosure obligations. The AI Convention impact assessment is a separate accountability obligation with a different legal basis and a different structure. No Geneva market participant has published a methodology for private sector wealth management firms.

### Gap 5: Carrier Third-Generation Questionnaire Pre-Signing Review

The market has services for answering carrier questionnaires (compliance review of responses). No service offers pre-signing review of what the MD is committing to personally through the signature. The MD signature on a carrier AI attestation is a personal liability instrument. The form is written by carrier legal teams. No independent review from the signatory's perspective has been offered as a discrete service. This is a specific structural absence: pre-signing review of a personal liability instrument, distinct from compliance adequacy review of questionnaire answers.

## 04 — Compliance Context | Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations

### FINMA — Supervisory Framework for AI

FINMA's guidance on AI (Aufsichtsmitteilung 08/2024 and the 2023 Risk Monitor) establishes principles for supervised institutions using AI: governance, risk management, transparency, and accountability. The framework is technology-neutral and principle-based, applying proportionally to all supervised institutions including boutique wealth managers. Key obligations:

- **Governance:** Clear ownership of AI decisions at the MD level; AI governance integrated into existing risk framework
- **Model risk management:** AI inventory, validation independent of development, stress testing of outputs, escalation protocols for model failures
- **Transparency:** AI-affected client outcomes must be explainable; human operators must be able to understand and justify AI-assisted decisions
- **Documentation:** AI system documentation, model validation records, human review records
- **Third-party AI risk:** Vendor due diligence, AI tool terms reviewed against firm obligations

FINMA began on-site inspections and supervisory exchanges on AI governance in late 2023 and has continued through 2025–2026. Implementation timelines for the new AI model risk governance circular (Q1 2026) set first compliance assessments for Q1 2027.

**Geneva-specific note:** Boutique wealth managers (50–200 employees) are within scope but face disproportionate implementation burden relative to larger institutions. FINMA's principle-based proportionality approach means firms must self-assess what adequate AI governance looks like at their scale — and bear the accountability if that self-assessment is wrong.

### FINMA Circular 2026/1 — Nature-Related Financial Risks

Effective January 2026 (phased for Category 1 and 2 institutions). Requires integration of nature-related financial risk into governance and risk management frameworks. While not AI-specific, the circular is evidence of FINMA's direction: new risk categories requiring new documentation, new governance integration, and new MD-level accountability. The AI governance framework is developing on the same trajectory.

### ASIP — Pension Fund AI Governance (Q3 2026 Deadline)

ASIP issued its model AI governance framework for occupational pension funds (February 2026) with a Q3 2026 self-assessment deadline. Geneva wealth managers managing corporate pension assets fall under ASIP frameworks for those mandates. The framework covers AI use in investment decision support and member communications. Small and mid-sized pension funds face the most acute gap: limited internal AI governance expertise, no vendor documentation proportionate to scale. The ASIP self-assessment deadline is an accountability occasion for Geneva boutiques managing pension assets.

### SFAMA / AMAS — Asset Management Standards

SFAMA (Swiss Fund and Asset Management Association) and AMAS (Asset Management Association Switzerland) provide guidance on AI use in asset management, particularly for fund managers. Boutique wealth managers operating advisory rather than fund management may assume SFAMA frameworks do not apply — but the boundary between advisory and asset management in SFAMA's framework does not map cleanly to boutique AI use cases. The SFAMA AI circular documentation obligations may exist but are not being applied at boutique scale because of the assumed boundary. This is an untested assumption.

### ISSB S2 — Sustainability Disclosure and AI Data Lineage

ISSB S2 (Climate-related Disclosures) applies to listed entities and, in Swiss implementation, to larger asset managers and institutional investors. AI is used to assemble ESG data for ISSB-aligned client reports — carbon footprint estimation, transition risk assessment, climate exposure analysis. The specific exposure: AI estimates data points where actual entity-specific asset-level electricity data (Scope 2) does not exist. The report presents AI estimates in ISSB-aligned format. The precision is in the presentation, not the underlying data. The firm presenting the report may be unaware that the ISSB alignment is achieved through AI formatting of unreliable data. No boutique-scale methodology exists for documenting the AI assembly of ESG data as distinct from the underlying data quality.

### nFADP — Data Protection with Specific Wealth Management Application

The revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP, in force January 2024) applies to client data processing by Geneva wealth managers. Key obligations with specific application to AI:

- **Art. 6(1)(b) Legitimate Interest Balancing Test:** AI inference on client portfolio data — behavioral profiling, risk propensity projection, financial objective inference — requires documented balancing test: firm legitimate interest versus data subject rights. Test must be reassessed when inference use cases change. No Geneva boutique has conducted this balancing test. The processing is happening on an implied basis, not a documented one.
- **Art. 8 Automated Decision Profiling Notification:** AI-assisted portfolio construction involves profiling. Data subjects have the right to be notified when profiling is used in decisions affecting them, and to request human review. Geneva wealth managers have not issued profiling notifications as a category. The obligation is present-tense and unnamed.
- **Art. 13/21 Access and Explanation Requests:** Clients may request explanation of how AI systems affected decisions in their portfolio. The system documentation required to answer these requests does not exist at boutique scale.
- **Art. 9 Third-Party Processing Authorization:** Where AI vendor infrastructure processes client data, authorization basis must be documented. Engagement letters at most Geneva boutiques were written before AI adoption and do not address this authorization.

### AI Convention — Council of Europe (Council of Europe AI Convention)

The AI Convention (Treaty No. 225, entered into force 2024) creates accountability obligations for AI systems used in consequential decisions. Applicable to private sector actors in signatory states. Switzerland has signed but not ratified. Key obligations once applicable: impact assessment requirements for high-risk AI systems, transparency obligations for AI-affected decisions, human oversight requirements, accountability for AI-assisted decisions. At boutique scale, the impact assessment obligation is undefined in methodology. Geneva market has not produced a private sector impact assessment framework for wealth management firms.

### EU AI Act — Extraterritorial Reach for Swiss Boutiques with EU Clients

The EU AI Act (high-risk obligations effective August 2026) applies to Swiss firms deploying AI systems whose output is used within the EU, or supplying AI systems to EU entities. Geneva boutiques with EU-based clients or EU counterparty mandates may be within scope without awareness. The EU AI Act does not stop at the Swiss border for firms with EU activity. No Geneva boutique has evaluated whether EU AI Act obligations apply to their portfolio of EU-facing mandates.

## 06 — Findings Summary | Six Net-New Insights, Distinct from C214–C275 Phase 02

### Finding 01 — The Periodic Client Report as a Discrete AI Disclosure Documentation Moment

Every periodic client report is a specific accountability occasion for AI use. The disclosure question is not "did we disclose AI use in general" — it is "what role did AI play in this specific report, what methodology was used, and what can the client infer from that methodology?" A general engagement letter disclosure does not close this obligation. It creates a reference point that clients use to evaluate specific reports. When a client asks a methodology question about a specific report, a general disclosure may increase exposure by acknowledging AI use without satisfying the specific question. This is a discrete documentation moment requiring a specific methodology disclosure architecture for periodic client reports, distinct from general AI use disclosure at engagement. **Distinct from C275 Finding 1 (institutional methodology disclosure vs policy compliance) — this finding names the periodic client report as the specific disclosure occasion with a different evidence requirement than institutional questionnaires or carrier examinations.**

### Finding 02 — The Investment Policy Statement Structural Incompatibility with AI-Assisted Portfolio Construction

The IPS is architecturally incompatible with AI-assisted portfolio construction — not additively but structurally. The IPS is designed as a static document describing a relatively stable decision process. AI-assisted decision-making is a dynamic, continuously updating process. Adding AI language to an existing IPS does not resolve structural incompatibility because the IPS architecture cannot accommodate the documentation requirements of a continuously updating AI system. The specific documentation need is a discrete AI governance supplement that operates in parallel to the IPS, with its own update cycle and its own evidence standard, not AI language inserted into an existing static document. No Geneva boutique has built this supplement. No Geneva service has offered it as a discrete product. **Distinct from C274 Finding 6 (documentation system coherence as firm-level property) — this finding names the specific structural incompatibility between IPS architecture and AI system behavior as the mechanism creating the documentation gap, not documentation coherence quality between individual documents.**

### Finding 03 — The MD-PM Accountability Line as a Discrete Organizational Documentation Question at Boutique Scale

At boutique wealth management scale (50–200 employees), the portfolio manager uses AI tools and the MD is personally accountable for client outcomes. The accountability line between MD and PM for AI-assisted decisions is not a chain-of-command question — it is a documentation question with specific evidence requirements. The question is: at what threshold does an AI-assisted recommendation from a PM require documented MD review before going to a client? What does that documentation consist of? What happens when AI-assisted recommendations are consistently adopted without documented review? This is distinct from oversight quality because it addresses documentation architecture, not oversight process. The documentation of MD review is a different artifact from the PM's AI output. No Geneva service has built a framework for this at boutique scale. **Distinct from C273 Finding 3 (institutional counterparty consistency) and C274 Finding 4 (dialogue consistency) — those findings addressed multi-party consistency across conversations; this finding addresses the organizational documentation line within the firm between MD and PM for AI-assisted decisions specifically.**

### Finding 04 — The Institutional Client Questionnaire as Competitive Intelligence Instrument with Structural Disclosure Tension

Institutional AI due diligence questionnaires sent to Geneva boutiques perform two simultaneous functions that are in structural tension: compliance exercise and competitive intelligence instrument. The compliance function requires disclosure. The competitive intelligence function requires discretion. Both functions are real and neither can be optimized without affecting the other. No Geneva boutique has a documented framework for managing this tension — distinguishing between questions that can be answered directly, questions that require prior documentation to be built, and questions where disclosure is not required and the competitive risk of the question itself must be managed. This is a discrete advisory service category unnamed in the Geneva market. **Distinct from C268 Finding 4 (carrier questionnaire pre-signing review) and C272 Finding 3 (attribution disclosure reveal) — those findings addressed carrier and counterparty questionnaire adequacy; this finding names the specific competitive intelligence exposure embedded in institutional client questionnaires as a structurally distinct service category requiring discrete advisory response.**

### Finding 05 — nFADP Article 8 Profiling Notification as Present-Tense Unnamed Obligation for AI-Assisted Portfolio Construction

AI-assisted portfolio construction at Geneva wealth management firms constitutes profiling under nFADP Article 8: behavioral inference about client risk tolerance, financial objectives, and investment horizon. Profiling is an automated decision process. Swiss data protection law gives data subjects the right to notification when profiling is used and to request human review of profiling-based decisions. Geneva wealth management boutiques have not issued profiling notifications to their clients as a category. The obligation is present-tense: it exists now, regardless of whether the firm has identified it. Activating the obligation requires determining which client interactions constitute profiling, what notification language satisfies the obligation, what human review mechanism must be available, and how the notification integrates with existing client communication frameworks. No Geneva boutique has completed this analysis. No Geneva service has published a profiling notification methodology for wealth management firms. **Distinct from C270 Finding 3 (FIDLEG Art.26 algorithmic routine disclosure) and C271 Finding 1 (nFADP Art.6 legitimate interest balancing test) — those findings addressed different articles of the nFADP; this finding names Art.8 profiling notification as a distinct present-tense unnamed obligation with a different activation pathway than Art.6 balancing test or Art.21 explanation requests.**

### Finding 06 — AI Usage Documentation vs. AI Tool Documentation as Structurally Different Artifacts with Different Ownership

The market produces AI tool documentation (vendor-supplied, describing what the AI system does) and does not produce AI usage documentation (firm-specific, describing how the firm uses the AI system within its own decision architecture). These are structurally different artifacts with different ownership, different update cycles, and different accountability functions. AI tool documentation is the vendor's document. AI usage documentation is the firm's document. A firm cannot use vendor AI tool documentation to answer an institutional methodology question about its own AI-assisted decision process. No Geneva service has built AI usage documentation as a discrete service category for boutique wealth managers. The consequence: every Geneva boutique with AI-assisted client reporting has vendor AI tool documentation and no firm-specific AI usage documentation. The accountability gap is structural, not curable by better tool documentation. **Distinct from C268 Finding 5 (reasoning chain as discrete accountability object) and C273 Finding 6 (regulatory documentation integrity gap) — those findings addressed reasoning chain and documentation quality/adequacy; this finding names the specific structural distinction between AI tool documentation and AI usage documentation as two different artifact types requiring different solution architectures.**
