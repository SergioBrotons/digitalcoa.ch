## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Portfolio-wide AI model concentration as accumulating undisclosed systemic exposure at boutique scale**
Boutique Geneva wealth managers run the same AI model across all client portfolios simultaneously. Model-driven adjustments propagate across the full book at the same time. No Geneva market participant tracks the correlated effect across mandates. The accumulation of identical AI-direction across portfolios creates a portfolio-level exposure that is structurally different from individual mandate exposure. When the model is wrong in the same direction for everyone at the same time, the boutique's entire book is affected before any individual client raises a question. This is a systemic risk property that is not visible from inside any single client relationship and is not addressed by any Geneva advisory service.

**Signal 2: Third-party AI vendor model update liability allocation as unnamed commercial exposure with no contractual framework**
AI vendors update models without contractual obligation to notify clients. Wealth managers send client reports generated under a model version that changed without their knowledge. When clients question why recommendations changed, the MD is accountable for a decision the vendor made silently. No Geneva market participant has a contractual framework addressing model update notification obligations, version documentation requirements for client report purposes, or liability allocation when a silent model update produces changed recommendations. The gap is present-tense and structural. The vendor's terms of service typically disclaim liability for downstream consequences of model changes. The firm absorbs the client relationship consequence.

**Signal 3: Post-mandate AI documentation obligation as discrete unnamed multi-framework retention problem**
When a Geneva wealth management mandate terminates, the firm must navigate simultaneous retention obligations under FINMA file retention requirements, nFADP data minimization and erasure obligations, and ISSB documentation requirements if sustainability reports were produced. These frameworks point in different directions. Retention for regulatory purposes may conflict with data minimization obligations. ISSB documentation standards may require retaining data the nFADP requires to be erased. No Geneva market service has produced a post-mandate documentation protocol that maps these three frameworks simultaneously. The problem is not that the frameworks exist — it is that they are in tension and no methodology exists for resolving the tension at boutique scale.

**Signal 4: ISSB S2 scope 2 climate disclosure and AI data lineage gap as specific compliance problem distinct from general ISSB alignment**
ISSB S2 requires entity-specific Scope 2 electricity consumption data attributed to specific assets. Boutique Geneva wealth managers managing portfolios of listed equities, private markets, and alternative allocations face a specific problem: actual electricity consumption data at the asset level is not available for most holdings. AI systems estimate it. The AI-generated estimate enters the ISSB-aligned client report as a data point. The report calls itself ISSB-aligned. The alignment is with ISSB framework structure, not with ISSB's requirement for actual, entity-specific, asset-level data. The gap between what the AI can produce and what ISSB S2 actually requires is a specific compliance exposure that has not been named in Geneva market discourse.

**Signal 5: Personal MD liability exposure across simultaneous accountability instruments without separation or coverage review**
Managing directors of Geneva boutique wealth management firms sign multiple accountability instruments simultaneously: FINMA attestations, carrier AI governance questionnaires, institutional client counterparty questionnaires, and ISSB disclosure sign-offs. Each signature carries a different type of personal exposure, a different legal consequence, and potentially different insurance coverage. No Geneva market participant has produced a framework that separates these exposures, identifies which attach personally to the MD versus the firm, or maps coverage adequacy across them. The instruments are being signed in a cluster with no separation of the exposures they create. Carrier questionnaires increasingly probe AI governance adequacy with questions that generate personal liability for the signing MD with no clarity about what coverage attaches.

**Signal 6: Geneva cross-border wealth management AI accountability as multi-jurisdictional obligation intersection without mapping methodology**
Geneva wealth management mandates are structurally cross-border: Swiss law governs the mandate, the client is resident abroad, assets are held across multiple jurisdictions, and AI vendor infrastructure crosses jurisdictions the firm has not mapped. The accountability obligations do not stop at the Swiss border. Client domicile law may impose AI disclosure obligations on the firm. The AI vendor's data processing jurisdictions create data protection obligations for the firm as data controller. The SFAMA, FINMA, and nFADP frameworks are Swiss domestic; they do not account for the client's local law layer. No Geneva market service has produced a multi-jurisdictional AI accountability mapping methodology for the cross-border Geneva wealth management context. The problem is not that obligations are unknown — it is that they accumulate across jurisdictions without a framework that tells the boutique which obligations take priority when they conflict.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA and the AI model concentration exposure:**
FINMA's suitability and best execution obligations apply at the individual mandate level. When AI-driven adjustments propagate simultaneously across all mandates, the individual suitability documentation does not capture the portfolio-level accumulation effect. FINMA has not issued guidance on whether AI model concentration across a boutique's full book constitutes a separate supervisory concern. The exposure is not addressed by existing FINMA examination practice.

**FIDLEG and cross-border mandate AI disclosure obligations:**
FIDLEG governs investment advice provided to Swiss-resident clients. When a Geneva wealth manager advises a non-Swiss-resident client under a Swiss-law mandate, the disclosure obligations under FIDLEG apply alongside the client's local law obligations. The client's jurisdiction may impose AI-specific disclosure requirements that FIDLEG does not address. The firm may be subject to two disclosure frameworks simultaneously without a methodology for reconciling them. No Geneva authority has addressed this specific intersection.

**ASIP/BVG occupational pension and cross-border AI advisory obligations:**
When BVG pension entitlements are a component of a client's overall wealth plan, the AI-assisted advisory falls under ASIP oversight for the pension component. ASIP requirements for AI documentation in pension advisory have not been published. The intersection of ASIP obligations with FINMA advisory obligations for the same client AI recommendation is an unresolved multi-framework problem at boutique scale.

**SFAMA documentation obligations and the post-mandate retention tension:**
SFAMA's documentation requirements for AI-assisted asset management require retention of records supporting recommendations and portfolio construction decisions. These requirements may conflict with nFADP data minimization obligations when the same records contain client personal data that the client has requested erasure of after the mandate terminates. No published guidance resolves this tension for the specific context of boutique wealth management mandates that generated AI-assisted sustainability documentation.

**ISSB S2 and the AI data lineage problem:**
ISSB S2 requires actual, entity-specific Scope 2 electricity consumption data attributed to specific assets. For listed equities, private markets, and alternatives, this data is not available at the asset level from standard data providers. AI systems estimate it. The estimation methodology and its limitations must be disclosed under ISSB's transparency requirements. The disclosure must distinguish between actual data and estimates, and the estimate methodology must be documented. The AI data lineage — what the AI estimated, how, from what inputs — must be part of the disclosure record. No boutique-scale methodology exists for building this record.

**nFADP and multi-jurisdictional processing obligations:**
nFADP applies to the processing of Swiss-resident data subjects' personal data. When a Geneva wealth manager processes client portfolio data in AI vendor infrastructure that operates outside Switzerland, the firm is exporting personal data. The nFADP Art.9 third-party transfer provisions require adequate safeguards. The AI vendor's infrastructure may span jurisdictions that the firm has not identified. The multi-jurisdictional data flow is not documented. The firm's obligations as data controller extend to the entire processing chain, including jurisdictions the firm has not mapped. The accumulation of unmapped cross-border processing is present-tense and compounding.
