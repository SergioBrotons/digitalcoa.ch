Typical language patterns:

- "We have client portfolios that were constructed with AI recommendations from eighteen months ago. If those recommendations were wrong, or the model changed, we cannot re-run the analysis on the same inputs. The output is gone but the client still holds the position."

## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

### Problem 1: The Liability Tail — AI Outputs That Outlive the Model

The most structurally underappreciated problem at boutique Geneva wealth management firms is the liability tail: AI-generated recommendations that have been incorporated into client portfolios and that now exist independently of the AI system that produced them. When the portfolio analytics AI was updated in Q3 2025, client positions that were built on Q1 2025 recommendations are still held. If those recommendations were calibrated differently under the prior model — different risk tolerance settings, different correlation assumptions — the positions may be inappropriate under the current model, but the client holds them and the firm cannot reconstruct what the prior model actually recommended.

The failure mode: the firm discovers the gap only when a client dispute arises, a FINMA examination asks about historical recommendation chains, or the AI vendor discloses that the prior model's methodology was substantially different from the current one. By that point, the documentation to reconstruct the decision has degraded or does not exist.

### Problem 2: The Counterparty Cascade — EU AI Governance DD Flowing into Geneva Boutique Firms

Geneva boutique wealth management firms are receiving AI governance due diligence questionnaires from EU institutional counterparties — asset managers in Frankfurt, family offices in Luxembourg, pension funds in Amsterdam — as part of their standard counterparty onboarding process. These questionnaires are not FINMA requirements. They are the result of EU AI Act implementation cascading through the institutional investment chain. Swiss boutique firms that assumed their regulatory obligations ended at the border are discovering that their counterparty relationships impose AI governance standards from Brussels.

The paradox: the firm cannot control what EU regulatory frameworks require, but their business relationships with EU counterparties are now conditional on demonstrating AI governance practices that no Swiss authority has mandated. The competitive consequence: counterparties with a choice will route business to firms that can produce the questionnaire response, all else being equal.

### Problem 3: The Documentation Ownership Gap

Boutique Geneva wealth management firms have AI governance documentation — the compliance team has produced records, the managing director has reviewed them. But the documentation exists in the compliance folder, not in the client file. When a client dispute arises or a relationship manager needs to reconstruct what was recommended and why, the record that exists is the compliance record, not the advisory record. These are not the same thing.

The compliance record shows that a review occurred. The advisory record would show what the review found, what was changed, what the client was told. The gap between these two records is where accountability dissolves. No one at the boutique firm has been assigned to own this gap — the compliance team assumes advisory owns it, the relationship managers assume compliance owns it, and the managing director assumes it is handled.

### Problem 4: AI Vendor Sustainability as Unowned Business Continuity Risk

Boutique Geneva wealth management firms are running AI tools from vendors that have not been evaluated for sustainability — whether the vendor has sufficient runway, whether the pricing model is stable, whether the contract includes data portability provisions. When an AI vendor announces a price change, a model discontinuation, or a company wind-down, the boutique firm has no migration plan, no contractual basis to require continuity, and client data sitting in a system that may become inaccessible or unaffordable.

The failure mode is not hypothetical. AI vendors restructure, discontinue models, raise prices, and shut down. The boutique firm that has integrated AI into its client reporting workflow discovers the dependency only when the vendor event occurs. At that point, the client data is the firm's liability, and extracting it depends on contractual provisions that were not negotiated with this scenario in mind.

### Problem 5: The Human Review Adequacy Problem — No Definition of Sufficient

Every boutique Geneva wealth management firm with AI in the advisory workflow has a human review process. Every one of those firms has never defined what a sufficient review looks like. The process exists: an advisor reviews the AI output before sending to the client. The standard does not exist: how thorough must the review be, what specifically must the reviewer verify, and what does the documentation of a review that was done properly look like?

The consequence is that human review provides less protection than the firm believes it does. A FINMA examiner asking about the substantive quality of human review will find that the firm cannot demonstrate what the review consisted of — only that it occurred. The distinction between process documentation (review happened) and substantive documentation (review met a defined standard) is the difference between a defense and a gap.

### Problem 6: The Regulatory Arbitrage Illusion

Geneva boutique wealth management firms operate under the assumption that Swiss regulatory obligations — primarily FINMA — define the full scope of their AI governance requirements. The assumption is structurally incorrect. Swiss firms with EU counterparties, EU clients, or EU-listed investments are subject to EU AI Act obligations that flow through those relationships, regardless of Swiss domestic regulation. The EU AI Act does not stop at the border for Swiss firms engaged in cross-border advisory activities.

The regulatory arbitrage illusion: the firm believes it is exempt from EU requirements because it is not EU-based, while simultaneously building client relationships that expose it to exactly those requirements. The discovery moment — when a counterparty questionnaire, a client DD request, or a regulatory examination surfaces the actual scope of obligations — arrives after the exposure has accumulated.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis: Gaps, What No One Is Addressing

### Gap 1: AI Output Duration Tracking and Portfolio Recomputation Protocol

No advisory service exists to help boutique Geneva wealth management firms manage the liability tail problem — the systematic tracking of AI-generated recommendations that persist in client portfolios after the AI model has changed. This requires a methodology for identifying which client positions were recommended under which model version, assessing whether current positions remain appropriate given model evolution, and documenting the recomputation or confirmation process. This is distinct from model versioning (which tracks the technology) and from audit documentation (which records decisions made). This specifically addresses what happens to the firm's advisory accountability when the AI tool that generated the original recommendation no longer operates at the same calibration.

### Gap 2: EU Counterparty AI Governance DD Response Service for Swiss Boutiques

No advisory provider offers a specific service to help Geneva boutique wealth management firms respond to AI governance due diligence questionnaires from EU institutional counterparties — a structured methodology for understanding which EU AI Act obligations apply to a Swiss boutique firm through its counterparty relationships, producing a questionnaire response that is both accurate and strategically useful. This is distinct from Swiss regulatory compliance (FINMA-aligned) and from general AI governance frameworks. It specifically addresses the cascade problem: regulatory pressure from jurisdiction A flowing through business relationship B into a Swiss boutique firm with no dedicated regulatory affairs function.

### Gap 3: Documentation Ownership and Location Framework for Boutique AI Governance Records

No advisory service addresses the specific problem of where AI governance documentation belongs operationally — which records go into the client file, which into the compliance file, who owns the update obligation, and how the two records are reconciled when a client dispute arises. This is a process design problem, not a policy problem. The boutique firm does not need a policy that says AI governance documentation should be complete. It needs a structural solution that places documentation in the right location, assigns ownership of the update obligation, and creates a reconciliation mechanism between advisory records and compliance records.

### Gap 4: AI Vendor Sustainability Assessment and Wind-Down Contingency Planning

No advisory provider offers a methodology specifically designed for boutique Geneva wealth management firms to evaluate AI vendor sustainability — assessing whether SaaS AI vendors have sufficient commercial longevity, contractual data portability provisions, and a viable migration path if the vendor discontinues the tool, raises pricing substantially, or exits the market. This is distinct from technology due diligence (which examines the tool's current functionality) and from vendor contract review (which examines the legal provisions). This specifically addresses the business continuity dimension: if this vendor disappears, what happens to the client data and the advisory processes built on this tool.

### Gap 5: Human Review Adequacy Standard for Boutique AI-Assisted Advisory

No service exists to help boutique Geneva wealth management firms define what a substantively adequate human review of AI output looks like — a standard that is operationally achievable for a firm of fifty to two hundred employees, that produces documentation of review quality rather than just review occurrence, and that can be explained to a FINMA examiner as a defined process with defined outputs. This is not a question of adding more documentation. It is a question of designing a review process that is substantive enough to constitute genuine oversight, and documenting it in a way that demonstrates quality rather than just presence.

### Gap 6: Regulatory Scope Mapping for Swiss Boutique Firms with Cross-Border Advisory Exposure

No advisory provider has developed a practical regulatory scope mapping tool specifically for Geneva boutique wealth management firms — a structured assessment of which regulatory frameworks apply to the firm through which business relationships, what obligations each framework creates, and how to prioritize compliance investment when multiple jurisdictions are in scope simultaneously. This is distinct from general regulatory compliance mapping (which is designed for large institutions) and from Swiss domestic compliance frameworks (which do not address the cross-border cascade problem). This specifically helps the managing director understand the actual regulatory landscape — not just FINMA, but the aggregate of obligations flowing from counterparty relationships, client jurisdictions, and cross-border advisory activities.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations

### FINMA — AI Model Risk Governance Timeline and Supervisory Reality

FINMA is developing an AI model risk governance framework with publication anticipated in Q4 2026, following the consultation process completed in 2025. The framework is expected to address three tiers of supervised institutions, with the tier relevant to boutique Geneva wealth management firms requiring documentation of AI model inventory, validation records, human review standards, and incident reporting procedures.

The supervisory reality that is not yet captured in the published framework: FINMA examiners are asking boutique firms about AI governance practices in current examinations, using informal questions that are more advanced than the published consultation documents. The private supervisory standard — what is actually being asked in the examination room — is distinct from the public framework and will remain so until the formal framework is published and implemented.

For boutique wealth management firms, the relevant FINMA obligations arise under the existing Supervisory Organization Ordinance (FINMA-FSO) and the Anti-Money Laundering Act, both of which require adequate documentation of advisory processes. The AI model risk governance framework, once published, will add specific documentation requirements that are not yet formally binding but are already influencing examination questions.

### ASIP — Occupational Pension Fund AI Governance Overlay

The Swiss Occupational Benefit Plans Act (BVG) applies to pension fund management, but Geneva boutique wealth management firms advising occupational pension funds on asset allocation and investment decisions are subject to ASIP oversight for the pension fund itself. When AI-assisted recommendations enter pension fund governance decisions, the question of whether the AI tool's output meets the BVG's duty of care requirements becomes relevant. ASIP has not published specific AI guidance for pension fund advisors, but the underlying duty of care obligations apply regardless of whether the advisory process is AI-assisted or human-only.

For boutique wealth management firms with occupational pension fund clients, ASIP oversight creates a second-layer accountability: the pension fund's trustees are responsible for AI governance decisions affecting fund assets, and the boutique firm advising the fund is a component of that decision chain. Documentation of AI tool use in pension fund advisory contexts is not optional — it is the record that demonstrates the fund's duty of care was discharged.

### SFAMA — Asset Management AI Documentation and Performance Standards

The Swiss Fund and Asset Management Association (SFAMA) has incorporated AI governance considerations into its guidance for member firms, with specific attention to documentation of AI-assisted portfolio management decisions. For boutique Geneva firms that are SFAMA members or that interact with SFAMA-member institutions as counterparties, SFAMA standards represent a market-formed benchmark that operates alongside FINMA requirements.

The practical relevance for boutique firms: when a SFAMA-member institution conducts counterparty due diligence on a boutique firm, SFAMA AI governance expectations are part of the assessment. The market standard and the regulatory standard are beginning to converge, and firms that can demonstrate alignment with SFAMA expectations have a counterparty advantage that is independent of FINMA compliance status.

### ISSB — International Sustainability Standards Board Disclosure Requirements

The ISSB has published IFRS ISSB standards on climate-related disclosures, with adoption accelerating among institutional investors and counterparties. For Geneva boutique wealth management firms advising clients on sustainable investment portfolios, ISSB-aligned disclosure expectations are beginning to appear in client due diligence processes — particularly for clients subject to ISSB disclosure obligations through EU or UK listing requirements.

The practical implication: AI tools used in sustainable investment analysis must be able to produce documentation that supports ISSB-aligned disclosure claims. If the AI tool generates sustainability ratings or climate risk assessments that enter client disclosures, the boutique firm needs to be able to explain the AI tool's methodology, data sources, and limitations in ISSB-appropriate terms. This is not yet a formal regulatory requirement for Swiss boutique firms, but it is an emerging counterparty and client expectation that is accelerating.

### nFADP — Client Data Processing in AI Tool Infrastructure

The Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) applies to client data processing in AI tools deployed by Geneva wealth management firms. The specific obligation that creates the most acute exposure: client portfolio data — holdings, income, risk profiles, family circumstances — flowing into third-party AI SaaS tools triggers data processing notification requirements under nFADP Article 12. The client must be informed that their data is being processed by a third-party AI system, the legal basis for processing must be established, and the data processing must be documented.

The practical problem at boutique scale: the data processing agreements with AI vendors were not drafted to address nFADP requirements, the clients have not been notified in the required form, and the documentation of legal basis is incomplete or absent. The exposure is not speculative — it is present-tense for every boutique firm that has integrated AI tools without reviewing its nFADP compliance architecture. FDPIC has inspection powers and has been active in examining financial services AI data processing practices.

## 06 — Findings Summary: 6 Net-New Insights Distinct from C214–C226 Phase 02

### Finding 1: The Liability Tail Problem — AI Output Duration Creating Unresolved Portfolio Accountability

The liability tail problem is distinct from all prior Phase 02 findings because it addresses a temporal dimension no prior cycle has examined: client positions that were AI-recommended under a prior model version, that persist in portfolios after the model has changed, and for which the firm has no systematic recomputation or confirmation protocol. Prior findings addressed attribution failure (which AI system produced the output) and model update silence (undetected model changes). This finding addresses what happens to advisory accountability when the recommending model no longer operates at its original calibration and the position it recommended is still held. This is the primary liability exposure for boutique firms with long-hold client portfolios — not the AI tool's current state, but the accumulated state of all AI recommendations ever made that remain live in client accounts.

### Finding 2: EU Counterparty AI Governance DD as De Facto Regulatory Layer for Swiss Boutiques

The EU AI Act cascade through counterparty relationships creates a de facto regulatory layer for Geneva boutique wealth management firms that is not FINMA, not ASIP, not SFAMA, and not Swiss domestic law — but that is operationally equivalent to a regulatory requirement because non-compliance means losing counterparty relationships. This finding is distinct from prior Phase 02 findings because prior cycles addressed the EU AI Act's direct applicability to Swiss firms with EU operations; this finding addresses the specific mechanism by which EU AI Act pressure reaches Swiss boutique firms that have no EU presence: the counterparty questionnaire as regulatory enforcement pathway. The finding reframes the problem from "does the EU AI Act apply to us" to "which of our counterparty relationships is already applying EU AI Act standards to us, and do we have a response."

### Finding 3: Documentation Ownership Gap as Structural Process Failure Distinct from Policy Failure

The documentation ownership gap — AI governance records existing in the compliance folder rather than the client file, with no clear owner of the reconciliation obligation between the two — is a structural process failure, not a policy failure. This finding is distinct from prior Phase 02 findings because prior cycles addressed documentation debt (records not being produced) and documentation theater (records being produced but not protective). This finding addresses the orthogonal problem of documentation existing but being in the wrong operational location — meaning the right record is not available when it is needed, even though it exists somewhere in the firm's files. The solution is not more documentation; it is a process that places documentation in the location where it will be needed when a client dispute or regulatory examination occurs.

### Finding 4: AI Vendor Sustainability as Unowned Business Continuity Exposure at Boutique Scale

AI vendor sustainability risk — the scenario where a SaaS AI vendor discontinues a tool, raises pricing substantially, or exits the market, leaving the boutique firm with client data in an inaccessible system and no contractual basis for continuity — is unowned at boutique scale. This finding is distinct from prior Phase 02 findings because prior cycles addressed vendor compliance claims (unverified vendor assurances), vendor concentration (too many tools from one vendor), and vendor data flow documentation (where data goes). This finding addresses vendor viability as a business continuity dimension: the AI tool may be fully compliant, fully documented, and fully integrated — and still become unavailable in a way that creates immediate client service disruption and data extraction obligations with no pre-negotiated exit path.

### Finding 5: Human Review Adequacy as a Distinct Standard Problem — Process vs. Substantive Documentation

The human review adequacy problem — boutique firms having a human review process but never defining what adequate review looks like, producing documentation of review occurrence rather than review quality — is distinct from all prior Phase 02 findings because it identifies the specific gap between process documentation and substantive documentation. Prior findings addressed documentation debt, documentation theater, and documentation ownership. This finding addresses the quality dimension: even when documentation is produced and correctly located, it may document that a review happened without documenting that the review was substantive. For a FINMA examiner asking whether human review constitutes genuine oversight of AI output, the documentation must demonstrate quality of review — not just presence of review. This finding identifies a specific standard that is achievable at boutique scale and produces documentation that can survive examination.

### Finding 6: Regulatory Arbitrage Illusion as Distinct from Regulatory Scope Gap

The regulatory arbitrage illusion — the assumption that Swiss domestic regulatory obligations (primarily FINMA) define the full scope of AI governance requirements, while cross-border counterparty and client relationships are simultaneously creating exposure to EU AI Act obligations, ISSB disclosure expectations, and SFAMA market standards — is distinct from prior Phase 02 findings because it addresses the belief structure, not the obligation structure. Prior findings identified specific cross-border obligations and specific counterparty cascades. This finding identifies the specific cognitive error: Swiss boutique firms believing they are outside EU regulatory scope because they are not EU-based, while building exactly the client and counterparty relationships that bring EU regulatory standards into their advisory practice. The finding reframes the strategic question from "are we subject to EU AI Act" to "which parts of the EU AI Act are already applying to us through our business relationships, and is our current governance posture consistent with what our counterparties and clients are beginning to require."
