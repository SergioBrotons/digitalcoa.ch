## 01 — ICP Language: How This ICP Actually Describes AI Problems

### English

The managing director or senior advisor at a boutique Geneva wealth management firm is not asking whether AI will create accountability exposure. They are noticing that the accountability occasions are arriving before the documentation architecture to survive them is in place.

Where an external observer might frame the situation as "AI governance framework implementation," the Geneva wealth manager describes it as: *"We got the policy written. The policy is a document. The question nobody told us to ask is what happens when someone asks you to show them the record behind the policy."*

The problem is expressed as a **structural timing mismatch**: the accountability occasions arrive on schedules the firm did not design for (carrier renewals, institutional client questionnaires, FINMA examinations), and the documentation that would allow the MD to answer those occasions with evidence was never built against those specific schedules.

Language that surfaces in this ICP's own framing:

- *"We have a policy. What we don't have is the other thing — the record of what actually happened"* — the distinction between a governance document and an operational evidence archive
- *"The carrier is now asking for something I've never been asked before, and it sounds like they want evidence, not a document"* — the shift from policy existence to operational evidence architecture as the specific new demand
- *"My clients who run compliance functions are asking questions that are more specific than the ones regulators asked last year"* — institutional client accountability expectations outpacing regulatory requirements
- *"We adopted AI and now the question is how to document what we adopted"* — adoption documentation gap surfacing only in retrospect
- *"The AI inventory is the thing we haven't built yet — we know we need one, we don't know what it looks like at our scale"* — the AI inventory obligation as named but unresolved at boutique scale

The ICP does not describe this as a technology adoption problem. They describe it as a **record architecture problem**: the firm adopted AI, the adoption created a record, and that record is the accountability exposure that will be examined when the next accountability occasion arrives.

## 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Failure Modes, Paradoxes, Complaints

### The Policy-Existence vs. Evidence-Architecture Gap Widening at Carrier Renewal

The most operationally acute market signal: firms with AI policies in place are discovering that the policy document and the operational evidence archive are two different artifacts. The policy answers the question "does the firm have a framework?" The evidence architecture must answer the question "can the MD describe, with contemporaneous records, how AI affected the specific decisions the carrier is asking about?"

Carriers are asking questions that policy documents cannot answer. The gap is not a documentation quality problem — it is a category mismatch. A policy is one kind of artifact. An evidence archive is a different kind of artifact, built against different evidence standards, serving different accountability functions. Most Geneva wealth managers have the first artifact and not the second.

### The FINMA Guidance 08/2024 AI Inventory Obligation Nobody Has Built at Boutique Scale

FINMA Guidance 08/2024 (December 2024) requires institutions to maintain comprehensive AI inventories, classifying applications by materiality and risk. The obligation is now formal. The boutique-specific problem: no Geneva market service has produced a proportionate AI inventory methodology for a firm with three to five people making investment decisions — a methodology that defines what counts as an AI application, what materiality threshold applies at boutique scale, and how the inventory is maintained by the same people whose job is not inventory management.

The complaint is not "we don't know about the inventory requirement." The complaint is "we know we need one, we don't know what it looks like when it's ours."

### The SIF Q2 2026 Regulatory Gap Analysis as Anticipatory Compliance Window

The State Secretariat for International Finance (SIF) is expected to publish a comprehensive analysis of financial market regulatory gaps for AI in Q2 2026. The anticipatory compliance implication for Geneva wealth managers: the analysis will describe the state of market practice as of mid-2026. Documentation architecture built before the analysis is published will be described as representative of market practice in that analysis. Documentation not built before publication will be described as a gap in the analysis.

This creates a specific anticipatory compliance occasion distinct from waiting for regulatory guidance. The window between now and Q2 2026 is when documentation positioning is visible to the regulatory gap analysis.

### The Agentic AI Third-Generation Accountability Question Arriving Before First-Generation Documentation Is Resolved

Early 2025 industry research shows 78% of wealth and asset managers are exploring agentic AI — AI systems designed to take autonomous actions across multiple steps. The boutique wealth manager who has not yet resolved first-generation AI documentation (how AI was used in portfolio decisions, what human review preceded each) is now confronting a third-generation question: what documentation does agentic AI require when the AI is taking sequential actions without human review at each step?

The paradox: firms that adopted AI without building documentation architecture are now being asked to document AI that operates differently from the AI they adopted. The documentation problem is compounding faster than the solutions.

### The Federal Council CoE AI Convention Ratification Timeline Creating Unnamed Private Law Obligations

Switzerland signed the Council of Europe AI Convention in March 2025 and the Federal Council decided to enable ratification in February 2025. Ratification creates obligations flowing through existing client contracts, carrier underwriting terms, and professional liability standards — before implementation legislation or published guidance. The specific private law operationalization pathway is unnamed in Geneva market discourse.

The complaint from sophisticated wealth managers: *"I keep hearing about the AI Convention. Nobody has told me what it means for the contracts I have active right now."*

### The Institutional Client Questionnaire as Institutional Standard-Formation Signal, Not Just Compliance Exercise

Institutional clients are sending AI governance questionnaires that are non-identical but converging. The convergence is not accidental — it reflects the formation of shared accountability standards within the institutional investment community. Each questionnaire that arrives is simultaneously a compliance exercise and a signal about what the institutional community considers adequate AI governance disclosure.

The market signal embedded in this: the institutional standard is forming through the convergence of individual questionnaires, before any Geneva-specific guidance on what adequate compliance means for boutique wealth managers. Firms that can read the convergence signal have a positioning advantage over firms waiting for published guidance.

## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations

### FINMA Guidance 08/2024: Formalized AI Governance Expectations

FINMA published Guidance 08/2024 in December 2024, formalizing AI governance expectations for financial institutions. The guidance is principle-based, tailored to Swiss financial sector risks, and specifically requires:

- **AI inventories**: Comprehensive inventories of AI applications, classified by materiality and risk likelihood
- **Three-tier model classification**: Standard, elevated, and high risk, determining validation and documentation requirements
- **Human oversight obligations**: Responsibility for AI-influenced decisions cannot be delegated to AI systems or third parties
- **Documentation and explainability**: AI outputs must be understandable and verifiable
- **Continuous monitoring**: Ongoing monitoring of AI system performance and risk

The accountability gap for Geneva wealth managers: FINMA's guidance is designed for institutions with compliance functions. The specific question the guidance raises but does not answer: what does "comprehensive AI inventory maintained by three to five people" look like in a 50–200 person boutique wealth management firm? No proportionate guidance exists.

### The SIF Q2 2026 Regulatory Gap Analysis

The State Secretariat for International Finance (SIF) is expected to publish a comprehensive analysis of financial market regulatory gaps for AI in Q2 2026. This analysis will:

- Assess all financial market regulations for gaps and obstacles for AI use
- Inform future regulatory direction in the financial sector
- Become a reference document for what "market practice" means in Swiss financial AI governance

The anticipatory compliance implication: documentation architecture in place before the analysis is published will be characterized as representative. Documentation absent at publication will be characterized as a gap. The timing is a structural property of the occasion, not a feature of the analysis.

### Federal Council CoE AI Convention Ratification Path

Switzerland signed the Council of Europe AI Convention in March 2025. The Federal Council decided to enable ratification in February 2025. The FDJP is tasked with preparing implementation legislation by end of 2026.

The present-tense implication: ratification creates immediate obligations through existing legal relationships. The Convention's obligations flow through:
- Client mandate contracts
- Carrier underwriting terms
- Professional liability standards
- FINMA supervisory expectations

No Geneva market participant has published the specific operational pathway from Convention ratification to boutique wealth management obligations. The exposure is present-tense and unnamed.

### ASIP (Association Suisse des Institutions de Prévoyance)

ASIP governs pension fund AI governance obligations. Pension funds receiving AI-assisted allocation recommendations from Geneva wealth managers have their own accountability requirements. The wealth manager's AI system operates within a two-layer accountability structure: its own FINMA/nFADP obligations and the pension fund client's ASIP requirements flowing backward through the advisory relationship.

No Geneva service has mapped this specific two-layer accountability architecture for boutique wealth managers.

### SFAMA and FIDLEG Article 26

SFAMA guidance on AI use in asset management is developing alongside FINMA supervisory expectations. FIDLEG Article 26 requires algorithmic routine disclosure, with different disclosure architecture for standard algorithmic execution vs. AI-assisted portfolio construction. The boutique-specific gap: no proportionate methodology for applying Article 26 disclosure requirements to mandates where AI contribution is partial, continuous, and integrated into ongoing advisory relationships rather than discrete and identifiable.

### nFADP (Revised, Operative September 2023)

The revised nFADP creates specific obligations Geneva wealth managers are still evaluating:

- **Article 6**: Lawful basis for processing client data through AI vendor infrastructure — purpose limitation violations may occur through AI model improvement use of data originally collected for portfolio management
- **Article 8**: Profiling notification — AI-assisted portfolio construction constitutes automated profiling, requiring notification and human review offer
- **Article 15**: Accuracy correction rights for AI-generated inferences about clients embedded in reports — present-tense obligation for inferences that may be inaccurate
- **Article 21**: Automated decision explanation — when a client requests explanation of how AI affected a specific recommendation

None of these obligations has been evaluated against the specific intersection of AI-generated advisory content in client reports at boutique scale.

### ISSB S1/S2 Climate Disclosure Standards

ISSB S2 requires entity-specific Scope 2 electricity consumption data. AI systems used in portfolio analysis produce estimates, not entity-specific asset-level data. Firms presenting AI-generated estimates as ISSB-aligned Scope 2 data have a disclosure accuracy problem not addressed by any Geneva market service.
