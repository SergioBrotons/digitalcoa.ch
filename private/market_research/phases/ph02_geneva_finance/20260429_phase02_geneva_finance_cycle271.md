## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Boutique wealth management AI infrastructure economics creating structural single-model concentration that has not been assessed as a correlated risk**
The economics of boutique-scale AI procurement naturally produce single-model architecture. One vendor relationship is manageable; multiple parallel AI systems are not. The result is structural: a single analytical framework driving portfolio construction, risk assessment, and rebalancing signals across every client mandate simultaneously. The concentration has not been named as a correlated risk. The assumption that consistent positioning is safer has not been tested against a correlated failure scenario. The boutique is carrying portfolio-wide exposure to a single model's failure mode. The exposure is invisible from inside any single client relationship. No Geneva market participant has produced a concentration assessment framework calibrated to boutique wealth management operations.

**Signal 2: AI vendor indemnification clause review absent from standard legal due diligence on vendor agreements**
Vendor terms of service universally contain indemnification provisions written by the vendor's legal team. Wealth managers and their counsel reviewing these agreements focus on data processing language and confidentiality terms. The indemnification clause — determining who is liable when the tool produces adverse outcomes — is not evaluated. The clause indemnifies the vendor against claims arising from client use of the tool; it remains silent on indemnifying the client for consequences of the vendor's tool behavior. The wealth manager signed without evaluating what the clause actually commits them to absorb. No Geneva market service has named indemnification architecture review as a discrete legal due diligence requirement for AI vendor agreements in wealth management.

**Signal 3: Simultaneous AI-human co-contribution as accountability question that surfaces before the methodology to answer it**
Clients are beginning to ask specific questions about AI contribution to recommendations. The question is not about the reasoning chain — it is about causal attribution: what part did the AI play versus what part did the human advisor play in the same recommendation. The simultaneous co-contribution structure of modern AI-assisted advisory makes this attribution structurally difficult. The law requires explaining algorithmic basis and automated decision logic — not attributing causal contribution between AI and human within a collaborative output. The MD's professional accountability requires knowing what produced the recommendation. These are different questions. The question surfaces in client conversations before any Geneva boutique has a methodology to answer it accurately. The market signal is the question arriving before the answer exists.

**Signal 4: Institutional counterparty AI questionnaire portfolio creating cumulative inconsistency exposure that has not been mapped**
Geneva boutiques receiving multiple AI governance questionnaires from different institutional clients face a consistency problem no one has named. Different personnel answer different questionnaires at different times. The answers will not be perfectly consistent. The inconsistencies are not deliberate. But inconsistent answers on the same subject matter across multiple commercial relationships may constitute representations that contradict each other under commercial law. No methodology exists for maintaining answer consistency across a client portfolio, flagging inconsistencies before submission, or updating prior answers when the AI system changes. The cumulative exposure from inconsistent answers is unnamed and unmapped.

**Signal 5: MD accountability instrument cluster operating without separation analysis between personal and corporate liability**
The Geneva boutique wealth management MD signs a cluster of accountability instruments: FINMA attestations, carrier questionnaires, institutional counterparty AI governance responses, ISSB disclosures. Each creates a different type of exposure. Some attach to the firm as a corporate entity; some attach to the signing individual personally. Corporate liability is limited by the corporate form. Personal professional liability is not. The magnitude of exposure differs across instruments. Coverage responds differently across each. No Geneva market participant has separated which instruments create personal MD liability versus firm liability, or produced a liability separation framework specifically calibrated to the Geneva boutique wealth management MD role. The signing decision is made without this separation, and all signatures are treated as equivalent risk events.

**Signal 6: Post-mandate data retention obligations in direct structural tension with no resolution protocol available**
When a mandate terminates, three obligations converge with no resolution methodology: nFADP data minimization (retain only strictly necessary, erase what is not), regulatory liability defense documentation (retain records documenting AI system behavior during the mandate), and ISSB sustainability documentation retention (retain support records for sustainability disclosures). These obligations are in direct structural tension. nFADP minimization requires erasure; liability defense and ISSB documentation require retention. No Geneva authority has provided resolution methodology. The nFADP Arts. 6, 13, and 21 compound the tension: Article 13 requires data subject access to automated decision records; minimization limits how long those records can be retained. The three provisions create a compound obligation for which no single framework provides resolution. Present-tense for every mandate that has terminated or will terminate.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA suitability oversight and the single-model concentration gap:**
FINMA's suitability framework is structured around individual mandate documentation. The question of whether correlated AI-driven positioning across all client mandates simultaneously constitutes a portfolio-level supervisory concern has not been addressed in FINMA guidance. FINMA has not examined whether a boutique's use of a single AI model architecture creates a separate suitability issue distinct from individual mandate suitability. The concentration question sits in a supervisory gap: individual mandate documentation does not capture book-wide correlated positioning, and no framework exists for assessing the specific correlated failure property of single-model architecture at boutique scale.

**FIDLEG Article 26 and the AI-human co-contribution attribution gap:**
FIDLEG Article 26 requires disclosure when investment advice is based on automated processing. The obligation addresses the existence of automated processing — it does not require attribution of causal contribution between the AI system and the human advisor within a collaborative recommendation. The law requires explaining the algorithmic basis; it does not require determining which component produced which part of the recommendation. The MD's professional accountability — understanding what the recommendation is and being able to stand behind it — requires a different analysis than what FIDLEG discharge requires. These are different questions with different answers, and the compliance gap is that they have been treated as the same question.

**ASIP/BVG and AI governance documentation at the pension-advisory intersection:**
ASIP has not published guidance on what AI governance documentation standards apply to wealth management advisory that incorporates BVG pension entitlements. When AI-assisted recommendations affect the pension component of a client's overall wealth plan, ASIP oversight reaches into the advisory process without a defined documentation standard. The pension layer creates obligations under a framework that has not addressed AI documentation; the advisory layer creates obligations under FINMA and FIDLEG frameworks that have. No framework resolves the documentation standard for the intersection. The gap is operational: boutiques serving clients with BVG pension components are operating under undocumented documentation standards at the intersection of two frameworks.

**SFAMA circular applicability to boutique wealth management advisory:**
SFAMA's circular on AI in asset management specifies documentation expectations for fund managers. Whether it applies to boutique wealth management advisory is contested and unnamed. The documentation standard exists; whether it applies, what it would require at boutique scale, and how to reconcile it with FINMA's advisory documentation expectations remain unresolved. Carriers and institutional counterparties are beginning to probe whether boutiques apply a documentation standard. The SFAMA circular functions as a reference point even for firms that believe it does not apply to them. The applicability question is untested and creating anticipatory compliance behavior in both directions: firms either over-applying the standard or ignoring it based on untested assumption.

**nFADP Articles 6, 13, and 21 and the compound attribution-retention tension:**
nFADP Article 6 requires a lawful basis for processing client portfolio data in AI systems. Article 21 requires explanation of the logic underlying automated decisions affecting clients. Article 13 requires that data subjects can access and understand how automated decisions were made about them. These three provisions intersect at the attribution question: explaining what the AI contributed requires knowing what the AI contributed. When the AI and human produced the recommendation simultaneously, the specific contribution of each cannot be reconstructed from documentation not designed to capture it. The retention obligation — keeping records long enough to discharge Article 13 and Article 21 obligations — collides with the minimization obligation: keeping records longer than necessary violates Article 6's principle that data should not be retained beyond its purpose. The three provisions create a compound tension for which no Geneva authority has provided resolution methodology, and which is present-tense for every active mandate.

**ISSB documentation retention and the nFADP erasure intersection at mandate termination:**
ISSB-aligned client reports produced during a mandate create documentation obligations that survive mandate termination. ISSB requires maintaining records supporting sustainability disclosures. When the mandate terminates, the nFADP erasure obligations apply to the client personal data underlying those reports. Retaining ISSB documentation necessarily contains client personal data; erasing client personal data under nFADP may eliminate ISSB documentation support records. The intersection is unresolved. No Geneva authority has addressed what ISSB documentation retention means in the context of nFADP data minimization obligations for terminated mandates, and the conflict is structural — the two obligations cannot be satisfied simultaneously through any known methodology.

**Finding 01: Single-model portfolio concentration as a discrete correlated AI risk category operating below the detection threshold of individual mandate oversight**
C270 Finding 01 named single-model concentration as a correlated AI risk, establishing the mechanism. This finding addresses a different dimension: the concentration operates below the detection threshold of the oversight architecture currently in place. Individual mandate suitability documentation is designed to assess each mandate independently. It cannot capture correlated directional positioning across the full client book simultaneously. The boutique is not merely carrying a concentration — it is carrying a concentration that its own oversight system was not designed to detect. The detection gap is structural: the supervisory architecture matches the documentation format for individual mandates, not the risk architecture of a single AI model driving all mandates simultaneously. FINMA's framework has not addressed whether correlated AI positioning across a boutique book is a distinct supervisory question from individual mandate suitability. No Geneva market participant has produced a detection methodology for correlated AI concentration that works within the boutique-scale oversight architecture. This is distinct from C270 Finding 01 in focus: C270 established the concentration mechanism; this finding addresses the specific detection gap created by the mismatch between the correlated risk architecture and the individual-mandate oversight architecture.

**Finding 02: AI vendor indemnification clause review as a discrete legal due diligence category absent from standard AI vendor contracting practice**
C270 Finding 02 identified the indemnification clause as the specific contractual mechanism creating unallocated downstream liability. This finding takes a different angle: the exposure is not merely that the clause exists and was not reviewed — it is that indemnification clause review has not been established as a discrete legal due diligence category in Geneva AI vendor contracting practice. Data processing provisions, confidentiality terms, and service level agreements are reviewed. The indemnification architecture is not. The reason is structural: legal review of vendor agreements focuses on provisions the client can evaluate without deep technical knowledge of the AI system. The indemnification clause requires understanding what the AI system is designed to do, what failure modes it exhibits, and what consequences follow from those failure modes — technical knowledge that legal review typically does not include and that the vendor is not motivated to disclose. No Geneva market service has positioned indemnification architecture review as a discrete legal due diligence product that requires both legal and technical evaluation capabilities. This is distinct from C270 Finding 02 in framing: C270 named the specific clause and its asymmetry; this finding identifies the structural reason the clause is not reviewed and the specific capability gap required to review it.

**Finding 03: AI-human simultaneous co-contribution attribution as a professional accountability question operating before any attribution methodology exists**
C270 Finding 03 identified AI-human simultaneous co-contribution as an attribution problem distinct from reasoning chain reconstruction, establishing that these are different questions. This finding addresses a different dimension: the attribution question is arriving in client conversations before any methodology exists to answer it accurately, and the professional risk of the question arriving before the methodology is different from the professional risk of not having a post-hoc explanation. The MD who answers confidently without an attribution methodology is not merely discharging an obligation inadequately — they are making a representation about their own knowledge of the recommendation that they cannot support. The representation risk is distinct from the explanation risk. A client who concludes that the MD did not know what produced the recommendation has a different basis for challenging the recommendation than a client who concluded the explanation was insufficient. No Geneva market participant has separated the representation risk of answering attribution questions without methodology from the disclosure risk of failing to explain algorithmic logic. This is distinct from C270 Finding 03 in consequence: C270 established the structural distinction between attribution and reasoning chain; this finding identifies the specific professional risk of making attribution representations without a supporting methodology.

**Finding 04: Institutional counterparty AI questionnaire answer inconsistency as cumulative commercial representation exposure with no active management mechanism**
C270 Finding 04 named institutional counterparty questionnaire inconsistency as cumulative undisclosed exposure, establishing that inconsistent answers may constitute commercial representations that contradict each other. This finding addresses a different dimension: the inconsistency is not being actively managed because no management mechanism exists, and the absence of active management is itself the exposure. The typical boutique response to multiple institutional questionnaires is to answer each one independently as it arrives, without cross-questionnaire consistency tracking, without an internal answer standard, and without a protocol for updating prior answers when the AI system changes. The inconsistency accumulates across the client portfolio with each questionnaire. The exposure compounds not because the firm made incorrect answers, but because it made answers it cannot now audit for consistency. No Geneva market service has produced an institutional questionnaire consistency management product that addresses the specific problem of cross-portfolio answer consistency tracking as a discrete service component. This is distinct from C270 Finding 04 in mechanism: C270 established the inconsistency exposure; this finding identifies the specific mechanism of unmanaged accumulation across the client portfolio as the operative exposure.

**Finding 05: MD personal professional liability separation as unnamed accountability infrastructure requirement before the accountability instruments are signed**
C270 Finding 05 identified the absence of personal MD liability separation across the cluster of accountability instruments, establishing that MDs treat all signatures as equivalent risk events. This finding takes a different angle: the separation analysis needs to occur before the instruments are signed, not after a problem surfaces. The current state — where MDs sign accountability instruments without understanding personal exposure under each — means that the accountability architecture is being built around the firm without any analysis of the individual who is personally accountable for it. The personal liability separation analysis is not a retrospective exercise. It is a prospective accountability infrastructure requirement: before signing, the MD needs to know which instruments create personal exposure, what the magnitude of that exposure is, and what coverage responds to it. No Geneva market service has positioned personal MD liability separation as a prospective signing requirement rather than a retrospective exposure review. This is distinct from C270 Finding 05 in timing: C270 identified the absence of separation methodology; this finding identifies the specific timing problem — that the separation analysis needs to precede the signing decision, not follow it.

**Finding 06: nFADP ISSB documentation retention intersection as structurally irreconcilable without a layered retention protocol**
C270 Finding 06 identified the structural conflict between nFADP data minimization and regulatory liability defense documentation retention, naming the three-framework tension across nFADP, FINMA, and ISSB. This finding addresses a more specific intersection: the specific conflict between nFADP minimization obligations and ISSB sustainability documentation retention requirements for the client personal data underlying those reports. The conflict is more acute than the general retention tension because ISSB documentation must be retained to support sustainability disclosures that have been delivered to clients and that clients may rely on. Erasing the underlying client personal data to satisfy nFADP may eliminate the support record for a disclosure the client is entitled to rely on. The nFADP does not provide an exception for ISSB documentation. No Geneva authority has addressed whether ISSB sustainability disclosure support records qualify for any nFADP retention exception, or how to document the conflict resolution in a way that both satisfies ISSB accountability and complies with nFADP minimization. This is distinct from C270 Finding 06 in specific intersection: C270 named the general three-framework retention tension; this finding identifies the specific irreconcilability between nFADP minimization and ISSB documentation retention for the client personal data underlying sustainability disclosures, which is more acute than the general retention tension because the documentation supports client-entitled disclosures with no exception pathway currently published.
