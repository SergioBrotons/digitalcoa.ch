## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Approver-architecture mismatch as primary boutique-scale accountability exposure**
The person who approved the AI system does not know what the system is doing now. The approval-reality divergence is the primary boutique-scale accountability exposure. No market participant has named this specific configuration.

**Signal 2: AI recommendation survival in client files without reconstruction capability**
AI-assisted recommendations survive in client files after the engagement ends. When clients ask about them, the firm cannot reconstruct what the AI independently produced versus what the human evaluated. The reconstruction failure is a present-tense liability.

**Signal 3: Custodian AI model update latency as invisible client report accuracy problem**
Custodian AI model updates happen without notification. Client reports reflecting updated data cannot be traced to the update. The report accuracy problem is invisible to the firm until a client asks a question the firm cannot answer.

**Signal 4: Continuous suitability drift without monitoring architecture**
Approved suitability profiles drift without detection. The AI system continues operating within an approved range that may no longer reflect current market conditions. The absence of monitoring means the firm is operating on outdated assumptions without knowing it.

**Signal 5: Client-side AI capability asymmetry as present-tense credibility exposure**
Clients who use AI can interrogate firm outputs better than the firm can explain them. The asymmetry is present-tense and growing as client AI sophistication increases. The exposure manifests in client conversations, not in compliance checklists.

**Signal 6: Departing staff AI configuration knowledge loss as operational continuity exposure**
The person who configured the AI system departs and takes configuration knowledge with them. The system continues operating without anyone understanding its current behavior. The operational continuity exposure is named for the first time as a discrete boutique-scale problem.

## 03 — Competitive Analysis

**Gap 1: Instance-level attribution records for AI-initiated decisions**
No Geneva market participant has produced a methodology for maintaining instance-level attribution records that can answer which decisions AI initiated versus which humans initiated. The attribution chain is the foundational documentation requirement; no one has built the methodology.

**Gap 2: Post-approval suitability monitoring architecture**
No architecture exists for monitoring whether approved suitability profiles remain adequate over time. The architecture would need to specify what triggers reassessment and how to document that the reassessment occurred. Present-tense exposure with no market solution.

**Gap 3: Post-departure AI configuration reconstruction**
No methodology exists for reconstructing how an AI system is configured after the person who configured it departs. The methodology would need to be achievable at boutique scale without vendor cooperation. The knowledge loss problem is structural.

**Gap 4: Custodian AI update notification protocol**
No protocol exists for managing custodian AI model updates as a client report accuracy event. The protocol would need to address how to trace report changes back to specific updates and how to document the traceability gap when it cannot be closed.

**Gap 5: Client-side AI capability asymmetry response framework**
No framework exists for managing the asymmetry between client-side AI sophistication and firm documentation adequacy. The framework would need to address how to respond when clients can interrogate firm outputs better than the firm can explain them.

**Gap 6: Multi-regulatory obligation intersection methodology**
No methodology exists for managing four non-harmonized regulatory frameworks applying to the same AI system simultaneously. The intersection is the actual operating environment; no Geneva service addresses it as a unified problem.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA suitability obligations for AI-assisted recommendations:**
FINMA requires that suitability assessments be documented and current. AI-assisted suitability recommendations that have drifted without detection create a documentation gap between what was approved and what the system is currently doing. The gap is structural.

**ASIP/BVG occupational pension AI obligations:**
BVG pension fund advisors using AI for retirement recommendations face ASIP documentation requirements. The intersection of occupational pension obligations with AI-assisted advice creates a layered compliance problem that no framework addresses at boutique scale.

**SFAMA fund governance and AI-assisted reporting:**
SFAMA compliance for AI-assisted fund reporting requires documentation of who reviewed AI-generated content. The documentation standard for review adequacy is not defined at boutique scale. The gap between requirement and implementation is unnamed.

**ISSB sustainability disclosure AI assembly documentation:**
ISSB-aligned sustainability reports assembled with AI require documentation of what the AI contributed versus what humans verified. The dual-evidence documentation requirement is active and unnamed at boutique scale.

**nFADP Article 21 derivation documentation:**
Article 21 requires documentation of AI-assisted decision logic. AI systems not designed to retain per-instance reasoning traces cannot discharge this obligation. The gap between obligation and system architecture is structural and present-tense.

**Behavioral profiling consent architecture:**
Behavioral profiling under nFADP Articles 6 and 8 requires consent that is structurally impossible to obtain correctly at boutique scale because per-instance consent is not technically feasible. The obligation cannot be discharged correctly; only approximately.
