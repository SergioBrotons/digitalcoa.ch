## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: Institutional client AI governance questionnaires as secondary regulatory channel bypassing FINMA**
Institutional clients — family offices, pension fund boards, corporate treasuries — are sending AI governance questionnaires to Geneva wealth managers. The questions are specific and methodological. Some institutional clients have dedicated AI governance teams. This channel operates entirely outside FINMA's visibility. The answers provided may carry legal weight in commercial relationships that FINMA-issued documentation does not. No Geneva market service has mapped what this channel is, how answers function in commercial law, or what happens when answers across multiple institutional questionnaires are inconsistent with each other.

**Signal 2: AI reasoning chain as distinct accountability object from recommendation output**
The AI-generated recommendation and the AI-generated reasoning chain are structurally different artifacts. The recommendation is the conclusion. The reasoning chain is the logical path that produced the conclusion. Clients asking "why did the system recommend this?" are asking for the reasoning chain, not the recommendation itself. The nFADP Article 21 explanation obligation requires explaining the logic of the decision, not restating the decision. The FIDLEG adequacy-of-explanation requirement has the same structure. Most Geneva boutiques are disclosing the recommendation without having the reasoning chain as a discrete accountable artifact. The two objects have different accountability profiles and different disclosure requirements.

**Signal 3: nFADP lawful basis evaluation for AI-inferred portfolio insights absent from Geneva boutique practice**
Client portfolio data processed through AI to generate inferences — behavioral profiles, risk propensities, projected needs — requires a lawful basis under nFADP. The legitimate interest basis under Article 6(1)(b) is the most commonly implied basis. But Article 6(1)(b) requires a balancing test: does the firm's interest in processing outweigh the data subject's rights? No Geneva boutique has documented this balancing test for AI inference on client portfolio data. The balancing test is specific to each processing purpose and must be renewed when processing changes. As AI inference use cases multiply within the same client relationship, the balancing test may need reassessment. The absence of this documentation is a present-tense exposure, not a future risk.

**Signal 4: FIDLEG algorithmic routine disclosure obligation is specific and unnamed**
FIDLEG Article 26 requires disclosure when investment advice is based on automated processing. The disclosure obligation is not merely that AI is used. It is that the client must be informed when the recommendation is based on an algorithmic routine. The word "routine" is specific — it means a process that operates systematically, not ad hoc. AI-assisted portfolio construction is the definition of a systematic algorithmic process. The obligation to disclose this as a "routine" — and to explain what that means in accessible language — is distinct from general AI transparency statements. Most Geneva boutiques have not identified this as a discrete disclosure obligation. The absence is not because the obligation is unclear; it is because the obligation is unnamed in market discourse.

**Signal 5: AI-assembled ESG data in ISSB-aligned client reports creates specific misrepresentation liability distinct from portfolio composition disclosure**
When sustainability ratings and climate exposure data are assembled by AI for client reports, the report presents AI-processed estimates as data points. If the underlying sustainability data is of poor quality — which it typically is — the AI assembles poor-quality data into a professional-looking document. The misrepresentation exposure is not about portfolio holdings. It is about the accuracy of the AI assembly process for sustainability data that the firm has presented as ISSB-aligned. This is a different liability category from suitability documentation gaps. The AI is producing the specific content that generates the liability.

**Signal 6: SFAMA AI circular documentation obligations not applied to boutique wealth management advisory practices**
SFAMA published a circular on AI use in asset management. Most Geneva boutique wealth managers have not applied it to their advisory practices on the assumption that the circular applies only to fund managers. The boundary between asset management and advisory in the SFAMA framework may not map cleanly onto how boutiques actually use AI. The documentation standard that SFAMA specified — and how it applies to client advisory AI at boutique scale — has not been translated into operational guidance for the Geneva wealth management segment that most needs it.

## 04 — Compliance Context

**FINMA suitability and the explanation adequacy question:**
FINMA requires that investment advice be based on adequate analysis of the client's profile and that the recommendation be suitable. When AI contributes to the recommendation, the question of what "adequate analysis" means becomes more complex. The explanation that satisfies FINMA's adequacy requirement is not the same as the recommendation itself. The reasoning process that produced the recommendation must be accessible to the reviewing human. AI systems that do not produce retrievable reasoning chains create a structural incompatibility: the human cannot demonstrate adequate analysis of what the system produced if the system does not make its reasoning accessible. FINMA has not specified how firms should document the human review of AI-generated recommendations. The adequacy question remains open.

**FIDLEG Article 26 algorithmic routine disclosure:**
FIDLEG Article 26 requires that clients be informed when investment advice is based on automated processing. The provision uses the term "algorithmic routine." An algorithmic routine is a systematic, rule-based process — the definition of AI-assisted portfolio construction. The disclosure obligation is triggered by the routine's existence, not by a materiality threshold. Geneva boutiques using AI in advisory are subject to this obligation. The content of the disclosure must be in language the client can understand: what the routine does, how it affects the recommendation, what the limitations are. Most boutiques have not identified this as a separate disclosure obligation from general AI transparency language. The obligation exists; the discharge documentation does not.

**ASIP/BVG occupational pension AI advisory obligations:**
ASIP oversees occupational pension fund advisory. When Geneva wealth managers provide AI-assisted retirement recommendations to clients whose assets include BVG pension entitlements, they are operating at the intersection of FINMA advisory obligations and ASIP pension governance requirements. ASIP has not issued guidance on what AI documentation standards apply to BVG-affected advisory. The documentation gap is specific to the BVG layer — the intersection where pension entitlements are affected by AI-assisted recommendations. No framework addresses this at boutique scale.

**SFAMA AI circular and its application to wealth management advisory:**
SFAMA's circular on AI in asset management specifies documentation expectations for AI-assisted investment processes. The circular was written for fund managers; its application to boutique wealth management advisory is not explicit. Wealth managers who use AI in client advisory — constructing portfolios, generating recommendations, producing sustainability reports — are uncertain whether the SFAMA standard applies to them. The documentation standard exists; its applicability to advisory is contested and unnamed.

**ISSB sustainability disclosure and AI-processed ESG data accuracy:**
ISSB-aligned client reports containing AI-assembled sustainability data require that the data be accurate and the assembly process be documented. When AI compiles carbon footprint estimates, transition risk assessments, and portfolio climate exposure from underlying data, the assembly process introduces estimation uncertainty that may not be visible in the report output. The ISSB framework requires accurate disclosure; the AI assembly may produce outputs that look precise but are based on data of uncertain quality. The accuracy of the assembly process is the wealth manager's responsibility, not the AI vendor's. No boutique-scale verification methodology exists.

**nFADP Articles 6 and 21 and AI inference on client portfolio data:**
nFADP Article 6(1)(b) permits processing based on legitimate interest where the data subject's rights do not override the controller's interest. AI inference on client portfolio data — behavioral profiling, risk propensity estimation, needs projection — is processing that requires a lawful basis. The balancing test under Article 6(1)(b) must be documented, purpose-specific, and updated when processing changes. Article 21 requires that clients receive explanation of the logic underlying automated decisions affecting them. AI-generated recommendations that influence portfolio construction are automated decisions. The reasoning chain is what Article 21 requires to be explained. When the AI system does not retain per-instance reasoning traces, the explanation obligation is structurally difficult to discharge. The two obligations — lawful basis for inference, explanation of decision logic — operate simultaneously and require different types of documentation.

Institutional clients — family offices, pension fund boards, corporate treasuries with Geneva wealth management mandates — are sending AI governance questionnaires that function as de facto regulatory instruments through commercial relationships. **Finding 02: AI reasoning chain as a discrete accountability object structurally distinct from the recommendation itself**
The AI-generated recommendation and the AI-generated reasoning chain are separate artifacts with different accountability profiles. The recommendation is what the system concluded. The reasoning chain is the logical process by which the conclusion was reached. nFADP Article 21 requires explaining the logic of automated decisions, not restating the decision. FIDLEG Article 26 requires disclosure of the algorithmic routine's basis and its consequences. Both provisions are describing the reasoning chain, not the output. AI systems designed for recommendation generation are not always designed to preserve per-instance reasoning traces. The reasoning chain may not exist as a retrievable artifact even when the recommendation does. This distinction is structurally unnamed in Geneva market discourse, where "explaining the AI" is treated as synonymous with "explaining the recommendation." This is distinct from:
- C267 Finding 03 (client review conversation attribution decomposition) — which addressed the conversational timing of attribution requests, not the structural distinction between the reasoning chain and the recommendation as two different accountability objects
- C262 Finding 01 (AI attribution chain disaggregation) — which addressed what AI contributed vs. what human contributed, not the specific question of whether the reasoning methodology itself is available as a discrete accountable artifact
- C265 Finding 04 (documentation architecture engineering) — which addressed the architectural problem of reactive documentation accumulation, not the specific property that makes the reasoning chain a different type of object from the recommendation output
*New angle: The reasoning chain is a specific accountability object with its own disclosure requirements under nFADP and FIDLEG. It is structurally different from the recommendation. Most Geneva boutiques are discharging explanation obligations with the wrong artifact — the recommendation — when the law is asking for the reasoning chain.*

**Finding 03: nFADP Article 6(1)(b) legitimate interest balancing test absent from all Geneva boutique AI governance documentation**
Client portfolio data processed through AI to generate behavioral inferences, risk propensities, and projected financial needs constitutes personal data processing under nFADP. The legitimate interest basis under Article 6(1)(b) is the most commonly implied lawful basis for this processing. Article 6(1)(b) requires a documented balancing test: does the firm's legitimate interest in the processing override the data subject's rights and freedoms? The balancing test is purpose-specific and must be reassessed when processing changes. As AI inference use cases accumulate within a client relationship — profiling, needs projection, risk assessment, behavioral prediction — each use case may require its own balancing test. No Geneva boutique has documented this balancing test for any AI inference purpose. The exposure is present-tense, not anticipatory. The lawful basis is implied, not documented. This is distinct from:
- C267 Finding 02 (client portfolio data training pipeline authorization under nFADP Article 9) — which addressed the training pipeline use case, not the inference use case or the Article 6(1)(b) balancing test
- C266 Finding 02 (nFADP Article 9 training pipeline authorization) — which addressed third-party processing authorization, not the legitimate interest balancing test for AI inference on portfolio data
- C259 Finding 06 (UHNWI client-initiated nFADP Article 21 activation) — which addressed Article 21 requests, not the Article 6 lawful basis question that precedes the explanation obligation
*New angle: The Article 6(1)(b) balancing test is a specific documented obligation for AI inference on client portfolio data. The test has not been conducted by any Geneva boutique. The processing is happening; the legal basis is implied. This is not a documentation gap — it is an absence of the legal analysis that would support the basis.*

**Finding 04: FIDLEG Article 26 algorithmic routine disclosure obligation is specifically unnamed as a discrete compliance obligation**
FIDLEG Article 26 requires that clients be informed when investment advice is based on automated processing. The provision specifies disclosure of the algorithm's basis and its consequences for the client. The term "algorithmic routine" is specific: it means a systematic, rule-based process. AI-assisted portfolio construction meets this definition. The disclosure obligation is not satisfied by a general statement that "we use AI." It requires explaining what the routine does, how it affects the recommendation, and what its limitations are — in language the client can understand. This is a distinct obligation from FINMA suitability documentation and from general AI transparency statements. Most Geneva boutiques have not identified Article 26 as a separate disclosure obligation. The obligation exists in the statute; it is absent from Geneva market discourse as a named discrete compliance requirement. This is distinct from:
- C265 Finding 01 (AI decision-initiation taxonomy) — which addressed the taxonomy of AI-initiated vs. human-initiated decisions, not the specific statutory disclosure obligation for algorithmic routines under FIDLEG Article 26
- C264 Finding 01 (approval-reality divergence) — which addressed approval architecture, not the explanation obligation for algorithmic processes under FIDLEG
- C266 Finding 04 (regulatory documentation duality trap) — which addressed the dual function of regulatory documents, not the specific content requirement of Article 26 algorithmic disclosure
*New angle: FIDLEG Article 26 is a specific, operative disclosure obligation with defined content requirements for algorithmic routine disclosure in investment advice. It is unnamed as a discrete compliance obligation in Geneva market discourse. Most boutiques are non-compliant with an obligation they have not identified.*

- C266 Finding 05 (client-facing AI output as reputational infrastructure) — which addressed the reputational system operating independently from governance documentation, not the specific misrepresentation liability of AI-assembled ESG data in formal client reports
- C265 Finding 04 (ISSB sustainability disclosure documentation) — which addressed the ISSB documentation framework generally, not the specific AI assembly accuracy problem for ESG data in client reports
- C254 Finding 02 (BVG/FINMA dual-framework regulatory collision) — which addressed regulatory collision, not the AI assembly accuracy problem for sustainability data
*New angle: AI-compiled ESG data in client reports is a specific content category with its own accuracy liability. The AI is not presenting data; it is assembling data into a representation. When the assembly process produces outputs that look precise but are based on poor-quality underlying data, the firm has presented inaccurate formal disclosures without anyone making a specific decision to be inaccurate.*

**Finding 06: SFAMA AI circular documentation obligations not applied to boutique wealth management advisory creating an unnamed documentation standard gap**
SFAMA published a circular specifying documentation expectations for AI-assisted investment processes in asset management. The circular was written for fund managers. Geneva boutique wealth managers have not applied it to their advisory practices on the assumption that the circular applies only to fund managers. The boundary between asset management and advisory in the SFAMA framework may not map cleanly onto how boutiques actually use AI — constructing portfolios, generating recommendations, and producing sustainability reports for clients. The documentation standard that SFAMA specified has not been translated into operational guidance for the advisory segment. Firms that believe they are not subject to SFAMA's AI documentation standard may be operating with an undocumented governance gap that carrier underwriters and institutional clients are beginning to probe. This is distinct from:
- C265 Finding 01 (AI decision-initiation taxonomy) — which addressed decision taxonomy, not the SFAMA circular applicability question for advisory practices
- C262 Finding 04 (compliance committee AI review comprehension gap) — which addressed comprehension adequacy, not the specific SFAMA documentation standard as applied to advisory AI
- C267 Finding 06 (post-examination response architecture absence) — which addressed the post-examination response gap, not the SFAMA documentation standard as a specific applicable framework that advisory boutiques are not applying
*New angle: SFAMA's AI documentation standard exists and is being ignored by boutique advisory firms on an untested assumption about its scope. The assumption may be wrong. No Geneva market service has examined whether the SFAMA circular applies to boutique wealth management advisory, what it would require if it does, and what the documentation gap looks like for firms operating under the assumption that it does not.*
