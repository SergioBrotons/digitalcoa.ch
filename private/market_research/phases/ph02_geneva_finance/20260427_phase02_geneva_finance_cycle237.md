## 02 — Market Signals

**Signal 1: AI reporting cadence acceleration outpacing compliance documentation cycle**
AI tools enable continuous or weekly client reporting. Compliance review cycles remain quarterly by institutional design. The documentation that exists is built for the compliance review cadence, not the AI reporting cadence. The result is a documentation gap that accumulates silently between review periods — every AI-assisted report produced since the last compliance review is in production without a documentation record that reflects the actual reporting frequency.

**Signal 2: AI portfolio optimization producing professionally-endorsed allocations the advisor cannot independently explain**
When an AI optimization system generates an allocation the portfolio manager would not have chosen independently, the manager faces two separate questions: was the AI's recommendation sound, and was the professional's endorsement sound? These are different questions. The documentation record conflates them. The evidential standard for each is different. No methodology currently exists for maintaining separate evidence trails for the AI recommendation and the professional endorsement.

**Signal 3: FINMA Circular 2016/1 investor segregation obligations applied to AI-generated investment commentary unexamined**
Compliance officers have not determined whether AI-generated market commentary making investment suggestions triggers the same documentation obligations as equivalent human-generated content under FINMA Circular 2016/1. The obligations for investor segregation, suitability documentation, and disclosure are written for human production. The AI case has not been analyzed. This is an active compliance uncertainty, not a future concern.

**Signal 4: Client-supplied AI analysis reconciliation as emerging client expectation**
Clients using their own AI tools are beginning to produce analyses they bring to advisory relationships for reconciliation. The firm receiving client-supplied AI analysis faces a professional endorsement decision with no governing framework: how to respond, what to reconcile, what to endorse, what documentation is required. This is not addressed in any current boutique AI governance framework.

**Signal 5: AI reporting tool data residency and post-engagement data disposition unverified**
The AI reporting tools used by boutique wealth management firms aggregate client portfolio data from custodian feeds. The data processing terms are vendor-standard. Firms have not independently verified where data is processed and stored, what happens to aggregated data during and after the engagement, or whether the vendor's data processing terms satisfy the firm's own nFADP obligations as data processor for client information. This verification gap is silent.

**Signal 6: Third-party AI model integration producing un-owned accountability boundaries**
Boutique wealth management firms increasingly integrate third-party AI models — from custodian APIs, data providers, or embedded analytics — into client-facing workflows. The firm endorses outputs from systems it did not build, whose methodology it did not design, and whose limitations it has not independently evaluated. The accountability boundary between professional judgment and third-party AI model output is structurally undefined at boutique scale.

A documentation architecture designed for the mismatch between continuous AI-assisted reporting and periodic compliance review — establishing what documentation must be produced at what frequency to maintain an evidential record between compliance review dates — does not exist as a discrete boutique-scale advisory product. ## 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Swiss Regulatory Obligations

**FINMA — Circular 2016/1 (Investor Segregation)**
FINMA Circular 2016/1 requires documentation of investment recommendations, suitability analysis, and investor segregation obligations. AI-generated investment commentary that constitutes a suggestion within the meaning of the Circular triggers the same documentation requirements as human-generated content. The analysis of whether AI-generated text constitutes a suggestion has not been published. Compliance officers are operating without this determination.

**FINMA — Supervisory Framework for AI/ML (2023)**
FINMA's existing guidance on AI/ML in Swiss financial institutions acknowledges model risk governance, explainability requirements, and documentation obligations. At boutique wealth management scale (50–200 employees), the practical implementation of these expectations remains without published boutique-specific guidance. The supervisory examinations are expected to intensifying in Q3–Q4 2026, creating a preparation window.

**ASIP — Pension Fund AI Decision Documentation**
Pension funds (Institutions de prévoyance) regulated by ASIP that use AI-assisted analysis for investment decisions face specific documentation obligations for decisions affecting beneficiary benefits. The ASIP guidelines on digital governance require documented decision trails. AI-assisted analysis that contributes to pension investment decisions requires documentation of the AI's contribution that standard pension fund administration systems were not designed to produce.

**SFAMA — Fund Management Documentation Standards**
The Swiss Fund and Asset Management Association standards require documentation of investment process and fund management decisions. AI-assisted portfolio construction that generates allocation decisions requires documentation of the methodology, the inputs, and the professional endorsement — not just the output recommendation. SFAMA members using AI for fund management face these obligations with boutique-scale infrastructure.

**ISSB — Sustainability-Linked AI Disclosure (2026–2027 Pipeline)**
The International Sustainability Standards Board disclosure standards, being integrated into Swiss regulatory frameworks for listed entities and large non-listed firms, create new documentation requirements for AI systems used in sustainability-linked investment analysis. The ISSB standards require disclosure of how AI contributes to sustainability assessments. Boutique wealth management firms advising ISSB-obligated clients face derivative documentation obligations.

**nFADP — Data Processing Accountability for AI Reporting Tools**
The revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection requires that data processors maintain documentation of data processing activities, including data transmitted to third-party AI systems. AI reporting tools that aggregate client portfolio data from custodian feeds and process that data through AI models create specific data processing documentation obligations. Firms have not established whether their AI reporting tools' data processing terms satisfy these obligations independently of vendor attestations. The FDPIC has not published guidance specific to this use case in wealth management.

**Emerging: Federal Council AI Ordinance Pipeline (H2 2026)**
The Federal Council is expected to advance AI-specific regulatory requirements in H2 2026. For boutique wealth management, the relevant question is not whether AI governance obligations will arrive — they will — but what evidence standard the arriving requirements will impose and whether the documentation accumulated now will satisfy it. The formation process direction suggests a trend toward explicit documentation requirements for AI-assisted client communications.

## 06 — Findings Summary

**Finding 1: AI reporting cadence versus compliance documentation cycle mismatch as structural documentation gap**
The most under-addressed problem at boutique wealth management firms is not AI tool deployment — it is the structural mismatch between AI-enabled continuous client reporting and periodic compliance review cycles. The documentation that accumulates between compliance review dates is not being produced at the cadence AI actually operates. This is not a training problem. It is an architecture problem requiring a documentation frequency solution designed for boutique scale.

**Finding 2: Dual-evidence trail methodology as distinct requirement for AI-assisted allocation decisions**
When an AI optimization produces an allocation the portfolio manager would not have independently chosen, the AI's recommendation and the professional's endorsement are two separate decisions with different evidential standards. Conflating them in documentation destroys the evidential value of both. A methodology for maintaining dual, independently auditable evidence trails — preserving each record against the appropriate standard — is a distinct boutique-scale requirement that has not been addressed in advisory offerings.

**Finding 3: FINMA Circular 2016/1 AI investment commentary obligations are present-tense, not anticipated**
The question of whether AI-generated investment commentary triggers the same investor segregation and suitability documentation obligations as equivalent human-generated content is an active compliance uncertainty. It is not a forward-looking concern. Compliance officers are making approval decisions without a documented analysis of what the Circular actually requires for AI-generated content. A written compliance position on this specific question — producible for supervisory examination — is a discrete boutique-scale advisory product.

**Finding 4: Client-supplied AI analysis endorsement as a structurally distinct professional accountability situation**
When a client brings their own AI-generated analysis into an advisory relationship and asks the firm to reconcile or comment on it, the firm is being asked to provide professional endorsement of content it did not produce, using methodology it did not design, with no governing framework for what due diligence is required before that endorsement is given. This is distinct from general AI governance. It is specific to the client-initiated situation and creates a discrete accountability surface with no current boutique-scale framework.

**Finding 5: AI reporting tool data residency verification as nFADP independent obligation unowned by vendor attestations**
Boutique wealth management firms relying on vendor-standard data processing terms for AI reporting tools have not independently verified where client portfolio data is processed, where it is stored, and what happens to it after the engagement ends. Vendor attestations do not discharge the firm's own nFADP obligations as data controller for client information. An independent verification protocol — producing the firm's own documented position rather than relying on vendor disclosures — is a discrete gap at boutique scale.

**Finding 6: Third-party AI model integration accountability boundary as unaddressed boutique-scale exposure**
Boutique wealth management firms integrating AI from custodian APIs, data providers, and embedded analytics into client-facing workflows are endorsing third-party AI outputs without a framework defining what due diligence is required, what limitations must be disclosed, and what documentation must be maintained to sustain the accountability boundary between professional judgment and third-party model contribution. The market has not addressed this gap as a discrete advisory product. The accountability exposure accumulates silently as more third-party AI is embedded in client-facing workflows.
