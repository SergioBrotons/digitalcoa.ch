## Primary Regulatory Frameworks

### 1. Revised Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) — Effective July 1, 2024

**What it does:** The revised FADP establishes Switzerland's updated data protection framework, aligned in direction with GDPR but calibrated to Swiss institutional context. It creates new obligations for data controllers and processors, new rights for data subjects, and new enforcement mechanisms.

**What it means for DigitalCoa.ch's ICPs (all four verticals):**
- **Documentation obligations:** Art.9 requires data controllers to maintain records of processing activities. For AI-assisted work, this creates an obligation to document what AI processing occurs, on what basis, and with what human oversight.
- **Art.20/21 right to explanation:** Data subjects have the right to understand the logic involved in automated decision-making. For finance ICPs using algorithm-assisted recommendations, this creates a documentation obligation that conflicts with proprietary methodology protection.
- **Art.63 personal liability:** Criminal liability provisions apply to responsible individuals — separate from entity-level administrative exposure. This applies across all four verticals.
- **CHF 250,000 ceiling for personal liability:** nFADP Art.63 establishes a specific personal criminal liability ceiling that is consistently under-communicated to the Geneva professional market.
- **DPA requirements:** Revised FADP requires data processing agreements with all AI tool vendors. Most Geneva boutiques have not established these agreements.

**DigitalCoa.ch positioning implications:**
- The nFADP documentation window is active and has been since July 2024
- Personal liability provisions are the primary urgency trigger across all four verticals
- The right to explanation vs. methodology protection conflict is the primary finance-specific complexity
- Evidence architecture design is the direct response to nFADP documentation obligations

**What DigitalCoa.ch does NOT do:** DigitalCoa.ch is not a FADP compliance auditor and does not provide legal opinions on FADP interpretation. The practice provides evidence architecture design — the documentation system that FADP compliance requires, proportionate to boutique scale.

### 2. FINMA Guidance 08/2024 — AI in Financial Services

**What it does:** FINMA published guidance on the use of AI in financial services, with specific expectations around governance, risk management, and disclosure. The guidance applies to FINMA-supervised institutions, which includes some but not all of DigitalCoa.ch's finance vertical ICPs.

**What it means for DigitalCoa.ch's finance ICPs:**
- AI-assisted decisions must be attributable to a responsible human
- Algorithm-assisted recommendations must be documentable
- AI risk management must be proportionate to the institution's exposure
- Client disclosure obligations for AI-assisted advice are increasing

**FINMA personal accountability:** FINMA applies personal accountability standards to managing directors and board members of supervised institutions. This operates independently of nFADP Art.63 and creates a separate personal liability exposure for finance ICPs who are also subject to FINMA supervision.

**DigitalCoa.ch positioning implications:**
- FINMA guidance creates urgency for finance ICPs who are supervised entities
- The institutional DDQ effect (institutional clients asking about AI governance) is downstream of FINMA guidance
- SRO frameworks are now incorporating FINMA direction into their supervised entity expectations
- The personal accountability provisions are not being communicated clearly by any finance market participant

### 3. Geneva Bar Professional Standards — AI Guidance in Development

**What it does:** The Geneva Bar is developing AI guidance for members, expected to converge toward undefined adequacy standards — similar to what carrier forms are already asking. The guidance will create expectations that are effectively binding on Geneva attorneys.

**What it means for DigitalCoa.ch's legal ICPs:**
- "Adequate AI governance" will receive Geneva Bar calibration — currently undefined
- Associate shadow AI use will receive specific attention as a professional responsibility issue
- Client-directed AI restrictions will be addressed from a professional ethics angle
- Geneva Bar guidance will reference nFADP obligations

**Formation circuit participation:** DigitalCoa.ch's participation in Geneva Bar AI working group processes creates intelligence and credibility advantages that are not available to providers who are not in the formation process.

**DigitalCoa.ch positioning implications:**
- Geneva Bar guidance is forming now — the ICP who builds accountability architecture before guidance is final is building proactive evidence
- The timing window before guidance is final is the same window nFADP creates
- Associate AI use governance is the specific legal vertical complexity that Geneva Bar guidance is expected to address

### 4. FMH Professional Standards — AI Oversight in Medical Practice

**What it applies to:** FMH (Swiss Medical Association) standards apply to physicians in independent practice, group practice, and institutional settings. FMH oversight expectations for AI-assisted medical decisions are under active development.

**What it means for DigitalCoa.ch's healthcare ICPs:**
- Human oversight of AI-assisted decisions — principle without operational definition at small practice scale
- Carrier attestation requires physician signature with named personal liability provisions
- Patient-generated AI content entering medical records creates documentation obligations
- AI-assisted second opinion chains create derivative accountability that is not currently governed

**Swissdamed (July 2026):** The Swissdamed medical device registry preparation deadline creates an acute timing pressure specifically for healthcare ICPs. The registry requires documentation of AI-assisted medical devices used in clinical practice. Independent practices that have not documented their AI tool inventory are at acute compliance risk before this deadline.

**DigitalCoa.ch positioning implications:**
- Swissdamed deadline creates the most time-bounded urgency opportunity in any vertical
- Independent practice ICP (2–10 physicians) is the most clearly underserved healthcare segment
- FMH oversight operationalization at boutique scale is a specific service design challenge

### 5. Budapest AI Convention — Ratification in Progress

**What it does:** The Council of Europe AI Convention (Budapest, 2024) establishes a framework for AI accountability across signatory states, including obligations for high-risk AI systems, transparency requirements, and human oversight provisions. Switzerland's Parliament ratification process is active.

**What it means for the Geneva accountability market:**
- Will create new accountability obligations that go beyond nFADP
- Specifically affects legal services, financial services, and healthcare as high-risk sectors
- Implementation will require documentation standards that are not yet defined
- Creates a second regulatory horizon beyond the immediate nFADP window

**DigitalCoa.ch positioning implications:**
- The AI Convention ratification is a specific, time-bounded positioning intelligence opportunity
- Most Geneva professionals have not heard of it — demonstrating awareness signals regulatory timing intelligence
- The current documentation window is partly preparation for what the Convention will require
- The provider who connects nFADP documentation obligations to AI Convention requirements provides a longer regulatory horizon framing that no other Geneva advisor is providing

### 6. SRO Framework Development — AI Governance Standards for Supervised Entities

**What it does:** Self-Regulatory Organizations (SROs) in the finance and insurance sectors are incorporating FINMA AI guidance into their supervised entity expectations. SROs are forming AI governance standards that apply to their member institutions — including boutique institutions that DigitalCoa.ch serves.

**What it means for DigitalCoa.ch's finance ICPs:**
- SRO standards create de facto adequacy standards before regulatory definition arrives
- Institutional DDQ pressure is partly downstream of SRO expectation formation
- The SRO framework development process is opaque to the boutique institutions it affects
- Boutique institutions lack access to the formation process, creating institutional formation exclusion

**DigitalCoa.ch positioning implications:**
- Participation in SRO framework development processes creates intelligence advantage
- The gap between SRO-formed standards and boutique institutional capacity is the specific service opportunity
- The provider who participates in the formation process has credibility that cannot be replicated by external analysis

## The Documentation Window: What It Means Practically

### The Window Is Active Now

The nFADP documentation window has been open since July 1, 2024. The FDPIC enforcement posture has been active since May 2025. The Geneva Bar guidance is forming. The AI Convention ratification is in process. Swissdamed has a July 2026 deadline.

**The practical implication for the ICP:** The professional who builds evidence architecture now is building proactive accountability evidence. The professional who waits for regulatory definition is building reactive compliance. The difference in accountability positioning is material.

### What "Adequate" Means at Boutique Scale

No regulator has defined "adequate AI governance" at boutique scale. The adequacy standard is being formed through:
- Carrier form iterations (each year asking more specific questions)
- Institutional DDQ requirements (converging toward undefined adequacy)
- Professional association guidance (principles without operational definition)
- Peer comparison effects (professionals comparing their responses to what they assume others are doing)

**DigitalCoa.ch's role:** The practice builds a library of what "adequate" means in practice at specific firm configurations — not from regulatory definition, but from observed attestation responses, DDQ requirements, and documentation standards across the client base.

## Compliance Risk for the Practice Itself

### The Practice's Own Regulatory Exposure

DigitalCoa.ch uses AI tools in its own operations. The practice has signed vendor terms, uses AI-assisted research and documentation, and operates in the same Geneva regulatory environment as its ICPs.

**The practice's own obligations:**
- nFADP: Vendor DPAs required for all AI tools used in client work
- Documentation: Evidence of human authorization for AI-assisted deliverables
- Personal liability: Sergio's personal exposure as accountable professional

**The risk:** The practice's own documentation gap is the same as every ICP's documentation gap. If the practice cannot demonstrate its own evidence architecture to the standard it advises ICPs to build, the practice has a credibility exposure that is material to conversion.

**Mitigation priority:** DigitalCoa.ch's own evidence architecture must be complete, documented, and ready to demonstrate. The practice's own documentation is not a lower priority than client work — it is a conversion prerequisite.
