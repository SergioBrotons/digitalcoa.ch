## Cross-Vertical Regulatory Patterns — What Holds Across All Four Sectors

The regulatory environment that affects Geneva professional practices has cross-vertical structural patterns that are more important than vertical-specific regulatory differences. The patterns that hold across all four verticals define the service opportunity more precisely than vertical-specific regulatory analysis.

---

## Pattern 01: The Standard Is Stated Without Being Defined — and the ICP Signs to It

**The cross-vertical structure:** In every vertical, the regulatory or contractual framework states a standard ("adequate" AI governance, "human oversight," "appropriate supervision") without providing an operational definition of what constitutes compliance. The ICP is then asked to sign or attest to meeting that undefined standard.

**Legal:** Carrier attestation form asks the managing partner to commit to "adequate" AI supervision. "Adequate" is not defined by the carrier, the Ordre, or any published guidance. The managing partner signs a personal commitment to a standard that has not been defined.

**Finance:** FINMA Guidance 08/2024 requires "robust governance frameworks" and "sufficient AI expertise" among staff. Neither term is operationally defined. The managing director signs carrier Form v4 attesting to practices the guidance does not specify.

**Consulting:** Client AI governance questionnaires ask about "adequate AI oversight mechanisms." The nFADP requires "appropriate technical and organizational measures" for data processing. Neither has an operational endpoint published for 2-50 person consulting firms.

**Healthcare:** FMH requires "human oversight" of AI-assisted decisions. No Geneva-specific protocol defines what human oversight means in practice for a 2-10 physician office. The physician signs carrier attestation forms referencing FMH standards that are stated without definition.

**The cross-vertical implication:** The ICP is being asked to sign to a standard that has been stated but not defined. This is not unique to any vertical — it is the foundational regulatory structure across all four. The service implication is the same in every vertical: produce evidence that demonstrates a serious, documented approach to AI governance, even where the standard is undefined. The evidence shows that the ICP took the standard seriously — which is what regulators and carriers are actually evaluating.

## Pattern 02: Personal Liability and Carrier Coverage Are Separate Legal Tracks — and the ICP Doesn't Know It

**The cross-vertical structure:** In every vertical, the ICP's personal liability exposure (under professional codes, criminal law, or administrative law) is a separate legal track from their carrier's coverage obligations. The carrier pays settlements; carrier coverage does not discharge personal liability. The ICP believes they are protected by carrier coverage; they are not.

**Legal (Art.321 CC):** Managing partner's personal criminal exposure under Article 321 CC for professional secrecy violations involving AI vendor data processing. Carrier E&O coverage addresses firm-level claims; it does not protect the managing partner's personal criminal liability. The broker's E&O coverage may not extend to explanation obligations on carrier forms.

**Finance (Art.63 nFADP):** Managing director's personal administrative liability under Art.63 for automated decisions that significantly affect clients. Carrier subrogation rights can pursue the managing director's personal assets if the attestation was inaccurate. Carrier coverage and Art.63 personal liability are separate resolution mechanisms.

**Consulting (Art.52 nFADP):** Managing partner's personal administrative liability for data processing violations — fines up to CHF 250,000 for intentional violations. The firm's professional liability insurance may not cover administrative fines imposed on individuals. Personal exposure is direct and not mitigated by firm-level coverage.

**Healthcare (Art.321 CC + Art.63 + FMH):** Physician's personal criminal exposure under Art.321 CC for professional secrecy violations through AI vendor data processing. Physician's personal administrative exposure under Art.63 for automated clinical decisions. FMH professional license implications for AI governance failures. Carrier coverage does not protect professional license standing or personal criminal exposure.

**The cross-vertical implication:** The separation of carrier coverage and personal liability is built into the Swiss legal framework. It applies to every ICP in every vertical. The misunderstanding is structural, not informational — the ICP is not missing information, they are operating under a mental model (coverage = protection) that doesn't match the legal structure. The service that produces personal evidence protection documents the ICP's individual oversight — not the firm's compliance posture.

## Pattern 03: Vendor Contract Terms Define Professional Accountability — and the ICP Has Not Reviewed Them

**The cross-vertical structure:** In every vertical, the ICP accepted vendor terms of service (click-through acceptance at AI tool adoption) that include data processing provisions, sub-processor relationships, model training provisions, and data retention terms. These provisions define the ICP's actual data processing obligations under professional secrecy, data protection, and personal liability frameworks. The ICP has not reviewed these terms against their professional obligations.

**Legal:** Article 321 CC professional secrecy obligations bind the attorney to confidentiality of client information. AI vendor terms accepted without review may include provisions allowing vendor access to, retention of, or use of client-related data for model training. The attorney cannot eliminate these provisions without losing AI tool functionality — creating structurally enforced exposure.

**Finance:** nFADP Art.26 requires documentation of data processing activities. The AI vendor integration chain (portfolio analytics, risk modeling, automated rebalancing, client reporting) creates data flows between multiple vendor platforms. The vendor terms define what each vendor can do with client data. The ICP has not documented this integration chain.

**Consulting:** AI tool vendor terms include provisions about how client information can be used, stored, and shared. Sub-consultant AI tools create additional vendor relationships with terms the managing partner has not reviewed. The managing partner is accountable for client data processing through all vendor relationships in the service chain.

**Healthcare:** AI tool vendor terms include model training provisions — allowing the vendor to use de-identified patient data to improve AI models. The physician cannot eliminate model training provisions without losing AI tool functionality. The structurally enforced choice between operational necessity and professional secrecy/criminal exposure is present in every vertical.

**The cross-vertical implication:** The vendor contract review is not optional — it is the mechanism through which professional accountability is actually defined. The ICP's professional obligations are not just what the professional code says; they are also what the vendor contracts allow. The service that systematically reviews vendor contracts against professional obligations produces documentation that no existing service provides.

## Pattern 04: Multi-Authority Documentation Requirements Are Non-Aligned — and the ICP Has No Mapping

**The cross-vertical structure:** In every vertical, the ICP faces documentation demands from multiple authorities simultaneously — carrier, professional association, supervisory authority, clients, and courts. Each authority requests different documentation types. The documentation architecture that satisfies one authority may not satisfy another. No existing service provides cross-regulator documentation mapping for any vertical.

**Legal:** Carrier attestation (self-reported, aggregate evidence) vs. Ordre disciplinary documentation (actual supervision process) vs. litigation discovery (specific matter AI disclosure) vs. FINMA requests (if financial services involved) vs. FDPIC data processing challenges. Five authority types, five documentation requirements, no mapping.

**Finance:** FINMA supervisory review (governance capability evidence) vs. carrier Form v4 (specific practice attestation) vs. institutional client DDQ (oversight records) vs. Art.63 personal due diligence (individual protection) vs. EU AI Act deployer documentation (if EU nexus). Five authority types, no unified documentation system.

**Consulting:** Client AI governance questionnaires (commercial gate documentation) vs. carrier attestation (insurance condition) vs. Art.52 personal liability documentation (individual protection) vs. EU AI Act GPAI transparency (if EU nexus). Four authority types, no unified framework.

**Healthcare:** FMH human oversight documentation (professional standard) vs. carrier attestation (insurance condition) vs. Art.63 personal evidence (criminal standard) vs. FADP Art.21 + Art.22 obligations (data subject rights) vs. HIN compliance documentation (transport security). Five non-aligned standards, no mapping.

**The cross-vertical implication:** The multi-regulator documentation problem is structural — it arises from Switzerland's sector-specific regulatory approach (no horizontal Swiss AI Act) combined with the EU AI Act's extraterritorial reach. The problem will intensify as Swiss Federal Council AI consultation produces additional obligations. The ICP needs a cross-regulator evidence architecture — documentation that satisfies multiple authorities simultaneously — not separate documentation tracks for each.

## Pattern 05: The Documentation Requirement Arrives Before the ICP Knows How to Meet It

**The cross-vertical structure:** In every vertical, the documentation requirement is created before the ICP has a method for producing the required documentation. The carrier attestation form, client questionnaire, or supervisory guidance creates a documentation obligation — but no Geneva-calibrated methodology exists for how a small professional practice actually produces the required documentation.

**Legal:** Carrier attestation form creates evidence requirements. No Geneva small firm methodology exists for how to produce contemporaneous AI supervision records, vendor contract reviews against Art.321 CC, or cross-regulator documentation architecture.

**Finance:** FINMA Guidance 08/2024 creates governance documentation expectations. No FINMA-calibrated methodology exists for how a boutique wealth management firm produces the specific evidence artifacts (model validation records, oversight logs, integration chain evidence) a supervisory review would require.

**Consulting:** Client AI governance questionnaires create documentation requirements. No consulting boutique-specific methodology exists for producing the AI governance documentation that satisfies commercial gate requirements.

**Healthcare:** FMH human oversight standard creates documentation expectations. No Geneva independent practice-specific methodology exists for operationalizing FMH human oversight or documenting AI contribution to clinical decisions.

**The cross-vertical implication:** The documentation gap is not about the ICP's compliance intent — it is about the absence of a Geneva-calibrated, professional-practice-appropriate method for producing the required documentation. The service that provides this method (not a policy template, not a framework description) is missing from all four verticals. The method must be operationally specific: what to document, when, in what format, using what system.

## Pattern 06: Evidence Production Infrastructure Is Absent — and the Gap Is Structural

**The cross-vertical structure:** In every vertical, the ICP needs evidence production infrastructure — a system that captures contemporaneous documentation of AI governance decisions, vendor relationships, supervision activities, and oversight reviews. No such infrastructure exists in any vertical. The ICP has policies (documents that describe intent) but not evidence systems (systems that capture what actually happened).

**Legal:** The firm has an AI policy on the shelf. It does not have a system for documenting which AI tools were used in which matters, what supervision occurred, what vendor contracts obligate, or what decisions were made at each AI use event.

**Finance:** The firm has an AI governance framework. It does not have a system for documenting continuous AI system operation, oversight reviews, model validation evidence, or vendor integration chain changes.

**Consulting:** The firm has an AI governance approach. It does not have a system for documenting sub-consultant AI use, client AI governance questionnaire responses, or proposal methodology defensibility.

**Healthcare:** The practice has FMH human oversight intentions. It does not have a system for documenting AI tool boundary crossings, patient AI portal outputs entering clinical records, or layered AI interpretation chains.

**The cross-vertical implication:** The evidence production infrastructure gap is structural — it cannot be closed by producing more documents within the existing policy-documentation paradigm. It requires a different architecture: a system designed to capture what actually happened, not what should happen. This is the service opportunity that exists across all four verticals.

## The Cross-Vertical Compliance Service Opportunity

The six regulatory patterns define a single cross-vertical service opportunity:

**Evidence Architecture Design** — building the documentation infrastructure that produces contemporaneous evidence records, not policy documents. The architecture must work across all four verticals (same structural problems) with vertical-specific calibration (different regulatory frameworks).

**Personal Exposure Documentation** — producing evidence that serves the ICP's personal liability protection, not the firm's compliance posture. The evidence package is personal — it documents what the individual did, not what the firm did.

**Vendor Contract Gap Analysis** — systematically reviewing vendor contracts against professional obligations in each vertical. The output is a gap analysis identifying where vendor terms create exposure under Art.321 CC, Art.63, Art.52, or FMH professional standards.

**Cross-Regulator Documentation Mapping** — designing an evidence architecture that satisfies multiple authority demands simultaneously. The ICP builds one evidence system that works across carrier, professional association, supervisory authority, and litigation.

**Documentation Method Development** — providing the operationally specific method for how the ICP actually produces the required documentation in the context of a 2-50 person practice or 2-10 physician office. Not a template — a system.
