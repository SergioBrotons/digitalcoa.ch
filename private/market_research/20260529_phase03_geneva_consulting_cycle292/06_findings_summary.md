## Top 6 NET-NEW Findings (C292 Phase 03)

### Finding 1: AI Vendor Audit Rights as Discrete Confidentiality Obligation Conflict

**Full finding:** Small Geneva consulting firms accepted AI vendor terms through click-through acceptance without evaluating what rights they granted. Vendor terms include broad audit rights — access to prompts, client data, and analytical frameworks — for purposes the firm did not separately evaluate. These rights may conflict with the firm's engagement letter confidentiality provisions. The conflict is structural: engagement letters are binding professional obligations; vendor terms were accepted without review. The firm cannot document that its AI vendor use is consistent with its client confidentiality commitments because it has not evaluated whether it is. No Geneva market service offers a discrete vendor term audit methodology proportionate to boutique consulting scale.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You clicked through the vendor terms. You never read them. What you signed may have granted a US-based AI company access to your client's data, your prompts, and your analytical frameworks. Your engagement letter says you wouldn't do that. You probably don't know whether those two documents are consistent with each other."

**Implication:** Vendor term audit is a discrete service category — evaluating AI vendor audit rights against client confidentiality obligations in engagement letters. Named, scoped, proportionate to boutique scale. First to name this as a service category captures the market positioning.

### Finding 2: Client MSA AI Governance Clauses as Undisclosed Acceptance Pathway

**Full finding:** Geneva consulting firms are accepting AI governance clauses in client master service agreements and scopes of work without recognizing them as AI governance obligations. The clauses — tool disclosure requirements, methodology documentation standards, human oversight verification, AI output audit rights — are being evaluated as contract provisions by legal reviewers unfamiliar with AI-specific obligations. Acceptance creates enforceable commercial representations against which the firm's performance is measured. The firm has contractually committed to AI governance standards it has not evaluated, cannot demonstrate compliance with, and did not knowingly accept as a governance decision. No Geneva protocol exists for identifying, evaluating, and managing AI governance clauses in client contracts at boutique consulting scale.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your client changed their MSA. Added an AI governance section. Your lawyer said it looked standard. But the AI governance section has specific obligations — tool disclosure, methodology documentation, human oversight verification. You're now contractually committed to standards you didn't know you were accepting."

**Implication:** Client contract AI clause review is a discrete service category — identifying AI governance obligations embedded in client contracts before acceptance. Named and scoped. The firm that offers this prevents client-induced accountability surprises and occupies a trusted advisory position in the client relationship.

### Finding 3: AI Convention Deployer Obligations Creating Unnamed Dual-Compliance Track

**Full finding:** The Council of Europe AI Convention creates deployer obligations for Geneva consulting firms with EU client nexus. The Convention is not the EU AI Act — it is a separate instrument with different scope and different obligations. Geneva firms with EU clients are simultaneously subject to AI Convention deployer obligations (transparency, human oversight, accountability documentation) and nFADP obligations (data protection lawfulness, Art.9 processor agreements, Art.21 automated decision obligations). These are two separate accountability tracks, operating simultaneously, enforced by different authorities, with no Geneva consulting-specific dual-compliance mapping methodology. The AI Convention obligation is unnamed in Geneva consulting market discourse. The dual-compliance challenge is unnamed as a discrete service category.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The AI Convention is not the EU AI Act. It's a separate framework. And if you have EU clients, it applies to you. You have Convention obligations and nFADP obligations running at the same time. Nobody has published a Geneva consulting-specific map of what that means at your scale."

**Implication:** AI Convention deployer mapping is a discrete service category — Geneva consulting-specific, EU-nexus boutique-scale. First to produce this mapping captures the positioning in a space where Bern guidance has not arrived and informal circuit intelligence is the only source.

### Finding 4: Non-Traditional Standard-Setting Displacing Boutique Accountability Autonomy

**Full finding:** The Geneva consulting accountability vacuum is being filled by non-traditional actors — AI vendors publishing "compliance guides," carriers issuing AI governance checklists, industry associations releasing self-assessment frameworks — all designed for their own interests, not for Geneva consulting boutique accountability. Firms adopting these frameworks accept someone else's definition of "adequate AI governance" as their operative standard. This creates a structural accountability displacement: the firm's governance is measured against standards set by parties with different interests, without the firm evaluating whether those standards serve its specific accountability position. The displacement is structural because the vacuum is structural — no Geneva consulting-specific body exists to set standards proportionate to boutique scale.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your carrier sent you an AI governance checklist. Your vendor sent you their compliance guide. An industry body published a self-assessment framework. You're following standards designed by parties whose primary interest isn't your accountability protection. Adopting someone else's definition of adequate governance means accepting their accountability framework."

**Implication:** External standard evaluation is a discrete service category — evaluating whether externally-imposed AI governance frameworks serve the firm's specific accountability interest. Different from publishing one's own framework. This is a critical thinking skill that requires independence from all three standard-setting sources (vendors, carriers, industry bodies). The independence is itself the competitive moat.

### Finding 5: AI Tool Deprecation Creating Active Deliverable Provenance Gap

**Full finding:** Major AI providers retire model versions as new ones are released. Geneva consulting firms that produced client deliverables using specific AI model versions in 2024–2025 are discovering those versions no longer exist. The deliverable was produced under conditions that cannot be reproduced. The firm cannot verify or reproduce the exact output it delivered when the client asks how a recommendation was produced. The timestamp documents presentation date, not analytical framework validity under mandate-period conditions — a property Art.398 CO requires for proper performance. This is not a future risk. It is a present-tense property of every AI-assisted deliverable currently in active client use. The problem is structurally invisible: firms do not know they have it until a client asks.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The analysis you delivered six months ago was produced under a model version that no longer exists. Your current tool produces different outputs. When the client asks how you produced that recommendation — the one with their logo on it — you cannot show them what the tool actually did. The timestamp is when you presented it. It doesn't tell you what the tool was thinking."

**Implication:** Deliverable provenance architecture is a discrete service category — tracking AI model version state across active client mandates at boutique scale. Named and scoped. Proportionate methodology for the mandate book, not enterprise IT. Prevents the specific failure mode of being unable to verify or reproduce a deliverable the firm is accountable for.

### Finding 6: Geneva Accountability Formation Velocity Widening Creating Operative Standards Gap

**Full finding:** The density of simultaneous AI accountability formation channels in Geneva — carrier questionnaire iterations, client contract AI clauses, EU regulatory developments (AI Convention, EU AI Act), FDPIC enforcement posture, Geneva institutional circuit exchanges — is creating operative accountability standards faster than published guidance can reflect. The documentation window before standards crystallize is not uniform: firms sustained in the informal Geneva circuit observe standards forming in real time through carrier iterations and institutional exchanges. Firms outside sustained participation build documentation against published standards that have already been superseded in operative form through informal channels. The velocity differential is larger and more consequential than at any prior point because the number of simultaneous formation channels has increased. The firm's documentation may be adequate against what was published and inadequate against what is operative.

**Ready-to-use language:** "By the time anything gets published, the decision has already been made somewhere. The question is whether you were in the room. The carrier's latest questionnaire iteration, the clause your client's lawyer inserted into their MSA, the question that came up at a Geneva institutional exchange — those are where the standards are forming. Not in the published guidance."

**Implication:** Formation velocity intelligence is a discrete advisory function — sustained tracking of how Geneva AI accountability standards are forming through informal channels, separate from monitoring published doctrine. This is the specific value of sustained informal circuit participation: observing the formation process, not just the arrived standard. The advisory function translates from non-publishable formation intelligence to actionable boutique-scale positioning.
