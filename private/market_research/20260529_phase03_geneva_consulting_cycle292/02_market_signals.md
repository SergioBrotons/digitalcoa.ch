## Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

---

### Signal 1: AI Vendor Audit Rights Accepted Without Evaluation

**What's happening:** Small Geneva consulting firms are signing AI vendor terms without reading them. The terms include broad data access and audit rights granted to US-based AI vendors. Firms do not know they have granted these rights. They do not know the scope of access they have authorized. They have not evaluated whether the access they granted is compatible with their confidentiality obligations to clients.

**The failure mode:** The firm uses an AI tool on client data. The vendor's terms grant the vendor the right to access, review, and use that data — including client-specific prompts and analytical frameworks — for purposes the firm did not separately evaluate. The firm's engagement letters contain confidentiality provisions. Those provisions may be inconsistent with what the firm contractually authorized the vendor to do through click-through acceptance.

**Why it's surfacing now:** As carrier attestation and client AI questionnaires ask firms to document their AI vendor relationships, firms are discovering they cannot describe what they authorized because they do not know what they authorized. The vendor terms were accepted as a condition of accessing the tool, not as a legal obligation the firm evaluated.

**ICP language:** *"We signed up for the AI tool. Clicked through the terms. Never read them. Now I wonder what we actually agreed to."*

**Different from prior findings:** All prior vendor term findings addressed data processing authorization (Art.6 nFADP basis) or vendor continuity risk. None addressed vendor audit rights — the specific contractual right to access firm data and prompts — as a confidentiality obligation conflict requiring discrete evaluation against client engagement letter confidentiality provisions.

### Signal 2: Client MSA AI Clauses Accepted Without Recognition

**What's happening:** Geneva consulting firms are receiving amended master service agreements and scopes of work from clients — particularly EU-based clients and multinationals — that include AI governance clauses. The clauses impose specific obligations: AI tool disclosure requirements, methodology documentation standards, human oversight verification, AI output audit rights for the client. Firms are accepting these clauses without recognizing them as AI governance obligations. They are being treated as standard contractual provisions by legal reviewers unfamiliar with AI-specific obligations.

**The failure mode:** The firm signs a contract that includes AI governance requirements it did not evaluate. The requirements may be inconsistent with the firm's actual AI practice. When the client subsequently asks to verify compliance — or when something goes wrong — the firm discovers it contractually committed to standards it did not know it was accepting. The accepted clause creates an enforceable commercial representation against which the firm's performance is measured.

**Why it's surfacing now:** EU-based clients are inserting AI governance clauses as a standard contract amendment. Geneva firms with EU client exposure are encountering these clauses for the first time. The clauses are surfacing as contract review items, not as AI governance items — meaning the firm's decision-maker (the MD) is not evaluating them as AI governance decisions.

**ICP language:** *"The client changed their MSA. Added an AI governance section. Our lawyer said it looked standard. I'm not sure it is."*

**Different from prior findings:** All prior client questionnaire findings addressed AI governance questionnaires as standalone documents. None addressed AI governance obligations embedded in client contracts (MSAs, SOWs) as a discrete acceptance pathway creating undisclosed commercial representations. Different mechanism, different accountability logic.

### Signal 3: AI Convention Deployer Obligations Creating Unnamed Dual-Compliance Track

**What's happening:** The Council of Europe AI Convention (Convention on Artificial Intelligence and Human Rights, Democracy and the Rule of Law) creates deployer obligations that apply to Geneva consulting firms with EU client nexus. The Convention distinguishes between deployers and operators, with deployer obligations focused on transparency, human oversight, and accountability measures. Geneva firms with EU-based clients are unknowingly operating under Convention deployer obligations without awareness or response architecture.

**The failure mode:** The firm has EU clients. The firm uses AI in delivering services to those clients. The firm is a "deployer" under the Convention. The firm has deployer obligations it has not identified, mapped to its practice, or built documentation for. When those obligations are tested — through client audit, carrier inquiry, or regulatory contact — the firm has no position.

**Why it's surfacing now:** The Convention entered into force and Geneva firms are beginning to hear about it. The discussion is happening at enterprise level. Small Geneva boutiques with EU clients are not in the rooms where this is being discussed, but the obligations apply to them. The EU AI Act gets attention; the AI Convention does not — even though the AI Convention obligations may be more immediately applicable to a small Geneva firm with EU clients.

**ICP language:** *"Our biggest client is EU-based. I keep hearing the AI Convention might apply to us. Nobody seems to know."*

**Different from prior findings:** All prior AI Convention findings addressed the Convention as an instrument creating general awareness or future obligations. None isolated the specific deployer obligations for a Geneva consulting boutique with EU nexus as a discrete dual-compliance track (Convention + nFADP) requiring its own mapping methodology at boutique scale. Different mechanism, different compliance architecture required.

### Signal 4: Geneva AI Governance Standard-Setting Displacing to Non-Traditional Players

**What's happening:** The Geneva consulting accountability vacuum is being filled by non-traditional actors. Insurance carriers, AI technology vendors, and industry associations are increasingly publishing AI governance standards, checklists, and certification criteria aimed at consulting firms. These standards are being adopted as operative benchmarks without Geneva consulting input, evaluation, or calibration to boutique scale. Firms are following vendor-designed or carrier-designed governance frameworks without understanding that following those frameworks means accepting someone else's definition of adequate governance.

**The failure mode:** The firm implements a governance framework designed by its AI vendor or carrier. The framework is designed to serve the vendor's or carrier's interests, not the firm's. The firm's adoption of the framework creates an implicit representation that its governance meets the standard the framework describes. The firm has not evaluated whether the framework's standard is appropriate for its specific practice, client mix, or risk profile. The firm's accountability is determined by a standard set by a party with different interests.

**Why it's surfacing now:** Carrier AI attestation forms increasingly reference external frameworks. AI vendors are publishing "compliance guides" for their users. Industry associations are releasing AI governance self-assessment tools. Firms are encountering these as they try to respond to client AI questionnaires and carrier attestations. The response is to adopt someone else's framework without evaluating whether the framework serves the firm's accountability interests.

**ICP language:** *"The carrier sent us their AI governance checklist. We filled it in. I'm not sure the checklist actually covers what we're doing."*

**Different from prior findings:** All prior accountability vacuum findings addressed the absence of Geneva consulting-specific standards. None identified the specific mechanism: non-traditional players (vendors, carriers, industry bodies) filling the vacuum with standards designed for their own interests, and the structural consequences of adopting externally-designed governance frameworks as operative benchmarks without boutique-calibrated evaluation.

### Signal 5: AI Tool Deprecation Creating Active Deliverable Provenance Gap

**What's happening:** Major AI providers (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Microsoft) are retiring older model versions as they release new ones. Geneva consulting firms that produced client deliverables using specific AI model versions in 2024–2025 are discovering those model versions no longer exist or have been substantially updated. The deliverable was produced under conditions that can no longer be reproduced. The firm cannot show what the tool actually did, under what model version state, when the deliverable was produced.

**The failure mode:** A client challenges a recommendation or analysis produced six months ago. The firm needs to explain the methodology and conditions under which it was produced. The AI model version that produced the analysis has been retired. The firm's current version produces different outputs under the same inputs. The firm cannot reproduce or verify the deliverable it sent. The timestamp on the deliverable does not reflect the analytical conditions under which it was produced.

**Why it's surfacing now:** AI model release velocity is accelerating. The gap between version releases is compressing. Firms that adopted AI tools in 2023–2024 are now experiencing the first wave of version retirements affecting their active deliverable portfolios. The problem is present-tense, not future-tense — it affects deliverables currently in client use.

**ICP language:** *"We produced an analysis six months ago. The tool changed since then. Can we still show exactly what we sent?"*

**Different from prior findings:** All prior silent update findings addressed version staleness or analytical drift. None isolated the specific deliverable provenance problem: active client deliverables were produced under model version states that no longer exist, creating an impossibility of reproducing or verifying the exact output the firm delivered. This is not a future risk — it is a present-tense property of the current deliverable portfolio.

### Signal 6: Geneva Consulting Accountability Formation Velocity Differential Widening

**What's happening:** Accountability standards for Geneva consulting firms are forming faster through informal channels than through published doctrine. Carrier questionnaire iterations, EU regulatory developments, client contract AI clauses, and Geneva institutional circuit exchanges are creating operative standards faster than any Geneva consulting-specific guidance can be produced. The documentation window — the period before standards crystallize — is not merely closing; it has different implications at different firm positions. Firms in the informal circuit have intelligence on what is forming. Firms outside it are building documentation against standards that have already arrived in different form through informal channels.

**The failure mode:** The firm builds AI governance documentation based on published guidance or publicly available frameworks. The documentation satisfies the firm's understanding of the standard. The standard that actually governs the firm's accountability was set through informal Geneva channels — carrier questionnaire iterations, peer exchanges, institutional circuit conversations — and the published guidance does not reflect it. The firm's documentation is adequate against the published standard and inadequate against the operative standard.

**Why it's surfacing now:** The density of AI accountability signals in Geneva is increasing. The EU AI Act, the AI Convention, carrier attestation evolution, client contract AI clauses, and FDPIC enforcement posture are all creating accountability signals simultaneously. Firms outside sustained informal participation cannot process the velocity of formation. The gap between what's forming and what's published is wider than ever.

**ICP language:** *"By the time anything gets published, the decision has already been made somewhere. The question is whether you were in the room."*

**Different from prior findings:** All prior formation velocity findings addressed the documentation window close as retrospective reconstruction impossibility. None isolated the specific current-state consequence: the velocity differential means firms building documentation against published standards are potentially building against superseded operative standards, and the gap is larger and more consequential than at any prior point because the density of simultaneous formation channels has increased.
