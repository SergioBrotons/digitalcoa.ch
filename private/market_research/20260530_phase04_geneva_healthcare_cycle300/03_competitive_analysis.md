# 03 — Competitive Analysis: What Exists vs. What's Missing
## Phase 04 C300 | Geneva Healthcare | Independent Physician / Small Group Practice (2–10 physicians)

---

## What Exists in the Geneva Healthcare AI Governance Market

### What Exists in May 2026

**1. Vendor-Coordinated Compliance Notice Infrastructure**
Vendors have built an active communication system for Swissdamed deadline notices. This infrastructure is commercially motivated — the vendor is positioning to retain the client relationship through the compliance interaction. The notices create anxiety and offer resolution through vendor contact. This is a business development system disguised as compliance guidance.

**2. Carrier Attestation Forms (Current Format, Evolved)**
Carrier AI attestation now asks version-specific, audit-trail-demanding questions. This represents market evolution from prior cycles — carriers have updated their instruments in response to market development and claims experience. The forms point toward what adequate documentation infrastructure looks like (even though no small practice has it).

**3. FMH Principle-Level AI Guidance**
FMH has published on AI use in medical practice. The publications are principles-level: human oversight required, physician ultimately responsible, AI tool selection requires physician evaluation. No operational translation exists for independent practice scale. FMH guidance is useful for regulatory defense ("FMH told us to apply oversight") but does not provide actionable Tuesday at 8:30 AM governance methodology.

**4. General Data Protection Compliance Advisory**
General GDPR/FADP data protection advisors exist in the Geneva market. Their focus is data protection law — they can advise on lawful basis documentation, Art.9 processor chain notices, and automated decision obligations. They are not medical practice specialists and do not address the intersection of data protection law with medical professional liability in AI-assisted clinical decisions.

**5. Medical Device Regulatory Consultants**
Medical device regulatory consultants exist who can advise on Swissdamed registration for manufacturers and their products. They are not calibrated for independent practice classification assessment — determining whether a tool used in a specific independent practice configuration qualifies as a Tier 1 or Tier 2 medical device.

**6. EHR Vendor Compliance Guides**
EHR vendors provide compliance documentation for their AI features. These documents explain what the vendor's system does and how it meets regulatory obligations. They do not address how the physician should document their own oversight decisions, their own professional accountability, or their own independent evaluation of AI tool outputs.

### What's Missing (The White Space)

**1. Vendor Contract Exit Governance Service — NEW C300 Category**

What it is: A structured assessment of AI vendor contract terms to determine: data portability rights, AI model training data retention implications, termination provisions and switching costs, ongoing vendor relationship governance mechanism, sub-processor chain disclosure adequacy.

What it addresses: The physician who has discovered that vendor exit is blocked by accumulated contract terms. The practice that wants to implement better AI governance but cannot switch tools without losing patient documentation infrastructure and workflow continuity they have built over 18-24 months.

Why it doesn't exist: This is a new category — the problem only recently surfaced. Many practices have not yet confronted the exit barrier. The market has not yet recognized the service category because vendor exit issues are being discovered in real time, not experienced as known problems. The service requires Geneva legal and AI governance competence simultaneously — a combination rare in the market.

**2. Swissdamed AI Tool Classification Determination for Independent Practice — NEW C300 Category**

What it is: A methodology for answering the specific question an independent practice faces: "Does my specific AI tool, as I use it in my specific practice configuration, qualify as a Tier 1 or Tier 2 medical device under MedDO, requiring Swissdamed registration?" Proportionate to 2-10 physician scale. Not manufacturer-oriented.

What it addresses: Vendor communications are currently generating anxiety about classification that can only be resolved by contacting the vendor — who profits from broad interpretation. The physician needs a neutral determination methodology specifically designed for independent practice operational context, not manufacturer context.

Why it doesn't exist: The classification methodology for this specific use pattern — independent practice configuring vendor-provided AI tools in outpatient clinical workflow — has not been developed because the regulation is new and the market is only now confronting the obligation. A proportionate determination methodology for independent-practice-scale AI tools vs. manufacturer products requires regulatory interpretation combined with independent practice operational knowledge.

**3. nFADP Art.9 Processor Chain Disclosure Verification Service — NEW C300 Category**

What it is: A service that receives AI vendor sub-processor documentation, evaluates the chain for nFADP compliance, identifies the physician's obligations as data controller, and provides a documented assessment of whether vendor disclosures satisfy regulatory requirements.

What it addresses: The physician who discovers they are accountable for a vendor sub-processor chain they have never evaluated. The service intercepts the choice between accepting comfortable ignorance and requesting vendor disclosure (which the vendor may not provide in useful form).

Why it doesn't exist: No Geneva-calibrated service exists because the problem is only recently surfaced as a named category. Most practices have not yet discovered the Art.9 processor chain. The service is ahead of widespread market awareness — but early movers calling on practices now will encounter the problem in acute phase.

**4. Proportionate AI Governance Architecture for Independent Practice (Named Service Category) — NEW C300 Positioning**

What it is: A documented, repeatable governance system designed for 2-10 physician scale. Addresses practice-level AI surface documentation, multi-physician coordination, version tracking, update awareness, and evidence standards proportionate to carrier attestation requirements. Does not require compliance infrastructure the practice doesn't have.

What it addresses: All vendor-provided documentation and general governance frameworks are designed for hospital or manufacturer context. The independent practice has the same accountability exposure without the institutional infrastructure to satisfy enterprise-designed frameworks. The "proportionate framework" category established in prior cycles is now a named, specific service — the actual deliverable is a governance architecture appropriate to scale.

Why it doesn't exist: Most Geneva advisors treat AI governance as an abstract discipline. The translation to independent practice operational reality — meaning what does governance look like for a 3-physician practice without dedicated IT staff, compliance officer, or legal department — requires advisory specialization in independent practice healthcare that does not currently exist in the Geneva market.

**5. AI Tool Evaluation for Independent Practice (Switzerland-Specific) — NEW C300 Category**

What it is: A structured evaluation framework helping the physician assess AI tools against Swiss-specific obligations before adoption: nFADP compliance assessment (not just GDPR), Art.321 CP extension analysis, Swissdamed classification determination, FMH oversight concept mapping to tool function, carrier attestation fitness evaluation.

What it addresses: The physician selecting an AI tool for their practice needs a pre-adoption evaluation that no existing service provides. The evaluation framework prevents the problem being discovered after adoption — when vendor dependencies have already accumulated.

Why it doesn't exist: The service requires simultaneous competence in Swiss regulatory law, medical professional liability, and independent practice operational context. No Geneva firm has positioned this as a specific offering because the market has not yet developed sustained demand. The Swissdamed deadline is now generating the demand signal.

**6. Carrier Attestation Pre-Signing Review for Medical Practices — NEW C300 Category**

What it is: A medical-specific analogue to the pre-signing carrier review service that exists in the finance sector. Evaluates carrier AI attestation from physician's professional liability perspective before signature. Identifies what the physician is certifying to. Assesses gap between attestation standard and practice's current AI governance state. Documents the pre-signing review as evidence of due diligence if challenged.

What it addresses: The physician receiving carrier attestation with a deadline, unsure what the attestation actually requires, unable to produce adequate documentation before the deadline, and signing under time pressure without knowing what they certified.

Why it doesn't exist: Medical practice carrier attestation market is 6-12 months behind finance/legal sector in development. The pre-signing review service has not yet been developed for the medical practice segment. The market need is present-tense — the service is not yet in market.

## Competitive Positioning Assessment

### Who Could Fill These Gaps

**EHR vendors:** Will not provide independent practice governance framework — creates liability exposure for vendor's own system
**General management consultants:** Not Geneva-calibrated for medical professional accountability framing
**Medical device regulatory consultants:** Calibrated for manufacturers, not outpatient practice operations
**FMH/professional associations:** Publish principle-level guidance without operational implementation pathway
**Swiss law firms:** Data protection expertise, not independently-scaled medical practice operations expertise
**Carriers:** Will not provide pre-signing review — underwriting function conflict prevents neutral advisory positioning

### Structural Moat

The Geneva clinical AI accountability service space has a structural moat created by the information economy: operationally relevant intelligence (how carrier assessors actually evaluate attestation, how FDPIC actually enforces nFADP against medical practices, how FMH human oversight is actually interpreted by canton authorities) is formed through sustained informal circuit participation — not derivable from published regulatory texts alone.

This moat is more durable than analytical framework replication because the pattern knowledge cannot be produced without participation in the specific formation mechanisms: carrier review meetings, canton medical society discussions, informal circuits among Geneva healthcare advisors.

The service provider who participates in these circuits consistently develops and maintains an information advantage that cannot be replicated by published methodology alone.

### Service Segment Positioning

The white space identified in C300 represents a service market that is:
- Not yet recognized by established Geneva advisory firms
- Not addressable by enterprise-designed solutions
- Accessible only to advisors with specific independent practice operational and Swiss regulatory competence
- Structured around specific events (carrier attestation, Swissdamed deadline) with high market visibility as forcing functions

The combination of high market visibility (Swissdamed deadline 5 weeks away, carrier attestation in market) and absence of established solution creates first-mover positioning advantage for the advisor who names the service categories clearly and can deliver them at independent practice scale.
