## Phase 04 C300 | Geneva Healthcare | Independent Physician / Small Group Practice (2–10 physicians)

## Current Problems Surfacing in Geneva Healthcare Right Now

### Signal 1: Vendor-Coordinated Swissdamed Notice Campaign as New Market Behavior

**What's happening (now, May 2026):** AI tool vendors are sending proactive communications to Geneva independent practices about Swissdamed registration obligations. These communications arrive now — approximately 5 weeks before the July 1, 2026 deadline — in a coordinated pattern. The communications are designed to position the vendor as the solution to the compliance problem they are simultaneously creating anxiety about.

**The mechanism:** Vendor creates the compliance question, then offers to answer it. "Your AI diagnostic module may require Swissdamed registration. Contact us for guidance." This is commercial strategy disguised as compliance notification. The vendor has commercial interest in broad classification (creates dependency) and is not a neutral classification authority.

**Market signal:** Practices report receiving near-identical communications from multiple vendors simultaneously. The pattern suggests vendor coordination or shared professional guidance to the same market segment. The physician receives the notice, cannot independently evaluate the classification question, and has no Geneva authority to consult for a proportionate determination.

**Failure mode:** Physician accepts vendor's classification determination as final. The vendor's interest and the physician's interest in proportionate compliance are not aligned. The physician's registration obligation is determined by a party who profits from broad interpretation.

**Distinct from prior cycles:** C214–C296 named Swissdamed deadline as a general obligation. C300 names the specific vendor-coordinated notice campaign as a market behavior — the commercial infrastructure actively creating and monetizing compliance anxiety rather than resolving it.

### Signal 2: Carrier Attestation Questions Becoming Version-Specific and Audit-Trail-Demanding

**What's happening:** Carrier attestation forms are evolving from generic "do you use AI" questions to version-specific queries. Forms now ask about specific AI tool versions deployed, documentation of AI system updates, and evidence that the practice has an update-tracking mechanism. Carriers are beginning to ask questions that require affirmative documentation infrastructure that most practices do not have.

**Market signal:** Practices receiving renewal attestations in May/June 2026 report the questions have changed from prior cycles. The attestation now requires evidence of governance infrastructure the physician does not have. The physician reads the questions and understands, for the first time, what the attestation actually requires — and realizes they cannot provide it.

**Failure mode:** The practice scrambles to produce retroactive documentation. Vendor audit logs, configuration records, and version history exist — but the practice has no process for accessing them, interpreting them, or presenting them to a carrier. The gap between what the carrier asks for and what the practice can produce is now visible and produces paralysis rather than action.

**Distinct from prior cycles:** C214–C296 named carrier attestation as a general pressure. C300 names the specific evolution of attestation format from generic to version-specific, and isolates the resulting documentation failure as a specific market signal — the practice can now see exactly what they cannot produce.

### Signal 3: Vendor Contract Exit Barriers Revealed Under Swissdamed Anxiety

**What's happening:** As Swissdamed anxiety increases and carrier attestation requirements become more specific, practices that want to change AI vendors discover they cannot. Vendor contracts contain data portability limitations, AI model training data retention clauses, and termination provisions that make exit operationally complex or commercially punitive. The "switching cost" of AI tools is being discovered retroactively at the moment when switching is most urgently needed.

**Market signal:** Practices attempting to evaluate alternative AI tools discover that their current vendor's contract creates exit barriers. Data accumulated in the vendor system (patient documentation for AI training, historical records, configuration defaults) cannot be easily transferred. The practice that wants to "govern AI better" by selecting a different tool is structurally prevented from doing so by contract terms they accepted in click-through agreements.

**The physician's experience:** "I didn't know I was signing that." The contract was presented as standard terms acceptance during initial setup. The implications — that they would be unable to exit the vendor relationship without losing accumulated patient documentation and AI-assisted workflow — became visible only when the physician first tried to evaluate alternative governance options.

**Failure mode:** The practices that understand their vendor exit constraints are the practices with highest governance anxiety. They know they are in an ungovernable vendor relationship and cannot exit it. This produces a specific form of paralysis: the physician knows what they should do structurally (evaluate alternative tools, establish exit capability) and cannot do it because of accumulated vendor dependency.

**Distinct from prior cycles:** C214–C296 named shadow AI ungoverned stack. C300 names the specific vendor contract exit barrier mechanism preventing governance improvement through vendor switching — a structural moat added to governance impossibility.

### Signal 4: nFADP Processor Chain Discovery as Acute Exposure Discovery

**What's happening:** Practices are beginning to understand that the click-through vendor contract they accepted when activating their AI tool created a processor chain under nFADP Art.9. The practice, as data controller, has obligations to Document, assess, and take responsibility for vendor sub-processors. Most practices have never evaluated their vendor's sub-processor list. Most does not know the sub-processor list exists as a compliance obligation.

**Market signal:** Practices receiving carrier attestation or Swissdamed notices are beginning to ask whether their AI vendor's data flows create nFADP exposure. The question is new, specific, and produces immediate anxiety: the physician understands that patient health data is processed by sub-processors they have never evaluated, in jurisdictions they haven't considered, under terms they haven't read.

**Failure mode:** The physician who discovers the Art.9 processor chain faces an impossible choice: request vendor disclosure of sub-processors (revealing they didn't know, potentially triggering carrier attention), accept the chain as unknown (comfortable ignorance), or attempt to audit it (no proportionate methodology exists). Most choose comfortable ignorance until an event forces disclosure.

**Distinct from prior cycles:** C214–C296 named nFADP compliance gaps for commercial AI tools generally. C300 names the specific mechanism of processor chain discovery — physician discovers sub-processor obligations only at the moment they begin taking governance seriously, and finds the vendor has not provided accessible disclosure.

### Signal 5: Retroactive Documentation Impossibility as Named Crisis

**What's happening:** Practices attempting to respond to carrier attestations or prepare for Swissdamed classification review are attempting to build retroactive documentation of AI governance decisions that were never made. The physician approaches the task as if documentation can be constructed after the fact: "I need to document what we decided about AI tool X." The problem is that for tools activated 18-24 months ago through software updates they didn't request, no decision event occurred. There is nothing to document.

**Market signal:** The physician attempting retroactive documentation confronts the documentation paradox: governance decisions that were never made cannot be documented retroactively. The attempt to build documentation from nothing produces either fraudulent documentation (fabricating decision events that didn't occur) or honest blank pages (no decision to document). The physician experiences this as a crisis — they understand at this moment that their AI governance has been architectural, not procedural, and that architecture cannot be retrofitted.

**Failure mode:** The physician escalates to external counsel expecting a solution — discover that external counsel cannot help. No documentation methodology exists for a governance architecture that doesn't exist. The lawyer can advise on forward-looking governance structure; they cannot create retroactive documentation that corresponds to actual decisions that didn't occur.

**Distinct from prior cycles:** C214–C296 named documentation void as a governance gap. C300 names the specific crisis of retroactive documentation impossibility — the physician understands that the gap is not a documentation failure they can close retroactively, but an architectural gap that requires forward-looking structural resolution.

### Signal 6: Peer-Recommended Professional Isolation as Information Economy Artifact

**What's happening:** The Geneva independent physician's primary information source for AI governance questions is peer network. During an acute event (carrier attestation arrival, Swissdamed notice), the physician calls a colleague who has received the same document. The colleague cannot help because they are in the same situation. The exchange produces shared anxiety but no actionable information. The physician concludes they are further behind than they thought because the colleague has no解决方案.

**Market signal:** The information economy of Geneva independent practice AI governance is producing systematic failure: peers can confirm that the problem exists, but cannot provide actionable governance intelligence because they have none. The routing of information-seeking to peer circuit means the physician is receiving confirmations of the problem rather than solutions. This is particularly acute because the physician who calls a colleague is often the physician most actively trying to respond — their action orientation meets information poverty.

**Failure mode:** The most governance-aware physicians are the most isolated in their peer circuit because they recognize the problem more clearly and therefore are further from solutions than they realized. The physician who calls a colleague for guidance discovers that the colleague has the same questions with fewer answers. The exchange produces no forward progress.

**Distinct from prior cycles:** C214–C296 named professional isolation as a market condition. C300 names the specific information economy mechanism that produces peer-confirmed paralysis rather than peer-enabled solution-sharing, and identifies this as a structural information market failure, not a documentation problem.
