**Source:** web_search (PwC CH, ethics-compliance.ch, Europa.eu)
**Angle:** Not covered in C124-C127 Geneva Legal phases.

The EU Data Act (Regulation 2023/2854) became applicable September 12, 2025, with a critical B2B data sharing milestone hitting September 12, 2026: manufacturers of connected products must design new products to allow users direct access to data generated by those products. Swiss companies operating in or serving the EU market face extraterritorial obligations: fair/non-discriminatory B2B contract terms for data sharing, user access rights to product-generated data, and third-party data sharing obligations — covering both personal and non-personal data.

Geneva law firms advising Swiss manufacturers (precision instruments, medtech, industrial equipment with EU sales) and B2B service providers face a new advisory frontier: EU Data Act compliance as a distinct service line covering contract review, product design legal review, and data sharing dispute resolution. This is distinct from FADP (personal data) and GDPR (EU-based) — the EU Data Act is an independent compliance obligation with its own enforcement logic. Geneva as a hub for precision manufacturing and industrial tech companies makes this particularly relevant to the local legal market.

### Finding 2 — Swiss Climate Litigation Firsts: Holcim Zug Ruling + KIG/KIV Effective January 2025

**Angle:** Not covered in C124-C127 (C127 mentioned "ESG arbitration wave rising" but not Swiss domestic climate litigation milestones).

A landmark development: on December 17, 2025, the Cantonal Court of Zug declared admissible a private climate lawsuit against Holcim AG — a first for Swiss civil courts. The ruling confirms Swiss courts will hear private climate-related claims including worldwide subsidiary activities, creating direct precedent for director liability for failing to anticipate climate harm.

The Climate and Innovation Act (KIG) and its Ordinance (KIV) entered force January 1, 2025, creating legally binding Swiss climate targets and requiring companies seeking financial support to develop transition plans. The financial industry is mandated to align financial flows with climate objectives.

Direct board liability under Article 964 OR (effective July 20, 2025): board members approving sustainability reports face personal fines up to CHF 100,000 for false statements, CHF 50,000 for negligence. The Responsible Business Initiative (launched January 2025) is pushing for even stricter rules with a counterproposal expected from the Federal Council in early 2026.

Geneva law firms advising boards, listed companies, and financial institutions now face a new class of clients: directors seeking ESG liability protection, companies defending climate claims, and financial institutions navigating KIG-aligned lending and investment obligations.

### Finding 3 — Swiss Verbandsklage Reform Rejected: Only Injunctive Relief Available Under Art. 89 SCCP
**Source:** web_search (Swiss Holdings, Chambers Practice Guide, Charles Russell Speechlys)
**Angle:** Not covered in any recent cycle; explicitly clarifies a commonly misunderstood Swiss legal landscape point.

Switzerland has definitively rejected introducing a broad Verbandsklage (class action) mechanism for damages. Both the Council of States and the Legal Committee of the National Council have rejected comprehensive class action bills, citing abuse risk, court overburden, and cultural objections. As of January 1, 2026 (when other procedural amendments took effect), no general class action for damages exists in Swiss law.

The only collective mechanism available is Article 89 of the Swiss Code of Civil Procedure (SCCP/ZPO), which allows associations of national/regional importance to bring actions for injunctive relief, removal, or declaratory judgments — but NOT damages or monetary satisfaction for a group.

This has significant practical implications for Geneva litigation strategy: consumer protection, investor protection, and mass harm situations cannot use class action mechanisms in Switzerland. Plaintiffs must use party joinder (Art. 70-71 SCCP) or individual actions. Geneva courts handle these differently from class actions — more complex, higher litigation costs per claimant.

For Geneva law firms, this creates: (1) advice to international clients seeking class action recovery against Swiss defendants — these proceed differently; (2) strategic advice to Swiss companies as defendants — class action exposure is limited; (3) investor protection association actions under Art. 89 — a narrow but real mechanism; (4) comparison with EU class action regimes for cross-border litigation planning.

**Advisory opportunity:** Swiss class action defense (companies), cross-border EU vs. Swiss litigation strategy, Art. 89 association action guidance.

### Finding 5 — Switzerland's NIS2-Adjacent Critical Infrastructure Regime: Emerging Legal Obligations for Financial Sector
**Source:** Domain knowledge — distinct from DORA coverage in C126
**Angle:** NIS2-equivalent cyber resilience obligations not covered in C124-C127 Geneva Legal phases.

While Switzerland has no formal NIS2 implementation (NIS2 is an EU Directive), Switzerland's Critical Infrastructure Ordinance (CYIO) and sector-specific regulations create overlapping cyber resilience obligations for financial institutions. Geneva banks and asset managers with EU cross-border operations may face NIS2-equivalent expectations from counterparties and EU regulators.

The Federal Office for Civil Protection (FOCP) oversees CYIO implementation. Recent amendments have expanded covered sectors. Financial sector critical infrastructure (SIFEM, systemically important financial institutions) faces heightened operational resilience requirements that go beyond FINMA's standard supervisory expectations.

Geneva financial law practices advising banks, insurance companies, and asset managers need to address: (1) CYIO compliance for Swiss-domiciled critical infrastructure; (2) NIS2-equivalent contractual requirements from EU counterparties; (3) incident reporting obligations across Swiss and EU regimes; (4) cyber resilience governance for financial sector clients.

This is distinct from the DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act) discussion in C126 — DORA is EU law with extraterritorial effect, while CYIO is Swiss domestic law. Both create advisory demand but through different legal instruments.

**Advisory opportunity:** CYIO compliance for Geneva financial institutions, NIS2 contractual requirements advisory, cyber incident response legal framework.

### Finding 6 — Legal Privilege Standards in Swiss Internal Investigations: Financial Institution Exposure
**Source:** Domain knowledge — not previously a dedicated finding in Geneva Legal phases
**Angle:** Internal investigation privilege is distinct from AML/sanctions coverage in earlier phases.

Swiss law does not recognize attorney-client privilege in internal investigations the way US/UK law does. When Swiss financial institutions (Geneva banks, asset managers) conduct internal investigations — whether prompted by FINMA, by suspicious activity concerns, or by board-level requests — the scope of protection for investigation findings is legally ambiguous.

Key issues: (1) Work product of internal investigations may be discoverable by FINMA in supervisory proceedings; (2) Voluntary disclosure of investigation findings to authorities may be treated as waiver of privilege in related proceedings; (3) External counsel involvement does not automatically create privilege under Swiss law; (4) Cross-border internal investigations (US DOJ, UK FCA, Swiss FINMA simultaneously) create complex privilege management challenges.

Geneva financial institutions facing simultaneous multi-jurisdictional regulatory scrutiny need legal advisory on: structuring internal investigations to maximize privilege protection under Swiss law; coordinating privilege strategy across US/UK/CH jurisdictions; managing the tension between voluntary cooperation credit and privilege preservation; protecting board-level investigation findings.

This is distinct from the AML/sanctions enforcement coverage in earlier cycles — it's about the procedural and evidentiary framework for how investigations are conducted, not the substance of what they uncover.

**Advisory opportunity:** Internal investigation structuring for privilege protection, cross-border multi-jurisdictional investigation coordination, FINMA investigation response advisory.
