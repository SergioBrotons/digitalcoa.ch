## Finding 01: PK/LDI De-Risking Advisory — SNB Easing + OAK Interest Rate Risk Guidance

The SNB's March 2026 rate cut to 0.25% and the revised OAK (Oesterliche Kontrollbank) interest rate risk guidance have triggered renewed pension fund (PK/KG) de-risking advisory demand in Geneva. PK funds with duration mismatches between assets and liabilities face ALM recalibration requirements under BVG Article 44. The SNB's shift to an easing bias creates a new interest rate environment where previous liability-driven investment (LDI) strategies face inversion risk: if SNB cuts further or if EUR/CHF parity concerns drive CHF appreciation, PK assets priced in EUR or USD generate negative currency-adjusted returns. Geneva institutional advisors are fielding inquiries from PK commissions on hedging strategies using interest rate swaps, swaptions, and CHF-denominated liability-matching instruments. The OAK guidance specifically flags the NAV -8-12% YoY compression in Swiss real estate funds as a concern for PK real estate allocations, recommending reduced exposure to open-ended Swiss real estate funds pending valuation normalization. The advisory window for PK de-risking is acute as the spring 2026 BVG reform transition period begins.

## Finding 02: Swiss Insurance Solvency II-Equivalence Tension — Reinsurance Market Access

Swiss (re)insurers face a structural tension between Solvency II-equivalent Swiss supervision and the EU's evolving Solvency II Review (SIIR) framework. The EU's Solvency II Review directive adopted in 2025 introduces enhanced group supervision provisions that may conflict with Swiss existing equivalence arrangements under the EU-Swiss bilateral agreements. Swiss reinsurers — particularly Geneva-based Swiss Re and specialty reinsurers — rely on EU market access for reinsurance capacity deployment. The equivalence determination is subject to annual EC review, and the SIIR's new provisions on group-level Model Parameter adjustments may trigger an EU re-evaluation of Swiss equivalence. Geneva reinsurance brokers and advisors are advising clients to prepare contingency plans for non-equivalence scenarios, including alternative collateral structures under Swiss law (ART. 14 Versicherungsaufsichtsgesetz) and reinsurance trust structures governed by New York law for US cedant business.

## Finding 03: BVG Reform Tokenized Securities Allocation — New Investment Category

The Federal Council's BVG reform dispatch introduces tokenized securities as a new permitted investment category for occupational pension funds (PK/KG), effective for the 2027 transition period. The amendment to BVV 2 ( ordinance on occupational retirement, survivors' and disability pension funds) adds "tokenized debt instruments and structured products issued on distributed ledger technology infrastructure" as a permitted asset class, subject to risk weightings to be determined by the OAK. Geneva asset managers with PK mandates are preparing tokenized securities issuance platforms and DLT-based custody solutions to offer PK clients new investment options. The tokenized category initially focuses on CHF-denominated structured products and infrastructure debt instruments, given the regulatory alignment with FinSA and the DLT Act framework. PK funds with existing alternative investment allocations may rebalance into tokenized formats for improved liquidity and transparency. The advisory opportunity spans custody setup, investment policy amendment drafting, and OAK reporting framework design.

## Finding 04: FINMA Climate Stress Testing Gap — Expectations vs. Practice

FINMA's 2025 guidance on climate stress testing for supervised institutions (FINMA Circular 2025/1) set expectations that Geneva-based banks, insurance companies, and asset managers conduct entity-level climate stress tests using scenario analysis aligned with NGFS (Network for Greening the Financial System) scenarios. However, the 2026 FINMA examination cycle reveals a significant gap between supervisory expectations and actual practice: fewer than 30% of supervised institutions have completed full climate stress tests using credible scenario methodologies. Most institutions are conducting qualitative assessments or using simplified sensitivity analysis that does not meet FINMA's stated expectations for quantitative scenario integration. Geneva asset managers with significant Swiss equity holdings face the sharpest gap, as FINMA has signaled interest in the transition risk exposure of equity portfolios to Swiss corporate climate liability regimes. The climate stress testing gap creates an examination risk for institutions with large supervised balance sheets, and the advisory opportunity for climate scenario modeling consultants is substantial.

## Finding 05: CHF/HKD-SGD Cross-Border Wealth Compliance — Geneva APAC Expansion

Geneva private banks and wealth managers expanding into Hong Kong and Singapore markets face a complex cross-border compliance environment involving SECA (Swiss Bankers Association) self-regulation, FINMA circular requirements, and APAC local regulations. The HKMA (Hong Kong Monetary Authority) and MAS (Monetary Authority of Singapore) have both intensified cross-border wealth compliance expectations, including beneficial ownership transparency requirements and Automatic Exchange of Information (AEOI-CRS) implementation for Swiss financial institutions with APAC client facing operations. Geneva banks with APAC expansion strategies — particularly those targeting mainland Chinese UHNW clients with Hong Kong family office structures — need advisory support on HK/SG regulatory requirements, CRS reporting obligations, and the intersection with Swiss domestic AML requirements under GwG Article 14. The advisory opportunity includes compliance framework design, staff training on APAC regulatory nuances, and coordination with local legal counsel in HK and SG jurisdictions.

## Finding 06: FINMA Bail-in/Write-down Capital Instrument Advisory — TBTF 2.0

FINMA's TBTF (Too-Big-To-Fail) 2.0 framework, operationalized through amendments to the Bank insolvency ordinance (BankIV-FINMA), establishes detailed requirements for bail-in and write-down capital instruments for Swiss systemic banks. The framework requires Swiss G-SIBs (UBS, CS successor entity, and any future designated institutions) to maintain minimum levels of outstanding write-down capital instruments and bail-in bonds meeting specific criteria under Swiss resolution law. Geneva advisory boutiques are advising non-systemic banks, insurance companies, and institutional investors on their holdings of write-down capital instruments issued by Swiss systemic banks, including stress testing of bail-in scenarios, valuation adjustments for write-down risk, and portfolio rebalancing decisions. The TBTF 2.0 framework also creates advisory work for corporate treasuries holding Swiss bank debt, as the bail-in instrument framework affects the risk profile of bank deposits and senior debt held by corporate treasury functions.
