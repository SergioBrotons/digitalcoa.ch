# Lead Magnet: "The Small Real Estate Agency AI Governance Question"
## Phase 06 — Geneva Real Estate — Cycle 42

---

## Positioning
For Geneva small/mid-market real estate agencies and independent agents who are already using AI tools for listings, tenant screening, property valuations, and contract drafting — but have no formal governance framework, and have been in violation of FADP Art. 5(c) since September 2023 (2.5+ years). The FADP has been applicable to AI since September 2023. Most RE agencies have been using AI tools without knowing they were creating FADP obligations.

**Primary urgency lever (Cycle 42):** 75% AI adoption = the HIGHEST of any vertical in the portfolio. The governance gap is ~47 points (75% actual vs. 28% formal adoption). This is the widest governance gap in the entire portfolio. The RE agency market is the most underserved and the most at-risk simultaneously.

**Secondary urgency lever:** FADP Art. 5(c) has been operative since September 2023 — 2.5+ years of violations accumulated. No grace period available. The unique entry point for RE: "govern the AI you already use" — because they are already using AI, they just need to formalize it.

---

## ICP Entry Points

### Decision 0: Shadow AI Audit (FADP Retroactive Entry)
Identify every AI tool currently used by the agency: listing description generators, property valuation tools, tenant screening platforms, contract drafting tools, client communication AI. Classify by data sensitivity (tenant PII, property data, financial information).

**Why this works:** 75% of RE agencies are using AI. The Shadow AI audit surfaces what is already in use. The FADP obligations have been running since September 2023 — the audit creates awareness of accumulated exposure without creating panic.

### Decision 1: FADP Retroactive Compliance Assessment
For each AI tool identified: assess whether FADP Art. 5(c) (transparency — disclose AI use to data subjects), Art. 21 (right to human review for automated decisions), and Art. 16 (data source disclosure) are satisfied. For RE agencies: tenant screening AI likely requires Art. 21 human review; property valuation AI likely requires DPIA.

**Why this works:** FADP has been operative since September 2023. Most RE agencies do not know they have been creating FADP obligations for 2.5+ years. The retroactive compliance assessment identifies accumulated exposure and creates urgency to close gaps now.

### Decision 2: Tenant Screening AI Human Review Protocol
For AI tools used in tenant selection: document whether a human reviews AI-generated tenant assessments before a decision is made. FADP Art. 21 requires the right to human review for automated decisions producing legal effects. Tenant rejection = likely legal effect.

**Why this works:** Tenant screening AI is the most likely RE AI use case to qualify as automated decision-making under FADP Art. 21. Most RE agencies do not know this. Implementing a human review protocol is a concrete, low-cost action that eliminates the Art. 21 violation.

### Decision 3: Property Valuation AI DPIA Assessment
For AI tools used for property valuations: assess whether a DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment) is required under FADP Art. 22. Property valuation AI likely qualifies as high-risk AI processing given the financial and legal implications.

**Why this works:** DPIA for property valuation AI is a specific, actionable obligation that most RE agencies are not aware of. The DPIA assessment creates a clear next step without requiring the agency to complete a full DPIA immediately.

### Decision 4: Data Residency Compliance Check
Verify that all AI tools used by the agency process data on Swiss or EU servers. US-based AI tools create CLOUD Act exposure — US authorities can demand data from US providers regardless of where data is stored. Property data, tenant data, landlord data stored on US servers = FADP + professional confidentiality violations.

**Why this works:** Data residency is a concrete, verifiable requirement. RE agencies using ChatGPT, Claude, or other US-based AI tools without knowing about CLOUD Act exposure are currently absorbing unknown risk.

### Decision 5: GAIGI Institutional Gap Documentation
Document the agency's need for RE-specific AI governance guidance. GAIGI (gaigi.ch) is institutional-only — no public RE AI guidance is available. The agency should document this gap as part of its FADP accountability framework (Art. 5(c) requires transparency about AI use and limitations).

**Why this works:** GAIGI's institutional-only stance is a structural gap that RE agencies can use to demonstrate good faith — they sought guidance but none was available. This is particularly relevant for the Federal Council end-2026 legislation, which may clarify RE-specific obligations.

## Compliance Stack (FADP 2.5+ Years Operative — No Grace Period)
1. **Art. 5(c):** Transparency — disclose AI use to landlords, tenants, buyers, sellers
2. **Art. 21:** Right to human review for AI-assisted tenant screening decisions
3. **Art. 16:** Data source disclosure for AI processing
4. **Art. 22:** DPIA required for high-risk AI processing (property valuations likely qualify)
5. **Privacy by Design:** AI procurement policy for new AI tool adoption
