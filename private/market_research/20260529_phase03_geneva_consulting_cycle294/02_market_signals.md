# Market Signals: Geneva Consulting — C294 Phase 03

## Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

### Primary Market Signals

**1. Client AI DDQ Engagement Blocking**
Consulting boutiques are declining client engagements because they cannot respond to AI governance questionnaires. This is a revenue and relationship problem — the firm loses the engagement, the client goes elsewhere, and the firm's inability to respond is noted in the client's records.

Failure mode: The MD receives a DDQ with 40+ questions about AI governance, realizes they have no documentation, and either spends an uncompensated week trying to piece together a response or declines the engagement. The uncompensated week is economically irrational. Declining the engagement damages the client relationship. Neither option is acceptable but both are happening.

**2. Proposal AI Methodology Defensibility**
Consultants who recommend AI tools or methodologies to clients — as part of a consulting engagement or as part of a proposed solution — have no documentation architecture to defend those recommendations. The recommendation is made on judgment, the output is produced by AI, and the liability for the recommendation falls on the consultant regardless of whether AI was used to inform it.

Failure mode: Client suffers a loss attributed to the AI-assisted recommendation. The consultant cannot demonstrate the methodology used, the data inputs, the model limitations considered, or the human judgment applied. The recommendation looked good; the process behind it cannot be reconstructed.

**3. Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Ungoverned**
Independent consultants sub-delegate work to sub-consultants who use AI tools. The primary consultant presents the sub-consultant's output as their own to the client. The primary consultant is accountable for the output regardless of how it was produced. No protocol exists for managing, documenting, or supervising sub-consultant AI use.

Failure mode: Sub-consultant uses an AI tool that generates a recommendation based on flawed data or model limitations. The recommendation enters the primary consultant's work product. The client relies on it. The primary consultant cannot reconstruct what the sub-consultant's AI did or why. The accountability chain is unbroken but the documentation chain is nonexistent.

**4. nFADP CHF 250,000 Personal Liability Unnamed**
Personal liability for data protection violations applies to individuals, not just firms. The CHF 250,000 ceiling is personal exposure. Small consulting boutique operators — who often sign client contracts, engagement letters, and vendor agreements personally — are not aware this applies to them.

Failure mode: A data incident occurs (AI tool data breach, unauthorized processing, client data sent to wrong model). The consultant signed the engagement letter, the vendor agreement, or the AI tool's terms of service. The fine is personal. The consultant's general liability insurance does not cover it. The consultant's personal assets are exposed.

**5. Insurance Market Pricing AI Risk**
Insurance brokers are beginning to ask about AI usage at proposal and renewal. Firms without documented AI governance architecture are being quoted higher premiums or declined. This is happening before any standardized AI governance framework exists for small consulting boutiques.

Failure mode: At renewal, the broker asks specific questions about AI tools used, AI governance documentation, and AI-related incidents. The MD cannot answer most of the questions. The insurer notes the absence of documentation. The premium increases or the firm is declined with no specific explanation.

**6. Productivity Adoption Without Governance**
AI tools were adopted for productivity reasons — faster turnaround, competitive pressure, client expectations, handling more work without hiring. The adoption happened before any governance evaluation. The tools are now embedded in client work. The governance gap is structural, not temporary.

Failure mode: The MD realizes that AI tools are being used across the firm in ways they did not evaluate, cannot document, and cannot fully supervise. The exposure is already present. The question is not whether to adopt AI — it is how to govern what has already been adopted.

- Sub-consultant AI delegation recognized as discrete problem — distinct from associate shadow AI in legal vertical

**Consistent with C293:**

- No boutique-scale methodology exists
- Budget authorization paradox (quantifiable investment, invisible ROI)
- The triggering event pattern (retrospective confirmation)

### The Geneva Consulting Boutique Market Size Signal

Geneva has a significant population of 2–50 person consulting boutiques across:
- Management consulting (strategy, operations, digital transformation)
- Financial consulting (M&A advisory, valuation, financial restructuring)
- Legal consulting (alternative to traditional law firm, specialized expertise)
- Technical consulting (IT, engineering, environmental)
- Human capital consulting (HR, organizational design, training)

These firms share the same accountability structure: the MD or partner signs everything personally, has no internal compliance infrastructure, and is personally exposed to everything the firm does.
