# Findings Summary: Geneva Consulting — C294 Phase 03

## 6 NET-NEW Findings: Distinct from C214–C293

---

### Finding 01 — Client AI DDQ Engagement Blocking

**The finding:** Client AI governance questionnaires are now a measurable engagement blocker for Geneva consulting boutiques. Firms are declining specific client engagements because they cannot respond to the AI DDQ. This is not a hypothetical future problem — firms are reporting specific lost engagements with specific clients over specific AI governance questions.

**Mechanism:** Larger clients (institutional, corporate, international) have begun including AI governance questionnaires in their standard engagement due diligence. The questionnaire asks about the consulting firm's AI governance framework, tool inventory, documentation processes, and human oversight mechanisms. The boutique has none of this documentation. The choices are: uncompensated week building documentation after the fact, or declining the engagement.

**Implication:** The client DDQ has become a sales qualification mechanism. Firms with documentation win the engagement. Firms without documentation lose it. This is not about compliance — it is about commercial access.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your client's AI DDQ is a sales qualification question. If you can't answer it, you don't get the engagement."

### Finding 02 — Proposal AI Methodology Defensibility Gap

**The finding:** Consulting firms that recommend AI tools or methodologies to clients — as part of a consulting engagement — have no documentation architecture to defend those recommendations. The recommendation is made on professional judgment; the output is produced by AI. The liability for the recommendation falls on the consultant regardless of the AI involvement.

**Mechanism:** The consultant evaluates an AI tool, forms a judgment that it is appropriate for the client's problem, recommends it, and the client acts on it. If the AI-assisted recommendation fails, the consultant cannot reconstruct the methodology: what data was used, what limitations were considered, what human judgment was applied, why the specific tool was selected over alternatives. The recommendation looked good; the process cannot be documented.

**Implication:** This is a discrete service component — proposal AI methodology documentation — that no provider offers at boutique scale. The consultant needs a documentation architecture that is built before the question is asked, not reconstructed after a problem occurs.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You're recommending AI tools to clients and you're accountable for those recommendations. If you can't show how you made the recommendation, you're exposed."

### Finding 03 — Sub-Consultant AI Delegation as Discrete Unmanaged Exposure

**The finding:** Sub-consultant AI delegation is a specific, named problem distinct from the associate shadow AI problem in legal firms. The primary consultant sub-delegates work to independent consultants who use AI tools. The primary consultant presents the sub-consultant's AI-assisted output to the client as their own. The primary consultant is accountable for the output regardless of how it was produced. No Geneva-specific protocol exists for managing, documenting, or supervising sub-consultant AI use.

**Mechanism:** The accountability chain is unbroken even though the production chain is fragmented. The client hired the primary consultant. The primary consultant hired the sub-consultant. The AI was used by the sub-consultant. If the output is wrong, the client holds the primary consultant responsible. The primary consultant cannot reconstruct what the sub-consultant's AI did or why.

**Implication:** The sub-delegation protocol is a specific, nameable service component: which sub-consultants use AI, what tools, what outputs are generated, how they are reviewed, how they are incorporated. This is not covered by any existing framework — ISO 42001, vendor compliance guides, and professional standards do not address project-based sub-delegation of AI-assisted work.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The accountability for AI-assisted work runs through whoever signed the client contract. Your sub-consultant's AI is your AI exposure."

### Finding 04 — nFADP CHF 250,000 Personal Liability Ceiling Under-Communicated

**The finding:** The nFADP CHF 250,000 personal liability ceiling applies to individuals, not just entities. Small consulting boutique operators — who sign engagement letters, vendor agreements, and AI tool terms of service personally — have personal exposure they have not named, quantified, or protected against. This is under-communicated to this specific ICP.

**Mechanism:** nFADP Art.63 creates criminal liability for unauthorized personal data processing. The liability ceiling applies to individuals. The consultant who signed the engagement letter (taking on data processing obligations to the client) and the vendor agreement (taking on obligations to the AI tool provider) may have personal exposure under both. General liability insurance does not cover nFADP criminal liability. Personal assets are exposed.

**Implication:** This is a personal risk management conversation, not a firm-level compliance conversation. The individual needs to know what they personally signed, what it committed them to personally, and what architecture protects their personal exposure.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The CHF 250,000 fine is personal. Your general liability doesn't cover it. Your firm's liability doesn't protect your personal assets."

### Finding 05 — Insurance Market AI Governance as Structural Sales Barrier

**The finding:** Insurance brokers are actively pricing AI governance risk at proposal and renewal. Firms without documented AI governance architecture are being quoted higher premiums or declined outright. This is happening before any standardized framework exists for small consulting boutiques — creating a market pressure with no proportionate solution available.

**Mechanism:** The insurance broker asks: what AI tools does the firm use, does the firm have documented AI governance, have there been any AI-related incidents, what is the firm's process for evaluating AI tool vendors. The boutique cannot answer most of these questions. The broker notes the absence of documentation. The premium reflects the unknown risk. Or the firm is declined with no specific explanation.

**Implication:** Insurance underwriting criteria are becoming a de facto market standard for AI governance. The firm that can present documented AI governance at renewal will be treated differently from the firm that cannot. This is a present-tense competitive differentiator, not a future compliance requirement.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your insurance broker is making judgments about your AI risk based on documentation you don't have. The question is whether you want to be the firm with documentation or without it."

---

### Finding 06 — No Boutique-Scale AI Governance Methodology Exists for Geneva Consulting

**The finding:** All existing AI governance frameworks are designed for large organizations with compliance infrastructure. No provider has produced a methodology specifically adapted to the Geneva consulting boutique — 2–50 employees, project-based client work, sub-consultant delegation, personal liability exposure, and client DDQ requirements — that is proportionate to the firm's actual scale and risk profile.

**Mechanism:** The market gap is specific and structural. The boutique needs:
- Client AI DDQ response architecture (not enterprise GRC)
- Proposal AI methodology documentation (not vendor assessment)
- Sub-consultant delegation protocol (not ISO 42001)
- Personal liability protection framework (not entity-level compliance)
- Insurance underwriting documentation (not regulatory compliance)

No provider offers this as an integrated package at boutique scale.

**Implication:** This is a first-mover position. The provider who names the specific methodology for Geneva consulting boutiques — and produces it in a form that is proportionate, practical, and directly applicable — will own this market.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Everything that exists for AI governance was designed for someone else. You're a consulting boutique. What you need is something designed for your size."
