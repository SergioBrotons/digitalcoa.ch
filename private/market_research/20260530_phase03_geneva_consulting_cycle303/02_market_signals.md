# 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Complaints, Failures

## Phase 03 — C303 | Geneva Consulting (2–50 employees)

---

## 1. Engagement Letter AI Clauses — The Contractual Blind Spot

**Signal:** Consulting engagement letters were drafted before AI existed. The standard engagement letter is silent on what AI tools may be used, what AI-generated content may be delivered, what the consultant warrants about AI outputs, and what liability attaches to AI-assisted recommendations. This silence was benign in 2019. In 2026 it creates a contractual accountability vacuum.

**What the ICP reports:**
- *"My engagement letter was drafted in 2018. It doesn't mention AI once."*
- *"I don't know what my engagement letter says about AI use — I didn't write it and I haven't reviewed it since AI became relevant."*
- *"If the client challenges an AI-generated recommendation, what does my contract actually cover?"*
- *"I assume the engagement letter protects me. I'm not sure it covers AI-generated deliverables."*
- *"When I updated my terms for GDPR, I didn't think about AI. Should I have?"*

**Failure mode:** The ICP discovers the contractual gap when a client challenges an AI-generated deliverable. At that point, the engagement letter provides no clear framework — for the ICP or the client. The uncertainty creates a credibility problem, a liability problem, and a negotiation problem simultaneously.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva consulting boutiques serve sophisticated clients — banks, commodity trading firms, pharmaceutical companies, family offices — who have their own AI governance frameworks. These clients will read the engagement letter carefully. The absence of AI language signals either evasion or ignorance. Neither is acceptable from a Geneva-level advisor.

---

## 2. Working Papers AI Documentation — The Internal Evidence Gap

**Signal:** Consulting working papers are the professional evidence standard — they document what analysis was conducted, what data was used, what methodology was applied, and how conclusions were reached. AI-assisted analysis has entered working papers without any equivalent documentation standard. The working papers gap is internal, structural, and feeds every external accountability problem.

**What the ICP reports:**
- *"We have a working papers standard for human analysis. I don't know what the equivalent is for AI-assisted analysis."*
- *"The junior consultant ran the analysis through ChatGPT. The output is in the working paper. What else should be there?"*
- *"I can reconstruct what a human analyst did. I cannot reconstruct what the AI tool did with the same input."*
- *"When I review working papers, I see AI outputs. I don't see the prompt, the dataset scope, or the exclusion criteria."*
- *"The working paper is supposed to be the evidence trail. AI has no standard evidence trail."*

**Failure mode:** The ICP cannot reconstruct AI-assisted analysis from working papers. When clients ask what role AI played, when carriers request evidence of supervision, when regulators ask how conclusions were reached, the working papers provide no answer. The gap is structural — it was never designed to capture AI-mediated analysis.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva consulting boutiques compete on intellectual credibility. The working paper is where intellectual credibility is demonstrated. If working papers cannot document AI's role in analysis, the firm's intellectual credibility cannot be established for AI-mediated work.

---

## 3. AI Review vs. AI Generation — The Methodology Attribution Problem

**Signal:** "AI-assisted" covers two fundamentally different methodological situations with different accountability profiles. AI review (human generates, AI reviews and challenges) keeps intellectual authority with the consultant. AI generation (AI generates the analysis, human approves) transfers intellectual authority to the AI. Clients want to know which is which. The ICP currently treats these as the same disclosure.

**What the ICP reports:**
- *"There's a big difference between AI checking my work and AI doing the work. I don't know how to document the difference."*
- *"When I say 'AI-assisted' in a proposal, the client doesn't know if I mean AI reviewed it or AI wrote it."*
- *"If the recommendation was AI-generated, did I actually make it? Is that a professional problem?"*
- *"The client asked me directly: did you or the AI make this recommendation? I didn't know how to answer."*
- *"At what point does AI assistance become AI delegation — and does it matter professionally?"*

**Failure mode:** The ICP either over-discloses (sounds like they outsourced judgment to AI) or under-discloses (creates liability when the truth emerges). Neither position protects the firm's credibility or the managing director's accountability. The ambiguity is itself a problem — sophisticated clients are starting to ask the question directly.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva consulting boutiques are hired for their judgment. If the market perceives that judgment has been outsourced to AI tools, the value proposition shifts fundamentally. The methodology attribution problem is not just a documentation problem — it goes to the core of what a Geneva consulting boutique sells.

---

## 4. EU AI Act High-Risk Classification — The Geneva Boutique Exposure

**Signal:** The EU AI Act classifies certain AI uses as high-risk, triggering conformity assessments, technical documentation requirements, human oversight obligations, and transparency duties. Geneva consulting firms serving clients in banking, insurance, and healthcare may themselves be operating under EU AI Act obligations — without knowing it.

**What the ICP reports:**
- *"My clients are Swiss banks and insurers. Some have EU operations. Does EU AI Act apply to what I do for them?"*
- *"I advise on AI strategy for a healthcare client. Is my consulting work subject to EU AI Act?"*
- *"Conformity assessment — is that something a 12-person firm can realistically do?"*
- *"The EU AI Act high-risk definition is so broad I can't tell if it covers my practice."*
- *"Nobody in Geneva has told me whether EU AI Act applies to a Swiss consulting boutique."*
- *"My client is regulated by FINMA. My client is regulated by EIOPA. Does my work for them make me part of their regulatory chain?"*

**Failure mode:** The ICP continues operating as if EU AI Act does not apply, without having made a formal determination. If it does apply and the ICP is non-compliant, the exposure is regulatory — fines, market access issues, client contract breaches. Geneva boutiques serving EU-regulated industries face a specific determination question that has no Geneva-calibrated answer.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva's concentration of banking, insurance, commodity trading, and pharmaceutical clients means a large proportion of Geneva consulting boutiques serve EU-regulated industries. The EU AI Act applies not just to EU-based firms — it applies to firms deploying AI systems that affect EU residents. The question of whether a Geneva consulting firm's AI-assisted client work triggers EU AI Act obligations is live, unanswered in the Geneva market, and creating genuine anxiety.

## 5. Multi-Framework Accountability Stack — The Managing Director Accumulation Problem

**Signal:** Small Geneva consulting boutiques have a single managing director or partner who is simultaneously named as the "responsible person" under nFADP Article 63, the signatory on carrier attestation forms, the respondent to client AI governance questionnaires, and potentially the accountable person under EU AI Act obligations. No Geneva service has named this accumulation as a specific structural problem.

**What the ICP reports:**
- *"I'm named in nFADP as responsible person. I'm the signatory on the carrier attestation. I'm the one who responds to client AI questionnaires. It's the same person — me."*
- *"Each framework names me personally. None of them tell me how to manage them as a package."*
- *"My carrier attestation and my client questionnaire response — are these the same documentation? They feel like the same problem."*
- *"I have documentation requirements from four frameworks. I have no system to manage them together."*
- *"The frameworks don't know about each other. But they all name me."*
- *"Nobody's quantified what the accountability stack means for a managing director of a 12-person firm."*

**Failure mode:** The ICP manages each framework separately, creating inconsistent documentation, duplicative effort, and the risk that documentation produced for one framework contradicts another. The managing director is structurally responsible for all of them but has no methodology for managing them as an integrated accountability system.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva's professional culture concentrates accountability in named individuals. The managing director of a Geneva boutique is not insulated by a compliance department or a legal team. The accountability stack hits a single person harder in a 12-person firm than it would in a 200-person firm — because there is no division of labor, no dedicated compliance function, and no institutional buffer between the individual and the obligation.

## 6. AI Knowledge-Authority Inversion at Partnership Level

**Signal:** The senior partners and managing directors who bear personal accountability for AI decisions have less AI fluency than the junior staff who actually use AI tools daily. This inversion creates a structural documentation gap — the person who can document AI use does not have the accountability, and the person who has accountability does not have the AI knowledge.

**What the ICP reports:**
- *"I don't know what AI tools my team is using. I approved the budget — I don't know what they actually do with them."*
- *"The junior consultant uses tools I can't evaluate. I review their work. I can't review the AI that produced it."*
- *"I was trained as a consultant. I wasn't trained as an AI auditor. But I'm the one accountable."*
- *"Partners ask me what AI we're using. I give them a list I compiled from memory — I'm not sure it's accurate."*
- *"The person with the most AI knowledge in my firm is my youngest consultant. The person with the most accountability is me."*

**Failure mode:** The managing director signs documents attesting to AI governance practices they cannot accurately describe. The documentation system depends on junior staff reporting their own AI use accurately — which requires both honesty and AI fluency that the junior staff may not know the managing director needs. The gap compounds silently until a triggering event surfaces it.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva consulting boutiques compete on intellectual authority and personal judgment. The knowledge-authority inversion threatens both. The senior partner's authority rests on demonstrated judgment. If that judgment is actually being made by AI tools the senior partner cannot explain, the firm's value proposition is undocumented and potentially misrepresented.

---

## 7. Client Due Diligence AI Obligations — The Consultant-as-Vendor Problem

**Signal:** Geneva consulting boutiques serving institutional clients (banks, public sector, family offices, commodity trading firms) are increasingly being treated as vendors subject to the client's own AI governance requirements. The client's AI governance questionnaire is not a compliance exercise — it is the client's due diligence on their own regulatory exposure. The consulting boutique is part of the client's evidence chain.

**What the ICP reports:**
- *"My client's compliance team is asking about my AI tools. They're not asking for curiosity — they're building their own evidence file."*
- *"My client is regulated. Their regulator asks them about their vendors' AI governance. They ask me."*
- *"I received a vendor AI governance questionnaire from a bank client. They're clearly under regulatory pressure to document their supply chain."*
- *"The client's questions are more specific than I expected. They want documentation, not policies."*

**Failure mode:** The ICP responds to vendor questionnaires as if they are compliance exercises rather than evidence transfers. The client is building a regulatory defense. The ICP provides policy documents. The client is not protected. The ICP does not know the documentation they provided is being used in a regulatory context.
