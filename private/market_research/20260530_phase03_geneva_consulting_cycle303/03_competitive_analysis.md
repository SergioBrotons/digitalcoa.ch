# 03 — Competitive Analysis: What Exists in Market vs. What's Missing

## Phase 03 — C303 | Geneva Consulting (2–50 employees)

---

## 1. What Exists in the Geneva Consulting AI Governance Market

### A. General AI Governance Frameworks (Available, But Not for Geneva Boutiques)

**What exists:**
- ISO/IEC 42001 (AI Management System standard) — enterprise-scale, certification-oriented, not Geneva boutique-calibrated
- NIST AI Risk Management Framework — U.S. federal government origin, not Swiss-specific, enterprise-oriented
- EU AI Act compliance guidance — broad, EU-enterprise-oriented, not consulting-boutique-specific
- Various AI governance toolkits from Big Four and consulting firms — scaled for large deployments, not small boutique operations

**Gap:** All existing frameworks assume organizational infrastructure that a 12-person Geneva boutique does not have. No framework addresses what "AI governance documentation" means at the scale where the managing director is personally named as responsible person, personally signs the carrier attestation, and personally responds to client questionnaires.

### B. Professional Association Communications (Acknowledgment Without Solution)

**What exists:**
- ODAGE communications acknowledging AI governance as a risk for Geneva professional services firms
- Swiss Bar Association (SBA) discussions of AI accountability at professional events
- FINMA communications on AI governance for supervised institutions (not for consultants who serve them)
- Various Geneva industry events acknowledging the problem — panels, roundtables, working groups

**Gap:** Acknowledgment of the problem is widespread. Specific, operational, Geneva-boutique-calibrated solutions are not available from professional associations. The gap between acknowledgment and actionable guidance is a market opportunity.

### C. Carrier/Insurance AI Governance Requirements (Exist, Create Demand)

**What exists:**
- Professional liability insurance carriers requiring AI governance documentation as part of renewal
- Carrier Form v4 requiring evidence of authorization records, not attestations
- Carrier risk assessment questionnaires asking about AI tool inventory and oversight practices

**Gap:** Carriers define what they require (evidence, records, documentation) without providing the methodology for producing it. The demand-side of the carrier requirement is clear. The supply side — a Geneva-boutique-specific evidence production methodology — is not served by carriers.

### D. EU AI Act Compliance Consultants (Exist, Wrong Scale)

**What exists:**
- EU-based AI governance consultancies offering conformity assessments and compliance programs
- EU AI Act technical documentation services
- Various compliance software platforms for EU AI Act (Large Language Model registries, conformity assessment tooling)

**Gap:** EU AI Act compliance services are designed for EU-based enterprises deploying high-risk AI systems at scale. They are not designed for Swiss consulting boutiques that may be incidentally affected by EU AI Act obligations through their client relationships. No Geneva-calibrated EU AI Act determination service exists at boutique scale.

### E. Data Protection Officers and nFADP Advisors (Exist, Different Focus)

**What exists:**
- Swiss DPO services addressing nFADP compliance
- Data protection law firms and consultants in Geneva
- Privacy compliance tooling for nFADP

**Gap:** nFADP-focused services address personal data protection — data processing, consent, data subject rights, cross-border transfers. They do not address the AI governance accountability questions specific to consulting deliverables (working papers, proposal methodology, engagement letter AI clauses, sub-consultant delegation). The nFADP services serve a related but distinct need.

## 2. What Is Missing from the Market

### A. Engagement Letter AI Clause Methodology

**What's missing:** A specific service that reviews or drafts engagement letter provisions addressing AI use — disclosure obligations, warranty language, liability allocation, intellectual property ownership of AI-generated deliverables, client notification requirements, and sub-consultant AI use provisions.

**Why it matters:** The engagement letter is the foundational contract. It defines the entire accountability relationship between the consulting firm and the client. No Geneva market service offers engagement letter AI clause review or drafting at boutique consulting scale.

**ICP willingness to pay:** High — the engagement letter is concrete, the ICP understands it, and the liability exposure from an inadequate engagement letter is clear.

### B. Working Papers AI Documentation Protocol

**What's missing:** A specific documentation standard for AI-assisted consulting working papers — what elements must be captured (prompt, dataset scope, exclusion criteria, AI output, human review notes, authorization), in what format, for which types of AI use.

**Why it matters:** Working papers are the internal evidence system that underlies both client deliverables and regulatory/carier evidence requests. A working papers AI documentation standard is foundational infrastructure — it feeds the engagement letter obligations, the client questionnaire responses, and the carrier attestation evidence requirements.

**ICP willingness to pay:** Moderate-to-high — working papers are familiar territory for consultants, the documentation standard concept is understood, but the AI-specific protocol has not been provided.

### C. AI Review vs. Generation Methodology Documentation Service

**What's missing:** A specific methodology documentation service that distinguishes between AI review (human generates, AI reviews) and AI generation (AI generates, human approves) — with distinct disclosure language, documentation requirements, and liability implications for each.

**Why it matters:** "AI-assisted" currently provides no meaningful disclosure to clients. A methodology documentation service that distinguishes AI review from AI generation gives the ICP a way to document the intellectual role AI played — and to communicate that role to clients in a way that protects the firm's intellectual credibility.

**ICP willingness to pay:** High — this goes to the core of what a consulting boutique sells (intellectual judgment) and what makes a Geneva boutique different from an AI tool.

### D. EU AI Act Determination Service for Geneva Consulting Boutiques

**What's missing:** A specific service that determines whether EU AI Act obligations apply to a Geneva consulting boutique — based on client industry, types of AI use in client engagements, EU nexus analysis, and if applicable, a Geneva-calibrated conformity assessment pathway.

**Why it matters:** Geneva consulting boutiques serving banking, insurance, and healthcare clients face a genuine question about EU AI Act applicability that no one in the Geneva market is answering specifically. The determination is valuable even when the answer is "not applicable" — because it removes uncertainty and allows the ICP to make informed decisions about AI tool adoption.

**ICP willingness to pay:** High — regulatory determination with personal liability implications is exactly the kind of service a managing director will pay for.

### E. Multi-Framework Accountability Stack Management

**What's missing:** A methodology for managing multiple accountability frameworks (nFADP, client AI governance questionnaires, carrier attestation, EU AI Act) as an integrated accountability system — one documentation architecture that satisfies multiple frameworks simultaneously, with a named responsible person coordination protocol.

**Why it matters:** Each framework names the managing director as the accountable person. No framework provides a methodology for managing them together. The accountability stack is a specific structural problem that requires a specific structural solution.

**ICP willingness to pay:** High — this problem is named clearly by the ICP ("four frameworks, one person, no coordination") and has no existing solution in the market.

### F. Geneva Boutique AI Governance Certification or Standard

**What's missing:** A Geneva-specific AI governance standard calibrated for 2–50 person consulting boutiques — not an enterprise standard scaled down, but a boutique-built standard with a definition of "Geneva standard" AI governance documentation that the ICP can point to.

**Why it matters:** "Geneva standard" is what the ICP is seeking — a reference point for what adequate AI governance documentation means at boutique scale. No one has defined it. The first party to define "Geneva standard" for AI governance documentation owns the standard — and becomes the reference point for the market.

**ICP willingness to pay:** Very high — peer legitimacy and competitive positioning are primary drivers. A "Geneva standard" AI governance designation would be a market differentiator.
