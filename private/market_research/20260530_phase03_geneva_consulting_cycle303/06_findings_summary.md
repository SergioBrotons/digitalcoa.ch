## NET-NEW Findings (C303 Phase 03) — 6 Distinct Insights

### Finding 1: Engagement Letter AI Clauses — The Contractual Accountability Foundation Is Absent

**Insight:** The engagement letter is the foundational contract of every consulting engagement. It defines what the client owes the firm and what the firm owes the client. Virtually all Geneva consulting engagement letters were drafted before AI existed and are silent on AI. This silence creates a contractual accountability vacuum — the firm has no documented framework for what AI use it discloses, permits, warrants, or protects in its core client contract.

**Mechanism:** When clients challenge AI-generated deliverables, they will point to the engagement letter. Swiss contract law's principle of good faith (OR Art. 2) means ambiguous terms are interpreted against the drafting party. The silence on AI does not favor the firm — it leaves the question open, and open questions in contract disputes favor the party that didn't draft the ambiguous term.

**Evidence:** ICPs report not having reviewed their engagement letters for AI provisions since AI became relevant. No Geneva market service offers engagement letter AI clause review specifically calibrated for consulting boutiques.

**Geneva dimension:** Geneva consulting clients are sophisticated — banks, commodity trading firms, pharmaceutical companies, family offices — who read engagement letters carefully and have their own AI governance frameworks. The absence of AI language signals either evasion or ignorance. Neither is acceptable from a Geneva-level advisor.

**Why NET-NEW vs. C302:** C302 Phase 03 found the proposal methodology defensibility problem (client-facing document). Finding 1 addresses the engagement letter — the prior-contractual document that precedes any proposal. The engagement letter is within the ICP's own control, distinct from both client questionnaires (external) and carrier attestation (insurance intermediary).

**Ready-to-use language:**
- *"Your engagement letter was written before AI existed. Here's what it should say — and why the silence is a liability."*
- *"The silence isn't neutral. Swiss contract law interprets ambiguity against the drafting party."*
- *"When a client challenges an AI-generated recommendation, they'll point to the engagement letter. What does it say about AI?"*
- *"You have a policy problem everywhere else. But your engagement letter — the most important document — is silent on the most important change in consulting history."*

### Finding 2: Working Papers AI Documentation — The Internal Evidence Standard Has No AI Equivalent

**Insight:** Working papers are the foundational evidence standard for the consulting profession — they document what analysis was conducted, what data was used, what methodology was applied, and how conclusions were reached. AI-assisted analysis has entered working papers without any equivalent documentation standard. The working papers gap is internal, structural, and feeds every external accountability problem.

**Mechanism:** When a junior consultant uses an AI tool in a client engagement, the working paper contains the AI output — nothing else. The prompt, the dataset scope, the exclusion criteria, the AI version, the human review notes, and the authorization are not captured. When carriers, clients, or regulators ask for evidence of AI oversight, the working papers provide no answer.

**Evidence:** ICPs report that AI outputs appear in working papers without documentation of the AI tool, the prompt, or the human review process. No Geneva market service offers a working papers AI documentation protocol calibrated for consulting boutiques.

**Geneva dimension:** Geneva consulting boutiques compete on intellectual credibility. The working paper is where intellectual credibility is documented. If working papers cannot document AI's role in analysis, the firm's intellectual credibility cannot be established for AI-mediated work — and cannot be defended when challenged.

**Why NET-NEW vs. C302:** C302 Phase 03 found proposal methodology defensibility (client-facing). Finding 2 addresses working papers — the internal evidence standard that underlies all external accountability. Working papers are distinct from proposals: they are internal documents, subject to professional standards, that feed the evidence requirements of carriers, clients, and regulators.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- *"Your working papers standard exists for human work. It doesn't exist for AI-assisted work. That's the gap."*
- *"The working paper is supposed to be the evidence trail. For AI-assisted work, there is no evidence trail."*
- *"AI outputs appear in working papers. The prompt, the scope, the authorization — none of it is captured. And that's every carrier request, every client question, every regulatory inquiry."*
- *"The working papers gap is internal. But everything external — the carrier form, the client questionnaire, the nFADP documentation — feeds from it."*

### Finding 3: AI Review vs. AI Generation — The Methodology Attribution Problem That "AI-Assisted" Hides

**Insight:** "AI-assisted" covers two fundamentally different methodological situations with different accountability profiles, different liability implications, and different client disclosure requirements. AI review (human generates, AI reviews) keeps intellectual authority with the consultant. AI generation (AI generates, human approves) transfers intellectual authority — at least partly — to the AI system. "AI-assisted" provides no meaningful disclosure to clients about which is which.

**Mechanism:** When AI generates the analysis, the consultant's professional role changes from author to reviewer. This affects what the consultant can warrant to the client, what liability attaches, how methodology should be documented, and how intellectual authority is represented. When AI reviews human-generated analysis, the consultant retains authorship — a fundamentally different professional position.

**Evidence:** ICPs report being unable to answer the client question "did you or the AI make this recommendation?" — and being uncertain themselves about the distinction. The question is arising more frequently in client due diligence. Sophisticated clients are starting to ask it directly.

**Geneva dimension:** Geneva consulting boutiques are hired for intellectual judgment. If the market perceives that judgment has been outsourced to AI tools the managing director cannot explain, the value proposition shifts fundamentally. The methodology attribution problem is not just a documentation problem — it goes to the core of what a Geneva consulting boutique sells.

**Why NET-NEW vs. C302:** C302 Phase 03 found methodology defensibility broadly. Finding 3 specifies the AI review vs. AI generation distinction as a distinct sub-category — one that has not been named in the market and has specific liability and disclosure implications that general methodology defensibility does not capture.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- *"There's AI review and AI generation. These are different risks. 'AI-assisted' hides both."*
- *"When AI reviews your analysis, you made the recommendation. When AI generates the recommendation, did you?"*
- *"The client asked: was this AI-generated or AI-assisted? These are different. And 'AI-assisted' tells them nothing."*
- *"At what point does AI assistance become AI delegation? Your engagement letter doesn't answer. Your methodology document doesn't answer. You're the one who signs — and you don't know."*

### Finding 4: EU AI Act High-Risk Classification — The Geneva Consulting Boutique Exposure Is Unnamed

**Insight:** The EU AI Act designates certain AI uses as high-risk, triggering conformity assessments, technical documentation requirements, human oversight obligations, and transparency duties. Geneva consulting firms serving clients in banking, insurance, and healthcare may themselves face EU AI Act obligations through their client work — without having made a formal determination. No Geneva market service offers EU AI Act determination specifically calibrated for consulting boutiques.

**Mechanism:** The EU AI Act applies extraterritorially to organizations deploying AI systems affecting EU residents. If a Geneva consulting boutique uses AI tools in engagements serving EU-regulated industries — banking, insurance, healthcare — the determination of whether EU AI Act applies requires a structured analysis of client types, AI use cases, and EU nexus factors. This determination is absent from the Geneva market.

**Evidence:** ICPs report uncertainty about EU AI Act applicability ("I assumed it didn't apply because my clients are Swiss") and no source of Geneva-calibrated guidance. The EU AI Act conformity timeline (~14 months) means the determination question is not speculative — it is present-tense.

**Geneva dimension:** Geneva's concentration of banking, insurance, commodity trading, and pharmaceutical clients means a disproportionate share of Geneva consulting boutiques serve EU-regulated industries. The EU AI Act determination question is live for almost every Geneva consulting boutique serving these sectors — and no one in the Geneva market is answering it specifically.

**Why NET-NEW vs. C302:** C302 Phase 02 (finance) found EU AI Act as a general framework concern. Finding 4 addresses the specific determination question for consulting boutiques — not "EU AI Act is coming" but "does EU AI Act apply to what I do, and if so, what do I do about it?"

**Ready-to-use language:**
- *"The EU AI Act may already apply to your Geneva consulting practice — specifically if your clients are in banking, insurance, or healthcare."*
- *"Conformity assessment is not a form you fill in. It's a technical documentation requirement designed for enterprises with engineering teams."*
- *"The determination question — does EU AI Act apply to me? — is something every Geneva consulting boutique serving regulated industries needs to answer now."*
- *"If the answer is yes, the timeline is already running. If the answer is no, you need that in writing."*

### Finding 5: Multi-Framework Accountability Stack — The Managing Director Is Named by All Frameworks, Served by None

**Insight:** Small Geneva consulting boutiques have a single managing director who is simultaneously named as the "responsible person" under nFADP Article 63, the signatory on carrier attestation forms, the respondent to client AI governance questionnaires, and potentially the accountable person under EU AI Act. No Geneva market service has named this accumulation as a specific structural problem — or provided a methodology for managing it as an integrated accountability system.

**Mechanism:** Each framework names the managing director personally, requires different documentation formats, uses different terminology, has different update cycles, and potentially requires different evidence standards. The managing director manages each framework separately — creating documentation inconsistency, duplicative effort, and the risk that documentation produced for one framework contradicts another.

**Evidence:** ICPs report the awareness of multiple frameworks naming them but no pathway to managing them as a package. The phrase "four frameworks, one person, no coordination" appears consistently in ICP descriptions of their situation.

**Geneva dimension:** Geneva's professional culture concentrates accountability in named individuals. The accountability stack hits harder in a 12-person firm than in a 200-person firm — because there is no compliance department, no legal team, no institutional buffer between the individual and the obligation. The managing director of a Geneva boutique bears concentrated personal accountability that no comparable infrastructure supports.

**Why NET-NEW vs. C302:** C302 Phase 02 (finance) found cross-track documentation coherence as a service category. Finding 5 specifies the accountability stack as the personal concentration problem — not just documentation coherence but the specific fact that all frameworks name the same person, who has no methodology for managing the convergence.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- *"Four frameworks name you personally. None of them tell you how to manage them together."*
- *"nFADP says you're responsible. Your carrier says you're responsible. Your client says you're responsible. What does 'responsible' mean across all of them — together?"*
- *"You're managing four documentation streams separately. Each framework names you. None of them knows about the others."*
- *"The accountability stack is a specific structural problem for a specific type of firm. It's not a general compliance problem. It's a Geneva boutique problem."*

### Finding 6: The AI Knowledge-Authority Inversion at Partnership Level Is Structurally Invisible

**Insight:** The senior partners and managing directors who bear personal accountability for AI decisions have less AI fluency than the junior staff who use AI tools daily. This inversion creates a structural documentation gap — the person who can document AI use does not have the accountability, and the person who has accountability does not have the AI knowledge. The gap compounds silently because no one has named it as a structural problem rather than a training deficiency.

**Mechanism:** Junior consultants adopt AI tools independently, without systematic disclosure to the managing director. By the time the senior partner learns about a tool, there is already no record of its use in client work. The managing director signs carrier attestations and client questionnaire responses based on incomplete knowledge of the firm's actual AI tool inventory. The documentation system depends on junior staff accurately reporting their own AI use — which requires both honesty and awareness of what the managing director needs.

**Evidence:** ICPs consistently report not knowing the full AI tool inventory of their own firm. The gap between the managing director's AI knowledge and the junior staff's AI knowledge is acknowledged but framed as a training gap rather than a structural documentation gap. The accountability and knowledge distribution are seen as a personal deficiency rather than a systemic design problem.

**Geneva dimension:** Geneva consulting boutiques compete on intellectual authority. The senior partner's authority rests on demonstrated professional judgment. If that judgment is actually being formed or executed by AI tools the senior partner cannot evaluate, the firm's value proposition is undocumented and potentially misrepresented to clients.

**Why NET-NEW vs. C303 Phase 01:** C303 Phase 01 (legal) found the junior-senior fluency inversion as a general AI tool visibility problem. Finding 6 specifies the inversion in the consulting context where: (a) intellectual authority is the core business asset, (b) the managing director's authority is the firm-level competitive differentiator, and (c) the inversion threatens both the accountability documentation and the intellectual credibility positioning simultaneously.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- *"The person who signs doesn't know what AI tools are being used. The person who knows doesn't sign."*
- *"Your carrier attestation warrants that you supervise AI output. The person supervising doesn't know what AI is being used."*
- *"The accountability gap and the knowledge gap are the same gap. Solving one requires solving the other."*
- *"This isn't a training problem. It's a structural documentation problem. And you can't train your way out of a structural problem."*
