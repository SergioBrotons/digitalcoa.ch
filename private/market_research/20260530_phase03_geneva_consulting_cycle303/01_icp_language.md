# 01 — ICP Language: How Geneva Consulting Boutiques Talk About AI Problems

## Phase 03 — C303 | Geneva Consulting (2–50 employees)

---

## 1. Primary Problem Framing

The Geneva consulting boutique ICP frames AI risk through **personal accountability, contractual exposure, and methodology credibility**. The managing director or partner signs the engagement letter — personally. They sign the carrier attestation — personally. They respond to the client's AI governance questionnaire — personally accountable for the accuracy of the response. Multiple accountability vectors converge on one person.

**Characteristic phrases:**

- *"My engagement letter doesn't mention AI. Should it?"*
- *"If I used AI to generate this analysis — what did I actually sell the client?"*
- *"I need to show the client what role AI played. 'AI-assisted' doesn't tell them anything."*
- *"The EU AI Act — does it apply to what I do for my banking clients?"*
- *"I have four frameworks naming me as the responsible person. Nobody's told me what that means together."*
- *"The working papers standard — we have it for human work. What do we have for AI-assisted work?"*
- *"If the AI-generated recommendation is wrong, what does my engagement letter actually cover?"*

---

## 2. Language Vectors — How Problems Are Described

### A. Engagement Letter / Contractual Language
- *"My engagement letter was drafted before AI was a consideration"*
- *"What do I warrant to the client about AI use?"*
- *"Is there an AI clause in my engagement letter? I don't know — I didn't write it"*
- *"If the client asks what AI was used and how — what does the contract say I have to show?"*
- *"Engagement letter silence on AI — is that a liability or protection?"*
- *"The client will point to the engagement letter when something goes wrong. What does it say about AI?"*

### B. Working Papers / Documentation Language
- *"We have a working papers standard for human analysis — we have nothing for AI-assisted analysis"*
- *"What exactly needs to be in the working paper when AI is involved?"*
- *"I can reconstruct a human analyst's work. I can't reconstruct what the AI did."*
- *"The working paper is the evidence. AI has no working paper standard."*
- *"Junior staff use AI in working papers. By the time I review it, the AI output is in the deliverable."*

### C. Methodology Attribution Language
- *"AI-assisted — that could mean anything"*
- *"There's a big difference between AI reviewing my analysis and AI generating the analysis"*
- *"If AI generated the recommendation, did I actually make it? Does it matter legally?"*
- *"The client wants to know: was this AI-generated, AI-assisted, or AI-reviewed? These are completely different."*
- *"I'm the author of this recommendation. Or am I? What's my intellectual role when AI generates it?"*
- *"When does AI assistance become AI delegation?"*

### D. EU AI Act / Regulatory Classification Language
- *"The EU AI Act — does it apply to what I do for my Swiss banking clients?"*
- *"I advise insurers. Insurers are regulated. Does that make me a high-risk AI deployer?"*
- *"My clients are in banking, insurance, healthcare — they have EU AI Act obligations. Do I have them too?"*
- *"Conformity assessment — is that something a 12-person firm can actually do?"*
- *"High-risk AI system — the definition is so broad I don't know if it covers what I do"*
- *"Nobody's told me whether EU AI Act applies to my Geneva consulting practice."*

### E. Multi-Framework Accountability Stack Language
- *"I'm named as responsible person in four different frameworks. Nobody's put them together."*
- *"nFADP says I'm responsible. My carrier says I'm responsible. My client says I'm responsible. What does 'responsible' mean across all of them?"*
- *"I have a carrier attestation, a client questionnaire response, and nFADP obligations — are these the same documentation?"*
- *"The frameworks all name me. They don't tell me how to manage them as a package."*
- *"How do I document once for multiple frameworks? Is that even possible?"*
- *"The accountability stack — nobody's quantified what it means for a managing director of a small firm."*

### F. AI Knowledge-Authority Inversion Language
- *"I don't know what AI tools my team is using. I'm the one who signs."*
- *"The person with the most AI knowledge has the least accountability. And vice versa."*
- *"Partners don't understand the AI tools. Junior staff understand them but have no accountability."*
- *"I can't review what I don't understand. But I sign anyway."*
- *"My AI fluency is lower than my junior staff's. They make decisions I can't evaluate."*
- *"The accountability gap and the knowledge gap are the same gap."*

## 3. Emotional State Markers

The ICP is in **compounding accountability anxiety** — not about a single obligation, but about the growing stack of obligations that all converge on them personally. New dimensions:

1. **Engagement letter review** — discovers contractual silence on AI, creating new uncertainty
2. **Client AI governance questionnaire** — forces documentation of what the contract doesn't define
3. **EU AI Act classification question** — discovers possible regulatory exposure with no Geneva-calibrated guidance
4. **Working papers review** — discovers AI use in client work with no documentation standard
5. **Accountability stack awareness** — realizes multiple frameworks are naming them simultaneously
6. **Peer competitor event** — learns a peer firm lost a mandate over AI documentation posture

**Emotional signature:** *"I signed the engagement letter. I signed the carrier attestation. I'm named in nFADP. The client is asking about AI. And I don't think any of these documents are talking to each other. I'm the one in the middle."*

## 4. Trusted Information Sources

- Geneva professional associations (SBA, ODAGE, industry-specific bodies)
- Peer networks — informal circles, trusted advisors
- Carrier communications (read carefully, with growing concern)
- EU AI Act coverage in professional press
- U.S. enforcement events that circulate in Geneva professional circles
- Geneva boutique consulting peers (real conversations, not conference panels)

---

## 5. Language That Converts

**What makes them stop and act:**

- *"Your engagement letter was written before AI existed. Here's what it should say — and why the silence is a liability."*
- *"There's AI review and AI generation. These are different risks. 'AI-assisted' hides both."*
- *"The EU AI Act may already apply to your practice — specifically if your clients are in banking, insurance, or healthcare."*
- *"Your working papers standard exists for human work. It doesn't exist for AI-assisted work. That's the gap."*
- *"Four frameworks name you as responsible person. None of them tell you how to manage all four together."*
- *"The person who signs doesn't know what the AI does. The person who knows doesn't sign. That's a structural inversion."*

---

## 6. Language to Avoid

- Abstract compliance language ("you should comply with EU AI Act")
- Generic AI governance frameworks without consulting-specific grounding
- "AI policy" language — they don't have a policy problem, they have a **contractual documentation problem**
- Technical control language — they need accountability language, not technical specifications
- Regulatory optimism — "Swiss law is still developing" sounds like inaction to the ICP who is already exposed
- Large-enterprise frameworks — they need Geneva boutique-calibrated guidance, not enterprise-scaled-down advice
