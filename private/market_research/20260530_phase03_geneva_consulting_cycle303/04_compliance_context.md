# 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Switzerland Regulatory Landscape

## Phase 03 — C303 | Geneva Consulting (2–50 employees)

---

## 1. nFADP — The Personal Accountability Foundation

**Framework:** Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP), revised version in force since June 2023, with full enforcement transition ongoing.

**Why it matters for consulting ICP:** The consulting boutique processes highly sensitive client data — business strategy, M&A targets, organizational restructuring, financial profiles. Article 63 creates personal liability for the designated "responsible person." For small consulting boutiques, this is always the managing director.

**Article 63 specifics:**
- Administrative offense liability for violations of nFADP obligations
- Personal liability of the responsible person — not just the firm
- Fines up to CHF 250,000 per violation for the responsible person personally
- Criminal liability exposure in serious cases involving data breaches

**What this means for the consulting ICP:**
- The managing director is personally liable for nFADP compliance failures involving AI tools
- If an AI vendor used by the firm processes personal data improperly, the responsible person faces personal exposure
- The documentation obligations under nFADP are specific — Art. 5(c) authorization requirements, Art. 9 data protection impact assessments, Art. 26 processor agreements
- Evidence of compliance (not just policy existence) is what protects the responsible person

**Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva consulting boutiques handle data at the highest sensitivity end of the spectrum — client data that would cause significant harm if exposed. The EDÖB (Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner) has been increasing enforcement activity. Geneva firms are in the EDÖB's jurisdiction and enforcement area.

## 2. EU AI Act — The Extraterritorial Exposure Question

**Framework:** EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689), entered into force August 2024, first conformity requirements for high-risk AI systems from August 2026 (~14 months).

**The Geneva consulting question:** Does the EU AI Act apply to Swiss consulting boutiques? The answer is not automatically "no" — and no Geneva service has addressed this determination question specifically for consulting boutiques.

**When EU AI Act may apply to a Geneva consulting boutique:**
1. The firm deploys an AI system that processes data of EU residents
2. The firm's AI system produces outputs used in EU-regulated sectors (banking, insurance, healthcare)
3. The firm provides services to EU-based clients using AI systems
4. The firm's AI system affects EU residents' access to services or fundamental rights

**High-risk AI system categories potentially relevant to consulting:**
- AI systems used in employment decisions (organizational consulting)
- AI systems used in credit decisions (financial consulting)
- AI systems used in education and training (executive coaching, professional development consulting)
- AI systems used in law enforcement (investigative consulting)
- AI systems used in healthcare (consulting for healthcare organizations)

**What high-risk classification triggers:**
- Conformity assessment before deployment
- Technical documentation (Art. 11) — extensive documentation requirements
- Human oversight measures (Art. 14)
- Transparency obligations toward affected persons (Art. 13)
- Quality management system requirements
- Logging and record-keeping obligations

**Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva's concentration of banking, insurance, and pharmaceutical clients means a large proportion of Geneva consulting boutiques have EU nexus through their client base. The determination question ("does EU AI Act apply to me?") is live and unanswered for almost every Geneva consulting boutique serving regulated industries.

**Practical implication:** The EU AI Act determination is a specific service — a structured analysis of whether the Act applies to a specific consulting practice, based on client types, AI use cases, and EU nexus factors. The output is either "not applicable" (with documented rationale) or "applicable — here's the pathway."

## 3. FINMA Supervision — The Indirect Exposure Through Clients

**Framework:** FINMA Circular 08/2024 (AI governance for supervised institutions) and FINMA 2025/02 guidance on AI use in financial services.

**Why it matters for consulting ICP:** Geneva consulting boutiques serve FINMA-supervised clients (banks, insurers, securities dealers). When those clients receive FINMA guidance on AI governance, they extend requirements to their vendors — including consulting boutiques.

**The vendor chain effect:**
- FINMA requires supervised institutions to document AI governance of their service providers
- FINMA-supervised clients send AI governance questionnaires to consulting vendors
- The consulting boutique becomes part of the client's FINMA compliance evidence chain
- The boutique's documentation directly affects the client's compliance posture

**What this means:**
- The FINMA AI governance framework creates documentation requirements that flow downstream to Geneva consulting boutiques
- A Geneva consulting boutique serving a FINMA-supervised client faces FINMA-adjacent documentation requirements without being directly supervised by FINMA
- The boutique's AI governance documentation must satisfy not only its own obligations but also the client's FINMA requirements

**Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva's position as Switzerland's financial center means a disproportionate share of Geneva consulting boutiques serve banks and financial institutions. The FINMA downstream effect is stronger in Geneva than in any other Swiss city.

---

## 4. Client AI Governance Requirements — The De Facto Standards

**Signal:** Institutional clients (banks, family offices, public sector entities, commodity trading firms) are sending AI governance questionnaires to consulting vendors. These questionnaires represent the most immediate compliance pressure on the ICP — more immediate than regulatory frameworks, because they arrive with deadlines and competitive consequences.

**Client questionnaire common elements:**
- AI tool inventory (what tools, what version, what data access)
- Authorization records (who approved each tool, when, with what oversight)
- Decision attribution documentation (what role did AI play in client deliverables)
- Vendor data processing agreements (does the firm have DPAs with AI vendors)
- Data breach notification procedures
- Sub-consultant AI governance provisions

**The evidence requirement:**
- Clients ask for records, not policies
- The gap between what clients ask and what boutiques can produce is wide and growing
- A boutique's questionnaire response becomes part of the client's own regulatory evidence file
- Incorrect or incomplete responses create liability for the client — which creates urgency for the boutique

## 5. Carrier Attestation — Professional Liability Documentation

**Framework:** Professional liability insurance carriers serving the professional services sector have updated their AI governance requirements from attestation-based to evidence-based documentation.

**Carrier Form v4 requirements:**
- Last three authorization records per AI tool in use
- Documentation of who approved each AI tool's deployment
- Description of human oversight measures for AI outputs
- Vendor data processing agreement documentation
- Evidence of regular review of AI tool effectiveness and compliance

**The evidence gap:**
- Authorization records for AI tools deployed 2–3 years ago were never captured
- Retroactive reconstruction of authorization decisions is structurally impossible
- The carrier attestation created a record of attestation — not a record of compliance
- The managing director's signature on the attestation creates personal liability exposure

**Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva professional liability carriers are themselves navigating AI governance standards. The uncertainty about what constitutes acceptable evidence creates additional pressure on the ICP — who is simultaneously trying to satisfy carrier requirements, client questionnaires, and regulatory obligations with no integrated methodology.

## 6. Engagement Letter Contractual Framework

**The Geneva consulting engagement letter context:**
- Swiss contract law (OR — Federal Act on the Amendment of the Swiss Civil Code) governs consulting engagement letters
- Swiss engagement letter standards require clear scope definition, limitation of liability, and duty of care provisions
- AI provisions are absent from virtually all existing Geneva consulting engagement letters

**What engagement letters should address (operational framework):**
1. **Disclosure:** Whether and how AI may be used in the engagement
2. **Warranties:** What the consulting firm warrants about AI-generated content
3. **Limitation of liability:** How AI-generated output liability is allocated between firm and client
4. **Intellectual property:** Who owns AI-generated deliverables — the firm or the client?
5. **Data processing:** What client data may be processed through AI tools, and under what conditions
6. **Sub-consultant AI:** How AI use by sub-consultants is handled in the engagement scope
7. **Notification obligations:** When the client must be notified about AI use in deliverables
8. **Termination provisions:** What happens to AI-generated work product if the engagement terminates

**The contractual risk:** An engagement letter silent on AI is not neutral — it creates ambiguity that favors the client in a dispute. Swiss contract law's principle of good faith (OR Art. 2) means ambiguous terms are interpreted against the drafting party. For Geneva consulting boutiques that did not draft their engagement letters with AI in mind, silence on AI creates structural ambiguity that works against the firm.

## 7. Cross-Framework Documentation Architecture

**The problem:** The consulting ICP faces simultaneous documentation requirements from:
1. nFADP (Swiss data protection)
2. EU AI Act (if applicable — determination needed)
3. Client AI governance questionnaires (client-specific, immediate)
4. Carrier Form v4 (professional liability, active)
5. Engagement letter obligations (contractual, ongoing)
6. Working papers standards (professional evidence standard)
7. Sub-consultant AI delegation (client and contractual)

**Each framework:**
- Uses different terminology
- Requires different documentation formats
- Names a potentially different "responsible person"
- Has different update cycles and review requirements

**The cross-framework coherence solution:**
A single documentation architecture that satisfies multiple frameworks simultaneously — using a common evidence base (AI tool inventory, authorization records, decision attribution logs, vendor processor chain documentation) organized to satisfy each framework's specific reporting format.

**No Geneva service currently offers cross-framework documentation architecture at boutique consulting scale.**
