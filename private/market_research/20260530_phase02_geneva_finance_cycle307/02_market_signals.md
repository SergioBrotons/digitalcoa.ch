## Executive Summary

Geneva boutique wealth management firms at 50–200 employees face a compound accountability acceleration driven by institutional AI governance questionnaires functioning as competitive gatekeepers, third-generation carrier attestation probing vendor processor chains, and an active documentation window where early responders are being evaluated against escalating standards. The ICP has moved from "am I exposed?" to "what do I do now, in what order?" — an action-phase shift that changes the entire sales dynamic. Six NET-NEW findings surface distinct structural problems at the intersection of institutional client gatekeeping, carrier pre-signing exposure, portfolio construction authorization chain documentation, vendor processor chain accountability, SRO vacuum compounding, and cross-mandate documentation coherence.

## 6 NET-NEW Findings (Distinct from C214–C296 Phase 02)

### Finding 01: Institutional AI governance questionnaire as competitive gatekeeper — mandate access risk unnamed in market

**Mechanism:** Institutional clients sending AI governance questionnaires to Geneva boutique WM firms are not merely collecting information — they are using questionnaire responses as a comparative screening instrument. A boutique that cannot respond to institutional client AI governance standards at the required level of detail is flagged as a risk in the mandate pipeline. The institutional client's selection committee receives AI governance questionnaire responses alongside performance data, fee structures, and team credentials. An incomplete or vague AI governance response functions as a negative signal in a selection process where the institutional client has multiple boutiques to choose from. The questionnaire is functioning as a de facto mandate access gate: inability to demonstrate adequate AI governance methodology creates real exclusion from future mandate flows. This is distinct from C286/C296 institutional questionnaire naming which identified the questionnaire as an acute trigger and the40-page format as creating documentation burden, without isolating the specific competitive gatekeeper mechanism where inability to respond adequately translates directly into mandate exclusion risk that has nothing to do with investment performance.

**Market signal:** Institutional clients are now explicitly linking AI governance questionnaire adequacy to manager selection decisions — not just "nice to have" compliance content.

**ICP language:** "Our biggest client asked us to complete their AI governance questionnaire. I didn't realize it was part of their manager review process." / "If we don't answer this properly, we're out of the running for the new mandate."

**FR:** "Le client institutionnel nous a demandé de compléter leur questionnaire de gouvernance IA. Je ne savais pas que c'était lié à leur décision de sélection."

### Finding 02: Carrier pre-signing review gap — MD personal exposure from attestation language not evaluated before signature

**Mechanism:** Managing directors at Geneva boutique WM firms are signing carrier AI governance attestation documents without independent review of what the attestation language actually commits them to. The carrier attestation form contains representations about AI governance adequacy, vendor due diligence completeness, and ongoing monitoring obligations that the MD signs without understanding that the language creates contractual commitments to the carrier and potentially evidentiary exposure in professional liability proceedings. The critical gap is not that the boutique lacks AI governance documentation — it is that the MD is signing attestation language that may commit to standards the boutique does not actually meet, without ever reading the specific language against their actual practice. Carrier pre-signing review is not a document review — it is a personal liability exposure mapping exercise: what does my signature actually commit me to, and does my actual practice satisfy those commitments? This is distinct from C287/C288 carrier attestation naming which identified the personal signature urgency without isolating that the specific exposure is in the attestation language itself — not the AI governance framework the language references — and that the MD has almost certainly never read the specific language they signed.

**Market signal:** D&O and professional liability carriers are now explicitly including AI governance attestation in renewal documentation. The MD's signature is personal.

**ICP language:** "I signed the attestation. I wasn't sure what it meant." / "The broker brought the form. I signed it. I didn't read every word." / "What does 'adequate AI governance controls' actually mean in that context?"

**FR:** "J'ai signé l'attestation. Je n'étais pas sûr de ce que cela signifiait." / "Le courtier a apporté le formulaire. Je l'ai signé sans lire chaque mot."

### Finding 03: Portfolio construction AI authorization chain documentation as sequential integrity requirement unnamed in boutique context

**Mechanism:** Geneva boutiques using AI in portfolio construction must document a sequential authorization chain: AI generates an allocation recommendation, the investment committee reviews it, the PM approves and implements it, results are reported to the client. Each step requires a documented human authorization moment. Current AI governance documentation frameworks address the endpoint (AI-assisted recommendation) or the tool (vendor inventory, data processing agreements). None address the authorization chain as a discrete documentation requirement with its own evidence standard proportionate to boutique investment committee process. When the institutional client or carrier asks "can you document the human authorization moments preceding each AI-assisted portfolio decision?", the boutique must reconstruct a sequential authorization record it never built. The documentation gap is specifically in the chaining between AI output and human authorization moments — not the content of the AI recommendation, not the vendor terms, but the specific sequential documentation of what human said yes to what AI output at what point in the mandate cycle. This is distinct from C292/C296 authorization chain naming which addressed evidence of human review without isolating that the structural gap is specifically the chaining documentation between AI output and authorization moment with its own sequential integrity requirement proportionate to boutique process.

**Market signal:** FINMA Guidance 08/2024 expects documented human oversight of AI-assisted decisions. No Geneva methodology exists for building a sequential authorization chain documentation architecture proportionate to boutique investment committee process.

**ICP language:** "We have an investment committee. AI generates ideas. We discuss them. We approve or modify. But I can't document what the AI actually recommended at each step." / "If someone asks me to show the trail from AI output to final decision, I don't have it."

**FR:** "Nous utilisons l'IA dans la construction de portefeuille. L'IA propose, le comité approuve. Mais je ne peux pas prouver la séquence."

### Finding 04: Third-generation carrier evidence requirements targeting AI vendor processor chain as undisclosed third-party compliance obligation

**Mechanism:** Third-generation carrier attestation questionnaires are beginning to ask not just about the boutique's own AI governance but about the AI vendor processor chain: what AI vendors does the boutique use, what data do those vendors process, what are the sub-processor arrangements, what happens to data if the vendor is acquired or updated or enters insolvency proceedings. These questions probe nFADP Art.9 third-party processor obligations that the boutique's vendor contracts either don't address or address through click-through terms the boutique never read. The carrier is functioning as a de facto nFADP enforcement instrument requiring the boutique to disclose processor chain details the boutique may not have, because the vendor terms create undisclosed processing the boutique accepted without evaluation. Carrier third-generation evidence targeting vendor processor chain = undisclosed third-party nFADP compliance obligation the MD did not know existed when vendor terms were accepted. This is distinct from C293/C292/C296 vendor due diligence naming which addressed vendor terms generally without isolating the specific mechanism where third-generation carrier questions probe processor chain creating nFADP Art.9 disclosure obligations triggered by carrier interrogation that the boutique has been accumulating through years of click-through acceptance.

**Market signal:** Carriers are now asking about vendor sub-processors and data termination protocols — not just internal AI governance policy.

**ICP language:** "The carrier asked about our AI vendors' sub-processors. I didn't know we were supposed to know that." / "What does our vendor do if they're acquired? I have no idea."

**FR:** "L'assureur nous a demandé des informations sur les sous-traitants de nos fournisseurs IA. Je ne savais pas que nous étions censés le savoir."

### Finding 05: SRO licensing silence on AI creating structural accountability vacuum with no Geneva methodology for resolution

**Mechanism:** Geneva boutique WM firms affiliated with SROs (VQF, FINIA) are in a structurally aggravated accountability position: the SRO is their primary regulatory interface, the SRO has been entirely silent on AI, and the boutique is simultaneously subject to FINMA Guidance expectations, carrier attestation requirements, institutional client questionnaires, and nFADP obligations — all with no guidance from the one regulatory body the boutique interacts with routinely. SRO silence is not passive absence; it is an active structural condition. The absence of SRO AI guidance creates a vacuum where the boutique cannot use its SRO relationship as an accountability reference point for AI governance. When the boutique asks "what does my SRO expect of me on AI?" the answer is silence. When the SRO sends renewal communications, there is no AI content. The SRO is requiring ongoing AML/KYC compliance using AI tools without telling the boutique what AI governance obligations that creates. This is distinct from C287/C288/C296 SRO vacuum naming which identified the structural vacuum without isolating the specific aggravated condition where SRO silence operates simultaneously with FINMA Guidance, carrier attestation, institutional questionnaire, and nFADP all creating obligations the SRO has never acknowledged or contextualized for the boutique's regime.

**Market signal:** VQF and FINIA affiliate communications are entirely devoid of AI content, even as their member firms are adopting AI tools subject to multiple other accountability tracks.

**ICP language:** "Our SRO has said absolutely nothing about AI. We pay them to supervise us and they've never mentioned it." / "If our SRO has no position, what do we tell FINMA if they ask?"

**FR:** "Notre OAR n'a absolutamente rien dit sur l'IA. C'est le silence complet."

### Finding 06: Documentation coherence paradox across multiple simultaneous AI governance accountability mandates

**Mechanism:** Geneva boutique WM firms are simultaneously managing AI governance accountability documentation requirements from four non-integrated sources: (1) FINMA Guidance 08/2024 governance documentation, (2) institutional client AI governance questionnaire response documentation, (3) nFADP ROPA, DPIA, and Art.26 disclosure documentation, and (4) carrier attestation evidence documentation. Each documentation system requires different formatting, different evidence standards, different update cycles, different named responsible persons, and different disclosure scopes. The boutique is producing four independent documentation programs for what is substantively the same AI governance architecture, maintained by different people at different times with no Geneva methodology for cross-document coherence. When a carrier asks for evidence, the boutique produces carrier-standard documentation that may not satisfy the institutional client questionnaire response standard. When an institutional client probes AI methodology, the response references governance documentation the boutique has not connected to carrier evidence. Documentation coherence across multiple simultaneous mandates is unnamed as a discrete service category. This is distinct from C274/C296 three-track parallel documentation naming which addressed the general parallel accountability without isolating the specific documentation coherence paradox where four non-integrated systems are describing the same AI governance reality without cross-reference methodology and producing documentation that may be internally inconsistent despite individually satisfying each accountability track.

**Market signal:** No Geneva service offers cross-document AI governance coherence assessment — where the boutique's FINMA documentation, institutional client response, nFADP records, and carrier evidence are evaluated as an integrated cross-referenced documentation portfolio.

**ICP language:** "We have our FINMA documentation. We have what we sent the client. We have our nFADP ROPA. And we have the carrier form. They don't talk to each other. I'm not sure they're even consistent."

**FR:** "Nous avons quatre flux de documentation pour la même architecture IA. Je ne sais pas si ils sont cohérents entre eux."
