## Operative Regulatory Landscape (May 2026)

### 1. FINMA Guidance 08/2024 — AI Governance in Financial Institutions

**Status:** Active (published December 18, 2024)

**Key Content:**
- Forward-looking, principles-based AI governance framework
- Proportionality principle: framework must be appropriate to institution size, complexity, risk profile
- Requires documented human oversight of AI-assisted decisions
- Requires AI tool inventory and vendor due diligence
- Requires ongoing monitoring of AI system performance and risk
- No prescriptive methodology — institutions responsible for operationalizing proportionate framework

**Boutique Exposure:**
- Documentation adequacy is the primary gap — proportionate principle without operational calibration
- AI governance documentation exists in fragmented form (vendor agreements, some policy language) but lacks coherent integrated architecture
- No Geneva methodology for proportionate implementation at boutique WM scale
- Documentation window is active — early responders now evaluated against escalating standards

**What FINMA Cannot Do for Boutiques:**
- Cannot provide operational methodology — only principles
- Cannot resolve SRO vacuum — FINMA and SROs are separate regulatory tracks
- Cannot harmonize institutional client questionnaire requirements
- Cannot resolve carrier attestation standard ambiguity

### 2. nFADP (Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection) — AI-Specific Obligations

**Status:** In force September 1, 2023. FDPIC enforcement active following May 8, 2025 clarification on AI-based data processing.

**Key Articles for Boutique WM:**

**Art.6 — Lawfulness of Processing**
- AI-based processing of client financial data requires legal basis
- For WM firms: likely legitimate interest (Art.6(1)(e)) or contract performance (Art.6(1)(b))
- Vendor terms (click-through) may not constitute valid legal basis for all processing

**Art.9 — Third-Party Processor Obligations**
- Data controllers must have written agreements with processors
- Sub-processor arrangements require notification and agreement
- AI vendor click-through terms may not satisfy Art.9 written agreement requirement
- Boutique exposure: undisclosed sub-processor arrangements created through years of click-through acceptance

**Art.26 — Automated Decision Processing**
- Clients have right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing
- Investment decisions with significant effect require human involvement documentation
- Portfolio construction AI authorization chain = Art.26 compliance evidence requirement

**Art.63 — Personal Criminal Liability**
- Managing director personal criminal liability for data protection violations
- Not carrier-covered. Not D&O-covered in standard policies.
- Unnamed in current boutique AI governance market discourse — most MDs do not know Art.63 applies to them personally
- The FDPIC May 2025 clarification activated enforcement on AI-based data processing

**ROPA (Register of Processing Activities)**
- Required under nFADP Art.22
- Most Geneva boutiques do not have a current ROPA
- ROPA must include: processing purposes, data categories, recipient categories, retention periods, security measures, processor agreements
- AI vendor processing must be included in ROPA

**DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment)**
- Required under nFADP Art.28 for high-risk processing
- AI-assisted portfolio construction likely qualifies as high-risk processing requiring DPIA
- Most boutiques have not conducted DPIA for their AI tool usage

### 4. Carrier Attestation Requirements

**Status:** Active. Third-generation questionnaires now probing vendor processor chains.

**What Carriers Are Asking (Gen 1-2):**
- AI governance policy existence
- Vendor due diligence procedures
- Human oversight documentation
- Incident response procedures

**What Carriers Are Asking (Gen 3 — New):**
- Vendor sub-processor arrangements
- Data termination protocols
- Vendor acquisition/insolvency contingencies
- Sub-processor chain disclosure

**Personal Exposure:**
- MD signature required on attestation
- Representations about AI governance adequacy
- Ongoing monitoring obligations committed to by signature
- Carrier may use attestation language in professional liability proceedings

**Documentation Problem:**
- Carrier attestation documentation built for underwriting risk evaluation
- May be architecturally inadequate for criminal defense or FINMA examination
- MD signed language committing to standards without reading specific language against actual practice

### 5. SRO Regulatory Silence (VQF, FINIA)

**Status:** Active structural vacuum. No AI guidance issued to members.

**The Paradox:**
- SROs are the primary regulatory interface for Geneva boutique WM firms
- SROs require AML/KYC compliance using AI tools
- SROs have issued zero guidance on AI governance obligations
- SROs cannot point boutiques to any AI governance methodology

**Structural Effect:**
- Boutique cannot use SRO relationship as accountability reference point for AI governance
- SRO silence + FINMA Guidance + institutional + nFADP + carrier = four-track vacuum with no Geneva methodology for integration
- Boutique is simultaneously subject to four accountability tracks from four different sources, none of which are the SRO it interacts with routinely
