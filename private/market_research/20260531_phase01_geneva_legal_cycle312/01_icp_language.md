# ICP Language: Geneva Legal — C312 Phase 01

## How the ICP Talks About AI Problems

---

## English

### The Post-Attestation ICP

"I signed the form. Like everyone does."

The managing partner signed the carrier attestation. They answered the questions honestly — yes, they use AI tools. Yes, they review output. Yes, they supervise how it's used.

The renewal went through. They moved on.

Then they started hearing things. Colleagues mentioning carrier audits. Forums discussing documentation requirements. The OaF survey creating two-tier visibility. Someone mentioned that the form had changed — that it now asked for something different than what they answered.

They went back to look at the form. They couldn't remember what they signed.

**The documentation gap is no longer abstract. It's personal.**

### The Language They Use

**"I don't know what I attested to."**
This is the most common opening line. The form was Architecture — it created a standard they didn't know they were committing to. They know they signed something, but they can't reconstruct what it required.

**"I have a policy. Isn't that enough?"**
The policy-first assumption. They built a policy because that's what the market offered. They don't know that the carrier form changed question types — from firm-level binary attestation to matter-level open-form description requiring documentation infrastructure.

**"My associates use AI more than I do."**
The competence inversion. The managing partner is personally accountable for AI decisions they cannot personally evaluate. The associate is more fluent. The accountability and the capability are inversely related.

**"I didn't authorize that."**
The unauthorized delegation discovery. The managing partner is finding out that associates have been using personal AI tools on firm matters. They didn't authorize it. They didn't know about it. They're personally liable for it.

**"The form asked questions I couldn't answer."**
The documentation debt confession. The form required evidence of actual practice. They couldn't produce it. They thought it was a gap in their knowledge. They didn't know it was a gap in their documentation system.

**"I thought the vendor took care of this."**
The vendor compliance assumption. The vendor said they were compliant. The managing partner assumed the vendor's compliance covered their obligations. It doesn't. The vendor's terms of service may create exposure the vendor is not obligated to disclose.

**"What does 'adequate' actually mean?"**
The adequacy paralysis. No one has defined what adequate means for a two-to-five attorney Geneva firm. The carrier asks for adequate. The bar acknowledges the obligation. No one provides the definition.

## The Emotional State

The Geneva legal ICP is not worried about AI in the abstract. They are worried about:

- Having signed something they don't understand
- Not being able to produce evidence if the carrier asks
- Personal liability for AI decisions they didn't know were being made
- The form changing question types without notification
- Not knowing what "adequate" means for their firm size
- Being personally accountable for things they can't supervise

The emotional driver is personal vulnerability — not regulatory compliance. They want to protect their license, their clients, and their practice. They want someone who understands the Geneva context specifically.

The service that names their specific situation in their own language gets the call.
