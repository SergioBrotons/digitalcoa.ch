## The Regulatory Landscape: Five-Layer Structure

Phase 05 C290 identified the five-level vertical accountability geometry operating across all four verticals:

1. **Federal AI Coordination Office** (new) — coordinating, not standard-setting authority
2. **Cantonal regulators** (Geneva cantonal authorities) — limited specific Geneva AI guidance
3. **Geneva institutional ecosystem** (bar, SRO, FMH) — publishing principles without operational translation
4. **Carrier underwriting** — fastest-iterating standard formation mechanism, creating undisclosed governance constraints via pricing economics
5. **Institutional counterparty** — client questionnaires functioning as de facto accountability standard

This five-layer structure makes horizontal coherence structurally impossible by design. No Geneva authority is charged with reconciling the five layers. The accountable professional must navigate all five simultaneously, with no methodology for managing them as a portfolio.

## Key Regulatory Developments Affecting Practice

### Development 01 — EU AI Act Article 4 Extraterritorial Live Exposure

**Status:** Live since February 2025. Applies to Geneva firms with EU client relationships, EU data flows, or serving EU-incorporated entities. No Swiss authority has instructed Geneva firms to implement. No Geneva market service assessing this gap.

**Specific exposure:** Article 4 requires providers and deployers of AI systems to have "sufficient technical documentation and instructions for use" and to "implement appropriate human oversight measures." The documentation standard is extraterritorial — Swiss firms serving EU clients are held to EU AI Act documentation standards regardless of Swiss domestic law.

**Carrier interaction:** Phase 06 C292 found carriers treating EU AI Act compliance performance as governance quality signal — even though the framework doesn't formally apply to Swiss firms. The pressure to perform EU AI Act compliance is arriving through carrier underwriting before any formal regulatory requirement.

**Differential:** EU AI Act Art.4 compliance documentation is architecturally different from nFADP documentation requirements. The same AI tool usage may require different documentation for EU AI Act purposes vs. Swiss domestic compliance. The documentation architecture must account for both simultaneously.

**Implication for practice:** EU AI Act Art.4 assessment is a discrete service category with present-tense exposure. The practice that can assess a Geneva boutique's EU AI Act Art.4 compliance gap and propose proportionate documentation is serving a market that has no other address.

### Development 02 — nFADP Enforcement Maturation

**Status:** FDPIC enforcement is now live. FDPIC has confirmed portfolio changes since original attestations (from Phase 05 C297). FDPIC has confirmed enforcement activity in financial services sector. Retrospective confirmation replacing anticipatory anxiety as primary market driver.

**Art.63 personal criminal liability:** CHF 250,000 for intentional data protection violations. The provision operates on different evidentiary standard (beyond reasonable doubt), different enforcement logic (criminal prosecution), different defense requirements than administrative fines. Existing AI governance and pre-signing review designed for administrative exposure are inadequate for criminal defense.

**Documentation architecture distinction:** Phase 06 C292 isolated the specific documentation architecture difference: documentation satisfying carrier attestation is not designed for criminal defense. The attestation submission may contain representations that cannot be supported when examined for criminal liability purposes. Architecturally different service category.

**Art.9 DPA gap:** Phase 01 C293 confirmed the DPA gap for Swiss law firms is larger than expected. Standard commercial DPA covers GDPR-style processing but doesn't embed Art.13 BGFA / Art.321 StGB professional secrecy obligations. Most AI vendors don't offer revFADP Art.9 compliant DPA for Swiss law firms. Even firms that identified DPA as requirement and requested DPAs from vendors face ongoing exposure because vendors have not provided compliant DPAs.

**Implication for practice:** Art.63 framing is the emotional core of urgency. The pre-signing review must assess attestation responses from both evidentiary perspectives simultaneously: does this response satisfy the carrier AND is it consistent with what I could defend under criminal proceedings?

## Cross-Framework Compliance Coherence Problem

### The Structural Impossibility

Phase 05 C290 identified the five-level vertical accountability geometry as making horizontal coherence structurally impossible by design. Phase 05 C297 identified attestation adequacy paradox as instrument design flaw: the form measures documentation completeness not governance effectiveness. Both parties using proxy as if it were standard.

The boutique is facing:
- nFADP enforcement (FDPIC, Art.63 criminal)
- FINMA Guidance 08/2024 (operationalization gap)
- EU AI Act Art.4 (extraterritorial, undocumented in Swiss domestic guidance)
- Swissdamed (healthcare implementation)
- AI Convention (pending implementation, flowing through contracts)
- Carrier attestation (evolving language, different evidentiary standard)
- Institutional client questionnaires (competitive intelligence, not compliance instrument)

Each framework operates independently. None reference each other. The documentation built for one may not serve another. The practice must build documentation architecture that satisfies multiple frameworks simultaneously — or discover that the same AI tool usage creates incompatible documentation requirements under different frameworks.
