# Market Signals: Geneva Legal — C305 Phase 01

## Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

### Primary Market Signals

**1. Carrier Attestation Creates Documented Standard Without Evidence**
The carrier form asks questions about AI governance. When the managing partner answers, they create a documented standard — a representation about what their practice does. The form doesn't require evidence at signing. But the architecture assumes evidence exists. When the carrier asks for evidence (at the next renewal or in an audit), the firm cannot produce it. The attestation created the exposure.

**2. Professional Secrecy Exposure Via Unreviewed Vendor Contracts**
Article 321 of the Swiss Criminal Code prohibits disclosure of information covered by professional secrecy. Most AI vendor contracts involve data processing on vendor servers, potentially in non-Swiss jurisdictions. The vendor terms of service were accepted without review against Article 321 obligations. The exposure is active and specific.

**3. Retention Debt Compounding With No Defined Stop**
AI tools are used in almost every case. There's no record of which tools, which prompts, what data was processed, or what decisions were made with AI assistance. The retention period for these records is undefined — no Geneva guidance, no carrier guidance, no bar guidance. Every case adds to the compounding debt.

**4. Client Intake AI Question Has No Clear Answer**
Clients are starting to ask during intake whether AI will be used on their case and how their information will be protected. Engagement letters have vague "technology-assisted tools" clauses that don't actually address AI governance. The managing partner cannot give a clear, confident answer.

**5. Junior Associate AI Use Creates Personal Supervisory Liability**
Associates use AI more fluently than the managing partner. The managing partner doesn't know what data associates are entering into AI tools, what vendor servers that data goes to, or what the vendors are doing with the prompts. The carrier attestation covers supervision of AI usage in the practice — including by associates. The managing partner is personally accountable for supervision they cannot perform.

**6. "Adequate" Is Undefined for Small Geneva Firms**
The carrier asks for "adequate AI governance." The bar has acknowledged the obligation exists. No Geneva-specific, size-specific definition of "adequate" exists for 2–5 attorney firms. The managing partner cannot determine whether they meet a standard that has never been defined.

---

## Failure Modes

**Attestation audit failure:** Carrier asks for evidence of AI supervision at next renewal. Firm cannot produce documentation of supervision process, tool usage, or review decisions. Attestation becomes evidence of inadequate governance.

**Article 321 failure:** Data processed through AI vendor servers in violation of professional secrecy obligations. No documentation of vendor contract review against Article 321. Exposure is active and undocumented.

**Retention gap failure:** Matter is challenged years later. AI use in the matter cannot be reconstructed. No documentation of prompt, data scope, tool used, or decision. The reconstruction is impossible.

**Client trust failure:** Client asks about AI use at intake. Managing partner cannot give clear answer. Client loses confidence in the firm's ability to protect their information.

---

## The Structural Problem

The legal ICP has a specific structural problem: the carrier form created documented standards at the moment of signing, but the evidence to support those standards was never created. The gap accumulated silently through tool adoption without documentation events. Now the carrier form architecture makes the gap visible — and the managing partner is personally accountable for evidence they don't have.
