# Findings Summary: Geneva Legal — C305 Phase 01

## 6 NET-NEW Findings: Distinct from C214–C304

---

### Finding 01 — The Attestation Architecture Problem Is Universal and Active

**The finding:** The carrier form is architecture, not just a form. When a managing partner answers the attestation questions, they are creating a documented standard — a representation about what their practice does. The form doesn't require evidence at signing. But the architecture assumes evidence exists. The managing partner signs believing they are acknowledging something, while actually they are committing to a standard they cannot demonstrate they meet. The exposure is active, documented, and personal.

**Mechanism:** Carrier attestation → personal signature → "adequate supervision" representation → no documentation created → carrier asks for evidence at renewal → firm cannot produce documentation → attestation becomes evidence of inadequate governance.

**Implication:** The service is not "help me complete the form." The service is "help me produce the evidence the form architecture assumes I have." This distinction is critical — the form was never asking for acknowledgment, it was asking for evidence.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The form wasn't asking you to acknowledge something. It was asking you to commit to a standard. And now the carrier wants to see if you can prove you meet it."

---

### Finding 02 — Article 321 CC Vendor Contract Review Is a Discrete and Urgent Service

**The finding:** Article 321 of the Swiss Criminal Code creates professional secrecy obligations that apply to all information in professional context. AI vendor contracts involve data processing on vendor servers — potentially in non-Swiss jurisdictions. The vendor terms of service were accepted without review against Article 321 obligations. The managing partner doesn't know whether their AI tool use violates professional secrecy. The exposure is active, specific, and undocumented. No Geneva-specific vendor contract review methodology exists.

**Mechanism:** Tool adoption → data processed on vendor servers → vendor contract accepted without review → Article 321 exposure unknown → no documentation of vendor contract analysis → if challenged, cannot demonstrate compliance.

**Implication:** A systematic Article 321 vendor contract review is a discrete service for Geneva law firms. The review produces documentation of: where data goes, who can access it, what obligations the vendor has, whether professional secrecy is protected.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your AI vendor processes data on their servers. Their terms of service — which you accepted — define what they can do with that data. Article 321 requires you to know whether that conflicts with your professional secrecy obligations. Most firms haven't looked. That's your exposure."

### Finding 03 — Retention Debt Has No Geneva-Defined Stop

**The finding:** AI tools are used in almost every legal matter. There's no record of which tools, which prompts, what data was processed, or what decisions were made with AI assistance. The retention period for these records is undefined — no Geneva guidance, no carrier guidance, no bar guidance. Every matter adds to the compounding debt. The retention problem is not "how long should I keep this?" — it is "the debt is accumulating and I don't know how to stop it."

**Mechanism:** Matter completed → AI used → no documentation of AI use → retention period undefined → next matter adds more → compounding debt with no static state and no Geneva-defined stop.

**Implication:** A Geneva-specific AI retention protocol for legal matters is a discrete service. The protocol defines: what records to keep, how long, in what format, and when the debt stops accumulating.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Every matter adds to your retention debt. You're not sure how long you need to keep records of AI use. There's no Geneva guidance. I can give you a protocol."

### Finding 04 — Client Intake AI Question Reveals Engagement Letter AI Silence

**The finding:** Clients are starting to ask during intake whether AI will be used on their case and how their information will be protected. Engagement letters have vague "technology-assisted tools" clauses that don't actually address AI governance. The managing partner cannot give a clear, confident answer. The engagement letter — the foundational contract — is silent on the most significant operational risk in modern legal practice.

**Mechanism:** Client intake → client asks about AI → engagement letter has vague clause → managing partner cannot explain AI governance → client loses confidence → relationship starts with uncertainty.

**Implication:** An engagement letter AI clause framework is a discrete service. The clause is specific — it names AI tools, defines data processing obligations, allocates risk, and builds client trust through transparency.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your engagement letter says 'technology-assisted tools.' It doesn't say anything about AI. Your clients are starting to ask specifically. I can give you a clause that actually covers what you're doing."

---

### Finding 05 — Junior-Senior Fluency Inversion Creates Supervisory Liability Without Supervision Capability

**The finding:** Associates use AI more fluently than the managing partner. The managing partner doesn't know what data associates are entering into AI tools, what vendor servers that data goes to, or what the vendors are doing with the prompts. The carrier attestation covers supervision of AI usage in the practice — including by associates. The managing partner is personally accountable for supervision they cannot perform. The accountability and the capability are inversely related.

**Mechanism:** Associate uses AI → managing partner doesn't know what AI tools, what data, what vendors → carrier asks about supervision → managing partner personally accountable → cannot demonstrate supervision occurred → attestation creates exposure.

**Implication:** A supervision documentation framework that enables accountability visibility without requiring fluency is a discrete service. The managing director doesn't need to understand AI — they need to be able to document that supervision occurred.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You're responsible for supervising AI use in your practice. Your associates are more fluent than you. You can't supervise what you don't understand. But you can document it. I can show you how."

---

### Finding 06 — "Adequate" Remains Undefined and That Undefinition Is the First-Mover Opportunity

**The finding:** No Geneva-specific, size-specific, operational definition of "adequate AI governance" exists for 2–5 attorney law firms. The carrier asks for "adequate" without defining it. The FSA has acknowledged the obligation exists without defining it. The managing partner cannot determine whether they meet a standard that has never been defined. First to define becomes the authority.

**Mechanism:** Carrier asks for adequate → FSA acknowledges obligation → no definition exists → managing partner operates in uncertainty → if challenged, no standard to defend against → first to define controls the standard.

**Implication:** Defining "adequate" for small Geneva firms is the long-term brand moat. The first to publish a specific, operational definition that the carrier form architecture assumes will become the reference standard.

**Ready-to-use language:** "No one has defined what 'adequate' means for a two-to-five attorney Geneva firm. The carrier assumes it exists. It doesn't. I can define it for you — and that's how you become the standard."
