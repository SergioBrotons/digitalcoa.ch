# Market Signals: Geneva Legal — C308 Phase 01

## Current Problems, Complaints, Failures — C308

---

## Signal 01: Matter-Level AI Use Documentation is the Carrier's Actual Ask — Firms Have Only Firm-Level Documentation

**Signal:** Carriers are increasingly asking not just for AI governance policies but for matter-level evidence of AI use. The specific question at renewal is no longer "do you have an AI policy?" It is "show us how AI was used in specific client matters." The documentation gap is not at the firm level — firms can produce a policy. The gap is at the matter level: which AI tools were used, on which client matters, with what supervision, and with what evidence of human authorization.

**Evidence pattern:** Carrier renewal conversations reporting specific questions about matter-level AI use. Managing partners reporting that they cannot demonstrate which AI tools were used in which client files. The firm-level policy satisfies the surface request but fails the follow-up.

**Market implication:** The service need shifts from "AI governance policy" to "matter-level AI use documentation system." The managing partner needs to be able to produce, on demand, a matter-by-matter record of AI use authorization and supervision. This is a fundamentally different documentation architecture than firm-wide policy documentation.

**Commercial angle:** "Your carrier is asking for matter-level records, not firm-level policies. I can build the documentation system that lets you show, for each client file, who authorized the AI use, what the AI did, and how you supervised it."

## Signal 02: Associate Shadow AI Creates Unmanaged Vendor Relationships on Firm Matters

**Signal:** Associates at small Geneva firms are using personal AI tool subscriptions on firm client matters — without the managing partner's knowledge, without firm-level authorization, and without the vendor contract having been reviewed against firm obligations. The personal subscription means: the vendor contract is between the associate and the vendor, not between the firm and the vendor. The firm has no Data Processing Agreement with the vendor. The associate's personal data and the firm's client data are co-mingled in the vendor's system. No Geneva service addresses this specific exposure.

**Evidence pattern:** Associates using personal ChatGPT, Claude, or other AI accounts to process client work. Managing partners discovering this retrospectively. The vendor contract that governs the associate's personal account was accepted by the associate personally — not reviewed for firm-level professional obligations.

**Market implication:** Shadow AI by associates creates three distinct problems: (1) vendor contract is personal, not firm-level, (2) client matter data is processed through a personal account, potentially co-mingling personal and professional data, (3) the firm has no visibility into which AI tools are processing client data. The service need is both a tool authorization framework and a vendor contract audit.

**Commercial angle:** "Your associates are using personal AI subscriptions on client matters — and the vendor contract is between them and the vendor, not between your firm and the vendor. I need to audit every AI tool touching your client files, authorized or not."

## Signal 03: Geneva Civil Procedure Has No AI Disclosure Protocol — Creating Unresolved Discovery Exposure

**Signal:** When a Geneva small firm using AI tools enters civil litigation, there is no established cantonal procedure for disclosing AI use in client matters. The Federal Code of Civil Procedure (CPC) requires disclosure of evidence and documents material to the dispute. If AI was used in preparing a brief, conducting legal research, drafting correspondence, or analyzing documents, the question of what to disclose — and to whom — is unanswered. No Geneva court has issued guidance. No Geneva bar opinion exists. The managing partner preparing for litigation does not know what AI use records to preserve.

**Evidence pattern:** Managing partners reporting uncertainty about what AI disclosure obligations exist in active litigation. No cantonal guidance from the Geneva courts on AI disclosure. OFL silence on the question. No Geneva-specific litigation AI protocol available from any source.

**Market implication:** Pre-litigation AI governance documentation is the only protection. The managing partner who builds matter-level AI use records before litigation creates evidence of appropriate supervision and authorization. The managing partner who does not build these records has nothing to disclose — and the absence of records may itself be treated as evidence of inadequate governance.

**Commercial angle:** "If your client matter goes to litigation, the court may ask what AI was used and how. You need matter-level records before the question is asked in a courtroom — not after."

## Signal 04: Swissdamed Timing Creates Documented Confusion — The Regulation Doesn't Cover Legal AI

**Signal:** Swissdamed (the Swiss medical device registry) has a September 2026 implementation date for medical device scope. However, the general regulatory awareness around Swissdamed's AI provisions is creating confusion in the Geneva legal market: attorneys are assuming Swissdamed applies to their AI tool use, when in fact Swissdamed's current scope covers medical devices specifically, not general AI applications for legal services. The confusion is causing two problems: (1) firms are waiting for Swissdamed to clarify their obligations, delaying action on AI governance they should be building now; (2) clients are asking about Swissdamed as if it covers their attorney's AI use, creating client communication pressure on firms that don't have answers.

**Evidence pattern:** Managing partners referencing Swissdamed as the reason they are "waiting" to take AI governance action. English-speaking clients asking whether the firm is Swissdamed-compliant, conflating medical device scope with general AI accountability. Brokers reinforcing the Swissdamed-waiting behavior.

**Market implication:** The Swissdamed confusion creates both a delay risk and a client communication risk. The service need is: (1) clarify what Swissdamed does and does not cover for legal services, (2) build the AI governance that Swissdamed does not require, before Swissdamed creates a false sense of security. The confusion is exploitable — firms that understand the distinction act faster and better.

**Commercial angle:** "Swissdamed doesn't cover legal AI — it covers medical devices. But your clients may not know the difference, and they're asking. I can give you the distinction and the framework for what you actually need to build."

## Signal 05: Insurance Renewal Window is a Distinct Trigger from Initial Exposure Recognition — Different Emotional State

**Signal:** The carrier attestation trigger at initial contact and the renewal window trigger are producing different emotional states and different service readiness. At initial contact, the ICP is in shock and discovery mode. At renewal, the ICP has had time to investigate, has talked to peers, and is arriving with specific questions. The renewal ICP is not the same buyer as the initial-attestation ICP. Renewal conversations report: more specific questions about what documentation to produce, greater peer-influenced anxiety, more developed understanding of the personal liability stakes, and greater urgency.

**Evidence pattern:** Renewal conversations reporting more specific questions than initial attestation contact. Managing partners citing peer conversations as the source of their urgency. Renewal as the point where managing partners "started taking it seriously." Renewal window as the point where firms that did nothing after initial attestation are forced to confront the gap again.

**Market implication:** The renewal window is a distinct market entry moment. The service positioning for renewal should be different from initial contact: more technical, more peer-aware, more action-sequenced. The ICP arriving at renewal has already done preliminary investigation and needs a specific implementation sequence, not a problem-framing conversation.

**Commercial angle:** "The renewal window is coming. You got the first form, you didn't do much. This time, the carrier is going to ask differently — and I know exactly what they're going to ask for."

## Signal 06: Matter-Type AI Use Taxonomy Creates Documentation Uniformity That Doesn't Match Practice Reality

**Signal:** Small Geneva firms handle multiple practice types — litigation, corporate/M&A, employment, real estate, family law — with fundamentally different AI risk profiles. Litigation matters involve court disclosure obligations. Corporate matters involve deal confidentiality and privileged deal communications. Employment matters involve employee personal data. Real estate matters involve property data and financial information. Family matters involve the highest sensitivity personal data. Yet firms are treating AI governance as a single firm-wide policy that applies uniformly across all matter types.

**Evidence pattern:** Managing partners describing their AI policy as "one document that covers everything." No differentiation by matter type in AI governance documentation. No consideration that litigation matters require different documentation than transactional matters. No recognition that client data sensitivity varies by matter type.

**Market implication:** The matter-type taxonomy gap means that the firm's documentation is likely inadequate for its highest-risk matters (litigation, family, employment) while potentially excessive for lower-risk matters (routine real estate transactions). The service need is a matter-type risk stratification that maps AI governance requirements to specific practice areas — within a single firm.

**Commercial angle:** "Your litigation matters and your corporate matters have different AI risks and different disclosure obligations. One firm-wide policy doesn't cover both. I can build the matter-specific documentation that matches your actual practice."
