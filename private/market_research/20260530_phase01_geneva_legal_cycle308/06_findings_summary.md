### Finding 01: Matter-Level AI Use Documentation is the Carrier's Actual Ask — Not Firm-Level Policy

**The finding:** The carrier attestation question has evolved from "do you have an AI policy?" to "show us matter by matter what AI you used." The documentation gap is not at the firm level — firms that built a policy in response to initial attestation can produce it. The gap is at the matter level: no record exists for each client file showing which AI tool was used, on what, with what supervision, and how a human authorized the output. This is a fundamentally different documentation architecture than firm-level policy documentation. The managing partner who can produce a firm-level policy but cannot produce a matter-level AI use record is under-prepared for the renewal question.

**Mechanism:** Carrier attestation at renewal → carrier asks for matter-level AI use evidence → firm has only firm-level policy → documentation gap confirmed → carrier has grounds to deny coverage or condition renewal on specific documentation.

**Implication:** The service deliverable shifts from "AI governance policy" to "matter-level AI use documentation system." The output is a lightweight per-matter record architecture that attorneys can use practically — not a compliance framework, but an operational documentation tool.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your carrier is asking for matter-level records, not firm-level policies. I can build the documentation system that lets you show, for each client file, who authorized the AI use, what the AI did, and how you supervised it."

### Finding 02: Associate Personal AI Subscriptions Create Unmanaged Vendor Relationships on Firm Matters

**The finding:** Associates at small Geneva firms are using personal AI tool subscriptions on firm client matters without firm-level authorization, without a vendor contract between the firm and the vendor, and without the managing partner's knowledge. The vendor contract is between the associate personally and the AI vendor — it is not a firm contract, does not bind the firm, and was not reviewed for the firm's professional obligations. The associate's personal data and the firm's client data are co-mingled in the vendor's system. This creates three distinct exposure categories simultaneously: nFADP DPA exposure (no firm-level DPA exists), Article 321 CC exposure (client data processed through an unauthorized third-party system), and carrier attestation exposure (unauthorized AI tools in use on client matters that the firm cannot document).

**Mechanism:** Associate uses personal AI subscription on client matter → managing partner unaware → no firm-level vendor contract exists → no DPA reviewed → client data flows through personal associate account → carrier asks about AI tools at renewal → firm cannot document the tool → exposure confirmed.

**Implication:** The associate shadow AI problem is a discrete structural exposure that requires a specific audit and authorization framework — not general AI governance training. The service requires: (1) identifying all personal AI subscriptions in use on firm matters, (2) mapping each vendor relationship to firm obligations, (3) recommending which tools to authorize, restrict, or replace, (4) establishing a vendor authorization framework for ongoing associate AI use.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your associates are using personal AI subscriptions on client matters — and the vendor contract is between them and the vendor, not between your firm and the vendor. I need to audit every AI tool touching your client files, authorized or not."

### Finding 03: Geneva Civil Procedure Has No AI Disclosure Protocol — Creating Unresolved Litigation Exposure

**The finding:** When a Geneva small firm using AI tools enters civil litigation, no established cantonal procedure exists for disclosing AI use in client matters. The Federal Code of Civil Procedure (CPC) requires disclosure of evidence and documents material to the dispute. Whether AI-generated work product, AI-assisted research, or AI-processed documents constitute CPC-disclosable materials is genuinely unanswered in Geneva. No Geneva court has issued guidance. No OFL advisory opinion exists. The managing partner preparing for litigation does not know what AI use records to preserve, what to disclose voluntarily, or what the opponent can compel. The absence of guidance is not permission — it is uncertainty that shifts all risk to the managing partner who has not prepared.

**Mechanism:** Matter goes to litigation → opposing counsel asks about AI use in the matter → no Geneva court protocol exists → managing partner has no guidance on what to disclose → matter-level AI use records absent → inadequate governance inferred from absence of records → adverse inference possible.

**Implication:** Pre-litigation AI governance documentation is the only defensible position. The managing partner who builds matter-level AI use records before a dispute arises has something to show. The managing partner who does not build records has nothing — and the absence of records may itself be treated as evidence of inadequate governance. The service need is a pre-litigation preparation framework that produces defensible matter-level records without requiring court guidance that does not exist.

**Ready-to-use language:** "If your client matter goes to litigation, the court may ask what AI was used and how. You need matter-level records before the question is asked in a courtroom — not after."

### Finding 04: Swissdamed Timing Creates Documented Confusion — The Regulation Does Not Cover Legal AI

**The finding:** Swissdamed (Swiss Medical Device Registry) has a September 2026 implementation date for medical device scope under the Therapeutic Products Act (TPA). However, the regulatory awareness around Swissdamed is creating confusion in the Geneva legal market: attorneys are assuming Swissdamed applies to their AI tool use, when Swissdamed's scope covers medical devices, not general AI applications for legal services. This confusion is causing firms to delay action on AI governance obligations that already exist under nFADP and Article 321 CC, waiting instead for a regulation that does not apply to them. Simultaneously, English-speaking clients are asking Geneva firms about Swissdamed compliance, conflating medical device scope with general AI accountability — and firms have no calibrated response.

**Mechanism:** Swissdamed September 2026 deadline approaches → Geneva firms assume it covers their AI tools → firms delay AI governance action waiting for Swissdamed clarification → nFADP and Article 321 CC obligations remain unaddressed during waiting period → clients ask about Swissdamed compliance → firms give vague answers → client relationship friction.

**Implication:** Swissdamed scope clarification is a discrete service deliverable — a brief memo that explains what Swissdamed covers (medical devices only), what it does not cover (legal AI), and what AI governance obligations apply to Geneva attorneys regardless of Swissdamed. The clarification releases firms from the waiting posture and creates urgency around obligations that actually exist.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Swissdamed doesn't cover legal AI — it covers medical devices. But your clients may not know the difference, and they're asking. I can give you the distinction and the framework for what you actually need to build."

### Finding 05: Insurance Renewal Window is a Distinct Trigger from Initial Attestation — Different ICP Psychology

**The finding:** The carrier attestation trigger at initial contact and the renewal window trigger produce different emotional states and different service readiness in the ICP. At initial contact, the ICP is in shock, discovery, and confusion. At renewal, the ICP has had time to investigate, has talked to peers, and is arriving with specific questions and a peer-informed sense of urgency. The renewal ICP is not the same buyer as the initial-attestation ICP. The renewal ICP has heard from peers about what carriers are asking, has begun to understand the personal liability stakes more precisely, and needs a specific action sequence — not a problem-framing conversation.

**Mechanism:** Initial attestation signed → ICP processes personally → peer conversations at bar events → ICP learns that others received the same form and don't know what to do → ICP investigates further → renewal arrives with carrier asking more specific questions → peer-informed urgency activates → service adoption decision triggered at renewal, not at initial attestation.

**Implication:** The renewal window is a distinct market entry moment that requires different positioning than initial contact. The renewal ICP needs: more technical language, peer-aware framing, specific documentation demands mapped to carrier question patterns, and a clear action sequence. The service that positions for renewal as a specific moment — rather than treating it as another initial contact — converts more effectively.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The renewal window is coming. You got the first form, you didn't do much. This time, the carrier is going to ask differently — and I know exactly what they're going to ask for."

### Finding 06: Matter-Type AI Use Taxonomy Creates Documentation Uniformity That Doesn't Match Practice Reality

**The finding:** Small Geneva firms handle multiple practice types — litigation, corporate/M&A, employment, family, real estate — with fundamentally different AI risk profiles, different client data sensitivities, and potentially different disclosure obligations. Yet firms are treating AI governance as a single firm-wide policy that applies uniformly across all matter types. This uniformity creates a specific problem: the firm's documentation is likely inadequate for its highest-risk matters (litigation, family, employment) while potentially excessive for lower-risk matters (routine real estate). The matter-type taxonomy gap means the firm's "adequate" AI governance may be simultaneously inadequate for litigation matters and unnecessary for simple transactional matters.

**Mechanism:** Firm builds one AI governance policy → applies uniformly to all matter types → litigation matter goes to court → court asks about AI disclosure in litigation context → firm's uniform policy does not address litigation-specific disclosure requirements → inadequate documentation confirmed retroactively.

**Implication:** The service deliverable is a matter-type risk stratification matrix — mapping which AI uses require what level of documentation, authorization, and supervision for each practice area. The output is a tiered documentation system: what minimum record is required for all matters, what additional documentation is required for litigation matters, what additional requirements apply to employment and family matters. The stratification produces both appropriate protection and operational efficiency.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your litigation matters and your corporate matters have different AI risks and different disclosure obligations. One firm-wide policy doesn't cover both. I can build the matter-specific documentation that matches your actual practice."
