# ICP Language: Geneva Legal — C308 Phase 01

## How the ICP Talks About AI Problems — C308

---

## Primary Language Register

The Geneva legal ICP at C308 speaks in a specific register shaped by three accumulated experiences:

1. They signed something they did not fully understand
2. They discovered that the associate knows more about AI than they do
3. They now need to do something — but they are not sure what comes first

The ICP no longer asks "am I exposed?" They ask "what do I do now, and in what order?" This is the decisive language shift from C307 to C308.

---

## The Carrier Attestation Language (What They Signed)

The ICP describes the carrier attestation in very specific terms:

- **"They sent me a form"** — not "I received a compliance request"
- **"I had to sign it"** — not "I attested to AI governance standards"
- **"I didn't know what I was signing"** — not "I was uncertain about the attestation requirements"
- **"It was my personal signature"** — the word "personal" is now loaded
- **"I think it means something"** — uncertainty about what exactly was committed

The ICP understands the carrier form as a document that created personal exposure, not a document that created compliance. This is the foundational language event.

---

## The AI Tool Language (What's in Use)

The ICP describes their AI tool situation in terms of discovery rather than management:

- **"I didn't know we were using that"** — discovery of associate AI use
- **"They brought their own tools"** — personal subscriptions used on firm matters
- **"I can't evaluate what it produces"** — inability to substantively review AI output
- **"I just review the final version"** — supervisory review without AI-specific evaluation
- **"I don't know what tools are on my systems"** — inventory gap

The language is retrospective discovery, not prospective management. The ICP is describing what they found, not what they designed.

---

## The Vendor Contract Language (What They Accepted)

The ICP describes vendor relationships in terms of acceptance without review:

- **"We just clicked through"** — standard Terms of Service acceptance
- **"The contract is probably fine"** — assumption of vendor adequacy
- **"I didn't read it"** — standard legal professional accepting unread contracts
- **"It's a big company"** — brand trust as contract adequacy proxy
- **"I didn't know we needed a specific agreement"** — no DPA understanding

The language reveals that standard legal professional habits (read the contract, understand the terms) were not applied to AI vendor relationships. The ICP now suspects this was a mistake.

---

## The Privilege Language (What They Fear)

The ICP connects vendor access to privilege in intuitive but imprecise terms:

- **"If the AI sees the file, who else sees it?"** — vendor access concern
- **"I don't think that client information should be going to a server somewhere"** — data residency concern
- **"We deal with sensitive matters"** — privilege as assumed context
- **"The client didn't consent to that"** — implied consent gap
- **"I don't know if this creates a problem for me"** — unresolved uncertainty

The privilege language is intuitive and imprecise. The ICP understands that something uncomfortable happened with client data and AI tools, but they cannot name the specific mechanism.

---

## The Documentation Language (What They Don't Have)

The ICP describes their documentation situation in terms of absence:

- **"We don't have anything written down"** — no AI governance documentation
- **"I don't know what we would write"** — no documentation framework
- **"The form asked for things we couldn't show"** — documentation gap visible in attestation
- **"I don't want to create a record that makes it worse"** — documentation fear
- **"I have nothing to show the carrier if they ask"** — evidence absence

The documentation language reveals paralysis born from uncertainty about what to create and fear that created documents will be used against the firm.

---

## The Bilingual Client Language (What They Can't Say)

The ICP describes client communication in terms of evasion:

- **"My French clients don't ask"** — no proactive AI disclosure to French clients
- **"My English clients ask more directly"** — English-speaking clients surface AI questions
- **"I give a vague answer"** — no calibrated response exists
- **"I don't know what to put in the engagement letter"** — no AI clause language
- **"In French it feels awkward to even mention it"** — cultural framing problem

The bilingual language reveals that two different evasion patterns exist: passive (French clients don't ask) and active (English clients ask and get vague answers). Neither is a real answer.

---

## The Authority Language (Who They Fear)

The ICP maps authority concern in a specific hierarchy:

1. **Ordre des Avocats de Genève (OFL)** — "They could take my license"
2. **Carrier** — "They could not renew, or could deny coverage"
3. **FDPIC** — "They could investigate us"
4. **Clients** — "They could complain or sue"
5. **Civil courts** — "If we go to trial, what do we disclose?"

The OFL is the primary fear. The personal license is the ultimate stake. Carrier and FDPIC are proximate risks. Client civil exposure is the operational reality.

---

## The Timing Language (When They Feel It)

The ICP experiences AI governance concern as calendar-driven:

- **"Renewal season"** — carrier attestation timing as acute trigger
- **"When something goes wrong"** — incident-driven recognition
- **"When the associate tells me what they used"** — discovery trigger
- **"When I read about another law firm having a problem"** — peer example as trigger
- **"Before the OFL says something"** — anticipatory anxiety about bar guidance

The timing language reveals that the ICP needs to see the problem as actionable NOW. "Someday" language doesn't convert. Calendar-bound language does.

---

## The Action Language (What They Think They Need)

The ICP describes next steps in very specific terms:

- **"I need someone to tell me what to do"** — sequential action guidance
- **"I need to know what's actually required"** — standard definition
- **"I need something I can actually implement"** — operational practicality
- **"I need something that works for a small firm"** — scale recognition
- **"I need someone who understands Geneva"** — jurisdiction specificity

The action language reveals that the ICP is past education. They need a sequence: what to do first, what second, what third. The ICP needs a decision framework, not a principles document.

---

## The Geneva-Specific Language Markers

The ICP uses Geneva-specific professional language that reveals their context:

- **"Article 321"** — the Code Civil provision on professional secrecy, now actively invoked
- **"Ordre"** — the Geneva bar, as a named authority and fear object
- **"CCP"** — the Code of Civil Procedure, as the discovery context
- **"nFADP"** — the Swiss data protection law, increasingly cited
- **"Attestation carrier"** — the insurance carrier attestation form, as the specific document that created exposure
- **"Genevois"** — the Geneva-specific professional context, as distinct from Zurich or Bern

---

## What the ICP Does NOT Say

The ICP language reveals what they do not yet use:

- They do not say "evidence architecture"
- They do not say "supervision framework"
- They do not say "vendor privilege review"
- They do not say "cross-authority documentation design"
- They do not say "matter-level AI use log"
- They do not say "Article 321 CC compliance mapping"

The gap between what the ICP experiences and what the market offers is the naming gap. The service that names the gap in the ICP's language wins the positioning.
