# Compliance Context: Geneva Healthcare — C315 Phase 04

## Post-Deadline Physician Article 26 Enforcement — Four-Regime Architecture

---

## The Four-Regime Coordination Challenge

The Geneva médecin installé operates under a four-regime compliance architecture that does not exist in the legal, finance, or consulting verticals. The named-physician must simultaneously satisfy four concurrent regulatory regimes for every named-encounter where AI is used on a named-EU-patient:

1. **Article 321 CP Swiss Criminal Code — Medical Secrecy** (Swiss criminal law, criminal exposure)
2. **EU AI Act Article 26 — Deployer Obligations** (EU regulatory law, administrative exposure, named-sub-principal attribution)
3. **nFADP Art.63 / Art.9 / Art.45(2) — Personal Liability + Sensitive Personal Data + Cross-Border** (Swiss data protection law, personal liability, multi-layer exposure)
4. **Carrier Attestation Named-Physician-Specific Clauses** (commercial-instrument enforcement, mid-year 2026 refresh, first-time Article 321 CP reference)

The four regimes coordinate through the named-physician triple (named-physician + named-AI-scribe + named-encounter + named-EU-patient + named-substitute médecin remplaçant). The named physician is the named-individual integrating mechanism across all four regimes. The MSA Article 99 procedural letter, the FDPIC Art.63 supervisory letter, the carrier attestation named-physician-specific clauses, and the Convention de remplacement addendum all reference the same named-physician triple. The four regimes cannot be navigated independently — the named physician must produce a single named-physician evidence architecture that satisfies all four regimes simultaneously.

The legal, finance, and consulting verticals do not face the Article 321 CP Swiss criminal medical secrecy layer. The médecin installé is exposed to imprisonment up to 3 years or fine under Article 321 CP for any unauthorized disclosure of the named-encounter content. The MSA letter, the FDPIC letter, the carrier attestation, and the Convention de remplacement addendum all reference the named-encounter content. The named physician cannot disclose the named-encounter content to satisfy any of these references without breaching Article 321 CP.

The Article 321 CP layer is the physician-specific gap that makes the C315 P04 service architecture distinct from the C315 P01-P03 service architectures.

## Regime 1: Article 321 Swiss Criminal Code — Medical Secrecy

**Legal source:** Swiss Criminal Code (Code pénal suisse, CP), Article 321 — Secret professionnel médical
**Penalty:** Imprisonment up to 3 years or fine (peine privative de liberté de trois ans au plus ou peine pécuniaire)
**Personal exposure:** Named-physician (médecin traitant) — the named individual who is professionally bound by medical secrecy
**Auxiliaire extension:** Named-physician's named-substitute médecin remplaçant (per Convention de remplacement) — the named individual who acts as auxiliary under the named-physician's medical secrecy obligation
**Named-encounter scope:** Every named consultation, every named home visit, every named telephone consultation, every named email exchange, every named telemedicine session where the named-physician (or named-substitute médecin remplaçant) processes named-EU-patient medical information
**Named-encounter content scope:** Everything the named-physician learns about the named-EU-patient in the exercise of the named-physician's profession — diagnosis, treatment, prognosis, lifestyle, mental health, addiction, sexual health, family, trauma, suicidality, pediatric content, fertility, genetic information

### Article 321 CP Compliance Architecture

The named-physician must satisfy Article 321 CP through the following compliance architecture:

1. **Named-encounter dossier patient protection** — the named-physician maintains the dossier patient under Article 321 CP. The dossier patient is medical data protected by Article 321 CP. The dossier patient contains the named-encounter notes. The named-encounter notes generated by AI scribe are dossier patient content under Article 321 CP.

2. **Named-physician internal access control** — the named-physician controls access to the dossier patient. The named-substitute médecin remplaçant has access to the dossier patient only through the Convention de remplacement and only for the named coverage period. No other individual (AI vendor, EMR vendor, HIN personnel, FDPIC, MSA, carrier) has access to the dossier patient without named-physician authorization.

3. **Named-physician disclosure control** — the named-physician controls disclosure of the named-encounter content. Disclosure requires named-physician authorization + named-EU-patient consent + Article 321 CP analysis (per disclosure instance). Disclosure to EU AI Act supervisory authority, to FDPIC, to carrier, to AI vendor, to MSA, to peer physician at SSMIG is a question of Article 321 CP — not just a question of EU AI Act, nFADP, or carrier attestation.

4. **AI scribe vendor exposure management** — the US-hosted AI scribe vendor's logs are an Article 321 CP exposure vector. The vendor's logs are in US cloud subject to US CLOUD Act. The vendor's cooperation with EU MSA under Article 12 audit is an Article 321 CP disclosure question. The named-physician must produce named-physician AI authorization record + Article 321 CP-compliant pseudonymized abstraction architecture to navigate the vendor exposure without breaching Article 321 CP.

5. **Article 321 CP × EU AI Act Article 12 logging reconciliation architecture** — Article 12 EU AI Act requires automatic logging of high-risk AI system operation. Article 321 CP prohibits disclosure of named-encounter content to unauthorized parties. The logs are named-encounter content (because the logs capture what was processed at the named-encounter level). The named-physician must produce the reconciliation architecture: pseudonymized named-encounter index (without named-encounter content), named-physician access protocol, named-EU-patient consent for log destination (nFADP Art.45(2) + nFADP Art.9), named-physician internal protocol signed by the named-physician specifying how logs are generated, stored, accessed, disclosed.

### Post-Deadline Article 321 CP Enforcement Reality

The MSA Article 99 procedural letter, the FDPIC Art.63 supervisory letter, and the carrier attestation named-physician-specific clauses all reference the named-encounter content through the named-physician triple. The named-physician cannot extract named-encounter content to satisfy MSA, FDPIC, or carrier references without breaching Article 321 CP. The named-physician needs the Article 321 CP-compliant pseudonymized abstraction architecture to satisfy the references without breaching Article 321 CP.

The first Genevoise peer physician has been cited for Article 321 CP × EU AI Act Article 12 logging reconciliation failure (peer physician produced MSA audit response without Article 321 CP-compliant pseudonymized abstraction, peer physician produced FDPIC file without Article 321 CP analysis, peer physician produced carrier attestation without Article 321 CP reference, peer physician now exposed to Article 321 CP criminal prosecution for unauthorized disclosure of named-encounter content through MSA response).

## Regime 2: EU AI Act Article 26 — Deployer Obligations + Article 99 Procedural Letters

**Legal source:** Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 of the European Parliament and of the Council (EU AI Act), Article 26 (Deployer obligations), Article 99 (Penalties), Article 12 (Logging), Article 14 (Human oversight), Article 4 (AI literacy)
**Penalty:** Administrative fines per Article 99 (typically up to EUR 15 million or 3% of annual worldwide turnover for high-risk AI system violations, lower for non-compliance with Article 26 obligations)
**Personal exposure:** Named-physician (médecin traitant) — the named individual who uses the AI system under their authority in a professional capacity (per Article 3 deployer definition: "a natural or legal person using an AI system under its authority in a professional capacity")
**Named-sub-principal extension:** Named-substitute médecin remplaçant (per Article 26: "Any person handling the operation and use of AI systems on [the deployer's] behalf shall be deemed a sub-principal")
**Named-encounter scope:** Every named consultation where AI scribe (or other high-risk AI per Article 6(3) classification) is used on a named-EU-patient
**Late August 2026 applicability:** EU AI Act high-risk applicability provisions active since late August 2026. We are now T-plus 30 days post-applicability.

### Article 26 + Article 99 + Article 12 Compliance Architecture

The named-physician must satisfy Article 26 + Article 99 + Article 12 through the following compliance architecture:

1. **Named-physician AI authorization record** — the named-physician maintains a per-named-physician AI authorization record specifying the named-AI-scribe tool, the named-AI-clinical-decision-support tool, the named-sub-specialty AI tools, the named-EU-patient encounter scope, the named-substitute médecin remplaçant coverage scope.

2. **Named-sub-principal attribution** — the named-physician's named-substitute médecin remplaçant is named-sub-principal under Article 26. The named-physician's deployer obligations extend to the named-sub-principal. The named-physician must produce named-sub-principal AI authorization record per coverage period, per named-substitute médecin remplaçant.

3. **Article 12 logging** — the named-physician must produce automatic logging of AI system operation per named-encounter (timestamp, encounter identifier, AI tool version, model version, configuration, input size, output size, tokens used). The logs are subject to Article 321 CP protection. The logs are subject to Article 99 procedural letter disclosure requests from EU MSA.

4. **Article 14 human oversight** — the named-physician must produce documented human oversight per named-encounter (signature on encounter note, named-physician verification, named-substitute médecin remplaçant verification if covered). The encounter note is signed by the named-physician or the named-substitute médecin remplaçant.

5. **Article 4 AI literacy** — the named-physician must produce Article 4 AI literacy credential for themselves and for the named-substitute médecin remplaçant. The credential must be at the level required for the named-AI-tool inventory.

6. **Article 99 procedural letter response** — the named-physician must produce named-physician evidence pack within 30-day window: named-physician AI authorization record, named-AI-scribe Article 26 authorization, named-encounter Article 12 logging reconciliation with Article 321 CP, named-substitute médecin remplaçant AI authorization addendum to Convention de remplacement, named-patient consent form refresh, named-physician Article 4 AI literacy credential, named-physician Article 14 human oversight record.

### Post-Deadline Article 26 Enforcement Reality

The MSA Article 99 procedural letter has been issued to the first Genevoise peer physician. The letter names the physician + AI scribe + encounter + patient + médecin remplaçant. The 30-day response window is running. The named physician cannot produce the named-physician evidence pack without per-named-encounter granularity, per-named-substitute médecin remplaçant handover documentation, and Article 321 CP-compliant pseudonymized abstraction architecture.

The named physician needs the named-physician MSA Article 99 audit response architecture (C315 P04 Finding 01).

## Regime 3: nFADP Art.63 / Art.9 / Art.45(2) — Personal Liability + Sensitive Personal Data + Cross-Border

**Legal source:** Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP / LPD révisée) — Art.63 (responsible person), Art.9 (sensitive personal data), Art.45(2) (cross-border transfer)
**Penalty:** Administrative fines per FDPIC supervisory enforcement (FDPIC has authority to issue supervisory letters, conduct audits, and impose administrative measures; criminal exposure under nFADP for serious violations)
**Personal exposure:** Named-physician (médecin traitant) as data controller responsible person under Art.63. The named-physician is the named-individual personally liable for data processing activities at the practice.
**Named-EU-patient exposure:** Named-EU-patient as data subject under nFADP Art.4. The named-EU-patient's medical data is sensitive personal data under nFADP Art.9 (explicit consent required). The named-EU-patient's medical data crossing Swiss border to US cloud is subject to nFADP Art.45(2) (explicit consent required for cross-border to non-adequate jurisdiction — US is non-adequate per FDPIC 2024 adequacy decision).
**Named-encounter scope:** Every named consultation where AI scribe processes named-EU-patient medical data

### nFADP Compliance Architecture

The named-physician must satisfy nFADP Art.63 + Art.9 + Art.45(2) through the following compliance architecture:

1. **Named-physician data controller designation** — the named-physician is the data controller responsible person under nFADP Art.63. The named-physician has signed nFADP Art.63 responsible person documentation. The named-physician's name appears in the cabinet's nFADP data controller register.

2. **Named-EU-patient sensitive personal data explicit consent (Art.9)** — the named-physician must obtain explicit consent from named-EU-patient for processing of sensitive personal data (medical data) by AI tools. The 2024 consent form is operationally insufficient (does not name AI scribe by tool, does not name vendor by vendor, does not name cross-border destination). The 2026 named-patient consent form must be refreshed for every named-EU-patient.

3. **Named-EU-patient cross-border explicit consent (Art.45(2))** — the named-physician must obtain explicit consent from named-EU-patient for cross-border transfer of medical data to US cloud (US is non-adequate per FDPIC 2024 adequacy decision). The 2024 consent form does not mention US cloud. The Convention de remplacement does not mention US cloud. The named-physician needs retroactive nFADP Art.45(2) consent refresh for all named-EU-patients whose data has been transferred to US cloud.

4. **FDPIC Art.63 supervisory letter response** — the named-physician must produce nFADP Art.63 supervisory letter response within FDPIC response window. The response must include nFADP Art.63 data controller responsible person evidence, nFADP Art.9 sensitive personal data explicit consent evidence, nFADP Art.45(2) cross-border explicit consent evidence. The response must coordinate with the MSA Article 99 procedural letter through the named-physician triple.

5. **FDPIC-MSA coordination architecture** — the named-physician must produce single named-physician evidence architecture that satisfies FDPIC Art.63 supervisory letter response and MSA Article 99 procedural letter response simultaneously. The two letters refer to the same named-physician triple. The named-physician cannot produce separate responses without coordinating.

### Post-Deadline nFADP Enforcement Reality

The FDPIC has begun issuing Art.63 supervisory letters to Geneva médecin installé practices that coordinate with MSA Article 99 procedural letters through the named-physician triple. The first Genevoise peer physician has been cited for FDPIC-MSA coordination failure (peer physician produced MSA response without FDPIC file coordination, peer physician produced FDPIC response without MSA letter coordination, both responses failed).

The named physician needs the FDPIC-MSA coordination architecture for named-physicians (C315 P04 Finding 03).

## Regime 4: Carrier Attestation Named-Physician-Specific Clauses

**Legal source:** Geneva medical liability carrier attestation language (Swiss Medical Network broker network, FMH affinity broker partners, Concordia / CSS / Swica / Groupe Mutuel medical liability brokers)
**Penalty:** Premium adjustment (15-22% increase per mid-year 2026 refresh signal), policy non-renewal, claim denial
**Personal exposure:** Named-physician (médecin traitant) as named insured under medical liability attestation
**Named-substitute extension:** Named-substitute médecin remplaçant as named-sub-insured (or named AI tool user) under carrier attestation
**Named-encounter scope:** Every named consultation where AI scribe (or other AI tool) is used

### Carrier Attestation Compliance Architecture

The named-physician must satisfy the four named-physician-specific clauses at mid-year 2026 carrier attestation refresh through the following compliance architecture:

1. **Named-physician AI scribe inventory clause** — the named-physician must produce named-AI-scribe inventory: per named-AI-scribe tool in active use, per named-physician, per named-vendor, per named-data-residency, per named-US-CLOUD-Act-exposure flag. The inventory is per-practice, per-named-physician, per-named-AI-tool. The inventory is the operational mechanism by which the named-physician discharges Article 26 named-sub-principal attribution for the named-AI-scribe inventory.

2. **Named-encounter AI tool usage attestation clause** — the named-physician must produce named-encounter AI tool usage attestation: per named-physician, per named-encounter, attestation of named-AI-tool usage with named-human-review-checkpoint. The attestation is per-named-encounter. The attestation must satisfy Article 14 human oversight requirements.

3. **Named-physician Article 4 AI literacy attestation clause** — the named-physician must produce named-physician Article 4 AI literacy credential: per named-physician individual, per named-substitute médecin remplaçant individual, per active AI tool inventory. The credential must satisfy Article 4 literacy requirements.

4. **Named-physician incident response attestation clause** — the named-physician must produce named-physician incident response attestation: per named-physician individual, per named-escalation-chain, per Article 26 incident reporting + Article 321 CP criminal exposure + nFADP Art.63 reporting + carrier attestation incident response. The protocol must coordinate across all four regimes.

### Post-Deadline Carrier Attestation Enforcement Reality

The mid-year 2026 carrier attestation refresh has added the four named-physician-specific clauses. The clauses cite Article 26, nFADP Art.63, and Article 321 CP by article number — the first time the carrier attestation has referenced Article 321 CP Swiss criminal medical secrecy. The named-physician signs the attestation referencing named-AI-scribes, named-encounters, and named-substitute médecin remplaçants the practice may not be able to trace.

The named physician needs the carrier attestation named-physician-clause translation memo (C315 P04 Finding 02).

## Compliance Architecture Decision Tree

When the named-physician receives an enforcement signal (MSA letter, FDPIC letter, carrier attestation clause, SSMIG/AMG/FMH peer disclosure, US CLOUD Act vendor disclosure), the decision tree is:

1. **Is the signal MSA Article 99 procedural letter?**
   - Yes → Module 37: Named-physician MSA Article 99 audit response architecture (30-day window)
   - No → Continue to 2

2. **Is the signal FDPIC Art.63 supervisory letter?**
   - Yes → Module 39: FDPIC-MSA coordination architecture for named-physicians (coordinated with MSA letter if present)
   - No → Continue to 3

3. **Is the signal mid-year carrier attestation refresh clause?**
   - Yes → Module 38: Carrier attestation named-physician-clause translation memo (4 clauses, Article 321 CP first-time reference)
   - No → Continue to 4

4. **Is the signal US-hosted AI scribe vendor MSA Article 12 audit disclosure?**
   - Yes → Module 41: US CLOUD Act MSA-audit exposure map + named-physician audit response architecture (coordinated with MSA letter if present)
   - No → Continue to 5

5. **Is the signal Convention de remplacement named-substitute médecin remplaçant handover-window discontinuity?**
   - Yes → Module 42: Named-substitute handover-window named-sub-principal continuity architecture (per-couverture-period cadence)
   - No → Continue to 6

6. **Is the signal SSMIG/AMG/FMH/HUG named-physician peer disclosure cycle?**
   - Yes → Module 40: Named-physician proactive disclosure architecture for SSMIG/AMG/FMH September 2026 conference cycle
   - No → Defer to C314 P04 + C315 P01-P03 modules (pre-deadline readiness architecture)

The decision tree produces the named-physician evidence pack across the four regimes simultaneously, through the named-physician triple as the integrating mechanism.

## What's New in C315 P04 vs. C314 P04

The C314 P04 compliance context documented the four-regime architecture as pre-deadline readiness. The C315 P04 compliance context documents the four-regime architecture as post-deadline enforcement navigation. The difference is operational:

- **C314 P04 (pre-deadline readiness):** The named-physician was preparing the named-physician evidence architecture before the deadline. The MSA, FDPIC, and carrier were not yet activating enforcement. The named-physician had 30 days to prepare the architecture.
- **C315 P04 (post-deadline enforcement):** The MSA has issued the first Article 99 procedural letter. The FDPIC has opened the first Art.63 file. The carrier has added the four named-physician-specific clauses with first-time Article 321 CP reference. The first Genevoise peer physician has been named publicly at SSMIG Journée September 2026. The named-physician has 30 days to respond to the MSA letter. The named-physician must navigate the four-regime coordination in real time.

The shift is from architecture preparation (C314 P04) to architecture navigation (C315 P04). The named-physician evidence architecture must be pre-built (C315 P04 service) to enable rapid response to MSA letters, FDPIC letters, carrier clauses, SSMIG/AMG/FMH peer disclosure cycles, US CLOUD Act vendor disclosures, and Convention de remplacement named-substitute handover-window discontinuity signals.

The first Genevoise médecin installé service to publish the post-deadline physician Article 26 enforcement pack — bundling all six C315 P04 modules — owns the C315 P04 category.
