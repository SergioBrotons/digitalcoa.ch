## Primary Regulatory Framework

### FINMA Supervision

**Key document:** FINMA Guidance 08/2024 — "Artificial Intelligence — Supervisory Expectations for Financial Institutions"

**What it says:** FINMA published guidance on AI use by supervised institutions including asset managers. Key expectations include:
- Governance and controls for AI use
- Documentation of AI system use
- Human oversight of AI decisions
- Transparency obligations to clients
- Risk management integration

**What it doesn't say:** Proportionate implementation pathway for boutique 50–200 employee wealth managers. Specific evidence standards for what "adequate" looks like. Specific timeline. Specific enforcement approach for smaller institutions.

**Gap:** FINMA guidance creates obligation without proportionate implementation methodology for boutique scale. MD knows guidance exists, knows it applies, doesn't know what it requires specifically or how to demonstrate compliance at their scale.

### SRO Obligations

**Key SROs:** Asset Management Association Switzerland (AMAS), Swiss Funds & Asset Management Association (SFAMA)

**What they say:** Minimal specific AI guidance. SRO silence on AI is itself a signal. No published AI governance standard for asset managers. No proportionate methodology for boutique WM. No specific SRO interpretation of FINMA guidance for smaller firms.

**Gap:** SRO vacuum is structural, not temporary. Boutique cannot look to SRO for Geneva-specific AI governance methodology. Carrier questionnaires become de facto operative standard through SRO guidance vacuum displacement mechanism.

### nFADP (Federal Act on Data Protection, revised 2023)

**Key articles for boutique WM:**

**Art. 6 — Lawfulness of processing:**
- Personal data must have lawful basis
- AI inference about client financial behavior requires documented lawful basis
- Inference processing undocumented = present-tense non-compliance

**Art. 8 — Profiling notification:**
- AI-assisted portfolio construction constitutes profiling under nFADP
- Data subjects (clients) right to notification and human review
- Profiling notifications not being issued by Geneva boutiques = active exposure

**Art. 20/21 — Right to explanation for automated decisions:**
- Clients have right to know how AI influenced decisions about them
- Right applies to AI-assisted portfolio decisions
- Genuine explanation vs. vague disclosure gap = undisclosed liability

**Art. 63 — Criminal liability:**
- Personal criminal liability for serious data protection violations
- MD personal liability for AI governance decisions with data protection implications
- Criminal standard different from administrative compliance documentation

**Gap:** nFADP applies to boutique WM client data processing. Obligations present-tense regardless of awareness. No Geneva market service offering nFADP compliance methodology for AI-assisted wealth management specifically. Art.8 profiling notifications absent from boutique WM practice. Art.63 personal criminal liability unnamed in boutique WM discourse despite being operative.

### Carrier Attestation Evolution (Third Generation)

**First generation:** Tool inventory requirements. "What AI tools do you use?" — documentation exercise, not evidence of governance.

**Second generation:** Policy document requirements. "Do you have an AI governance policy?" — policy existence, not policy performance.

**Third generation:** Specific human authorization moment evidence. "Show me three AI-assisted decisions, what human reviewed each, what evidence of review." — performance evidence, not policy existence.

**Current state:** Third-generation questionnaires arriving at Geneva boutiques. MDs signing attestations against undefined "adequate AI governance controls" standard without understanding third-generation evidence requirements. Documentation built for first/second generation doesn't satisfy third-generation questions.

**Pattern:** Carrier iteration tracking reveals expected evolution trajectory. Fourth generation likely probes agentic AI continuous authorization specifically. Boutique building documentation for current generation while carriers asking about next generation.

## Geneva-Specific Market Dynamics

### Documentation Window Close

**What's happening:** Documentation window is present-tense closing, not future event. Carrier third-generation questionnaires arriving now. Institutional client sophistication increasing now. FINMA supervisory expectations crystallizing now. Window close creates burden-of-proof reversal — firms building documentation during window vs. firms explaining absence after window closes.

**Implication:** Pre-architecture built during window produces structurally different evidence than retroactive documentation. Boutiques building now have irreversible positioning advantage. Boutiques waiting face retrospective reconstruction impossibility.

### Cross-Firm Comparison Effect

**What's happening:** Institutional clients running simultaneous multi-boutique AI governance comparisons. Each boutique responds independently. Aggregate responses create de facto market standard. Documentation sophistication rewarded over actual architecture quality.

**Implication:** Boutique documentation strategy is also competitive positioning strategy. Response quality affects institutional relationship standing, not just compliance checkbox.

### Carrier Pre-Renewal Review Timing

**What's happening:** Carrier renewal cycles create predictable documentation deadlines. 90-day pre-renewal window is acute urgency occasion when MD must demonstrate AI governance adequacy. Documentation must exist at renewal date regardless of when decisions were made.

**Implication:** Proportionate pre-renewal review service — evaluating current documentation against carrier evidence requirements before renewal — unnamed in Geneva market. Highest-conversion entry point for MD with visible renewal deadline.

### Geneva Circuit Participation Effect

**What's happening:** Accountability expectations forming through informal Geneva channels before Bern formal doctrine arrives. Standards forming in rooms boutique MDs don't access. Intelligence available only through sustained institutional participation.

**Implication:** Boutique without Geneva circuit access building documentation against arrived standards while operative standards set through informal channels different from published. Structural intelligence disadvantage for non-participating boutiques.

## Evidence Standard Hierarchy

**Carrier evidence standard (current third generation):**

- Performance evidence, not policy existence
- Documentary trail of human review preceding AI action
- Replicable, contemporaneous documentation

**nFADP Art.63 criminal defense standard:**
- Different evidentiary architecture than administrative compliance
- What satisfies carrier may not satisfy criminal defense
- Documentation built for carrier attestation inadequate for criminal liability protection
- Criminal defense requires own documentation architecture

**Gap:** No Geneva market participant offering documentation architecture designed for both carrier evidence requirements AND criminal defense evidentiary standards simultaneously.
