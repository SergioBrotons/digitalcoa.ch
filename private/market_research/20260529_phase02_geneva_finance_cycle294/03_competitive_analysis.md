# Geneva Finance ICP: Boutique Wealth Management (50–200 employees)

## What Exists in Market vs. What's Missing

---

## What Exists

### Large Financial Institution Frameworks

**What exists:** Large banks (UBS, Credit Suisse successor entities, cantonal banks) have published AI governance frameworks, dedicated compliance teams, and regulatory relationships with FINMA and SROs. These frameworks are publicly referenced and sometimes used as reference points by carriers.

**What's missing for boutique:** These frameworks are designed for institutions with compliance departments, AI governance committees, and institutional infrastructure. They don't translate to 50–200 employee boutiques where MD personally signs attestations and has no compliance department. The frameworks exist but provide no proportionate methodology for boutique scale.

**Gap:** No large institution framework includes a boutique-scale implementation pathway. Reference frameworks create awareness of what adequate looks like without providing a roadmap for how a boutique gets there.

---

### Generic AI Governance Consultancies

**What exists:** International consulting firms (Big Four and their AI governance practices) offering AI governance frameworks, risk assessments, and compliance documentation for financial institutions.

**What's missing for boutique:** Priced for institutional engagements. Minimum engagement sizes far exceed what a Geneva boutique WM can justify. Methodology designed for large institutional compliance programs, not personal MD liability exposure. No Geneva calibration. No understanding of Swiss Banking Ombudsman jurisdiction or FINMA/SRO specific expectations.

**Gap:** Generic international frameworks that don't address Swiss regulatory specifics, carrier attestation evolution, or Geneva ecosystem dynamics. Geneva boutique paying for international framework gets documentation that doesn't fit their accountability structure.

---

### Legal Firms Offering AI Governance Advisory

**What exists:** Geneva law firms with technology practices offering AI governance advice, data protection compliance, and regulatory analysis.

**What's missing for boutique:** Legal advice addresses legal obligations (nFADP, FINMA guidance) without proportionate operational methodology for portfolio management AI governance. Legal counsel explains what the law says; doesn't build what MD needs to show carrier. Separate from wealth management specific AI governance accountability architecture. No carrier attestation review against undefined adequacy standard.

**Gap:** Legal firms good at explaining regulatory framework; less good at building operational evidence architecture that satisfies carrier third-generation requirements. Different skill set than what boutiques need.

---

### Carrier-Provided AI Governance Resources

**What exists:** Carriers (Swiss insurance companies offering PI and professional liability coverage to wealth managers) providing AI governance questionnaires, attestation forms, and sometimes published AI governance guidance as part of underwriting.

**What's missing for boutique:** Carrier resources designed to evaluate risk for underwriting purposes, not to protect MD personal liability or build proportionate governance architecture. Carrier questionnaires become de facto operative standard despite being designed for carrier risk evaluation. No carrier publishes what "adequate" actually means. No pre-signing review service. No methodology for satisfying carrier evidence requirements beyond general guidance.

**Gap:** Carrier resources treat documentation as compliance exercise, not as MD personal liability protection. Boutique follows carrier guidance, signs attestation, then discovers evidence standard doesn't match what carrier actually requires at claim time.

---

### SRO Guidance (SFAMA / AMAS)

**What exists:** Swiss Fund Management Association (SFAMA) and Asset Management Association Switzerland (AMAS) publish industry guidance, best practices, and standards for Swiss asset managers.

**What's missing for boutique:** SRO guidance on AI specifically is minimal or absent. No Geneva-specific AI governance guidance for boutique wealth managers. SRO silence on AI is itself a signal boutiques interpret as "no specific obligation yet." Guidance vacuum is structural, not temporary.

**Gap:** SRO guidance vacuum means boutique has no sector-standard methodology to follow. Carrier questionnaires function as de facto operative standard because no SRO alternative exists. Boutique operating without sector standard while being measured against carrier-designed standards.

---

## What's Missing (White Space)

### 1. Pre-Signing Carrier Attestation Review Service

**What's missing:** No Geneva market service offering MD-side review of carrier AI attestation before signature. Forms designed by carrier underwriting teams; MD signing commits to undefined adequacy standard. No independent analysis of whether signature is appropriate, what evidence would satisfy the standard, what gaps exist in current documentation.

**Why it matters:** Carrier attestation creates personal liability. Signature is personal. No service helps MD understand what they're committing to or how to close gaps before signing. Post-signature realization of exposure is too late.

**Pricing opportunity:** Proportionate to boutique scale. 50–200 employee firm. 90-minute briefing + attestation review. Named problem, concrete action.

---

### 2. Geneva Boutique WM AI Governance Methodology

**What's missing:** No published methodology for Geneva boutique wealth management MD to use in building AI governance architecture proportionate to their exposure. What does "adequate AI governance controls" mean for a 50-person boutique with AI-assisted portfolio management? What evidence does MD need to show carrier? What documentation survives institutional client questionnaire? No Geneva-calibrated answer.

**Why it matters:** MD personally accountable but no methodology exists to build appropriate governance. MD following large institution frameworks (wrong scale) or arbitrary documentation (inadequate). Documentation built doesn't answer the questions carriers actually ask.

**Pricing opportunity:** Geneva-specific. 90-minute briefing. 90-minute + documentation review. CHF 2,200–3,800. Named Geneva specificity as trust signal.

---

### 3. Institutional Client Questionnaire Response Architecture

**What's missing:** No service helping boutique prepare institutional client AI governance questionnaires as strategic documents, not just compliance responses. How to answer questions that establish competitive positioning. How to avoid revealing methodology that should remain confidential. How to manage disclosure vs. competitive intelligence tension. How to prepare for follow-up questions.

**Why it matters:** Institutional clients are running simultaneous comparisons across managers. How boutique responds affects relationship standing, not just compliance checkbox. Responses create commercial representations the firm must live with.

**Pricing opportunity:** Per-engagement. Per questionnaire. Crisis pricing for urgent responses. Strategic framing for systematic roll-out.

---

### 4. Agentic AI Authorization Architecture for Boutique WM

**What's missing:** No Geneva methodology for boutique wealth management using continuous AI portfolio management (agentic AI). FINMA Guidance 08/2024 written for generative AI. No proportionate standard for boutique using AI that executes continuously. What authorization architecture satisfies carrier evidence requirements? What documentation proves human review preceded AI autonomous decisions?

**Why it matters:** Agentic AI use increasing. Authorization architecture absent. Boutiques with continuous AI portfolio management have no methodology for documenting human authorization of continuous AI actions.

**Pricing opportunity:** Niche but growing. 90-minute briefing + documentation framework. Premium for novel problem with no market alternative.

---

### 5. nFADP + FINMA Compound Disclosure Integration Methodology

**What's missing:** No methodology for managing simultaneous nFADP Art.21 automated decision obligations and FINMA Circular 2016/01 documentation requirements as integrated disclosure framework. Boutiques managing two parallel accountability tracks without map between them. Integrated response architecture absent from market.

**Why it matters:** Same AI-assisted decision triggers both obligations simultaneously. Responding to each independently produces documents that technically satisfy each requirement but substantively fail when client or regulator probes deeper. Compound disclosure failure creates parallel exposure.

**Pricing opportunity:** Technical integration problem with high stakes. 90-minute briefing + framework document. Premium for solving parallel track coordination.

---

### 6. Portfolio Construction AI Documentation Service

**What's missing:** No discrete service category addressing portfolio construction AI as named accountability exposure. Allocation recommendation logic is most consequential AI decision boutique makes. Institutional clients specifically asking portfolio construction methodology questions. No service helping boutique document portfolio construction AI specifically.

**Why it matters:** Portfolio construction AI is different from reporting AI or compliance AI. Different accountability requirements. Different evidence standards. Different client exposure. boutiques treating all AI use uniformly when accountability disaggregation required.

**Pricing opportunity:** Named specific AI use case. Premium for solving a problem that isn't yet widely named but is arriving fast.

---

### 7. Documentation Window Pre-Architecture Service

**What's missing:** No service framing documentation as pre-architecture problem (building before window closes) rather than compliance deadline problem (building after standards arrive). Burden-of-proof reversal positioning. Proportionate pre-architecture methodology for 50–200 employee boutique WM.

**Why it matters:** Documentation window close creates irreversible positioning. After close, firms cannot reconstruct window-period reasoning. Pre-architecture built during window is structurally different from retroactive documentation. No market naming this distinction or offering pre-architecture service.

**Pricing opportunity:** Urgency-based pricing. Window closing creates immediate action requirement. 90-minute briefing + 6-month architecture program.

---

### 8. MD Personal Liability Mapping vs. PI Coverage

**What's missing:** No service helping MD map personal liability exposure against existing PI coverage before incident occurs. PI policies not written for AI-adjacent decisions. Gap between assumed coverage and explicit coverage only visible post-incident. Pre-incident mapping of where personal exposure exists, what coverage addresses, what gap remains uninsured — unnamed service.

**Why it matters:** MD personally liable for AI governance decisions. PI coverage assumed to cover this. Assumptions may not match policy terms. Gap only discoverable after claim denied.

**Pricing opportunity:** Pre-incident planning. 90-minute briefing + coverage mapping. CHF 1,800–2,200. Named problem with high consequence.
