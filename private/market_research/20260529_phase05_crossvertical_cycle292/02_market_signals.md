## The Six Cross-Vertical Market Signals

---

## Signal 01: Retrospective Confirmation Effect — The Market Has Moved from Anticipatory to Confirmed

**The signal:** In C292, the dominant market signal is retrospective confirmation. ICPs across all four verticals are not describing fear of future exposure — they are describing confirmed past exposure. The carrier attestation they signed in 2023 is now inconsistent with FINMA Guidance 08/2024 published in December 2024. The AI tool portfolio they declared in last year's Swissmedic registration has changed through updates and staff activations. The client contract clause they accepted without review is now being enforced. These are not projections. They are events.

**Evidence from C292 verticals:**
- Phase 01: Art. 167a CPC (January 2025) creates structural accountability split for hybrid firms. Attorneys who signed carrier attestations before this change are now operating under a framework the attestation didn't account for.
- Phase 02: FINMA Guidance 08/2024 creates documented inconsistency with existing carrier attestations. The MD who signed a 2023 attestation is now, in 2026, holding a document that commits to standards different from what the regulator published.
- Phase 03: Client MSA AI governance clauses are being enforced by clients who inserted them. The consulting firm that accepted "standard contract provisions" is now receiving compliance verification requests from those clients.
- Phase 04: Swissmedic AI tool registration follow-ups are arriving for physicians who attested to tools in their portfolio twelve months ago. The tool portfolio has changed since the attestation.

**The cross-vertical pattern:** All four verticals are experiencing the same moment — the retrospective confirmation that past commitments are inconsistent with current operative standards. The timing is synchronized across verticals because the regulatory developments (FINMA Guidance, CPC amendment, AI Convention, Swissmedic follow-ups) arrived within a concentrated window.

**Market implication:** The briefing that addresses confirmed past exposure ("here is what your prior attestation committed you to, here is what changed, here is what you can do now") converts at higher rates than briefings addressing anticipated future risk. The ICP who already knows they have a confirmed problem is more ready to engage than the ICP who fears they might have one.

## Signal 02: Client Questionnaire as Primary Pressure Trigger — Clients Ahead of Carriers

**The signal:** In C292, client AI governance questionnaires are the primary external pressure trigger across all four verticals, operating ahead of carrier attestation requirements and ahead of published regulatory guidance. Clients are applying standards through due diligence processes that carriers and regulators have not yet codified.

**Evidence from C292 verticals:**
- Phase 01: Corporate clients are sending AI governance questionnaires to outside counsel as proposal evaluation criteria. The questionnaire is part of the selection process — firms without documented AI governance are marked down.
- Phase 02: Institutional clients send AI governance DDQs as competitive intelligence instruments. They compare responses across the manager roster. A response without underlying architecture is worse than no response because it creates commercial representations the boutique cannot support.
- Phase 03: Strategy clients request AI methodology documentation as proposal evaluation. The AI governance methodology is a competitive differentiator in pitch processes — not just a compliance record.
- Phase 04: Private patients are beginning to ask about AI use in treatment decisions. The question appears in intake forms and in direct physician conversation. Not yet widespread, but present and growing.

**The cross-vertical pattern:** Client pressure is not limited to finance (where institutional DDQs were already present in prior cycles). In C292, client questionnaire pressure appears in legal, consulting, and healthcare — with the same mechanism operating across all four sectors. The client questionnaire is the mechanism through which accountability standards form in the absence of published doctrine.

**Market implication:** The documentation that clients are asking for is the documentation that carriers and regulators will eventually require. The firm that builds client-response documentation architecture captures the positioning advantage of serving standards before they become regulatory requirements. This is the primary business development opportunity in the C292 market.

## Signal 03: Agentic AI Arrival — The Market Has Not Named the Accountability Problem

**The signal:** Agentic AI — systems that execute autonomously without discrete human authorization moments — is arriving in all four verticals before any Geneva-calibrated governance methodology addresses it. Portfolio rebalancing, compliance monitoring, case management, diagnostic support: AI that acts, not just advises. The accountability question this creates — "did you authorize what AI did autonomously, continuously?" — is unnamed in the Geneva market.

**Evidence from C292 verticals:**
- Phase 01: AI case management systems process continuously throughout the day. The firm can show it turned on the system. It cannot show what happened every time the system made a decision between then and now.
- Phase 02: Portfolio construction AI rebalances continuously. The carrier's third-generation form asks what specific AI-assisted decisions the MD reviewed. The system does not create those decision points. The evidence required does not exist.
- Phase 03: AI analysis tools process client deliverables continuously. The MD reviews the final output. They cannot show what the model did when it processed the data at 2am.
- Phase 04: AI diagnostic support runs in the background continuously. The physician can show they activated the feature. They cannot show what oversight occurred when the system processed a patient.

**The cross-vertical pattern:** Agentic AI is not sector-specific. It is arriving across all four verticals simultaneously with the same structural problem: continuous autonomous execution creates an evidence gap for the documented human authorization moments that third-generation carrier forms require. The problem is unnamed in all four sectors.

**Market implication:** Agentic AI governance methodology is a discrete, unnamed service category. No Geneva-calibrated methodology exists for determining what continuous authorization documentation looks like for boutique-scale operations. First to name this as a service category and produce a proportionate methodology captures the market positioning in a space where Bern guidance has not arrived.

## Signal 04: Non-Traditional Standard-Setting Displacement — Carriers and Vendors Setting Operative Standards

**The signal:** The Geneva accountability vacuum is being filled by non-traditional actors — AI vendors publishing compliance guides, carriers issuing AI governance checklists, industry bodies releasing self-assessment frameworks — all designed for their own interests, not for Geneva boutique accountability. The operative standard for AI governance in Geneva is increasingly set by parties whose primary interest is not the boutique practitioner's accountability protection.

**Evidence from C292 verticals:**
- Phase 01: SBA guidelines are too general for firm-level decisions. Ordre des Avocats de Genève has not issued canton-specific guidance. The carrier's AI governance checklist is more specific than published Geneva-specific guidance.
- Phase 02: Carrier third-generation forms define what "adequate AI governance controls" means for boutique WMs. The form is written by the carrier's legal team for underwriting risk evaluation. It is the operative standard.
- Phase 03: Vendor compliance guides, carrier AI governance checklists, and industry self-assessment frameworks are all available. None were designed for Geneva consulting boutique accountability. The firm adopting them accepts someone else's definition of adequate governance.
- Phase 04: FMH human oversight principle provides no operational endpoint. Carrier attestation questions define operative standards for independent practices. Swissmedic tool registration requirements define what "adequate" means for AI tools. All three are external frameworks applied to independent practices.

**The cross-vertical pattern:** The displacement mechanism is identical across all four verticals: external parties set standards designed for their own accountability interests, and boutique practitioners adopt those standards as operative benchmarks without evaluating whether they serve their specific exposure profile. The vacuum is structural and the displacement is structural.

**Market implication:** External standard evaluation is a discrete service category — evaluating whether externally-imposed AI governance frameworks serve the firm's specific accountability interest. This is distinct from publishing one's own framework. The independence from all three standard-setting sources (vendors, carriers, industry bodies) is itself the competitive moat.

## Signal 05: Institutional Circuit Intelligence Structural Exclusion — Formation Without Participation

**The signal:** Operative accountability standards are forming through non-public channels — carrier questionnaire iterations, institutional circuit exchanges, client contract clause developments — faster than published guidance reflects. Boutique practitioners and independent physicians outside sustained Geneva circuit participation are building documentation against published standards that have already been superseded in operative form through informal channels.

**Evidence from C292 verticals:**
- Phase 01: HUG/CHUV have AI governance committees making decisions that form operative standards. Independent practitioners do not access these rooms. The standards apply to them without their participation.
- Phase 02: The institutional circuit in Geneva is where accountability expectations actually form. Carrier iteration patterns, informal exchanges, institutional decisions — these are the formation channels. By the time anything gets published, the decision has been made.
- Phase 03: The accountability formation velocity in Geneva is faster than published guidance reflects. The carrier's latest questionnaire iteration, the clause the client's lawyer inserted into their MSA, the question that came up at a Geneva institutional exchange — these are where standards form. Not in the published guidance.
- Phase 04: Institutional decisions at HUG/CHUV create precedent that carriers, patients, and legal challengers can reference as operative standard. Independent practices build governance in isolation against standards set by institutional decisions they never observed.

**The cross-vertical pattern:** The formation mechanism is identical across all four sectors. Institutional decisions at well-resourced entities form operative standards that apply to independent practitioners and boutique firms without their participation. The circuit intelligence is the structural moat — it cannot be replicated through secondary research.

**Market implication:** Sustained circuit participation is the primary competitive advantage that content investment cannot replicate. The advisory value comes from non-publishable formation intelligence — observing the formation process, not just the arrived standard. The moat is participation, not content.

## Signal 06: Documentation Window Operative Property — Competitive Asset, Not Compliance Buffer

**The signal:** The documentation window is not closing in the future — it is operating as a present-tense competitive positioning property. Firms that built documentation during the window are experiencing direct business value: client retention through AI governance questionnaire responses, competitive differentiation in proposal processes, lower administrative burden when carrier and regulatory communications arrive. The documentation window is a business development asset, not just a compliance record.

**Evidence from C292 verticals:**
- Phase 01: Attorneys with documented AI governance can respond to client questionnaires. Attorneys without documentation cannot — and lose the client as a consequence.
- Phase 02: Boutique WMs with documented AI governance can respond to institutional client DDQs with evidence. Those without documentation produce responses that are identified as inadequate by institutional clients comparing across their manager roster.
- Phase 03: Consulting firms with methodology documentation have a competitive differentiator in pitch processes. Firms without documentation are marked down in proposal evaluations that include AI governance criteria.
- Phase 04: Independent practices with documented AI governance have an answer when Swissmedic follows up on tool registration. Practices without documentation have no response mechanism.

**The cross-vertical pattern:** The competitive effect of documentation is present-tense in all four verticals. Firms with documentation experience measurable business value from it. Firms without documentation experience the absence as a present-tense competitive liability. The timing precision of the window is an operative market condition, not a theoretical property.

**Market implication:** The documentation window is a business development investment with compliance co-benefits, not a compliance cost. The positioning affects pricing psychology and ICP qualification. "I'm investing in my documentation window position" vs "I'm paying for compliance" — the same product, different frame, different conversion rate.
