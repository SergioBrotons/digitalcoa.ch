## What the Market Looks Like Right Now

Let me describe what I'm seeing in Geneva right now, across all four of these professional contexts.

**The documentation window has closed.** Not in the future — now. In December 2024, FINMA published Guidance 08/2024. The CPC amendment creating Art. 167a CPC became law in January 2025. The Council of Europe AI Convention was signed in March 2025. The FDPIC confirmed in May 2025 that existing data protection law applies directly to AI with enforcement now possible.

If you signed a carrier attestation before these developments, your attestation is inconsistent with the current regulatory landscape. You signed something that committed you to standards that have since changed. The question is not whether this happened. The question is what you do now.

**The carrier attestation is not written for your protection.** Carrier forms are written by the carrier's legal team for underwriting risk evaluation. They require attestation to "adequate AI governance controls" — a phrase that means something different to the underwriter than it does to you. The form is designed to help the carrier evaluate whether your AI usage creates risk for them. It is not designed to help you understand what you are committing to, or to protect you if a dispute arises.

When your carrier asks you to sign the attestation, you are signing a document written by someone else's legal team, committing to a standard nobody has defined, in an environment where enforcement is now live. And there is no Geneva service that reviews that form from your perspective before you sign.

**The client questionnaire is setting standards your carrier hasn't codified yet.** In every professional context I'm working in — legal, finance, consulting, healthcare — clients are sending AI governance questionnaires. Corporate counsel is asking outside law firms about AI methodology. Institutional clients are sending detailed AI governance DDQs to wealth managers as competitive intelligence instruments. Strategy clients are evaluating AI governance documentation as part of proposal evaluation. Private patients are asking about AI use in treatment decisions.

The client questionnaire is ahead of the carrier form. The client is applying standards through their due diligence process that carriers and regulators have not yet codified. And there is no Geneva service that helps you respond to the client questionnaire in a way that builds your governance record while satisfying their requirements.

**Agentic AI is arriving before your governance architecture exists to receive it.** The accountability question for the next generation of carrier forms is not "did you use AI?" It is "did you authorize what AI did autonomously, continuously, and can you demonstrate that authorization?"

Portfolio construction AI that rebalances continuously. AI case management systems that process throughout the day. AI diagnostic support that runs in the background. These systems do not create the discrete decision points that carrier forms ask you to document. You can show you turned the system on. You cannot show what happened every time it made a decision between then and now.

The evidence standard required by third-generation carrier forms is structurally impossible for continuous AI systems. And no Geneva-calibrated methodology exists to document continuous authorization at boutique scale.
