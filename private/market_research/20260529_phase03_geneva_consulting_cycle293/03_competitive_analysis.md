# 03 — Competitive Analysis: What Exists vs. What's Missing

**Phase:** 03 — Geneva Consulting (2–50 employees)
**Cycle:** 293
**Date:** 2026-05-29

---

## Competitive Landscape

### Who Is Serving This Market (Inadequately)

The following providers offer AI governance-related services relevant to small Swiss consulting firms:

---

#### 1. Big Four / Large Advisory Firms

**Players:** KPMG Switzerland, PwC Switzerland, Deloitte, EY

**What they offer:**
- AI governance frameworks and risk assessments
- EU AI Act compliance programs
- ISO/IEC 42001 implementation
- nFADP compliance audits

**Why they don't serve this ICP well:**
- Engagement sizes are typically CHF 50,000+ minimum
- Frameworks are enterprise-grade and require significant internal resources to implement
- Not designed for the specific context of a small boutique using consumer AI tools in client engagements
- No proportionate offering for 2–50 person firms
- These firms are often the source of the AI DDQs that small boutiques struggle to respond to

**Gap:** No one from the Big Four is offering a "small firm AI governance starter kit" at an accessible price point.

---

#### 2. Swiss Digital / Industry Associations

**Players:** digitalswitzerland, Swiss Business Federation, Swiss Consulting Society

**What they offer:**
- AI playbooks for SMEs (digitalswitzerland has published an AI playbook for Swiss SMEs)
- General AI guidance, events, awareness campaigns

**Why they don't serve this ICP well:**
- Playbooks are generic and not calibrated to professional services firms
- No specific guidance on client AI DDQ response, insurance attestation, or sub-consultant delegation
- No practical tools or templates for small consulting boutiques
- Industry associations have not produced consulting-specific AI governance guidance

**Gap:** No association has produced a "Geneva consulting firm AI governance guide" or a ready-to-use template library for small boutiques.

---

#### 3. Legal Advisory Firms (Specialized)

**Players:** MME (Zurich), Pestalozzi Attorneys, CMS, Baker McKenzie

**What they offer:**
- MME has published a "Questionnaire for AI-focused Due Diligence Process" for M&A contexts
- Legal opinions on nFADP compliance and liability
- Contract drafting for AI-specific clauses

**Why they don't serve this ICP well:**
- Legal advisory is priced for corporate clients, not small boutiques
- MME's AI due diligence questionnaire is designed for M&A transactions, not ongoing consulting firm operations
- No practical, proportionate AI governance framework specifically for consulting firms
- Legal advisors tell clients what the law requires but don't offer implementation tools

**Gap:** Legal advisors diagnose the problem but don't offer operational, tool-level solutions for small consulting firms.

---

#### 4. Specialized AI Governance Consultancies

**Players:** GAIGI (Geneva AI Governance Institute), Eraneos, TECHNO VISE (Geneva-based), INQ Consulting, SGS AI Trust Check

**What they offer:**
- GAIGI: Comprehensive AI governance frameworks, training, assessments
- Eraneos: Enterprise-wide AI adoption and governance aligned with EU AI Act, GDPR, FINMA
- TECHNO VISE: AI roadmaps, governance design, implementation (Geneva and Haute-Savoie)
- INQ Consulting: AI diagnostic tools and questionnaires
- SGS: AI Trust Check — online self-assessment for AI governance maturity

**Why they don't serve this ICP well:**
- GAIGI, while Geneva-based, focuses on institutional and enterprise-level governance
- Eraneos targets larger Swiss and multinational organizations
- TECHNO VISE is more technology-implementation focused than governance/compliance focused
- INQ Consulting's tools are structured questionnaires but not consulting-firm-specific
- SGS AI Trust Check is a self-assessment tool, not a consultative engagement — useful but not sufficient for a firm that doesn't know where to start

**Gap:** No provider offers a "done-for-you AI governance program" specifically designed for a 5–30 person Geneva consulting boutique, priced accessibly, and addressing the specific ICP concerns (client DDQ, insurance attestation, sub-consultant delegation, nFADP personal liability).

---

#### 5. AI Tool Vendors (OpenAI, Anthropic, Microsoft)

**What they offer:**
- Enterprise agreements with data processing addenda
- Compliance documentation (SOC 2, GDPR commitments)
- AI use policies and guidelines

**Why they don't serve this ICP well:**
- Vendors provide compliance at the tool level, not the consulting-firm-usage level
- A boutique using ChatGPT or Claude via standard subscriptions does not have enterprise agreements
- Vendor documentation doesn't address how the ICP should respond to client AI questionnaires about their methodology
- Vendors have no interest in the ICP's specific concerns (proposal defensibility, sub-consultant delegation, personal liability)

**Gap:** Vendors solve the "are these tools compliant?" question but not the "how do I use these tools compliantly in my consulting practice?" question.

---

#### 6. Insurance Brokers

**What they offer:**
- Professional liability policies with AI-related clauses (mostly exclusions)
- Supplementary AI questionnaires at renewal

**Why they don't serve this ICP well:**
- Brokers are adding exclusions reactively, not offering risk-based coverage solutions
- No broker has developed a differentiated insurance product for small consulting firms that use AI thoughtfully and with governance controls
- The conversation is framed as "AI = exclusion" rather than "AI with governance = manageable risk"

**Gap:** No insurance product or broker offering in Switzerland is specifically designed for small professional services firms that want to use AI with proportionate governance controls and obtain appropriate coverage.

---

## What's Missing (The Gaps That Represent Opportunities)

| Gap | Description |
|---|---|
| **No SME-calibrated AI governance framework** | Nothing exists specifically for 2–50 person consulting firms in Geneva/Switzerland. All existing frameworks are either enterprise-grade or generic SME playbooks without consulting-firm-specific content. |
| **No client AI DDQ response template library** | Small firms are inventing responses from scratch. No one has produced a ready-to-use, customizable response library for common AI governance DDQ questions. |
| **No sub-consultant AI clause template** | The market has no simple, legally-reviewed, ready-to-use AI delegation clause for consulting sub-contracts. |
| **No proportionate nFADP AI liability briefing for partners** | No one has produced a plain-language, Geneva-specific briefing on nFADP personal liability written specifically for managing partners of small consulting firms. |
| **No insurance-GA governance bridge product** | No provider offers a product that simultaneously addresses AI governance implementation AND provides documentation sufficient to obtain favorable insurance terms. |
| **No AI defensibility protocol for consulting research** | No practical, proportionate protocol exists for how small consulting firms should use AI in research and analysis without producing hallucination-laden deliverables. |

---

## NET-NEW Findings (C293 Phase 03)

1. **The Big Four create the demand (AI DDQs) but don't serve the supply side (small firm compliance).** The firms sending AI questionnaires have no incentive to make it easy for small boutiques to respond.

2. **No player is occupying the "small Geneva consulting boutique AI governance" position.** This is a genuine market positioning gap — not served by Big Four, not served by legal advisors, not served by tool vendors, not served by industry associations.

3. **GAIGI (Geneva AI Governance Institute) is the closest institutional player but focuses on enterprise/international governance** — not on the operational, day-to-day AI governance challenges of a 10-person boutique.

4. **The insurance market is creating a compliance pull** — small firms that can demonstrate governance controls may be able to negotiate better insurance terms, creating an economic case for a governance product.

5. **Sub-consultant AI delegation is the most unmanaged risk** — every other problem has at least some market attention; the delegation chain problem has almost none.

6. **The combination of all six gaps creates a natural "AI accountability program" product opportunity** — no single provider has bundled these into a coherent, accessible offering for small Geneva consulting firms.
