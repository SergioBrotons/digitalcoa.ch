# 01 — ICP Language: How Geneva Consulting Boutiques Talk About AI

**Phase:** 03 — Geneva Consulting (2–50 employees)
**Cycle:** 293
**Date:** 2026-05-29
**Language:** EN

---

## How the ICP Actually Speaks About AI Problems

The following language patterns reflect how small Geneva-based consulting firms (2–50 employees) describe their AI-related challenges. This is documented in their own words and phrasing — not the language of vendors, regulators, or enterprise consulting firms.

---

### 1. On Client AI Governance Questionnaires

> "Our client sent us a 40-question AI governance DDQ. We didn't know how to answer half of it — not because we're doing anything wrong, but because there's no standard format we can point to."

> "Large banks are now sending AI questionnaires to all their vendors and sub-consultants. We're being asked to confirm our AI usage policies, data handling, model explainability. We don't have any of this written down."

> "When a client asks if our methodology is 'AI-compliant,' I don't know what checkbox they're looking for. We use AI tools — does that make us an AI company?"

**Language they use:** AI DDQ, client AI questionnaire, vendor AI assessment, AI governance due diligence, AI compliance questionnaire, client's AI requirements

---

### 2. On Proposal Methodology Defensibility

> "I've started adding disclaimers to my proposals about AI tools. But I'm not a lawyer — I don't know if those disclaimers actually protect me."

> "If a client later claims the advice I delivered was flawed because I relied on AI, how do I prove my methodology was sound?"

> "The big firms have entire risk management teams that sign off on proposals. I sign off on my own proposals. That's the difference."

> "I need to be able to explain to a skeptical client exactly how I arrived at a recommendation — including which tools I used and why."

**Language they use:** methodology defensibility, proposal risk, recommendation accountability, AI tool disclosure, audit trail, document my reasoning, how I arrived at this

---

### 3. On Carrier / Insurer AI Attestation

> "My professional liability insurer asked me to fill out a form about AI. The questions didn't make sense for a one-person strategy boutique."

> "I got a renewal notice that included new AI-related exclusions. I didn't even know AI was now explicitly mentioned in my policy."

> "Carriers are starting to ask: 'Do you use AI to produce client deliverables?' Answering 'yes' feels like opening a can of worms. Answering 'no' feels like lying."

**Language they use:** AI exclusion, carrier attestation, professional liability AI clause, insurance AI questionnaire, AI-related exclusion, policy renewal AI questions

---

### 4. On Sub-Consultant AI Delegation

> "When I bring in a sub-consultant to handle part of an engagement, I don't know what AI tools they're using. I can't control what I can't see."

> "If a sub-consultant's AI tool produces a bad analysis and I deliver it to my client — who's responsible?"

> "I've started adding AI-specific clauses to my sub-consultant contracts. But I'm making it up as I go."

**Language they use:** sub-consultant AI use, delegate to AI, downstream AI risk, subcontractor AI policy, AI in the supply chain, flow-down AI clauses

---

### 5. On Personal Liability Under nFADP

> "The new Swiss data protection law introduced personal fines for executives. If I process data through an AI tool and something goes wrong — is that on me personally?"

> "I've read about the nFADP. The part that scares me is not the company fine — it's that managers can be fined individually. Up to CHF 250,000."

> "I'm a partner. I have personal exposure. My clients' data is processed by AI tools I chose. That's a chain of responsibility that nobody has clearly mapped out."

**Language they use:** nFADP personal liability, manager liability, executive fine, CHF 250,000, personal exposure under data protection, my name on the contract

---

### 6. On General AI Deployment in Client Engagements

> "I want to use AI tools to serve clients better and faster. But I'm worried about what I don't know about these tools — the hidden risks."

> "My clients are sophisticated. They know I'm not a tech firm. They don't expect me to have enterprise-grade AI governance. But they're starting to ask questions."

> "There's no clear guidebook for someone like me. The KPMG and PwC frameworks are designed for large organizations. I need something that fits a 5-person firm."

**Language they use:** AI deployment, AI risk, AI tool selection, which AI tools can I use, AI for a small consulting firm, proportionate AI governance

## Emotional and Psychological State

The dominant emotional register is **anxious pragmatism**:

- They are not anti-AI. They want to use it.
- They feel exposed — personally, professionally, and legally.
- They feel the frameworks available are designed for larger firms.
- They want clear, simple guidance that acknowledges their size and resource constraints.
- They distrust both: (a) vendors who oversell AI simplicity, and (b) regulators/insurers who add vague new requirements without clear compliance paths.
