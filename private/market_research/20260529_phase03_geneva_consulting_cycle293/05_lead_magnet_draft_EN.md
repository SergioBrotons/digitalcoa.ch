## Part 1: What's Actually Happening in the Market

### Your Clients Are Now Asking About AI

If you work with financial institutions, large corporates, or any organization with a serious risk management function, you've probably received — or will soon receive — an AI governance due diligence questionnaire. These are no longer rare. They're becoming standard vendor onboarding.

**What they're typically asking:**
- Do you use AI tools in your client engagements?
- How do you validate AI-assisted outputs?
- What AI tools do you use, and how do you select them?
- Do you have an AI usage policy?
- How do you oversee AI use by sub-consultants?

If you're answering these questions on the fly, without documentation — you're building liability. Not necessarily bad liability. But unexamined liability. And unexamined liability is the kind that surprises you.

### Your Insurer Is Starting to Notice

Professional liability renewals are now including AI-specific language. In some cases, exclusions. In other cases, questions about your AI usage that you're not sure how to answer.

Here's what I realized: insurers don't have a model for "small consulting firm using consumer-grade AI tools thoughtfully." So they're treating all AI use as a black box — and black boxes get exclusions.

The opportunity here is to be the firm that can demonstrate it has governance around AI use. That changes the conversation from "you're using AI, therefore exclusion" to "you're using AI with controls, therefore covered."

### Personal Liability Is Real

The revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) introduced something that GDPR doesn't have: personal criminal liability for managers. Up to CHF 250,000. For intentional breaches.

Let me be precise: I'm not a lawyer. But I've spoken to several, and the consensus is that if you're a managing partner who's selected and deployed AI tools without any governance framework, and those tools process personal data, and something goes wrong — you're in a conversation you don't want to have.

This isn't scaremongering. It's accountability. The kind that should be on your radar before, not after, something happens.

## Part 2: The Five Accountability Gaps I See in Small Consulting Firms

### Gap 1: No AI Tool Policy

Most small firms have no documented policy for which AI tools they use, why they use them, and how they review outputs. This isn't a criticism — it's an observation. There's been no reason to document it before.

But if a client asks, and you say "we use ChatGPT for research and we review everything carefully" — that answer is unsatisfying to anyone who needs to check a compliance box. You need something in writing.

**What a basic AI tool policy actually needs:**
- A list of approved AI tools (and why)
- A statement on how outputs are reviewed and validated
- A statement on data handling (what you do and don't put into AI tools)
- A statement on human oversight

You don't need 20 pages. You need clarity. Four clear sections is enough.

### Gap 2: No Client AI DDQ Response Framework

When a client sends you a 40-question AI governance questionnaire, you're starting from zero. You're answering questions in isolation, without knowing what they actually need to hear, or what a good answer looks like.

The real problem isn't answering the questions. It's understanding that a client AI DDQ is a signal: your client is managing their own regulatory risk, and they're asking you to be part of their compliance story. You can be an answered question in their file, or you can be a documented, governed partner.

**What a DDQ response framework actually needs:**
- A library of pre-written, proportionate responses to common AI DDQ questions
- A process for updating responses when your AI toolset changes
- A way to demonstrate governance without over-committing to things you can't deliver

### Gap 3: No Sub-Consultant AI Oversight

If you bring in a specialist on a project — a sector expert, a researcher, a technical advisor — and they're using AI tools, you have a chain of accountability problem. You don't know what they're using. You may not have a contractual basis to ask. But if their AI-assisted output ends up in your client deliverable, it's your name on the cover.

This is manageable. You need:
- A basic AI clause in your sub-consultant agreements
- A data processing agreement if they're handling personal data (this is a legal requirement under nFADP, not optional)
- A minimum standard for how AI-assisted work must be documented

None of this needs to be complicated. It needs to exist.

### Gap 4: No Methodology Defensibility Protocol

Here's where most small firms are exposed: if a client later claims that a recommendation you made was flawed because it was influenced by an AI error — how do you defend your methodology?

You probably can't. Because you haven't documented how you use AI in your work. What prompts you ran. What you reviewed. What you rejected. What your expert judgment added.

This isn't about protecting yourself from liability. It's about being able to explain, clearly and credibly, exactly what you did and why.

**A basic methodology defensibility protocol needs:**
- A statement on how AI is used in research and analysis (and how it's not)
- A review standard for AI-assisted outputs
- A documentation practice — not of everything, but of key AI-assisted conclusions and the expert judgment applied to them

### Gap 5: No Insurance Navigation Strategy

When your renewal comes with AI-related questions, you're probably answering them for the first time, under pressure, without context. And when your insurer adds an exclusion you didn't expect, you're signing it because you don't have a alternative.

The firms that will get favorable insurance treatment in the next 2–3 years are the ones that can show:
- They use AI intentionally, with governance
- They have documentation
- They've thought through the liability questions

This isn't about getting insurance cheap. It's about not being automatically excluded because the carrier can't distinguish between a firm using AI recklessly and a firm using AI with controls.

## Part 3: What "Right-Size" AI Governance Actually Means

I'm not suggesting you build a compliance department. I'm suggesting you build enough governance that you can answer three questions honestly and confidently:

**1. What AI tools do you use, and why?**
You should have a short document that answers this. Not a policy treatise. A clear, honest statement.

**2. How do you ensure the work you deliver is sound?**
You should have a review standard — not complicated, but explicit. Something you can show a client if asked.

**3. Who is accountable?**
As the managing partner, you are. But accountability requires knowledge. You should know what AI tools are being used in your firm, by whom, and on what data.

That's it. Three questions. The governance that flows from answering them honestly is proportionate to a small firm's actual risk profile.
