# 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

**Phase:** 03 — Geneva Consulting (2–50 employees)
**Cycle:** 293
**Date:** 2026-05-29

---

## Current Market Problems and Failures

This file documents the observable market failures, complaints, and pain points circulating in the Swiss Romande consulting market — specifically affecting small boutiques (2–50 employees) in Geneva.

---

## Problem Cluster 1: Clients Are Sending AI DDQs and Consultants Don't Know How to Respond

**Signal:**
Financial institutions, large corporates, and institutional clients in Geneva are now sending formal AI governance due diligence questionnaires (AI DDQs) to all service providers — including small boutiques. This is being driven by:
- FINMA's increased focus on AI governance for regulated financial entities
- EU AI Act compliance obligations flowing down supply chains
- Internal risk management functions at client organizations being told to inventory AI use by vendors

**Complaint:**
Small consulting firms are receiving questionnaires with 20–60 questions covering:
- What AI tools the firm uses in client engagements
- How AI-derived outputs are validated and reviewed
- Data handling and storage practices for AI-processed information
- Whether the firm has an AI governance policy
- Sub-consultant AI usage and oversight procedures

Most firms in the 2–50 employee range have no documented answers to these questions. They are being forced to either:
1. Scramble to create policies and documentation from scratch under time pressure
2. Answer incompletely or vaguely, risking being flagged as non-compliant
3. Lose the client engagement because they can't meet the client's vendor requirements

**Pain Point:** There is no "SME-friendly" AI DDQ template that small consulting firms can use to demonstrate proportionate compliance. The existing frameworks (ISO 42001, MME questionnaire, INQ Consulting tool) are designed for larger organizations or M&A contexts.

---

## Problem Cluster 2: Professional Liability Insurers Are Adding AI Exclusions Without Clear Guidance

**Signal:**
Insurance brokers and carriers operating in the Swiss market have begun:
- Adding AI-specific exclusions or conditions to professional liability (PL) policies upon renewal
- Sending supplementary questionnaires asking about AI tool usage in client deliverables
- Introducing policy language that creates ambiguity about whether AI-assisted work is covered

**Complaint:**
Small boutique consultants report:
- Receiving renewal notices with new AI-related clause language they don't understand
- Being asked questions like "Do you use AI to produce client deliverables?" with no clear guidance on what "yes" or "no" means for their coverage
- Fear that admitting AI use might void their coverage or increase premiums significantly
- Fear that denying AI use when they do use it constitutes misrepresentation
- No access to brokers who understand the specific risk profile of a small consulting firm using consumer-grade AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.)

**Pain Point:** There is no standard, proportionate insurance framework for AI use by small professional services firms in Switzerland. The insurance industry is treating AI as a novel, undefined risk and responding with broad exclusions rather than nuanced, risk-based coverage products.

---

## Problem Cluster 3: Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Creates Undefined Liability Chains

**Signal:**
Small Geneva consulting firms routinely delegate parts of client engagements to sub-consultants — specialists, researchers, analysts, industry experts. As these sub-consultants adopt AI tools, the primary firm loses visibility into how work is being produced.

**Complaint:**
- Partners don't know what AI tools their sub-consultants are using
- There's no standard AI clause for sub-consultant contracts
- The primary firm's client contracts don't account for AI-assisted sub-delegation
- If an AI-assisted sub-consultant output contains errors, biases, or leads to client harm, the primary firm bears contractual and reputational responsibility despite having no control over the AI tool used
- DPAs (Data Processing Agreements) required under nFADP are not systematically put in place with sub-consultants who handle personal data

**Pain Point:** The market lacks simple, ready-to-use sub-consultant AI clauses that small firms can drop into their contracts. Legal advisors charge significant fees to draft bespoke clauses, making compliance economically impractical for small firms.

---

## Problem Cluster 4: nFADP Personal Liability Creates Partner-Level Anxiety

**Signal:**
The revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP), in force since September 1, 2023, introduced a provision not present in GDPR: **personal criminal liability for managers and executives** for intentional data protection breaches — with fines up to CHF 250,000.

**Complaint:**
- Small firm partners are realizing their personal assets may be at risk if AI-assisted data processing results in a breach
- Many don't know that AI tools processing client data may constitute "high-risk processing" requiring DPIAs
- There's confusion about whether using a US-based AI tool (e.g., ChatGPT via API) constitutes a data transfer to a country without adequate protection
- Privacy by Design and Privacy by Default principles embedded in nFADP require proactive measures that most small firms haven't implemented

**Pain Point:** The nFADP's personal liability provisions have not been clearly communicated to the small consulting community. Partners are learning about their personal exposure reactively — often after a near-miss or a client question — rather than proactively.

---

## Problem Cluster 5: AI "Hallucinations" Threaten Consulting Methodology Credibility

**Signal:**
Large language models used in consulting work (research, analysis, synthesis, first drafts) are known to produce plausible but incorrect outputs — "hallucinations." When these appear in client deliverables, the consulting firm's credibility is damaged regardless of the firm's other quality controls.

**Complaint:**
- Consultants have experienced or witnessed AI-generated citations, statistics, or case references that don't exist
- Traditional review processes aren't designed to catch AI hallucinations at scale
- The consulting industry's value proposition is partly built on expert knowledge and judgment — AI hallucinations undermine this directly
- There's no defensible protocol for how to use AI in research without exposing the firm to factual error in deliverables

**Pain Point:** The market lacks a clear, practical protocol for AI use in consulting research that small firms can implement. Most guidance either over-restricts ("don't use AI for anything") or under-restricts ("just use AI and review it").

---

## Problem Cluster 6: No Clear "Right Size" Framework for AI Governance

**Signal:**
Existing AI governance frameworks — ISO/IEC 42001, EU AI Act, FINMA Guidance 08/2024 — are designed for larger organizations. Small consulting boutiques in Geneva have no governance standard calibrated to their size, complexity, and risk profile.

**Complaint:**
- Small firms feel they are either over-complying (implementing enterprise frameworks that cost more than their annual revenue) or under-complying (ignoring governance entirely because no SME guidance exists)
- They don't know what "good enough" AI governance looks like for a 5-person firm vs. a 30-person firm
- There's a gap between generic AI governance advice and enterprise-scale compliance programs

**Pain Point:** The consulting industry's own professional bodies (e.g., Swiss Consulting Society, Chambers of Commerce) have not produced size-appropriate AI governance guidance for small member firms.

---

## Summary of NET-NEW Findings (C293 Phase 03)

1. **AI DDQ anxiety is now a primary sales blocker** — small Geneva consulting firms are losing or avoiding client engagements because they can't respond to client AI governance questionnaires with adequate documentation.

2. **Insurance AI exclusions are being added silently** — renewals contain new AI clauses that small firms sign without understanding how it affects their coverage for AI-assisted work.

3. **Sub-consultant AI chains are invisible and unregulated** — no DPA, no AI policy, no oversight mechanism for sub-consultant AI use in client engagements.

4. **nFADP personal liability (CHF 250,000) is under-communicated** — most small partners in Geneva have not internalized that their personal assets may be at risk for AI-related data processing decisions.

5. **AI hallucinations in consulting deliverables are an emerging credibility risk** — no practical, proportionate protocol exists for small firms to use AI in research defensibly.

6. **The "right-size AI governance" gap is a genuine market opportunity** — no one is offering proportionate, SME-calibrated AI governance frameworks specifically for small Swiss consulting boutiques.
