# Findings Summary — Geneva Legal (Phase 01, C302)

## ICP Profile
Small law firm, 2–5 attorneys, Geneva-based. Managing partner or senior associate personally accountable for AI decisions. Concerned about attorney-client privilege in AI systems, carrier PI attestation, bar guidance gaps, and what "adequate" AI governance means at boutique scale.

---

## Key Insights

### Insight 1: Carrier Form v4 Has Created an Evidence Debt That Cannot Be Retroactively Resolved

**The finding:**
Carrier form v4 requires "last three authorization records" as evidence standard — not policy attestation. The ICP does not have these records. The systems that would produce them were never built. Retroactive reconstruction is structurally impossible because the underlying data capture infrastructure doesn't exist.

**Why it matters:**
The ICP is being asked to personally sign an attestation that requires evidence they cannot produce. This is not a compliance gap — it is a personal liability moment. The evidence debt is real, accumulated, and cannot be paid down through normal work.

**Ready-to-use language:**
*"The form asks for your last three authorization records. Not your next three — your last three. That's the evidence your carrier is asking for right now."*

**Market implication:**
The ICP needs evidence reconstruction, not policy templates. The market opportunity is a system that produces carrier-compatible evidence without requiring the ICP to stop billing clients.

---

### Insight 2: The Junior-Senior Fluency Inversion Creates a Structural Accountability Gap

**The finding:**
In a 2–5 person Geneva firm, the person who signs carrier attestations (senior partner) is not the person who uses AI tools daily (junior associate). The accountable person lacks the technical fluency to independently verify AI outputs. The person with the fluency lacks the accountability. This inversion is structural — it cannot be resolved by "supervising better" without tools.

**Why it matters:**
SBA guidelines require supervision of AI output. But "adequate supervision" for a senior attorney who cannot independently verify AI output is undefined. The ICP is personally liable for supervision they cannot actually perform.

**Ready-to-use language:**
*"You sign the forms. She uses the tools. The person accountable doesn't have the fluency. The person with the fluency isn't the one signing."*

**Market implication:**
The ICP needs a supervision support system — not a lecture on responsibility, but a tool that makes the accountable senior partner operationally capable of supervising AI output they cannot independently verify.

---

### Insight 3: Vendor Contract Trap Prevents Historical Resolution Through Switching

**The finding:**
AI vendor contracts signed in 2023–2024 often include data retention for model training provisions. The ICP discovered these terms after signing. Switching vendors does not resolve historical data exposure — the data has already been retained. New contracts don't retroactively fix past terms. The trap is permanent in its historical dimension.

**Why it matters:**
The ICP understands there is a problem with their vendor stack but cannot unwind it. The only resolution is forward-looking evidence systems that prevent future exposure accumulation while acknowledging past exposure cannot be erased.

**Ready-to-use language:**
*"You signed the agreement. It seemed standard. Six months later you realize your client data is in a training set. You can't undo that. You can only build forward."*

**Market implication:**
Evidence reconstruction services must acknowledge the vendor contract trap as a permanent condition, not offer false resolution through switching. The ICP needs to understand that past exposure exists but that forward-looking systems reduce future accumulation.

---

### Insight 4: The Geneva Label Functions as a Quality Threshold Marker

**The finding:**
Geneva ICPs understand "Geneva standard" as a quality marker — clients and counterparties expect Geneva firms to meet a higher documentation standard. ODAGE's March 21 event ("IA comme ICARE — L'avocate.sen s'approcher sans se brûler") confirms professional body acknowledgment of the problem but absence of solution supply. The demand for Geneva-standard guidance is acute; the supply is thin.

**Why it matters:**
The ICP wants to demonstrate Geneva-standard AI governance but doesn't know what that standard is. "Geneva standard" is aspirational but undefined in AI documentation context. Defining it first positions you as the authority.

**Ready-to-use language:**
*"Clients ask about your AI tools now. You need a coherent answer — one that sounds Geneva-standard, not just checkbox compliance."*

**Market implication:**
Being the first to define "Geneva-standard AI governance documentation" for small firms creates first-mover authority advantage. The ICP will seek out whoever defines the standard.

---

### Insight 5: Peer Enforcement Events Are the Conversion Trigger, Not Regulatory Awareness

**The finding:**
U.S. federal court rulings on AI-generated legal citations (fabricated case citations leading to sanctions) are being discussed in Geneva professional circles. ODAGE held a specific event on March 21 about approaching AI "without burning." The documented peer enforcement event converts abstract AI anxiety into specific purchase intent in a way that regulatory awareness does not.

**Why it matters:**
The ICP who has been avoiding the carrier attestation problem will take action when they see a peer face consequences. The peer enforcement story makes the personal liability concrete in a way that abstract regulatory frameworks do not.

**Ready-to-use language:**
*"A lawyer in a U.S. federal court submitted AI-generated citations. The cases didn't exist. The court sanctioned the attorney. Not the AI. The attorney. That's the exposure you're managing."*

**Market implication:**
Conversion happens when the ICP recognizes themselves in the enforcement story. Using specific, documented peer events (not hypotheticals) in outreach triggers the conversion mechanism.

---

### Insight 6: June 19 SBA Day of Lawyers Creates a 2–3 Week Acute Engagement Window

**The finding:**
The SBA Day of Lawyers on June 19 (approximately two weeks from research date) creates a forcing function for self-assessment. The ICP attending this event will be in a peer context where AI governance is being discussed. Abstract concern converts to action in peer context. The 2–3 week window before this event is the highest-probability conversion period.

**Why it matters:**
Outreach in the 2 weeks before June 19 has the highest conversion probability. The ICP who hasn't dealt with carrier attestation will face peer comparison at the event. They will act before the event to avoid being the person without documentation in that room.

**Ready-to-use language:**
*"June 19 is two weeks away. You're going to be in a room with colleagues who have dealt with this. The question isn't whether to act — it's whether to act before the event or after."*

**Market implication:**
This cycle's outreach should be timed to land in the 2-week window before June 19. The timing is strategic, not arbitrary.

## Strategic Recommendations

1. **Time outreach for June 19 window** — 2–3 weeks before the SBA Day of Lawyers is the highest-conversion period for this ICP

2. **Lead with evidence problem, not framework** — the ICP responds to "you need authorization records and you don't have them" more than "you need an AI governance framework"

3. **Use peer enforcement stories** — documented enforcement events (U.S. federal court citations) convert abstract concern to action; regulatory frameworks do not

4. **Define "Geneva standard" first** — whoever defines the documentation standard for small Geneva firms becomes the authority; this is a first-mover opportunity

5. **Address the accountability gap directly** — acknowledge that the senior partner signing the form cannot independently verify AI output; position the solution as making supervision operationally possible

6. **Don't offer false resolution on vendor contracts** — acknowledge the trap is permanent for historical exposure; offer forward-looking evidence systems, not vendor-switching solutions
