## Top 6 NET-NEW Findings (C291 Phase 03)

### Finding 1: AI Governance Is Actually Liability Governance

The Geneva small consulting ICP does not think about AI governance as a technology problem. They think about it as a **personal liability problem**.

Every concern — client questionnaires, carrier attestation, sub-consultant delegation, methodology documentation — is ultimately about one question: *what happens to me personally if this goes wrong?*

**Ready-to-use language:** "This isn't about AI strategy. It's about making sure that when a client asks how you produced something — or when a carrier asks what you're using — you have an answer that protects you, not one that exposes you."

**Implication:** All positioning, all content, all offers must be framed in liability terms, not efficiency terms. The Geneva managing director responds to: "how does this protect me?" not "how does this make me faster?"

---

### Finding 2: Client AI Questionnaires Are a Leading Indicator of Seriousness

The client AI questionnaire is the first concrete touchpoint where AI risk becomes operational for a small consulting firm.

Every firm that's received one has:
- Answered it without full understanding of what they committed to
- Created a paper trail they can't actually support
- Not gone back to document their position after the fact

**Ready-to-use language:** "The questionnaire is the least of your problems. The problem is what happens when the client asks you to show them your AI governance documentation — and you don't have any."

**Implication:** The questionnaire support service is a low-friction entry point. A firm that received a questionnaire is already motivated. They don't need to be convinced AI governance matters — they're already feeling the pressure. Meeting them there with a specific, actionable response is the highest-probability first engagement.

---

### Finding 3: Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Is the Blind Spot

The most unmanaged risk in small consulting operations is the **sub-consultant AI relationship**.

No framework exists. No contract language exists. No disclosure standard exists. Primary firms are routinely accepting AI-generated deliverables from sub-consultants without:
- Knowing which tools were used
- Having documentation of the process
- Understanding their liability position
- Being able to verify the quality

**Ready-to-use language:** "You're responsible for everything that goes out under your name. That includes work produced by your sub-consultants. If their AI produces an error and you deliver it to the client, it's your reputation, your client, your liability. And you probably have no idea what their AI actually did."

**Implication:** The sub-consultant AI delegation framework is a standalone product that no one else is offering. It's specific, implementable, and directly addresses a risk the managing director feels but hasn't had language to name.

---

### Finding 4: Methodology Documentation Is the Core Deliverable

Every risk in the ICP's AI exposure can be addressed by one thing: **the ability to document how a deliverable was produced**.

Not a formal quality system. Not an enterprise compliance framework. A simple, practical documentation habit that fits into actual project delivery.

The managing directors who have this are protected. The ones who don't are exposed — regardless of how careful they think they are being with AI.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Documentation isn't bureaucracy. It's the thing that separates 'we used AI carefully' from 'we have no idea what happened.' When a client asks how you produced something, the answer isn't an explanation — it's a document."

**Implication:** The methodology documentation system — a template, a process, a habit — is the single highest-leverage intervention for a small consulting firm. It addresses carrier attestation, client questionnaires, methodology defensibility, and nFADP documentation requirements simultaneously.

---

### Finding 5: Personal Liability Under nFADP Is the Unspoken Fear

The revised nFADP is live. The transition period is over. Personal liability for managing directors is real. And almost no small Geneva consulting firm has addressed it properly.

The fear is:
- Not knowing whether they're compliant
- Not knowing what documentation they need
- Not knowing what happens to them personally if there's a data incident involving AI tools

**Ready-to-use language:** "The nFADP isn't an abstract regulatory risk. It's a personal liability risk. If a data incident occurs and you can't show documentation of your data protection measures, the burden of proof falls on you — and the consequences are personal. CHF 250,000 fines. Personal liability. Your name on the door means your name on the problem."

**Implication:** nFADP personal liability framing is a high-conversion entry point. It's specific, Swiss, personal, and has a clear trigger event (the transition period ended). It speaks directly to the managing director's deepest concern: being personally exposed for something they didn't fully understand.

---

### Finding 6: The Language Gap Is a Trust Gap

No existing AI governance content is written:
- For small consulting firms (not enterprises)
- In the managing director's voice (not a compliance officer's voice)
- In liability terms (not efficiency terms)
- In Swiss context (nFADP, not just GDPR)
- In English and French (Geneva's two business languages)

The content that exists is either too enterprise, too technology-focused, too generic, or too one-language to build trust with this ICP.

**Ready-to-use language:** "I've read the AI governance guides. They're written for companies with legal departments. They're not written for a 12-person firm where the managing director is personally on the line for everything that goes out the door."

**Implication:** Bilingual, liability-focused, boutique-specific content is a trust-building asset that no competitor is providing. It is the foundation of the entire lead nurturing sequence. It is not marketing — it is the first proof point of the difference between DigitalCoa.ch and every other AI governance provider.
