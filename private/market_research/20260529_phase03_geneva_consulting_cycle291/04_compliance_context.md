## Swiss Regulatory Framework for Small Consulting Firms

### Overview

Geneva-based small consulting firms (2–50 employees) operate in one of Europe's most sophisticated regulatory environments. The relevant compliance landscape for AI accountability spans three primary layers:

1. **Swiss Data Protection (nFADP)** — the Swiss-specific framework, not GDPR
2. **EU AI Act** — indirectly applicable for firms working with EU-based clients
3. **Professional Liability Standards** — carrier requirements, not regulatory but practically binding

---

### nFADP (Loi Fédérale sur la Protection des Données)

**Current Status:** The revised nFADP entered into force on 1 September 2023, with a transition period extending to 1 September 2024 for full compliance.

**Key Obligations for Small Consulting Firms:**

#### Data Controller Responsibilities

When a consulting firm uses AI tools in client engagements, they typically act as the **data controller** — determining the purposes and means of processing personal data.

This means:
- The firm is responsible for ensuring lawful processing
- The AI tool vendor is typically a data processor
- The managing director can bear personal liability for breaches
- Documentation of the processing relationship is required

**Practical implication:** Using an AI tool (e.g., ChatGPT, Claude, Midjourney) for client work means the firm must have:
- A documented basis for processing (consent, contract, legitimate interest)
- A data processing agreement with the AI vendor
- Data minimization controls (not sending more client data than necessary)
- A breach notification procedure

#### What Small Firms Are Actually Doing (vs. What's Required)

**Reality:** Most 2–50 person consulting firms in Geneva are not fully compliant with nFADP regarding AI tool use. They:
- Use consumer AI tools without data processing agreements
- Send client information to US-based AI vendors without clear legal basis
- Have no documented data minimization policy
- Don't know if they're acting as controller or processor in specific contexts

**Why:** The complexity is real and the guidance is insufficient. Enterprise-grade compliance frameworks don't translate to boutique operations.

#### The Personal Liability Angle

The revised nFADP includes provisions for administrative sanctions. For managing directors of small firms:
- Fines can reach CHF 250,000 for serious violations
- Personal liability is possible when the director is directly responsible
- The burden of demonstrating compliance falls on the firm

**What this means in practice:** A managing director who cannot show documentation of their AI data protection measures has no defense when asked.

### EU AI Act (Indirect Application)

**Status:** The EU AI Act entered into force in August 2024, with provisions phased in through 2027.

**Why It Matters to Geneva Firms:** Even if the firm itself is not EU-based, working with EU-based clients creates indirect obligations:

- Clients may ask about EU AI Act compliance
- Proposals and deliverables may need to meet EU AI Act standards
- Sub-consultants working on EU engagements may be subject to EU AI Act obligations

**Relevant Provisions for Consulting Firms:**

- **Transparency obligations** — users of certain AI systems must be informed they are interacting with AI
- **Documentation requirements** — high-risk AI systems require documented risk assessments
- **Human oversight** — systems must allow for human intervention

**Practical implication:** A Geneva firm using AI in client work needs to be able to demonstrate human oversight and transparency. Most current AI use by small consultancies doesn't meet these standards.

### Professional Liability Carrier Requirements

**Current State:** Carrier AI provisions are:
- Vague ("Do you use AI?")
- Binary (yes/no)
- Not accompanied by guidance on what compliant AI use looks like

**What Carriers Are Actually Asking:**

Typical questions on proposal/renewal forms:
1. "Do you use artificial intelligence in your consulting work?"
2. "If yes, describe your AI governance procedures."
3. "Do you have documentation of AI tool usage in client engagements?"
4. "How do you verify AI-generated outputs?"

**The Problem:** A firm that answers "yes" to AI use without documentation is in a worse position than a firm that answers "no" — because they've created a paper trail of undocumented AI use.

**What managing directors tell us:** They answer the carrier's questions as vaguely as possible because they don't know what the carrier actually expects and don't want to commit to something they can't deliver.

### The Geneva Regulatory Culture

Geneva has a specific regulatory culture that shapes how small consulting firms approach compliance:

- **Precision over volume** — they prefer fewer, more accurate actions over many approximate ones
- **Documentation as protection** — a well-documented decision is better than an undocumented perfect decision
- **Personal accountability** — managing directors expect to be personally responsible and plan accordingly
- **Skepticism of generic frameworks** — they distrust solutions that don't account for their specific context

**Implication for AI governance:** A compliance framework for Geneva small consulting firms must be:
- Precise and specific (not generic)
- Documented (with paper trails that protect, not expose)
- Calibrated to personal liability (not abstract corporate compliance)
- Adapted to the firm's actual size and operations
- Available in English and French (Geneva's business languages)

---

### The Compliance Gap

**The gap is not knowledge — it is implementation.**

Geneva managing directors are not unaware of AI risks. They are not ignoring compliance. They are:

- Overwhelmed by the complexity of enterprise-grade frameworks
- Uncertain about which rules apply to a firm their size
- Unable to find guidance in their language (EN/FR)
- Without a clear, implementable path that fits their operations
- Concerned about personal liability in ways that generic compliance guides don't address

**The market opportunity:** Bridge the gap between regulatory awareness and implementable practice. Give them a framework that fits a 12-person firm, speaks to their liability concerns, and can actually be operationalized without a dedicated compliance team.
