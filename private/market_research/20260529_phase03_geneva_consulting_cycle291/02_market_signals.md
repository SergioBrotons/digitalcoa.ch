# Market Signals: Geneva Small Consulting Boutiques
## Phase 03 — C291 | DigitalCoa.ch Market Research

---

## Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

### 1. The Client AI Questionnaire Problem

**Signal:** Geneva consulting firms are being hit with AI governance questionnaires from clients — particularly in finance, pharma, and multinational sectors. These questionnaires are often:

- Inconsistent in format and question framing
- Designed by the client's legal or procurement team without AI expertise
- Asking for information the consulting firm doesn't know how to provide
- Creating a paper trail the firm doesn't understand

**The failure mode:** A managing director fills in the questionnaire without knowing if their answers create liability. They say "we use AI responsibly" because it's the safest answer. The client accepts it. But if something goes wrong later, the consulting firm has no documentation showing what "responsible" actually means.

**What they feel:** Unprepared, exposed, unclear about what they committed to.

---

### 2. The Methodology Defensibility Problem

**Signal:** Consultants producing proposals and deliverables are increasingly unable to explain — to clients, to carriers, to courts — exactly how a piece of work was produced.

The problem is not that they're using AI. The problem is:
- They don't know what the AI actually did
- They can't reproduce the exact output from the same input
- They have no documentation trail
- The deliverable looks like it was produced by a human, but it wasn't

**The failure mode:** A client questions a recommendation. The consultant can't explain the methodology. The AI-generated component can't be distinguished from the human-generated component. Trust collapses.

**What they feel:** That they have lost control of their own work product.

---

### 3. The Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Problem

**Signal:** Small Geneva firms regularly work with sub-consultants and freelancers. Many of these now use AI tools. The primary firm has no visibility into:
- Which sub-consultants are using AI
- Which tools they're using
- What quality controls they have
- What their liability exposure is when AI-generated work is delivered under the primary firm's name

**The failure mode:** The primary firm delivers a report containing AI-generated content from a sub-consultant. The content is wrong. The client holds the primary firm responsible. The sub-consultant is judgment-proof or unreachable.

**What they feel:** Exposed for something they didn't know was happening.

---

### 4. The Carrier Attestation Problem

**Signal:** Professional liability carriers are now asking specific questions about AI use in proposal and renewal forms. These questions are:
- Vague ("Do you use AI in your work?")
- Binary (yes/no when the reality is nuanced)
- Potentially binding without clear understanding of implications

**The failure mode:** A firm answers "yes" to AI use and doesn't know if they've just expanded their coverage obligations. A firm answers "no" and later a claim arises from AI use they didn't disclose. Either way, the firm is in a bad position.

**What they feel:** That they're signing something they don't understand, and it could come back to cost them dearly.

---

### 5. The Personal Liability Problem (nFADP)

**Signal:** Swiss consultants are increasingly aware that the nFADP (Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection) creates personal liability for managing directors. When AI tools process client data:
- The consulting firm may be the data controller
- The AI vendor may be the data processor
- The managing director may bear personal responsibility for breaches
- The documentation requirements are unclear and complex

**The failure mode:** A data incident occurs. The carrier asks what data protection measures were in place. The managing director can't show documentation. Personal liability follows.

**What they feel:** Alone in understanding a regulation that feels designed for large enterprises, not 12-person boutiques.

---

### 6. The Reputational Risk Problem

**Signal:** Geneva consulting firms are acutely aware of their reputation. They built it over years. AI adoption creates a new failure mode: producing a deliverable that looks competent but contains errors — errors that a client or peer will notice.

**The failure mode:** An AI-generated section contains a factual error. The client notices. The firm's reputation for quality — their core asset — is damaged. The error has an AI fingerprint, but the firm takes the blame.

**What they feel:** That AI is offering them efficiency while creating a new class of reputational risk they have no tools to manage.

---

## Key Pattern: The Disconnect

The market signal that underlies all of these: **there is a massive gap between how fast Geneva consulting firms are adopting AI tools and how fast they're building the governance infrastructure to support that adoption.**

They are using AI faster than they're understanding it. They are deploying it in client work faster than they're documenting it. They are relying on it faster than they're verifying it.

This gap is the market opportunity.
