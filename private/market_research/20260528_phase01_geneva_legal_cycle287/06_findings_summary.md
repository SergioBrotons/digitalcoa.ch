## 6 NET-NEW Findings for C287 Phase 01

### Finding 1: The Carrier Attestation Is the Entry Point, Not the Finish Line

Small Geneva firms are encountering AI governance questions for the first time through carrier renewal forms — not through strategic planning. This means the trigger for action is external (insurer pressure), not internal (strategic intent).

**Implication:** The first touchpoint is often a moment of anxiety ("what do I answer here?"). Whoever answers that question with a credible, actionable solution wins the relationship. The bar has not provided this. Carriers have not provided this. The gap is the opportunity.

**Use this language:**
> "Your carrier is asking questions about AI governance. The form is a disclosure, not a formality. Answering it accurately — and having the documentation to back it up — is now part of your professional obligation."

---

### Finding 2: nFADP Art. 9 Is the Underappreciated Legal Risk

Most small firm attorney awareness of nFADP is GDPR-adjacent (general data protection). What is less understood is Art. 9's specific third-party processor regime — and that using a US-based AI tool with client data creates a formal data controller/processor relationship that requires a compliant DPA.

**Implication:** The DPA requirement is not optional and is not satisfied by the vendor's standard terms of service. Small firms are unknowingly in breach. The FDPIC has enforcement authority. This is not theoretical.

**Use this language:**
> "When you upload client data to a US-based AI tool, you're a data controller under nFADP Art. 9. The vendor's standard contract almost certainly doesn't meet the requirements. You need a compliant data processing agreement — and most vendors won't give you one unless you know to ask for it."

---

### Finding 3: "Anonymization" Is Not a Safe Harbor

Multiple attorneys have asked "if I anonymize the data, am I covered?" The honest answer is: no Swiss court has ruled on this. The legal theory is untested. The SBA guidelines mention anonymization as a technical measure but do not establish it as a privilege protection safe harbor. Continuing to rely on anonymization as a primary defense is increasingly risky.

**Implication:** The better strategy is tool compliance (use tools that meet the confidentiality standard), not data manipulation as a workaround.

**Use this language:**
> "Anonymization reduces risk but doesn't eliminate it. No Swiss court has tested whether anonymized client data in a general-purpose AI tool preserves privilege. The safer path is using AI tools built to meet professional confidentiality standards — not trying to engineer your way around a tool that wasn't designed for attorneys."

---

### Finding 4: Staff Shadow AI Is the Governance Blind Spot

Small firms have a structural vulnerability: the managing partner or senior attorney does not know what AI tools their staff are using. This is not a technology problem — it's an organizational visibility problem. No formal inventory exists, no approval process for new tools, no audit trail.

**Implication:** The governance framework must include a simple, low-friction tool inventory and staff acknowledgment process — not an IT system, a lightweight policy mechanism that works at boutique scale.

**Use this language:**
> "Your paralegal is using a free AI tool on client matters. You don't know about it. When your carrier asks what AI tools you use, you have two bad options: say yes and not know what you're attesting to, or say no and lie. There's a third option: know what tools are in use, document them, and govern them. That's what this framework does."

---

### Finding 5: The SBA Guidelines Create Obligation Without Providing Implementation

The June 2024 SBA guidelines are the closest thing to an authoritative standard that exists. They are also principle-based and operationally empty for small firms. An attorney reads "maintain confidentiality" and "remain responsible" and has no actionable next step.

**Implication:** Anyone who provides the operational translation of the SBA guidelines into a small-firm implementable framework will have high credibility. The guidelines are the reference point — the gap between principles and practice is where value lives.

**Use this language:**
> "The SBA guidelines say you must maintain confidentiality and remain responsible for AI-assisted work. They don't tell you what that looks like on a Tuesday. I translated them into a checklist that works for a 3-attorney Geneva study — because I needed it and couldn't find it."

---

### Finding 6: The AI Convention Creates Future Urgency That Can Drive Present Action

The Council of Europe AI Convention (Treaty No. 225), signed by Switzerland, will create binding deployer obligations for AI systems. The obligations include accountability, human oversight, and transparency documentation. These are not yet enforced — but ratification is progressing.

**Implication:** The AI Convention creates a future enforcement horizon that can be used to justify present action to a risk-averse attorney ("you'll need this anyway when the Convention applies"). It also signals that the regulatory landscape is not going to simplify — governance requirements will increase, not decrease.

**Use this language:**
> "The Council of Europe AI Convention will create binding deployer obligations for firms using AI systems. It won't be enforced tomorrow — but it will be enforced. Building your governance framework now means you're ready when it applies. It also means you're already compliant with what carriers and the SBA are already asking for."
