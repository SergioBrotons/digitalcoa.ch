# 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Complaints, Failures

**Phase:** C311 Phase 01 | **ICP:** Geneva Boutique, 2–5 Attorneys | **Date:** 2026-05-31

---

## Signal 1: The Carrier Attestation Has Changed Question Type

**Observed pattern (C311 NET-NEW):** Multiple carriers have modified their AI attestation forms between Q3 2024 and Q2 2026. The change is not cosmetic — it is structural. Forms that previously asked binary yes/no questions (Do you use AI? Yes/No) have been replaced with open-form questions that require descriptive answers.

**Example of what the new form asks:**
> *"Describe your firm's protocols for documenting AI use across client matters. Include the tools used, the purposes for which they were employed, and the internal review process for outputs generated with AI assistance."*

**The problem this creates:**
A firm that answered the old form in 30 seconds ("Yes, we use AI") cannot answer the new form without a comprehensive documentation infrastructure they do not have. The attestation they signed in 2024 does not prepare them for the renewal question in 2026.

**The market signal:** This is not random carrier behavior. This is carrier underwriting responding to claims data, regulatory pressure, and reinsurance scrutiny. The attestation arms race has begun — and boutiques are unprepared.

---

## Signal 2: Mid-June Bar Survey (OaF — Reported)

**Observed pattern:** The Ordre des Avocats de Genève (OaF) issued a survey in mid-June 2025 regarding AI use in member firms. The survey was described in professional circles as "voluntary" and "informational." 

**The market signal:** Whatever the OaF learns from that survey will become the basis for future guidance. Firms that participated have already demonstrated awareness. Firms that did not participate have no standing in the conversation about what "adequate" looks like.

**Why this matters for C311:** The survey results are likely being analyzed at time of writing (Q2 2026). Guidance is anticipated. The market does not yet know what the guidance will say — but it knows the guidance is coming.

## Signal 3: "Authorized User" Problem in Boutique Firms

**Observed pattern:** When a carrier form asks "Who in your firm uses AI tools?" the answer in a 3-attorney Geneva boutique is almost always: "the junior associate, on their own laptop."

**The structural failure:**
- The AI tool (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) is licensed to the individual, not to the firm
- The firm has no visibility into what tools are being used
- The firm has no way to audit outputs
- The firm has no retention policy for AI-generated content
- The attorney-client privilege question is completely unaddressed

**The market signal:** Boutique firms have an unauthorized AI surface that is invisible to the managing partner. This is not a compliance failure — it is a structural feature of how small firms work. The junior associate uses what works. The managing partner doesn't know. The carrier doesn't know. No one is managing this surface.

---

## Signal 4: Retroactive Reconstruction Is Already Becoming a Problem

**Observed pattern (C311 NET-NEW):** Firms that are now being asked for documentation of past AI use (carrier renewal, matter review, privilege audit) are attempting to reconstruct records that do not exist.

**The failure mode:**
- No timestamp on AI tool usage
- No record of which client matters involved AI
- No way to distinguish AI-assisted work from fully manual work
- No chain of custody for AI-generated drafts

**The market signal:** The firms trying to reconstruct are discovering that retroactive documentation is:
1. Technically possible but time-consuming and expensive
2. Legally fragile — reconstructed records carry no evidentiary weight
3. Already required — they just didn't know it when they signed

**The firms that will be in the worst position:** Those that signed attestations without any subsequent documentation practice, and are now facing a carrier question or bar audit with zero records.

---

## Signal 5: Geneva Boutique Firms Are Structurally Excluded from AI Governance Conversations

**Observed pattern:** AI governance content, frameworks, tools, and service providers are designed for larger firms. The Geneva boutique (2–5 attorneys) encounters a market that:
- Speaks enterprise compliance language
- References frameworks they don't use (SOC 2, ISO 27001, NIST)
- Assumes a compliance department they don't have
- Prices services at a level that signals "not for you"

**The market signal:** The Geneva boutique has self-selected out of AI governance not because they don't care — but because the market has not spoken to them in a language they recognize. The first provider that translates AI governance into "here is what you need as a 3-attorney Geneva firm, at a price that makes sense, in a language you understand" will own this segment.

---

## Signal 6: Personal Liability Is the Anxiety That Surfaces First

**Observed pattern:** In every sales conversation, discovery call, or proposal discussion with a Geneva managing partner, the first anxiety that surfaces is not "our firm is exposed" — it is "I signed this personally."

**The specific fear:** The managing partner signed the carrier attestation in their own name. They believe — correctly — that this makes them personally exposed. Not the firm. Them.

**Why this is a Geneva-specific market signal:** Switzerland's legal environment creates a specific intersection of:
- Personal professional liability for managing partners
- Carrier attestation that is signed individually
- No bar guidance creating a safe harbor
- A professional culture where individual accountability is taken seriously

**The market signal:** Any solution that does not address the personal liability dimension will not land with the Geneva managing partner. They are not worried about the firm's exposure. They are worried about their own.
