# 06 — Findings Summary: Key Insights and Ready-to-Use Language

**Phase:** C311 Phase 01 | **ICP:** Geneva Boutique, 2–5 Attorneys | **Date:** 2026-05-31

---

## NET-NEW Findings (Distinct from C214–C310)

### Finding 1: Carrier Form Structural Shift (Highest Urgency)

**The insight:** Carrier AI attestation forms have changed question type — from binary yes/no to open-form descriptive. The renewal question now requires infrastructure that does not exist in most Geneva boutiques.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "The form you signed in 2024 asked a thirty-second question. Your next renewal asks for a documentation system. These are not the same thing."

**Why it matters for positioning:** This is the immediate trigger. Every managing partner who has renewed or will renew in 2025–2026 is encountering the new form. The firms that don't know this are the most urgent outreach targets.

---

### Finding 2: OaF Survey = Future Guidance Baseline

**The insight:** The Ordre des Avocats de Genève ran a voluntary AI usage survey in mid-2025. The results are being used to shape future guidance. The participation gap creates a two-tier structure: firms that engaged are visible to the bar; firms that didn't are not.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "The OaF knows which firms participated in their AI survey. When guidance comes — and it will — they'll know who was paying attention."

**Why it matters for positioning:** This introduces urgency without alarm. The managing partner who hasn't thought about AI governance in months suddenly has a reason to act now: not because something bad is happening, but because something is being documented that will shape future requirements.

---

### Finding 3: The Junior Associate AI Surface Is the Actual Risk Source

**The insight:** In a 3-attorney Geneva boutique, the AI exposure is generated by the junior associate — on personal devices, using tools never evaluated by the firm — and the managing partner has zero visibility into it.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "Your carrier doesn't ask whether your junior associate uses AI responsibly. They ask what tools were used, on which matters, with what review. If you don't know the answer — that's the exposure."

**Why it matters for positioning:** This reframes the problem from "do we use AI" (abstract, large-firm) to "who in your firm is using AI and what are they doing with it" (concrete, personal, actionable). It identifies the specific gap without blaming the managing partner.

---

### Finding 4: Retroactive Reconstruction Is Both Possible and Legally Fragile

**The insight:** Firms that signed attestations without subsequent documentation are now being asked to reconstruct what happened. Retroactive reconstruction is technically possible but legally fragile — reconstructed records carry no evidentiary weight. The market needs both a solution for going forward AND a credible solution for firms already exposed.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "Retroactive documentation is not the same as having documented at the time. Reconstructed records don't carry the same weight. If you've already signed an attestation without documentation — we need to address that differently than if you're preparing for a future renewal."

**Why it matters for positioning:** This is where "I produce evidence" becomes concrete. The unique capability is not just building forward — it's establishing what already happened in a form that is credible and defensible.

---

### Finding 5: "Adequate" — The Word That Paralyzes Every Managing Partner

**The insight:** "Adequate" appears on every carrier attestation but has never been defined by any carrier, any bar, or any regulator. Every managing partner reads the word and has no reference point for what it means for their firm.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "The word 'adequate' on your carrier form has never been defined — not by your carrier, not by the OaF, not by anyone. 'Adequate' for a 3-attorney Geneva boutique is proportionate. But you need to be able to describe what it looks like for your firm — specifically, in writing, before your carrier asks."

**Why it matters for positioning:** This is the anxiety that surfaces in every managing partner conversation. Naming it directly — "you've been staring at the word 'adequate' for months and no one has told you what it means" — creates immediate connection and establishes the value of a specific, Geneva-sized answer.

---

### Finding 6: Personal Liability Is the Dominant Anxiety — and the Correct Framing

**The insight:** Every managing partner's first response to AI governance is personal: "I signed this in my own name." This anxiety is accurate and well-founded. The solution must address the personal liability dimension directly, not redirect to firm-level risk.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "You signed in your own name. Not the firm's. Yours. If an AI-assisted error triggers a malpractice claim — your personal license, your personal reputation, your personal assets are in the conversation. That is not abstract. It is the reason this matters."

**Why it matters for positioning:** This is where trust is established. Most AI governance providers talk about "your firm's exposure." Sergio talks about your personal exposure. This is the register the managing partner is actually in — and it is the register that creates real engagement.

## Ready-to-Use Language by ICP Moment

### First Contact (Discovery Call Opener)
> "The carrier attestation you signed — do you know exactly what it requires from you, specifically, as a 3-attorney Geneva firm?"

### Carrier Form Trigger
> "The form you signed has changed. The new version asks questions that require documentation infrastructure you may not have yet. This is the conversation to have before your renewal, not after."

### Personal Liability Anchor
> "You signed in your own name. That means this isn't about your firm's exposure — it's about yours. That's a different conversation, and it deserves a specific answer."

### "What is adequate" Resolution
> "Adequate, for a firm your size, in Geneva, means: you can describe what AI was used, by whom, on which matters, when, and what your review process was. That's the definition. Now the question is whether your documentation exists."

### OaF Urgency Signal
> "The OaF ran a survey in 2025. They know which firms participated. When their guidance arrives — and it will — the firms that already have documentation are in a different position than the ones who waited."

### Retroactive Solution (for Already-Exposed Firms)
> "Retroactive reconstruction is possible. It's not clean. But if you've already signed an attestation without documentation — the question isn't whether to do it. It's how to do it so it actually holds."

## Positioning Statement (C311 Update)

**From C310:** "The market offers frameworks. I produce evidence. Your form is the design specification."

**C311 refinement:** The carrier form is not just the design specification — it is the immediate trigger. The managing partner is not abstractly worried about "AI governance." They are specifically worried about what the form they signed actually requires, what "adequate" means for their firm, and whether their personal signature exposes them. The evidence architecture is the response to all three questions.

**Updated positioning language:**
> "The market offers frameworks. I produce evidence — and right now, your carrier's renewal form is asking for evidence you don't have. I can tell you exactly what the form requires, where your documentation stands, and what 'adequate' looks like for your firm in Geneva. That's where we start."
