# 03 — Competitive Analysis: What Exists vs. What's Missing

**Phase:** C311 Phase 01 | **ICP:** Geneva Boutique, 2–5 Attorneys | **Date:** 2026-05-31

---

## What the Market Currently Offers

### Large Firm Solutions (Not Fit for Geneva Boutique)

**Big Law AI Governance Platforms**
- Vendors like Relativity, Everlaw, Ironclad offer AI governance modules
- Price point: €50K–€500K+ annually
- Assumes: compliance team, IT infrastructure, formal document management system
- Geneva reality: A 3-attorney firm has none of these
- **Gap:** These platforms solve a large firm problem that Geneva boutiques don't have

**Generalist Cybersecurity Frameworks**
- ISO 27001, SOC 2, NIST CSF-based consultants
- Offer AI governance as a module within broader security compliance
- Price point: €20K–€100K for implementation
- Assumes: IT department, formalISMS, ongoing audit cycle
- Geneva reality: The attestation question is not about IT security — it's about documenting AI use in legal matters
- **Gap:** Wrong framework, wrong language, wrong price point

**Legal TechVendors (International)**
- Kira Systems, Luminance, LawGeex operate in the AI governance adjacent space
- Primary focus: contract analysis, due diligence automation
- Not positioned as AI governance for law firm operations
- **Gap:** These tools ARE the AI use case — but no one is addressing the governance of using them

## What's Missing (The Unoccupied Positions)

### Position 1: Pre-Signing Carrier Form Review

**What exists:** Nothing. The market offers no service that reviews carrier attestation forms before signing.

**What Geneva boutique needs:** A structured review of what the carrier form actually requires, translated into concrete actions for a small firm. The managing partner signs the form without understanding the commitment. No one has offered to review that form with them before they sign.

**Why it's unoccupied:** Carriers don't want it (it would expose how vague their forms are). Brokers don't offer it (they don't have the expertise). Law firms don't know to ask for it.

**The opportunity:** A fixed-fee carrier form review — specific to the firm's attestation — produces immediate value, creates engagement, and identifies the first concrete gap to close. It is the highest-value first step in the sales sequence.

---

### Position 2: Geneva Boutique AI Governance (Not Generalist Compliance)

**What exists:** No Switzerland-specific or Geneva-specific AI governance service for small law firms.

**What the ICP needs:** Something that:
- Speaks French (or bilingual EN/FR)
- Understands the OaF context
- References Swiss carrier forms
- Is priced for 2–5 attorney firms
- Addresses personal liability of the managing partner
- Does not assume a compliance team

**Why it's unoccupied:** Every existing provider is either:
- English-language only
- Enterprise-priced
- Framework-first (ISO, NIST) rather than problem-first
- Not connected to the Geneva legal market specifically

**The opportunity:** A Geneva-localized AI governance service — positioned as "built for Geneva boutiques, by people who understand Geneva law practice" — has no direct competition.

---

### Position 3: Junior Associate AI Use Documentation

**What exists:** Nothing. No service addresses the specific problem of "my junior associate is using AI tools on their own device and I have no visibility."

**What the ICP needs:** A lightweight system that:
- Documents what AI tools are being used, by whom, on which matters
- Does not require the junior associate to change their behavior significantly
- Creates a defensible record without being burdensome
- Lives at the firm level (not the individual level)

**Why it's unoccupied:** This is a niche within a niche. It requires understanding both (a) how AI tools are actually used in boutique legal practice, and (b) what carrier forms and bar guidance actually require. The intersection is small and specific.

**The opportunity:** A "digital trail light" solution — documentation infrastructure that is proportionate to a small firm's actual AI use — fills a real gap.

---

### Position 4: Retroactive Reconstruction (Not Retroactive Prevention)

**What exists:** Nothing. Every AI governance provider offers forward-looking frameworks. None offer a credible retroactive reconstruction service.

**What the ICP needs (for firms already exposed):** A structured process for reconstructing AI use records for past matters — not to fabricate evidence, but to establish what was done, when, by whom, and on which clients. This has genuine legal and evidentiary value.

**Why it's unoccupied:** Retroactive reconstruction is harder, messier, and less scalable than framework implementation. Providers avoid it because the output is imperfect and the liability is unclear. But for the firm that already signed an attestation without documentation — this is not optional. They already need it.

**The opportunity:** The market that needs retroactive reconstruction has no one to call. This is a unique positioning: "I don't just help you go forward — I help you establish what already happened."

---

### Position 5: Broker Channel — Structural Gap

**What exists:** Some brokers have added AI governance questions to renewal conversations. Very few have a structured offering or a clear referral pathway.

**What the broker needs:** A partner-level service they can refer to with confidence — one that:
- Understands the broker's carrier relationship
- Doesn't compete with them
- Produces a document the carrier will accept
- Is priced appropriately for the small firm segment

**Why it's unoccupied:** The broker channel has been approached by enterprise vendors with enterprise-priced solutions. The broker has nothing to offer their small law firm clients. They are not a distribution channel for a product that doesn't exist yet.

**The opportunity:** A broker-specific value proposition — "here is what you say, here is what you send them to, here is what comes back" — creates a channel that currently has no product to distribute.

---

### Position 6: "Adequate" Defined for Geneva Boutique

**What exists:** No definition. The carrier form says "adequate" AI governance. The bar says nothing. The ICP has no reference point.

**What the ICP needs:** A Geneva-specific, firm-size-specific, proportionate answer to "what does adequate look like for a 3-attorney firm." Not a general framework — a concrete, defensible description of what the firm does, documented, and sufficient.

**Why it's unoccupied:** This requires someone to take a position. To say: for this type of firm, in this jurisdiction, with this risk profile — here is what adequate looks like. No one has. The bar won't. The carriers won't. The brokers can't. The law firm itself is paralyzed.

**The opportunity:** The first provider that defines "adequate" for the Geneva boutique segment — specifically, concretely, in a way that the carrier will accept and the bar would recognize — becomes the de facto standard. This is the highest-value unoccupied position in the market.

## NET-NEW Competitive Findings for C311

1. **Carrier form pre-signing review — unoccupied:** The highest-value intervention point has zero market competition
2. **Geneva localization — unoccupied:** No provider speaks to Geneva boutiques in their language, at their price, with their specific frame
3. **Junior associate AI surface — unoccupied:** The actual risk source (personal device + no oversight) has no dedicated solution
4. **Retroactive reconstruction — unoccupied:** Forward-looking frameworks ignore the firms that need to look backward
5. **Broker channel product — unoccupied:** The broker has no small-firm AI governance product to distribute; they are an idle channel
6. **"Adequate" definition for Geneva boutique — unoccupied:** The word on the carrier form that paralyzes every managing partner has no Geneva-specific, size-specific interpretation in the market
