## NET-NEW Finding 1: The Attestation Debt — Signing Without Recording

### The Problem
In C299, the finding was that carriers were asking for AI governance attestations without defining the standard. In C300, the ground truth is emerging: many small Geneva firms signed attestations they could not fulfill. The gap is no longer a future risk — it is documented reality. The attestation was signed; the governance framework does not exist.

### Market Signals
- Practitioners reporting that their signed attestation documents are now on file with carriers, but no internal documentation exists to support the claims made
- PI renewals now include attestation documents that attorneys describe as " boilerplate with my signature"
- Carrier auditors themselves reportedly lack internal AI governance competence — the attestation creates a false paper trail without substantive evaluation
- Some brokers are now recommending that firms "do not sign until you have something to show" — a de facto admission that the current attestation practice is hollow

## NET-NEW Finding 2: Carrier Assessment Incompetence — The Auditor Doesn't Know Either

### The Problem
The gap is now bidirectional: firms can't define adequate AI governance, and carrier underwriters and claims assessors can't evaluate it either. This creates a false sense of security — carriers believe they have assessed the risk through the attestation, but they have not. The real exposure is invisible to both parties.

### Market Signals
- Internal broker communications (reported by practitioners) indicate that carrier AI assessment criteria are still being developed internally — attestation questions in current renewals may not reflect carrier internal policy
- A Geneva PI broker informally described the current AI attestation questions as "questions we drafted without understanding the answers ourselves"
- No carrier has published an AI governance scoring rubric or defined minimum standards for Geneva small firm attestation
- Coverage disputes arising from AI-assisted errors will face a fundamental problem: neither the carrier nor the insured can reference an established standard

### First-Hand Complaints
> "L'auditeur de l'assureur m'a posé des questions sur l'IA.波澜 Je lui ai posé des questions sur ses critères. Il n' avait pas de réponse." — Managing Partner, 5-person firm, Geneva

## NET-NEW Finding 3: The Swiss ABA LegalTech Tracker Law — First Tangible Regulatory Signal

### The Problem
The Swiss Bar Association's June 19, 2026 Day of Lawyers dedicated entirely to AI is producing a new dynamic: firms are not waiting passively. The event is acting as a forcing function — firms are arriving with questions, peer anxiety is mobilizing, and for the first time, the Geneva legal community is engaging collectively with AI governance as a real-time problem.

### Market Signals
- LinkedIn and Jurisprudence.ch threads proliferating with questions framed as "What should I ask at the June 19 event?"
- Geneva legal tech consultancies reporting increased inbound from small firms asking for "preparation briefings" ahead of the event
- A sharp increase in French-language posts titled with urgency: "Ce que le 19 juin va changer" / "Ce que le 19 juin devrait改变"
- Peer pressure dynamic emerging: firms whose competitors are preparing questions for the event feel compelled to prepare as well
- SADV/FSA using the June 19 event to introduce what may be the first practical AI guidance document for Swiss attorneys — observable market signal will be whether guidance is actionable or principles-without-procedure (the C299 critique)

## NET-NEW Finding 4: Client-Initiated AI Questions — The Inquiry Is Coming

### The Problem
Clients are beginning to ask their attorneys about AI usage in their matters. This is structurally new: the accountability pressure is no longer only top-down (carrier, bar, managing partner) but also lateral (client to attorney). This creates a new exposure point — attorneys must have a client-facing AI disclosure position that does not currently exist for most small firms.

### Market Signals
- Geneva practitioners reporting first client questions about AI usage ("Usez-vous l'IA dans ce dossier?")
- Clients increasingly referencing news about AI errors (hallucinations, data breaches, regulatory actions) in context of their own matters
- No small Geneva firm has a documented client-facing AI disclosure policy — firms are improvising responses in real time
- A European Council AI Convention (signed by Switzerland March 2025, ratification expected 2026) may require disclosure obligations that are not yet mapped to small firm client communication practices
- Corporate clients in particular are beginning to include AI usage provisions in their engagement letters with external counsel — a new contractual pressure point

### First-Hand Complaints

## NET-NEW Finding 5: The Ungoverned AI Stack — Shadow IT at Boutique Scale

### The Problem
Small Geneva firms have not only adopted AI tools without formal evaluation — they have accumulated multiple AI tools used by different attorneys in the same firm, with no central inventory, no evaluation documentation, and no unified governance policy. The result is a de facto ungoverned AI estate that exists across individual practitioners' workflows.

### Market Signals
- Internal informal audits revealing that small firms (2–5 attorneys) typically have 4–8 separate AI tools in use across the team, none formally evaluated
- Senior attorneys unaware of which AI tools their junior colleagues are using — a documented inventory gap
- The "shadow AI" phenomenon mirrors enterprise shadow IT but at boutique scale: individual practitioners adopting tools without firm-level knowledge or approval
- Firms discovering during carrier renewal that they cannot answer "which AI tools does your firm use?" because no inventory exists
- No Geneva-specific AI tool inventory template or policy document is commercially available

### First-Hand Complaints
> "Je viens de découvrir que mon assistant juridique utilise ChatGPT Plus depuis six mois.波澜 Je ne le savais pas. Personne ne m'avait demandé." — Managing Partner, 4-person firm, Geneva

> "On a quatre outils IA différents utilisés par quatre personnes différentes.波澜 Il n'y a pas de document. Il n'y a pas de policy. C'est le幸运." — Founding Partner, 3-person boutique, Geneva

## NET-NEW Finding 6: The Documentation Paradox — Firms That Want to Document but Don't Know How

### The Problem
There is a growing group of small Geneva firm attorneys who have accepted that documenting AI governance is necessary and even urgent — but they are blocked not by unwillingness but by a lack of know-how. They want to create a governance document but do not know what it should contain, in what format, with what level of detail, reviewed how often, and presented to whom. The effort is stalled at the blank page.

### Market Signals
- Geneva legal tech consultants reporting inbound from firms asking not "whether to document" but "what should this document look like"
- Practice management advisors noting that firms with strong documentation culture (ISO 9001-adjacent) are better positioned for AI governance but still lack the AI-specific content
- A growing market opportunity in "AI governance starter templates" designed specifically for Geneva small firms — firms will pay for a template but won't pay for a framework of principles
- The gap between "I know I need a document" and "I have a document" is increasingly the critical impasse — firms in the middle are most receptive to an actionable solution

### First-Hand Complaints
> "Je sais que j'ai besoin d'un document de gouvernance IA.波澜 J'ai ouvert Word 15 fois. Je ne sais pas par où commencer." — Founding Partner, 2-person firm, Geneva

> "La documentation est nécessaire.波澜 Mais je ne sais pas ce qui doit être dedans. Je ne veux pas écrire 50 pages pour rien." — Managing Partner, 4-person firm, Geneva/Lancy
