# The Geneva Consulting AI Accountability Briefing: The Six Structural Problems That Exist Before the Frameworks Named Them

## An Executive Briefing for Managing Directors and Partners at Small Geneva Consulting Boutiques

### The Entry Point

I have a form on my desk. It requires my personal signature.

I have three client questionnaires I haven't answered yet. I don't know what they're actually asking.

I work with independent sub-consultants. I don't know what AI they use.

I think I have six problems. I haven't named them clearly enough to fix any of them.

This briefing names them.

---

### Problem 1: The Client Questionnaire Portfolio Effect

Three clients sent me AI governance questionnaires in the same quarter. I handled each one separately.

I didn't notice the problem until I tried to answer the second one and realized my answer would contradict the first.

Client A asked about AI methodology. I gave a generic answer. Client B — in the same quarter — asked a follow-up question that the generic answer didn't cover. Client C sent their questionnaire to all their consultants. They are building a benchmark. They will compare my responses against my peers.

I have no system for tracking what I committed to across all three questionnaires. I answered each one independently. They are not consistent with each other.

And on every proposal I've sent in the last two years, there is an AI methodology paragraph. I cannot tell you which tools I committed to in each one, or what I meant by "evidence-based AI-assisted analysis."

The client questionnaire portfolio effect is already underway. You've already created accumulation inconsistency below firm awareness. The problem is not the next questionnaire — it is the portfolio picture you're not tracking.

---

### Problem 2: Carrier Attestation Post-Signature Realization

The carrier form requires my personal signature.

I reviewed it quickly. I signed it. I submitted it.

Two weeks later, I realized what I had agreed to: a personal commitment to undefined adequacy standards for AI practices I had not evaluated.

The form uses language adapted from institutional contexts — it doesn't define what methodology means for a small consulting boutique doing bespoke analytical work. It asks me to confirm my AI governance is adequate. Adequate against what?

Nobody offered to review the form with me before I signed it. The market doesn't have a service for that.

And here's what I understand now: the carrier attestation is a personal liability instrument. Signing it doesn't confirm I'm covered — it confirms I've accepted personal accountability for AI practices I can't describe.

The post-signature realization is not a my-diligence problem. It is a structural absence in the Geneva consulting market.

---

### Problem 3: Sub-Consultant AI Delegation — The Three-Party Chain

I work with independent sub-consultants on major engagements. My engagement letter governs my relationship with the client. It doesn't say anything about AI.

My sub-consultant used an AI tool in our joint deliverable. I don't know which tool, which model, or which version.

The chain is this: Client → Me → Sub-consultant → AI tool. I am primary. The sub-consultant is a processor under data protection law. The AI tool is another layer I have not mapped.

My engagement letter predates my AI adoption. It has no AI use clause. It doesn't define what sub-consultant AI disclosure to me is required. It doesn't define what adequate sub-consultant AI oversight means.

Article 9 of the Swiss Data Protection Act flows third-party processor obligations from me to my sub-consultants. I hadn't thought about what that means in practice. I hadn't thought about what it means when my sub-consultant's AI vendor terms are structurally incompatible with my Article 9 obligations as the primary controller.

The three-party delegation chain is already operating. I signed engagement letters that predate it.

---

### Problem 4: The Silent Model Update Problem

I use AI tools in my analytical work. I document my methodology in proposals.

Between the date I sent my proposal and the date I'm still defending a conclusion from that engagement, the AI model updated. I didn't notice. The vendor doesn't announce it.

I cannot stand behind what analytical framework produced the conclusion the client still references, because I don't know which model version was active when.

My engagement letter has no version-locked deliverable standard. It doesn't say which AI model or which version I'm committing to. It says "evidence-based analysis" — a phrase that meant something when I wrote it and now means something different.

The silent update propagation is structurally unmanaged. Every active client mandate carries it. I have not built any version provenance into my analytical documentation. The problem exists before any failure occurs.

---

### Problem 5: The Documentation Window That Closes as an Inversion

There is a documentation window. I think of it as a compliance deadline.

It is not a compliance deadline. It is a burden-of-proof inversion.

Before the window closes: I demonstrate engagement with AI governance questions.

After the window closes: I must demonstrate adequacy of AI governance practices.

The retroactive problem is different from the prospective problem. Building documentation after the window closes requires reconstructing reasoning that was not documented during the window. That reconstruction is practically impossible. It requires fabricating evidence after the facts, which is not the same as documenting during the decision.

I'm using the window to wait for guidance. The guidance is not arriving. When it arrives, I'll face the problem: I have decisions made without documentation, and I cannot go back and fix the documentation without creating evidence quality problems of a different kind.

The documentation window is a present-tense opportunity becoming a past-tense constraint.

---

### Problem 6: The Geneva Accountability Vacuum

There is no association for management consultants in Geneva that handles these questions. I checked.

Management consulting has no SVA equivalent. No ORDRE equivalent. No sector body with AI governance guidance authority. The carrier form has become the closest thing to a standard because no other standard exists.

I am managing this independently. The MDs I talk to are also managing it independently. We exchange information informally but there is no institutional forum. The carrier created the third-generation questionnaire. The carriers are not Geneva consulting specialists. There is no Geneva consulting voice in the standard formation.

The accountability vacuum amplifies the other five problems. In other professions, the institutional structure distributes the pressure: the FMH for doctors, the Geneva Bar for attorneys, the SRO for financial advisors. For management consultants, the pressure arrives individually.

I think the vacuum is the signal. If there is no guidance, there is no institutional structure that has claimed authority over these questions. That means the first person to name the problem and offer a framework captures the standard-setting function — before someone else does.
