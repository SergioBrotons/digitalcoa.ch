## Section 2: What AI Tools Is Your Firm Already Using?

Before assessing obligations, take inventory. Most small law firms are already using AI in ways they haven't fully governed:

- **Microsoft 365 Copilot** — drafting, summarization, email
- **ChatGPT / Claude** — legal research, contract review drafts, client communication
- **Clio** (with AI features) — practice management + AI-assisted billing and notes
- **Westlaw Edge / Neos** — AI-assisted case law research
- **AI document review** (specific tools) — due diligence, contract analysis

For each tool: Is client data being processed? Is that processing disclosed to the client? Has a Data Protection Impact Assessment been conducted?

**If you don't know the answers, you're not ready for FDPIC scrutiny.**

## Section 5: The Three Decisions Every Small Law Firm Must Make

**Decision 1: Do we disclose AI use to clients?**
If you're using AI on client matters — and you almost certainly are — you have an obligation under nFADP Art. 5(c) to disclose AI involvement. Most firms haven't done this formally. The fix: a one-page AI disclosure addendum to your engagement letter.

**Decision 2: Have we conducted a Data Protection Impact Assessment for AI tools?**
If any AI tool processes client data, a DPIA is required under nFADP Art. 22 for high-risk processing. For most small firms, a simplified DPIA covering your 3-5 primary AI tools is sufficient and defensible.

**Decision 3: Do we have a policy for AI tool use that staff actually follow?**
Policies written and not implemented don't provide protection. The FDPIC looks at actual practices, not documented intentions. A practical AI use policy — covering approved tools, prohibited uses (e.g., uploading confidential briefs to public AI services), and incident response — is the operational backbone of compliance.

## Section 6: The EU AI Act — Does It Actually Apply to My Firm?

Short answer: maybe, and most Geneva small firms haven't checked.

The EU AI Act applies extraterritorially when:
- You place an AI system on the EU market
- You operate an AI system whose output is intended for EU data subjects
- You provide AI-enabled services to EU clients where the output affects EU individuals
- You act as importer or distributor of AI systems in the EU

For a Geneva attorney advising EU-based clients, using an EU-hosted AI tool, or whose legal opinions affect EU data subjects — the EU AI Act's high-risk obligations may be triggered.

The high-risk category most relevant to legal practice: **AI systems used in employment contexts** (hiring, promotion, termination decisions), **AI in access to essential services** (financial, legal services).

**If you have EU clients or EU-facing practice:** EU AI Act compliance should be on your checklist now.

The Swiss Bar Association (ANBLES) has been silent on AI governance for approximately 20 months since the revised nFADP came into force (September 2023).

The Geneva Bar has issued no AI guidance.

This silence creates both risk and opportunity:
- **Risk:** Small firms have no professional body signal on what AI compliance looks like for attorneys
- **Opportunity:** Advisors who move now become the de facto voice before ANBLES acts

When ANBLES eventually publishes guidance, firms that are already compliant will absorb it as a confirmation. Firms that are non-compliant will face both the guidance and the enforcement risk simultaneously.

## Section 8: A Practical 5-Step Compliance Path

**Step 1 — AI Inventory (Week 1–2):**
List every AI tool used in the firm. For each: what data enters, what processing occurs, where does data go, who has access. Document this in a simple spreadsheet.

**Step 2 — Client Disclosure Draft (Week 2–3):**
Draft a one-page AI disclosure addendum for engagement letters. Key elements: what AI tools are used, in what capacity, for what purposes, and that the client may request human review of any AI-assisted work.

**Step 3 — DPIA Lite (Week 3–4):**
Conduct a simplified Data Protection Impact Assessment for your top 3 AI tools. Template-based, 2-3 hours of attorney time. Document your assessment, residual risks, and mitigation measures.

**Step 4 — Staff Policy (Week 4):**
Draft a one-page AI use policy: approved tools, prohibited uses (especially: never upload client files to public AI services without explicit consent), what to do if an AI error causes a problem.

**Step 5 — Annual Review Trigger:**
Set a calendar reminder to review AI tool inventory and regulatory developments annually — or when a new tool is adopted.

The firms that already have documented frameworks in place will be the ones that look prepared. The firms that don't will be the ones scrambling.
