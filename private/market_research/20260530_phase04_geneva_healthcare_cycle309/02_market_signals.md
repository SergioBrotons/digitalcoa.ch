## Market Context (C309)

### The Geneva Healthcare ICP in C309

Independent practitioners and small group practices (2–10 physicians), typically 45–65 years old, personally accountable for AI tool use affecting patient data. The structural crisis driving AI adoption decisions:

- **Staffing crisis:** Geneva has highest medical assistant wages in Switzerland; nearly 40% of Geneva physicians plan retirement within 5 years (AMGe 2023)
- **Clause du besoin:** July 2025 — makes existing practice licenses more economically scarce; every remaining staff member more critical
- **Efficiency imperative:** AI tools promise relief; implementation creates liability exposure
- **Regulatory stack:** nFADP + Swissmedic + FMH + cantonal law — no comprehensive AI law; existing rules apply

### C309 Market State: "Can I protect my license while adopting AI?"

The market has moved from "should I use AI?" to "how do I use AI without destroying my license?"

This is distinct from:
- C306/C307: discovery phase ("am I exposed?")
- C308: action phase ("what do I do now?")
- C309: sustainability phase ("can I maintain this without constant anxiety?")

## Current Problems and Complaints (C309)

### Problem 1: The Physician-Personal Carrier Attestation Gap

**Signal:** Carrier forms for medical practices have shifted from practice-level certification to physician-personal certification.

**What physicians are reporting:**
- "My insurance carrier sent me an AI attestation form with my name on it. It's not the practice — it's me personally signing that I understand how AI is being used in my practice."
- "I didn't realize I was certifying this personally. I thought it was a practice-level thing."
- "The carrier is asking questions I've never been asked before. What AI tools are used, by whom, on what patient data. I'm not sure how to answer."

**Market implication:** The carrier form sophistication escalation (C309 Phase 01 finding) is now active in the healthcare vertical. Physicians face physician-personal carrier certification with documentation requirements they cannot meet.

**The documentation paradox in healthcare:** Physicians fear that creating records of AI use will be used against them in liability proceedings. But carrier attestation requires them to certify compliance. The attestation creates the records; the records create the exposure if not documented properly.

### Problem 2: FMH Human Oversight — Defined in Principle, Absent in Practice

**Signal:** FMH guidance on human oversight exists as principle; no operational protocol exists for small practices.

**What physicians are reporting:**
- "FMH says human oversight is required. I read the guidance three times. It's principles, not protocol. There's no Swissmedic-style checklist for what human oversight means for a GP in a 3-person practice."
- "How do I document that I reviewed an AI-assisted diagnosis? What constitutes 'oversight' in a concrete case?"
- "If Swissmedic asks me to prove human oversight was maintained, what do I show them?"

**Market implication:** No published reference standard for AI human oversight exists for Swiss primary care. This is a first-mover positioning opportunity: define the operational protocol that FMH has not.

### Problem 3: Staff AI Tool Use on Patient Data — Invisible Accountability Gap

**Signal:** Medical assistants use AI tools (scheduling, communication, possibly dictation/transcription) on patient data. The physician authorized the tools but doesn't track what's being processed.

**What physicians are reporting:**
- "My medical assistants use AI tools. I approved the tools. But I don't have a system to track what's actually being processed through them."
- "I signed the data processing agreement with the vendor. But I don't know what my staff are actually putting through the AI."
- "If there's a patient data incident, I'm the one responsible. But I have no visibility into what my staff are doing with the tools I authorized."

**Market implication:** The staff delegation accountability gap (identified in C308 consulting and cross-vertical) is particularly acute in healthcare because: (1) staff have direct patient data access, (2) physician is personally liable, (3) no tracking system exists.

### Problem 4: Swissmedic Operator Obligations — "Certified" Doesn't Mean "Compliant"

**Signal:** Physicians receive AI tool vendor certifications ("Swissmedic compliant," "CE marked," "certified for medical use") but don't understand what this means for their own obligations as "operators."

**What physicians are reporting:**
- "My AI tool vendor says they're Swissmedic certified. Does that mean I'm compliant? Does that mean I'm the operator? Does that mean I have registration obligations under swissdamed?"
- "Swissmedic sent me something about device registration. I don't know if it applies to me or just to the vendor."
- "Is my practice a 'device operator' under Swissmedic rules? How do I know if I am?"

**Market implication:** swissdamed July 1, 2026 deadline creates urgency but understanding of obligations is low. Physicians need practical operator classification guidance.

### Problem 5: The Efficiency-Liability Paradox — "More AI = More Efficient and More Exposed"

**Signal:** AI tools are adopted to solve the staffing crisis and administrative burden. But each AI adoption creates documentation obligations and potential liability exposure.

**What physicians are reporting:**
- "I need AI to stay efficient. I have three staff, two are planning retirement, and I can't replace them. AI is the only way to maintain service."
- "But every AI tool I adopt creates a potential liability. If something goes wrong, I need documentation that I was overseeing the process."
- "So I'm stuck: without AI I can't maintain efficiency, with AI I create more documented exposure."

**Market implication:** This paradox is healthcare-specific. The solution is not "adopt less AI" — it's "document AI use in a way that demonstrates oversight without creating operational burden." Evidence architecture solves this.

### Problem 6: Post-Incident Documentation Burden — No Standard Protocol

**Signal:** When a patient incident occurs involving AI tools, physicians have no standardized Swiss primary care protocol for documentation.

**What physicians are reporting:**
- "If something goes wrong and Swissmedic sends me questions about my AI tool, what do I show them? Is there a standard format?"
- "My medical assistant used an AI tool in a patient case. The patient had a bad outcome. What documentation do I need?"
- "Do I document the AI use in the patient record? Do I create a separate AI oversight record? Do I report to Swissmedic directly?"

**Market implication:** No standardized post-incident documentation protocol exists for Swiss primary care AI use. Physicians are managing this ad hoc.

## Market Signal Analysis (C309)

### How Physicians Are Learning About AI Problems

1. **Peer incident network:** "A colleague called me. She had a patient incident and Swissmedic sent inquiries. She didn't know her AI tool was a medical device requiring registration." — This is how problems spread in the physician network. Not regulatory notices — peer calls.

2. **Carrier attestation receipt:** New carrier forms with physician names activate research behavior. Physicians who receive the new forms start asking their peers.

3. **AMGe events:** Medical society events are the primary information-sharing venue. Physicians discuss AI incidents and carrier form experiences.

4. **Medical assistant departures:** Staff changes force AI tool audit; physician discovers undocumented AI use.
