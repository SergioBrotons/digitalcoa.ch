## Headline Findings

### 1. Carrier Attestation Has Shifted to Physician-Personal Certification

**C309 NET-NEW finding:** Carrier forms are now naming the individual physician — not the practice — in AI attestation requirements. This creates direct nFADP Art. 63 personal liability for the signing physician.

**Evidence:** Physicians report receiving forms "with my name on it, not just the practice name." The carrier is creating a record of physician-level certification.

**Implication:** The documentation gap is no longer practice-level — it's physician-personal. Documentation must match what the physician individually certified.

### 2. The Efficiency-Liability Paradox Is Now Explicit

**C309 NET-NEW finding:** Physicians are stating the paradox explicitly: "More AI makes me more efficient and more exposed simultaneously." This is not a rhetorical device — it's a genuine operational contradiction with no resolution in standard frameworks.

**Evidence:** Physicians articulate that AI adoption is necessary for practice sustainability (staffing crisis, administrative burden) but creates documentation obligations and liability exposure with each adoption.

**Implication:** The solution is not "use less AI" — it's "document AI use in a way that demonstrates oversight without creating unsustainable operational burden." Evidence architecture solves this; frameworks do not.

### 3. Staff AI Delegation Creates Physician Liability With No Tracking System

**C309 NET-NEW finding:** Medical assistants use AI tools on patient data. The physician authorized the tools but has no system to track what is being processed. The physician is personally liable for AI-assisted decisions made by staff.

**Evidence:** Physicians describe: "I signed the DPA with the vendor. But I don't know what my staff are actually putting through the AI. And I'm the one responsible."

**Implication:** The staff delegation accountability gap is healthcare-specific in its intensity because: (1) staff have direct patient data access, (2) physician license is on the line personally, (3) no tracking system exists in standard practice management.

### 4. Swissmedic Operator Classification for Small Practice Is Undefined

**C309 NET-NEW finding:** Physicians receive vendor "certification" claims (CE mark, "Swissmedic compliant") but don't understand whether they are classified as device operators under Swissmedic rules, and what operator obligations apply to a small practice (2–10 physicians).

**Evidence:** Physicians report confusion about: whether they're operators, what operator compliance documentation is required, whether vendor certification satisfies their obligations.

**Implication:** swissdamed July 1, 2026 deadline creates urgency — but the classification guide for small practice operators doesn't exist. First-mover advisory opportunity.

### 5. FMH Human Oversight Has No Operational Protocol for Small Practice

**C309 NET-NEW finding:** FMH states human oversight as a principle but publishes no operational protocol for what human oversight means in a 2–10 physician practice. Physicians operate without a reference standard.

**Evidence:** Physicians read FMH guidance and find it principles-based: "They tell me oversight is required. They don't tell me what to document or how to prove oversight was maintained."

**Implication:** The absence of an official standard is not protection — it's a documentation requirement. Physicians need to make their own decisions and document them. No competitor offers a small practice FMH-compliant protocol.

### 6. Post-Incident Documentation Protocol Is Absent for Swiss Primary Care

**C309 NET-NEW finding:** No standardized Swiss primary care protocol exists for documenting AI-assisted decisions when a patient incident occurs. Physicians manage post-incident documentation ad hoc.

**Evidence:** Physicians report uncertainty about: what to show Swissmedic in an inquiry, how to document AI use in patient records, whether to create separate AI oversight records, when to report directly to Swissmedic.

**Implication:** This gap creates liability exposure that compounds with each AI tool adoption. The evidence architecture includes post-incident documentation format as a component.

## ICP Language Summary (C309)

### How Physicians Talk About AI Problems

**The core frame:** "I signed my carrier's AI attestation and didn't realize my physician license was on the line personally."

**The five pressures physicians manage:**
1. FMH human oversight stated without operational definition
2. Carrier attestation shifting from practice-level to physician-personal
3. Efficiency-liability paradox: more AI = more efficient and more exposed
4. Staff AI tool use on patient data — invisible accountability gap
5. Swissmedic operator obligations — ambiguous and undefined for small practice

**The evidence language physicians respond to:**
- "Documentation that matches what you certified"
- "Records that show you were actually overseeing the AI"
- "Evidence that your judgment was involved"
- "Swissmedic-compliant device registration record"

**What physicians are NOT saying:**
- "I need an AI governance framework"
- "I want to understand the regulatory landscape"
- "Can you help me with my AI strategy?"

**What physicians ARE saying:**
- "How do I document that I was actually overseeing the AI without spending all my time on documentation?"
- "My carrier is asking questions I don't know how to answer"
- "If Swissmedic sends me an inquiry, what do I show them?"
- "My medical assistant uses AI on patient data — how do I know what's going through?"
- "If something goes wrong and a patient is harmed, what documentation do I need to protect myself?"

**The personal license anchor:** "For physicians, evidence is personal. And your license is on the line."
