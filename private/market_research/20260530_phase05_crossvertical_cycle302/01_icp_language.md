# 01 — Cross-Vertical ICP Language Patterns

## Phase 05 — C302 | Cross-Vertical Synthesis

---

## Cross-Vertical ICP Definition

**Who they are:** Senior decision-maker in a small Geneva professional firm — 2–50 employees across law, finance, consulting, or healthcare. Personally accountable. No compliance infrastructure. Carrier or client asking AI governance questions.

**The problem is not capability.** It is documentation and accountability under conditions of uncertainty.

---

## Universal Language Patterns (All Four Verticals)

### Pattern 1: "I signed the form. I don't know what I signed for."

This phrase appears across all four verticals — legal, finance, consulting, healthcare. The accountable person signed an attestation, carrier form, or questionnaire without understanding what it commits them to. The signature is personal. The liability is personal. The documentation doesn't exist.

**Variations by vertical:**

- **Legal:** *"I signed the carrier PI attestation. What does it actually require me to demonstrate?"*
- **Finance:** *"I signed the carrier form v4. It asks for authorization records. I don't have them."*
- **Consulting:** *"I signed the client AI governance questionnaire response. I certified our AI governance framework. I can't produce the documentation."*
- **Healthcare:** *"I signed the carrier AI attestation form. My broker couldn't help me answer it. I still can't answer it."*

**Why this language matters:** The ICP is not in denial. They know they signed something they don't understand. The anxiety is specific and personal. The language is admission-seeking, not defensive.

---

### Pattern 2: "I can't reconstruct what happened."

Retroactive reconstruction is structurally impossible across all verticals. The systems were never built to capture the data. The ICP understands this but cannot articulate it to clients, carriers, or regulators.

**Variations by vertical:**

- **Legal:** *"I can't reconstruct which AI tool was used for which client research. The authorization wasn't documented."*
- **Finance:** *"I can't reconstruct which AI system influenced which client investment decision. There's no attribution record."*
- **Consulting:** *"I can't reconstruct the AI's role in the methodology. I can't show the reasoning chain."*
- **Healthcare:** *"I can't reconstruct which AI tool accessed which patient record. The access log doesn't exist."*

**Why this language matters:** The ICP has reached the conclusion independently — they know reconstruction is impossible. They are not looking for a solution that promises reconstruction. They are looking for a system that produces evidence going forward.

---

### Pattern 3: "The person who signs doesn't use the tools. The person who uses the tools doesn't sign."

The junior-senior fluency inversion is universal. The accountable person (senior partner, managing director) lacks technical fluency. The technically fluent person (junior associate, junior consultant) lacks accountability. This is structural, not solvable by "supervising better."

**Variations by vertical:**

- **Legal:** *"I supervise the work. I can't independently verify what the AI actually produced."*
- **Finance:** *"I approve the AI tools. I don't know what data they access or how they make recommendations."*
- **Consulting:** *"I sign the client questionnaire. My junior staff use AI tools I don't track."*
- **Healthcare:** *"I authorize the AI tools. I don't know what patient data they access or how they function."*

**Why this language matters:** The ICP has diagnosed the accountability gap correctly. They are not looking for someone to tell them to supervise better. They are looking for a tool that makes the accountable person operationally capable of supervising AI output they cannot independently verify.

---

### Pattern 4: "I signed a contract I didn't read."

The vendor contract trap is universal. AI vendor contracts signed in 2023–2024 included data retention for model training provisions. The ICP discovered this after signing. Switching vendors does not resolve historical exposure.

**Variations by vertical:**

- **Legal:** *"Our AI vendor contract — I signed it. I didn't read the data retention clause. Six months later I realized."*
- **Finance:** *"We switched vendors. The new contract is better. But the old data is already in the training set."*
- **Consulting:** *"My sub-consultant uses AI tools. I have no idea what their vendor contracts say about data retention."*
- **Healthcare:** *"The AI tool vendor said data is secure. I never asked about training data. I should have."*

**Why this language matters:** The ICP is embarrassed but honest. They understand the trap is permanent for the historical dimension. They are not looking for false resolution through switching. They are looking for forward-looking evidence systems.

---

### Pattern 5: "The carrier/form/client is asking for something I can't produce."

The evidence gap is universal and acute. The primary forcing function varies by vertical — carrier attestation (legal, finance, healthcare) or client questionnaire (consulting, finance) — but the underlying problem is identical: the form asks for evidence that doesn't exist.

**Variations by vertical:**

- **Legal:** *"The carrier form asks for my last three authorization records. I don't have them."*
- **Finance:** *"The institutional client questionnaire asks for AI tool inventory, authorization records, and decision attribution. I can't produce it."*
- **Consulting:** *"The client RFP scores AI governance. I submitted a response but I can't back it up with documentation."*
- **Healthcare:** *"The carrier attestation form asks me to confirm human oversight of AI. I don't know what that means in practice."*

**Why this language matters:** The ICP knows the gap exists. They are not looking for someone to explain the problem. They are looking for someone to help them produce the documentation — quickly, practically, without a compliance department.

---

### Pattern 6: "I'm the responsible person. What does that actually mean for me personally?"

nFADP Article 63 personal liability is the universal wake-up call. The "responsible person" designation means personal assets at risk. This is true across all four verticals — attorney, wealth manager, managing consultant, physician.

**Variations by vertical:**

- **Legal:** *"I'm the managing partner. I'm the responsible person under nFADP. What happens to me personally if there's a breach?"*
- **Finance:** *"As managing director, I'm personally liable under Article 63. My broker didn't tell me that."*
- **Consulting:** *"I'm the managing director. I'm responsible for data protection compliance including AI. What does that mean for me?"*
- **Healthcare:** *"I'm the responsible physician. Article 63 plus FMH oversight — what am I actually personally liable for?"*

**Why this language matters:** The personal liability dimension converts abstract concern to action. The ICP who understands this is not asking whether to act — they are asking how to document their responsible-person oversight in a way that is defensible.
