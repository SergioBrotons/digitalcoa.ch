# Compliance Context — Geneva Consulting AI Accountability (C287 Phase 03)

## Geneva/Switzerland Regulatory Landscape for Consulting Boutiques

### 1. nFADP (Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection) — Key Provisions for Geneva Consulting

**Art. 6 — Lawfulness of Processing**
Processing must have a lawful basis. Consulting firms using AI tools for client work are processing personal data through vendor infrastructure. The lawful basis (typically contract performance or legitimate interest) must be established and documented. Vendor terms changes may have altered the original authorization scope without firm awareness.

**Art. 9 — Third-Party Data Processors**
When a consulting boutique uses an AI vendor as third-party data processor, specific obligations apply:
- Data processing agreement must be in place
- Authorization scope must be defined and documented
- Sub-processor changes must be approved
- For sub-consultant AI delegation: prime boutique may be the data controller, sub-consultant the processor, AI vendor a sub-processor — chain requires documented authorization architecture

**Art. 63 — Personal Liability**
nFADP Art. 63 provides for personal liability of individuals who breach the Act in the course of professional activity. For a Geneva consulting MD, this means personal liability exposure is not limited to the entity — it extends to the individual making or authorizing AI-related decisions.

**Art. 21 — Automated Decision-Making**
If AI-assisted consulting recommendations constitute automated decision-making affecting clients, Art. 21 requires:
- Disclosure to data subjects that automated decision-making was used
- Ability to explain the logic involved
- Right for data subjects to request human review
- Right to express their point of view
Consulting firms with AI-assisted deliverables should assume Art. 21 applies and build disclosure architecture accordingly.

**DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment)**
For high-risk processing, nFADP requires a DPIA before processing begins. AI-assisted client work with personal data processing may constitute high-risk processing requiring DPIA. Most Geneva consulting boutiques have not conducted one.

### 2. AI Convention (Council of Europe AI Convention) — Obligations for Consulting Deliverables

**Status:** Switzerland signed the AI Convention. Ratification creates present-tense obligations flowing through existing client contracts and professional liability coverage before implementing legislation arrives.

**Key Obligations for Consulting Firms:**
- Parties must ensure AI systems used respect human rights and fundamental freedoms
- Deployers must monitor AI system outputs for compliance
- High-risk AI use requires conformity assessment before deployment
- Accountability measures must be documented and maintained

**Consulting Deliverable Implications:**
- Deliverables incorporating AI-generated analysis must be reviewed by human authorized person
- Documentation of human review must be maintained
- AI system used must be documented with conformity assessment records
- When AI system behavior changes (model updates), existing conformity may no longer hold

**The Documentation Problem:**
The AI Convention ratification creates obligations that most Geneva consulting boutiques cannot currently satisfy. The gap between obligation and current practice is a present-tense exposure, not a future compliance challenge.

### 3. Carrier Attestation — Third-Generation AI Questionnaire

**What Carriers Are Asking (Third-Gen):**
1. Describe the firm's AI governance framework
2. Identify three AI-assisted decisions made in the past 12 months
3. For each decision: what was the decision, who made it, what review preceded it, what evidence was reviewed
4. How does the MD maintain personal accountability for AI-assisted deliverables
5. What methodology does the firm use to ensure AI outputs meet professional standards
6. How does the firm handle AI model updates affecting active client deliverables

**Personal Signature Requirement:**
The MD signs personally, attesting to the adequacy of the firm's AI governance. This creates personal liability exposure against a standard that has not been defined for Geneva consulting sector by any Geneva body.

**Coverage Question:**
PI insurance policies do not contain explicit language defining coverage scope for AI-assisted consulting errors. The attestation commits to adequacy representation that may exceed what the policy covers. No Geneva market participant has published policy language interpretation for this specific gap.

### 4. CO Art. 398 — Proper Performance and AI-Assisted Deliverables

**The Structural Conflict:**
Swiss Code of Obligations Art. 398 requires the service provider to perform properly. For a consulting firm using AI:
- "Properly" requires documented process demonstrating professional standard was met
- AI vendor terms may require something inconsistent with how the firm must perform under Art. 398
- MD personal liability under nFADP may require different documentation than Art. 398 proper performance standard
- Carrier attestation requires evidence of specific review decisions — not addressed in engagement letters
- These four obligations interact: satisfying one may affect position on another

**The Engagement Letter Architecture Problem:**
Most Geneva consulting engagement letters were written before AI was used in deliverables. They do not:
- Define AI's role in the delivery process
- Establish version-locked deliverable standards
- Address sub-consultant AI delegation
- Specify what human review must occur before client delivery
- Define what happens when AI model updates change analytical framework of deliverables in active use

The engagement letter is the primary contractual governance document for client relationships. It is currently architecturally incapable of handling AI-assisted delivery at Geneva consulting boutique scale.

### 5. Geneva Consulting Accountability Ecosystem — Five-Level Geometry

The Geneva consulting AI accountability architecture operates through five levels that interact, not parallel channels:

1. **Federal AI Coordination Office** — contact point function crystallizing, defines coordination vs standard-setting
2. **Cantonal Regulators (SIC/FINMA)** — sector-specific obligations with different timelines
3. **Geneva Institutional Ecosystem** — institutional counterparty requirements forming operative standards through due diligence questionnaires
4. **Carrier Underwriting** — third-gen attestation operating as de facto sector standard
5. **Client Accountability Demands** — institutional clients, international orgs, diplomatic partners sending AI governance questionnaires

Decisions at one level are evaluated against standards at another. A response that satisfies carrier underwriting requirements may not satisfy institutional client expectations. A client questionnaire response that satisfies one counterparty may create representations that affect another. No Geneva consulting firm has mapped coherence across all five levels simultaneously.

### 6. Documentation Window Close — Burden-of-Proof Reversal

**The Timing:**
Mid-2026 documentation window close creates a specific urgency mechanism:

**Before window closes:**
- Firm builds documentation evaluated against forming standards (what carriers/institutions/regulators are beginning to require)
- Documentation can be positioned as anticipatory compliance
- Burden of proof on firms building architecture

**After window closes:**
- Firm explains why documentation that does not exist was not built
- Against arrived standards, not forming standards
- Cannot reconstruct the window-period reasoning after the fact

**What This Means for Geneva Consulting:**
The 90-day prioritization exercise is a burden-of-proof positioning exercise, not merely a documentation improvement exercise. What is built during the window determines the firm's position relative to standards that will arrive. What is not built during the window becomes permanent exposure, not correctable through retroactive documentation.

### 7. Geneva Consulting Accountability Vacuum — Structural

**The Vacuum:**
- No SVA equivalent for consulting (SVA covers public sector finance, not consulting)
- No Ordre des Avocats equivalent (bar has jurisdiction over attorneys, not consulting advisors)
- No SFAMA/AMAS equivalent (asset management industry body, not consulting sector)
- Carrier questionnaire is the only operative standard for the sector

**The Amplification:**
Because no Geneva body exists to publish what adequacy means for consulting, the carrier's undefined standard operates as the de facto sector standard. MDs sign personal attestations to adequacy against a carrier-designed standard with no Geneva consulting input into its definition. The vacuum is not merely informational — it is structurally creating personal liability exposure through unsigned instruments.

**What No Geneva Authority Has Named:**
- What does carrier questionnaire adequacy mean for a 2–15 employee Geneva consulting boutique?
- What evidence standard must an MD demonstrate for AI-assisted decisions in consulting deliverables?
- How does sub-consultant AI delegation affect prime boutique's carrier attestation adequacy representation?
- What documentation must exist for proposals currently in client possession?

None of these questions have Geneva-specific published answers. The documentation window is closing without the sector having developed its own interpretation of what it is being asked to demonstrate.
