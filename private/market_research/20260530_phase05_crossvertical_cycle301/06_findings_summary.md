### Finding 01 — The Evidence Standard Has Replaced the Policy Standard (NET-NEW C301)

**The finding:** Carrier forms in C301 have evolved from asking "do you have a policy?" to asking "show us your last three authorization records." The evidence standard requires documentation that captures specific decision events — not retrospective descriptions of governance intent. The policy document that answered carrier form questions in 2024–2025 does not answer carrier form questions in 2026.

**Mechanism:** Carrier form iteration reveals what was inadequate about the previous version. Each new version asks for more specific evidence of operational governance. The ICP who built documentation for v2/v3 questions is now being asked v4 questions.

**Cross-vertical pattern:** Legal (authorization records for AI tools used in client matters), Finance (portfolio AI decision audit trail), Consulting (methodology provenance records by engagement), Healthcare (human oversight evidence for specific AI tools on specific dates) — all four verticals face the same evidence demand.

**Implication:** The service that builds evidence infrastructure — not policy documentation — is the C301 service. The ICP who arrives at the next carrier renewal with evidence infrastructure wins the renewal. The ICP who arrives with policy documentation is already behind.

**Ready-to-use language:** "A year ago your carrier asked 'do you have a policy?' Now they ask 'show us your last three authorization records.' Your policy answers the old question. I'm building infrastructure for the new one."

### Finding 02 — The Decision Attribution Problem Is Now the Named Primary Exposure (NET-NEW C301)

**The finding:** The C301 ICP is naming the decision attribution problem — "I cannot reconstruct who made which AI decision in which client matter" — as their primary exposure, not the attestation debt or processor chain. This is a structural maturation of the problem from C300. The ICP can argue they didn't understand the attestation they signed. They cannot argue they don't know what happened in their own client files.

**Mechanism:** AI tools entered the firm through vendor updates, click-through acceptance, and unsolicited feature rollouts without creating discrete decision events. The firm cannot reconstruct the decision chain retroactively — because the decisions never occurred as documented events.

**Cross-vertical pattern:** Legal ("I can't show who used which AI tool in which matter"), Finance ("I can't show the decision chain for AI-influenced portfolio decisions"), Consulting ("I can't show methodology provenance for the proposal"), Healthcare ("I can't show oversight decision for this specific AI tool") — all four have the same unreconstructable decision chain problem.

**Implication:** The decision attribution service is the C301 entry point. Not "do you have a policy?" — "can you show us what happened in your last 10 client matters?" This is a different question that requires a different service.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Can you show me who made which AI decision in your last 10 client matters? That's the question your carrier is now asking. And if you can't show it — that's the exposure."

### Finding 03 — The Vendor Contract Trap Has Become a Governance Architecture Constraint (NET-NEW C301)

**The finding:** Vendor contracts — accepted without evaluation through years of click-through acceptance — have become governance architecture constraints. The terms those contracts contain determine what governance improvements are possible, what data can be exported, and what decisions the firm can make about its AI tool portfolio. The contract is no longer a commercial document. It is a governance document.

**Mechanism:** Unilateral capability change provisions, model training data retention, data portability limitations, and termination friction together create exit barriers that prevent governance improvement through vendor switching. The ICP who wants to improve governance by switching vendors cannot do so — not because of technical constraints, but because of commercial terms.

**Cross-vertical pattern:** Legal SaaS research tools, finance portfolio management AI, consulting proposal generation AI, and healthcare clinical AI tools — all have the same contract structure. All four verticals face the same governance constraint from the same contract terms.

**Implication:** Vendor contract exit planning is a governance architecture prerequisite — not a legal review exercise. The ICP needs to know what's in the contract before they can plan governance improvement.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Before you can improve your AI governance, you need to know what your current vendor contracts actually say about unilateral changes, model training, and termination. Most firms don't know what they signed."

### Finding 04 — The Compliance Fatigue Loop Is Self-Reinforcing and Structurally Blocking Real Action (NET-NEW C301)

**The finding:** The ICP has developed a sophisticated compliance performance practice — attending webinars, downloading guides, updating policies, responding to questionnaires — that substitutes for actual structural decisions. Each compliance performance produces documentation that answers yesterday's questions. The carrier then asks new questions. The ICP updates their policy. The cycle continues. The performance loop prevents the firm from confronting the structural decisions that would create evidence.

**Mechanism:** Real action on AI governance requires making classification decisions — "which tier am I, which tools are we authorized for, what's our oversight protocol for this specific tool." Each decision produces a document that can be scrutinized. The ICP avoids the decision to avoid the scrutiny. The compliance performance substitutes for the decision.

**The vendor and carrier roles:** Vendors benefit from compliance fatigue (selling the feeling of progress without the cost of a decision). Carriers benefit from compliance fatigue (each form iteration reveals what was inadequate in the previous version). Professional bodies benefit from compliance fatigue (publishing principles without procedure creates compliance performance material without actionable decision criteria).

**Implication:** The service that breaks the compliance fatigue loop is the service that names it — specifically. "You've been compliant with the old version of the standard. Here's what the new version requires. Here's how to build it without being in this loop again."

**Ready-to-use language:** "You've been to three webinars, downloaded two guides, updated your policy twice, and responded to the carrier questionnaire. And when they come back with the new version asking for evidence — you're back at the beginning. The loop is the problem."

### Finding 05 — Peer Enforcement Is the Conversion Trigger That Abstract Risk Cannot Produce (NET-NEW C301)

**The finding:** Peer enforcement events — a Geneva attorney questioned by the bar, a wealth manager fined for sub-processor violations, a consulting firm losing a client over methodology, a physician facing FMH scrutiny for AI tool use — are the conversion trigger that external deadlines alone cannot produce. The peer signal creates personal identification ("that could be me") that abstract regulatory language cannot.

**Mechanism:** External deadline awareness creates "I should probably do something." Peer enforcement creates "that could be me in that situation." The conversion rate from peer enforcement is higher than from any other trigger, including regulatory deadlines.

**The specific C301 signals:**
- Legal: A peer firm was asked to produce AI governance documentation for a client matter. They couldn't. The client left.
- Finance: A peer wealth manager was fined for nFADP sub-processor violations. The fine was published. The peer's carrier asked all clients about their processor chains.
- Consulting: A peer consulting firm lost a methodology dispute with a client. The client claimed AI was used without disclosure. The firm couldn't demonstrate provenance.
- Healthcare: A peer physician received an FMH complaint related to AI tool oversight. The complaint cited inability to demonstrate human oversight.

**Implication:** The peer enforcement response service is the highest-converting C301 service. The 2-3 week window after a peer enforcement event becomes known in the Geneva professional network is the maximum receptivity window.

**Ready-to-use language:** "A Geneva attorney was asked to produce AI decision records for a client matter last month. They couldn't. The client left. That's not a regulatory risk — that's a business risk. Here's specifically what happened and what you can do before it happens to you."

### Finding 06 — Strategic Classification Deferral Has Become Risk Accumulation, Not Risk Reduction (NET-NEW C301)

**The finding:** The ICP is strategically deferring Swissdamed Tier 1/Tier 2 classification decisions — consciously choosing not to classify because making the decision creates documentation exposure. The deferral is not reducing risk. It is converting regulatory exposure into documentation exposure plus regulatory exposure, and accumulating both during the deferral period.

**Mechanism:** Making the classification decision requires documenting the AI tool portfolio — including tools adopted through vendor updates and click-through acceptance with no prior authorization record. The documentation that classification requires reveals the attestation debt. The ICP chooses uncertainty over documentation because documentation of a bad decision is worse than no documentation.

**The irony:** The firm continues using AI tools during the deferral period. If Swissdamed requires classification as a precondition for use, the firm is operating without authorization during the deferral period. The deferral creates more exposure, not less.

**The timing trap:** Swissdamed July 1, 2026 registration deadline creates a time-limited opportunity for strategic deferral. The ICP is betting on grace periods, extensions, or ambiguity. The enforcement signal from peer events suggests that betting on non-enforcement is the wrong bet.

**Implication:** The strategic classification decision support service breaks the deferral pattern by reframing the deferral cost. "You're not reducing risk by deferring. You're accumulating it in a form that's harder to address."

**Ready-to-use language:** "You're deferring the Swissdamed classification because making the decision creates documentation exposure. But you're still using the tools. And if the July 1 deadline is enforced — you're operating without authorization. The deferral isn't protecting you. It's making the exposure worse."
