### Signal 1: The Decision Attribution Problem Is Now the Primary Named Exposure (NET-NEW C301)

**What the signal says:** In C301, the cross-vertical ICP is naming the decision attribution problem — "I cannot reconstruct who made which AI decision in which client matter" — as their primary exposure, not the attestation debt or processor chain. The shift is from "I signed something" (C300) to "I cannot show what happened after I signed" (C301). This is a structural maturation of the problem.

**The progression:**
- C297–C299: "I signed something I didn't understand." (attestation debt)
- C300: "The vendor can change the tool without asking me." (contract trap)
- C301: "I can't reconstruct the decision chain for any client matter in the last 18 months." (decision attribution failure)

**Why this matters for positioning:** The decision attribution problem is more intimate and more frightening than the attestation debt. The ICP can argue that the attestation was a technical oversight. They cannot argue that they don't know what happened in their own client files. The decision attribution failure is a direct challenge to professional identity — "you don't know what your firm did."

**The specific trigger:** When a carrier, client, or professional body asks "how did AI influence the decision in this specific matter?" — and the ICP cannot answer — that is the moment of maximum receptivity. The question is no longer "do you have a policy?" The question is "can you show us what actually happened?"

**Cross-vertical confirmation:**
- Legal: "The client asked how AI was used in their file. I could not answer. The file was produced by a combination of my work, the AI assistant, the research tool, and the vendor update. I cannot reconstruct."
- Finance: "The carrier asked about AI-influenced portfolio decisions. I have the output. I don't have the decision chain. The AI made micro-decisions continuously. I cannot show which decisions were AI-influenced."
- Consulting: "The client asked for the methodology provenance for the proposal. The proposal was produced through multiple iterations with AI tools, sub-consultant inputs, and my own analysis. I cannot show which element came from where."
- Healthcare: "Swissmedic could ask how the AI tool was used for this patient. The resident used it. The tool was updated. The patient requested something. The decision chain is unreconstructable."

**Service implication:** The decision attribution service is the C301 entry point. Not "do you have a policy?" — "can you show us what happened in your last 20 client matters?" This is a different question than "do you have documentation." It requires a different service.

### Signal 2: The Compliance Fatigue Performance Is Structurally Blocking Real Action (NET-NEW C301)

**What the signal says:** In C301, the ICP has developed a sophisticated compliance performance practice — attending webinars, downloading guides, updating policies, responding to questionnaires — that substitutes for actual structural decisions. This performance is self-reinforcing: the ICP feels they're engaged, the carrier still asks the same questions, the ICP updates their policy and attends another webinar. The performance loop prevents the firm from confronting the structural decisions that would create documentation exposure.

**The mechanism:** Real action on AI governance requires making classification decisions — "which tier am I, which tools are we authorized for, what's our oversight protocol for this specific tool." Each decision produces a document that can be scrutinized. The ICP avoids the decision to avoid the scrutiny. The compliance performance substitutes for the decision.

**The vendor role:** Vendors benefit from compliance fatigue. "Subscribe to our compliance monitoring tool and you'll always be up to date" is appealing because it offers the feeling of progress without the cost of a decision. The vendor has commercial interest in keeping the firm in the performance loop.

**The carrier role:** Carrier forms that ask questions without defining the answer standard create compliance fatigue. The ICP updates their policy, responds to the questionnaire, and the carrier comes back with a new version asking different questions. The carrier benefits from the compliance-anxiety loop — each form iteration reveals what the carrier decided was inadequate in the previous version.

**The professional body role:** FMH published principles without operational procedure. The bar published guidance without templates. The SRO published requirements without scale-calibrated methodology. Each publication creates compliance performance material without creating actionable decision criteria.

**The structural trap:** The ICP is performing compliance without making structural decisions. The performance loop is sustainable until a peer enforcement event or carrier demand for evidence forces the firm to confront that the performance documentation doesn't show what actually happened.

**Service implication:** The service that breaks the compliance fatigue loop is the service that names it — "you've been compliant with the old version of the standard. Here's what the new version requires, and here's how to build it without being in this loop again."

### Signal 3: The Vendor Contract Trap Is Now the Named Primary Structural Problem (NET-NEW C301)

**What the signal says:** In C301, the ICP is naming the vendor contract trap — unilateral capability changes, data portability limitations, model training retention, termination friction — as the primary structural problem preventing governance improvement. This is distinct from C300's "processor chain" framing, which focused on sub-processor disclosure. C301 focuses on the vendor contract as an instrument of governance constraint.

**The progression:**
- C298–C299: "The vendor has sub-processors I didn't know about." (processor chain awareness)
- C300: "The vendor can change what the tool does without my consent." (contract trap recognition)
- C301: "The contract I signed prevents me from improving my governance." (contract as governance barrier)

**Why this matters for positioning:** The ICP who understands the contract trap recognizes that governance improvement is not simply a documentation problem — it is constrained by commercial terms they accepted without evaluation. The path to improved governance requires vendor contract evaluation before it requires documentation building.

**The specific contract trap elements:**
1. **Unilateral capability change:** "The vendor can add AI capabilities without my explicit consent — as long as they send an email I don't read."
2. **Model training data retention:** "If I switch vendors, my interaction history is retained for model training. I lose the benefit of prior AI-assisted work."
3. **Data portability limitation:** "My data is not portable in a format I can use. The vendor's export is not the vendor's import."
4. **Termination friction:** "The contract requires 90-day notice, data extraction periods, and transition assistance fees that make switching impractical."
5. **Sub-processor authorization via click-through:** "I authorized sub-processors through terms acceptance I didn't read. The authorization chain is complete but invisible."

**Cross-vertical confirmation:**
- Legal: SaaS legal research tool added generative AI capability via terms update. The attorney didn't make a decision. The attorney is now using a capability that wasn't in scope when they signed.
- Finance: Portfolio management AI tool changed its optimization algorithm via update. The managing director didn't authorize the change. The AI-influenced decision attribution is now impossible.
- Consulting: Proposal generation AI tool added content synthesis via vendor update. The consulting firm can't document which content was AI-generated vs. human-authored.
- Healthcare: EHR system's AI diagnostic assistance updated its routing logic via vendor push. The physician didn't make a decision. The oversight documentation for the specific routing decision doesn't exist.

**Service implication:** Vendor contract exit planning is a discrete service offering in C301 — not as a legal review exercise, but as a governance architecture prerequisite. The ICP needs to know what's in the contract before they can plan governance improvement.

### Signal 4: The Evidence-Policy Gap Is the Carrier's Primary Demand Evolution (NET-NEW C301)

**What the signal says:** In C301, carrier forms have evolved from asking "do you have a policy?" to asking "show us evidence of what actually happened." The evidence-policy gap — documentation describing intended governance vs. documentation proving operational governance occurred — is now the primary documentation demand.

**The evolution:**
- Carrier form v1: "Do you use AI tools?" (yes/no)
- Carrier form v2: "Do you have an AI governance policy?" (policy existence)
- Carrier form v3: "Describe your AI governance framework." (policy content evaluation)
- Carrier form v4 (current): "Show us your last three AI governance decision records." "Show us your last tool authorization." "Show us your last oversight review." (evidence of operational governance)

**The ICP's experience of this evolution:** Each carrier form iteration creates a documentation update cycle. The ICP updates their policy. The carrier comes back with a new question that reveals what was inadequate about the previous version. The ICP is always answering yesterday's questions with yesterday's documentation.

**The evidence standard problem:** Carrier forms now ask for evidence — not a policy document. Evidence requires a decision event that was documented. For AI tools adopted 12–24 months ago through vendor updates and click-through acceptance, no decision event occurred. There is no evidence to produce, regardless of how complete the policy document is.

**Cross-vertical confirmation:**
- Legal: Carrier asks for "last AI tool authorization record." The firm has a policy that says "all AI tools must be authorized." The last authorization record is from 18 months ago. The tools in use were not authorized after the policy was written.
- Finance: Carrier asks for "portfolio AI decision audit trail." The firm's AI tools produce continuous micro-decisions. No audit trail was built. The portfolio AI vendor's logs show activity — not the managing director's governance decisions.
- Consulting: Client asks for "methodology provenance for this proposal." The proposal was produced through an undocumented combination of human and AI work. No provenance record exists.
- Healthcare: Carrier asks for "human oversight evidence for this specific AI tool." The physician has a policy that says "physician oversees AI." The physician cannot show the oversight decision for the specific tool on the specific date.

**Service implication:** The evidence-building service is the C301 documentation service. Not "build a policy" — "build the evidence infrastructure that shows what actually happened." The policy is table stakes. The evidence is what the carrier is actually asking for.

### Signal 5: Peer Enforcement Signals Are Converting Awareness Into Engagement (NET-NEW C301)

**What the signal says:** In C301, peer enforcement events — a Geneva attorney questioned by the bar, a Geneva wealth manager fined under nFADP, a consulting firm losing a client over methodology provenance, a physician facing FMH scrutiny for digital tool use — are the conversion trigger that external deadlines alone cannot produce. The peer signal creates personal identification that abstract regulatory language cannot.

**The mechanism:** External deadline awareness creates "I should probably do something." Peer enforcement creates "that could be me." The difference is personal identification — the ICP recognizes themselves in the peer's situation, not just in the category of risk.

**The peer signal hierarchy:**
1. **Direct personal connection:** "I know the attorney who was questioned by the bar." — maximum conversion
2. **Professional network connection:** "Someone in my network knows that firm." — high conversion
3. **Geneva market event:** "I heard about this at a professional event." — medium conversion
4. **Published case:** "I read about the fine in the newspaper." — low conversion

**The C301 specific signals:**
- Geneva legal: A peer firm was asked to produce AI governance documentation for a client matter. They couldn't. The client asked for a different firm.
- Geneva finance: A peer wealth manager was fined for nFADP violations related to AI vendor sub-processors. The fine was published. The peer's carrier asked all clients about their processor chains.
- Geneva consulting: A peer consulting firm lost a methodology dispute with a client. The client claimed AI was used in the work product without disclosure. The firm couldn't demonstrate its methodology provenance.
- Geneva healthcare: A peer physician received an FMH complaint related to digital tool oversight. The complaint cited the physician's inability to demonstrate human oversight of an AI tool.

**Service implication:** The peer signal creates the receptivity window. The service that arrives in that window with specific naming of the peer's exact situation — not generic guidance — converts at maximum rate. "A Geneva attorney was asked to produce AI decision records for a client matter" is the engagement trigger, not "you need AI governance documentation."

### Signal 6: The Strategic Classification Deferral Is Now the Named Risk Behavior (NET-NEW C301)

**What the signal says:** In C301, the ICP is naming the strategic classification deferral — consciously choosing not to make the Swissdamed Tier 1/Tier 2 classification decision, or the EU AI Act nexus determination, because making the decision forces acknowledgment of commitments already made — as the primary risk behavior. This is distinct from paralysis. It is strategic avoidance.

**The mechanism:** Making the classification decision requires evaluating the firm's AI tool portfolio against classification criteria. That evaluation produces a document — a classification decision record — that can be scrutinized by regulators, carriers, and clients. The ICP chooses uncertainty (future risk) over documentation (present exposure) because documentation of a bad decision is worse than no documentation.

**The specific pattern:**
1. The firm is using AI tools.
2. The firm doesn't know their Swissdamed tier.
3. Making the classification decision requires documenting the AI tool use.
4. Documenting the AI tool use creates evidence of commitments already made.
5. The ICP defers the classification decision to avoid the documentation exposure.

**The irony:** The deferral doesn't reduce risk — it increases it. The firm continues using AI tools while in classification ambiguity. If Swissdamed requires classification as a precondition for use, the firm is using tools without authorization. The deferral creates more exposure, not less.

**The Geneva-specific timing:** Swissdamed July 1, 2026 registration deadline creates a time-limited opportunity for strategic deferral. The ICP is planning to classify after the deadline (hoping for grace periods, extensions, or ambiguity). The strategic deferral is a bet that the deadline will not be enforced.

**Service implication:** The service that names the strategic deferral pattern — "you're deferring because making the decision creates documentation exposure — and that exposure is growing while you defer" — breaks the deferral pattern. The ICP needs to understand that the deferral is not protecting them.

## The Structural Moat: What Persists Regardless of Vertical (C301)

1. **The decision attribution architecture was never built:** AI tools entered the firm through vendor updates and click-through acceptance without creating decision events. The firm cannot reconstruct the decision chain retroactively.
2. **The compliance fatigue loop is self-reinforcing:** The ICP performs compliance with the current standard while the standard is moving. Each compliance performance produces documentation that answers yesterday's questions.
3. **Vendor contracts constrain governance improvement:** The terms accepted without reading prevent governance improvement through vendor switching. The exit barrier is commercial, not technical.
4. **Evidence demand exceeds policy documentation:** Carrier forms now ask for evidence of operational governance. The firm's documentation describes intended governance. The gap is structural.
5. **Peer enforcement converts where abstract risk cannot:** The ICP who hears about a peer being questioned, fined, or losing a client converts at a higher rate than the ICP who reads about regulatory deadlines.
6. **The classification deferral increases exposure:** Strategic avoidance of classification decisions keeps the firm in uncertainty while exposure accumulates. The deferral is not risk reduction — it is risk accumulation in a different form.
