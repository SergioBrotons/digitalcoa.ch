# 03 — Competitive Analysis: What Exists vs. What's Missing
## Phase 02 | Cycle C304 | Geneva Finance / Boutique Wealth Management

---

## Current Market Landscape

### What Exists

#### 1. Big 4 Advisory — Framework Producers
**What they offer:** AI governance frameworks, risk assessments, policy documents, regulatory gap analyses for financial institutions.

**Where they operate:** Large institutions (systemically important financial institutions, banks, insurance groups). Minimum engagement sizes that make them inaccessible to boutique WM firms.

**The gap:** Big 4 produce frameworks — documents that describe what should exist. They do not produce authorization records in the format carrier Form v4 requires. Their deliverable is a framework document, not an evidence file. The finance ICP needs evidence; Big 4 produce policy.

**The proportionality failure:** Big 4 engagement minimums are designed for institutional scale. A CHF 2B AUM boutique WM firm cannot justify the minimum engagement fee for a Big 4 framework project. The deliverable (framework) does not address the ICP's actual problem (evidence absence).

---

#### 2. General Management Consultants — Generic AI Governance
**What they offer:** AI governance frameworks, ISO 42001 certification prep, technology risk assessments.

**Where they operate:** Cross-industry, including financial services. Generalist approach.

**The gap:** General management consultants address AI governance at the framework level — what policies should exist, what governance structure should be implemented. They do not produce the authorization records, decision attribution files, and vendor processor chain documentation that satisfy carrier Form v4. The consultant's deliverable (framework) is structurally different from what the carrier requires (evidence).

**The Swiss-specificity failure:** General consultants cannot speak to the Swiss regulatory cluster (FINMA 08/2024, FINMA 2025/02, nFADP Art. 63, CRS 2.0, EU AI Act nexus) with the precision required for boutique WM documentation. Their methodology is calibrated for generic cross-industry application, not Swiss regulatory architecture.

---

#### 3. Legal Firms — Regulatory Interpretation
**What they offer:** Legal opinions on AI governance obligations, regulatory interpretation, nFADP compliance counsel, contract review for AI vendor agreements.

**Where they operate:** Legal compliance, contract review, regulatory interpretation. Limited to legal dimensions.

**The gap:** Legal firms provide legal interpretation — what the obligations are under Swiss law. They do not produce the authorization records, decision attribution files, and evidence packages that satisfy carrier attestation and institutional client questionnaires. The gap between legal opinion and operational evidence is not addressed by legal counsel.

**The documentation-production failure:** Legal counsel understands nFADP Art. 63 personal liability in the abstract but does not produce the operational evidence package that would serve as evidence of due diligence if a claim arises. The legal opinion and the evidence file are different documents with different purposes.

---

#### 4. Compliance Software Vendors — Documentation Platforms
**What they offer:** Compliance management platforms, policy management systems, audit trail software, regulatory tracking tools.

**Where they operate:** Enterprise compliance management, document retention, audit workflows.

**The gap:** Compliance software platforms manage the storage and retrieval of documents the firm produces. They do not produce the underlying documentation. A boutique WM firm without authorization records cannot produce them by buying a compliance software platform. The software manages what exists; it does not create what doesn't exist.

**The assumption failure:** The platform assumes the firm has documentation to manage. The finance ICP does not have the underlying authorization records, decision attribution files, and evidence packages. Buying platform access does not solve the documentation gap — it organizes documentation that doesn't exist.

---

#### 5. Cybersecurity Firms — Vendor Security Assessments
**What they offer:** Vendor security assessments, due diligence questionnaires, AI tool security evaluations, data processing reviews.

**Where they operate:** Vendor security, technology risk, third-party risk management.

**The gap:** Cybersecurity firms assess vendor security posture — whether the vendor's systems are secure. They do not assess vendor contractual compliance — whether the vendor's data processing terms create obligations under nFADP Art. 9 and carrier attestation requirements. The security assessment and the contractual compliance assessment are different exercises.

**The narrow scope failure:** Cybersecurity firms address the vendor security question, not the vendor data processing compliance question. The finance ICP needs both — and the gap between them is unaddressed. Additionally, cybersecurity firms do not address the EU AI Act deployer obligation problem for non-EU vendors.

---

### What's Missing

#### Missing 1: Vendor Terms Drift Monitoring and Re-Verification — The Post-Signature Compliance Problem
**What is missing:** A service that monitors AI vendor terms for post-signature changes, tracks sub-processor additions, documents model version changes, and produces a current-state vendor terms assessment that verifies attestation accuracy.

**Why it doesn't exist:** The market treats vendor contract signature as a one-time compliance event. The ongoing obligation to monitor vendor terms changes between signature and renewal is not addressed. No Geneva service produces post-signature vendor terms re-verification for AI SaaS tools used by boutique WM firms.

**The addressable gap:** A service that produces a current-state vendor terms assessment — documenting what has changed since original signature, which changes affect AI governance obligations, and what the managing director needs to know to certify attestation accuracy at renewal.

---

#### Missing 2: Continuous AI Authorization Documentation Architecture — The24/7 AI Documentation Problem
**What is missing:** A documentation methodology specifically designed for continuous AI systems — automated portfolio rebalancing, algorithmic risk profiling, AI-driven allocation engines — that produces authorization records satisfying carrier Form v4 requirements for systems that operate without discrete authorization events.

**Why it doesn't exist:** All existing documentation methodologies assume discrete AI deployments — a system is authorized, it operates, it's reviewed. The documentation framework for continuous AI systems (24/7 operation, no discrete decision events, ongoing human approval that is theoretical rather than documented) has not been developed. No Geneva service addresses this specific documentation architecture.

**The addressable gap:** A documentation architecture designed for continuous AI systems — defining what "authorization record" means for a system that operates continuously, what "human approval" means for automated rebalancing, and what "review cycle" means for AI that adjusts portfolios without discrete human decision events.

---

#### Missing 3: Cross-Framework Documentation Architecture — The FINMA-nFADP Alignment Problem
**What is missing:** A documentation methodology that produces evidence satisfying both FINMA and nFADP simultaneously — where a single documentation event produces records that satisfy both frameworks without creating internal inconsistency between what FINMA documentation shows and what nFADP Art. 63 protection requires.

**Why it doesn't exist:** FINMA compliance and nFADP Art. 63 personal liability protection are addressed by different specialists producing different documentation types. No service exists that designs a unified documentation architecture serving both objectives simultaneously. The market treats these as separate compliance problems; they are separate documentation problems requiring unified architecture.

**The addressable gap:** A documentation architecture that produces FINMA-compliant authorization records AND Art. 63 personal due diligence evidence simultaneously — where the same documentation event serves both protection objectives without requiring duplicate documentation effort.

---

#### Missing 4: EU AI Act Deployer Due Diligence for Non-EU Vendors — The Structural Conformity Problem
**What is missing:** A service that defines and produces the specific documentation demonstrating EU AI Act deployer due diligence when EU Declaration of Conformity is structurally unavailable from the AI vendor (non-EU vendor).

**Why it doesn't exist:** The EU AI Act's deployer obligation is new (August 2026 implementation). The specific documentation that demonstrates "deployer due diligence" when the vendor cannot provide EU DoC has not been defined. No Geneva service addresses this specific problem for Swiss firms using US-based AI vendors with EU client exposure.

**The addressable gap:** A service that produces deployer due diligence documentation for non-EU vendor configurations — documenting what due diligence was performed, what the firm discovered about the vendor's conformity limitations, and what compensating controls the firm has implemented to satisfy deployer obligations when vendor conformity is structurally unavailable.

---

#### Missing 5: Documentation Debt Quantification and Payoff Methodology — The Compounding Debt Problem
**What is missing:** A service that quantifies the documentation debt across the firm's entire AI tool stack, produces a debt payoff roadmap, and establishes a documentation architecture preventing new debt accumulation.

**Why it doesn't exist:** The market treats documentation as a compliance deliverable — produce the required document, satisfy the requirement. The compounding debt model (each AI tool deployed without records adds to the debt; each year the debt compounds; no static state) is not addressed as a quantifiable problem with a specific payoff methodology. No service quantifies documentation debt or produces a structured payoff plan.

**The addressable gap:** A documentation debt assessment service — quantifying the debt across all AI tools, producing a prioritized authorization record production roadmap, and establishing a documentation architecture that prevents new debt accumulation going forward.

---

#### Missing 6: Art. 63 Personal Due Diligence Evidence Package — The Personal Asset Protection Problem
**What is missing:** A service that produces the specific documentation the managing director needs to demonstrate personal due diligence under nFADP Art. 63 — evidence that protects personal assets from criminal liability, distinct from and in addition to carrier attestation evidence.

**Why it doesn't exist:** The carrier attestation and Art. 63 personal liability protection serve different protection objectives. The carrier's evidence requirements protect the carrier's subrogation position. Art. 63 personal due diligence evidence protects the managing director's personal assets from criminal exposure. No Geneva service distinguishes between these two protection objectives or produces documentation serving the Art. 63 objective specifically.

**The addressable gap:** A personal due diligence evidence package service — producing documentation showing the managing director's personal oversight activities, controls implemented, accountability structures, and evidence of ongoing review for AI systems processing client data.
