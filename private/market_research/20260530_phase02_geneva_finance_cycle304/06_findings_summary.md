## Key Insights

### NET-NEW Findings (C304 Phase 02 — Finance Vertical)

**1. Vendor Terms Drift Has Created Attestation Inaccuracy Nobody Is Monitoring**
Boutique wealth management firms signed AI vendor data processing agreements in 2022–2024. Those agreements have been amended, updated, or supplemented since signing — vendor terms drift is continuous. The managing director cannot verify whether the attestation they signed remains accurate. The carrier attestation requires certifying that vendor conditions have not changed in ways that affect AI governance — but the firm has no mechanism to verify this. The attestation is functionally inaccurate before the renewal cycle arrives. The specific documentation that would verify attestation accuracy (current-state vendor terms assessment, post-signature amendment tracking, model version documentation) does not exist and was never commissioned.

**2. Authorization Records for Continuous AI Systems Were Never Designed to Exist**
The carrier Form v4 requires "the last three authorization records per AI tool." Boutique wealth management firms are discovering that authorization records for continuous AI systems — automated portfolio rebalancing, algorithmic risk profiling, AI-driven allocation engines — were never created in any format. The documentation architecture for continuous AI systems (24/7 operation, no discrete authorization events, theoretical human approval) was never designed. This is not a documentation backlog — it's documentation that was never conceived to exist. Retroactive reconstruction is structurally impossible because the systems were never built to capture this data. The gap is architectural, not incremental.

**3. FINMA Documentation Does Not Satisfy nFADP Art. 63 Personal Liability Protection**
Managing directors who built AI governance documentation to satisfy FINMA requirements are discovering that FINMA-compliant documentation does not satisfy nFADP Art. 63 personal liability requirements. The two frameworks require different documentation types serving different protection objectives. FINMA requires authorization records demonstrating firm-level governance. Art. 63 requires evidence of personal due diligence by the responsible person — a documentation type that FINMA documentation does not produce. The firm that believes it is "in compliance" because it satisfies FINMA may have zero personal criminal liability protection. Legal counsel that advises on FINMA compliance is not providing Art. 63 personal liability protection. These are parallel documentation requirements requiring unified architecture — and no Geneva service has addressed this gap.

**4. EU AI Act Deployer Obligation With Non-EU Vendors Is Structurally Unsolvable at Boutique WM Scale**
Geneva boutique wealth management firms with EU-based clients are discovering that EU AI Act obligations fall on the "deployer" of AI systems. The deployer is responsible for ensuring the AI system used meets EU conformity requirements. Non-EU AI vendors cannot provide EU Declaration of Conformity. The Geneva firm serving EU clients is structurally unable to satisfy EU AI Act deployer obligations using non-EU AI vendors — and no Geneva service has defined what "deployer due diligence" looks like when EU DoC is structurally unavailable. This is an active, unsolved problem, not a future risk. The specific documentation that demonstrates deployer due diligence for non-EU vendor configurations has not been defined anywhere in the Geneva market.

**5. Documentation Debt Is Quantifiable and Compounding With No Payment Mechanism**
The documentation debt model from C303 is now quantifiable: each AI tool deployed without authorization records adds to the debt in real time. Each year without record creation means the debt compounds across five to eight AI tools simultaneously. The carrier renewal cycle requires annual attestation — each year the gap deepens without a payment mechanism. The debt is not static: it grows with each deployment and each year of non-documentation. The firm that deploys a new AI tool without authorization records adds to the debt in real time. Without a specific documentation production intervention, the debt grows indefinitely. The documentation debt quantification and payoff methodology is a distinct service category with clear addressable demand.

**6. Carrier Attestation and Art. 63 Personal Liability Protection Serve Different Objectives**
The carrier insurance product responds to claims — paying settlements, legal fees, and regulatory fines. However, nFADP Art. 63 personal criminal liability is not discharged by carrier payment. The carrier's evidence requirements (Form v4) are designed to protect the carrier from subrogation exposure — not to protect the managing director's personal assets from Art. 63 criminal exposure. These are different protection objectives that the same attestation does not simultaneously satisfy. The managing director who believes the carrier attestation protects them personally is operating under a structural misunderstanding. The Art. 63 personal due diligence evidence package is a distinct document type serving a distinct protection objective — and no Geneva service distinguishes between them or produces both.
