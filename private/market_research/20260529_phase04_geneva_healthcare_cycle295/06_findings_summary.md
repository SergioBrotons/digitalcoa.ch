# 06 — Findings Summary: Geneva Independent Healthcare Practice

## Phase 04 | Cycle 295 | Geneva Healthcare ICP

---

## 6 NET-NEW Findings (Distinct from C214–C294 Phase 04)

### Finding 01: AI Tool Switching Liability as Discrete Unnamed Exposure Category

**The finding:** When independent practices switch AI clinical tool vendors — moving from one AI-supported EHR to another, or from one AI diagnostic support platform to a competitor — the medical record becomes a continuous stream of AI-generated content from multiple systems with no transition documentation. The physician cannot identify which AI system generated which clinical content. The record is authoritative under the physician's name with no AI attribution architecture.

**Distinct from prior findings:** All prior Phase 04 findings addressed AI tool adoption, AI accumulation, AI deprecation, or AI vendor contract signature as liability creation. This finding specifically names **AI tool switching mid-record** as a discrete liability scenario that is distinct from first adoption and distinct from deprecation — because it creates a cascade of AI-to-AI influence where the physician is accountable for AI-generated content from multiple systems that they cannot reconstruct or attribute.

**Ready-to-use language:** "When you switched from Tool A to Tool B, the AI-generated content from Tool A stayed in your records under your name. You cannot tell a carrier or a court which clinical conclusions came from which AI system. That's not a documentation quality problem — it's a structural accountability impossibility that already exists in your records."

---

### Finding 02: Patient-Override-of-AI Documentation Void as Named Clinical Documentation Category

**The finding:** When a patient arrives with consumer AI-generated health analyses and the physician exercises independent clinical judgment to make a different decision than the AI recommended, the medical record documents the patient's AI-provided information and the physician's final decision — but not the specific clinical deliberation moment where AI input was evaluated and rejected. This is a discrete documentation category (AI-influenced-but-not-AI-determined clinical decision) that has no Geneva protocol, no naming, and no defined documentation requirement — despite being a moment of high clinical accountability.

**Distinct from prior findings:** Patient-generated AI content entering medical records was named across C287–C294. The specific scenario of **physician override of patient-provided AI recommendations** as a discrete documentation moment requiring own documentation architecture has not been named. The accountability question here is different from physician-deployed AI (prior findings) or vendor AI (prior findings): the physician's independent judgment was exercised against AI input, and no Geneva guidance defines what that documentation should contain.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your patient comes in with AI results saying X. You evaluate it. You disagree. You decide Y. The record shows Y. It doesn't show that you considered and rejected X. If something goes wrong, the question won't be whether Y was right — it'll be whether you actually exercised independent judgment or just followed the AI. You have no documentation of the moment that proves you did."

---

### Finding 03: Multi-Physician Practice Internal Accountability Diffusion as Named Structural Problem

**The finding:** In a Geneva small group practice (3–6 physicians), each physician independently adopts different AI tools, activates different EHR AI features, and implements different AI-assisted documentation practices — without practice-level AI governance coordination. When the practice receives a carrier attestation questionnaire, the signing physician certifies to an AI surface they cannot fully enumerate because each partner's AI decisions were made independently. No Geneva methodology exists for addressing internal accountability diffusion at small-group practice scale.

**Distinct from prior findings:** Prior Phase 04 findings addressed single-physician governance impossibility (one person holds user/compliance/billing roles simultaneously). This finding specifically names **internal accountability diffusion across multiple physicians within the same independent group practice** — a governance failure that is specific to group practice scale and creates documentation exposure at the practice level that no individual physician can satisfy alone.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You're signing for a practice where you don't know everything your partners are using. Three physicians, three independent AI decisions, no coordination. When the carrier asks what AI tools you use, you have a number you can name — and a larger surface you can't. That gap is your liability."

---

### Finding 04: AI-Associated Adverse Outcome Documentation Architecture Absence as Discrete Service Category

**The finding:** When an adverse clinical outcome occurs in an independent practice, the existing incident documentation infrastructure — internal review, carrier reporting, cantonal reporting — was designed for human clinical decision-making and has no category for: AI system active in the encounter, AI tool version/state at time of event, AI recommendation made and whether it was followed, physician's AI-influenced vs. AI-independent decisions. The physician's name is on the record. The AI's contribution is invisible to the review process. No Geneva medical body or carrier has published AI-associated adverse outcome documentation requirements proportionate to independent practice.

**Distinct from prior findings:** Medical record AI authorship ambiguity (C288/C289) was about records going forward. This finding specifically names the **post-incident documentation architecture gap** — the specific categories required for AI-associated events that existing incident review infrastructure cannot accommodate. The difference is temporal: authorship ambiguity is a present-tense problem; adverse outcome documentation is a retrospective problem that reveals itself when something goes wrong.

**Ready-to-use language:** "When something goes wrong, the incident review will look at your records. Your signature is there. The AI's contribution isn't — because there's no category for it. The review will conclude you made the decision. The AI system that recommended something different, that was running a version from six months ago, that no one documented — that's invisible. You're carrying the accountability for a process you can't reconstruct."

---

### Finding 05: AI Competency Documentation Structural Inadequacy as Wrong-Standard Compliance Paradox

**The finding:** Independent practices are building AI competency documentation (vendor training certificates, staff attendance records, online course completions) to satisfy implied FMH AI competency requirements — requirements that FMH has never defined with operational specificity. The documentation being created is inadequate to the evidentiary purpose it's meant to serve, but the practice believes it is complying. The false adequacy created by wrong-standard compliance is more dangerous than no documentation: the practice has evidence of compliance that does not actually comply.

**Distinct from prior findings:** AI training documentation as a discrete exposure category was named in C287/C289. This finding specifically isolates the **structural inadequacy of current practice-level AI competency documentation** — not that documentation is absent, but that it is built to the wrong standard and would not survive a substantive challenge while creating false confidence that compliance has been achieved.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You're documenting AI competency with vendor training certificates because you think that's what FMH wants. FMH has never told you what adequate AI competency documentation means. Your documentation would not survive a carrier or FMH challenge — but you have it, so you think you're compliant. That's the dangerous part. You're not just ungoverned — you're ungoverned with documentation that looks like governance."

---

### Finding 06: Carrier Attestation Stampede Effect as Self-Incriminating Documentation Pattern

**The finding:** As carrier AI attestation requirements arrive in Geneva healthcare, independent practices are experiencing a **stampede response** — receiving attestation questionnaires and attempting to document AI governance retroactively under time pressure (typically 2–4 weeks), using vendor-downloaded templates and internet searches, creating documentation that: does not accurately represent actual AI practice, creates commercial representations to the carrier that cannot be supported with evidence, and is signed by physicians who have not had time to evaluate whether the documentation is accurate. The attempt to comply with carrier attestation requirements is creating documentation that will be used against the signing physician if a challenge occurs.

**Distinct from prior findings:** Carrier attestation pressure, attestation as personal liability instrument, and pre-signing review absence were all named in prior Phase 04 cycles. This finding specifically names the **stampede response pattern** as a market behavior mechanism — urgency-driven documentation creation that produces self-incriminating evidence. The specific paradox: the attempt to comply creates the documentation that will be weaponized.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your carrier gave you four weeks. You downloaded templates. You filled them in over two nights. You signed. Now you have documentation that says your AI governance looks like X — and when they ask you to show them X, you can't, because it doesn't actually look like that. You used the attestation to tell your carrier a story about your practice. When they audit, they'll use that story against you."

## Key Insights for ICP Engagement

### Why Phase 04 Geneva Healthcare ICP Is Highly Qualified

1. **They are personally exposed** — physician license, personal liability, name on record, career-ending consequence
2. **They have no infrastructure** — no IT department, no compliance team, no governance staff
3. **They are already being measured** — carrier attestation arriving, FDPIC enforcement live
4. **They cannot comply** — multiple frameworks simultaneously applicable with no proportionate pathway
5. **They are alone** — institutional intelligence excluded, no Geneva-calibrated service exists for their scale

### What Activates Them

- **"Before your carrier asks"** — external urgency creates immediate action trigger
- **"Personally accountable"** — personal liability frame, not institutional
- **CHF 250,000 fine, name on license** — specific, visceral, career-ending magnitude
- **"No Geneva protocol exists"** — validates their isolation; they're not alone because they're behind, they're alone because the guidance doesn't exist
- **"Nobody told you what to do"** — validates their experience as systemic not personal failure

### What Doesn't Activate Them

- Abstract regulatory framework descriptions
- Efficiency/productivity improvement framing
- Generic "best practice" checklists
- Technology-focused AI discussions

### The Briefing Format Advantage

The 90-minute briefing format works because:
1. **Geneva independent physicians have 90 minutes** — not a half-day, not a full-day; 90 minutes is the maximum available for a non-billable compliance session
2. **The six named problems** — naming the unnamed creates immediate recognition and validates their isolation as systemic
3. **Documented outputs** — they leave with something tangible: AI surface inventory, named problems, compliance roadmap
4. **Personal liability intensity** — the frame must be personal, not institutional, not practice-level
5. **CHF 2,200** — proportionate to the exposure, not framed as "affordable," framed as what the accountability is worth before the carrier asks
