# 02 — Market Signals: Geneva Independent Healthcare Practice

## Phase 04 | Cycle 295 | Geneva Healthcare ICP

---

## Current Market Problems, Complaints, and Failures

### Signal 1: AI Tool Switching Liability as Unnamed Discrete Exposure

**Signal type:** Clinical accountability gap emerging from active practice behavior

**What is happening:**
Independent practices are beginning to switch AI tool vendors — moving from one clinical decision support platform to another, or from one EHR AI feature set to a competitor. When a patient record contains AI-generated content from Tool A and the practice switches to Tool B, the historical AI-generated content from Tool A remains in the medical record under the physician's authoritative name. The new AI tool (Tool B) may generate different recommendations based on the Tool A-generated content in the record, creating a cascade of AI-to-AI influence with no documentation of the transition point or the original AI attribution.

**The specific failure mode:**
- Patient seen in 2024: AI Tool A generates clinical observation recommendations
- 2025: Practice migrates to AI Tool B
- Patient returns in 2026: Tool B analyzes records including Tool A-generated content
- Current physician cannot explain which clinical conclusions originated from which AI system
- Medical record is authoritative under physician's name with no AI attribution architecture

**Why this is distinct from prior findings:**
Prior findings addressed AI tool selection at adoption and AI tool deprecation. This finding addresses the specific liability created by **mid-record AI tool switching** — a practice behavior emerging now as vendor competition in clinical AI increases. No Geneva guidance addresses this. No consent framework accounts for it. The physician's record is a continuous stream of AI-influenced content from multiple systems with no transition documentation.

**Market complaint language:**
> *"We switched systems and now I don't know what was generated by the old AI and what by the new one — but it's all under my name."*

### Signal 2: AI-Informed but Patient-Override Decisions Creating Documentation Void

**Signal type:** Informed refusal/override pathway gap

**What is happening:**
Physicians are increasingly encountering patients who arrive with consumer AI-generated health analyses — symptom checkers, AI-assisted lab interpretations, consumer wearable data summaries — that the patient presents as clinical input. The physician evaluates the AI-generated content, exercises independent medical judgment, and makes a different clinical decision than the AI suggested. The patient overrides the AI recommendation through the physician.

This creates a **three-layer documentation problem**:
1. Patient-generated AI content enters record as patient-provided information
2. Physician's independent clinical evaluation documented (standard medical record)
3. The specific moment of AI-influenced-but-not-AI-determined decision — the override — is not a named documentation category

**The failure mode:**
When an adverse outcome occurs, the record shows patient-provided AI information and physician's decision, but does not document that the physician specifically considered and rejected AI-generated recommendations. The evidentiary gap is not about whether the physician made a good decision, but about the existence of a structured AI-influenced clinical deliberation process.

**Why this is distinct from prior findings:**
Patient-generated AI content was named in C287/C288/C289/C290/C291/C293/C294 as entering the medical record. The specific scenario of **physician override of patient-provided AI recommendations** as a discrete documentation category has not been named. The accountability question is different: the physician's independent judgment was exercised against AI input, and no Geneva protocol defines what that documentation should contain.

**Market complaint language:**
> *"Patient came in with AI results saying one thing. I disagreed. Made my own call. But now if something goes wrong, the record just shows what I decided — not that I actually evaluated and rejected the AI input."*

### Signal 3: Multi-Physician Practice AI Governance Proliferation Creating Internal Accountability Diffusion

**Signal type:** Small group practice internal governance failure

**What is happening:**
A 3-physician Geneva practice has three independent physicians each independently adopting different AI tools, different EHR AI features, and different AI-assisted documentation practices. No practice-level AI governance exists. Each physician's AI surface is different. When the practice receives a carrier attestation questionnaire, the practice principal (or single designated signing physician) is signing for an AI environment they do not fully know across all three physicians' individual practices.

The internal accountability diffusion creates a structural problem: **no single physician can document the full AI surface of the practice**, because each physician's AI decisions were made independently without practice-level coordination.

**The specific failure mode:**
- Physician A uses AI Tool X for clinical notes
- Physician B uses AI Tool Y for diagnostic imaging support  
- Physician C uses AI Tool Z for administrative scheduling and patient communication
- Carrier attestation asks about AI tools used in the practice
- Signing physician certifies to an AI surface they cannot fully enumerate

**Why this is distinct from prior findings:**
Prior findings addressed AI governance at the individual physician level and the impossibility of small-practice governance due to single-person role concentration. This finding specifically names **internal accountability diffusion across multiple independent physicians within the same small group practice** — a governance failure that is specific to group practice scale (2–10 physicians) and creates documentation exposure at the practice level that no individual physician can satisfy.

**Market complaint language:**
> *"I'm signing for a practice where I don't even know everything my two partners are using. We're three independent physicians. Nobody sits down to discuss AI governance together."*

### Signal 4: Adverse Outcome Documentation Architecture Absence for AI-Associated Clinical Events

**Signal type:** Post-incident accountability infrastructure gap

**What is happening:**
When an adverse clinical outcome occurs in an independent practice, the existing incident documentation infrastructure (internal review, carrier reporting, cantonal reporting if required) was designed for human clinical decision-making. It does not have a category for:
- AI system active in the clinical encounter
- Which AI tool was operating and what it recommended
- What the physician's AI-influenced vs. AI-independent decisions were
- What version/state the AI system was in at the time

The adverse outcome review will reconstruct what the physician did. It will not reconstruct what AI did, because no documentation architecture captures AI system state at the time of clinical events. The physician's name is on the record. The AI's contribution is invisible to the review process.

**Why this is distinct from prior findings:**
Medical record authorship ambiguity was named in C288/C289 (AI content under physician's name with no attribution). This finding specifically names the **post-incident documentation architecture absence** — the specific documentation categories required for AI-associated adverse events that current incident review infrastructure cannot accommodate. The difference: authorship ambiguity is about the record going forward; adverse outcome documentation architecture is about what happens when the record is reviewed after something goes wrong.

**Market complaint language:**
> *"If something happens and they look at my records, they'll see my signature. They won't see that the AI recommended something I overrode, or that the AI was running an older version. There's no category for any of that."*

### Signal 5: AI Competency Documentation as Discrete FMH Requirement With No Defined Standard

**Signal type:** Regulatory compliance infrastructure gap

**What is happening:**
The FMH has indicated that physicians bear responsibility for ensuring AI tool competency. No FMH guidance defines:
- What AI competency documentation must contain
- What constitutes sufficient AI training for clinical use
- Whether formal AI competency records are required or optional
- How AI competency should be maintained as AI tools evolve
- What documentation survives a carrier or cantonal challenge

Independent practices are attempting to comply with an implied AI competency requirement by documenting staff training — recording that staff attended a vendor demo, completed an online training module, or received verbal instruction. None of these constitute what a carrier or FMH challenge would recognize as **AI competency documentation**.

**The specific problem:**
The documentation that practices are creating to satisfy implied AI competency requirements is:
1. Not designed for the actual evidentiary standard a challenge would require
2. Not mapping to FMH accountability requirements (which are undefined, making adequate documentation impossible to construct)
3. Creating a false sense of compliance — documentation exists but would not survive a substantive challenge

**Why this is distinct from prior findings:**
AI training documentation as a discrete exposure category was named in C287/C289. This finding specifically isolates the **structural inadequacy of current practice-level AI competency documentation** relative to what a challenge would require — not just that documentation is absent, but that the documentation being created is inadequate to the evidentiary purpose it's meant to serve.

**Market complaint language:**
> *"I documented that my assistant did the vendor training. But I have no idea if that's what FMH or my carrier would consider adequate. I don't even know what adequate means."*

### Signal 6: Geneva Independent Practice AI Governance Stampede Effect

**Signal type:** Market behavior pattern creating compounding exposure

**What is happening:**
As carrier attestation requirements begin arriving in the Geneva healthcare market (6–12 months behind legal/finance sectors), independent practices are experiencing a **stampede response** — receiving attestation questionnaires and suddenly attempting to document AI governance retroactively, build AI policies overnight, and create documentation frameworks that should have been built over months.

The stampede creates a specific secondary exposure: practices are building AI governance documentation under time pressure without methodology, using templates downloaded from vendor websites or found through brief internet searches, creating documentation that:
1. May not accurately represent their actual AI practice
2. Creates commercial representations to the carrier that the practice cannot support with evidence
3. Is being signed by physicians who have not had time to evaluate whether the documentation is accurate

**The paradox:**
The attempt to comply with carrier attestation requirements is creating the documentation that will be used against the practice if a challenge occurs — because the documentation was built under time pressure without foundational governance decisions, and represents what the practice thinks the carrier wants to see rather than what the practice actually does.

**Why this is distinct from prior findings:**
This specifically names the **stampede response pattern** as a market behavior creating compounding liability — distinct from the carrier attestation wave arrival timing (named in C291) and distinct from the documentation quality gap (named across multiple cycles). The specific mechanism: urgency-driven documentation creation that produces evidence that will be used against the signing physician.

**Market complaint language:**
> *"The carrier sent the form and said I had four weeks. Four weeks to document everything about AI in my practice. I spent two nights downloading templates and filling them in. I signed it. I have no idea if it's right."*
