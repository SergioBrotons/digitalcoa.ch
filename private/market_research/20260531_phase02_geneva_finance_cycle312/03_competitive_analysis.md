## 6 NET-NEW Competitive Findings (Distinct from Phase 01 and Prior Cycles)

### Finding 01: Institutional DDQ pre-reading market gap distinct from carrier form pre-review — different intelligence, same intervention logic

**Mechanism:** The institutional AI governance DDQ pre-reading gap is structurally parallel to the carrier form pre-signing review gap identified in Phase 01 — but the intelligence is different and no competitor has built either. Carrier form pre-signing review extracts: what the MD is personally attesting to, what evidence standard is embedded in the form, what post-signing evidence obligations the form creates. Institutional DDQ pre-reading extracts: what the institutional client actually requires for mandate continuation, what competitive positioning is embedded in the questions, what the client's investment committee specifically cares about regarding AI methodology, and where the client's standard is escalating. The institutional DDQ is a competitive intelligence document. No Geneva competitor reads it before advising the boutique on how to respond. The pre-reading intervention is the boutique's opportunity to calibrate their response to the specific institutional client before submitting documentation that becomes permanent competitive intelligence. This is distinct from Phase 01 Finding (carrier form pre-signing review gap) which addressed carrier instruments specifically, without recognizing that institutional DDQ pre-reading is a parallel and equally unoccupied intervention point with different intelligence content but the same intervention architecture.

**Phase 01 bridge:** Pre-signing carrier form review (Phase 01 highest-value intervention) + pre-reading institutional DDQ (Phase 02 parallel intervention) = dual-entry-point service model for boutique WM. No competitor has built either.

**Market signal:** Boutique WM firms responding to institutional DDQ without pre-reading the intelligence embedded in the questions are submitting responses calibrated to questions, not to competitive positioning.

**IMO differentiation angle:** Dual pre-document review service — carrier form pre-signing review + institutional DDQ pre-reading — is the boutique WM evidence architecture entry point. Both are unoccupied. Both are highest-value. Both are the same intervention logic applied to different documents.

### Finding 02: Geneva boutique WM market AI governance signal hierarchy not correlated with actual authorization chain documentation quality

**Mechanism:** The market uses observable signals to evaluate boutique WM AI governance quality: presence/absence of written AI policy, ISO certifications, nFADP compliance documentation, carrier attestation engagement, institutional DDQ response completeness. None of these signals are validated against actual authorization chain documentation quality — specifically the sequential documentation of what human reviewed what AI output at what decision point in the portfolio construction process. A boutique with a well-written AI policy and no authorization chain documentation scores higher on current market signals than a boutique with undocumented but operationally robust review processes. The signal hierarchy — which produces competitive differentiation in institutional client selection — is not correlated with the underlying authorization chain quality the signals are supposed to proxy for. This means: (1) boutiques with strong authorization chains but weak documentation appear weaker than boutiques with weak authorization chains and strong policy documentation; (2) the institutional client cannot distinguish these; (3) the competitive moat in actual governance quality is invisible under current signal hierarchies. This is distinct from all prior signal hierarchy findings which addressed general governance quality signals without isolating the specific authorization chain documentation as the WM-specific signal that is both highest-stakes and most poorly proxied by current market signals.

**Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 Finding (OaF survey two-tier visibility) is the mechanism — what the MD thinks is happening (policy) vs. what is actually documented (authorization chain). Current market signals measure policy, not authorization chain documentation.

**Market signal:** Institutional clients selecting between boutique WM firms are comparing AI governance signals that do not validate authorization chain quality — the selection criterion is decoupled from the actual risk.

**IMO differentiation angle:** Authorization chain documentation as the specific evidence category that institutional clients actually need but currently cannot evaluate through existing market signals — and no competitor is producing authorization chain documentation as evidence.

### Finding 03: Geneva boutique WM AI governance vendor market operating on sub-disclosure terms creating structural competitive distortion in portfolio construction

**Mechanism:** Geneva WM firms are purchasing AI portfolio construction tooling from vendors operating on terms the boutiques have not evaluated and cannot renegotiate. Vendors with Swiss-hosted positioning, FINMA-relevant compliance framing, or wealth management-specific marketing are selling to Geneva boutiques on click-through contracts that create: (1) undisclosed model training data use rights — the vendor uses boutique client data to improve shared models; (2) sub-processor arrangements the boutique has not evaluated; (3) liability limitations that do not align with the boutique's carrier coverage architecture. The competitive distortion: a boutique that accepted vendor terms it didn't evaluate is in materially different competitive position than a boutique that evaluated vendor terms before purchase — but the market cannot observe this because the vendor terms are confidential and the competitive consequence (vendor data incident, model failure, sub-processor disclosure) is latent. When a vendor-related incident occurs, the difference becomes visible. Until then, vendors with the most aggressive terms may have the most competitive pricing (because they're monetizing boutique data through model training) and the strongest market positioning. The boutique making decisions based on "Swiss-hosted" labels without evaluating vendor contract terms is structurally disadvantaged against a boutique that evaluated terms — without knowing it. This is distinct from Phase 01 Finding (vendor compliance assumption creates hidden exposure) which named the general terms acceptance problem without isolating the specific portfolio construction vendor context where the model training data use right is the most commercially sensitive undisclosed term.

**Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 named the vendor compliance assumption; Phase 02 specifies the portfolio construction vendor context as the highest-stakes sub-category where model training data use creates both nFADP Art.9 exposure and competitive intelligence exposure simultaneously.

**Market signal:** Portfolio construction AI vendors with the most competitive pricing may be subsidizing that price through data monetization the boutique doesn't know it agreed to.

**IMO differentiation angle:** Portfolio construction vendor contract evaluation as discrete advisory service — the only Geneva service evaluating what the boutique actually agreed to when they clicked "accept" on their AI tool terms.

### Finding 04: Scale calibration gap preventing boutique WM market activation despite high urgency — structural market design failure

**Mechanism:** Enterprise AI governance frameworks are designed for organizations with dedicated compliance staff, board-level AI governance committees, and institutional procurement processes. Boutique WM firms at 50–200 employees have none of these — the managing director IS the compliance function, the board, and the procurement process simultaneously. The market has produced enterprise frameworks and has not produced boutique-scale proportionate alternatives for institutional DDQ management. This is a structural market design failure, not a firm capability gap: the frameworks are designed for organizations that don't exist at the boutique WM scale. The competitive consequence: boutiques are being measured against institutional DDQ standards designed for large asset managers while having no proportionate methodology to achieve them. The urgency is high (DDQ deadline, mandate risk) but the available solutions are enterprise-scale (wrong fit) or generic Swiss compliance (wrong content). The market design failure creates an activation barrier: the boutique wants to act, cannot find a proportionate solution, and delays while the DDQ deadline approaches. This is distinct from all prior scale disadvantage findings which named the gap conceptually without isolating the specific structural market design failure mechanism — where institutional DDQ is creating urgency the market cannot respond to because the available solutions are structurally misaligned with the buyer's organizational constraints.

**Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 Finding ("adequate" undefined and universally paralyzing) is the specific paralysis mechanism — the boutique can't act because "adequate" has no proportionate definition for their specific situation.

**Market signal:** Boutique WM firms in acute institutional DDQ situations are most likely to need immediate advisory support and most likely to find that current market offerings require longer engagement timelines or larger organizational capacity than the urgency allows.

**IMO differentiation angle:** Explicit positioning as the only Geneva-calibrated proportionate methodology for boutique WM firms facing institutional DDQ deadlines — not simplified enterprise framework, but specifically designed for the 65-person investment committee structure.

### Finding 05: Dominant Geneva boutique WM AI governance intelligence flow bypassing formal advisory channels creating invisible competitive displacement

**Mechanism:** The dominant AI governance intelligence flow in the Geneva boutique WM market is not through formal advisory or consulting channels — it is through informal peer networks: Geneva private banking alumni networks, Geneva wealth management professional associations, Geneva finance networking events, and Geneva family office relationships. A boutique MD who attended a Geneva family office event in Q1 2026 is getting AI governance intelligence through that network that a non-attending boutique MD is not getting. The informal circuit is faster and more operationally specific than published guidance. The competitive consequence: boutiques that participate in this informal circuit have AI governance positioning advantage over boutiques that do not — not because of better analysis or superior methodology, but because of network position. This informal network-based competitive intelligence system is structurally unavailable to boutiques without existing Geneva finance network access, creating a network-based competitive moat that has nothing to do with governance quality and everything to do with informal circuit participation. This is distinct from all prior circuit participation findings which addressed general market intelligence flow without isolating the specific mechanism where institutional DDQ competitive intelligence (which boutiques are getting which questions, what institutional clients are prioritizing) is flowing through informal networks faster than formal channels and creating competitive positioning advantages for network participants.

**Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 Finding (OaF survey two-tier visibility) is relevant here — the MD's perception of their AI governance state is shaped by what they hear in the informal circuit, not by systematic internal review.

**Market signal:** The boutiques most visible in Geneva AI governance discourse may not be the ones with the strongest governance frameworks — they may simply be the ones with the best informal network position and earliest institutional DDQ intelligence.

**IMO differentiation angle:** Informal circuit access + institutional DDQ intelligence translation is not replicable through published documents — sustained institutional participation in Geneva networks is the moat, and the positioning is available to any boutique willing to engage substantively.

### Finding 06: Entry-point service concentration for Geneva WM creating market access barrier for highest-urgency boutique firms

**Mechanism:** Current market entry-point services for Geneva boutique WM AI governance are calibrated for the top of the funnel — general AI governance awareness content, introductory checklists, awareness-level briefings about FINMA Guidance. These materials attract boutique WM firms at the awareness stage. The highest-urgency segment — boutiques with an active institutional DDQ deadline, a carrier attestation renewal, or a peer conversation that revealed competitive exposure — needs an entry-point service calibrated for acute urgency, not awareness-stage content. The acute-urgency segment is structurally underserved: current entry point is content designed for the awareness-to-evaluation transition; the acute segment needs evaluation-to-decision facilitation under time pressure. The competitive consequence: boutiques that most need institutional DDQ management guidance are least served by current market entry-point services, which are designed for lower-urgency segments and require longer engagement timelines than institutional DDQ deadlines allow. This is distinct from all prior entry-point findings which addressed general market entry without isolating the specific acute-urgency segment for boutique WM firms facing institutional DDQ deadlines where the mandate is at immediate risk.

**Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 Finding (post-attestation evidence reconstruction primary need) describes the acute-urgency segment — firms that already signed carrier attestation and now need retroactive evidence. Phase 02 extends this to the institutional DDQ context.

**Market signal:** Boutique WM firms with institutional DDQ deadlines in 2-4 weeks are most likely to need immediate advisory support and most likely to find that current market entry offerings require longer timelines than the urgency allows.

**IMO differentiation angle:** Acute-urgency entry-point service calibrated specifically for firms with institutional DDQ deadlines — 2-week intensive engagement structure for firms that need navigation now, not frameworks for firms that are considering whether there's a problem.
