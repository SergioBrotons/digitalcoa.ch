# Phase 02 — Cycle 312 — Compliance Context: Geneva Boutique Wealth Management

## Vertical: Finance / SMB — Boutique Wealth Management Firms
## Cycle: 312 | Date: 2026-05-31
## Status: 6 NET-NEW findings for Phase 02 — distinct from C312 Phase 01 (legal) and C214–C311 Phase 02

## Regulatory Framework Summary

Geneva boutique WM firms at 50–200 employees face a dual-accountability regulatory structure: institutional client AI governance DDQ operating as the immediate forcing function, while carrier attestation renewal creates personal liability exposure simultaneously. FINMA Guidance 08/2024 provides proportionate framework expectations without operational definition. nFADP Art.63 personal criminal liability applies to managing directors. SRO regulatory silence leaves boutiques without their primary regulatory interface for AI governance. The six NET-NEW findings in Phase 02 surface specific structural intersections between these regulatory frameworks in the boutique WM context — where portfolio construction AI creates distinct documentation requirements, institutional DDQ re-review activates model drift obligations retroactively, and cross-mandate coherence becomes a visible regulatory and competitive problem.

## 6 NET-NEW Compliance Findings (Distinct from Phase 01 and Prior Cycles)

### Finding 01: FINMA Guidance 08/2024 proportionality principle without operational calibration creates portfolio construction documentation adequacy illusion

**Mechanism:** FINMA Guidance 08/2024 states that AI governance frameworks must be proportionate to firm size and risk profile — but does not define what proportionality means operationally for a 50–200 person Geneva boutique WM firm making investment decisions with AI-assisted portfolio construction. The proportionality principle is aspirational in the Guidance, not operational. This creates a documentation adequacy illusion: boutiques producing firm-level AI governance policy feel they are satisfying FINMA's proportionate framework requirement because their policy is appropriately scaled to their firm size. But the institutional client DDQ and carrier attestation are asking about portfolio-specific authorization chain documentation — not firm-level policy. A boutique that built proportionate firm-level policy may still have inadequate authorization chain documentation for specific mandates because proportionality was evaluated against firm size rather than against portfolio construction AI-specific risk factors. The proportionality principle without operational calibration creates false comfort: boutiques believe proportionate size = adequate governance for all AI use cases, when the two are not equivalent under FINMA's risk-based supervision framework or institutional client expectations. This is distinct from all prior proportionality findings which named the principle without isolating the specific portfolio construction documentation adequacy illusion mechanism where size-proportionate policy satisfies the proportionality language but not the authorization chain evidence standard.

**Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 Finding ("adequate" undefined and universally paralyzing) is the specific paralysis mechanism — the boutique can't determine what "adequate" means for portfolio construction authorization chain documentation because FINMA hasn't defined it operationally.

**Regulatory intersection:** FINMA Guidance 08/2024 proportionality + institutional client DDQ portfolio-specific evidence requirements + carrier attestation authorization chain questions = boutiques cannot determine whether their proportionate documentation covers their specific portfolio construction AI risk exposure to an evidential standard that would survive institutional client or carrier inquiry.

**ICP language:** "We built a proportionate framework. It's the right size for our firm. But the institutional client is asking about specific mandates. I don't know if our framework covers that."

**IMO compliance angle:** Proportionate AI governance adequacy assessment for portfolio construction AI — evaluating whether a boutique's size-proportionate policy actually covers its specific authorization chain documentation requirements for AI-assisted portfolio decisions.

### Finding 02: nFADP Art.26 automated decision disclosure compounding with institutional client methodology questions creating dual disclosure architecture requirement for portfolio decisions

**Mechanism:** nFADP Art.26 requires that firms disclose when AI-based processing produces automated decisions that significantly affect data subjects (clients) — and clients have the right to request human review of those decisions. Institutional clients sending AI governance DDQs to Geneva boutiques are simultaneously asking how AI was used in portfolio construction and what human review occurred at key decision points. These are parallel disclosure obligations with different legal bases, different audiences, different disclosure scopes, and different response timelines: Art.26 disclosure obligations are toward the client as data subject; institutional client methodology questions are toward the institutional client as counterparty to the mandate relationship. The obligations compound because satisfying an institutional client's methodology questions may require disclosure of the same decision process that Art.26 requires disclosing to the client as data subject. If the institutional client asks about AI-assisted portfolio decisions and the boutique explains the AI decision architecture, the explanation may constitute an Art.26 disclosure the boutique was required to provide independently. Conversely: if the boutique provided Art.26 disclosure to the client but the institutional client methodology question requires different framing or additional detail, the Art.26 disclosure does not discharge the institutional client obligation. No Geneva methodology exists for managing this dual disclosure architecture requirement — where Art.26 obligations toward clients as data subjects and institutional client methodology obligations toward the same clients as mandate counterparties operate in parallel with different disclosure standards for the same underlying portfolio construction AI decisions. This is distinct from all prior Art.26 findings which named the disclosure obligation generally without isolating the specific dual disclosure architecture requirement for portfolio construction AI decisions where the same clients hold both data subject and mandate counterparty status.

**Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 Finding (post-attestation evidence reconstruction primary need) is the general evidence gap; this finding specifies the Art.26 + institutional DDQ dual disclosure as the specific compounding obligation for portfolio construction AI.

**Regulatory intersection:** nFADP Art.26 (client-as-data-subject disclosure) + institutional client AI methodology DDQ (client-as-counterparty disclosure) — two different disclosure obligations to the same underlying portfolio construction AI decision architecture with no Geneva methodology for integration.

**ICP language:** "We disclosed to the client that AI was used in portfolio decisions. That probably covers our Art.26 obligation. But the institutional client asked a detailed question about methodology. I wasn't sure which disclosure standard I was supposed to be applying."

**FR:** "Nous avons informé le client de l'utilisation de l'IA dans les décisions de portefeuille. Cela couvre probablement l'Art.26. Mais le client institutionnel a posé une question détaillée sur la méthodologie. Je ne savais pas quelle norme de divulgation appliquer."

### Finding 03: nFADP Art.63 personal criminal liability framing conflict with carrier attestation indemnification architecture creating undisclosed legal exposure for portfolio decisions

**Mechanism:** nFADP Art.63 establishes personal criminal liability for managing directors who knowingly violate data protection obligations in ways that cause harm or expose individuals to risk. In the portfolio construction context, this raises a specific unresolved question: if AI-assisted portfolio construction decisions cause client financial harm, does Art.63 apply? The boutique MD is managing personal criminal exposure through a carrier professional liability insurance product that is not designed to address criminal defense. When a carrier pays out a professional liability claim, it is resolving a civil/administrative exposure. When an MD faces personal criminal liability under Art.63, carrier indemnification does not discharge the criminal exposure — the criminal standard is different and personal. The structural conflict: the MD is managing personal criminal exposure through an insurance product that is not designed to address it. If an AI governance failure in portfolio construction rises to the level of Art.63 criminal conduct, the MD's carrier insurance provides no criminal defense. The MD who signed carrier attestation documents attesting to AI governance controls for portfolio construction does not have an indemnification pathway for the scenario those documents are most directly relevant to. Carrier attestation documents may actually increase Art.63 exposure by creating documented evidence of MD knowledge and commitment that supports the "knowingly" element of Art.63 if a client financial harm incident occurs. This is distinct from all prior Art.63 findings which identified the personal criminal liability framing without isolating the specific portfolio construction AI context where the question of whether Art.63 applies to AI-assisted investment decisions is genuinely unresolved and the carrier indemnification architecture is most clearly inadequate for criminal defense.

**Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 Finding (carrier form architecture creates documented standard) is relevant — the documented standard from carrier attestation creates evidence that can simultaneously satisfy carrier underwriting requirements and increase Art.63 criminal exposure.

**Regulatory intersection:** nFADP Art.63 criminal liability framework + carrier PI insurance indemnification architecture + institutional client DDQ evidence requirements = three incompatible frameworks addressing the same MD portfolio construction AI decision record with different standards, different evidentiary requirements, and no integrated management methodology.

**ICP language:** "Article 63 — that's for data breaches, right? But what if AI-assisted portfolio construction causes client harm? Is that different?" / "I have professional liability insurance. That covers me if something goes wrong. But does it cover criminal? I'm not sure."

**IMO documentation angle:** Carrier attestation pre-signing review must evaluate criminal exposure separately from civil/administrative exposure for portfolio construction AI — documentation that satisfies carrier underwriting requirements may increase criminal exposure without providing criminal defense.

### Finding 04: SRO AI guidance vacuum creating structural accountability displacement by institutional client DDQ operating as uncodified operative standard

**Mechanism:** SRO silence on AI creates a regulatory vacuum that is being filled by institutional client AI governance DDQs functioning as de facto operative standards for Geneva boutique WM firms. This is structural displacement: the standard-setting function that should belong to the SRO (as the boutique's primary regulatory interface) has been displaced to institutional client questionnaires. The boutique is being governed by institutional client standard-setting rather than regulatory standard-setting — and institutional client standards are designed to manage the client's investment selection and risk management problems, not boutique WM governance problems. When an institutional client sends an AI governance DDQ, its operative standard is shaped by what the client needs to evaluate manager selection and portfolio risk — not what FINMA expects of the boutique, not what nFADP requires, not what proportionate governance means at boutique scale. The displacement is ongoing: each institutional client iteration of its DDQ further codifies what "adequate AI governance" means for the boutique in that client's eyes, without any Geneva-calibrated input into what that standard should be. SRO regulatory vacuum + institutional client DDQ displacement = boutique governed by client-specific underwriting standard for AI governance rather than by regulatory standards specifically designed for WM firms. This is distinct from all prior SRO vacuum findings which named the vacuum without isolating the specific institutional client DDQ displacement as active uncodified operative standard-setting mechanism operating because of SRO silence, creating a regulatory environment shaped by institutional client selection logic rather than WM regulatory doctrine.

**Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 Finding (SRO silence) named the general vacuum; Phase 02 specifies institutional client DDQ as the displacement mechanism filling that vacuum.

**Regulatory intersection:** SRO regulatory silence + institutional client DDQ structural displacement + FINMA Guidance operative as future expectation + nFADP obligations creating active compliance requirement = four-track regulatory environment where the most operationally immediate standard (institutional DDQ) is shaped by client selection logic rather than Geneva-calibrated regulatory doctrine.

**ICP language:** "The institutional client's DDQ is effectively asking us what our AI governance standard is. We're filling it out without knowing what standard we're being measured against." / "Our SRO has said nothing about AI. Our institutional client is asking detailed questions. The client's standard is what matters for our mandate."

**IMO advisory angle:** Institutional DDQ pre-reading as competitive intelligence — understanding what standard the client is applying before responding is the only way to calibrate the boutique's response to the actual operative standard being imposed.

### Finding 05: Model drift monitoring as undocumented regulatory expectation now activated through institutional client re-review practice

**Mechanism:** FINMA Guidance 08/2024 expects documented human oversight of AI-assisted decisions, which implicitly includes monitoring of AI model performance over time. Model drift — the tendency of AI models to produce progressively less accurate or appropriate outputs as underlying market conditions change — is a specific well-documented risk in quantitative finance that is not hypothetical. The institutional clients that received first-generation AI governance DDQ responses from Geneva boutiques in 2024-2025 are now conducting re-reviews specifically asking about model monitoring evidence. This re-review practice has converted a theoretical regulatory expectation into a specific documented evidence obligation retroactively. A boutique that responded to the 2024 DDQ with a firm-level AI governance policy did not include model drift monitoring documentation because it was not a specific question in the 2024 DDQ. The institutional client's 2026 re-review is asking specifically: "What is your model drift monitoring practice? Show us evidence of monitoring cadence and review outcomes." The 12-24 months of undocumented model drift monitoring (per Phase 01 Finding: unauthorized delegation active and undocumented) is now a visible evidence gap. The boutique cannot produce evidence of monitoring it never documented. This is distinct from all prior model drift findings which named the theoretical risk without isolating the specific mechanism where institutional client re-review practice is retroactively activating the documentation obligation — converting a FINMA Guidance implicit expectation into a specific evidence requirement with a 12-24 month retroactive gap now visible to institutional clients.

**Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 Finding (unauthorized delegation active and undocumented) + Phase 01 Finding (post-attestation evidence reconstruction primary need) = the general condition; this finding specifies model drift monitoring as the specific evidence sub-category now being activated.

**Regulatory intersection:** FINMA Guidance 08/2024 human oversight expectation + institutional client re-review practice + carrier third-generation questions on monitoring = three-track activation of model drift documentation as a live evidence obligation.

**ICP language:** "The institutional client asked specifically about model monitoring. We don't have documented evidence of that. We've been operating for 18 months without a monitoring system." / "Model drift — I know it's a real risk. I don't have documentation of what we've been doing. Now the client wants evidence."

**IMO advisory angle:** Model drift monitoring documentation architecture as discrete service — building a proportionate monitoring documentation system that satisfies institutional client re-review requirements and produces evidence that would survive FINMA inquiry.

### Finding 06: Documentation window temporal compression creating evidence standard escalation paradox where institutional DDQ standards are escalating faster than boutique documentation cycles

**Mechanism:** The documentation window for Geneva boutique WM AI governance is not just that documentation must be built before an inquiry arrives — it is that institutional DDQ standards are escalating faster than documentation cycles can respond. A boutique that produced AI governance documentation in Q4 2024 in response to the first-generation institutional DDQ wave is being evaluated against 2025-2026 standards that have materially escalated. The first-generation institutional DDQs asked about firm-level AI governance policy. The current institutional DDQs being sent in 2026 ask about vendor processor chains, sequential authorization documentation, model drift monitoring evidence, and sub-processor disclosures. Documentation produced for the 2024 questions does not satisfy the 2026 questions. The boutique that was an early responder and built documentation quickly is now in a worse position than a boutique that built documentation later — the early responder built to a lower standard and is being evaluated against a higher standard. Documentation window is compressing: the window is not just "build before the questions arrive" but "build against a standard that is escalating faster than your documentation cycle" and "update documentation before the institutional client re-review arrives." This is distinct from all prior documentation window findings which named the window close generally without isolating the specific temporal compression mechanism where institutional client re-review practice specifically targets documentation that was adequate for the first DDQ but is now inadequate for the re-review.

**Phase 01 bridge:** Phase 01 Finding (adequate undefined and universally paralyzing) is the reason the boutique can't calibrate its documentation to the escalating standard — without a definition of adequate, each documentation iteration is built on a different implicit standard that continues to escalate.

**Regulatory intersection:** Institutional DDQ standard escalation velocity > boutique documentation update cycle + carrier questionnaire escalation cadence + FINMA Guidance evolution = present-tense documentation inadequacy even for boutiques that acted early.

**ICP language:** "We moved quickly. We built our AI governance documentation in 2024. Now the institutional client wants model monitoring evidence and vendor processor information. It looks like it's not enough." / "The standard keeps moving. Every time we update, they're asking about something new."

**IMO advisory angle:** Documentation cadence calibration — advising boutiques on documentation update frequency calibrated to institutional client re-review practice and carrier questionnaire escalation cadence, not annual review cycles.

### FINMA Guidance 08/2024 — Key Obligations for Boutiques

1. **AI inventory** — written inventory of all AI tools, purposes, client data processed
2. **Governance policy** — named responsible person, human oversight documentation
3. **Vendor due diligence** — contractual and technical controls for external AI SaaS (91% of Swiss FIs)
4. **Human oversight documentation** — reviewable AI-assisted decisions with evidence of human review
5. **Incident response** — 72-hour FDPIC breach notification for AI-related incidents
6. **DPIA** — for high-risk AI processing (including AI-assisted portfolio construction)

6. **Vendor processor chain** — sub-processor arrangements, data termination protocols
