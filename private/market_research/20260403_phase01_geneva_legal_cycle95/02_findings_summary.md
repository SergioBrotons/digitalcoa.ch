## F1: SECO Enforcement Statistics — 68 Proceedings, 24 Fines Since March 2022
**Source:** Baker McKenzie / Swiss government data
**Signal:** HIGH — compliance urgency, enforcement reality gap

SECO has opened 68 administrative criminal penalty proceedings and issued 24 final fines since the Swiss Ordinance on Russia/Belarus came into force (March 2022). Nine convictions occurred since September 2024 alone — enforcement is accelerating.

Key enforcement data:
- 21 proceedings opened by March 2023; 68 by March 2026 — more than 3x in three years
- Fine types: negligent import of weapon parts from Russia, attempted export of luxury goods, failure to identify prohibited product codes
- No voluntary self-disclosure program exists formally — but voluntary disclosures have led to penalty mitigation in practice
- Switzerland has declined to join the REPO Task Force (oligarch asset seizure coordination) despite international pressure

Geneva legal market signal: Most companies underestimate Swiss-specific enforcement risk. The gap between EU and Swiss sanctions regimes creates false comfort. Geneva companies subject to Swiss-only jurisdiction get more flexibility from SECO (broader deposit exemptions, limited deposit restrictions) — but face the same enforcement gravity.

## F2: Switzerland Integrates Sanctions Compliance into AML Framework — FINMA's Expanded Role
**Source:** Lindemann Law, FINMA
**Signal:** HIGH — regulatory convergence, financial sector compliance burden

Switzerland is now embedding sanctions compliance into Anti-Money Laundering law, expanding oversight beyond SECO to FINMA and Self-Regulatory Organizations (SROs). This is a significant structural shift:

- Financial institutions now required to: conduct comprehensive risk assessments; regularly update client information and beneficial ownership; implement robust internal controls; train staff on sanctions applicability; establish reporting procedures
- FINMA and SECO jointly responsible for inspections — non-compliance = legal proceedings, profit confiscation, license revocation
- FINMA has been updating financial sanctions listings throughout March 2026 (ISIL/Al-Qaida, Taliban)
- Aligns Switzerland with FATF standards and EU AML regulations

Geneva financial sector implication: Banks, wealth managers, and financial intermediaries now face dual-track AML + sanctions compliance obligations. Advisory demand for compliance framework redesign.

## F3: The A7A5 Stablecoin Ban — Switzerland's First Crypto-Asset Designation
**Source:** Baker McKenzie / SECO Ukraine Ordinance Art. 20a
**Signal:** MEDIUM-HIGH — crypto sanctions innovation, Geneva fintech exposure

Switzerland banned transactions involving the A7A5 — a ruble-backed cryptocurrency stablecoin — via newly added Annex 13a of the Ukraine Ordinance (Art. 20a). This is Switzerland's first specific crypto-asset designation:

- Direct or indirect involvement in A7A5 transactions is prohibited
- Extends to crypto-wallets, crypto-accounts, and crypto-custody services
- Russian nationals and Russia-resident individuals prohibited from acquiring ownership in or managing Swiss/EEA-based crypto service providers (Art. 20 para. 2bis)

Geneva angle: Geneva-based crypto exchanges, fintech custody providers, and commodity traders using crypto settlement face new compliance obligations. Advisory need: crypto-asset due diligence, wallet screening, stablecoin transaction monitoring.

## F4: SECO's Restrictive Healthcare Exemption Interpretation — Supply Chain Traceability Required
**Source:** Chambers Practice Guide / Baker McKenzie
**Signal:** MEDIUM-HIGH — healthcare sector advisory gap

SECO applies a restrictive interpretation of the medical/pharmaceutical exemption (Article 6(1)(b) Ukraine Ordinance) and the licensing ground for medical goods (Article 11a(4)(a)). Key concern: diversion risk.

SECO requires operators to demonstrate:
- Robust supply chain traceability systems
- Identification of end-users
- Oversight of entire supply chain

This is more demanding than most companies' existing compliance frameworks anticipate. Geneva healthcare companies, pharmaceutical traders, and medical device exporters are disproportionately affected. The advisory gap: most Geneva companies do not have supply chain traceability systems sufficient to satisfy SECO's standard.

## F5: Switzerland's Selective Departures from EU Sanctions — The Hidden Compliance Risk
**Source:** ICLG Switzerland, Chambers Practice Guide
**Signal:** MEDIUM — Swiss-only jurisdiction companies at risk

Switzerland does NOT automatically implement every EU sanctions package and has deviated in meaningful ways:

1. Switzerland explicitly chose NOT to implement EU's Common High Priority Items due diligence procedures (14th package)
2. SECO has adopted broader exemptions for deposits, trusts, and transferable securities — giving Swiss-only companies more flexibility
3. Switzerland has not implemented EU's "thematic" sanctions (chemical weapons, cybersecurity, human rights) — creating potential gaps
4. Switzerland is not legally bound to EU sanctions — timing and scope can differ

For Geneva companies that are Swiss-only (not EU-subject): the applicable regime differs from what their EU-compliant advisors assume. This creates a specific advisory opportunity for Geneva law firms that understand the Swiss-specific deviations.

## F6: Circumvention as the Primary Enforcement Focus — EU 15th-17th Package Priority
**Source:** Chambers Practice Guide / Baker McKenzie
**Signal:** MEDIUM — compliance infrastructure, advisory demand

EU 15th, 16th, and 17th packages all made circumvention the main enforcement priority. Switzerland's Ukraine Ordinance follows this lead. Key circumvention vectors targeted:

- Third-country subsidiaries of Russian companies
- Re-routing through neutral third countries
- Goods of economic significance to Russia (Annex 20)
- Services ban: software, IT, consulting, legal, accounting, PR services

For Geneva companies with global supply chains or cross-border service operations: the circumvention risk extends far beyond direct Russia/Belarus transactions. Companies need comprehensive compliance programs covering:
- Counterparty screening (including payment providers)
- Supply chain due diligence
- Services restriction monitoring
- Third-country subsidiary exposure assessment
