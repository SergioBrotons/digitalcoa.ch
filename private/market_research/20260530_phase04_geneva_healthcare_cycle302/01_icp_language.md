## ICP Language — How They Talk About AI Problems

> **EN Summary:** Geneva independent physicians (2–10, outpatient) talk about AI risk in the language of **personal professional accountability**. They do not say "AI governance gap." They say "my license is on the line." FMH human oversight is referenced but undefined. The carrier attestation is the acute forcing function — not abstract compliance, but a concrete document with a deadline that could affect their ability to practice.

> **FR Summary:** Les médecins indépendants genevois (2–10, ambulatoire) parlent du risque IA dans le langage de **la responsabilité professionnelle personnelle**. Ils ne disent pas « lacune de gouvernance IA ». Ils disent « ma licence est en jeu ». La supervision humaine FMH est mentionnée mais non définie. L'attestation de l'assureur est le facteur forcing aigu — pas une conformité abstraite, mais un document concret avec une échéance qui pourrait affecter leur capacité à exercer.

## Language Cluster 1: Personal License Accountability

### How They Say It
- "My FMH certification is irreplaceable — I can't afford a disciplinary action"
- "If something goes wrong with an AI-assisted decision, I'm the one who answers for it"
- "The FMH says human oversight is required, but no one tells me what that actually means in practice"
- "I'm personally responsible for every decision made in my practice — AI doesn't change that"
- "My medical license is not something I can afford to risk for an AI tool"

### What This Reveals
The physician license is the **irreplaceable asset** — more acute than the attorney (can retake bar) or WM manager (can change careers). This is the highest personal stake of any vertical. The language is personal and absolute, not organizational.

### Why This Matters for Positioning
Do not frame AI governance as "protecting your organization." Frame it as "protecting your FMH certification and personal professional standing." The physician hears: "you are personally accountable" — reflect that back in their language.

## Language Cluster 2: FMH Human Oversight — Stated but Undefined

### How They Say It
- "The FMH requires human oversight of AI, but what does that actually mean operationally?"
- "I keep hearing 'human in the loop' but no one has explained what that looks like in a small practice"
- "How do I document that I actually reviewed an AI system's output?"
- "Is checking an AI-generated note enough, or do I need to re-examine the patient?"
- "The FMH guidance on AI is vague — where's the practical standard for a3-person practice?"
- "I've asked my FMH section about this and they directed me to Swissmedic, who directed me back"

### What This Reveals
**FMH human oversight is stated without operational definition.** This is a structural gap — the professional body acknowledges the problem but provides no implementable standard. The physician is left personally accountable for a requirement that has not been defined. This creates anxiety without resolution.

### Why This Matters for Positioning
The FMH human oversight gap is the **core anxiety point**. Address it directly: "Human oversight sounds vague because it is vague. Here's what it actually means in a small practice context, and how to document it." Credibility comes from making the undefined defined.

## Language Cluster 3: Carrier Attestation — The Concrete Forcing Function

### How They Say It
- "My malpractice carrier sent me an AI attestation form — I don't know what to put on it"
- "The carrier is asking for evidence of my AI governance process — I don't have one"
- "If I don't attest correctly, does that affect my coverage?"
- "The carrier form asks about AI tools I've never even heard of"
- "I signed the attestation but I'm not sure what I actually attested to"
- "My broker couldn't explain the AI questions on the form"

### What This Reveals
The **carrier attestation is the acute forcing function** for independent practices — parallel to how institutional client questionnaires convert finance ICPs. The carrier has shifted from passive insurer to active compliance monitor. The physician has a concrete document with a deadline, and a broker who cannot help. This is the moment of acute concern.

### Why This Matters for Positioning
The carrier form is the **entry point of highest urgency**. The physician does not know what the form means, fears coverage implications, and has no one to ask. Offer: "I'll review your carrier attestation form and tell you exactly what it requires and how to respond." Low-friction, high-urgency, immediately useful.

## Language Cluster 4: Patient Confidentiality with AI Tools

### How They Say It
- "I'm using Doctolib and Dragon Medical — are those even compliant?"
- "I sent a patient report through an AI tool — should I be worried about that?"
- "The AI vendor says they're GDPR-compliant — does that mean they're nFADP-compliant too?"
- "I don't actually know where patient data goes when I use these tools"
- "My assistant uses ChatGPT to draft responses — is that a data protection issue?"
- "I've heard about data breaches in healthcare — I don't want to be the next headline"

### What This Reveals
The physician uses AI tools **without knowing where patient data goes**. The "my IT vendor handles this" assumption is prevalent. But the personal liability for a data breach falls on the physician, not the vendor. This is a hidden personal risk — present and operative, but not yet acute because no incident has occurred.

### Why This Matters for Positioning
The hidden risk language ("I don't want to be the next headline") is fear-based, not yet action-based. Positioning: "Most small practices are using AI tools without a data processing agreement. Here's how to know if yours is compliant — and why it matters for your personal liability." The gap between use and governance is where the risk lives.

## Language Cluster 5: Small Practice Reality — No Resources, No Time

### How They Say It
- "I don't have an IT department — I'm the practice, the physician, and the compliance officer"
- "I know I should have a governance policy, but I don't have time to write one"
- "Big consultancy prices are for hospitals, not for a3-person practice"
- "I've looked at AI governance frameworks — they're all designed for organizations with 500 people"
- "I need something practical that works for my actual situation, not a100-page policy template"
- "Every consultant I call quotes me CHF 20,000+ for an initial engagement"

### What This Reveals
The **resource-constraint reality** is core to how healthcare ICPs see themselves. They are not ignoring compliance — they are aware of it but feel the available solutions are designed for organizations they are not. The market gap is not awareness; it is affordable, practical, small-practice-appropriate advisory.

### Why This Matters for Positioning
Price anchoring matters. Position the discovery call as "20 minutes, no cost" and the readiness check as "CHF 500–1,000 scoping engagement" — not "starting at CHF 3,000." The ICP must see a pathway that is accessible to a 3-person practice, not a Fortune500 engagement.

## Language Cluster 6: Peer Network — Trust Through Trusted Peers

### How They Say It
- "My colleague Dr. [X] uses [AI tool] — do you think that's safe?"
- "The AMGe meeting last month had a session on AI — the speakers didn't answer the practical questions"
- "I asked at the FMH section meeting — nobody had clear answers"
- "A colleague had a data breach scare — that got my attention faster than any article"
- "I only trust what a physician colleague tells me, not what a vendor says"

### What This Reveals
**Peer validation is the primary trust mechanism.** The physician does not trust vendor marketing, consultant brochures, or regulatory guidance as much as they trust a peer who has been through the problem. AMGe and FMH section meetings are the informal information network where real questions get asked and answered (or not).

### Why This Matters for Positioning
Build presence in **AMGe and FMH section contexts** — not as a vendor, but as a credible peer-adjacent resource. The physician wants to be able to say "I spoke with someone who actually understands this" before they will engage. Content that appears in AMGe-adjacent contexts (even indirectly) carries peer-trust transfer.
