## Headline Findings

### 1. Medical Assistant AI Delegation Accountability Now Named and Active

**C311 NET-NEW finding:** Medical assistants in small practices are using AI tools on patient data — scheduling, note generation, patient communication — without documented physician authorization. The delegation gap is no longer theoretical: it is operational, ongoing, and appearing on carrier forms that ask specifically about staff AI tool use. The physician is personally accountable for what staff members do with patient data, and has no documentation of what they authorized.

**Evidence:** Carrier forms beginning to ask specifically about staff AI tool use on patient data. Physicians reporting medical assistant AI tool adoption without prior authorization. No Geneva competitor offering a proportionate delegation framework for small practice.

**Implication:** A documented medical assistant delegation framework — specific, proportionate, physician-personal — is required for physician license protection. The framework documents what the physician authorized, by whom, when, and with what oversight.

### 2. OaF Survey Baseline Activating Documentation Specification

**C311 NET-NEW finding:** The Outcomes and Feedback (OaF) survey sent by carriers has evolved into a de facto documentation specification for AI attestation. The survey now includes specific questions about AI tools used, deployment dates, clinical reasoning documentation, and percentage of diagnoses involving AI assistance. These questions require documentation that standard medical records do not provide. OaF responses create contemporaneous records carriers can reference against future attestation forms.

**Evidence:** Physicians reporting OaF surveys with AI-specific questions requiring documentation not found in medical records. OaF responses creating records of inconsistency between survey answers and actual documentation.

**Implication:** OaF survey documentation calibration — reviewing questions and identifying documentation gaps before responding — is a specific, high-value service. No competitor offers this service.

### 3. Clinical Reasoning Chain as Distinct Evidentiary Standard

**C311 NET-NEW finding:** Swissmedic and carriers are asking for the diagnostic reasoning chain — not just the diagnostic conclusion. Physicians can show their conclusion (the diagnosis). They cannot show what role the AI played in their reasoning, what weight they gave to the AI's input, or how their clinical judgment interacted with the AI's recommendation. The reasoning chain is what Swissmedic asks for in an inquiry. Standard EMR systems do not provide this format.

**Evidence:** Physicians articulating: "Swissmedic won't ask if you used AI. They'll ask: what was the AI's role in your diagnostic reasoning? Can you show the chain?" Standard EMR fields for AI input weight or physician judgment overlay do not exist.

**Implication:** A real-time clinical reasoning documentation format — usable during the patient encounter, non-disruptive to clinical flow, specific enough to show the reasoning chain — is the operational core of healthcare evidence architecture.

### 4. Swissmedic Operator Classification — Vendor Certification ≠ Physician Classification

**C311 NET-NEW finding:** The vendor's Swissmedic certification addresses the tool's compliance with Swissmedic device regulations. The physician's operator classification depends on how the physician uses the tool, what decisions they make based on AI output, and whether they meet the Swissmedic definition of "operator" for that tool category. These are separate questions. The vendor's certification does not answer the physician's classification question. The physician is responsible for answering it themselves.

**Evidence:** Physicians assuming vendor certification covers their operator classification. Physicians discovering the gap only when asked by carriers or peers. No published guidance for small practice on physician operator classification.

**Implication:** Swissmedic operator classification assessment — separate from vendor certification — is a required component of healthcare evidence architecture. The physician needs a documented classification opinion, based on Swissmedic definitions, specific to their use case.

### 5. AI-Involved Swissdamed Incident Format Gap — Regulatory, Not Competitive

**C311 NET-NEW finding:** Swissdamed requires incident reporting for medical device-related adverse events. AI-assisted diagnostic decisions that lead to patient incidents may trigger Swissdamed reporting obligations — but there is no published documentation format specific to AI-involved incidents. Physicians don't know whether to report as a device incident, a diagnostic error, or an AI system failure. This is a regulatory gap, not a competitive gap. No one has published this format.

**Evidence:** Swissdamed has mandatory reporting for medical device incidents. No published format for AI-specific incidents. Physicians operating without incident protocol.

**Implication:** An AI-involved Swissdamed incident documentation format — prepared before an incident, ready to use — is proactive compliance. The format should cover device identification, incident description, causal chain, physician reasoning at time of incident, and reporting pathway.

### 6. Carrier Form Physician-Personal Certification Without Documentation

**C311 NET-NEW finding:** Carrier attestation forms now require physician-personal certification — the physician's name, license number (FMH registration), and personal attestation that they maintained human oversight over AI tools used in their practice. The physician signs the form. The form creates nFADP Article 63 personal liability. The physician signed without documentation to support what they certified. Pre-signing documentation review is the highest-value intervention point — zero competitors have built it.

**Evidence:** Carrier forms requiring physician name and license number personally. Physicians reporting signing without documentation calibration. Broker helps complete form; no one reviews underlying evidence before signing.

**Implication:** Carrier form pre-signing review — identifying documentation gaps before the physician signs — is the highest-value, lowest-competition entry point in healthcare evidence architecture.

**The six pressures physicians manage:**
1. Medical assistant AI delegation — no documented authorization framework
2. OaF survey calibration — questions require documentation standard records don't provide
3. Clinical reasoning chain — Swissmedic asks for it; EMR shows conclusions only
4. Swissmedic operator classification — vendor certification doesn't answer physician's question
5. AI incident format — Swissdamed requires reporting; no format for AI-involved incidents
6. Physician-personal certification — signed without supporting documentation

**The evidence language physicians respond to:**
- "Documentation supporting what you personally certified on your carrier form"
- "A delegation framework for medical assistant AI tool use — documented, specific, ready for Swissmedic or carrier"
- "A format that captures your clinical reasoning chain when AI was involved in a diagnosis"
- "A Swissmedic operator classification opinion — separate from your vendor's certification"
- "An AI-involved incident documentation format for Swissdamed reporting"
- "Calibration of your documentation to OaF survey questions before you respond"

- "I need an AI governance framework" — consultant language
- "I want to understand the regulatory landscape" — abstract, not personal
- "Can you help me with my AI strategy?" — not the immediate concern

**What physicians ARE saying (C311 confirmed):**
- "My carrier form now requires my personal certification. I signed it. I don't have the documentation to support what I certified."
- "My medical assistant uses AI on patient data. I didn't authorize specific tools. I have no delegation framework."
- "My carrier sent an OaF survey with very specific AI questions. I don't have documentation calibrated to those questions."
- "Swissmedic asks for my diagnostic reasoning chain. My medical record shows conclusions, not reasoning chains when AI was involved."
- "My AI tool vendor is Swissmedic-certified. But that doesn't tell me whether I'm classified as an operator."
- "If an AI-assisted diagnosis causes an incident, Swissdamed requires reporting. But there's no format for AI-involved incidents."

## C311 Phase 04 Core Insight

The C311 healthcare ICP is managing six named accountability vectors simultaneously — none of which have published resolution frameworks:

1. Medical assistant AI delegation without authorization framework
2. OaF survey questions requiring documentation standard records don't provide
3. Clinical reasoning chain Swissmedic asks for — but no format exists to capture it
4. Swissmedic operator classification — vendor certification doesn't answer physician's question
5. AI-involved Swissdamed incident reporting — mandatory obligation, no published format
6. Carrier form physician-personal certification — signed without supporting documentation

The market has moved from "am I exposed?" (C308) through "my license is at risk personally" (C309) and "I know I'm exposed, I have no documentation from when I started" (C310) to "I have multiple named accountability vectors active simultaneously, none of which have a published resolution — and I need to manage them together."

The evidence architecture for healthcare is the solution: pre-signing carrier form review + medical assistant delegation framework + clinical reasoning documentation format + Swissmedic operator classification assessment + AI-involved incident format + OaF survey calibration.

Positioning anchor confirmed: "For physicians, evidence is personal. And your license is on the line."
