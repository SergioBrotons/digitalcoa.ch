## Phase 04 C311 Market Signals

### Overview

The Geneva healthcare ICP (2–10 physicians) is now managing multiple named accountability vectors simultaneously — none of which have published resolution frameworks. The market signal in C311 is the convergence of: physician-personal carrier certification, OaF survey baseline activation, medical assistant AI delegation visibility, clinical reasoning chain as evidentiary standard, Swissmedic operator classification gap, and AI incident documentation format absence. Physicians are not in discovery mode. They have named the problems. They are seeking a protocol for managing them together.

---

## Current Market Problems and Complaints (C311)

### Problem 1: Medical Assistant AI Delegation Without Authorization Framework

**What's happening:** Medical assistants in small practices are using AI tools on patient data — scheduling AI, note generation AI, patient communication AI. The physician did not authorize specific tools. There is no documented delegation framework. The physician is personally accountable for AI use on patient data that they did not specifically authorize.

**Physician complaint:** "I didn't know she was using AI note generation on patient records. She didn't ask. I didn't approve it. But if Swissmedic asks, I'm the physician responsible for what happens with patient data in my practice."

**Carrier signal:** Carrier attestation forms are beginning to ask specifically about staff AI tool use on patient data — not just physician AI use. The delegation chain is becoming a specific documentation requirement.

**Market urgency:** High. Medical assistants are adopting AI tools faster than physicians are designing authorization frameworks. The gap is operational, ongoing, and personally exposing.

### Problem 2: OaF Survey Baseline Creating Documentation Calibration Gap

**What's happening:** Carriers are sending Outcomes and Feedback (OaF) surveys that now include specific AI-related questions — asking about AI tools used, when deployment began, and how clinical reasoning was documented when AI was involved in patient care. These questions require documentation that standard medical records do not provide.

**Physician complaint:** "My carrier sent me an outcomes survey with very specific questions about AI tools. When did I start using them? How did I document clinical reasoning? What percentage of diagnoses involved AI-assisted decision support? I don't have records calibrated to those specific questions."

**Market signal:** OaF survey questions are becoming the de facto documentation specification for carrier attestation — distinct from the attestation form itself. Physicians who respond to OaF surveys without calibrated documentation are creating a record of inconsistency between their survey responses and their actual documentation.

**Market urgency:** Acute. OaF survey responses are contemporaneous records that carriers can reference against future attestation forms.

### Problem 3: Clinical Reasoning Chain Documentation Gap

**What's happening:** Swissmedic and carriers are asking for the diagnostic reasoning chain — not just the diagnostic conclusion. Physicians can show their conclusion (the diagnosis). They cannot show what role the AI played in their reasoning, what weight they gave to the AI's input, or how their clinical judgment interacted with the AI's recommendation.

**Physician complaint:** "The AI suggested a diagnosis. I agreed with it. I documented my agreement. But Swissmedic doesn't ask 'did you agree with the AI?' They ask: can you show me your clinical reasoning chain when AI was involved? What was the AI's role? What was your judgment? My record shows a conclusion. It doesn't show a reasoning chain."

**Market signal:** Clinical reasoning chain documentation is the specific evidentiary gap Swissmedic and carriers are now targeting. Tool use documentation ("I used AI") is no longer sufficient. Reasoning chain documentation ("here's what the AI contributed, here's what I contributed, here's how I weighted them") is now the standard.

**Market urgency:** High. Reasoning chain documentation requires a format that can be used in real time during a patient encounter — non-disruptive, fast, and specific enough to show the interaction between physician judgment and AI recommendation.

### Problem 4: Swissmedic Operator Classification Gap

**What's happening:** AI diagnostic tool vendors hold Swissmedic certifications for their products. Physicians who use these tools assume this certification answers the Swissmedic operator question. It does not. The vendor's certification addresses the tool's compliance with Swissmedic device regulations. The physician's operator classification depends on how the physician uses the tool, what decisions the physician makes based on AI output, and whether the physician meets the Swissmedic definition of "operator" for that tool category.

**Physician complaint:** "My AI diagnostic tool is Swissmedic-certified. But that doesn't tell me whether I'm classified as an operator. My vendor says they're certified. My vendor doesn't tell me whether I'm an operator. That's my question to answer — and I don't know the answer."

**Market signal:** Swissmedic has not published clear guidance on physician operator classification for clinical AI use. The question is now surfacing in peer conversations and at medical society events. Physicians are realizing the vendor's certification doesn't cover their exposure.

**Market urgency:** Medium-High. The July 1, 2026 swissdamed registration deadline is approaching. Operator classification determines which registration obligations apply. The question is becoming urgent even without published clarity.

### Problem 5: AI Incident Documentation Format Gap

**What's happening:** Swissdamed requires incident reporting for medical device-related adverse events. AI-assisted diagnostic decisions that lead to patient incidents may trigger Swissdamed reporting obligations — but there is no published documentation format for AI-involved incidents. Physicians don't know whether to report as a device incident, a diagnostic error, or an AI system failure.

**Physician complaint:** "If an AI-assisted diagnosis leads to a patient incident, Swissdamed requires reporting. But there's no specific format for AI-involved incidents. Do I document it as a medical device incident? An AI system failure? A diagnostic error? The format doesn't exist — but the reporting obligation does."

**Market signal:** This gap is becoming visible as more practices deploy AI diagnostic tools. The first AI-involved incident in a small Geneva practice will force the documentation format question. Physicians who don't have a protocol are exposed to reporting delays and inadequate documentation.

**Market urgency:** Low-Medium currently, High if an incident occurs. The gap is latent — it becomes acute only after an incident. Proactive physicians are seeking the protocol before an incident.

### Problem 6: Carrier Attestation Physician-Personal Certification Gap

**What's happening:** Carrier attestation forms for medical practices now require physician-personal certification — the physician's name, license number, and personal attestation that they maintained human oversight over AI tools used in their practice. The physician signs the form. The form creates nFADP Article 63 personal liability. The physician signs without documentation to support what they certified.

**Physician complaint:** "The carrier form asks me to certify personally — not the practice, not the group. Me. My name. My license number. My attestation that I maintained human oversight. I signed it. I don't have the documentation to support what I certified."

**Market signal:** Carrier forms have shifted from practice-level attestation to physician-personal certification. The signature creates documented personal liability under nFADP Article 63. The physician certified oversight of AI tools they may not have systematically documented.

**Market urgency:** High and immediate. Physicians are signing these forms now. The certification without documentation is live exposure.

## Failures and Friction Points (C311)

### Failure Mode 1: The Pre-Signing Gap

**What fails:** The physician signs the carrier attestation form — with their name and license number personally — without having documentation calibrated to what they're certifying. The form is signed. The exposure is documented. The evidence doesn't exist.

**Sequence:** Carrier sends form → Physician reviews → Physician can't answer specific questions → Physician signs anyway → Documentation gap is now attested-to rather than remediated.

**Why it persists:** There's no pre-signing review service. The physician doesn't know they need to request one. The form arrives, gets signed, gets returned.

### Failure Mode 2: The Medical Assistant AI Adoption Gap

**What fails:** Medical assistants adopt AI tools before the physician has designed an authorization framework. The adoption is invisible to the physician until a carrier asks about staff AI use, or until a peer conversation surfaces it.

**Sequence:** Medical assistant discovers AI note tool → Starts using on patient data → Physician doesn't know → Carrier asks about staff AI use → Physician discovers gap → No documentation of what was authorized, when, by whom.

**Why it persists:** AI tool adoption by staff is faster than physician oversight capacity. No proportional authorization framework exists for small practices. The gap is structural, not behavioral.

### Failure Mode 3: The Retroactive Documentation Gap

**What fails:** Physicians who deployed AI tools 12–24 months ago have no documentation from that period. The carrier form now asks specific questions about that period. The physician cannot answer accurately because the documentation was never created.

**Sequence:** Physician deploys AI tool in 2023 → No documentation created at deployment → 2026 carrier form asks about 2023 deployment → Physician has no records → Attestation becomes an educated guess.

**Why it persists:** Documentation wasn't created because the obligation wasn't visible. The obligation is now visible. The historical documentation doesn't exist. Retroactive reconstruction is required.

### Failure Mode 4: The Swissmedic Operator Classification Gap

**What fails:** Physician assumes vendor's Swissmedic certification answers their operator classification question. It doesn't. The physician operates as a potential Swissmedic operator without knowing whether they meet the classification criteria.

**Sequence:** Vendor certified by Swissmedic → Physician uses tool → Physician assumes vendor certification covers them → Swissmedic or carrier asks about operator status → Physician realizes vendor certification ≠ physician classification.

**Why it persists:** No published guidance distinguishes vendor certification from physician operator classification. The physician has to find and interpret the relevant Swissmedic definitions themselves.

### Failure Mode 5: The Clinical Reasoning Format Gap

**What fails:** Physician attempts to document AI's role in clinical reasoning but has no format for doing so. They document "AI-assisted diagnosis" in the medical record. Swissmedic asks for the reasoning chain. The physician can't produce it.

**Sequence:** AI suggests diagnosis → Physician agrees → Physician documents agreement → Swissmedic asks for reasoning chain → Physician has no format for documenting the interaction between AI recommendation and clinical judgment → Documentation gap is exposed.

**Why it persists:** Standard EMR systems don't have fields for "AI input weight" or "physician judgment overlay." No published format exists for clinical reasoning chain documentation in AI-assisted decisions.
