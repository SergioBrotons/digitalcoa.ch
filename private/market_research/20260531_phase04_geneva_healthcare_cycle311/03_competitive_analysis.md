## Phase 04 C311 Competitive Analysis

### Overview

The Geneva healthcare market for AI accountability services has a specific structure: existing competitors address adjacent problems with the wrong model for small practice. The market is not "AI governance for healthcare" — it is "evidence architecture for physician license protection." No competitor has built this specifically. The physician-personal certification, medical assistant delegation, clinical reasoning chain, Swissmedic operator classification, and AI incident format gaps are all unoccupied.

---

## Current Competitive Landscape (C311)

### Competitor Category 1: Medical AI Tool Vendors

**What they offer:** AI diagnostic tools, clinical decision support systems, medical imaging AI — certified under Swissmedic as medical devices. Their certification is product-level, not physician-practice-level.

**The gap:** Vendor certification does not address physician operator classification, delegation framework design, clinical reasoning documentation, OaF survey calibration, or carrier form pre-signing review. Vendors are not positioned to review what physicians certified on carrier forms. Vendor certification is not physician protection.

**Physician experience:** "My AI diagnostic tool vendor is Swissmedic-certified. They told me their certification covers the regulatory question. It doesn't. I still don't know whether I'm classified as an operator."

**Competitive limitation:** Vendors benefit from physicians using their tools without restriction. Any service that helps physicians document AI use in a way that constrains tool use is against vendor commercial interest.

### Competitor Category 2: Healthcare Compliance Consultants

**What they offer:** General healthcare compliance consulting — data protection (nFADP/FADP), medical device regulatory compliance, quality management for medical practices.

**The gap:** General healthcare compliance consultants do not address AI accountability architecture specifically. They address nFADP data protection compliance, not the specific documentation requirements for AI-assisted clinical reasoning. The FMH human oversight requirement is not a data protection question.

**Physician experience:** "My healthcare compliance consultant reviewed my nFADP data protection documentation. They didn't address the clinical reasoning chain documentation gap or the FMH human oversight protocol."

**Competitive limitation:** Generalist healthcare compliance is not AI-specific. The physician-personal certification on carrier forms is not a general compliance question — it requires specific evidence architecture knowledge.

### Competitor Category 3: Medical Society Programs (FMH, AMGe)

**What they offer:** Guidelines, principles, continuing medical education (CME), peer networking. FMH has stated the human oversight principle without providing operational protocol.

**The gap:** FMH has stated that human oversight of AI is required. FMH has not provided a protocol for what human oversight means in a small practice with 2–10 physicians. The principle is published; the procedure is not.

**Physician experience:** "I asked FMH directly what human oversight means in my practice. They pointed me to the principle. They didn't give me a protocol. I had to design my own."

**Competitive limitation:** Medical societies are professional bodies, not service providers. They cannot review individual physician carrier forms or provide personalized evidence architecture. Their guidance is principles-based, not operationally specific.

### Competitor Category 4: Swissmedic Regulatory Advisors

**What they offer:** Regulatory strategy for medical device companies, Swissmedic registration support, CE/FDA equivalent compliance.

**The gap:** Swissmedic advisors serve the medical device industry — not the physician practice using the device. The physician operator classification question is adjacent to their practice but not their service.

**Physician experience:** "I contacted a Swissmedic regulatory advisor about my AI diagnostic tool. They confirmed the vendor's certification and told me I was fine. They didn't address whether I was classified as an operator."

**Competitive limitation:** Swissmedic advisors focus on device manufacturers, not device users. The physician-as-operator question is not their primary service area.

### Competitor Category 5: Swissdamed Incident Reporting Services

**What they offer:** Medical device incident reporting under the Swissdamed system, compliance support for device manufacturers and healthcare institutions.

**The gap:** No specific Swissdamed documentation format for AI-involved incidents exists — it's a market gap, not a competitor gap. No one has published this format.

**Competitive limitation:** The absence of a format is a regulatory gap, not a competitive opportunity that anyone has seized. This is white space.

### Competitor Category 6: General AI Governance Consultants

**What they offer:** AI governance frameworks, policy documents, compliance checklists — primarily for larger organizations, English-language dominant.

**The gap:** General AI governance frameworks are not calibrated to physician-personal certification, FMH human oversight requirements, Swissmedic operator classification, or the specific documentation needs of small Geneva practices. The frameworks are language-saturated and not physician-personal.

**Physician experience:** "I looked at AI governance frameworks from major consulting firms. They're written for large organizations. They don't address what a 3-physician Geneva practice needs to document."

**Competitive limitation:** General frameworks are not proportionate to small practice needs. The physician-personal certification and clinical reasoning chain gaps are not addressed by general frameworks.

### Competitor Category 7: Medical Brokers and Insurance Advisors

**What they offer:** Medical practice insurance placement, carrier form completion guidance, professional liability coverage.

**The gap:** Brokers help physicians complete carrier forms but do not review the documentation underlying what physicians certify. The pre-signing review gap is not addressed by brokers.

**Physician experience:** "My broker helped me complete the carrier form. They didn't tell me I needed documentation to support what I was certifying. They just helped me fill in the form."

**Competitive limitation:** Brokers benefit from form completion and submission. Documentation review before signing is not in their commercial model. The pre-signing review is unoccupied.

## White Spaces in Healthcare (C311)

### White Space 1: Pre-Signing Carrier Form Review

**What's missing:** No one reviews carrier forms with physicians before signing. The physician signs documentation that creates nFADP Article 63 personal liability without supporting evidence. The pre-signing review is the highest-value intervention point — zero competitors occupy it.

**Why it's white space:** Brokers complete forms. Vendors certify products. No one addresses the documentation gap before the form is signed. The physician doesn't know they need this review.

**Market opportunity:** A physician-personal carrier form review service — identifying what documentation the physician needs to support what they're certifying — is the highest-value, lowest-competition entry point.

### White Space 2: Medical Assistant Delegation Framework

**What's missing:** No one provides a documented delegation framework for medical assistant AI tool use on patient data in small practices. The delegation chain is invisible and unaddressed.

**Why it's white space:** AI governance frameworks address the physician's own AI use. None address staff AI delegation in a way that's proportionate to a 2–10 physician practice.

**Market opportunity:** A delegation framework specific to medical assistants using AI on patient data — documented, proportionate, physician-personal — fills a specific gap.

### White Space 3: Clinical Reasoning Chain Documentation Format

**What's missing:** A documentation format for capturing clinical reasoning when AI was involved in a diagnostic decision. Must be usable in real time during a patient encounter, non-disruptive to clinical flow, and specific enough to show the reasoning chain.

**Why it's white space:** Standard EMR systems don't have fields for this. No published format exists for physician judgment documentation in AI-assisted decisions.

**Market opportunity:** A real-time clinical reasoning documentation format — physician-designed, FMH-aligned, Swissmedic-recognizable — is the operational core of healthcare evidence architecture.

### White Space 4: Swissmedic Operator Classification Assessment

**What's missing:** A physician-specific Swissmedic operator classification assessment — separate from vendor certification — that gives the physician a documented answer to whether they meet the operator definition for the AI tools they use.

**Why it's white space:** Swissmedic has not published clear guidance distinguishing vendor certification from physician operator classification. Physicians are left to interpret this themselves.

**Market opportunity:** A structured assessment that produces a documented classification opinion — based on Swissmedic definitions, specific to the physician's use case — fills a named gap.

### White Space 5: AI-Involved Swissdamed Incident Documentation Format

**What's missing:** A Swissdamed-compliant documentation format for AI-involved incidents — covering device classification, incident description, causal chain, and physician reasoning at time of incident.

**Why it's white space:** Swissdamed has mandatory reporting for medical device incidents. AI-involved incidents fall into an unclear category with no published format. This is regulatory white space.

**Market opportunity:** A documented Swissdamed incident documentation format for AI-involved incidents — prepared before an incident, ready to use — is proactive protection that no competitor offers.

### White Space 6: OaF Survey Documentation Calibration

**What's missing:** A service that calibrates physician documentation to specific OaF survey questions before the physician responds. OaF responses create contemporaneous records that carriers reference against future attestation.

**Why it's white space:** No service reviews OaF survey questions and identifies documentation gaps before the physician responds. The OaF survey is becoming a documentation specification — no one is helping physicians meet it.

**Market opportunity:** Calibrating physician documentation to OaF survey questions before response — and documenting the calibration — is a specific, high-value service.
