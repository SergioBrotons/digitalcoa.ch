## How Physicians Talk About AI Problems (C311)

### Primary ICP Language Pattern

**The Core Frame (C311):** "My carrier form now has my name on it personally. My medical assistant is using AI on patient data. Swissmedic might classify me as an operator. And if a patient incident occurs with AI involved, I don't know what format to use for documentation."

This is distinct from C310. In C310, the primary ICP anxiety was "I don't have documentation from when I started." In C311, the frame has shifted to: "Multiple accountability vectors are now named, active, and potentially simultaneous — and I don't have a protocol for managing them together."

### The Six Simultaneous Pressures (C311 Frame)

C311 identifies six pressures that are distinct from prior cycle findings:

1. **Medical assistant AI delegation accountability now named and active**
   - Physician language: "My medical assistant uses AI tools on patient data — scheduling, clinical note generation, patient communication. I didn't authorize specific tools. I don't have a delegation framework. If Swissmedic or a carrier asks, I have no documentation of what she was authorized to do with AI on patient data."
   - This is distinct from C310: the staff AI delegation gap is now named, documented in peer conversation, and appearing on carrier forms. It's not theoretical — it's operational.

2. **OaF survey baseline activating physician-specific documentation requirements**
   - Physician language: "My carrier sent an outcomes and feedback survey. It now includes specific questions about AI tools used in patient care, when I started using them, and how I documented clinical reasoning. I don't have documentation calibrated to those specific questions."
   - This is distinct from firm-level policy attestation. The OaF survey is physician-personal and asks about specific patient outcomes and AI involvement — creating documentation requirements that existing medical records don't address.

3. **Clinical reasoning chain as distinct evidentiary standard**
   - Physician language: "Swissmedic doesn't ask if I used AI. They ask: can you show me the diagnostic reasoning chain? What was the AI's role? What was your clinical judgment? My medical record shows my conclusion. It doesn't show the reasoning chain when AI was involved."
   - This is distinct from documenting tool use. The reasoning chain is what Swissmedic and carriers are now asking for — a format that captures physician judgment in real time during the diagnostic process.

4. **Swissmedic operator classification gap — vendor certification ≠ physician operator status**
   - Physician language: "The AI diagnostic tool vendor is Swissmedic-certified. But that doesn't tell me whether I, as the physician using it, am classified as an operator under Swissmedic rules. Those are different questions — and I don't know the answer to mine."
   - This is distinct from C308-C310 findings on Swissmedic. The specific question now is: vendor certification does not answer the physician's operator classification. That's a separate question — and it's the physician's question to answer.

5. **AI incident documentation format gap — Swissdamed doesn't have one**
   - Physician language: "If an AI-assisted diagnosis leads to a patient incident, Swissdamed requires incident reporting. But there's no specific format for AI-involved incidents. Do I document it as a device incident? A diagnostic error? An AI system failure? The format doesn't exist — and the reporting obligation does."
   - This is distinct from general incident documentation. Swissdamed covers medical device incidents. AI-involved incidents fall into an unclear category with no published documentation format.

6. **Carrier form physician-personal certification shift — signed individually now**
   - Physician language: "The carrier form now asks me to certify personally — not the practice, not the group. Me. My name. My license number. My attestation that I maintained human oversight over AI tools used in my practice. I signed it. I don't have the documentation to support what I certified."
   - This is distinct from prior carrier attestation. The form now creates physician-personal certification under conditions of potential nFADP Article 63 personal liability — the physician signed a document certifying something they cannot fully evidence.

### The Physician-to-Physician Channel (C311)

Physicians learn about AI accountability failures through peer networks:

- **How physicians hear about problems:** "At the last AMGe event, a colleague mentioned her carrier sent her an OaF survey with very specific AI questions. She didn't know what to answer. Three others had the same experience."
- **How physicians share solutions:** Medical society conversations (AMGe, AMGm), WhatsApp groups of Geneva physicians — peer networks where specific incidents are discussed by name.
- **Trust formation:** "I trust what my colleague tells me from their actual experience — what they were asked, what they signed, what happened next."
- **Decision trigger:** "When a colleague tells me her carrier form now requires her personal certification on AI tools — and she has no documentation to support what she certified — I go home and check my own situation."

### The Peer Question Sequence (C311)

1. "Does your carrier form now require your personal certification — your name, your license number — on AI tools used in your practice?"
2. "What have you documented about your medical assistant's AI tool use on patient data? Do you have a delegation framework?"
3. "Has your carrier sent you an outcomes and feedback survey with specific AI questions? What did it ask?"
4. "If Swissmedic asked you to show your diagnostic reasoning chain on a specific case where AI was involved — could you? What format would you use?"
5. "Are you classified as an operator under Swissmedic rules for the AI tools you use? Does your vendor's certification answer that question?"
6. "If an AI-assisted diagnosis led to a patient incident requiring Swissdamed reporting, do you know what format to use? Does Swissdamed have one for AI-involved incidents?"

### The Evidence Language (C311)

Physicians in C311 do not respond to "AI governance framework." They respond to:

- "A documentation format that captures your clinical reasoning chain when AI was involved in a diagnosis"
- "A delegation framework for medical assistant AI tool use on patient data — documented, specific"
- "Evidence supporting what you personally certified on your carrier form"
- "Swissmedic operator classification — separate from your vendor's certification"
- "An AI incident documentation format that Swissdamed will accept"
- "A protocol for managing multiple accountability vectors simultaneously"

### The Personal License Frame (C311 confirmed)

**Core positioning anchor (C311 confirmed):** "For physicians, evidence is personal."

This is confirmed as the highest-stakes vertical anchor. A wealth manager's license can be suspended. A physician's license — the patient safety consequences are immediate, and the license is irreplaceable.

### Decision Trigger Sequence (C311)

1. **Physician-personal carrier form arrives:** Name and license number required on AI attestation — physician signs without documentation.
2. **OaF survey with specific AI questions:** Carrier sends outcomes survey asking about AI tools, start dates, and clinical reasoning documentation.
3. **Medical assistant AI tool use becomes visible:** Staff using AI on patient data without physician authorization framework — peer discussion surfaces this.
4. **Swissmedic operator classification question:** Physician realizes vendor certification doesn't answer whether they're classified as an operator.
5. **AI-involved patient incident:** Near-miss or actual incident with AI-assisted diagnosis — Swissdamed reporting obligation activates with no clear format.
6. **Medical society discussion:** AMGe event where peers compare notes on what they certified on carrier forms and what documentation they have.
