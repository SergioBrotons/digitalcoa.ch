## What Others in This Space Are Saying

### AI Tool Vendors
- **Positioning:** Efficiency, speed, cost reduction — "save hours on contract review", "AI-powered legal research"
- **The gap:** Zero focus on the governance decision or the liability question
- **Swiss-hosted tools** (Legartis, Omnilex, LARI, Swiss-Noxtua, Silex): marketed as "compliant" but rarely define what compliance means for a small firm's actual workflow
- **Clio AI, Harvey AI, Westlaw Precision:** international tools with no Swiss professional secrecy framing
- **Problem:** No vendor is addressing the "should I, and under what conditions?" question — only "use our tool"

### Training Providers
- **OAG Geneva:** Active modules on AI — but teach tool usage, not decision architecture
- **SAV/FSA:** Guidelines published June 2024 — widely referenced, operationally insufficient for small firms
- **CCBE guide (October 2025):** Comprehensive but not translated into a small-firm action checklist
- **Problem:** Training solves awareness, not governance. Attorneys leave training knowing more about AI risks but still without a decision structure.

### Legal Publishers
- **Lexis+ AI, Westlaw AI:** Focus on search quality and research speed — not on confidentiality governance
- **Problem:** These tools are for larger firms with established verification workflows. Not built for 2–5 attorney decision-making.

### Professional Liability Insurers
- **Emerging dynamic (NEW):** Carriers adding AI-related questions to renewal applications
- **The gap:** No industry standard for what "adequate AI governance" looks like at boutique scale
- **What carriers seem to want:** Documentation, defined use cases, verification processes, human oversight
- **What attorneys need:** A simple framework they can point to and say "this is our approach"
- **Gap:** The attestation question has no standard answer for small firms

### Consulting / Advisory entrants
- **Some independent consultants positioning on "AI governance for law firms"**
- **Problem:** Often borrowed from corporate IT governance, not adapted to professional obligation realities
- **The opportunity:** Attorneys need governance framed through professional responsibility law, not IT frameworks

## What the Market Is Getting Wrong

### Error 1: Framing AI as an Adoption Question

Everyone asks "should we adopt AI?" — but for small firm attorneys it's a **governance decision question**, not an adoption question. The decision is: "for which client matters, using which tools, with what verification, under what conditions?" Vendors and trainers treat it as binary: adopt or don't.

### Error 2: "Swiss-hosted" as a Sufficient Answer

Being marketed as a complete solution. But server location is one input into a decision about data flows, access controls, training data usage, sub-processor agreements, and professional obligation. Attorneys who assumed Swiss hosting = compliant are discovering the distinction.

### Error 3: Compliance Checklists for Large Firms

Most governance frameworks available assume:
- A compliance officer or IT team to implement them
- Formal DPIA processes
- AI committees or working groups
- 90-day pilot programs with formal evaluation criteria

None of this fits a 3–5 attorney firm. The result: attorneys feel the frameworks don't apply to them, so they do nothing.

### Error 4: Efficiency as the Lead Message

"Save X hours per week" does not resonate when the attorney is primarily afraid of getting something wrong and facing personal professional liability. The efficiency angle is secondary to the protection angle.

### Error 5: Treating the Shadow AI Problem as Solved by Banning Tools

Firms that banned generic AI tools without providing practical alternatives created the shadow IT problem. The ban did not eliminate use — it drove it underground.

1. **The insurance attestation question for small firms** — what to say, what standard applies, how to document
2. **The decision framework that fits a 3-attorney firm** — not scaled down from a 30-attorney firm, but built for that context
3. **The specific AI governance questions clients are starting to ask** — and how to answer them professionally
4. **The near-miss economy** — attorneys sharing AI errors that almost reached courts or clients, and what they learned
5. **The verification standard question** — what "reasonable verification" means for AI-assisted legal research at boutique scale
6. **How to answer the OAG/FSA question** — "what does adequate AI oversight look like?" — when you're 3 people
