## Swiss Legal Confidentiality Framework

### Core Professional Obligation
- **Article 13 LLCA** (Loi sur la profession d'avocat): secret professionnel obligatoire — absolute duty, cannot be contracted away
- **Article 4 CPC** (Code de procédure civile): confidentiality of proceedings
- **Article 321 CP** (Swiss Criminal Code): criminal protection of professional secrets
- **Article 13 FSA (LLCA)**: the duty is personal to the attorney — cannot delegate confidentiality obligation to a tool or vendor

### The Delegation Problem (NEW framing)
When an attorney uses an AI tool, the tool functions as an "auxiliaire" (auxiliary). Under Swiss law:
- Attorney remains fully responsible for theauxiliaire's work
- Must ensure theauxiliaire meets professional standards
- Must verify all outputs before relying on them
- The "I didn't know" defense does not exist for professional obligation

### Swiss Data Protection Law
- **revFADP** (LPD révisée): in force September 1, 2023 — applies directly to AI data processing
- Requires: lawfulness, purpose limitation, proportionality, data minimization
- **Externalisation informatique**: must follow FSA outsourcing guidelines when using cloud AI
- Sensitive personal data (client matters = sensitive) requires heightened protection
- Data processing by US cloud providers may require adequacy assessment or SCCs

## The Insurance Dimension (NEW in C291)

### Professional Liability Coverage and AI
- **Standard RC professionnelle (BROI policy):** typically covers negligence in professional services
- **Key question:** Does AI-assisted work that contains an error fall within coverage?
- **Carrier attestation trend:** Insurers now asking specific AI usage questions on renewal forms
- **No industry standard:** for what "adequate AI governance" means at small firm scale
- **Coverage risk scenarios:**
  - Attorney submits AI-generated brief with fake citations → coverage dispute
  - AI-assisted contract review misses material clause → client claims malpractice
  - Confidential data processed through non-compliant AI tool → breach of secrecy claim
- **What carriers appear to require for coverage:** documented AI use policy, verification processes, human oversight, defined acceptable use cases

### The Attestation Gap
Attorneys face a practical paradox:
- Disclosing AI use to insurer may result in higher premiums or exclusions
- NOT disclosing AI use when required may void coverage
- No standard attestation language exists for small Swiss law firms
- The question "what do I tell my insurer?" has no official answer

## Regulatory Developments

### Switzerland
- **SAV/FSA AI Guidelines (June 2024):** first formal guidance — emphasizes ultimate attorney responsibility, verification duty, transparency with clients
- **Swiss AI Convention (Council of Europe):** ratified February 2025 — implications for legal practice still being absorbed
- **Loi sur l'IA:** expected draft legislation before end of 2026 — likely to mirror EU AI Act structure
- **FINMA:** AI use in supervised entities (fintech, asset management) requires additional governance — relevant for Geneva attorneys with financial services clients

### European Union
- **EU AI Act:** in force since August 2024; core obligations apply from February 2025
- **AI competence obligation (Article 4):** organizations must ensure personnel AI competence — relevant for Swiss firms with EU client exposure
- **August 2026:** stricter rules for high-risk AI systems take effect
- **GDPR + AI:** data protection requirements叠加 AI obligations — attorneys advising EU clients face dual compliance requirements

### Geneva Bar (OAG)
- **Training modules on AI:** widely available, well-attended — signals that OAG recognizes AI as a structural practice change
- **Key OAG message:** "You remain responsible for everything AI assists with" — but no operational framework provided for small firms
- **What OAG hasn't resolved:** the practical decision structure for boutique firms

## What Is Legally Required vs. What's Practically Unclear

### Required (Clear)
- Human verification of all AI outputs before use
- No submission of AI-generated content without confirmation of accuracy
- Confidentiality of client data — must use only compliant tools/services
- Attorney personal responsibility forauxiliaire outputs
- Disclosure to clients if AI materially affects their matter (emerging norm)

### Practically Unclear (The Gap)
- What "reasonable verification" means for AI-assisted research (specific standard)
- Whether Swiss-hosted AI tools require sub-processor agreements or DPAIs
- What to tell your insurance carrier about AI use (attestation language)
- What "adequate supervision" looks like when a paralegal uses AI
- Whether "internal policy" needs formal documentation to satisfy the standard of care
- How to handle client requests to NOT use AI for their matter

## Geneva-Specific Regulatory Temperature

Geneva's legal market has specific characteristics that affect AI governance requirements:

1. **High concentration of HNW clients** — matters often involve wealth structures, M&A, family law, fintech — high sensitivity
2. **International client base** — EU, US, UK clients bring GDPR, EU AI Act considerations into the matter scope
3. **FINMA-adjacent practice** — attorneys acting for financial services clients face regulatory叠加 complexity
4. **Tradition of confidentiality** — Geneva bar culture has exceptionally high sensitivity to professional secrecy — adoption friction is structural, not just cautious

## The Compliance Vacuum

The gap between "you are responsible for AI use" (clear from FSA guidelines) and "here is what you must do in practice" (nonexistent for small firms) is the compliance vacuum in which attorneys are currently operating. They know they need to do something. They do not know what "something" looks like at their firm size.

This vacuum is the entry point for governance advisory services.
