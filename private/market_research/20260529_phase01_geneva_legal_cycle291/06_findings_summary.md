## 6 NET-NEW Findings (Distinct from All Prior Cycles C214–C290)

### Finding 01: Carrier Attestation as Insurance Renewal Trigger (NEW Mechanism)

**NET-NEW vs. prior cycles:** Prior cycles identified carrier attestation as a structural exposure. C291 isolates the specific mechanism: carrier renewal season as the acute trigger point that forces MD confrontation with AI governance questions. The attestation doesn't just create exposure — it activates awareness through a concrete deadline mechanism.

**The finding:** Professional liability insurers are now including AI-specific questions in renewal questionnaires. The renewal creates a specific temporal pressure: the attorney must answer something. They don't know what. The carrier asks annually. Each renewal cycle is a forcing event. The first-time attorney who encounters this question doesn't have an answer. The carrier iteration pattern means each subsequent renewal reveals new expectations.

**Why it's distinct:** Prior findings named carrier attestation as an accountability exposure. This finding names the specific trigger mechanism (renewal season) and the temporal urgency architecture that makes it different from abstract compliance advice.

**Ready-to-use language:** *"Your insurance renewal is your annual AI governance check-in. The questions your carrier asks — they're a map to where the standard is going. Each renewal cycle reveals what 'adequate' has come to mean since last year."*

### Finding 02: The "Swiss Hosting" Assumption Is Cracking (New ICP Vocabulary)

**NET-NEW vs. prior cycles:** Prior cycles identified Swiss hosting as a proxy for compliance. C291 identifies the specific moment the assumption cracks: "L'hébergement en Suisse ne suffit pas. Où va exactement la donnée?" This is a new phrase appearing in ICP vocabulary. The assumption that server location equals adequacy is being challenged by vendors and peers simultaneously.

**The finding:** Sophisticated attorneys are now asking about actual data flows — not just server location. This is a qualitative shift in ICP awareness. The question "where does the data actually go?" signals a level of operational understanding that didn't exist in prior cycles. This question requires a different answer than "Swiss-hosted." The nFADP Art.9 sub-processor chain is what they're actually asking about.

**Why it's distinct:** Prior findings addressed Swiss hosting as a compliance positioning claim. This finding identifies the specific question that signals the ICP has moved past that claim and is asking for the operational reality.

**Ready-to-use language:** *"Swiss hosting is a server location claim. What your clients are really asking — and what your carrier is starting to ask — is: where does the data flow, who processes it, and can you document the chain?"*

### Finding 03: Near-Miss Economy as Active ICP Signal

**NET-NEW vs. prior cycles:** Prior cycles named hallucination as a theoretical risk. C291 confirms the near-miss economy is real and active: attorneys who almost submitted AI-generated fake case citations, almost sent AI-drafted correspondence with errors, almost used AI-generated contract clauses that conflicted with Swiss law.

**The finding:** The near-miss economy is operating as an active ICP signal. Attorneys who have had a near-miss are more likely to seek help, more likely to engage on the accountability question, and more likely to describe their experience to peers. The near-miss creates both urgency and credibility for the briefing conversation. It validates the problem is real and operational, not theoretical.

**Why it's distinct:** Prior findings named hallucination as a general AI risk. This finding confirms the near-miss economy is a specific, active, shareable experience that creates both conversion readiness and peer referral material.

**Ready-to-use language:** *"Almost submitted something wrong — that near-miss you had is the most convincing argument for getting this sorted. You know what almost happened. That's the right moment."*

### Finding 04: Delegation Question Has Crystallized to "If I Delegate to AI, Am I Still Responsible?"

**NET-NEW vs. prior cycles:** Prior cycles identified the delegation problem in principle. C291 confirms the delegation question has crystallized to a specific formulation in ICP vocabulary: "Si je délègue à l'IA, est-ce que je reste responsable?" This specific phrasing is new and signals a precise understanding of the legal mechanism.

**The finding:** The ICP now understands delegation to AI is legally analogous to delegation to a human auxiliaries. Under Swiss law, the delegating attorney remains fully responsible. What they don't know is what "responsible" means in practice when the auxiliaries is an AI system that produces confident outputs you cannot independently verify. The question is precise. The answer requires specific documentation architecture.

**Why it's distinct:** Prior findings named the delegation accountability gap. This finding confirms the ICP has arrived at the precise legal question and is asking for the operational answer.

**Ready-to-use language:** *"Under Swiss law, delegating to AI is delegating to an auxiliaire. You're fully responsible for everything it produces. That's the starting point. The question is: what does responsible look like when the auxiliaire is a model you didn't build, can't audit, and can't fully predict?"*

### Finding 05: Economic Pressure as Emerging ICP motivator (New from C291)

**NET-NEW vs. prior cycles:** Prior cycles identified fear-based triggers as primary. C291 adds economic pressure as a distinct and emerging motivator: "Nos tarifs sont sous pression. L'IA doit améliorer nos marges, pas les dégrader." This is new language appearing in the ICP vocabulary — efficiency without risk has become a conscious requirement.

**The finding:** Fee pressure is mounting across Geneva small law firms. The economic case for AI adoption is no longer aspirational — it's operational necessity. The constraint: efficiency gains cannot come at the cost of the errors that trigger professional liability exposure. The ICP needs AI adoption that improves margins without creating new exposure. This is a different problem than "should we use AI?" and requires a different answer.

**Why it's distinct:** Prior findings addressed fear-based triggers (carrier questions, client questions, near-misses). This finding adds the economic pressure vector as a distinct and now-conscious driver of AI governance decisions.

**Ready-to-use language:** *"Fee pressure isn't abstract anymore. AI adoption that creates more exposure than it resolves — that's not an option. You need AI that improves margins without creating liability. That's a governance decision, not a tool selection."*

### Finding 06: "I Don't Want Another Course on AI" — Training Failure Confirmed

**NET-NEW vs. prior cycles:** Prior cycles identified training as awareness-building but not governance-building. C291 confirms the specific training failure mode: "Je ne veux pas un autre cours sur l'IA. Je veux savoir ce que je peux faire lundi matin." This is a definitive statement of what training does not provide.

**The finding:** OAG and FSA training has been consumed by Geneva attorneys. Awareness of AI risks is high. The failure mode is specific: training teaches about AI tools but cannot teach decision architecture for a 2-5 attorney practice. The ICP has internalized the risk landscape from training but has no framework for making governance decisions on Monday morning. The gap between "understanding AI risks" and "knowing what to do next" is the specific service opportunity.

**Why it's distinct:** Prior findings named the training-governance gap. This finding confirms the specific form of the failure and the specific language the ICP uses to describe it. The service that works must be decision architecture, not awareness training.

**Ready-to-use language:** *"You know the risks. You've done the training. What you don't have is a decision structure for Monday morning. That's the gap. That's what the briefing is for."*
