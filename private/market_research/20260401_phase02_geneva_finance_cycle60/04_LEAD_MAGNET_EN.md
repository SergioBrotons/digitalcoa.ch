## Part I: The Three-Layer Regulatory Convergence

### Layer 1: CARF/CRS — Automatic Exchange of Financial Information
The Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework (CARF), being implemented alongside the existing Common Reporting Standard (CRS), introduces new AI-driven compliance documentation requirements. AI tools used in client portfolio reporting and financial data management must be assessed for CARF/CRS data quality and completeness obligations.

### Layer 2: Federal AI Ordinance — Human Oversight Requirements
In force since January 1, 2025 (15+ months), the ordinance requires human oversight of AI-assisted decisions in financial services. AI systems used for client suitability assessments, portfolio recommendations, or risk profiling require documented human review protocols.

### Layer 3: EU AI Act — GPAI Obligations Already In Force; High-Risk Assessments August 2, 2026
Since August 2, 2025, GPAI obligations are in force for any Swiss wealth manager placing AI tools on the EU market or whose AI outputs are used within the EU. By August 2, 2026, high-risk AI systems in financial services must undergo conformity assessment. Legacy GPAI model grandfathering expires on the same date.

## Part II: The Wealth Manager's AI Readiness Audit

Answer these six questions honestly:

**Q1: AI Inventory**
Do we have a documented, dated inventory of every AI tool used in our wealth management practice — including chatbots, portfolio analytics tools, compliance documentation systems, and client communication platforms?

**Q2: Data Sovereignty**
Are our AI tools hosted on Swiss or EU infrastructure? If you are using US BigTech AI infrastructure (Google, Microsoft, OpenAI, Anthropic), client portfolio data may be subject to US legal access provisions that FADP and FINMA outsourcing guidance do not accept without specific contractual protections.

**Q3: Vendor Contracts**
Do your AI vendor contracts address: (a) data residency and hosting location, (b) prohibition on using client data for AI training, (c) EU AI Act compliance representations, and (d) EU authorized representative designation if you serve EU-based clients?

**Q4: EU AI Act Exposure Assessment**
Have you assessed whether the EU AI Act applies to your practice — specifically whether you are "placing AI on the EU market" or whether AI outputs are being used within the EU by your clients? This is not a question you can answer by assuming the answer is no.

**Q5: Explainable AI (XAI)**
Do you have explainable AI capabilities for client-facing tools? For HNW clients who ask how an AI arrived at a portfolio recommendation, can you provide an answer that satisfies both the client and a FINMA examiner?

**Q6: FINMA Reporting**
Does your internal FINMA reporting include AI risk factors? Do you have documentation showing how AI tools are governed, monitored, and overseen within your compliance framework?

*If you answered "no," "not yet," or "I don't know" to any of these questions, your firm has an active AI governance gap that needs to be addressed before August 2, 2026.*

## Part III: The BigTech Dependency Risk

FINMA's supervisory guidance is explicit: outsourcing to third-party AI providers is a key operational risk. 91% of Swiss financial institutions are using generative AI chatbots from external providers. Most are not in-house AI developers.

The risk is compounded by data sovereignty considerations:
- Client portfolio data on US-hosted AI infrastructure may be subject to US legal access provisions
- FADP Art. 26 requires that data processing agreements with third-party providers meet Swiss data protection standards
- FINMA outsourcing guidance requires that critical operational functions have documented oversight mechanisms

The boutique wealth manager who migrates AI infrastructure to Swiss or EU-hosted providers — while simultaneously building EU AI Act-compliant governance documentation — has a defensible position with both FINMA and clients.

---

## Part IV: Agentic AI — The Emerging Liability Layer

Agentic AI systems — AI capable of autonomously executing complex financial tasks within defined parameters — are emerging in wealth management:

- Autonomous portfolio rebalancing within risk tolerance parameters
- Automated tax-loss harvesting execution
- AI-driven trade execution based on model signals
- Automated client rebalancing triggers

When AI acts (rather than advises), the governance obligation intensifies. The Federal AI Ordinance's human oversight requirement is not theoretical: it is a direct operational requirement for any WM firm using agentic AI. FINMA's position on this will likely sharpen as agentic AI adoption grows.
