## What This Briefing Covers

### 1. The Client Questionnaire Problem — You're Being Scored

Large corporate clients and institutional buyers have added AI governance criteria to their RFP scoring matrices. This is not a regulatory requirement — it's a commercial one. Your client is managing their own EU AI Act and nFADP obligations, and they're extending those obligations to their vendor list.

If you can't demonstrate AI governance maturity in a proposal, you're scored lower. Or excluded entirely.

I've watched boutique firms lose mandates they were positioned to win — not because of price, not because of methodology quality — because they couldn't answer the AI governance questions in the RFP. The client's procurement team asked specific questions about AI use, supervision, and documentation. The boutique couldn't respond with precision.

The commercial consequence is immediate. And it's accelerating.

###2. The Carrier Attestation Problem — You Signed Something

Your insurance carrier sent you an AI governance attestation form. You signed it. You may not have been certain about every answer, but the form looked standard, and the renewal was due.

Here's what I need you to understand: the attestation is a representation to your insurer. If a professional liability claim arises, your carrier will review what you attested to against what you actually do. If there's a gap — if you certified to AI governance procedures you don't actually have — that gap is a misrepresentation risk. Your carrier may seek to void or reduce coverage.

The attestation accuracy problem is not theoretical. I've reviewed carrier attestations for Geneva consulting boutiques. The gap between what was signed and what exists is almost always significant. The managing partner signed certifying to procedures they didn't have, documentation they hadn't produced, and monitoring systems they hadn't implemented.

The question is not whether the attestation is accurate. The question is: what would your carrier find if they reviewed your actual practice today?

### 3. The Sub-Consultant Delegation Problem — You're Responsible for Work You Didn't Touch

Your sub-consultant uses AI for research or presentation preparation. You reviewed the output, incorporated it into a client deliverable, and signed off. The work went out under your name.

Now the client is asking questions about the methodology. They want to understand how the analysis was produced. They want to know whether AI was used, how it was supervised, and how errors would be identified.

You can't answer with precision. You know AI was used somewhere in the process, but you don't have a systematic record of what the sub-consultant used, how they prompted the tools, or what data informed the analysis.

The accountability chain is personal but operationally invisible. You are responsible for the deliverable. You have no documentation of the AI contribution to it.

This is the sub-consultant delegation problem. And it is one of the most common accountability gaps I see in Geneva consulting boutiques.

### 4. The Methodology Defensibility Problem — Can You Walk Through Your Own Proposal?

When a client challenges a recommendation, they have a right to understand the methodology behind it. If you've used AI to build the proposal, the professional liability exposure is live — regardless of whether you disclosed the AI use.

The question is not "did you tell the client you used AI?" The question is: can you walk through the logic of the AI-assisted analysis? Can you identify where AI contributed? Can you explain how errors would be identified and corrected?

Most boutique managing partners cannot answer these questions with precision. Not because they did anything wrong — but because they never built the documentation system that would allow them to.

Methodology defensibility is not a disclosure exercise. It is a documentation practice. And it needs to be in place before the client asks the question.

### 5. The Personal Liability Problem — nFADP Article 52 Is Not Abstract

The revised Federal Data Protection Act (nFADP), in force since September 2023, creates personal administrative liability for managing partners and directors under Article 52. Fines up to CHF 250,000 for intentional violations. The liability is personal — it applies to you as a natural person, not just to your firm.

Your professional liability insurance covers client claims. It does not typically cover nFADP administrative fines imposed on you personally.

The documentation you maintain today is the evidence of due diligence tomorrow. If the FDPIC or a client raises a question about your AI governance practice, what documentation do you have?

Most managing partners I've worked with don't have a clear answer to that question. Not because they're careless — but because no one has ever told them specifically what they need to document.

###6. The Sequencing Problem — What Do You Do First?

Here's what I hear most often from managing partners: "I know I have multiple things to address. I have the client questionnaire, the carrier attestation, the sub-consultant delegation issue, and the methodology documentation gap. I don't know where to start."

This is the sequencing problem. And it is the most important thing I can help you solve.

The order is not "tackle everything at once." The order is: address the commercially urgent first, then the professionally critical, then the regulatorily important.

Client questionnaires and carrier attestations are already live. They have immediate commercial consequences. Personal nFADP exposure is already operative. EU AI Act obligations are approaching. Federal Council AI framework is coming within7 months.

The sequence matters. Getting the order right is the difference between acting strategically and reacting to pressure.
