# Phase 03 Findings Summary — Geneva Consulting Boutique
## C307 Phase 03 (2026-05-30)

## Key Insights

### NET-NEW Findings (Distinct from C214–C306 Consulting Phases)

**Finding 1: Client RFP AI Gates Are Replacing Scoping Conversations as Commercial Entry Points**
Large corporate clients and institutional buyers have embedded AI governance criteria into RFP scoring matrices. This is a structural shift in how consulting engagements are awarded. The commercial consequence is immediate: inability to respond to AI governance criteria in a proposal results in exclusion from the shortlist, regardless of price or methodology quality. The consulting boutique that cannot demonstrate AI governance maturity is competitively disadvantaged before the engagement begins. This is distinct from regulatory pressure — it is commercial gate pressure that requires a specific response capability.

**Finding 2: Carrier Attestation Misrepresentation Is the Primary Unaddressed Professional Liability Risk**
Managing partners have signed carrier AI governance attestations without understanding what they certified. The gap between the attestation language and actual practice is almost always significant. The misrepresentation risk is active: if a claim arises, the carrier will review the attestation against actual practice. This is not a future risk — it is a present exposure that exists for every boutique that has signed a carrier attestation without an accuracy review. The solution is not "do more attestation" — it is "review what you already signed."

**Finding 3: Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Creates a Structural Accountability Gap That Is Inherently Personal**
The managing partner in a small Geneva consulting boutique is professionally accountable for deliverables that incorporate AI they did not directly control. The sub-consultant delegation chain creates a structural accountability gap: there is no proportionate documentation system for small boutiques with 1–3 sub-consultants. Enterprise AI governance platforms are not designed for this scenario. The accountability gap is personal — the managing partner bears the professional liability — and operationally invisible — there is no systematic record of what AI the sub-consultant used. The solution requires a lightweight delegation register and use log designed for boutique scale.

**Finding 4: The Phase 03 ICP Is in Execution Mode, Not Discovery Mode — The Sales Dynamic Has Shifted**
Phase 01 (legal) firms asked: "Am I exposed?" Phase 02 (finance) firms asked: "What does my carrier need?" Phase 03 (consulting) firms ask: "I have five things hitting me at once — what do I do first?" The consulting boutique ICP is further along the awareness curve than legal or finance. They know AI governance matters. They've received questionnaires. They've signed attestations. The question is no longer "do I have a problem" but "what do I do first, in what order." This is the most significant sales dynamic shift across the C307 cycle. The advisory response must be sequenced, proportionate, and operationally concrete — not awareness-building.

**Finding 5: Proposal Methodology Defensibility Requires a Two-Dimensional Documentation System**
Proposal methodology defensibility has two distinct dimensions: disclosure (did you tell the client you used AI) and traceability (can you walk through the logic of the AI-assisted analysis and correct errors). Most boutiques have neither dimension covered. The professional liability exposure is active regardless of disclosure: if AI-assisted methodology produces a flawed recommendation, the liability is live. The documentation requirement is both retrospective (evidence of due diligence if a claim arises) and prospective (the process that prevents errors from reaching the client). Generic AI governance frameworks do not address proposal methodology specifically — they address AI tool deployment, not AI-assisted recommendation production.

**Finding 6: The "Proportionate AI Governance" Gap Defines the Service Design Opportunity**
The AI governance advisory market is oriented toward larger firms. Enterprise-grade platforms, comprehensive frameworks, and multi-jurisdiction compliance tools are designed for organizations with dedicated compliance functions. A5-person Geneva consulting boutique cannot implement these tools — and doesn't need most of what they offer. The market gap between "what large firms need" and "what small boutiques can actually implement" is a service design problem. The solution is not a scaled-down version of enterprise governance — it is a specifically designed set of proportionate services: client RFP AI response, carrier attestation accuracy review, sub-consultant delegation framework, proposal methodology defensibility package, and personal nFADP Article 52 exposure assessment.

## Service Model Implications

### Entry Sequence for Consulting Boutique ICP (C307)
1. **Client RFP AI Response** (bounded, specific, high commercial urgency) — immediate need, creates competitive advantage
2. **Carrier Attestation Accuracy Review** (high-intent, time-sensitive) — addresses signed document risk before a claim arises
3. **Proposal Methodology Defensibility Review** (medium-term, relationship-deepening) — professional liability protection
4. **Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Framework** (structural, ongoing) — accountability chain documentation
5. **Personal nFADP Article 52 Exposure Assessment** (trigger-based, high-intent) — managing partner personal liability
6. **Proportionate AI Governance Retainer** (ongoing, EN + FR) — durable revenue model

### Service Model Distinction from Phase 01 (Legal) and Phase 02 (Finance)
- Phase 01 (legal): regulatory accountability pressure from broker/dealer context, privilege exposure, multi-standard confusion
- Phase 02 (finance): institutional investor DDQ evidence requirements, carrier pre-signing review gap, portfolio construction AI authorization chain
- Phase 03 (consulting): commercial accountability pressure from clients and carriers — distinct from regulatory pressure — combined with personal liability exposure and sub-consultant delegation chain accountability

### Key Differentiation
The Phase 03 consulting boutique ICP is distinguished by **personal accountability at the managing partner level** combined with **commercial gate pressure** (client RFPs, carrier attestation) that is more immediately actionable than regulatory deadlines. The entry trigger is not "the regulator is asking" — it is "my client is asking" or "my carrier is asking." The ICP is in execution mode, not discovery mode. The advisory response must be sequenced and proportionate.

### For Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Framework
- "You're accountable for deliverables that incorporate AI you didn't directly control. Your sub-consultant uses AI for research and presentation. You sign the carrier attestation confirming you monitor AI use — but you don't have a monitoring system."
- "Sub-consultant AI governance framework: a lightweight documentation system designed for a 2–5 person boutique. Structure the accountability chain, document the AI use, answer client questions before they arise."

### For Proposal Methodology Defensibility
- "If a client challenges your proposal and you've used AI to build it, can you defend it? Not just 'did you disclose the AI use' — but 'can you walk through the logic and correct errors?'"
- "Methodology defensibility package: documentation that is both disclosure-ready and defensible under professional liability standards. Specifically for consulting boutiques."

### For Personal nFADP Article 52 Exposure Assessment
- "nFADP Article 52 makes you personally liable as a managing partner. Fine up to CHF 250,000 for intentional violations. Your professional liability insurance covers client claims — it doesn't cover administrative fines imposed on you personally."
- "Personal exposure assessment: what are you personally accountable for, what does your insurance actually cover, and what documentation do you need to demonstrate due diligence?"

## C307 Phase 03 Synthesis
Building on C307 Phase 01 (accountability inversion, privilege exposure via vendor, carrier-Ordre documentation conflict, bilingual client communication bifurcation, multi-standard confusion, active exposure recognition as decisive trigger) and Phase 02 (institutional DDQ competitive gate, carrier pre-signing review gap, portfolio construction AI authorization chain, third-generation carrier processor chain questions activating nFADP Art.9, SRO silence structural vacuum, cross-mandate documentation coherence paradox), Phase 03 adds the consulting boutique-specific layer: commercial gate pressure from client RFPs and carrier attestations creating personal liability exposure for managing partners through sub-consultant AI delegation chains. The three-layer model (commercial + insurance + regulatory) is distinct from Phase 01 and Phase 02's regulatory-primary framing. The most significant C307 Phase 03 insight is the execution-mode shift: the ICP is no longer asking "do I have a problem" but "what do I do first, in what order" — and the advisory response must match that shift.
