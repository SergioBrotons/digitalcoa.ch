# Phase 03 Compliance Context — Geneva Consulting Boutique
## C307 Phase 03 (2026-05-30)

## Geneva/Switzerland Regulatory and Commercial Context (C307 NET-NEW Layer)

### nFADP — Personal Liability Architecture for Managing Partners

The revised Federal Data Protection Act (nFADP), in force since September 1, 2023, creates specific personal liability provisions for natural persons. Article 52 establishes administrative liability for natural persons — including managing directors and partners of small consulting boutiques.

**Article 52 Key Provisions:**
- Administrative fines up to CHF 250,000 for intentional violations
- Liability applies to natural persons, not just corporations
- Managing partners and directors are natural persons with direct personal exposure
- Professional liability insurance typically covers client claims — it does not typically cover nFADP administrative fines imposed on the individual

**The Boutique-Specific Problem:**
In a small consulting boutique, the managing partner is often the same person who makes AI tool decisions, signs carrier attestations, and delivers client work. There is no institutional separation between the compliance function and the business function. The documentation gap is therefore a personal liability gap — not just an operational one.

**What C307 Adds:**
The personal liability framing is now operational for Phase 03 ICPs. Managing partners understand that nFADP fines come from their personal assets, not the firm's. The documentation they maintain today is the evidence of due diligence tomorrow. The question is no longer "am I exposed?" but "what documentation do I need to demonstrate due diligence?"

### Carrier Attestation — Insurance Sector AI Governance Pressure

Swiss insurance carriers have introduced AI governance attestation as a condition of professional liability policy renewal. This is distinct from regulatory pressure — it is commercial pressure controlled by the insurance carrier.

**The Attestation Mechanism:**
- Carriers send AI governance attestation forms to policyholders at renewal
- The form asks about AI tools used, data processing practices, sub-consultant AI use, and governance procedures
- Managing partners sign the attestation as a condition of continued coverage
- The attestation is a representation to the insurer — inaccuracies may constitute misrepresentation

**The Misrepresentation Risk:**
If a professional liability claim arises, the carrier will review the attestation against actual practice. If the attestation misrepresents actual AI governance practice, the carrier may seek to void or reduce coverage. The managing partner who signed the attestation is personally exposed — both to the carrier's subrogation action and to the underlying client claim.

**Carrier Attestation Accuracy — The Emerging Standard:**
Insurance brokers are beginning to recommend attestation accuracy reviews before renewal. The standard of care for attestation accuracy is emerging: the managing partner should be able to confirm that what was signed reflects actual practice, and should have documentation to support that confirmation.

### Client AI Governance Questionnaires — Commercial Gate Mechanics

Large corporate clients and institutional buyers are using AI governance questionnaires as supply-chain compliance mechanisms. This is not regulatory pressure — it is commercial pressure extending from the client's own AI governance obligations.

**The Mechanism:**
- Client faces EU AI Act, nFADP, or sector-specific AI governance obligations
- Client extends those obligations to their vendor list through procurement requirements
- Client sends AI governance questionnaire to consulting boutique as part of RFP or engagement onboarding
- Inability to respond may result in exclusion from the shortlist or termination of engagement

**The Geneva-Specific Dynamic:**
Geneva-based consulting boutiques frequently work with multinational corporate clients. The AI governance questionnaire often reflects the client's home jurisdiction requirements (EU AI Act for EU-headquartered clients, for example). The boutique must respond to questionnaires in languages and frameworks they may not be familiar with.

**The Commercial Consequence:**
The commercial consequence of non-response is immediate: the boutique loses the mandate to a competitor who can respond. This is distinct from regulatory consequences, which are typically delayed and appellate. The commercial gate creates a more immediate and compelling reason to act.

### EU AI Act — GPAI Provider Obligations Cascading to Consulting Firms

The EU AI Act's General Purpose AI (GPAI) provisions, operative since August 2, 2025, apply to AI providers. The compliance burden cascades to consulting firms through their AI tool providers.

**The Cascade Mechanism:**
- GPAI providers must maintain technical documentation, copyright compliance records, and transparency information
- Downstream users (consulting firms) are entitled to receive certain information from their AI providers
- The consulting firm's obligation to inform clients about AI use depends partly on what information the AI provider makes available
- Most boutique firms don't know which of their AI providers are GPAI-compliant, what documentation they maintain, or what information the consulting firm is entitled to receive

**The Documentation Gap:**
The gap between what GPAI providers are required to maintain and what consulting firms actually receive is significant. Boutique firms using AI tools have no systematic way to confirm GPAI compliance of their providers, and no documentation of what information they have received from those providers.

**Geneva-Specific Consideration:**
Geneva consulting boutiques with EU-based clients face particular exposure. The EU AI Act's extraterritorial reach means that advising an EU-based client may bring the boutique within the scope of EU AI Act deployer obligations — even if the boutique is not based in the EU.

### Sub-Consultant AI Delegation — Professional Standards and Liability

Geneva consulting boutiques operating with sub-consultants face a specific accountability problem: the managing partner is professionally accountable for deliverables that incorporate AI they didn't directly control.

**The Delegation Problem:**
- Sub-consultant uses AI for research, analysis, or presentation preparation
- Managing partner reviews output, incorporates into client deliverable, signs off
- Client challenges the methodology or the recommendation
- Managing partner cannot reconstruct the AI contribution to the analysis

**Professional Standards Context:**
Professional liability standards require the managing partner to be able to defend the methodology behind their recommendations. AI-assisted methodology does not exempt the managing partner from this requirement — it makes it more demanding. The managing partner must be able to walk through the logic, identify where AI contributed, and explain how errors would be identified and corrected.

**The Documentation Requirement:**
The sub-consultant AI delegation chain requires documentation that most boutiques don't have: a record of which AI tools were used, how they were prompted, what data informed the analysis, and how the managing partner supervised the output. Without this documentation, the accountability chain is invisible — and the professional liability exposure is active.

### Proposal Methodology Defensibility — Professional Liability Interface

Proposal methodology defensibility sits at the intersection of commercial practice and professional liability. The two dimensions are distinct but related:

**Dimension 1 — Disclosure:**
- Did the consulting firm disclose to the client that AI was used in the proposal methodology?
- Is disclosure required under the engagement contract or professional standards?
- What level of disclosure is appropriate — specific tools, general approach, or both?

**Dimension 2 — Traceability:**
- Can the managing partner walk through the logic of the AI-assisted analysis?
- Can errors or bias in the AI-assisted components be identified and corrected?
- Is the methodology reviewable by the client or by a professional standards body?

**The Professional Liability Interface:**
Professional liability exposure from AI-assisted recommendations is active regardless of disclosure. If AI-assisted methodology produces a flawed recommendation, the liability exposure is live. The documentation requirement is both retrospective (evidence of due diligence if a claim arises) and prospective (the process that prevents errors from reaching the client).

### Federal Council AI Consultation — Timeline and Policy Direction

The Swiss Federal Council's AI consultation is expected to conclude at the end of 2026 (~7 months from current date). The consultation will shape Switzerland's approach to AI regulation beyond the current nFADP framework.

**What the Consultation Covers:**
- Cross-sector AI regulatory framework
- SME-specific AI governance considerations
- Public sector AI use
- Innovation-friendly approach vs. precautionary approach

**Geneva Boutique Implications:**
The Federal Council's framework will likely create additional AI governance obligations for consulting firms. The timeline (~7 months) means that Phase 03 ICPs who begin addressing AI governance now will be better positioned when the framework is introduced. Early movers will have documentation systems in place; late movers will be scrambling to build them.

**The Competitive Window:**
The ~7-month window before the Federal Council's AI framework is introduced is strategically significant. Consulting boutiques that establish AI governance documentation practices now will be differentiated when the framework arrives. Those that wait will face both the new regulatory requirements and the commercial pressure from clients and carriers simultaneously.

## Key Compliance Insight (C307 Phase 03)
The Phase 03 consulting boutique faces a three-layer accountability structure: commercial gates (client questionnaires, carrier attestation), professional liability exposure (proposal methodology, sub-consultant delegation), and regulatory obligations (nFADP, EU AI Act). The three layers are distinct but overlapping. The sequencing question — "what do I address first" — is answered by urgency and commercial consequence, not by regulatory hierarchy. Client questionnaires and carrier attestations are already live and have immediate commercial consequences. Regulatory obligations are important but typically have longer timelines. The advisory service sequence should reflect this urgency ordering.
