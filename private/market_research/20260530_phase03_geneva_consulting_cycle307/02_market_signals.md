# Phase 03 Market Signals — Geneva Consulting Boutique
## C307 Phase 03 (2026-05-30)

## Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures (C307 NET-NEW Layer)

### Signal 1: Client RFP AI Gates Are Replacing Questionnaire-Based Scoping
The traditional client-consultant engagement process begins with a discovery meeting or a request for proposal. Geneva consulting boutiques are now seeing AI governance criteria embedded directly into RFP scoring matrices. This is a structural shift in how consulting engagements are awarded — not a temporary trend.

The mechanism: large corporate clients and institutional buyers face their own AI governance obligations under EU AI Act, nFADP, and sector-specific regulations. They extend those obligations to their vendor list through procurement requirements. A consulting boutique that cannot demonstrate AI governance maturity is scored lower on the RFP — or excluded entirely.

The complaint from the ICP: "I've been working with this client for three years. Now they want AI governance documentation for a proposal I wasn't expecting. I don't have it. I'm not sure what they'd even be asking for."

### Signal 2: Carrier Attestation Misrepresentation Risk Is Now a Known Problem
More managing partners are realizing that the carrier attestation they signed may not reflect actual practice. The awareness is coming from two directions: (1) peer conversations where someone mentions their carrier asked follow-up questions, and (2) brokers who are now flagging attestation accuracy as a coverage risk.

The complaint: "I signed the standard carrier attestation. Then I realized I was certifying that we have AI governance policies — and we don't have a documented policy. I have a generic tool usage guideline I wrote two years ago. Is that what I certified to?"

The failure mode: a carrier that paid out a professional liability claim discovers during subrogation review that the attestation signed at policy inception misrepresented the firm's actual AI governance practice. The carrier seeks to void or reduce the coverage. The managing partner's personal assets are now exposed.

### Signal 3: Sub-Consultant AI Use Is Invisible Until It Becomes a Problem
Geneva consulting boutiques typically work with 1–3 sub-consultants on larger engagements. The sub-consultant's AI tool use is not systematically documented. The managing partner reviews the output, incorporates it into client deliverables, and signs off — without a record of what AI tools were used, how they were prompted, or what data informed the analysis.

The failure event: a client challenges a recommendation. The managing partner cannot walk through the methodology with precision. They know AI was used somewhere in the research or presentation layer but cannot reconstruct the AI contribution to the analysis. The client infers that the methodology is not defensible — and the trust relationship deteriorates.

The complaint: "I reviewed the work. It looked good. But I can't defend exactly how it was produced. If the client asks me to walk through it, I'm exposed."

### Signal 4: Methodology Documentation Gaps Are Surfacing in Pitch Meetings
Consulting boutiques in Geneva are receiving more sophisticated questions during pitch meetings. Procurement teams and client compliance functions are asking about proposal methodology — specifically whether AI was used, how it was supervised, and how errors would be identified and corrected.

The gap: most boutiques cannot answer these questions systematically. Their proposal methodology documentation, where it exists at all, does not address AI use. The pitch meeting becomes uncomfortable when the client asks a detailed methodology question and the boutique cannot respond with precision.

The complaint: "The client's procurement person asked me specifically how I used AI in the analysis. I hedged. I could see they noticed."

### Signal 5: Personal nFADP Exposure Is Being Discussed Among Managing Partners
Peer conversations among Geneva managing partners are increasingly including personal liability under nFADP Article 52. The awareness is accelerating through two channels: (1) brokers raising the question during insurance renewals, and (2) peer-to-peer warning conversations.

The specific concern: managing partners are realizing that nFADP administrative fines (up to CHF 250,000 for intentional violations) are not covered by professional liability insurance. The documentation they maintain today is the evidence of due diligence tomorrow. But they don't know what documentation is adequate, or how to structure it.

The complaint: "My broker asked if I had considered personal nFADP coverage. I didn't know that was a thing. Now I'm wondering what I should have been documenting all along."

### Signal 6: The "Proportionate AI Governance" Gap Is Creating Service Market Opportunity
The AI governance tooling and advisory market is oriented toward larger firms. Enterprise-grade platforms, comprehensive frameworks, and multi-jurisdiction compliance tools are designed for organizations with dedicated compliance functions. A5-person Geneva consulting boutique cannot implement these tools — and doesn't need most of what they offer.

The market signal: there is no established advisory service in Geneva specifically designed for small consulting boutiques facing AI governance pressure. The existing options are either enterprise-grade tools the boutique can't use, or generic AI policy templates that don't address the boutique's specific risk profile (sub-consultant delegation, carrier attestation, client RFP gates, personal liability).

The complaint: "I looked at AI governance platforms. They're all built for companies with compliance departments. I need something that works for a 5-person firm where I'm the managing partner, the compliance function, and the business development lead."

## Key Market Insight (C307 Phase 03)
The consulting boutique ICP is not in discovery mode — they are in execution mode. They know AI governance matters. They've received the questionnaires and attestations. The sales conversation is no longer "do I have a problem" but "what do I do first, in what order." This is the most significant sales dynamic shift across the C307 cycle. The advisory response must be sequenced, proportionate, and operationally concrete.
