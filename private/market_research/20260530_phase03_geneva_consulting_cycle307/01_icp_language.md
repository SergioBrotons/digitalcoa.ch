# Phase 03 ICP Language — Geneva Consulting Boutique
## C307 Phase 03 (2026-05-30)

## Addressable Profile
Small consulting boutiques, 2–50 employees, Geneva-based. Managing director or partner personally accountable. Struggling with AI tool deployment in client engagements. Concerned about client AI governance questionnaires, carrier attestation, proposal methodology defensibility, sub-consultant AI delegation, and personal liability under nFADP.

## How They Talk About AI Problems (C307 NET-NEW Layer)

### The Sequencing Problem — "I Know I Have a Problem, I Don't Know Where to Start"
Phase 01 firms (legal) asked: "Am I exposed?"
Phase 02 firms (finance) asked: "What does my carrier need from me?"
Phase 03 firms (consulting) ask: "I have five things hitting me at once — what do I do first?"

The consulting boutique is further along the awareness curve than legal or finance. They know AI governance matters. They've received questionnaires. They've signed attestations. They're not asking whether the problem exists — they're asking about operational sequencing. The sales conversation shifts from awareness to methodology.

### The Client RFP AI Gate — "They'll Exclude Us If We Can't Answer This"
Large corporate clients and institutional buyers have added AI governance criteria to RFP scoring. Not as a formality — as a gate. A Geneva consulting boutique that cannot demonstrate AI governance maturity in a proposal will be excluded from the shortlist, regardless of price or methodology quality. The commercial consequence is immediate and direct.

### The Attestation Accuracy Problem — "I Signed Something I Didn't Understand"
Carrier attestation forms use language designed for larger institutions. A boutique managing partner signs attesting to "AI governance policies" and "sub-consultant monitoring procedures" — terms that imply infrastructure the firm doesn't have. The gap between what was signed and what exists is a misrepresentation risk. If a claim arises, the carrier will review the attestation against actual practice.

### The Sub-Consultant Accountability Gap — "I'm Responsible for Work I Didn't Touch"
A sub-consultant uses AI for market research or slide preparation. The managing partner reviews the output, incorporates it into a client deliverable, and signs off. The client later challenges the methodology. The managing partner has no systematic record of what AI the sub-consultant used, how it was prompted, or what data fed into it. The accountability chain is personal but operationally invisible.

### The Proposal Disclosure Confusion — "Do I Have to Tell Them I Used AI?"
Phase 03 firms are confused about disclosure requirements. Some believe they must disclose every AI tool used in a proposal. Others assume disclosure is optional. Neither position is systematically correct — the obligation depends on the client's procurement requirements, the nature of the AI use, and the professional standards applicable to the engagement. The confusion creates two risks: over-disclosure (creating unnecessary concern) and under-disclosure (missing a contractual or professional obligation).

### The Recommendation Liability Question — "If AI Assisted My Advice and It Was Wrong, Who Pays?"
When an AI-assisted recommendation proves flawed, the liability question is immediate. The client asks: "Did you use AI to build this?" The managing partner must answer — and must be able to walk through the logic, identify where AI contributed, and explain how errors would be corrected. The professional liability exposure is live regardless of whether AI was disclosed. The documentation requirement is retrospective and prospective simultaneously.

### The Personal Asset Exposure — "nFADP Fines Come Out of My Pocket"
Managing partners in small boutiques understand that nFADP Article 52 creates personal liability. They understand that professional liability insurance covers claims from clients — not administrative fines from the FDPIC. The connection between documentation practices today and personal asset protection tomorrow is becoming clear to them. The trigger is no longer regulatory abstract — it's personal financial consequence.

### The Proportionate Solution Problem — "Enterprise Tools Are Not Built for Us"
The AI governance tooling market is oriented toward larger firms. A5-person Geneva boutique cannot implement enterprise-grade AI governance platforms. They need proportionate solutions — documentation practices, attestation accuracy processes, sub-consultant AI governance frameworks — that fit their operational scale. The market gap between "what large firms need" and "what small boutiques can actually implement" is a service design problem.

## The Phase 03 ICP Decision Trigger (C307 Update)
The managing director or partner in a Geneva consulting boutique engages when:
- They receive a client RFP with AI governance scoring criteria
- A carrier sends an attestation renewal with AI-specific questions
- A sub-consultant delivers AI-assisted work that gets challenged by a client
- A peer mentions losing a mandate because of an AI governance gap
- They realize their proposal methodology documentation is inadequate before a pitch

## Emotional State at Point of Contact (C307)
- Sequentially overwhelmed: "I have four things hitting me — I don't know which to address first"
- Commercially anxious: "I might lose the next engagement if I can't answer the AI questions"
- Retrospectively worried: "I signed that attestation — what did I actually commit to?"
- Personally concerned: "If this goes wrong, the fine comes out of my pocket, not the firm's"
- Operationally uncertain: "I need something that works for a 5-person firm, not a 500-person firm"

## Entry Language (What Gets Them to Engage — C307)
- "I have a client RFP with AI governance criteria — can you help me score it properly?"
- "I need to understand what I actually certified when I signed that carrier attestation"
- "My sub-consultant uses AI — I need to document the accountability chain"
- "I need a methodology documentation system that works for a small firm"
- "Do I need personal nFADP coverage as a managing partner — and what documentation protects me?"

## Phase Distinction from C306 Phase 03
C306 Phase 03 identified the commercial gate pressure (client questionnaires, carrier attestation).
C307 Phase 03 adds the **operational sequencing problem** — the ICP knows multiple things need addressing but doesn't know the order. The entry conversation is now "what do I do first" rather than "do I have a problem." This is the hallmark of the action-phase ICP.
