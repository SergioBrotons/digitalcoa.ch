# Compliance Context: Geneva Legal AI Governance — Swiss and Geneva-Specific Framework

---

## The Swiss Professional Secrecy Framework

### Art. 321 CP: The Foundation

Article 321 of the Swiss Criminal Code establishes the professional secrecy obligation for attorneys (Anwaltsgeheimnis). The provision protects the confidentiality of information entrusted in professional confidence. Violation is a criminal offense.

**The AI intersection:** When an attorney processes client matter content through a cloud AI tool, the question is whether this processing constitutes a disclosure of information protected by Art. 321 CP. The answer depends on:
- Whether the AI vendor's processing constitutes a permitted disclosure
- Whether the client has authorized AI processing of their matter data
- Whether the vendor's contractual confidentiality provisions satisfy the statutory requirement

The Geneva bar has not issued guidance resolving these questions. The uncertainty is not a gray area that can be resolved by reading the statute. The statute predates cloud AI processing and does not address it.

### The nFADP Framework Applied to Law Firms

The Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP, revised 2023, in force 2024) creates a comprehensive data protection obligation for firms processing personal data in Switzerland. Law firms process significant volumes of personal data in the context of client matters.

**Art. 9 nFADP: Third-Party Processing**
Processing of personal data by third parties requires either consent of the data subject or a specific legal basis. When a law firm uses a cloud AI tool, the AI vendor processes personal data contained in client matters. This constitutes third-party processing.

**The specific gap:** Most Geneva law firm mandate letters do not contain AI processing authorization. The absence of authorization means the law firm is processing client personal data through the AI vendor without a documented legal basis under nFADP. This is not a theoretical exposure. It is a present-tense violation.

**Art. 21 nFADP: Data Subject Rights**
Clients (as data subjects) have the right to request disclosure of how their data was processed, including by AI tools. When a client submits an Art. 21 request, the attorney must be able to explain what AI processing occurred, on what basis, and with what safeguards.

**The specific gap:** Most attorneys cannot provide this explanation. The AI vendor's system does not produce a client-specific processing record that the attorney can access and relay. The attorney's obligation under Art. 21 exceeds the attorney's actual capability to respond.

**Art. 8 nFADP: Data Minimization and Purpose Limitation**
Data processed by AI tools must be limited to the purpose for which it was collected. Legal matter data processed by AI tools may be used for purposes beyond the original matter scope: model training (prohibited by most vendors), benchmarking, or vendor system improvement.

**The specific gap:** Even when vendors contractually prohibit training use, the attorney cannot independently verify compliance with data minimization obligations. The vendor's certification is the attorney's only evidence. The attorney's professional obligation is not satisfied by a vendor's certification.

### The FDPIC's Role and Enforcement Posture

The Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner (FDPIC) is the supervisory authority for nFADP compliance. The FDPIC has inspection powers and can impose sanctions for nFADP violations.

**Current posture:** The FDPIC has not published specific guidance on AI use by law firms. The FDPIC's published guidance on AI generally addresses data protection compliance, not the intersection with professional secrecy obligations.

**The uncertainty:** It is unclear whether the FDPIC's enforcement authority extends to assessing whether a law firm's AI processing complies with Art. 321 CP professional secrecy obligations, or whether that question falls exclusively within the bar's disciplinary jurisdiction. The institutional boundary between FDPIC and bar oversight for AI-related matters is undefined.

**The anticipation problem:** The FDPIC has begun conducting sector-specific audits. Financial institutions have been audited. Law firms have not yet been audited. The anticipation of an audit is itself a compliance obligation: firms should be able to demonstrate adequacy when questioned, before the audit arrives.

### Geneva Cantonal Framework

#### The Geneva Bar Association (Ordre des avocats de Genève)

The Geneva bar operates under cantonal supervision and the Federal Act on the Freedom of Opinion and Communication in the professions (BGFA). The bar issues professional conduct standards and has disciplinary jurisdiction over Geneva attorneys.

**The AI-specific situation:** The Geneva bar has not published AI-specific professional conduct guidance. General guidance stating that attorneys remain responsible for AI-assisted work does not define what responsible AI use looks like operationally.

**The disciplinary exposure:** An attorney can face bar disciplinary proceedings for AI-related professional conduct failures. The standard for adequate AI governance in disciplinary proceedings is whatever the bar says it is, after the fact, in a proceeding the attorney did not anticipate.

#### Geneva Civil Procedure and AI

The Geneva Code of Civil Procedure (CPCju) governs procedure in Geneva courts. The CPCju does not contain AI-specific disclosure requirements for filed documents. There is no published rule requiring attorneys to disclose AI assistance in briefs or memoranda.

**The operative reality:** Despite the absence of published rules, practitioners report that some judges ask about AI assistance, and some do not. The inconsistency is not a gap in the rules: it is the actual standard. Attorneys cannot rely on the absence of a rule because judicial practice is applying a non-standardized variable.

**The certification question:** When an attorney certifies to the court that filed documents are accurate and complete (standard certification in Geneva civil procedure), the certification now implicitly covers AI-assisted work. The certification has not been updated to reflect this. The attorney is certifying something the certification was not designed to cover.

### Professional Liability Insurance

Geneva law firm PI insurance is underwritten by specialty insurers operating in the Swiss professional liability market. Policies typically exclude coverage for intentional misconduct and may contain exclusions for technology-related risks that are ambiguously worded.

**The AI-specific situation:** PI insurers have begun sending AI-specific questionnaire supplements. The questionnaires probe AI tool usage, review protocols, and governance policies. The answers affect underwriting terms.

**The ambiguity:** AI-related exclusions in PI policies are being interpreted after incidents, not defined before them. The attorney does not know whether a specific AI-related error would be covered. The ambiguity is structural, not resolvable by reading the policy.

**The carrier questionnaire as standard:** The carrier's questionnaire is functioning as an informal standard for AI governance adequacy. What the carrier asks for is what the carrier considers material. This is not a published regulatory standard. It is an underwriting standard that attorneys are discovering through the questionnaire process.

### The EU AI Act Intersection for Geneva Law Firms

Geneva law firms serving EU-based clients may encounter EU AI Act obligations imposed by clients as a commercial condition. The EU AI Act does not formally apply to Swiss firms outside EU jurisdiction, but EU-connected clients can impose AI governance requirements contractually.

**The specific gap:** A Geneva law firm serving an EU-based client may be required to demonstrate EU AI Act compliance as a condition of the client relationship, without the framework formally applying and without a methodology for demonstrating compliance.

**The compliance paradox:** Swiss firms are being asked to perform compliance with a framework that does not formally apply, using methodologies that are not defined, to satisfy clients who may not have a clear understanding of what EU AI Act compliance requires.

---

### The Federal Council AI Legislative Process

The Federal Council is deliberating Swiss AI regulation. A legislative proposal is expected. The timeline and content are not yet finalized.

**For Geneva attorneys:** The Federal Council's deliberation establishes that AI regulation is arriving. The specific obligations will depend on the final legislative form. Attorneys with AI governance frameworks in place will be better positioned to adapt to new requirements than attorneys who have not addressed the question.

**The pre-deliberation window:** Before the legislation arrives, attorneys can build governance frameworks that will satisfy anticipated requirements. The frameworks built before the legislation are more valuable than frameworks built after: they demonstrate anticipatory compliance, and they can be designed to the anticipated requirements before those requirements are finalized.
