## Six Net-New Findings (Distinct from C214-C284 Phase 01)

### Finding 1: nFADP Obligation Activation Predates Attorney Awareness — The Clock Started Without Them

The nFADP clock activated when a Geneva attorney first processed personal data through a cloud AI tool in 2023 or 2024, not when the attorney became aware of the obligation. This means attorneys who believe they are in a preparation phase are, in many cases, in an active compliance failure phase.

The specific mechanism: Art. 9 nFADP requires lawful basis for third-party processing. Cloud AI tool processing of client matter content constitutes third-party processing. Most mandate letters do not contain AI processing authorization. The FDPIC has not yet audited a Geneva law firm. The absence of enforcement is being read as absence of obligation, which is a systematic misreading of how nFADP obligation functions.

The structural dimension that prior cycles did not isolate: this is not primarily an IT problem or a documentation problem. It is a legal obligation activation problem: the obligation exists and has existed since first use, regardless of whether the attorney was aware of it. Awareness does not activate the obligation. Use activates it.

**Implication for positioning:** The urgency frame is not "you should prepare." The urgency frame is "the clock started. You did not notice." This is a different conversion conversation.

---

### Finding 2: Art. 321 CP and nFADP Create Active Contradiction, Not a Hierarchy

Prior research identified the tension between professional secrecy (Art. 321 CP) and nFADP disclosure obligations. The net-new finding here is structural: these two obligations do not merely tension each other. They actively contradict each other in a specific operational sequence that prior cycles have not named.

The contradiction: Art. 321 CP prohibits disclosure of client-confidential information. nFADP Art. 21 creates a right for data subjects (clients) to request disclosure of how their data was processed. When an attorney uses AI and a client submits an Art. 21 request, the attorney must disclose AI processing details to the client. This disclosure is required by nFADP, potentially prohibited by Art. 321 CP, and governed by two different supervisory systems (FDPIC and the Geneva bar) with no published resolution of the conflict.

The specific naming: this is not a tension or a balance. It is an active contradiction with a specific operational sequence that occurs every time a client submits an Art. 21 request and the firm has used AI. The contradiction cannot be resolved by "balancing" the two obligations. One obligation must yield. No Geneva authority has published which one.

**Implication for positioning:** This is the specific question that should bring a Geneva attorney to a diagnostic conversation. "If your client submitted an Art. 21 request today, what would you tell them?"

### Finding 3: The Geneva Bar Disciplinary Standard for AI Is Defined After the Fact, by Each Proceeding

The Geneva bar has not published a standard defining what constitutes adequate AI governance for a Geneva attorney. The standard will be defined, when it is defined, in disciplinary proceedings after an AI-related incident occurs, by the specific facts of that proceeding, without precedential value for other attorneys.

This means: every Geneva attorney using AI tools is operating under a standard that does not exist yet, which will be created in a proceeding they cannot anticipate, based on facts they do not know will be material, with consequences they cannot predict.

The net-new finding: this is not uncertainty. It is a specific structural condition with a specific name: retroactive standard formation through disciplinary adjudication. The standard that governs the attorney's conduct is created after the conduct occurred, by a proceeding designed to address the specific case rather than to establish general rules.

**Implication for positioning:** "You are being governed by a standard that does not exist yet, will be created after the fact, and will apply to your situation specifically. I can help you build a position before the proceeding, not just respond to it after."

### Finding 4: The PI Carrier Questionnaire Has Become the De Facto Standard — And No One Said So

Geneva law firm PI insurers have introduced AI-specific questionnaire supplements to the renewal process. The net-new finding is not the questionnaire itself (prior cycles identified this). The net-new finding is the specific mechanism by which the carrier has become the standard-setter without announcing it.

The carrier's questionnaire defines what "adequate AI governance" looks like for underwriting purposes. The criteria embedded in the questionnaire (which tools, which review protocols, which governance policies) constitute the operative definition of adequate AI governance for insurance purposes. The attorney who completes the questionnaire is being measured against criteria the carrier has not published as a standard, has not justified as a regulatory requirement, and has not explained as a matter of legal obligation.

The structural dynamic: carriers iterate faster than regulators. The questionnaire has gone through at least two generations in two years. Each generation adds criteria. The standard is moving, and the attorney is required to hit a moving target without knowing where it is going.

**Implication for positioning:** "Your carrier's questionnaire is the closest thing to a published standard that exists for your practice. It is not a compliance document. It is an underwriting instrument. But it is the only document that is asking you specific questions about your AI governance and requiring specific answers. That makes it the most operationally relevant document you have."

### Finding 5: The Supervision Discharge Mechanism Problem Has a Specific Structure at Small Firm Scale

Prior cycles identified the supervision problem. The net-new finding is the specific structural reason the problem is unsolvable at small firm scale without external help.

The structure: adequate AI supervision requires access to the AI's reasoning process, not just its output. The attorney who signs off on AI-assisted work must be able to demonstrate what review covered. The attorney who cannot access the AI's reasoning process cannot demonstrate adequate review, regardless of their AI fluency or their professional experience.

This is not a limitation of the attorney's ability. It is a limitation of current AI architecture. The reasoning process is not accessible to the supervisor in a form that can be reviewed, documented, and defended.

At large firms: this is addressed by compliance departments, AI governance committees, and specialized legal operations functions that create institutional documentation of the review process.

At small firms (2-5 attorneys): no institutional function exists. The managing partner is simultaneously the AI governance decision-maker, the AI governance implementer, and the AI governance reviewer. The conflict is structural, not resource-based. Adding resources (hiring a compliance consultant) does not resolve the structural problem because the problem is the role, not the person.

**Implication for positioning:** "This is not a problem you can train your way out of. The limitation is architectural. I can help you design around it."

### Finding 6: Geneva Attorney AI Governance Has a French-Language Circuit Problem Distinct from the Anglophone Circuit

Geneva's professional accountability circuits operate in two languages with partially overlapping but distinct participant pools. The net-new finding is structural: the French-language circuit for AI governance intelligence in Geneva operates with different dynamics, different information, and different timing than the Anglophone circuit, and no Geneva advisory is addressing both simultaneously.

The French-language circuit characteristics:
- ORDRE des avocats de Genève and SVA as primary institutional anchors
- Swiss Federal Supreme Court (BGE) decisions as primary doctrinal reference points
- French legal academic tradition providing interpretive frame
- Information circulating through professional dinners, ORDRE events, and French-language professional publications
- Slower, more hierarchical information flow than the Anglophone circuit
- Decisions more durable, less likely to be reversed by subsequent developments

The Anglophone circuit characteristics:
- International Geneva professional community (chambers, international organizations, foreign attorneys)
- English-language legal technology publications and conferences
- US/UK legal AI governance developments as reference points
- Faster, more fluid information circulation
- More prone to revision as new developments arrive

The specific gap: the French-language circuit is producing operative AI governance standards for Geneva French-language attorneys, but the content is not circulating to advisors who serve the Anglophone international community, and vice versa. The two circuits are forming different adequacy standards without awareness of each other.

**Implication for positioning:** Bilingual advisory positioning is not a marketing feature. It is an operational necessity for serving the full Geneva attorney market, because the two linguistic communities are forming different standards simultaneously and no one is reading both circuits.
