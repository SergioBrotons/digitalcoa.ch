# Market Signals: Geneva Healthcare — Phase 04 C301

## Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

---

## 1. Carrier Attestation Without Definition

**The acute forcing function.** Swiss health insurance carriers are requesting AI attestations from physician practices. The physician is asked to confirm "human oversight" exists, but no one provides an operational definition of what that oversight must entail.

**What practices report:**
- Blank attestation forms with no accompanying guidance
- Carriers asking for confirmation that AI outputs are reviewed by a physician — with no specification of what "review" means
- Practices being told to self-assess with no benchmarks
- No distinction between AI used for documentation vs. clinical decision support
- Fear of signing something that could later be used against them in a liability scenario

**Signal:** Carrier attestation is ahead of regulatory clarity. Physicians are being asked to self-certify against an undefined standard.

---

## 2. nFADP Compliance Uncertainty for AI Vendors

**Patient data is going to AI vendors.** Independent practices using US-based or international AI tools for documentation, transcription, or clinical support are uncertain about their nFADP obligations.

**What practices report:**
- AI vendors claim GDPR compliance — but nFADP is not GDPR
- No clear guidance on whether BAAs (Business Associate Agreements) are required under Swiss law
- Uncertainty about cross-border data transfers for patient data
- Unclear whether AI vendors qualify as "data processors" under nFADP
- FDPIC guidance exists but is not specific to small medical practices

**Signal:** The data protection obligation is clear in principle but opaque in practice for AI tool deployment.

---

## 3. FMH Human Oversight — Principle Without Procedure

**FMH requires human oversight.** The FMH has stated the principle. No operational definition exists. Practices are exposed without a compliance roadmap.

**What practices report:**
- FMH guidelines describe the *principle* of human oversight, not the *procedure*
- No minimum documentation standard for what "oversight" looks like
- Uncertainty about whether verbal counter-signature, written review, or systematic double-check is required
- No distinction between low-risk documentation AI and high-risk diagnostic AI
- Liability exposure is personal — the physician's license — with no clear safe harbor

**Signal:** FMH's principle is correct but operationally undefined. This creates asymmetric risk for the physician.

---

## 4. Swissdamed Uncertainty Tax

**Swissdamed becomes mandatory July 1, 2026.** For medical devices. Independent practices cannot determine whether their AI tools constitute medical devices.

**What practices report:**
- AI documentation tools, scheduling tools, transcription tools — are they medical devices?
- No clear classification guidance from Swissmedic for general AI software
- The uncertainty itself is a cost — practices spend time and money trying to determine applicability
- Fear that they will be found non-compliant after the fact
- Confusion between Swissdamed (medical devices) and the separate AI regulatory framework

**Signal:** Swissdamed creates a compliance boundary problem for any AI tool with clinical relevance, however peripheral.

---

## 5. AI Accuracy and "Hallucination" Risk

**AI confidentially generates false information.** The core fear: the tool produces plausible-but-wrong clinical content that the physician endorses without sufficient scrutiny.

**What practices report:**
- Documentation AI introduces factual errors that go undetected
- Transcription AI mishears medication names, dosages, diagnoses
- Physicians feel they must re-read everything AI generates — negating time savings
- No validated way to distinguish AI errors from accurate outputs in real time
- Concern that "AI-assisted documentation" creates a new class of liability rather than reducing it

**Signal:** AI accuracy is the primary trust barrier. Reliability claims from vendors are not resolving this.

---

## 6. Documentation Debt Under Audit Pressure

**Attestation creates retroactive documentation demand.** When a carrier or regulator asks to see proof of human oversight, the practice must reconstruct what happened.

**What practices report:**
- EMR timestamps show AI-generated notes but not physician review
- No systematic way to demonstrate "I read this and agreed" beyond a signature
- Audit requests arrive months after the encounter
- Retroactive reconstruction of clinical reasoning is impossible and potentially fraudulent
- The more efficiently AI operates, the more documentation debt it potentially creates if not properly tracked

**Signal:** Documentation infrastructure was not designed for human-AI collaborative workflows. The gap between efficient generation and auditable proof is wide.
