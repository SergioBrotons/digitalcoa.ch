# Findings Summary: Geneva Healthcare — Phase 04 C301

## Key Insights and Ready-to-Use Language

---

## The Central Finding

The Geneva independent physician is not resisting AI adoption. They are managing personal exposure in the absence of clear operational standards. The forcing function is not ideological — it is legal, personal, and immediate.

**The acute pressure point**: Carrier attestation requests are arriving without accompanying guidance. Physicians are being asked to self-certify against undefined standards. The signature creates personal liability. The uncertainty creates paralysis.

---

## Six NET-NEW Findings (Distinct from C214–C300)

### Finding 1: The Attestation-Definition Gap Is the Primary Market Barrier

**The problem**: Carrier attestation asks physicians to confirm "human oversight" — a term with no operational definition from FMH, no documentation standard, and no minimum procedure. The physician signs something they cannot actually define.

**The language they use**: "I don't know what I'm attesting to. That's the problem."

**The implication**: Any solution that helps the physician document a defensible oversight procedure — not just describe AI — directly addresses the acute market barrier. The value is in the proof of review, not the AI tool itself.

---

### Finding 2: nFADP Data Processing Agreements Are a Silent Gap

**The problem**: Most AI vendors offer GDPR-aligned contracts. nFADP requirements differ. Swiss physicians using US-based or EU-based AI tools may have GDPR-compliant agreements but non-compliant nFADP arrangements. The physician doesn't know to ask the difference.

**The language they use**: "The vendor says they're compliant. But what does that mean for me specifically?"

**The implication**: A curated, pre-vetted AI vendor list with confirmed nFADP-compliant DPA templates removes a significant due-diligence burden from independent practices. No such curated resource currently exists for the Swiss market.

---

### Finding 3: FMH Human Oversight Is a Principle, Not a Procedure

**The problem**: FMH has stated the principle correctly. No translation to small-practice workflow exists. The physician must invent their own oversight procedure with no benchmarks, no templates, and no confirmation that their approach will satisfy a future auditor.

**The language they use**: "Does reading the output count? Does counter-signing? Does actually second-guessing the AI?"

**The implication**: An operational FMH-compliant human oversight procedure — documented, step-by-step, designed for a 2-10 person practice — is a standalone market offering. It does not currently exist as a product or service.

---

### Finding 4: Swissdamed Creates Classification Paralysis, Not Compliance

**The problem**: Swissdamed applies to medical devices. The July 1, 2026 deadline creates a compliance date. But the classification boundary for AI tools (is this tool a medical device or not?) is undefined for most AI products used in outpatient practices. Physicians spend disproportionate time on classification uncertainty rather than compliance action.

**The language they use**: "I can't tell if my AI tool is a medical device or just software."

**The implication**: A Swissdamed classification decision tool — simple, self-service, non-legal — would provide immediate value. Practices could determine quickly whether a vendor classification document exists or whether legal review is warranted.

---

### Finding 5: Documentation Debt Is the Unseen AI Cost

**The problem**: AI generates documentation efficiently. But the physician remains personally accountable for every clinical decision documented. When a carrier or regulator requests evidence of human oversight, the physician must prove they reviewed AI outputs — not just that AI generated them. EMRs and AI tools do not produce audit trails showing physician review. Retroactive documentation reconstruction is impossible and potentially fraudulent.

**The language they use**: "The more AI I use, the more documentation I potentially create — for myself."

**The implication**: An AI documentation system with native audit trail output — timestamps, review confirmation, physician acknowledgment — is not a nice-to-have. It is the only thing that makes AI use in clinical documentation defensible. No such system specifically designed for Swiss carrier attestation currently exists.

---

### Finding 6: The Liability Gap Is Personal and Irreplaceable

**The problem**: Every other vertical has institutional liability — the hospital, the corporation, the organization. The Geneva independent physician's license is personal and irreplaceable. AI deployment decisions made today will be evaluated against future standards. A physician who "used AI" but cannot demonstrate oversight procedure is in a worse position than a physician who documented a conservative approach.

**The language they use**: "My medical license is irreplaceable. AI can't take that risk from me."

**The implication**: The framing that resonates with this ICP is not "get AI right" — it is "protect your license while using AI." The product or service that helps the physician build a defensible position is more valuable than any AI tool. The liability protection angle is the primary emotional driver.

## Strategic Notes for Content and Positioning

1. **Do not lead with AI efficiency.** Lead with license protection. Efficiency is secondary to the physician who fears personal exposure.

2. **Do not provide regulatory definitions.** Provide operational procedures. The physician knows what the problem is. They need to know what to do.

3. **Do not use generic AI language.** Use accountability language: "demonstrate," "document," "defensible," "oversight," "audit trail."

4. **Address the carrier attestation directly.** Name it. It is the thing the physician is actually dealing with right now.

5. **Separate regulatory certainty from operational certainty.** The regulatory environment is uncertain — acknowledge this. But operational clarity is achievable regardless — demonstrate this.

6. **The bilingual component is essential.** Geneva is a bilingual/multiilingual canton. French is the primary professional language for many practitioners. The lead magnet must perform in French as a primary experience, not a translation.
