# The Geneva Physician's Guide to AI Accountability

## What You're Actually Signing When Your Carrier Asks About AI

---

**Your medical license is on the line.** Not your practice's reputation. Not a fine you can pay. Yours personally.

And right now, your health insurance carrier is asking you to sign something about AI. Maybe you've already received the form. Maybe you're dreading it.

Here's what no one has told you clearly: **the attestation form is the easy part. The problem is everything it assumes — which you don't have.**

---

## What This Guide Is For

I've spent the last several years working with independent practices across Switzerland on exactly this problem. Not as an academic. Not as a regulator. As someone who has sat in the room when a carrier letter arrives, when a physician opens it, and realizes they don't know what they're about to sign.

This guide is for the Geneva physician — the one running a 2-to-10 person outpatient practice, the one personally accountable for every clinical decision, the one who doesn't have a compliance department or a legal team on speed dial.

It is for the physician who wants to use AI tools to reduce administrative burden, but cannot afford to take on additional personal liability to do so.

---

## What Carriers Are Actually Asking

The attestation forms vary. But the underlying questions are almost always the same:

1. **Is AI being used in your practice?** (Yes or no — and if yes, which tools?)
2. **Is a licensed physician maintaining oversight of AI outputs?** (Yes — but what does oversight actually mean?)
3. **Is patient data being handled in compliance with data protection law?** (Yes — but which law, and what does compliant actually require for AI vendors?)
4. **Do you have a documented process for AI use?** (This is where most practices break down.)

The first three questions are answerable. The fourth is where the gap lives.

**A carrier form that asks whether you have a documented AI process — and you don't — is not a form you should be signing.**

---

## The Core Problem: Human Oversight Has No Operational Definition

FMH requires it. Your carrier asks you to confirm it. But no one has defined what "human oversight" means in a small practice context.

Does it mean:
- You read the AI-generated note before signing it?
- You counter-sign each AI output?
- You have a documented procedure for when to override the AI?
- You maintain a log of every AI-assisted decision?

The honest answer: **we don't know. The standard is being defined in real time by auditors, by carriers, by future case law — and by the physicians who are being held to it.**

That's not a reason to do nothing. It's a reason to build a defensible documentation structure before you need it — not after.

---

## The Six Things You Need Before Signing That Attestation

### 1. An AI Inventory

A current, written list of every AI tool used in your practice — documentation tools, scheduling, clinical support, communication. Include vendor, data flows, and data residency.

This is not optional. When your carrier asks "which AI tools?" you cannot answer from memory. You answer from a document.

### 2. A Data Processing Agreement with Every AI Vendor

Your AI vendor is processing patient data. Under nFADP, you need a data processing agreement with them. GDPR compliance is not sufficient. The agreement must be nFADP-aligned.

If your vendor doesn't have a specific nFADP-compliant DPA, you need to address this before signing anything.

### 3. A Human Oversight Procedure

A written, step-by-step description of how you review AI outputs — what you check, what triggers an override, how you document that review happened.

This does not need to be elaborate. It needs to be real, documented, and consistently applied.

### 4. A DPIA for High-Risk AI Use

If you are using AI across a large volume of patient records — which most documentation AI does — you are likely required to conduct a Data Protection Impact Assessment under nFADP.

This is not a checkbox. It is a substantive analysis of risk, mitigations, and residual risk. It must be documented and reviewed.

### 5. A Swissdamed Applicability Assessment

For each AI tool: is it a medical device under Swissmedic criteria? Get the vendor's classification documentation. If you can't determine applicability, escalate to legal review before July 1, 2026.

### 6. A Carrier Response Package

When you respond to the carrier, you are not just returning a signed form. You are creating a document that can be referenced in future disputes. Your response should include your AI inventory, your oversight procedure, and your data protection documentation — not just a yes or no.

## What You Should Not Do

**Do not sign the attestation form and hope for the best.** The signature is the start of your exposure, not the end of it.

**Do not assume your AI vendor's compliance certifications cover you.** They don't. The liability is personal.

**Do not wait for the regulatory definition to be finalized.** By the time it's finalized, it's too late. You need a defensible position now.

**Do not try to solve this alone.** The gap between "I use AI" and "I have documented, defensible AI oversight" is not trivial. It requires structured analysis, documentation, and a clear process.
