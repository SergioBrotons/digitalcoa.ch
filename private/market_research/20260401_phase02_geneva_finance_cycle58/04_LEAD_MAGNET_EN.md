## The Three-Layer Problem

### Layer 1: CARF/CRS + AI Audit Readiness

The OECD's Common Reporting Standard (CRS) and Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework (CARF) require financial institutions to collect, report, and document cross-border data exchanges. When AI tools process that client data — for portfolio analytics, risk assessment, or client reporting — the audit trail must show human oversight at key decision points.

If you can't demonstrate that a human reviewed AI-generated portfolio recommendations before they reached the client, you have a CARF exposure and an AI governance gap simultaneously.

### Layer 2: Federal AI Ordinance — Already operative

The Federal Council's AI Ordinance (Verordnung für künstliche Intelligenz), in force since January 1, 2025, classifies AI systems used in financial services as either "limited risk" or "high risk." Portfolio analytics tools and AI-assisted suitability assessments typically fall in the high-risk category.

High-risk AI use requires documented human oversight before affecting a client outcome. Most boutique WMs haven't documented this. Most don't know they need to.

### Layer 3: US Section 301 — The Cross-Border Multiplier

The Federal Council's April 1, 2026 engagement with US Section 301 investigations signals that Swiss cross-border financial services are not insulated from US trade policy. For Geneva WMs with any US-adjacent业务:

- AI tools used in US-related compliance work must be auditable
- Data flows involving US persons or entities create additional documentation requirements
- If US trade escalation occurs, documentation done now becomes evidence of good-faith compliance

## Swiss Data Sovereignty: The Competitive Angle

Swiss public trust in major tech platforms is declining (Swissinfo, April 2026). For your HNW clients, this translates into a specific question: "Where is my financial data processed?"

If you're using US-hosted AI platforms for portfolio analytics or client reporting, you face:
- FADP Art. 13 obligations regarding cross-border data transfers
- A client trust headwind among sovereignty-conscious Swiss HNW clients
- Potential competitive disadvantage versus firms that can demonstrate Swiss-based AI processing

The PSI/GEMS sovereign AI infrastructure initiative signals a Swiss strategic direction: AI independence from US hyperscalers is a policy goal. Boutique WMs that align with this direction — even at small scale — can use it as a trust signal.

## The 10-Point AI Governance Readiness Self-Assessment

**Answer each question about your current WM practice:**

1. Do you have a documented list of AI tools used in your portfolio management or client reporting processes?
2. Is there a documented human review step before any AI-assisted recommendation reaches a client?
3. Do you have an audit trail showing who reviewed AI-generated portfolio analytics?
4. Have you classified your AI tools according to the Federal AI Ordinance risk categories?
5. Does your FADP-compliant data processing documentation include AI tool usage?
6. For any cross-border client data (EU, US, other): is AI processing of that data documented?
7. Have you assessed whether your AI vendors transfer client data outside Switzerland?
8. Do you have an escalation protocol for AI errors or anomalous outputs?
9. Has your compliance framework been reviewed against CARF/CRS 2026 requirements?
10. Do you have documentation ready to show a FINMA examiner how AI is governed in your WM practice?

**Score 8-10:** You are audit-ready. The checklist helps you document and defend your position.

**Score 5-7:** You have identified gaps. The checklist is your remediation roadmap.

**Score 0-4:** You are exposed. This checklist is your starting point for H2 2026.
