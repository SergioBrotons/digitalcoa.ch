## Problem Statement (3 layers)

### Layer 1: Three Frameworks Converging Simultaneously (H2 2026)
Geneva boutique WMs face three concurrent compliance pressure points:
- **CARF/CRS 2026:** OECD Common Reporting Standard implementation — AI-processed client data must be auditable
- **Federal AI Ordinance (Jan 1, 2025):** Already operative 15+ months — AI tools used in portfolio analytics or client reporting require documented human oversight
- **US Section 301:** Federal Council's April 1, 2026 formal engagement — boutique WMs with US cross-border业务 face potential escalation in US trade policy

Using AI in portfolio management, client reporting, or compliance processes without a documented framework means you're exposed across all three simultaneously — and your compliance auditor will find the gap.

### Layer 2: Client Data Sovereignty Expectation
Swiss public mistrust of major tech platforms (confirmed April 2026 Swissinfo data) is translating into HNW client expectations: "Where is my financial data processed?" Boutique WMs using US-hosted AI platforms face FADP exposure plus a client trust headwind simultaneously. Data sovereignty is no longer just a compliance question — it is a competitive differentiator.

### Layer 3: The Boutique Compliance Cost Surge
Large banks have compliance teams and legal departments. Boutique WMs don't. The same frameworks apply to you — but you have a fraction of the resources. AI governance is the mechanism that allows a small team to manage a multi-framework compliance obligation without hiring a full-time compliance officer.
