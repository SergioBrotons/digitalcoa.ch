# 02 — Market Signals: Cross-Vertical Synthesis
## Phase 05 C309 | Cross-Vertical Synthesis | All Four Verticals

---

## Cross-Vertical Problems That Appear in All Four Verticals

The following market signals are confirmed across legal, finance, consulting, and healthcare. These are the problems that surface in every vertical — the universal friction points that drive the ICP to seek help.

---

## The Universal Problem Set

### Signal 01 — Personal Accountability Has Activated Across All Verticals

**What's happening:** The carrier form is naming the individual professional — not the firm. Managing partner in law, managing director in finance, broker in consulting, physician in healthcare. The signature is personal. The attestation is personal. The liability is personal.

**Cross-vertical confirmation:**
- Legal: Ordre proceedings name the individual attorney; carrier attestation requires personal certification
- Finance: FINMA requires managing director personal attestation; nFADP Art.63 names the individual
- Consulting: Carrier attestation requires personal broker certification; nFADP Art.52 activates through broker conversations
- Healthcare: Carrier attestation now names the physician individually; FMH oversight is physician-personal

**The pattern:** The ICP signed what they thought was a firm-level compliance document. They now understand it was a personal accountability commitment. The personal exposure has activated — and it activated before they built the evidence system to support it.

**Market signal language:** "I'm the one who signed. That means it's mine."

---

### Signal 02 — The Invisible Delegation Gap Is Universal

**What's happening:** Staff members, associates, medical assistants, and sub-consultants are using AI tools on client/patient matters without the accountable professional's knowledge or authorization. The accountable professional is personally liable for all of it.

**Cross-vertical confirmation:**
- Legal: Associates using personal AI subscriptions on firm matters without managing partner awareness
- Finance: Portfolio associates implementing AI tools without managing director authorization; AI shadow systems
- Consulting: Sub-consultants using AI on deliverables; client-facing work product affected without principal's knowledge
- Healthcare: Medical assistants using AI on patient data; physician personally liable with no tracking system

**The pattern:** The delegation happened informally. No authorization chain was documented. No monitoring system exists. The accountable professional discovered the delegation after the fact — and is now personally liable for all of it.

**Market signal language:** "I didn't know my associate was using that tool on client matters."

---

### Signal 03 — The Vendor-in-the-Circuit Problem Is Cross-Vertical

**What's happening:** The AI vendor holds the client's/patient's data and the system's operational records. The carrier asks the ICP for evidence of AI-assisted decisions. The vendor is not obligated to produce those records to the ICP — and won't produce them to the carrier. The ICP is accountable for decisions made using vendor systems they don't control.

**Cross-vertical confirmation:**
- Legal: Vendor compelled records privilege pathway — third-party vendor records not protected by attorney-client privilege; opposing counsel can subpoena vendor
- Finance: Vendor AI governance contract gap — liability allocation for AI-influenced investment decisions unresolved; vendor contracts silent on record production
- Healthcare: Swissmedic operator classification undefined for small practice — vendor "certification" doesn't clarify physician operator status or obligations
- Consulting: Sub-consultant AI delegation creates invisible accountability gap; vendor records are with the sub-consultant, not the principal

**The pattern:** The vendor is in the circuit but not accountable to the same authority. The ICP signs the attestation. The vendor holds the records. The carrier asks the ICP. The vendor is gone. This is structurally unresolved across all four verticals.

**Market signal language:** "My AI vendor isn't going to give me their system logs when the carrier asks."

---

### Signal 04 — The Competence Gap Is the Primary Barrier to Delegation Documentation

**What's happening:** The accountable professional cannot independently evaluate AI output — they can only review conclusions. This creates a structural problem: how do you document supervision of a system whose operation you cannot assess? The market has not resolved this. Most "AI governance" frameworks ignore it.

**Cross-vertical confirmation:**
- Legal: Managing partner AI competence gap — accountable for decisions they cannot evaluate; needs supervision architecture, not training
- Finance: FINMA "adequate" gap — principles-based guidance without operational definition; human oversight required but undefined
- Consulting: Proposal methodology defensibility has two dimensions (disclosure + traceability); boutique firms missing both
- Healthcare: FMH human oversight stated without operational protocol; physician operates without reference standard

**The pattern:** The ICP knows they need to "supervise" AI. They don't know what that means in practice. They cannot define the standard they are supposed to be meeting. The carrier form asks them to attest to "adequate supervision" — and they have no operational definition of what that is.

**Market signal language:** "I can't evaluate the output. I just review the conclusion."

---

### Signal 05 — The Retroactive Gap Is the Majority Market Problem

**What's happening:** The ICP adopted AI tools 12–24 months ago without documentation infrastructure. The carrier is now asking for evidence of that period. The ICP has no records. The retroactive documentation gap is not a niche — it is the majority market problem by headcount.

**Cross-vertical confirmation:**
- Legal: Documentation paradox as primary blocker — fear of created records is conversion blocker; retroactive reconstruction is the service the majority needs
- Finance: Portfolio AI shadow systems — associate-implemented AI without authorization; evidence gap is12–24 months deep
- Consulting: Client RFP AI scoring as primary commercial gate — boutique documentation hasn't caught up to client expectations
- Healthcare: Post-incident documentation protocol absent for Swiss primary care; no standardized AI incident documentation format exists

**The pattern:** The ICP knows they have a problem. They know linear forward-only solutions won't work. They know the retroactive gap is real. They don't know where to start — and the problem feels overwhelming because it's already happened.

**Market signal language:** "We've been using AI for two years. I have nothing to show for it."

---

### Signal 06 — The Carrier Form Is the Design Specification, Not a Compliance Checklist

**What's happening:** The carrier form creates the accountability standard. The ICP's signature commits them to it. The form is the design specification for the evidence system — not a checklist to be completed. Pre-signing review is the highest-value intervention point.

**Cross-vertical confirmation:**
- Legal: Carrier form sophistication escalating faster than firm documentation improves; calibration gap widening
- Finance: Institutional DDQ as competitive gate — DDQ failure = mandate loss; carrier form language defines the standard
- Consulting: Client RFP AI scoring criteria escalating beyond initial screening questions; carrier form language is the benchmark
- Healthcare: Carrier attestation physician-personal certification activating; forms now naming physician individually

**The pattern:** The ICP signed the form without reading the accountability language. They are now accountable to a standard they didn't know they were committing to. The pre-signing review is the intervention that prevents the problem — not the post-hoc remediation that treats it.

**Market signal language:** "The form has my name on it. Not the firm's."

## Cross-Vertical Problem Synthesis

### The Universal Problem Structure

All four verticals share the same underlying problem structure:

1. **Adopted AI without documentation infrastructure** — efficiency was the priority; evidence capture was not
2. **Personal accountability activated** — the form named them; they didn't know the form would do that
3. **Invisible delegation occurred** — staff used AI; no authorization chain; no monitoring system
4. **Vendor records gap created** — vendor holds data; ICP accountable; vendor not obligated to produce records
5. **Competence gap blocks action** — can't define "adequate supervision"; can't evaluate AI output; doesn't know what standard to meet
6. **Retroactive gap is overwhelming** — 12–24 months of undocumented AI use; linear solutions don't work; doesn't know where to start

### The Compounding Structure

The six problems compound each other:
- Retroactive gap + invisible delegation = the historical period is unreconstructable
- Personal accountability + competence gap = the ICP is personally liable for decisions they cannot evaluate
- Vendor records gap + carrier form = the ICP is accountable for records they don't hold
- Carrier form + retroactive gap = the attestation commits to evidence that doesn't exist

### The Resolution Sequence

The resolution sequence that works across all four verticals:
1. **Name the problems** — explain the structure so the ICP understands why the problem feels overwhelming
2. **Address the retroactive gap** — document the historical period using available evidence architecture
3. **Build the forward capture system** — document what happens going forward, including invisible delegation
4. **Close the vendor records gap** — establish the record production pathway before the audit
5. **Define the supervision standard** — establish what "adequate supervision" means operationally for this practice
6. **Review carrier form before signing** — prevent the next accountability gap from being created
