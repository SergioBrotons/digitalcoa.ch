# 06 — Findings Summary: Cross-Vertical — C309 Phase 05

## 6 NET-NEW Cross-Vertical Findings: Distinct from C214–C308

---

### Finding 01 — The Personal Accountability Gap Has Activated Through the Carrier Form (Not Just Regulatory Action)

**The finding:** Personal accountability is no longer a theoretical risk identified in regulatory guidance — it has activated through the carrier form itself. The carrier form names the individual professional (managing partner, managing director, physician, broker) and requires their personal signature. The signature creates a documented standard the individual is personally accountable for meeting. This is distinct from regulatory enforcement action: the carrier form creates the accountability architecture before any enforcement occurs.

**Mechanism:** Carrier form arrives → names individual professional → individual signs → individual has attested to a standard they cannot define → carrier audit tests whether evidence exists → personal liability activates through the attestation, not through regulatory action.

**Implication:** The pre-signing carrier form review is the highest-value intervention point. The ICP who understands what their signature commits them to before signing can build the evidence system to support it. The ICP who signs without understanding has created the accountability gap themselves.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The form has your name on it. Not the firm's. That's the first thing I tell every client — and the last thing they forget."

---

### Finding 02 — The Vendor Records Gap Is Now a Live Audit Question, Not a Theoretical Risk

**The finding:** Carriers are now asking ICPs for evidence of AI-assisted decisions that resides in vendor systems — system logs, decision records, output archives. The vendor is not obligated to produce these records to the ICP or the carrier. The vendor contract is almost universally silent on record production obligations. The vendor records gap is now a live audit question, not a theoretical risk identified in prior cycles.

**Mechanism:** Carrier audit → asks for evidence of AI-assisted decisions → evidence resides in vendor system → vendor not contractually obligated to produce → ICP personally accountable for records they don't hold → attestation misrepresentation risk activates.

**Implication:** Vendor contract review is a discrete, bounded, high-value service component. The vendor contract defines the accountability mechanism. Most ICPs have not had their vendor contracts reviewed for record production obligations, liability allocation for AI-influenced decisions, or operator/processor classification.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your AI vendor isn't going to hand over their system logs when the carrier asks. That's a records gap — and it's yours to close. I help you close it before the audit."

---

### Finding 03 — The Invisible Delegation Gap Is Now Visible to Carriers, Not Just to Principals

**The finding:** Staff AI use is no longer invisible to carriers. Carriers are asking about it in attestation forms. The principal is personally accountable for staff AI use they did not authorize, did not track, and cannot document. The invisible delegation gap has become a visible audit question.

**Mechanism:** Carrier form → asks about AI use by "all staff" or "anyone in the practice" → principal has no delegation chain documentation → principal has attested to supervision they cannot prove → evidence gap activates.

**Implication:** Delegation chain documentation is a discrete service component. The delegation chain (who delegated what, to whom, how it was reviewed, what the output was) is the evidence the carrier actually wants — not a policy statement about delegation principles.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your associate is using AI tools you didn't authorize. Your medical assistant is doing the same. That's the invisible delegation gap — and it's yours to close."

---

### Finding 04 — The Competence Gap Is the Primary Barrier to Delegation Documentation (Not Just to Tool Selection)

**The finding:** The accountable professional cannot independently evaluate AI output — they can only review conclusions. This creates a structural problem: how do you document supervision of a system whose operation you cannot assess? The market has not resolved this. Most "AI governance" frameworks ignore it. The competence gap is the reason the ICP cannot produce the evidence the carrier wants — not the reason they cannot select the right tool.

**Mechanism:** Carrier asks for evidence of "adequate supervision" → ICP cannot define "adequate" operationally → ICP cannot document what "supervision" means in practice → evidence gap is structural, not just large.

**Implication:** The service that defines "adequate supervision" operationally for the specific practice — not as a general framework, but as a specific evidence protocol — is the service that closes the gap between principles-based guidance and operational practice.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You can't evaluate the AI output. You're not supposed to. What you are supposed to do is document that you reviewed it. That's the evidence the carrier wants."

---

### Finding 05 — The Retroactive Gap Is the Majority Market Problem (And Linear Forward-Only Solutions Are Structurally Insufficient)

**The finding:** The majority market segment — the ICP who adopted AI tools 12–24 months ago without documentation infrastructure — has no dedicated service. Linear forward-only solutions address one compounding vector (time) while the other two vectors (tool count, staff use) continue compounding. The retroactive documentation gap is not a niche. By headcount, it is the largest segment of the Geneva professional market.

**Mechanism:** AI adopted without documentation → 12–24 months of undocumented use → carrier form asks for evidence of that period → retroactive gap is overwhelming → linear forward-only solution doesn't address the historical period → ICP has no service provider.

**Implication:** Retroactive reconstruction is the service the majority needs — not a specialized offering for unusual cases. The service that names the three compounding vectors and offers a multi-vector solution architecture earns credibility with sophisticated ICPs.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Most services help you before you adopt AI. I help you after. You've been using AI for eighteen months. Here's how to document that — and here's how to build the system going forward."

---

### Finding 06 — Geneva Bilingual Regulatory Complexity Is a Service Design Requirement (Not a Localization Task)

**The finding:** The Geneva professional operates in two regulatory language environments — English (carrier forms, client DDQs, EU AI Act) and French (Ordre proceedings, FINMA, Swiss domestic regulation). Documentation built in English may not serve proceedings conducted in French. The documentation system must be designed to function in both regulatory language environments simultaneously. This is not a translation problem — it is a design problem. Most competitors are English-only and invisible in the Francophone circuit.

**Mechanism:** English documentation → French regulatory proceeding → documentation language doesn't match proceeding language → evidence is incomplete or inadmissible → ICP personally exposed in French-language proceeding → documentation built in English doesn't protect ICP in French regulatory context.

**Implication:** Bilingual documentation is a service design decision, not a translation step after English documentation is complete. The service that builds documentation for both regulatory language environments simultaneously creates a Geneva-specific competitive position that English-only competitors cannot match.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Geneva has two regulatory languages. Your documentation has to work in both. Most services build in English and hope the French doesn't matter. I build for both simultaneously."

## Cross-Vertical Synthesis

### The Universal ICP State (C309)

The ICP in all four verticals has three problems simultaneously:
1. The form has their name on it — personal accountability gap
2. The vendor has the records — vendor records gap
3. Their staff is using AI they didn't authorize — invisible delegation gap

The ICP knows they have a problem. They don't know they have three problems simultaneously. They don't know where to start because the retroactive gap feels overwhelming and the vendor records gap feels unsolvable.

### The Universal Service Need (C309)

The universal service need is the same across all four verticals:
1. **Pre-signing carrier form review** — understand what the signature commits them to before signing
2. **Vendor contract review** — identify accountability allocation gaps before the audit
3. **Retroactive documentation reconstruction** — document the 12–24 month gap; produce evidence that satisfies carrier requirements for the historical period
4. **Delegation chain documentation** — document who delegated what, to whom, how it was reviewed
5. **Forward evidence capture system** — continuous documentation of AI use going forward, capturing all three compounding vectors
6. **Bilingual documentation design** — documentation that functions in both regulatory language environments

Across all four verticals, the entry sequence is:
1. **Broker referral** — broker sends ICP because broker's own exposure is reduced when ICP has evidence
2. **Vendor contract review** — reveals the full scope of the accountability gap (bounded, specific, CHF 1,100)
3. **Carrier form pre-signing review** — prevents the next accountability gap from being created
4. **Retroactive documentation reconstruction** — document the 12–24 month gap
5. **Delegation chain documentation** — document the delegation chain that already exists
6. **Forward evidence capture system** — build the continuous system
7. **Ongoing retainer** — sustained regulatory pressure environment requires ongoing evidence production support
