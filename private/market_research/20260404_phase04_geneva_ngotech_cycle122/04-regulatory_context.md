# Phase 04 — Regulatory Context: Geneva NGO & Tech — Cycle 122

## Overview
The regulatory context for Geneva NGO & Tech advisory spans multiple overlapping frameworks: Swiss financial regulation for crypto assets, international IP standards via WIPO, Swiss immigration rules for digital nomads, UN procurement frameworks, and emerging AI governance standards. This file provides the regulatory foundation for each of the six segments in Cycle 122.

## Regulatory Domain 1: FINMA Crypto/VASP Licensing Framework

### Governing Legislation and Authority
- **Primary Authority:** FINMA (Financial Market Supervisory Authority), Bern
- **Key Legislation:** Swiss Distributed Ledger Technology (DLT) Act (2021), Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA, amended), FINMA VASP Ordinance (2020), FINMA Circular 2011/1 (outsourcing), AMLA Ordinance-FINMA
- **Framework:** Switzerland does not have a single "crypto license" — rather, VASP (Virtual Asset Service Provider) licensing under AMLA is required for crypto custody, exchange, and transfer services. DLT Act provides a separate framework for DLT-based securities.

### VASP Licensing Requirements

**Eligibility:**
- Swiss legal entity (AG, SA, GmbH, Sàrl)
- Registered office and actual administration in Switzerland
- Subject to AMLA supervision as VASP
- Minimum capital: CHF 250,000 (for certain VASP categories) or CHF 100,000 + AMLA compliance capital requirements
- Fit and proper requirements for senior management and beneficial owners

**Application Process:**
1. Pre-application consultation with FINMA (strongly recommended)
2. Prepare AMLA compliance framework: AML officer appointment, KYC/CDD procedures, transaction monitoring, suspicious activity reporting
3. Submit formal VASP application to FINMA
4. FINMA review period: 6–18 months from complete submission (highly variable)
5. On-site inspection as part of review
6. License grant (or rejection/refusal with appeal rights)

**Ongoing Obligations:**
- Annual AML compliance audit
- Quarterly/semi-annual reporting to FINMA
- Beneficial owner register maintenance
- Travel Rule compliance (FATF Recommendation 16 / FINMA implementation)
- Registration in FINMA VASP public register
- Notification of material changes to business model or structure

**Key Cost Components:**
- Legal/compliance advisory for application: CHF 150K–400K
- AML software/systems: CHF 50K–150K
- Ongoing annual compliance: CHF 150K–400K/year
- FINMA fees: based on assets under management; CHF 25K–100K/year

### FINMA vs. Jurisdiction Comparison

| Dimension | Switzerland (FINMA/VASP) | Dubai (VARA) | Singapore (MAS/PSA) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **License Type** | VASP under AMLA + DLT Act optional | Virtual Asset License (VAL) | Major Payment Institution (MPI) under PSA |
| **Timeline** | 6–18 months | 3–6 months (simplified track) | 6–12 months |
| **Cost** | CHF 200K–500K+ total | USD 50K–150K | SGD 50K–150K |
| **AML Standards** | FATF compliant | FATF compliant | FATF compliant |
| **Crypto-Native Regulator** | Partial | Yes — dedicated VARA | Partial — MAS with Digital Payment Token rules |
| **Investor Protection** | Strong | Developing | Strong |
| **Market Access** | Swiss/EU adjacent | UAE/GCC market | APAC market |
| **Regulatory Dialogue** | Formal, structured | Active, entrepreneurial | Formal, rigorous |

### Implications for Advisory
- FINMA licensing complexity creates a genuine advisory need: companies need independent, expert guidance through the process and a strategic view of whether Switzerland is the optimal long-term jurisdiction
- The comparison with Dubai and Singapore is factually valid and increasingly relevant for client decisions
- Regulatory arbitrage advisory is not about "escaping regulation" — it is about finding the jurisdiction that aligns with business model, target market, and growth stage

## Regulatory Domain 2: WIPO AI IP Standards and International Framework

### Governing Framework
- **Primary Institution:** World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO), Geneva
- **Key Legal Instruments:** Paris Convention, Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT), Madrid System, Berne Convention, TRIPS Agreement (WTO)
- **Emerging:** WIPO Conversation on IP and AI — ongoing policy development process (9th session completed as of 2024–2025)

### Key AI IP Issues and Current Status

**AI Inventorship:**
- WIPO's position: AI cannot be designated as an inventor under current patent law frameworks; national laws follow
- Swiss IPI position: AI-assisted inventions are patentable; the human inventor must be identified
- EU: CJEU pending cases; no definitive ruling
- US: USPTO says AI cannot be inventor; courts in Thaler v. Vidal (2023) affirmed
- Practical implication: Companies using AI in R&D must ensure human inventors are clearly identified and documented

**AI-Generated Works:**
- Berne Convention: Protected works must have human authorship; current international framework does not recognize AI as author
- WIPO: No binding standard; guidance documents under development
- Swiss Copyright Act (LDA): Protects works of "intellectual creation" with human authorship; AI-generated works are not protected under current Swiss law unless human creative contribution is demonstrated
- EU: Text and Data Mining (TDM) exception under DSM Directive; commercial TDM requires rightsholder opt-out
- Practical implication: Companies using generative AI for content creation need clear policies on what constitutes human authorship and how AI outputs are used

**Training Data Licensing:**
- No WIPO-level standard; national courts leading
- EU AI Act (2024): Requires AI system providers to implement policies to comply with EU copyright law, including TDM provisions
- Switzerland: No specific TDM legislation; copyright exception for private use does not clearly cover AI training at scale
- Practical implication: Companies training AI models on third-party data need licensing strategy; risk of copyright infringement claims is real and growing

**Model Output Ownership:**
- No clear international standard; national law applies
- Swiss position: AI-generated outputs not automatically protected; human creative contribution determines copyrightability
- EU: Same framework; ongoing policy development
- Practical implication: Companies commercializing AI outputs need clear IP assignment frameworks with employees, contractors, and AI system providers

### Advisory Implications
- The WIPO framework is the starting point, but national implementation varies significantly
- Advisory positioning: bridging WIPO policy guidance and Swiss/EU national implementation
- Key advisory services: AI IP audit, training data licensing strategy, model IP ownership framework, international filing strategy for AI-related inventions

## Regulatory Domain 3: Swiss Digital Nomad Visa Terms

### Governing Framework
- **Primary Authority:** SEM (State Secretariat for Migration), Bern
- **Key Legislation:** Foreign Nationals and Integration Act (FNIA / LEI), Ordinance on Admission, Residence and Employment (OEAE), bilateral agreements (CH-EU)
- **Framework:** Switzerland launched a dedicated digital nomad visa scheme in January 2024 under existing FNIA provisions for self-employed persons and remote workers

### Digital Nomad Visa Framework (2024)

**Eligibility Requirements:**
- Non-EU/EFTA nationality (Swiss visa policy for third-country nationals)
- Self-employed or remote employee with non-Swiss employer
- Proof of monthly income: approximately CHF 3,000–5,000 (minimum threshold; SEM assesses on case-by-case basis)
- Health insurance with Swiss coverage (basic LAMal or equivalent)
- Clean criminal record
- No intention to work for Swiss employers (remote work only)

**Application Process:**
1. Identify appropriate visa category (self-employed / remote worker under FNIA Art. 9 or 16)
2. Gather required documentation: employment contract, income proof, health insurance, accommodation evidence
3. Submit visa application at Swiss representation abroad (embassy/consulate in home country or current residence)
4. SEM review: 3–6 months for initial decision
5. Entry permit issued upon approval; registration with cantonal migration office within 14 days of arrival
6. Renewal: annual; requires continued eligibility demonstration

**Permitted Activities:**
- Remote work for foreign employer
- Self-employment for foreign clients
- Limited Swiss economic activity (cannot replace Swiss workers)

**Tax Implications:**
- 90-day rule: Short stays (<90 days) may be permitted under visa-free arrangements for some nationalities
- Digital nomad visa leads to Swiss tax residency if stay exceeds 90 days in a calendar year
- Swiss residency triggers: worldwide income taxation in Switzerland; cantonal Geneva tax applies
- Double tax treaties: Switzerland has extensive treaty network; most digital nomads can claim treaty protection to avoid double taxation
- Swiss tax rates in Geneva: communal + cantonal + federal; effective rate CHF 50K–100K income approximately 20–35% depending on deductions
- Social security: Remote workers with non-Swiss employer may remain in home-country social security system under bilateral agreements (AETA for EU/EFTA nationals); non-EU/EFTA nationals have more complex social security positioning

**Duration and Limits:**
- Initial permit: 1 year (B permit for self-employed/remote workers)
- Renewal possible; no automatic path to permanent residency (C permit) through this category alone
- No integration path to Swiss citizenship through digital nomad visa alone (requires other residence categories)

### Advisory Implications
- Digital nomad visa compliance is complex: tax residency, social security, health insurance, banking access all interact
- Service providers need to understand the full stack of requirements to serve this segment
- Advisory opportunity: serving digital nomad service providers (coworking, relocation, tax advisors) as a regulatory navigator for this new category

## Regulatory Domain 4: UN Procurement Rules and Swiss Tech Access

### Governing Framework
- **Primary Authority:** UN Secretariat Procurement Division (UNPD), New York; individual agency procurement functions
- **Key Instruments:** UN Procurement Manual, UN General Assembly procurement resolutions, Individual Agency Procurement Guidelines
- **Geneva-Specific:** UNHCR, WHO, WFP, ICRC, ITU, UNDP Geneva offices all operate under agency-specific procurement rules

### UN Procurement Framework

**UN Procurement Principles:**
1. Best value for money
2. Fairness, integrity, and transparency
3. Effective international competition
4. Interest of the UN
5. Maximum efficiency

**Supplier Registration:**
- UN Global Marketplace (UNGM): Mandatory registration portal for all UN suppliers
- Registration is free but requires: company legal documentation, financial statements, anti-corruption policy, ISO certifications (where applicable)
- Registration reviewed by individual agencies; multiple registrations may be required for different agencies
- Process timeline: 3–12 months for registration approval

**Procurement Methods:**

| Method | Threshold | Typical Use |
|---|---|---|
| **International Competitive Bidding (ICB)** | > USD 150,000 | Technology systems, large contracts |
| **Limited Competitive Bidding (LCB)** | USD 30,000–150,000 | Specialized goods/services |
| **Single Source / Direct Contracting** | Case-by-case | Emergency, sole source justification | 
| **Request for Quotation (RFQ)** | < USD 30,000 | Low-value procurement |

**Swiss Tech Supplier Pathways:**
1. UNGM registration as first step
2. Identify specific agency procurement opportunities (UNHCR Innovation, WHO digital health, ITU standardization)
3. Respond to UN solicitation notices (UNGM portal, agency websites)
4. Vendor assessment process (VAP — Vendor Assessment Programme)
5. Contract negotiation and award

**ICRC-Specific Framework:**
- ICRC procurement governed by its own rules (neutrality framework)
- ICRC Technology Partnership Program: specific pathway for Swiss tech partnerships
- ICRC procurement focuses on: field communication, logistics, biometric ID, energy solutions
- Security and neutrality requirements add complexity

**Key Constraints:**
- UN procurement timelines: 12–24 months from initial contact to contract
- Security clearance requirements for humanitarian tech
- UN vendor assessment is rigorous; high documentation burden
- Preferred vendor lists may exclude new entrants without UN track record

### Advisory Implications
- UN procurement navigation requires expertise: UNGM registration, agency-specific requirements, vendor assessment, RFP response
- Advisory opportunity: helping Swiss tech startups understand and navigate UN procurement — specifically positioning as a "UN procurement readiness" advisor
- Humanitarian tech suppliers need: security compliance, data protection policies, neutral positioning, technology suitability for field conditions

## Regulatory Domain 5: AI Governance Standards — Swiss and International

### Governing Frameworks

**Swiss AI Regulation (Emerging):**
- Switzerland does not yet have dedicated AI legislation
- Federal Council AI report (2023): Switzerland favors a principles-based, sectoral approach rather than comprehensive AI law
- Key applicable frameworks: nFADP (data protection), Product Liability Act, sector-specific regulation (fintech, health)
- Swiss position: Monitor EU AI Act implementation; consider Swiss alignment depending on EU outcome
- National AI strategy: Swiss State Secretariat for Education, Research and Innovation (SERI) coordinating

**EU AI Act (2024 — Indirect Impact on Switzerland):**
- Switzerland is not in the EU; EEA membership not current
- EU AI Act applies to AI systems placed on the EU market or used in the EU
- Swiss companies serving EU clients or operating in EU markets must comply with EU AI Act
- This creates regulatory burden and advisory need for Swiss AI companies: EU AI Act compliance + Swiss framework navigation simultaneously
- Geneva-based AI companies with any EU activity face EU AI Act obligations

**International AI Governance Standards:**
- ISO/IEC 42001:2023 — AI Management System standard (published December 2023)
- NIST AI Risk Management Framework (US; globally influential)
- OECD AI Principles (adopted 2019; updated 2024)
- IEEE AI standards (various)
- WIPO AI and IP policy guidance (ongoing)

**Geneva AI Governance Actors:**
- EPFL: ISO 42001 adoption and AI safety research
- WIPO: AI and IP intersection policy development
- Graduate Institute Geneva: AI governance research and education
- Swiss Federal Institute of Technology: AI safety and alignment research

### Advisory Implications
- Swiss AI governance landscape is complex: no dedicated Swiss AI law, but EU AI Act extraterritorial reach, nFADP data protection obligations, and emerging international standards create overlapping compliance requirements
- AI safety organizations specifically need governance frameworks that align with: technical safety standards (ISO 42001), institutional governance best practices, and the evolving international AI governance landscape
- Advisory positioning: "AI governance navigator" for organizations facing multiple overlapping frameworks

## Cross-Cutting Regulatory Themes

### Theme A: Overlapping Jurisdiction Is the Norm, Not the Exception
Every segment in this cycle faces overlapping regulatory frameworks. Blockchain startups face FINMA + AMLA + FATF Travel Rule + potentially EU MiCA. WIPO AI IP clients face WIPO + Swiss IPI + EUIPO + national offices + EU AI Act. Corporate venture deals cross corporate law + venture finance + IP licensing + potentially antitrust. Advisors who can navigate overlapping frameworks — rather than siloing by legal domain — are most valuable.

### Theme B: International Institution Proximity Creates Specific Regulatory Intelligence Value
Geneva's institutional density means that WIPO, WHO, ITU, UNHCR, and other international institution policy developments are accessible in real time. Advisors who maintain active engagement with these communities have a genuine intelligence advantage over competitors who must rely on published documents.

### Theme C: Regulatory Complexity Creates Durable Advisory Demand
None of the regulatory frameworks in this cycle are simplifying. EU AI Act implementation will add complexity. FINMA enforcement activity is increasing. UN procurement reform is ongoing. Digital nomad visa guidance is still developing. The complexity is structural, not temporary.
