# The Small Real Estate Agency AI Governance Question
**What Geneva's real estate agencies need to know about AI compliance — before it's a problem**

---

Geneva's real estate agencies are using AI. Listing description generators. Automated Valuation Models. Tenant screening tools. Pricing analytics. Virtual tour narration.

Most agencies haven't stopped to ask: what are our legal obligations for these tools?

The answer matters — not just for compliance, but for the trust of your clients, the protection of your business, and the quality of the decisions you're making with AI assistance.

---

## The Four-Layer Problem

### Layer 1: Automated Valuation Models + FADP Art. 21

Here's the issue that most real estate agencies haven't addressed:

Every Automated Valuation Model (AVM) output that influences a transaction or lending decision requires human review under FADP Art. 21. This is not optional. It doesn't matter what the AVM vendor claims about accuracy. The legal obligation is human review before the valuation is used for a consequential decision.

**What this means in practice:**
- When you use an AVM to set a listing price: human review required
- When a lender uses your AVM data to determine a mortgage amount: the lender's FADP obligation is human review
- When you present an AVM valuation to a buyer or seller: they should know it was AI-generated and reviewed

The AVM vendor's accuracy rate — 92%, 95%, whatever the number — is irrelevant to this legal obligation. The human review requirement is independent of accuracy.

### Layer 2: Tenant Screening AI — The Most Legally Complex Use Case in Real Estate

AI tenant screening tools are powerful. They can process credit data, income verification, rental history, employment status, and behavioral signals from social media in minutes.

They are also the most legally complex AI application in Geneva real estate, because they trigger simultaneous obligations under multiple frameworks:

**FADP obligations:**
- Data minimization: only process what's necessary for the screening purpose
- Purpose limitation: don't use screening data for anything other than tenant evaluation
- Transparency: disclose to every applicant what criteria were used and what data was processed
- Legal basis: you need a documented basis for processing each category of screening data

**Geneva cantonal rental law:**
- Non-discrimination obligations apply to the screening criteria — AI or not
- An AI that systematically disadvantages a protected class creates liability even if the criteria were "objective"
- The burden is on you to demonstrate your screening process is non-discriminatory

**Most agencies using AI tenant screening have addressed none of these.** The AI vendor said it was compliant. That is not a legal defense.

### Layer 3: Property Management AI — The Widest Set of FADP Triggers

Property managers using AI for operational decisions face FADP Art. 21 in multiple directions:

**Rent-setting recommendations:** An AI system that recommends a rent increase based on market analysis is making an automated decision affecting the tenant. Human review required before the recommendation is acted upon.

**Maintenance prioritization:** When AI prioritizes one maintenance request over another based on cost, urgency, or tenant history, this is an automated decision. Tenants affected by prioritization deserve to know how decisions were made.

**Lease renewal assessments:** AI that flags a lease for non-renewal based on payment history, noise complaints, or property damage records is making an automated decision. FADP Art. 21 requires human review before the non-renewal recommendation becomes an action.

### Layer 4: The Widening Gap

90% of Geneva real estate agents believe AI will transform the industry. Fewer than 5% have a written AI governance policy.

The gap is widening: AI adoption is accelerating faster than governance is developing. Every month that governance isn't in place, the exposure accumulates. Every AI tool in use without a DPIA is a gap waiting to be found.

---

## The DPIA Requirement — Why It's Mandatory

Under FADP Art. 21, a Data Protection Impact Assessment is mandatory for high-risk data processing. AI-based AVMs, tenant screening tools, and property management decision systems are high-risk.

**A DPIA documents:**
- What AI systems you're using and what data they process
- The purpose of each processing activity
- The legal basis for each processing activity
- The risks to data subjects (tenants, buyers, sellers)
- The measures you've implemented to address those risks

**Most Geneva real estate agencies have not conducted a DPIA for their AI tools.** The DPIA is not optional for high-risk AI uses. It's a legal requirement. The absence of a DPIA is itself a compliance gap.

## Your AI Governance Checklist

**1. AVM Documentation**
For every AVM tool in use: vendor, data inputs, decision output, human review process documented.

**2. Tenant Screening Legal Basis**
For every AI screening tool: documented legal basis for each data category processed, criteria disclosed to applicants, non-discrimination assessment completed.

**3. Property Management AI Human Review**
For rent-setting, maintenance prioritization, and lease renewal AI: human review checkpoint documented in each process.

**4. DPIA Completeness**
Has a DPIA been conducted for each high-risk AI system in use? Is it current?

**5. Staff Training**
Do your agents and property managers understand when AI is making a recommendation that requires human review?

**6. Vendor DPA Coverage**
Do you have executed Data Processing Agreements with every AI vendor processing client or tenant data?

**7. Client/Tenant Transparency**
Are clients and tenants informed when AI is used in decisions that affect them?

**Score:**
- 0–2: Immediate attention required — significant exposure across multiple AI uses
- 3–4: Framework developing — AVM and tenant screening gaps likely
- 5–6: Ahead of most Geneva agencies — maintain and document
- 7: Governance in place — monitor regulatory developments
