# 03 — Competitive Analysis: What Exists vs. What's Missing

## Phase 01 | Cycle C294 | ICP: Geneva small law firm, 2–5 attorneys

---

## Competitive Landscape Overview

The market for AI tools for law firms is crowded at the high end (large international firms with compliance departments) and nearly empty at the boutique scale where this ICP operates. Providers that do exist tend to target either US-market general legal tech or large European firm portfolios. The specific governance, accountability, and professional secrecy concerns of a 3-person Geneva firm are not the primary design consideration for any mainstream player.

---

## Category 1: General AI Legal Tech Platforms

### Clio
- **Position:** Cloud-based practice management for law firms; acquired AI features via partnerships
- **Strengths:** Market-leading CRM/case management; strong brand recognition
- **Weaknesses for This ICP:** Not Swiss-specific; data hosting and DPA terms not designed for revFADP compliance; no professional secrecy attestation; oriented toward US/UK market
- **Price Point:** Mid-market subscription; pricing designed for established practices
- **Gap for ICP:** No guidance on SAV compliance; no attestation product; no professional secrecy framework

### Harvey AI
- **Position:** AI platform for large law firms and in-house legal departments
- **Strengths:** Deep integration with existing firm workflows; enterprise-grade security
- **Weaknesses for This ICP:** Designed for AmLaw 100 firms, not boutique; no Swiss/EU hosting clarity; no SAV-specific compliance path; likely too expensive for 2–5 person firm
- **Gap for ICP:** Completely out of scale for this ICP's needs and budget

### Ironclad / LawGeex / Kira Systems
- **Position:** Contract lifecycle management and AI-assisted review tools
- **Strengths:** Strong contract analysis capabilities; enterprise integrations
- **Weaknesses for This ICP:** US/EU enterprise focus; no Swiss professional secrecy alignment; no SAV framework; DPA complexity for small firms
- **Gap for ICP:** Not designed for Swiss legal context; no attestation offering

---

## Category 2: Swiss-Specific Providers

### LEXTIC (LexTech Institute-adjacent tools)
- **Position:** Research and academic context; Swiss legal AI research
- **Strengths:** Deep Swiss legal knowledge; academic rigor; SAV-aligned framing
- **Weaknesses for This ICP:** Primarily research-focused, not a commercial product; no attestation product; no AI governance toolkit for practitioners
- **Gap for ICP:** Knowledge without a product/service delivery mechanism for practicing lawyers

### Swiss legal tech startups (Various)
- **Position:** Various niche tools for Swiss legal market
- **Strengths:** Local context; Swiss hosting
- **Weaknesses for This ICP:** Fragmented market; no dominant player; most lack clear revFADP compliance documentation; no SAV-alignment certification
- **Gap for ICP:** No clear "safe harbor" product this ICP can point to and say "this is compliant"

---

## Category 3: Document / Contract AI Tools (General)

### ChatGPT / Claude / Gemini (Consumer-grade)
- **Position:** General-purpose AI assistants
- **Strengths:** Free or low cost; widely accessible; fast output
- **Weaknesses for This ICP:** No data processing agreement; no zero-retention guarantee; US-hosted; model training on user data; no professional secrecy protection; explicitly flagged as dangerous for client data by SAV
- **Gap for ICP:** The "shadow AI" problem — staff using these without firm authorization because they deliver immediate productivity with no perceived downside

### Microsoft 365 Copilot
- **Position:** Enterprise AI embedded in Microsoft 365 suite
- **Strengths:** Already in many firms' stack; familiar interface; enterprise licensing
- **Weaknesses for This ICP:** Data retained in tenant per standard retention policy; no clear DPA covering AI retention; US data flows possible; not specifically designed for legal professional requirements
- **Gap for ICP:** Deployed by IT without legal review — creating the specific problem that conversations are retained without compliant DPA configuration

### Google Workspace AI / Gemini for Work
- **Position:** Enterprise AI within Google workspace
- **Strengths:** Integration with Google ecosystem
- **Weaknesses for This ICP:** Similar to Microsoft Copilot — US hosting, retention policies, no legal-specific safeguards
- **Gap for ICP:** Not designed for Swiss legal context; no SAV-alignment

---

## Category 4: Generic Compliance / DPA Products

### OneTrust / TrustArc (Data Privacy Platforms)
- **Position:** Enterprise privacy compliance management
- **Strengths:** Robust DPIA frameworks; enterprise-grade
- **Weaknesses for This ICP:** Designed for DPO teams at large organizations; excessive for a 2–5 person firm; no AI-specific module; no Swiss law specificity
- **Gap for ICP:** Prohibitively complex and expensive for boutique scale

### Generic DPA template libraries (various online sources)
- **Position:** Downloadable DPA templates for revFADP/GDPR compliance
- **Strengths:** Free or low cost; covers basic GDPR/revFADP requirements
- **Weaknesses for This ICP:** Not AI-specific; not legal profession-specific; requires legal expertise to adapt; no attestation value
- **Gap for ICP:** Useful as a starting point but provides no "this is compliant for a law firm using AI" certainty

---

## Category 5: Informal / Unorganized Options

### DIY AI governance — law firms doing it themselves
- **Position:** Firms building their own AI policies based on SAV guidelines
- **Strengths:** Tailored to specific practice needs
- **Weaknesses for This ICP:** Requires expertise the firm doesn't have; time-intensive; no external validation; produces something defensible only if challenged and found adequate
- **Gap for ICP:** "I'm not a tech lawyer, I can't assess AI vendors" — so nothing gets done

### General IT consultants
- **Position:** IT firms offering AI tool implementation for law firms
- **Strengths:** Technical implementation capability
- **Weaknesses for This ICP:** Not legally specialized; don't know SAV guidelines or revFADP implications for legal profession; can't provide professional liability perspective
- **Gap for ICP:** IT implementation without legal accountability framing

## What's Actually Missing for This ICP

### Gap 1: The Accountability Bridge
No product or service specifically helps a 2–5 person Geneva firm answer: *"Can I use this tool for this matter type and defend it to the SAV?"*

What exists: SAV guidelines (framework, not decision tree).  
What's missing: A practical decision tool that maps specific tool categories to specific risk levels and practice areas for small Swiss firms.

### Gap 2: The Attestation Product
No vendor in the market offers a written attestation specifically designed for a law firm to demonstrate to clients, the SAV, or a court that their AI tool meets professional secrecy and revFADP requirements.

What exists: Generic security certifications, vendor terms of service.  
What's missing: A written attestation document — formatted for legal professional requirements — that a law firm can keep on file and produce if challenged.

### Gap 3: The Proportional Governance Framework
All available governance frameworks are designed for enterprises. Nothing acknowledges that a 3-person firm's governance needs are fundamentally different — and that "adequate" governance for a boutique firm is not the same as "adequate" for a 50-lawyer firm.

What exists: Enterprise compliance frameworks; generic AI governance checklists.  
What's missing: A scaled framework that says "for a firm of your size, with this practice profile, here is what adequate actually looks like — and here is how to document it."

### Gap 4: The SAV-Aligned Training / Briefing
No short-format resource — usable in under an hour — that tells a Geneva small-firm lawyer what the SAV guidelines actually require in practical terms, what the revFADP implications are for their AI use, and what steps to take this week.

What exists: Full text of SAV guidelines; academic analyses; vendor marketing.  
What's missing: A practitioner's briefing — direct, concise, in French and English — that distills the SAV guidelines into actionable本周 immediate steps.

## Strategic Implication

The competitive landscape confirms that this ICP is not well-served by any existing provider. The market opportunity is not to compete with Clio or Harvey — it's to own the specific niche that no one has claimed: **accountability infrastructure for small Swiss law firms using AI.**

The form this takes should speak to the ICP's specific language: not "AI legal platform," but "the attestation that lets you sleep at night."
