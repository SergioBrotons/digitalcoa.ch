# What I Wish I'd Known Before I Signed My Carrier's AI Attestation

I signed the form. Like everyone does.

It asked a series of questions about how I use AI in my practice. I answered them honestly — yes, I use AI tools. Yes, I review output before using it. Yes, I supervise how it's used in my firm.

The renewal went through. I moved on.

Six months later, I realized something that kept me up at night: **I had no idea what the form had actually asked me to attest to.**

It turns out the form was architecture, not just a form. When I said I "adequately supervise AI tools," I was creating a standard — a documented standard — that I couldn't demonstrate I met. The carrier didn't ask me to prove it at the time. But the form's architecture assumed proof existed. And I had none.

---

## The Documentation Gap No One Talks About

The conversation about AI in Geneva legal practice is full of frameworks, guidance, and principles. "Follow best practices." "Use AI responsibly." "Maintain professional standards."

What no one talks about is **the evidence**.

When the carrier asks whether you supervise AI tools adequately — they don't want to see your policy. They want to see what you actually documented about how you supervised those tools, for which matters, and what you found when you reviewed the output.

That's the gap.

Not the policy gap. The evidence gap.

---

## What I Learned About Professional Secrecy

I didn't think about professional secrecy when I started using AI tools. The tool was "internal." It was just me, using it to assist with case research and document review.

Then I realized: the tool processes everything on the vendor's servers. Not mine. The vendor can access it. Their sub-processors can access it. Depending on where the data is processed, law enforcement in the vendor's jurisdiction may be able to access it.

And the vendor's terms of service? I clicked through them. Like everyone does.

I went back and tried to read them. The data retention clause. The audit rights for the vendor. The jurisdiction clause. I had accepted all of it — without knowing whether any of it conflicts with what article 321 of the Swiss Criminal Code requires of me as a lawyer.

**I didn't know I was supposed to have reviewed the vendor contract against my professional secrecy obligations. No one told me. The vendor certainly didn't.**

## The Retention Problem No One Has Solved

We use AI in almost every case now. Research. Review. Draft assistance. Brief analysis.

There's no record of what we used. Which tools. Which prompts. What we reviewed. What we decided to use and what we rejected.

And I don't know how long I'm supposed to keep those records.

One year? Seven years? The duration of the matter plus some? There's no Geneva guidance. No carrier guidance. No bar guidance. Nothing.

Every case we handle adds to the retention debt. And I don't know when the clock stops.

## What Clients Are Starting to Ask

Last month, a new client asked me during intake: "Will you be using AI on my case? How will my information be protected?"

I had a vague clause in my engagement letter. Something about "technology-assisted tools." I wasn't sure what it meant or whether it was adequate.

I didn't have a clear answer for the client.

**They didn't ask me to explain my AI governance. They just asked a direct question. I didn't have a direct answer. That's a problem.**

---

## The Attestation Made It Worse

Here's what I didn't understand at the time I signed:

The carrier form didn't just ask questions. It created a record. And that record defined a standard.

When I attested to "adequate supervision of AI tools," I was saying: this is what I do. This is my standard. I meet it.

But I couldn't show what adequate supervision looks like in my practice. I couldn't produce documentation of the supervision process. I couldn't reconstruct a single matter and show how AI was used and reviewed.

**The attestation created a documented standard. The attestation exposed me to the very audit I thought the form was protecting me from.**

## The Junior Associate Problem

The associates use AI more than I do. They grew up with it. They know the tools better.

I don't know what they're entering into those tools. I don't know which client data is going where. I don't know what the vendors are doing with the prompts.

And I'm responsible for it. Personally. The form asks about my supervision of AI usage in my practice. That includes everyone in my practice.

**I can't supervise what I don't understand. But the form doesn't care about that distinction.**

## What "Adequate" Actually Means

I went looking for a Geneva standard. A definition of "adequate" AI governance for a small firm. Something I could point to and say: if I do this, I'm doing enough.

There isn't one.

The FSA has confirmed the obligation exists. The carrier attestation acknowledges it. The Swiss AI Act implies it.

**No one has defined what adequate means for a two-to-five attorney Geneva firm. Nobody. That's not a gap in the market. That's a first-mover opportunity.**
