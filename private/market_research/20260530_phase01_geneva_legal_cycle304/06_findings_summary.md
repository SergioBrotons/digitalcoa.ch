# Findings Summary: Geneva Legal — Small Law Firms (2–5 Attorneys)

**Cycle:** C304 Phase 01  
**Date:** 2026-05-30  
**6 NET-NEW findings for Phase 01**

## Key Insights

### Finding 1: Carrier Attestation Is Architecture, Not Just a Form

**Insight:** The carrier attestation form is not a compliance checkbox. It creates a documented standard that the firm attested to meeting. When the ICP said "yes" to adequate supervision, they created a record — and that record becomes the benchmark if a carrier audit occurs.

**What this means:** The attestation did not protect them. It documented the standard they must meet — and they cannot demonstrate they meet it. This is worse than not having attested: the form created a documented standard they are currently failing.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You said 'yes' to adequate supervision. The form didn't ask you to prove it. But the form's architecture assumes you can. That's the gap."

---

### Finding 2: Article 321 CC Is Being Violated Through AI Vendor Contracts No One Reviewed

**Insight:** Swiss professional secrecy under article 321 CC requires protection of client information in all professional contexts. Cloud-based AI tools process client data on vendor servers — potentially constituting a disclosure under article 321 CC. The vendor contracts governing this processing have not been reviewed by any Geneva firm against professional secrecy obligations.

**What this means:** The professional secrecy exposure is not hypothetical. It is a specific, concrete legal risk created by vendor arrangements the firm accepted without review. The vendor's terms — data retention, audit rights, jurisdiction, sub-processor access — may conflict with article 321 CC obligations.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You clicked through the vendor agreement. The data retention clause, the vendor audit rights, the jurisdiction clause — none of it was reviewed against your professional secrecy obligations. That's not an assumption. That's a fact."

---

### Finding 3: Documentation Retention Is an Unknown Compounding Debt

**Insight:** Geneva small firms use AI systematically across client matters. They have no retention policy for AI use records. They do not know how long they are required to keep AI use records under professional secrecy law. The retention dimension compounds the existing documentation debt: not only do they lack documentation, they don't know how long the obligation to retain it extends.

**What this means:** The documentation debt has a time axis. Every month of undocumented AI usage adds compounding exposure — and the clock has no Geneva-defined stop time.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You don't know how long to keep AI use records. Neither does anyone else — because no one has defined what a Geneva small firm is required to keep. Every case adds to the debt. The clock has no Geneva-defined stop."

---

### Finding 4: Client AI Questions Are a Live Lead Trigger, Not a Theoretical Scenario

**Insight:** Geneva law firms are now receiving specific AI-related questions from clients during intake and throughout matters. Firms have no protocol for responding. Engagement letters contain vague technology clauses written without knowing if they are adequate.

**What this means:** The client relationship is now an AI governance trigger — actively creating pressure for firms that have no prepared response. This is not future risk; it is present reality.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Clients are asking. You don't have a protocol. The engagement letter has a vague clause. That's not a policy — that's a liability."

---

### Finding 5: Staff AI Usage Is the Unsupervised Accountability Layer

**Insight:** Junior associates at small Geneva firms use AI more actively than partners. Managing partners are unaware of what tools are being used, what prompts are entered, and what client data is processed. The supervisory responsibility extends to staff AI usage — but there is no protocol for this at boutique scale.

**What this means:** The accountability gap is not just personal — it is generational and structural. The managing partner bears personal liability for AI usage they do not understand and cannot supervise with existing tools.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The junior associate uses AI on client matters. You don't know what they're entering. You don't know where it goes. You're responsible for it. That's the supervisory gap."

---

### Finding 6: The Geneva Standard Definition Is Unclaimed — First-Mover Advantage Is Active

**Insight:** No one has defined what "adequate AI governance documentation" means for a 2–5 attorney Geneva firm. The FSA has confirmed the obligation. The carrier attestation has created a standard. The Swiss AI Act implies requirements. No Geneva-specific, size-specific, actionable definition exists. The firm that defines it captures the market.

**What this means:** This is not a market gap waiting to be filled. It is a market position waiting to be claimed. The Geneva standard defined with enough specificity that carriers, bar, and clients accept it — is the positioning anchor.

**Ready-to-use language:** "No one has defined adequate AI governance documentation for a Geneva small firm. We have. The Geneva standard is our baseline — and it meets what the carrier expects, what the bar requires, and what the Swiss AI Act demands."
