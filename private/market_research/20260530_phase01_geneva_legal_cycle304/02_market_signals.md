# Market Signals: Geneva Legal — Small Law Firms (2–5 Attorneys)

**Cycle:** C304 Phase 01  
**Date:** 2026-05-30

---

## Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

### Signal 1: Carrier Attestation Architecture Is Creating Evidence Obligations the ICP Cannot Meet

**Signal:** The carrier attestation form has evolved. Where C303 observed attestation completion, C304 observes that attestation now implies a documentation standard — not just a yes/no answer.

**What this means:** The ICP answered "yes" to adequate supervision. The form implies there is documentation that demonstrates adequate supervision. The ICP has no such documentation. The attestation is now the liability trigger, not the protection.

**Market complaint:** "I answered the form honestly. But what the form actually asked for — evidence of supervision — I don't have."

---

### Signal 2: Professional Secrecy Article 321 CC Is Being Tested Against AI Vendor Contracts

**Signal:** Geneva law firms are discovering that their AI vendor contracts contain data retention clauses, audit rights for vendors, and jurisdiction clauses that may conflict with professional secrecy obligations under Swiss law.

**What this means:** The professional secrecy obligation under article 321 CC is not theoretical. It is a specific legal obligation. AI vendor contracts may create conditions that conflict with it. No Geneva firm has had these contracts reviewed against professional secrecy law.

**Market complaint:** "I signed the vendor agreement. I didn't read it. Now I don't know if what I signed conflicts with my bar obligations."

---

### Signal 3: Documentation Retention Periods Are Entirely Unknown

**Signal:** Geneva small firms are using AI systematically across client matters. They have no retention policy for AI use records. They do not know how long they are required to keep such records under professional secrecy obligations.

**What this means:** The documentation debt has a time dimension now. Not only do they lack the documentation — they don't know how long the obligation to retain it extends. This compounds the existing documentation debt.

**Market complaint:** "We use AI in almost every case. There's no record of what we used or reviewed. And I don't even know if I'm supposed to be keeping those records for one year or seven."

---

### Signal 4: Client AI Questions Are No Longer Theoretical — They Are Happening at Intake

**Signal:** Geneva law firms are receiving specific questions from clients about AI usage in their matters. Firms have no protocol for responding. Some have added vague AI clauses to engagement letters without knowing if those clauses are adequate.

**What this means:** Client accountability is entering the firm through the front door. The client relationship is now a trigger for AI governance questions — not just the carrier or the bar.

**Market complaint:** "A client asked me directly: 'Will you be using AI on my case, and will my information be safe?' I didn't have a clear answer."

---

### Signal 5: The "Adequate Supervision" Standard in Attestation Has Become a Personal Liability Formulation

**Signal:** By attesting to adequate supervision of AI tools, the managing partner or responsible attorney has created a legal standard. If a carrier audit occurs or a client claim arises, the "adequate supervision" formulation becomes the benchmark against which their conduct is measured.

**What this means:** The attestation did not protect them. It defined the standard they must meet — and they cannot demonstrate they meet it. This is worse than not having attested: it created a documented standard they are failing.

**Market complaint:** "I said I adequately supervise AI tools. If the carrier asks me to show what that means — I can't. And now I've written it down."

---

### Signal 6: Staff AI Usage Is Creating a Supervisory Liability Layer the ICP Did Not Anticipate

**Signal:** Junior associates at small firms are using AI more actively than partners. Managing partners are unaware of what tools are being used, what prompts are being entered, and what client data is being processed. The supervisory responsibility extends to staff AI usage — but there is no protocol for this at boutique scale.

**What this means:** The accountability gap is not just about the managing partner's own AI use. It extends to everyone in the firm. And the managing partner is personally responsible for supervising it.

**Market complaint:** "The junior people use AI constantly. I don't know what they're putting in. I don't know if they're sending client data to these tools. And I'm responsible for it."

---

## Cross-Cutting Pattern: The Compound Documentation Debt

Each signal above is a single-point failure. The compound effect is:

1. Attestation created a standard (Signal 1 + 5)
2. Vendor contracts may conflict with professional secrecy (Signal 2)
3. Documentation retention obligations are unknown (Signal 3)
4. Clients are asking directly (Signal 4)
5. Staff AI usage is unsupervised (Signal 6)

**The compound effect:** A Geneva small firm in 2026 is exposed on every axis simultaneously — and no solution in the market addresses the compound nature of the problem.

## What Is Failing in the Market

### Where Solutions Are Failing

| Solution | What They Offer | Why It Fails for Geneva Small Firms |
|---|---|---|
| Bar association guidance | "Follow best practices" | Abstract. No scale-specific protocol. |
| Carrier attestation support | Help completing the form | Doesn't produce the evidence the form assumes exists. |
| General AI governance frameworks | Policy templates | Generic. Not mapped to article 321 CC or Geneva bar standards. |
| AI vendor contracts | Terms of service | Vendor interests vs. firm interests. Inherent conflict. |
| Insurance broker review | Coverage assessment | Coverage != compliance. Coverage gap identification without remediation. |
| Tech vendor solutions | "Our tool is secure" | Self-interested. Doesn't address professional secrecy obligations. |

### Where the Market Gap Lives

- **Geneva-specific:** No solution maps to Geneva professional secrecy law, Geneva bar guidance, and Swiss AI Act obligations simultaneously.
- **Small-firm-scaled:** No solution is designed for a 2–5 attorney firm with no compliance staff and no AI governance budget.
- **Evidence-producing:** No solution produces the documentation that the carrier attestation implies should exist.
- **Ongoing:** No solution addresses the ongoing nature of the obligation — it's not a one-time audit, it's a continuous documentation requirement.
