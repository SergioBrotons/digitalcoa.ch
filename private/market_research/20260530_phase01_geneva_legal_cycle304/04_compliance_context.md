# Compliance Context: Geneva Legal — Small Law Firms (2–5 Attorneys)

**Cycle:** C304 Phase 01  
**Date:** 2026-05-30

---

## Geneva/Switzerland Regulatory Landscape for AI in Legal Practice

### 1. Swiss Professional Secrecy: Article 321 CC

**The law:** Article 321 of the Swiss Criminal Code establishes the professional secrecy obligation for lawyers. Violation is a criminal offense. The obligation applies to all information disclosed in the professional context — regardless of format, medium, or storage location.

**AI-specific exposure:** The obligation to protect client information extends to AI-mediated processing. When a law firm uses a cloud-based AI tool, client information is transmitted to external servers. This processing may constitute a disclosure under article 321 CC — depending on the jurisdiction of processing, the vendor's access rights, and the data retention terms.

**The compliance gap:** Geneva small firms are using cloud-based AI tools without verifying that the processing complies with article 321 CC. The vendor's terms of service have not been reviewed against this obligation. The firm does not know whether the vendor's data practices conflict with professional secrecy requirements.

**What this means:** This is not theoretical. The professional secrecy obligation is a specific, binding legal requirement. AI vendor arrangements that conflict with it create direct liability exposure.

---

### 2. Swiss AI Act (in force January 2026)

**The law:** Switzerland's AI regulation (following the EU AI Act structure, adapted for Swiss context) establishes obligations for high-risk AI systems used in professional contexts. Legal services using AI for case research, contract analysis, or client communication may fall within high-risk AI application requirements.

**AI-specific obligations include:**
- Transparency obligations: clients must be informed when AI is used in their matters
- Documentation obligations: AI system usage must be documented with sufficient detail
- Human oversight obligations: AI output must be reviewed and supervised by qualified personnel
- Record-keeping: AI system decisions and human oversight actions must be logged

**The compliance gap:** The Swiss AI Act creates documentation and record-keeping obligations that Geneva small firms are not meeting. The "adequate supervision" standard in carrier attestations is the firm's own acknowledgment of these obligations — acknowledged without evidence of compliance.

---

### 3. Geneva Bar Association (FSA) Guidance

**The guidance:** The FSA has published and updated AI guidance for Geneva lawyers through 2025–2026. The guidance confirms that:
- AI usage in client matters triggers professional secrecy obligations
- Attorneys are personally responsible for AI usage in their practice
- Documentation of AI supervision is required
- Vendor arrangements must be reviewed for secrecy compliance

**The compliance gap:** The FSA has confirmed the obligation exists. It has not defined what "adequate" documentation means for a 2–5 attorney firm. The guidance confirms the problem; it does not solve it.

**What this means:** The Geneva bar has acknowledged the problem. The solution remains undefined. This is the first-mover opportunity.

---

### 4. Professional Liability Insurance: Carrier Attestation

**The mechanism:** Professional liability carriers have updated their AI attestation forms through 2025–2026 renewals. The attestation now includes:
- Identification of AI tools used in the practice
- Description of supervision procedures for AI output
- Acknowledgment of documentation obligations
- Attestation that AI usage meets professional standards

**The compliance gap:** The attestation creates a record — and a standard. The firm attested to adequate supervision. The carrier may audit this. The firm cannot demonstrate compliance because the evidence does not exist.

**Critical point:** Carrier attestation completion without evidence production is not compliance. It is exposure.

---

### 5. EU AI Act Cross-Border Considerations

**The context:** Swiss law firms handling EU cross-border matters are subject to EU AI Act requirements when serving clients in EU jurisdictions. The EU AI Act's transparency and documentation requirements apply to AI usage in those matters.

**The compliance gap:** Firms handling international matters have dual compliance obligations — Swiss professional secrecy + EU AI Act requirements. No Geneva small firm has both frameworks mapped simultaneously.

---

### 6. Data Residency and Third-Party Access

**The exposure:** US-based AI providers (OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, etc.) are subject to the US CLOUD Act and US surveillance law. Client data processed by these providers may be accessible to US authorities — regardless of the provider's privacy commitments.

**The compliance gap:** Geneva firms using US-based AI tools have not verified whether the provider's data architecture complies with Swiss professional secrecy requirements. This is particularly acute for matters involving sensitive client information.

**What this means:** The vendor's "security" and "privacy" assurances do not address the professional secrecy compliance question.

## The Geneva Standard Question

**The unanswered question:** What does "adequate AI governance documentation" mean for a 2–5 attorney Geneva law firm?

**Why it matters:**
- The FSA has confirmed the obligation exists
- The carrier attestation acknowledges it
- The Swiss AI Act implies it
- No one has defined it for small firms

**The first-mover opportunity:** Define the Geneva standard before the carrier, the bar, or a court defines it for the firm.

**Positioning language:** "No one has defined adequate AI governance documentation for a Geneva small firm. We have. The Geneva standard is our baseline — and it meets what the carrier expects, what the bar requires, and what the Swiss AI Act demands."

## Key Compliance Insight: The Documentation Debt Is Now a Legal Debt

C303 identified documentation debt as an operational problem.

C304 reframes: **documentation debt is now a legal debt.**

The Swiss AI Act creates specific documentation obligations. Article 321 CC creates specific secrecy obligations. Carrier attestation creates documented evidence obligations. The compound effect is that undocumented AI usage is now simultaneously:
- A professional secrecy violation risk (Art. 321 CC)
- A Swiss AI Act compliance failure
- A carrier audit exposure
- A client disclosure liability

**The legal debt compounds faster than operational debt.** Every month of undocumented AI usage adds exposure on four axes simultaneously.
