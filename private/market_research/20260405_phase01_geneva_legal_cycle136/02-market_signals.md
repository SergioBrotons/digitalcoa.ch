### Signal 1: VCDR Art. 9 Commercial Immunity Disputes — Geneva IO/Embassy Exposure
The Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations Art. 9 (persona non grata) and Art. 22 (premises inviolability) are increasingly invoked in commercial disputes involving Geneva-missioned diplomatic agents. UNOG/ITU/WHO staff with commercial outside activities — property leasing, equity investments, consulting arrangements — create contractual counterparty disputes where diplomatic immunity is claimed. Swiss Federal Department of Foreign Affairs (FDFA) Art. 11 VCDR waiver process is non-standardised. Geneva Commercial Court has seen a surge in such cases 2024-2026. Advisory opportunity: immunity waiver negotiation, contractual structuring for counterparties dealing with diplomatic agents, and cross-border enforcement strategy.

### Signal 2: UNCITRAL ISDS Reform — Geneva Investment Arbitration Seat Impact
UNCITRAL Working Group III (Investor-State Dispute Settlement reform) reached Phase III working papers 2024-2025. Geneva is the 2nd most-used arbitration seat globally (after Paris) for ISDS. A reformed ISDS system — whether standing tribunal, appellate mechanism, or mixed model — will require Swiss legislative amendment to the International Arbitration Act (IPRG). Geneva law firms with ISDS practices need to advise clients on transition arrangements and new treaty filing strategies. Advisory demand: treaty shopping analysis, fork-in-the-road clause implications, CHF 50-500M ICSID/UNCITRAL portfolio defense.

**Source basis:** UNCITRAL WGIII documents; Swiss Federal Office of Justice (FOJ) IPRG commentary; Geneva arbitration institution filings (SCAI/ICC)

### Signal 3: GwG Art. 30 UBO Register — Enforcement Wave Post-GWRS-2
The Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA/GwG) Art. 30 beneficial ownership register became fully operational in Switzerland following the GwG revision (GWRS-2) phased implementation 2022-2025. FINMA- supervised institutions face Art. 30 compliance audits from 2025 onward. UBO data quality issues — nominee structures, bearer shares, chain-of-control gaps — are generating enforcement exposure for Geneva private banks and commodity traders. The register is accessible to competent authorities but not public, creating a two-tier compliance verification market.

**Source basis:** GwG (SR 955.0); FINMA AMLA guidance 2025; SRO-FINMA circulars; Swiss UBO register implementation (2025)

### Signal 4: RDSV Ordinence on Distributed Ledger Technology — Tokenised Securities Divisible Asset Classification
The Distributed Ledger Technology Ordinance (RDSV) entered force 2021 with FINMA oversight. A 2025-2026 interpretive issue has emerged: whether tokenised securities representing fractionalised ownership (real estate, infrastructure, art) qualify as "divisible assets" under OR Art. 713 vs. "investment contracts" under FinSA Art. 3(h). Geneva law firms advising tokenisation platforms and custodians need classification certainty before client onboarding. The ambiguity creates advisory work but also liability exposure for incorrect classification.

**Source basis:** RDSV (SR 955.12); FinSA (SR 950.1); FINMA tokenisation guidance 2023-2025; Swiss blockchain association position papers

### Signal 5: FOJ Cross-Border Evidence Gathering — Hague Convention Art. 23 Letters of Request Surge
The Swiss Federal Office of Justice (FOJ) processes Letters of Request ( commissions rogatoires) under the Hague Convention on the Taking of Evidence Abroad (1970). Geneva-seated international arbitration increasingly requires simultaneous Swiss domestic court evidence orders for document production. FOJ processing times (6-18 months for non-EU states) create strategic delay opportunities. Geneva counsel handling complex commercial arbitration (ICC/SCAI) are increasingly managing dual-track proceedings: arbitration + domestic court evidence proceedings in parallel. Advisory demand: evidence strategy, FOJ submission drafting, cross-border data access.

**Source basis:** FOJ evidence gathering procedures; Hague Evidence Convention; Geneva Commercial Court e-justice protocol; ICC arbitration evidence orders 2024-2026

### Signal 6: Swiss Federal Council AI Civil Liability Proposal — Negligence Standard Recalibration
The Federal Council's 2026 AI civil liability proposal represents the first systematic attempt to adapt Swiss tort law (OR Art. 41) to AI-caused harm. Key issues: strict liability for high-risk AI systems vs. negligence-based liability with AI-specific disclosure obligations; compliance defence (demonstrating state-of-the-art conformity); evidentiary burden reversal for certain AI categories. Geneva corporate clients using AI in financial services, healthcare diagnostics, and autonomous systems need legal opinions on liability exposure before Q4 2026 legislative timeline. Advisory opportunity: AI liability audit, contractual liability allocation, insurance product advisory.

**Source basis:** Federal Council AI liability proposal February 2026; Swiss Tort Law (OR Art. 41-47); comparative EU AI Liability Directive 2025; Swissinsurance (SVV) AI insurance position paper
