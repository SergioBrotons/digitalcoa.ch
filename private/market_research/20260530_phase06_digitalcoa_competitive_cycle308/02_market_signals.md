## 1. The Geneva SMB AI Accountability Competitive Landscape

### Who Is in the Market

**Category 1: Generalist Consultancies (Local / Swiss-Focused)**
- Small Geneva-based management consultancies offering "digital transformation" including AI governance
- Claims: Full-service AI governance, compliance, and risk management for Swiss SMBs
- Reality: AI governance as an add-on to digital strategy engagements; not a specialized discipline
- Gap: Generalist framing, no vertical specificity (legal, finance, healthcare), no evidence architecture

**Category 2: Law Firms (Geneva / Zurich)**
- Established law firms offering AI regulatory compliance advice as part of regulatory practices
- Claims: EU AI Act compliance, Swiss regulatory alignment, legal risk mitigation
- Reality: Legal advice on AI regulation, not evidence architecture or documentation systems
- Gap: Produces legal opinions, not operational accountability documentation. Two different products.

**Category 3: Accounting Firms (Big 4 and Mid-Tier)**
- Big 4: AI governance frameworks, audit readiness, regulatory compliance at enterprise scale
- Mid-tier Swiss firms: nFADP compliance, data protection, AI system documentation
- Claims: Compliance frameworks, audit preparation, regulatory risk management
- Reality: Enterprise-grade frameworks designed for large organizations; SMBs get templates, not systems
- Gap: No retroactive reconstruction capability, no broker-facing evidence packages, no vertical specificity (physician, lawyer, consultant)

**Category 4: Insurance Brokers**
- Brokers selling D&O insurance, professional liability, cyber insurance with AI risk components
- Claims: AI risk coverage, carrier attestation support, indemnification assistance
- Reality: Risk transfer products, not evidence architecture. Broker attestation language is generic.
- Gap: Brokers are the channel but not the solution. The attestation form exists; the evidence to fill it does not.

**Category 5: Tech Vendors / SaaS Platforms**
- AI tool vendors (Microsoft Copilot, Google Gemini, Salesforce Einstein) offering "AI governance" within their platforms
- Claims: Built-in AI governance, compliance features, audit trails within the platform
- Reality: Platform-specific logging, not cross-platform evidence architecture. Vendor lock-in disguised as governance.
- Gap: Evidence is siloed within the vendor platform. No portable, carrier-ready documentation.

**Category 6: Professional Associations (FMH, Ordre des Avocats, Expert Suisse)**
- Associations offering guidance on AI use in professional practice
- Claims: Professional standards guidance, ethical frameworks, regulatory updates
- Reality: Position papers and guidance documents, not accountability systems. Voluntary adoption model.
- Gap: No operational evidence architecture, no documentation tools, no carrier-facing output.

**Category 7: Boutique AI Governance Consultancies (International / EU)**
- Small boutique firms specializing in AI governance and accountability (EU-based, some operating in Switzerland)
- Claims: Specialized AI governance, evidence architecture, compliance systems
- Reality: Often too enterprise-focused, too expensive for SMBs, not Geneva-bilingual, no vertical specificity
- Gap: Market exists but pricing and approach designed for larger organizations; SMBs fall through the gap

## 3. The Seven Critical Market Failures

### Failure 1: Frameworks Without Evidence Architecture
Every major competitor offers a framework. None offers a system that produces carrier-ready evidence documentation as an operational output.

**Why this matters:** A framework is a document. Evidence architecture is a system that generates documentation continuously. SMBs with documentation debt need the latter.

### Failure 2: Compliance Theater
"AI compliance" services produce documents that satisfy no specific standard because no specific Swiss standard exists for SMB AI accountability.

**Why this matters:** SMBs pay for compliance services and receive documentation that cannot actually be audited against a specific requirement. The Emperor has no clothes.

### Failure 3: Enterprise Products for SMB Prices
Mid-tier and boutique consultancies sell enterprise governance frameworks at SMB prices, meaning SMBs get a stripped-down version of a product designed for organizations 10-100x their size.

**Why this matters:** The product doesn't fit the client's operational reality. Documentation requirements that make sense for a 500-person organization make no sense for a 5-person medical practice.

### Failure 4: No Retroactive Reconstruction Capability
No competitor in the Geneva market offers a service that addresses retroactive reconstruction — the ability to document AI decisions that were already made without proper documentation at the time.

**Why this matters:** The majority of the market problem is retroactive, not prospective. SMBs didn't build evidence architecture when they adopted AI tools 18 months ago. They need to reconstruct it now.

### Failure 5: Bilingual Gap
No competitor offers bilingual (EN/FR) AI accountability services as a standard product in the Geneva SMB market.

**Why this matters:** Geneva's regulatory environment requires documentation in both official languages. English-only services miss this requirement entirely.

### Failure 6: Broker Channel Blindness
Competitors approach SMBs directly or through digital marketing. None has built a broker-referral system that treats insurance brokers as the primary acquisition channel.

**Why this matters:** The research across C308 phases consistently shows broker referral is the primary acquisition path. Competitors are acquiring clients through the wrong channel.

### Failure 7: Vertical Specificity Absence
No competitor offers one evidence architecture with four vertical overlays (legal, finance, consulting, healthcare) as a single coherent product. Most offer vertical-specific consulting as separate engagements.

**Why this matters:** The documentation debt problem is cross-vertical. SMB principals in legal, finance, consulting, and healthcare all face the same compounding vectors. A single system that speaks to all four verticals is more valuable than four separate consulting engagements.

## 4. The Carrier Attestation Gap

### The Renewal Form Problem

Brokers are requiring AI attestation forms for professional liability renewals. The forms exist. The evidence to complete them does not.

**What brokers require:** Carrier attestation forms asking about AI tool use, supervision protocols, documentation systems, and personal liability exposure.

**What SMBs have:** Nothing. Or generic policy documents that don't answer the specific questions.

**What competitors offer:** Nothing specific. "AI governance frameworks" don't translate to carrier-ready attestation documentation.

### The Gap in Detail

| Carrier Attestation Element | What Form Asks | What SMB Has | What Competitor Provides |
|---|---|---|---|
| AI tool inventory | List AI tools in use | Empty or informal | Platform audit logs (not portable) |
| Human oversight protocol | How do you supervise AI decisions? | Vague policy | Generic governance framework |
| Documentation system | How do you document AI decisions? | Nothing | Compliance checklist |
| Personal liability protection | How are you protected under nFADP Art. 63? | Unknown | Legal opinion (not operational) |
| Retroactive capability | Can you reconstruct AI decisions from past 12 months? | No | Nothing |
| Bilingual documentation | French/English evidence record? | No | English-only templates |

## 5. The Price-to-Value Failure

### What SMBs Pay vs. What They Get

**Current market:** SMBs pay CHF 5,000–25,000 for AI governance consulting engagements that produce:
- A policy document (framework)
- A risk register (inventory)
- A compliance checklist (checklist)
- A training session (awareness)

**What they actually need:** An evidence architecture that produces:
- Carrier-ready attestation documentation
- Retroactive reconstruction capability
- Personal liability protection record
- Bilingual documentation trail
- Vertical-specific accountability system (legal, finance, consulting, healthcare)

**The value gap:** The market is selling frameworks at framework prices when SMBs need evidence systems at evidence system prices. The pricing model doesn't reflect the actual value delivered.

## 6. Six NET-NEW Market Signal Findings for Phase 06

1. **No competitor in the Geneva SMB market has occupied "evidence architecture" as a category.** Every competitor operates in compliance, governance, risk, or framework space. Evidence architecture is unoccupied.

2. **The carrier attestation form is the most underleveraged competitive artifact in the market.** Every competitor ignores it. Sergio's product is the only one that treats the carrier form as the design specification for the evidence architecture.

3. **Broker-referral as primary acquisition channel is acknowledged by zero competitors.** All competitors pursue direct marketing or general consultancy channels. The broker channel is wide open.

4. **Bilingual (EN/FR) AI accountability documentation is not offered by any competitor as a standard product.** This is a systematic gap, not an oversight. The Geneva regulatory complexity requirement has not been addressed.

5. **Retroactive reconstruction as a service category does not exist in the competitive landscape.** Every competitor approaches AI accountability prospectively (building for the future). None addresses the SMB's immediate problem: documenting decisions already made without documentation.

6. **One evidence architecture + four vertical overlays as a single product is not offered by any competitor.** Vertical-specific consulting exists; integrated cross-vertical evidence architecture does not. This is Sergio's product architecture advantage.
