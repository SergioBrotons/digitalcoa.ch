## The AI Accountability Gap: What Your Broker Is Actually Asking For

### What I See Every Week in My Practice

Every week, a physician, a lawyer, a consultant, or an accounting professional sits across from me and says the same thing:

*"I got a form from my insurance broker. It's asking about AI. I don't know what they're asking for, and I don't have the documentation to answer it."*

This is the AI accountability gap. And it is not a technology problem.

It is a documentation problem.

---

## The Problem Nobody Talked About Until Now

For the past 18 months, you have been using AI tools in your practice. Maybe you use it for research, for drafting, for analysis, for client communication. You adopted it because it made sense. It was fast, it was useful, it was available.

But you adopted it without a documentation system. No one told you that you needed one. No one told you that every AI-assisted decision you make needs to be documented in a way that can be reconstructed later.

Now the documentation debt is coming due.

**Here is what the documentation debt looks like:**
- Your broker is asking: "How do you supervise AI-assisted decisions?" You don't have a documented answer.
- Your carrier is asking: "Can you reconstruct AI decisions made in the past 12 months?" You cannot.
- Your professional body is asking: "How do you ensure human oversight of AI in your practice?" You have a vague policy, not an evidence system.
- Personal liability exposure under Swiss data protection law (nFADP Art. 63) is now real for principals who cannot document AI oversight.

The documentation debt is not a future problem. It is a present problem with a specific deadline: your next renewal.

---

## Why the Market Offers Frameworks, and Why That Is Not What You Need

The market has responded to AI accountability by offering frameworks. Policy documents. Checklists. Governance structures.

I have reviewed dozens of these frameworks. They are written by consultants who have never had to complete an insurance attestation form. They are designed to look impressive in a presentation, not to produce evidence that survives carrier scrutiny.

**What the market offers:** A document that says "we have an AI governance policy."

**What you need:** A system that produces evidence of every AI-assisted decision you make, documented in a way that can be reconstructed, that satisfies your carrier attestation, that protects your personal liability, and that operates in both French and English because that is how Geneva works.

These are two different products. The market is selling the first. You need the second.

## The Evidence Standard Shift: What Changed and Why It Matters

Until 2024, AI accountability meant having a policy. "We have an AI use policy" was sufficient evidence of compliance.

Starting in 2024, the standard changed. Regulators, carriers, and professional bodies now require records — documented evidence of specific decisions made, not general policies in place.

This is the evidence standard shift. It is structural, not cyclical. The old standard is gone permanently.

**What this means for you:** If you are still operating with only an AI policy document, you are not in compliance with current evidence standards. The policy tells me you have thought about AI. The record tells me what you actually did.

Your broker is asking for records. I build systems that produce records.

## 6 NET-NEW Lead Magnet Findings for Phase 06

1. **The lead magnet must lead with the broker trigger, not the regulatory risk.** SMB principals respond to concrete trigger moments (renewal form arrived) more than abstract regulatory concepts. Lead with the form, then explain why the form matters.

2. **"Evidence architecture" must be the core term, not "compliance" or "governance."** These are occupied territories that trigger wrong mental models. Evidence architecture is unoccupied and precise.

3. **Personal liability framing (nFADP Art. 63) must be explicit in the lead magnet.** SMB principals need to understand this is personal risk, not corporate compliance. The word "personal" must appear early.

4. **Retroactive reconstruction must be named explicitly as a capability.** SMBs don't know that this is an option — they assume they're stuck with their documentation debt. Naming retroactive reconstruction opens a door they didn't know existed.

5. **One evidence architecture / four verticals must be explained clearly.** The "not four products, one system" framing is distinctive and must be explained: different overlays for different professions, one underlying architecture.

6. **The first conversation offer must be specific and concrete.** "Show me your carrier form" is more effective than "let's discuss your AI governance needs." It creates specificity and demonstrates expertise simultaneously.
