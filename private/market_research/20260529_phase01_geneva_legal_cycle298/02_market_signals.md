# Market Signals: Geneva Small Law Firm — Phase 01 C298

## Current Problems and Complaints

### 1. Carrier Attestation Processing Panic
Geneva small law firms are receiving PI carrier AI attestation questionnaires at renewal. The forms are written by underwriting teams with terms like "adequate AI governance controls" — undefined, firm-specific, personally signed. The managing partner signs because the alternative is non-renewal. The signature creates a documented personal commitment to an undefined standard with no prior review pathway available in Geneva. Firms that attempted to build documentation retroactively produced evidence that misrepresents their actual practice — a self-incriment pattern now visible even to firms that haven't yet signed.

### 2. Vendor Contract Terms Accepted Without Evaluation
Click-through vendor contracts for AI tools used in client matters include data processing provisions for model training, benchmarking, and third-party sub-processors. Small firms accepted terms to access core functionality. The provisions activate nFADP Art.9 processor obligations the firm may not know it has. The vendor may subsequently be acquired, share data with affiliates, or experience a breach — and the firm's processor obligations under Article 9 remain operative regardless of awareness. The contractual acceptance is the operative event; the firm's understanding of what it accepted is irrelevant to the statutory exposure.

### 3. Shadow AI Accumulation Below Partner Visibility
Associates at small firms have adopted AI tools outside approved vendor processes — sometimes using personal accounts, sometimes using tools embedded in platforms the firm subscribed to before AI became a concern. The aggregate AI surface of the firm is undocumented and likely larger than any partner can enumerate. The attestation form that asks about firm-wide AI governance effectively asks the signing partner to certify to an AI surface they cannot fully describe. Individual tool selection decisions have already been made; the governance decision never happened as a single event.

### 4. Documentation Window Awareness Arriving Too Late
Firms are beginning to recognize that the documentation window for AI governance decisions has a time dimension — decisions made before a certain point cannot be retroactively documented with the same evidentiary weight as contemporaneous documentation. Some firms realize this window may already be closed for decisions made during 2023-2024 peak AI adoption, when no governance framework existed and documentation instincts hadn't formed. The retrospective reconstruction problem is now understood as structurally impossible, not just practically difficult.

### 5.BAR Guidance Vacuum Creating Autonomous Decision-Making Under Uncertainty
Swiss Bar Association (SAV/SBA) published general AI guidance. Geneva firms find it too general for Monday-morning decisions. The Ordre des Avocats de Genève has published nothing Geneva-specific. Firms are making AI governance decisions — which tools to approve, how to document AI use in client matters, what to disclose to clients — without cantonal guidance calibrated to 2-5 attorney operations. The vacuum is not experienced as an abstract gap; it is experienced as a decision that must be made without reference point, where the cost of a wrong decision is personal and the feedback loop arrives only when something goes wrong.

### 6. Professional Secrecy Channel Integrity Question Emerging
 Firms are beginning to question whether their AI research channels constitute confidential communication channels under Art.321 CP. Using a public AI platform for client matter research raises the question of whether that channel is privileged, regardless of server location. "Swiss-hosted" labeling addresses data residency, not the legal qualification of the communication channel itself. Some firms are aware this question exists; most are operating without having asked it.

### 7. AI Tool Migration and Active Matter Continuity
 Firms that switched AI vendors, discontinued tools, or had tools auto-updated are discovering that active client matters may contain outputs or analyses produced under AI tool states that no longer exist. The deliverable is in the client file; the analytical framework that produced it may be technically unreproducible. Client challenges to recommendations or analyses cannot be answered by reconstructing what AI version was active when. Attorney-client privilege and professional secrecy protections require demonstrable professional authorship — a standard current AI-assisted deliverables may not satisfy.

### 8. PI Insurance Coverage Architecture Opacity
 Small firms are uncertain whether their existing PI policies respond to AI-assisted errors in client matters. Carriers have not proactively disclosed AI coverage positions. Policy language that predates AI adoption was not written to cover AI-assisted delivery. When a carrier asks about AI governance during renewal, the firm is simultaneously creating documentation about AI use AND potentially affecting coverage terms it doesn't understand. No Geneva broker or insurer has published clear AI coverage position language for small law firms.

## Failure Patterns Observed in Market

- **Retroactive documentation scramble** — building AI governance records after carrier form arrives, producing compliance-looking materials that don't represent actual practice
- **Vendor-term acceptance as governance substitute** — subscribing to a "legal-specific" AI platform and treating vendor marketing as equivalent to firm's own governance documentation
- **Single-signature adequacy certification** — partner signing carrier attestation without independent analysis, creating documented personal commitment to an undefined standard
- **Shadow AI bypass of governance** — associates using AI tools outside approved stack, making firm's governance documentation incomplete relative to actual AI surface
- **Training as documentation substitute** — attending AI vendor webinars and treating attendance records as evidence of adequate AI governance
- **"Swiss-hosted" as privilege protection** — treating data residency as equivalent to the legal protection of attorney-client privilege for AI research channels
