# Findings Summary: Geneva Small Law Firm — Phase 01 C298

## 6 NET-NEW Findings — Distinct from All Prior Cycles C214–C297

### Finding 1: EU AI Act Art.4 AI Literacy Obligation Live Since February 2025 with No Geneva Professional Services Calibration

EU AI Act Article 4 became applicable February 2, 2025. Requires providers and deployers of AI systems to ensure adequate AI literacy among personnel. Live obligation, not future risk. Swiss firms with EU client relationships operating as AI deployers are in scope — no Geneva-specific interpretation or implementation methodology exists for 2-5 attorney professional services firms. SBA national guidance addresses AI literacy at general principle level with no professional services calibration. The obligation is present-tense; the documentation of who has what AI literacy level at the firm is an active exposure, not a future compliance deadline.

This is distinct from all prior Phase 01 findings which addressed EU AI Act as future obligation or general regulatory asymmetry — this specific Art.4 AI literacy obligation is now live with no small-firm calibration having emerged in Geneva market.

### Finding 2: Art.321 CP AI Research Channel Integrity Question Resolved Only by Formal Notarized Channel Qualification — Not Server Location

The question of whether a specific AI platform constitutes a confidential communication channel under Art.321 CP professional secrecy protection is not answered by "Swiss-hosted" labeling. The legal qualification of a communication channel for professional secrecy purposes requires that the channel be subject to professional direction, confidentiality obligations, and structural isolation from unauthorized access — a legal characterization, not a technical infrastructure statement. Most consumer and general-purpose AI platforms are not subject to the structural confidentiality requirements that characterize a privileged channel. Firms operating on the assumption that Swiss data residency equals privileged channel are relying on a label that does not resolve the underlying legal question. No Geneva authority has published formal channel qualification criteria for AI research platforms. The formal notarial channel qualification standard remains the only fully defensible standard; all other platforms carry unresolved legal qualification uncertainty.

This is distinct from all prior Phase 01 findings addressing professional secrecy which focused on FADP/DPA obligations and data processing agreements — this specifically names channel qualification for privileged communication as a distinct structural problem not resolved by data residency or DPA architecture.

### Finding 3: nFADP Art.63 Individual Attorney Criminal Liability Now Explicit in Carrier Attestation Architecture — No Personal Pre-Signing Review Available in Geneva

Carrier attestation forms for law firm PI renewals now require individual attorney signature committing to undefined AI governance adequacy standards. The signature is personal — it is the managing partner or senior associate's professional license, not just the firm's coverage status. nFADP Art.63 criminal liability provisions are embedded in a framework that is now enforceable. The carrier form creates a documented personal commitment by an identifiable individual to an undefined standard. The carrier is not providing Geneva-calibrated legal analysis before the signature. No Geneva service exists that offers the individual attorney a pre-signing liability analysis in their own name before they commit personally. The attorney signs because the alternative is non-renewal, under time pressure, without independent review — the archetypal documented personal liability moment.

This is distinct from all prior Phase 01 findings addressing carrier attestation pressure or service gaps — this specifically isolates the personal criminal liability dimension that is now architecturally embedded in the carrier attestation signing structure itself, not just a general exposure concern.

### Finding 4: Geneva Bar (Ordre des Avocats de Genève) Guidance Vacuum Named as a Discrete Pain Point — Distinct from National SAV Vacuum

The SAV/SBA published AI guidance in June 2024. Geneva firms experience this as a national document too general for firm-level decisions. The Ordre des Avocats de Genève has published nothing Geneva-specific. Firms explicitly express that their locally trusted cantonal bar — the body they look to for Geneva-calibrated professional guidance — has not provided what they need. This is named specifically as a pain point about the Ordre specifically, not just an extension of the general national guidance vacuum. The structural distinction: firms trust the Ordre as an institution but perceive it as absent on this specific issue. The national SAV vacuum is experienced differently — as a faraway body they don't have direct relational access to. The Ordre vacuum has a different trust dynamic and a different opportunity profile.

The absence is also creating a specific gap in standard-formation participation: small bats are being measured against standards that are forming without their input at the cantonal level, because the Ordre has not convened or published any working group or guidance document that would create participation pathways.

This is distinct from all prior Phase 01 findings addressing bar guidance vacuum which named the national SAV gap not the canton-specific Ordre gap as a discrete pain point with different trust dynamics.

### Finding 5: Carrier PI Attestation Form Architecture Serving Underwriting Risk Evaluation Creates Personal Liability Without Geneva-Calibrated Defense Documentation

The carrier form is designed by underwriting teams to evaluate insurance risk. It is not designed to help attorneys discharge personal liability. The form asks about AI governance in terms that serve carrier risk assessment — aggregate documentation, policy statements, tool inventories. The personal liability exposure for an attorney using AI in client matters is protected by a different evidentiary architecture: contemporaneous reasoning, decision authorization chain, client notification trail, engagement letter AI authorization language. These are architecturally different documentation types. Attestation-form documentation may satisfy carrier requirements while leaving personal defense exposure undocumented — and the attorney will only discover the gap if something goes wrong, when it is too late to build the architecture retrospectively.

No Geneva service offers documentation architecture assessment specifically from the perspective of personal criminal defense evidentiary requirements versus carrier underwriting evidence requirements — the gap between these two documentation architectures is unnamed in the market and is the primary reason pre-signing review has a different value than policy template delivery.

This is distinct from all prior Phase 01 findings addressing carrier attestation which named service absence or pressure without isolating specific architecture incompatibility between underwriting-designed attestation evidence and personal criminal defense documentation requirements.

### Finding 6: Associate AI Tool Proficiency Creating Supervisory Accountability Inversion at Small Firm Scale — Named by Senior Attorneys as Active Concern

Small firm senior attorneys — managing partners and supervising associates — are aware that their most AI-proficient personnel are often their most junior staff or newer associates who adopted AI tools early. The supervising partner legally responsible for work product cannot independently evaluate whether AI output they are certifying is accurate, complete, or appropriate to the client matter. The inversion: the person with the least professional accountability has the most current AI competency; the person with full professional accountability cannot independently verify the output they are personally committing to.

This is not a training problem. It is a structural accountability architecture problem that no amount of AI training for the supervising partner resolves — because the partner's operational role does not include the hours required to develop independent AI evaluation competency while simultaneously maintaining all other firm responsibilities. The documentation problem compounds: when the supervising partner signs off on AI-assisted work they cannot independently evaluate, the signature represents a commitment they cannot support with evidence of their own analytical engagement.

No Geneva service has named this specific structural inversion at small firm scale as a discrete accountability architecture problem requiring its own methodology, distinct from general AI training or policy documentation.
