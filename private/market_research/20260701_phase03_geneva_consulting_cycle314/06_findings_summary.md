### Finding 01: Article 3 Deployer Status — The Named-Individual Deliverable Accountability

**The finding:** The Geneva boutique consulting senior partner has been operating on the assumption that the EU AI Act deployer status applies to firms, not to individuals. It does not — at least not in the consulting vertical. Article 3 defines a deployer as "a natural or legal person using an AI system under its authority in a professional capacity." For the consulting firm, the deployer is the named individual whose name is on the deliverable, who signed the engagement letter, who conducts the kickoff call with the EU client, who is personally accountable under nFADP Art.63, who is the named insured under the carrier attestation. The named individual is the senior partner. The deployer status attaches to the senior partner by name, on the deliverable cover page, in the engagement letter, in the carrier attestation, in the FDPIC filing. The geographic test is the location of the affected person — the EU client's investment committee, the EU client's C-suite, the EU client's project sponsor — not the location of the consulting firm. The consulting senior partner who delivers AI-assisted methodology to an EU client is a deployer under Article 3 — by virtue of the deliverable being on an EU client engagement, not by virtue of the consulting firm being located in Geneva. The exposure is named. The deadline is in late August 2026. The senior partner does not know this. The carrier attestation does not address it. nFADP Art.63 does not address it. The EU AI Act addresses it — and the senior partner has not read it.

**Mechanism:** Geneva boutique consulting senior partner serves EU corporate client → uses AI on the client deliverable (market sizing, regulatory mapping, risk assessment, methodology development, implementation roadmap) → AI use affects EU natural persons (corporate client's investment committee, C-suite, project sponsor) → senior partner meets EU AI Act deployer definition for the engagement → Article 3 applicability triggered by named deliverable → Article 4, 14, 26, 50 obligations apply → late August 2026 deadline creates enforcement exposure → EU corporate client asks for documented readiness in RFP EU AI Act section → senior partner cannot produce deliverable-specific applicability memo → Article 26 deployer obligations attach to senior partner by name across the named deliverable.

**Implication:** Article 3 deployer applicability memo (consulting vertical) is a discrete, named C314 P03 service. It applies Article 3 and Article 6(3) to the consulting firm's specific client engagements. It identifies which engagements are in scope by named deliverable, which AI uses are high-risk under Article 6(3) (narrow procedural task, improves result of previously completed human activity, detects decision-making patterns, performs preparatory task), which are limited-risk, which are out of scope. It is dated, senior-partner-signed, and engagement-specific. It is the first deliverable the EU corporate client reads in the EU AI Act section of the RFP response. The first Geneva boutique consulting service to publish the Article 3 deployer applicability memo template (consulting vertical) owns the C314 P03 EU corporate client RFP category.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You serve EU corporate clients. You use AI on the deliverables. Article 3 of the EU AI Act defines deployer by use, not by location. The named individual on the deliverable is the deployer. The named individual is you. The deadline is in 30 days. I'm producing your applicability memo — which engagements are in scope, which deliverables are high-risk, which deliverables name you as deployer — before August."

### Finding 02: Sub-Consultant AI Authorization Chain Under Article 26 — The Permanent Deployer Extension

**The finding:** The Geneva boutique consulting senior partner who engages a sub-consultant has the most structurally permanent AI authorization chain gap in the consulting market. Sub-consultants are external contractors, not employees. The senior partner authorizes the sub-consultant's engagement through the engagement letter signature. The senior partner does NOT authorize the sub-consultant's AI tools. The sub-consultant uses AI on the client deliverable — drafting, analysis, research, modeling, methodology development — without the consulting boutique's authorization or awareness. EU AI Act Article 26 deployer obligations explicitly extend to monitoring AI systems "under [the deployer's] authority." The sub-consultant's AI use is under the senior partner's authority by virtue of the engagement letter. The sub-consultant's AI use is invisible to the senior partner by virtue of the absence of AI-specific authorization. Article 4's literacy obligation explicitly extends to "any other person handling the operation and use of AI systems on its behalf." The sub-consultant is such a person. The handover window is permanent — sub-consultant engagement has no employment-cycle boundary (legal) or mandate-cycle boundary (finance). Sub-consultants turn over monthly for some Geneva boutique consulting firms. The senior partner's Article 26 deployer obligations attach to sub-consultant AI use on every client deliverable. The senior partner's Article 4 literacy obligation extends to every sub-consultant's staff. The senior partner cannot personally train every sub-consultant. The delegation of literacy training re-creates the Article 4 gap in a new form. The exposure is structural. The senior partner has not recognized it. The EU AI Act EU corporate client RFP response forces the senior partner to recognize it.

**Mechanism:** Senior partner engages sub-consultant on EU client engagement (e.g., regulatory workstream) → sub-consultant activates their own AI tools on client methodology (ChatGPT, Claude, internal sub-consultant LLM wrappers) → senior partner unaware of specific tool use, sub-processor chain, or training data → sub-consultant delivers AI-assisted deliverable under senior partner's name → EU corporate client asks for sub-consultant AI authorization chain documentation in RFP EU AI Act section → senior partner cannot produce → Article 26 deployer obligations cover an undocumented chain → Article 4 literacy obligation covers untrained sub-consultant staff → exposure: Article 26 enforcement, Article 4 enforcement, nFADP Art.63 personal liability for sub-consultant data processing, carrier attestation inaccuracy, client contractual breach.

**Implication:** Sub-consultant AI authorization chain documentation system is a discrete, named C314 P03 service. It produces: an engagement letter addendum template specifying sub-consultant AI tool authorization, AI literacy requirement extension, supervision architecture, and disengagement documentation; a sub-consultant AI tool disclosure form capturing which tools the sub-consultant uses on each engagement; a per-engagement authorization record dated and signed by both the senior partner and the sub-consultant; a sub-consultant supervision architecture for ongoing monitoring; sub-consultant disengagement documentation for work product retention and AI processing continuity. The system is refreshed at the rate of sub-consultant engagement book change — not annual attestation cadence. The first Geneva boutique consulting service to publish the sub-consultant AI authorization chain documentation system template owns the C314 P03 sub-consultant AI deployer extension category.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You authorized your sub-consultant's engagement. You didn't authorize their AI tools. The deliverable goes to the EU client under your name. Article 26 deployer obligations apply to the AI use under your authority. Article 4 literacy obligation extends to your sub-consultant's staff. The handover window is permanent — sub-consultants don't have employment cycles or mandate cycles. They turn over monthly. I'm documenting the sub-consultant AI authorization chain — engagement letter addendum, per-engagement authorization record, supervision architecture, disengagement documentation — before August."

The AI summarization tool (ChatGPT, Claude, NotebookLM, internal firm LLM wrappers) processes the transcript and generates meeting notes. The AI analysis tool processes the notes and generates methodology insights. The deliverable is produced from the AI-processed content. The client methodology — confidential strategy, market positioning, operational details, regulatory posture — has flowed through three AI tools before reaching the deliverable stage. Each AI tool has its own vendor terms, its own sub-processor chain, its own data retention policy, its own licensing terms. The senior partner is the data controller under nFADP for the engagement. The senior partner is personally named under nFADP Art.63. The senior partner has authorized the AI tools — or has not authorized them but is accountable for them. The Art.63 personal exposure now extends to every documented client conversation that flowed through AI. The exposure is novel. The senior partner has not operationalized client consent for AI processing of conversation content. The AI tool sub-processor chains are unreviewed. The AI processing of client methodology is invisible to the client in the engagement letter. The Art.63 personal exposure travels with the senior partner to the next engagement, the next RFP cycle, the next carrier attestation. The exposure is now visible in the EU client's RFP EU AI Act section.

**Mechanism:** Senior partner conducts kickoff call with EU client (e.g., EU corporate investment committee) → Otter transcribes call → ChatGPT summarizes transcript → summary becomes basis for methodology development → methodology becomes basis for AI-assisted deliverable → client methodology (strategy, positioning, operational details) flows through three AI tools without documented client consent → senior partner is data controller under nFADP → senior partner is personally named under Art.63 → AI processing of client methodology is a novel Art.63 personal liability vector → EU client RFP asks for documented client conversation protocol → senior partner cannot produce → Art.63 personal exposure visible in EU client's procurement file.

**Implication:** Documented client conversation protocol is a discrete, named C314 P03 service. It produces: client consent language for AI processing of conversation content (engagement letter addendum + meeting confirmation); AI tool sub-processor chain disclosure for the conversation processing tools; nFADP Art.63 personal liability framing tied to the documented conversation; AI tool configuration requirements (zero-retention mode, privacy-preserving mode, conversation data deletion triggers, sub-processor restriction); per-meeting documentation record with named AI tools used, named sub-processor chain, and named senior partner accountability. The first Geneva boutique consulting service to publish the documented client conversation protocol template owns the C314 P03 client conversation Art.63 category.

### Finding 04: Article 50 Deliverable Transparency — The Disclosure That Changes the Commercial Conversation

**The finding:** EU AI Act Article 50 requires deployers of AI systems to inform natural persons that they are interacting with an AI system, where this is not obvious from the perspective of a reasonably well-informed natural person exercising due care. For the consulting senior partner, the "interaction" is the deliverable. The deliverable does not currently disclose AI use. The deliverable has historically been presented as the senior partner's methodology, the senior partner's judgment, the senior partner's expertise. After August 2026, undisclosed AI use in the deliverable is a violation. The senior partner must now disclose AI use in the deliverable. The disclosure changes the perceived value of the deliverable. The client paid for the senior partner's methodology, the senior partner's reputation, the senior partner's named expertise — not for AI output. The disclosure reveals that AI was a significant contributor. The commercial conversation changes. The client will ask "why am I paying the senior partner rate if AI did the work." The consulting firm that discloses faces the value proposition challenge. The consulting firm that does not disclose faces the August 2026 violation. The consulting firm that discloses generically ("we use AI tools") does not satisfy Article 50's "reasonably well-informed natural person exercising due care" standard. The consulting firm that discloses specifically ("we use ChatGPT to draft market sizing analyses with named human review at specific checkpoints") does satisfy Article 50 — and triggers the value proposition challenge.

**Mechanism:** Senior partner delivers AI-assisted methodology to EU client → EU AI Act Article 50 requires disclosure of AI use in the deliverable → senior partner must add disclosure language to engagement letter, deliverable cover page, methodology documentation → specific disclosure ("ChatGPT for market sizing with named human review at checkpoints") satisfies Article 50 → EU client reads disclosure → EU client questions value proposition (senior partner rate for AI-assisted work) → EU client either accepts disclosure with adjusted engagement economics OR rejects engagement on value proposition grounds → senior partner faces commercial conversation change → senior partner who does not disclose faces August 2026 violation → senior partner who discloses generically does not satisfy Article 50 → Article 50 transparency obligation is structurally entangled with the consulting value proposition in a way that does not exist for legal (matters, bar discipline) or finance (mandates, FINMA).

**Implication:** Article 50 deliverable transparency language is a discrete, named C314 P03 service. It produces: engagement letter addendum with AI use disclosure language; deliverable cover page AI use disclosure language; methodology documentation explaining AI contribution to the deliverable; reproducibility disclosure explaining the deliverable's AI dependency and the conditions under which the deliverable is reproducible. The language is consulting-specific — the deliverable is the unit of accountability, not the firm. The language satisfies EU AI Act Article 50, nFADP data subject information, and client contractual confidentiality in one integrated language set. The first Geneva boutique consulting service to publish the Article 50 deliverable transparency language template owns the C314 P03 deliverable disclosure category.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Article 50 requires informing the EU client that AI was used in the deliverable when it is not obvious. The deliverable is the interaction. The disclosure changes the value proposition — the client paid for your methodology, not for AI. Generic disclosure ('we use AI') doesn't satisfy Article 50. Specific disclosure ('ChatGPT for market sizing with named human review at specific checkpoints') does — and triggers the value proposition conversation. I'm producing the Article 50 deliverable transparency language — engagement letter addendum, deliverable cover page, methodology disclosure, reproducibility disclosure — before August."

## C314 P03 Core Synthesis

**The market has moved from "Your client just asked. The deadline is in 30 days." (C314 P01 legal) through "My institutional client just asked for EU AI Act readiness. The deadline is in 30 days. I am the answer they need, and I don't have it." (C314 P02 finance) to "My EU client just asked who is responsible for the AI use on this engagement, by name. The deadline is in 30 days. The answer is me. And I cannot produce the documentation that proves it." (C314 P03 consulting).**

C314 P01 captured the legal vertical's pre-deadline readiness gap through the bar + carrier + EU AI Act three-regime stack. C314 P02 captured the finance vertical's pre-deadline readiness gap through the nFADP + FINMA + EU AI Act + carrier four-regime stack applied to institutional mandates. C314 P03 captures the consulting vertical's pre-deadline deployer accountability gap through the EU AI Act + nFADP + carrier + client contractual + FINMA-adjacent five-regime stack applied to the named deliverable, the named sub-consultant, and the documented client conversation.

The C314 P03 ICP is structurally similar to the C314 P01 and P02 ICPs (all face the EU AI Act 30-day window, all have an external inquiry driving the urgency, all operate in a multi-regime context without named deliverables), but with three critical differences that make the consulting vertical specifically acute:

1. **The unit of accountability is the deliverable, not the firm.** The consulting senior partner's name is on the deliverable. The EU AI Act deployer status attaches to the named individual, not the firm. The senior partner cannot delegate deployer obligations to operations or compliance. The named deliverable is the named individual's personal liability vector. Legal has matters (case, transaction); finance has mandates (institutional client mandate); consulting has deliverables (named deliverable, named senior partner). The deliverable accountability is unique to consulting.

2. **The sub-consultant is the permanent AI authorization gap.** Sub-consultant engagement has no employment-cycle boundary (legal) or mandate-cycle boundary (finance). Sub-consultants turn over monthly for some Geneva boutique consulting firms. EU AI Act Article 26 deployer obligations extend to AI use "under [the deployer's] authority," which the sub-consultant's AI use is. Article 4 literacy obligation extends to "any other person handling the operation and use of AI systems on [the firm's] behalf," which the sub-consultant is. The sub-consultant AI authorization chain is the most structurally permanent gap in the consulting AI chain. The handover window never closes.

3. **The documented client conversation is a new personal liability vector.** AI transcription + summarization + analysis of client conversations creates a novel nFADP Art.63 personal liability vector that did not exist before AI transcription tools became pervasive in 2023-2024.
