# Competitive Analysis: Geneva Consulting — C314 Phase 03

## What Exists in Market vs. What Is Missing — EU AI Act 30-Day Window, Applied to the Consulting Vertical

---

## Overview

The Geneva boutique consulting AI accountability service market in July 2026 contains exactly five categories of provider. None of them produces the C314 P03 service for the consulting vertical. The C314 P03 service is the 30-day EU AI Act deployer readiness pack that addresses six specific deliverables — Article 3 deployer applicability memo (consulting vertical), sub-consultant AI authorization chain documentation system, documented client conversation protocol, Article 50 deliverable transparency language, FINMA-adjacent AI governance pack, and peer EU AI Act readiness gap analysis — for the Geneva boutique consulting senior partner serving EU corporate clients with sub-consultant engagement structures.

This document maps what exists, where each provider category fails to deliver the C314 P03 service, and what the gap looks like from the consulting senior partner's seat. The analysis is structurally similar to the C314 P02 (finance) competitive analysis in form, but the substance is distinct — the consulting senior partner's gaps are different from the WM MD's gaps because the consulting firm has sub-consultants, the deliverable is the unit of accountability (not the firm), and the EU AI Act Section is now structural in the client RFP (not just the institutional DDQ).

## Provider Category 1 — Tier-1 Strategy Consultancies (Big Four Advisory + MBB) — Out-of-Category

### What exists

McKinsey, BCG, Bain (MBB), the Big Four advisory arms (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG), Accenture, and the European tier-1 strategy boutiques (Roland Berger, Oliver Wyman, Kearney, Simon-Kucher) have published EU AI Act readiness frameworks, deployer applicability analyses, AI literacy programs, and AI governance consulting practices. These are enterprise-grade frameworks designed for tier-1 consultancy clients with multi-million-euro engagement budgets and dedicated AI governance teams.

### Where they fail for the Geneva boutique consulting firm

- **Scale mismatch.** The frameworks assume tier-1 consultancy organizational structure — AI steering committee, chief AI officer, dedicated AI ethics team, multi-region AI governance practice. The Geneva boutique consulting firm has a senior partner, an operations manager (often part-time), and an associate who handles AI vendor questions.
- **Documentation language is enterprise-formatted.** The frameworks produce 200-page reports with multi-tier governance diagrams and methodology certifications. The EU corporate client's RFP expects a 5-page EU AI Act section response with named senior partner attestation. The enterprise document does not produce what the EU client asks for.
- **Pricing model is tier-1.** The frameworks are bundled into multi-engagement transformation programs at six- and seven-figure price points. The Geneva boutique consulting firm cannot afford them and does not need them. The EU corporate client that uses a tier-1 consultancy is a tier-1 client, not a boutique consulting client.
- **They are not for sale as a service to the boutique.** The tier-1 consultancies offer these frameworks to their own clients, not to independent Geneva boutique consultancies. The boutique cannot buy what the tier-1 produces internally.
- **The frameworks are not Geneva-specific for the consulting vertical.** They are designed for EU-domiciled or US-domiciled tier-1 consultancies under direct EU AI Act or US AI governance jurisdiction. The Geneva boutique consulting firm operates under nFADP Art.63 + EU AI Act Article 26 + carrier attestation + client contractual confidentiality. The tier-1 frameworks do not address the consulting-specific sub-consultant AI authorization chain or the documented client conversation protocol.

### Gap they leave

The tier-1 consultancies do not serve the independent Geneva boutique consulting firm market. They serve their own tier-1 corporate clients. The boutique consulting firm is structurally outside their addressable market. They produce nothing the boutique can buy.

## Provider Category 2 — Geneva-Based Compliance and Regulatory Consultancies

### What exists

Geneva-based compliance consultancies (Big Four advisory local offices, mid-tier Swiss regulatory consultancies, FDPIC-exit consultants, ISO/IEC 42001 implementation specialists) offer nFADP compliance reviews, operational resilience assessments, ISO/IEC 42001 AI management system implementations, and FINMA-adjacent regulatory reviews. Some have started offering EU AI Act advisory services since 2024.

### Where they fail for the Geneva boutique consulting firm

- **Generic ISO/IEC 42001 framing.** The consultancies frame AI governance as an enterprise AI management system, modeled on the ISO/IEC 42001 framework. The boutique consulting firm does not need an AI management system — it needs a deployer accountability documentation system that satisfies EU AI Act Article 26 + nFADP Art.63 + carrier attestation + client contractual confidentiality in 30 days.
- **No EU AI Act deployer-specific deliverable for consulting.** The consultancies address EU AI Act from the perspective of an EU-domiciled or US-domiciled institution. They do not produce an Article 3 deployer applicability memo for a Swiss-domiciled boutique consulting firm serving EU corporate clients with sub-consultant engagement structures. They do not address the consulting-specific named-individual deployer accountability.
- **No sub-consultant AI authorization chain methodology.** The consultancies do not address the consulting-specific sub-consultant AI authorization chain. Their frameworks assume employee-based organizational structures. Sub-consultant engagements are external contractor relationships with no employment-cycle boundary. The consultancies do not produce documentation systems for this structure.
- **No nFADP Art.63 personal liability documentation.** The consultancies address nFADP at the firm level (data controller obligations). They do not produce Art.63 personal-liability documentation naming the consulting senior partner as the responsible person across the named deliverable. The Art.63 personal layer tied to the deliverable is not their deliverable.
- **No documented client conversation protocol.** The consultancies do not produce AI transcription + AI summarization consent language for client meetings. The documented client conversation as a personal liability vector is not in their framework.
- **No Article 50 deliverable transparency language.** The consultancies do not produce engagement letter AI use disclosure language or deliverable cover page AI use disclosure language. The deliverable-as-disclosure-vector is not in their framework.
- **No FINMA-adjacent trigger recognition.** The consultancies do not recognize that the consulting firm is exposed to FINMA Circular 2026/03 §7.4 through the fintech client channel by adjacency. The fintech-adjacent trigger is not in their framework.
- **No carrier attestation integration.** The consultancies do not coordinate their AI governance deliverables with the carrier attestation. The carrier asks for AI oversight documentation; the consultancy produces it independently; the consulting firm must reconcile them manually.
- **Long implementation timelines.** The consultancies quote 6–12 month engagements for AI governance implementation, modeled on enterprise ISO/IEC 42001 timelines. The boutique consulting firm has 30 days.
- **Pricing model is engagement-based.** CHF 30,000–100,000+ for a multi-month engagement. The boutique consulting firm's procurement decision is for a single named deliverable in 30 days.

### Gap they leave

The Geneva-based compliance consultancies produce generic AI governance documentation at enterprise pricing and timeline, with no consulting-specific deliverables (no sub-consultant AI authorization chain, no documented client conversation protocol, no Article 50 deliverable transparency language, no FINMA-adjacent trigger recognition). The boutique consulting firm cannot buy the C314 P03 service from them because the C314 P03 service does not exist in their offering.

## Provider Category 3 — AI Governance Software Vendors

### What exists

AI governance software vendors (OneTrust, TrustArc, Collibra, Securiti, Credo AI, Holistic AI, Fairly AI) offer AI governance platforms with policy templates, risk assessment modules, audit log functionality, AI literacy modules, and incident response protocols. Some offer EU AI Act-specific modules since 2024. The vendors typically price on annual subscription with per-seat licensing.

### Where they fail for the Geneva boutique consulting firm

- **Platform dependency.** The vendors deliver a platform, not a deliverable. The boutique consulting firm needs a 30-day deployer readiness pack they can attach to the EU client's RFP response, not a platform login that requires implementation and ongoing administration.
- **No Article 3 deployer applicability memo template for consulting.** The vendors address EU AI Act from the provider perspective (their customers are AI providers building AI products, not deployers in the C314 sense — service firms using AI on client deliverables). They do not produce a deployer-side applicability memo for a Swiss boutique consulting firm serving EU corporate clients with sub-consultant engagement structures.
- **No sub-consultant AI authorization chain template.** The vendors address employee-based organizational structures with internal AI use policies. Sub-consultant AI authorization at the rate of engagement change is not in their template library. The consulting firm's sub-consultant engagement turnover (monthly for some boutiques) is too rapid for the vendor's template approach.
- **No nFADP Art.63 personal liability framework.** The vendors address GDPR (EU) and CCPA (California), not nFADP Art.63 personal liability. The Swiss personal-liability layer tied to the named deliverable is not in their template library.
- **No FINMA Circular 2026/03 module.** The vendors do not address FINMA-specific operational resilience requirements. The boutique consulting firm's FINMA-adjacent trigger through the fintech client is not covered.
- **No documented client conversation protocol.** The vendors do not produce AI transcription + summarization consent language for client meetings. The personal liability vector tied to client conversation documentation is not in their framework.
- **No Article 50 deliverable transparency language.** The vendors do not produce engagement letter or deliverable cover page AI use disclosure language. The deliverable-as-transparency-vector is not in their framework.
- **No carrier attestation integration.** The vendors do not produce documentation that satisfies the carrier's AI oversight questions. The consulting firm must reconcile the vendor output with the carrier attestation independently.
- **Per-seat pricing.** The vendors price per seat or per active AI use case, typically USD 50,000–200,000+ annually for a consulting-sized deployment. The boutique consulting firm cannot afford the platform and does not need it for a 30-day deliverable.
- **Implementation time.** The vendors require 3–6 months for platform implementation, training, and integration. The boutique consulting firm has 30 days.

### Gap they leave

The AI governance software vendors deliver platforms, not deliverables. The boutique consulting firm needs a deliverable. The vendors produce nothing the consulting firm can attach to the EU corporate client's RFP response.

## Provider Category 4 — Boutique Geneva Law Firms with AI Practices

### What exists

Two or three Geneva boutique law firms have established AI practices offering AI governance advice, contract review for AI vendor agreements, and EU AI Act horizon monitoring. These firms typically have 2-5 attorneys specializing in technology and data protection law. They serve Geneva boutique consulting clients on AI vendor contract questions and nFADP compliance reviews.

### Where they fail for the Geneva boutique consulting firm

- **Legal advice framing.** The law firms deliver legal opinions and contract reviews. The boutique consulting firm needs operational documentation (sub-consultant AI authorization records, documented client conversation protocols, Article 50 deliverable transparency language). Legal advice is not operational documentation.
- **No EU AI Act deployer deliverable for consulting specifically.** The law firms address EU AI Act from the legal research and analysis perspective (Article 3 interpretation, Article 6(3) high-risk classification analysis, Article 26 deployer obligations analysis). They do not produce a deployer applicability memo for the specific deliverable scenarios of a Geneva boutique consulting firm with sub-consultant engagement structures.
- **No sub-consultant AI authorization documentation system.** The law firms advise on the need for sub-consultant AI authorization. They do not produce the documentation system itself — the templates, the engagement letter addenda, the per-engagement authorization records, the sub-consultant AI tool disclosure forms.
- **No documented client conversation protocol.** The law firms address nFADP Art.63 personal liability in legal opinions. They do not produce the operational protocol for AI transcription, AI summarization, and AI analysis of client conversations. The protocol is not a legal opinion; it is an operational documentation system.
- **No vendor sub-processor audit methodology for consulting vendors.** The law firms review vendor contracts. They do not produce a sub-processor audit methodology the consulting firm can use to map sub-processors of the consulting firm's own AI tools (transcription, summarization, analysis, deliverable drafting), classify extraterritorial routing, and document the audit for client RFP responses.
- **No Article 50 deliverable transparency language.** The law firms advise on Article 50 obligations. They do not produce the engagement letter addendum, deliverable cover page language, or methodology disclosure language that satisfies Article 50 in operational form.
- **No FINMA-adjacent trigger recognition.** The law firms are not FINMA-supervised and do not recognize the FINMA-adjacent trigger through the fintech client channel. The fintech client channel is not in their advisory scope.
- **Hourly pricing model.** The law firms bill hourly, typically at CHF 400–800/hour. A 30-day deployer readiness pack delivered through hourly billing is economically irrational for the boutique consulting firm.
- **Conflict of interest for peer boutiques.** Geneva consulting boutiques often share the same law firms. The consulting senior partner may be reluctant to use a law firm that also advises peer boutiques, particularly when disclosing peer EU AI Act readiness gap analysis.

### Gap they leave

The boutique Geneva law firms produce legal advice, not operational documentation. They address EU AI Act interpretation, not deployer applicability memo production. They advise on sub-consultant AI authorization, they do not produce the authorization system. They advise on Article 50 transparency, they do not produce the transparency language. The boutique consulting firm cannot buy the C314 P03 service from them.

## Provider Category 5 — Independent AI Governance Consultants (Sole Practitioners)

### What exists

A small number of independent AI governance consultants operate in Geneva and Zurich. They typically offer AI policy templates, AI governance coaching, and ad-hoc advisory services. They have some AI governance experience but rarely have consulting-vertical-specific expertise or Geneva-boutique-specific service architectures.

### Where they fail for the Geneva boutique consulting firm

- **No consulting-vertical specificity.** The independent consultants offer generic AI governance services applicable across verticals (legal, finance, healthcare, consulting). They do not have consulting-specific sub-consultant AI authorization chain documentation systems or consulting-specific Article 50 deliverable transparency language.
- **No EU AI Act deployer-specific deliverable for consulting.** The independent consultants address EU AI Act as a compliance framework, not as a deployer accountability framework. They do not produce Article 3 deployer applicability memos tied to specific client deliverables with named senior partner accountability.
- **No nFADP Art.63 personal liability documentation.** The independent consultants address nFADP at the firm level. They do not produce Art.63 personal-liability documentation tied to the named deliverable and the named documented client conversation. The Art.63 personal layer tied to the deliverable is not their deliverable.
- **No FINMA-adjacent trigger recognition.** The independent consultants do not recognize the FINMA-adjacent trigger through the fintech client channel. The fintech client channel is not in their service architecture.
- **Inconsistent quality and availability.** The independent consultants vary widely in experience and availability. The boutique consulting firm cannot rely on a sole practitioner for a 30-day deliverable with named senior partner attestation.
- **No carrier attestation integration.** The independent consultants do not coordinate their AI governance deliverables with the carrier attestation. The carrier asks for AI oversight documentation; the consultant produces it independently; the consulting firm must reconcile them manually.
- **Pricing model is hourly.** The independent consultants bill hourly, typically at CHF 200–400/hour. A 30-day deployer readiness pack delivered through hourly billing is economically irrational for the boutique consulting firm.

### Gap they leave

The independent AI governance consultants offer generic AI governance services without consulting-vertical-specific deliverables, without EU AI Act deployer-specific framing for consulting, without nFADP Art.63 personal liability documentation tied to the deliverable, without FINMA-adjacent trigger recognition, and without carrier attestation integration. The boutique consulting firm cannot buy the C314 P03 service from them.

## What the C314 P03 Service Owns — The Six Named White Spaces

The C314 P03 service for Geneva boutique consulting owns six discrete, named white spaces that no current provider addresses:

### White Space 1: Article 3 Deployer Applicability Memo (Consulting Vertical)

The memo applies Article 3 and Article 6(3) to the consulting firm's specific client engagements. It identifies which engagements are in scope by named deliverable, which AI uses are high-risk under Article 6(3) (narrow procedural task, improves result of previously completed human activity, detects decision-making patterns, performs preparatory task), which are limited-risk, which are out of scope. The memo is dated, senior-partner-signed, and engagement-specific. It is the first deliverable the EU corporate client reads in the EU AI Act section of the RFP response. **No current provider produces this deliverable for the consulting vertical.** Tier-1 consultancies produce generic applicability analyses. Compliance consultancies produce ISO/IEC 42001 governance documentation. Software vendors produce platform-based risk assessments. Law firms produce legal opinions on Article 3. None produces an Article 3 deployer applicability memo tied to specific consulting client deliverables with named senior partner attestation.

### White Space 2: Sub-Consultant AI Authorization Chain Documentation System

The system produces authorization chain documentation refreshed at the rate of sub-consultant engagement book change (monthly for some boutiques), with documented sub-consultant AI tool disclosure, sub-consultant data processing agreement terms, sub-consultant supervision architecture, and documented handover for both sub-consultant engagements and disengagements. The system is designed for the consulting firm's sub-consultant engagement turnover rate, which is structurally faster than employee turnover (legal) or mandate turnover (finance). **No current provider produces this documentation system for consulting firms.** Compliance consultancies do not address sub-consultant AI authorization. Software vendors do not address external contractor AI authorization. Law firms advise on the need but do not produce the system.

### White Space 3: Documented Client Conversation Protocol

The protocol produces operational documentation for AI transcription, AI summarization, and AI analysis of client conversations. It includes client consent language for AI processing of conversation content, AI tool sub-processor chain disclosure for the conversation processing tools, nFADP Art.63 personal liability framing tied to the documented conversation, and AI tool configuration for the conversation processing (zero-retention, privacy-preserving mode, conversation data deletion triggers). The protocol is referenced in the engagement letter and in the meeting confirmation. **No current provider produces this protocol.** Compliance consultancies do not address AI transcription + summarization in client conversations. Software vendors do not produce operational conversation protocols. Law firms address nFADP Art.63 in legal opinions, not in operational protocols.

### White Space 4: Article 50 Deliverable Transparency Language

The deliverable transparency language produces engagement letter addenda with AI use disclosure language, deliverable cover page AI use disclosure language, methodology disclosure language explaining AI contribution to the deliverable, and reproducibility disclosure language explaining the deliverable's AI dependency. The language is consulting-specific — the deliverable is the unit of accountability, not the firm. **No current provider produces this language for consulting.** Compliance consultancies address Article 50 generically. Software vendors address Article 50 through platform-based transparency logs. Law firms advise on Article 50 but do not produce engagement letter addenda or deliverable cover page language.

### White Space 5: FINMA-Adjacent AI Governance Pack

The pack produces AI governance documentation at FINMA Circular 2026/03 §7.4 specificity for the consulting firm's fintech clients. The pack includes AI system inventory for the consulting firm, named deployer accountability documentation, human oversight documentation for AI use on fintech client engagements, and incident response protocol. The pack is designed for indirect visibility to FINMA through the client's filing package. **No current provider produces this pack for consulting firms.** Compliance consultancies produce direct FINMA documentation for FINMA-supervised institutions, not indirect FINMA-adjacent documentation for sub-advisors. Software vendors do not address FINMA-adjacent triggers. Law firms do not recognize the FINMA-adjacent channel.

### White Space 6: Peer EU AI Act Readiness Gap Analysis

The gap analysis maps the consulting firm's current EU AI Act readiness posture against the public June 2026 Geneva consulting conference disclosures. It identifies specific gaps in deployer applicability analysis, sub-consultant AI authorization, documented client conversation protocol, Article 50 deliverable transparency, FINMA-adjacent documentation, and EU AI Act readiness narrative. The analysis is peer-benchmarked, documented, and bounded. **No current provider produces this gap analysis for consulting firms.** Compliance consultancies do not produce peer-benchmarked gap analyses. Software vendors do not address peer conference disclosure cycles. Law firms do not produce peer-benchmarked readiness analyses.

## The C314 P03 Service Bundle

The C314 P03 service is the six-module bundle addressing the six white spaces above. The bundle is named:

**"The Geneva Boutique Consulting EU AI Act 30-Day Deployer Readiness Pack"**

The bundle contains:

1. **Article 3 deployer applicability memo (consulting vertical)** — engagement-specific, deliverable-named, senior-partner-signed
2. **Sub-consultant AI authorization chain documentation system** — refreshed at engagement change, signed by both senior partner and sub-consultant
3. **Documented client conversation protocol** — AI transcription + summarization consent language, nFADP Art.63 framing
4. **Article 50 deliverable transparency language** — engagement letter + deliverable cover page + methodology disclosure
5. **FINMA-adjacent AI governance pack** — for the fintech client channel through FINMA Circular 2026/03 §7.4
6. **Peer EU AI Act readiness gap analysis** — mapped to the June 2026 Geneva consulting conference disclosures

The bundle is delivered as one evidence architecture with 18 modules (six C312 + six C313 + six C314 P03). 30-day delivery window. Fixed fee. Senior-partner-signed deliverables. EN + FR.

The first Geneva boutique consulting AI accountability service to publish this exact six-module 30-day deployer readiness pack owns the C314 P03 category. The first actor to deliver all six before late August 2026 owns the Geneva boutique consulting EU AI Act deployer accountability market for the EU corporate client vertical.

## Why No Current Provider Produces the C314 P03 Service

The C314 P03 service does not exist in the current market for five structural reasons:

1. **Vertical specificity.** The C314 P03 service is consulting-vertical-specific. No current provider offers consulting-vertical-specific AI accountability services at the boutique scale.
2. **Sub-consultant AI authorization chain.** The sub-consultant AI authorization chain documentation system is unique to the consulting market. No current provider addresses sub-consultant AI authorization because no other vertical has the same structural pattern (external contractors with no employment-cycle boundary, AI use invisible to the senior partner, deliverable under the senior partner's name).
3. **Documented client conversation as personal liability vector.** The documented client conversation as an nFADP Art.63 personal liability vector is unique to the consulting market because consulting firms generate the highest volume of AI-processed client conversations (kickoff calls, steering committees, methodology reviews, deliverable walkthroughs). No current provider addresses this vector.
4. **Deliverable as unit of accountability.** The deliverable as the unit of accountability is unique to the consulting market. Legal firms have matters (case, transaction). Finance firms have mandates. Consulting firms have deliverables. No current provider produces deliverable-specific AI governance documentation.
5. **FINMA-adjacent trigger.** The FINMA-adjacent trigger through the fintech client channel is unique to the consulting market. No current provider recognizes or addresses this trigger.

The C314 P03 service is the first service to address all five structural reasons. The first actor to publish the service owns the C314 P03 category.
