### Finding 1: FMH Human Oversight Stated Without Operational Definition

**The problem:** FMH has stated the human oversight requirement for AI-assisted medical decisions. But there is no published operational definition of what "human oversight" means in a small practice context. Physicians do not know:
- Whether reviewing AI output constitutes adequate oversight
- Whether they need a written review protocol
- Whether they need to document their review decisions
- What "review" means for different risk levels of AI assistance

**How it surfaces:** In conversations, physicians default to "I review everything before it goes out." They interpret human oversight as their personal review practice — not as a documented governance process. The gap is not awareness of the obligation. It is the absence of a defined compliance pathway.

**Market signal:** "FMH says I need human oversight. I review the output. That counts, right?" — This question is the opening of a consulting engagement, not a rhetorical one.

**Differentiation from prior cycles:** C306/C307 covered FMH CME AI evaluation chain. This is different — it is the clinical human oversight obligation, not the CME obligation.

---

### Finding 2: Efficiency-Liability Paradox — Every AI Gain Is a Liability Gain

**The problem:** In every other vertical, efficiency from AI is straightforwardly positive. In healthcare, the physician's personal liability exposure is structured so that reducing their own labor also reduces their documented decision trail. The more AI assists, the more the physician's personal judgment is replaced by AI output — and the physician remains personally liable for the decision.

**The paradox:** To be efficient, the physician must delegate to AI. Every delegation reduces the physician's documented direct involvement. Every reduction in documented involvement increases the personal liability exposure when the decision is challenged.

**How it surfaces:** Physicians who are most actively adopting AI tools (to cope with staff shortages) are also the most exposed to liability challenges. The efficiency-motivated AI adopters are the highest-risk segment.

**Market signal:** "The more I automate, the more I worry. Every time I sign an AI-assisted letter, I wonder." — The worry is rational. There is no known solution in the market.

**Business implication:** The service is not "AI governance" or "efficiency." It is "documented human authorization for AI-assisted clinical decisions" — which enables efficiency without proportional liability exposure increase.

### Finding 3: Swissdamed AI Tool Classification — Physician as "Operator" Obligations

**The problem:** Swissdamed mandatory registration deadline is July 1, 2026. For AI-enabled diagnostic tools used in small practices, the classification question is live: does the tool qualify as a medical device requiring registration? If yes, the physician using the tool is the "operator" under Swissmedic rules — with specific compliance obligations they are likely not meeting.

**The gap:** Most small practice physicians assume the vendor's CE marking or Swissdamed registration means the tool is cleared for their use context. It does not. The physician as operator has independent obligations regarding how the device is used in their specific practice.

**How it surfaces:** "My AI diagnostic tool — does that count as a medical device under Swissmedic?" — followed by "The vendor says their tool is registered. Should I verify that myself?"

**The physician answer:** Yes, they should verify independently. And most do not know to ask.

**Differentiation from prior cycles:** C307/C306 covered Swissdamed in the context of device registration deadline awareness. This is different — it is the operator accountability chain and the personal liability exposure from misclassification.

### Finding 4: Staff AI Tool Use on Patient Data — Invisible Accountability Gap

**The problem:** In small Geneva practices, medical assistants and administrative staff are actively using AI tools (ChatGPT, translation apps, scheduling AI, communication AI) on patient data — without the practice owner's knowledge or documented authorization. The physician is personally liable for every decision made in their practice, including those made by staff using AI tools they did not authorize.

**The failure mode:** The physician says "I told my staff not to use AI on patient data." The accountability gap is: (a) no documentation of the instruction, (b) no monitoring of compliance, (c) no defined exception process, (d) no incident response if a breach occurs through staff AI tool use.

**How it surfaces:** "I don't even know what tools my staff are using on their phones." — This is both a data protection failure mode and a potential criminal negligence exposure under Swiss medical liability law.

**Market signal:** This is not a theoretical risk. Medical assistant shortages mean practices are hiring less-experienced staff who are more likely to use consumer AI tools in ways the practice owner does not anticipate.

---

### Finding 5: Carrier Attestation Personalization — From Practice-Level to Physician-Level

**The problem:** Carrier AI attestation forms are shifting from "does your practice use AI?" to "do you personally, as the responsible physician, certify that your practice has adequate AI governance?" This personalization of the attestation question changes the accountability structure — it moves from institutional to individual.

**How it surfaces:** "I signed the attestation. Now I'm wondering if that was the right thing to do." — The physician signed what they thought was a practice-level form. The carrier is now treating it as a personal certification.

**The legal consequence:** If a carrier can demonstrate that the physician personally signed an attestation that misrepresented their AI governance state, the misrepresentation claim runs to the physician's license, not just the practice's coverage.

**Distinct from other verticals:** In legal and finance, attestation misrepresentation affects business liability. In healthcare, it affects the personal medical license — which cannot be replaced.

**Differentiation from prior cycles:** C306/C307 finance phase covered carrier attestation misrepresentation. This is different — it is carrier attestation activating physician license accountability specifically.

---

### Finding 6: Post-Incident Documentation Burden Falls on Individual Physician

**The problem:** When an AI-assisted clinical decision is challenged — by a patient, a cantonal medical authority, or a carrier — the burden of proof falls on the physician personally. There is no standardized medical record protocol for documenting AI-assisted decisions in Swiss primary care. The physician must reconstruct their reasoning in the absence of a defined documentation standard.

**How it surfaces:** "If AI makes a mistake and I signed off on it — that's on me." — The physician understands this. They do not know what documentation would have protected them.

**The market gap:** Swiss medical record standards (FMH / cantonal) do not yet include AI-assisted decision documentation requirements. This is a compliance gap that will be filled — and the physicians who have not established internal protocols will be exposed when it is filled.

**Timing implication:** The CoE AI Convention implementation expected end-2026 will add human oversight documentation obligations. The window to establish compliant protocols before the standard is codified is approximately 6–12 months.
