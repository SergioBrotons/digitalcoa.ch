### 1. "Lex On" — Swiss Federal IP Institute Reshapes Swissness Rules (NET-NEW C85)

**Source:** Le Temps, April 1, 2026 — "Loi Swissness: l'affaire des chaussures On met les spécialistes de la propriété intellectuelle en berne"

**What happened:**
The Swiss Federal Institute of Intellectual Property (IPI / Institut Fédéral de la Propriété Intellectuelle), based in Bern, announced on **March 23, 2026** a modification to Swissness practice rules, permitting the Swiss cross to appear between the words "Swiss" and "Engineering" or "Research" — even when the underlying production is **abroad**.

**Prior rule:** Industrial products labeled "Swiss" required ≥60% of production costs in Switzerland and at least one essential manufacturing step on Swiss territory. The Swiss cross (the national flag/emblem) was strictly reserved for genuinely Swiss-made products.

**New interpretation:** Products may now bear "Swiss Engineering" or "Swiss Research" with the Swiss cross visually inserted between "Swiss" and "Engineering/Research" — even if manufactured entirely outside Switzerland.

**Graphic rules specified by IPI:**
- Swiss cross must be placed exactly between the two terms (e.g., "Swiss ✚ Engineering")
- Square side length must not exceed the font size

**Legal community reaction:**
IP specialists are described as "en berne" (puzzled/concerned) — this is not seen as a minor cosmetic adjustment but as a substantive redefinition of Swissness protection. The national emblem (not just the cross shape but the full Swiss flag) sits at the top of the appellation hierarchy. The decision touches the visual identity of Swiss branding at a sensitive time.

**Consequence:** Swiss brand value — anchored in genuine "Swiss made" — may be diluted. Particularly relevant for Geneva's luxury, watchmaking, pharmaceutical, and precision engineering sectors.

**Status:** Active controversy; political/legal challenges possible.

### 2. Corela Clinic Case — Geneva Police Tribunal Ruling (April 1, 2026) (UPDATED)

**Source:** Le Temps, April 1, 2026 — "A Genève, l'affaire de la clinique Corela se dégonfle devant le Tribunal de police"

**Background (from prior coverage):**
- Corela Clinic, Geneva: operated since 2003 as a medical expert evaluation center for insurance companies
- Purpose: evaluate health status of insured persons on behalf of insurers
- Revelations began 2018 (press): suspected system of falsified expert reports to favor insurance companies, at expense of assessed individuals
- Authorization suspended 2018; clinic dissolved
- Prosecution: 8 problematic expert reports + falsified correspondence

**The system (as alleged by prosecution):**
- Doctors-experts were foreign, unfamiliar with Swiss procedures
- Draft expert projects prepared by clinic staff in Madagascar
- Reports then signed by doctors-experts without full review
- Reports subsequently modified after signatures

**April 1, 2026 Ruling:**
- **Guilty:** 1 count of falsification in documents (faux dans les titres) + 1 count of instigation to falsification (for one expert report and one falsified letter)
- **Acquitted:** 7 other cases + violation of professional secrecy (secret de fonction)
- **Penalty:** 90 day-fines at CHF 30 = CHF 2,700 total, with suspended sentence, 2-year probation
- **Co-accused (assistant):** acquitted on all counts

**Key procedural note:** Court explicitly cited a **6-year period of procedural inactivity** (no acts performed between 2018 and 2024) as a mitigating sentencing factor (violation of the principle of "célérité" — procedural timeliness/speed). The slowness of Swiss criminal procedure has concrete consequences.

**Significance for Geneva legal ecosystem:**
- Insurance expert fraud/misrepresentation remains a live regulatory concern
- Geneva courts are actively processing insurance-sector misconduct cases
- Principle of "célérité" emerging as explicit sentencing consideration
- The gap between alleged systemic wrongdoing and proven individual criminal acts is significant

### 3. WTO E-Commerce Moratorium Expires — 28-Year Digital Trade Consensus Ends (March 31, 2026)

**Source:** Le Temps, March 31, 2026 — "Faute d'accord à l'OMC, un moratoire vieux de vingt-huit ans expire et ouvre la voie à des taxes douanières sur les téléchargements"

**What happened:**
The 14th WTO Ministerial Conference (CM14) in Yaoundé ended **without consensus** on extending the moratorium on customs duties for digital transmissions. The moratorium, in place since 1998, expired on **March 31, 2026**.

**What was covered:** Downloads of software, e-books, video games, streaming services — all digital transmissions.

**Implications:** Countries may now impose tariffs on digital goods and services. Geneva-based digital trade businesses, software companies, and streaming services face new trade policy uncertainty. Geneva's position as a digital services hub is affected.

**Note from Le Temps:** Implementation would take time even if countries move to impose tariffs.

### 4. HUG Digital Sovereignty: Geneva Hospital Chooses Independence Over Epic (UPDATED)

**Source:** Le Temps, April 2, 2026 — "En développant ensemble leur propre logiciel clinique, les HUG et l'Hôpital du Valais misent sur l'indépendance"

**Situation:**
- HUG (Geneva University Hospitals) have developed their own integrated patient record (Dossier Patient Informatisé / DPI) for 30+ years
- Since June 2024, HUG and Hôpital du Valais are co-developing a new version: **DPI+**
- This contrasts sharply with CHUV (Lausanne) and Fédération des Hôpitaux Vaudois which are acquiring **Epic Systems** (US-based EHR giant)
- Vaudois parliamentarians are examining the Epic choice; data sovereignty and digital independence concerns raised in the Vaud Grand Council

**Relevance:** Geneva's approach prioritizes data sovereignty and collaborative health IT development. Relevant for Geneva digital health law, AI governance, and health tech business environment.

## Key NET-NEW Finding (C85 P01)

**"Lex On" Swissness IP Rule Change (March 23, 2026)** — The IPI's reinterpretation of Swissness rules represents a substantive shift in Swiss IP/branding law. The decision permits Swiss cross usage with "Swiss Engineering/Research" even for foreign-manufactured products — a departure from the strict ≥60% domestic cost rule. This is:
- Directly relevant to Geneva-based companies using Swiss branding for products with international manufacturing
- A potential wedge issue between genuine Swiss manufacturers and companies using Swiss branding for marketing
- A signal that Swiss brand protection institutions may be softening under commercial pressure
- Likely to generate legal/political challenges

**Secondary Finding: Corela Clinic Ruling** — The Geneva Police Tribunal ruling on insurance expert fraud demonstrates ongoing judicial activity in insurance sector misconduct cases. The explicit use of procedural delay ("célérité" principle) as a sentencing mitigating factor highlights a real structural problem in Geneva's criminal justice system.
