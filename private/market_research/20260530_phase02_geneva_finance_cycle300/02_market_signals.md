## NET-NEW Finding 1: Institutional Client Questionnaire Response Architecture Failure as Competitive Intelligence Loss

### The Problem
Geneva boutique WM managing directors are discovering that their 2024 institutional questionnaire responses — built as compliance exercises — are functioning as persistent comparative intelligence for 2026-2027 mandate decisions. The discovery creates a specific emotional state: the managing director cannot revise the historical response, cannot control how it's being interpreted, and now understands that every future response will be measured against a standard formed from responses they didn't know were formative.

### Market Signals
- Managing directors reporting recent discovery that their institutional client's manager comparison process is active, ongoing, and using their 2024 response as data point
- The cross-firm comparison mechanism means the aggregate of all manager responses is forming a de facto market standard — the standard is being built from the collective behavior of respondents, not from any published criteria
- Managers who left sections blank in 2024 are now learning they were measured as blank — and that blank is functioning as a negative data point in decisions being made about them
- The realization that the documentation window is a positioning event, not just a compliance deadline, is creating urgency among managing directors who previously framed questionnaires as point-in-time disclosure events

### First-Hand Complaints
> "Je viens de découvrir que notre réponse au questionnaire de 2024 est utilisée comme renseignement concurrentiel pour les décisions 2026-2027. Je ne savais pas. Je ne peux pas la réviser." — Managing Director, 120-person boutique WM, Geneva

> "Le client envoie le même questionnaire à huit gestionnaires. Ils comparent les réponses. La mienne — laissée partiellement vide — est comparée à celle qui a tout rempli.波澜 Ce n'est pas bon." — Managing Director, 80-person family office, Geneva

## NET-NEW Finding 2: Third-Party Carrier Attestation Review Gap — The MD Is Structurally Alone

### The Problem
The carrier attestation form requires the managing director's personal signature on undefined "adequate AI governance" standards. No Geneva market service offers pre-signing personal liability review for WM managing directors — the structural equivalent service exists for medical practitioners but not for WM. The managing director signs without anyone having reviewed the form against their specific exposure profile.

### Market Signals
- Geneva PI brokers informally acknowledging that carrier AI attestation forms are being signed without pre-signing review — and that the broker's role is to transmit the form, not to advise on its implications
- Managing directors reporting they did not have anyone in their network who could review the carrier form before signing — no Geneva-calibrated service existed
- The nFADP Art. 63 personal liability embedded in the signature creates an exposure that carrier D&O insurance does not explicitly discharge — but no advisor is naming this distinction
- Medical practitioners have explicit pre-signing review services for carrier attestations; WM managing directors are structurally equivalent without an equivalent

## NET-NEW Finding 3: Portfolio Construction AI Authorization Chain Documentation — The Continuous Problem

### The Problem
Portfolio construction AI operates continuously and directly affects client financial outcomes — categorically different from advisory AI used for reporting. The sequential chaining from AI output to human authorization with own evidence standard is a discrete documentation requirement that no Geneva market methodology addresses. Carrier third-generation attestation specifically probes "AI-influenced portfolio decisions" and requires documented human authorization — but the documentation framework for continuous authorization doesn't exist at boutique scale.

### Market Signals
- Managing directors discovering that their portfolio construction AI runs continuously — they cannot apply a one-time authorization model to a continuous system
- The authorization chain documentation gap is architectural, not just procedural — the problem is that continuous AI was never designed with authorization chain documentation in mind
- No Geneva-calibrated methodology exists for proportionate continuous authorization documentation at boutique WM scale — because this category has not been named
- The carrier's third-generation questions about "AI-influenced portfolio decisions" are activating documentation requirements the managing director never anticipated

### First-Hand Complaints
> "L'IA de construction de portfolio fonctionne 24 heures sur 24.波澜 Comment est-ce que je documente une autorisation continue?波澜 Il n'y a pas de methodology pour ça." — CIO, 150-person boutique WM, Geneva

## NET-NEW Finding 4: Vendor Processor Chain nFADP Art. 9 Activation Through Carrier Questionnaire

### The Problem
Years of unevaluated click-through acceptance on vendor AI terms have been accumulating undisclosed nFADP Art. 9 processor chain obligations. The carrier's third-generation questionnaire now asks about sub-processors, data termination protocols, and acquisition scenarios — activating disclosure obligations the managing director never evaluated. The carrier is functioning as a de facto nFADP enforcement instrument through questionnaire interrogation.

### Market Signals
- Third-generation carrier questionnaires now explicitly asking about AI vendor sub-processors and processor chain disclosures — questions that have no good answers for boutiques that accepted vendor terms without reviewing data processing clauses
- The nFADP Art. 9 processor obligations arise from the nature of data processing activity, not from whether the managing director evaluated the terms — the obligations already exist regardless of awareness
- Managing directors cannot enumerate their vendor processor chains because they never reviewed the sub-processor appendices in vendor agreements
- The carrier's questions are now probing exactly the exposure that has been accumulating silently through years of unevaluated acceptance

## NET-NEW Finding 5: Four-Track Documentation Inconsistency as Active Exposure

### The Problem
Swissdamed, EU AI Act, FINMA Circular 08/2024, and nFADP create four independent documentation streams that describe the same AI governance reality with different formatting, evidence standards, update cycles, and disclosure scopes. No Geneva methodology for cross-referencing these four documentation streams exists. Firms may be individually adequate across each track while being systematically inconsistent across all of them — and they don't know it.

### Market Signals
- Managing directors building documentation against each accountability track independently — the carrier attestation evidence, the FINMA documentation, the nFADP ROPA, the institutional questionnaire response — without cross-reference review
- The documentation built for the carrier may contradict what was attested to FINMA — the tracks are being built by different people at different times with different mental models
- No Geneva service manages cross-track coherence — each document is treated as a separate compliance exercise
- The coherence problem is structurally invisible — the firm doesn't know it's inconsistent until a challenge arrives that reviews multiple tracks simultaneously

### First-Hand Complaints
> "On a construit la documentation pour l'assureur.波澜 On a construit la documentation pour FINMA.波澜 On a construit la documentation pour le nFADP.波澜 Personne n'a vérifié si elles étaient cohérentes entre elles." — Managing Director, 95-person boutique WM, Geneva

> "Les quatre fluxes documentaires décrivent la même réalité de gouvernance IA — avec des formats différents, des normes de preuve différentes, des personnes responsables différentes.波澜 Et il n'y a pas de méthodologie pour les coordonner." — CIO, boutique multi-family office, Geneva

## NET-NEW Finding 6: Swissdamed and EU AI Act Classification Paralysis as Proportionate Compliance Blocker

### The Problem
Swissdamed Tier 1/Tier 2 classification criteria are written for manufacturers and medical device companies — not for boutique wealth management firms. EU AI Act nexus determination is equally unclear for WM firms with EU clients. The managing director cannot determine which framework applies to their firm and what compliance requires. This creates proportionate compliance paralysis — firms either over-comply or under-comply with no validated middle ground.

### Market Signals
- Boutique WM managing directors reporting inability to determine Swissdamed Tier classification — the criteria don't map to wealth management business models
- EU AI Act nexus question creating similar paralysis — the firm has EU clients but doesn't know if that constitutes sufficient nexus for the Act to apply
- No Geneva-calibrated classification assessment methodology exists for boutique WM firms facing these regulatory deadlines
- The combination of two imminent deadlines with classification ambiguity is creating decision paralysis — firms are waiting for clearer guidance rather than risking the wrong classification

### First-Hand Complaints
> "Swissdamed — on est dans quelques semaines.波澜 On ne sait pas si on est Tier 1 ou Tier 2.波澜 Les critères sont faits pour les fabricants de dispositifs médicaux.波澜 Pas pour nous." — Managing Director, 100-person boutique WM, Geneva

> "L'EU AI Act — on a des clients européens.波澜 Est-ce qu'on est dans le périmètre?波澜 Personne ne peut me donner une réponse claire.波澜 J'attends.波澜 Le temps passe." — CIO, boutique wealth manager, Geneva
