## Swissdamed: The Classification Problem That Blocks Proportionate Compliance

### Why the Classification Problem Is Different for WM

Swissdamed registration requirements are built around device and product classifications — Tier 1 for higher-risk AI systems, Tier 2 for lower-risk. The criteria were designed for medical device manufacturers and product companies, not for wealth management firms using AI in portfolio construction and client advisory contexts.

The WM managing director faces a specific problem: the classification criteria don't map to their business model. They know they use AI in portfolio construction and client reporting. They don't know if that maps to Tier 1 or Tier 2. There is no Geneva-calibrated classification guide for WM firms navigating Swissdamed registration.

### The Consequences of Getting It Wrong

Getting the classification wrong in either direction creates exposure:
- **Over-classification**: Compliance burden disproportionate to actual risk, resource drain on compliance function already stretched thin
- **Under-classification**: Regulatory risk — operating without required registration, documentation, or oversight mechanisms
- **Uncertainty paralysis**: Decision gridlock because the managing director cannot determine the correct classification and no external reference exists

### What the Classification Actually Determines

The Swissdamed classification determines:
- Required documentation of AI system intended purpose and risk classification
- Registration timeline and ongoing reporting obligations
- Human oversight requirements proportional to risk tier
- Post-market surveillance obligations

For the boutique WM firm, the classification also interacts with FINMA expectations — the FINMA Circular 08/2024 defines AI governance expectations that may parallel or overlap with Swissdamed requirements. The managing director who builds Swissdamed documentation and FINMA documentation independently may be creating inconsistent evidence across the two frameworks.

## EU AI Act: The Nexus Question That Blocks Proportionate Compliance

### Why the Nexus Problem Is Different for WM

The EU AI Act applies to systems used in the EU and to firms with EU nexus. For a Geneva boutique WM firm, EU nexus arises from having EU-based clients. The question of whether EU client relationships constitute sufficient nexus for the Act to apply is not clearly answered in published guidance.

### What AI Literacy Actually Requires at Boutique WM Scale

Art. 4 AI literacy requires that personnel using AI systems have appropriate knowledge and competence. For the boutique WM managing director, this means:
- Understanding what the firm's AI tools actually do in portfolio construction and client advisory contexts
- Documenting that understanding in a form that can be demonstrated if challenged
- Being able to show the reasoning chain from AI recommendation to human decision

This is not a training certificate. It is a demonstrated competency standard with a documentation requirement. The managing director needs to show not just that training was completed, but that the personnel who make AI-influenced decisions have documented reasoning capacity.

### The Proportionate Compliance Problem

The EU AI Act compliance path for a boutique WM firm with EU nexus is not defined in published Geneva-calibrated guidance. The managing director who tries to comply proportionally faces:
- No clear scope determination methodology for their firm type
- No proportionate AI literacy documentation standard for their scale
- No intersection guidance between EU AI Act requirements and existing Swiss regulatory obligations (FINMA, nFADP, CRS 2.0)

## FINMA Circular 08/2024: Still 17 Months Without a Checklist

### What Changed in C300

FINMA Circular 08/2024 is now 17+ months old. The gap between the circular's expectation of "adequate AI governance" and the practical implementation reality at boutique WM scale has widened. The circular requires:
- Documented AI inventory
- Defined governance framework for AI tool deployment
- Human oversight mechanisms
- Risk management integration
- Vendor due diligence

But it provides no proportionate implementation methodology for 50–200 employee firms without dedicated compliance functions. The managing director who reads the circular knows what FINMA expects in principle. They don't know what "adequate" means for their specific firm in practice.

### The SRO Vacuum Amplification

The SRO's structural silence on AI governance is not the same as FINMA silence. The managing director expects their SRO to translate FINMA expectations into practical member guidance — the SRO is the organization's primary mechanism for making regulatory expectations operational for members.

The SRO has not provided this translation. The managing director is not only without FINMA guidance; they are without their own industry's translation mechanism. The SRO vacuum creates a specific pain point: even the managing director's own industry body has abandoned them on AI governance.

## nFADP: The Documentation Architecture Incompatibility Problem

### The Incompatibility That Nobody Names

Carrier attestation documentation is designed for underwriting risk evaluation. It measures whether the firm has documentation that satisfies the carrier's risk assessment. This is architecturally different from documentation that would satisfy nFADP Art. 63 criminal defense requirements.

Criminal defense requires:
- Contemporaneous reasoning documentation at the time of decision
- Decision authorization chain showing human oversight of AI-influenced decisions
- Chain of custody for AI-influenced portfolio decisions
- Evidence of personal engagement with governance decisions at managing director level

Carrier attestation documentation may satisfy the carrier's underwriting questions while leaving the managing director's criminal defense exposure undocumented. The documentation that protects against a carrier coverage dispute is not the documentation that protects against an Art. 63 personal liability challenge.

### The Art. 9 Processor Chain Problem (Carrier Third-Gen Activation)

nFADP Art. 9 creates data processor obligations that arise from the nature of data processing activity — not from whether the managing director evaluated the vendor terms. Years of click-through acceptance on portfolio analytics AI, client reporting AI, and CRM AI terms have been accumulating undisclosed Art. 9 processor chain obligations.

The carrier's third-generation questionnaire now explicitly asks about:
- AI vendor sub-processors
- Data termination protocols
- Acquisition scenarios affecting processor chains
- Sub-contractor disclosure obligations

These questions are activating Art. 9 disclosure obligations the managing director has never evaluated. The carrier is functioning as a de facto nFADP enforcement instrument through questionnaire interrogation — asking questions that trigger obligations under Swiss law that the managing director never knew existed.

## CRS 2.0: The AI Governance Obligation Nobody Integrated

### The Structural Gap

CRS 2.0 has been live since January 2026. The standard requires AI governance obligations that were not in the original AML framework. Boutique WM firms built their CRS compliance programs before AI became a core operational component — the AI governance obligations under CRS 2.0 have not been integrated into existing compliance frameworks.

The integration problem is structural: CRS 2.0 AI governance obligations exist separately from existing AML documentation. No cross-reference methodology exists for managing CRS 2.0 AI obligations alongside the other accountability tracks (FINMA, institutional questionnaires, nFADP, carrier attestation).

### What CRS 2.0 Actually Requires for AI Systems

CRS 2.0's AI governance requirements include:
- AI system inventory integrated with AML compliance documentation
- AI-influenced decision audit trail for client due diligence and transaction monitoring
- Human oversight documentation for AI-assisted compliance decisions
- AI vendor due diligence integrated with third-party risk management

None of these requirements were in the original CRS compliance framework. The managing director who built their CRS compliance program in 2023-2024 does not have AI governance integrated into it — and may not know that CRS 2.0 requires it.

## Carrier Attestation: The Convergence Point Where All Obligations Collide

### Third-Generation Carrier Questions as Regulatory Enforcement Proxy

The carrier's third-generation questionnaire is the practical convergence point for obligations across multiple frameworks:
- **FINMA**: Human oversight documentation for AI tool deployment
- **nFADP Art. 9**: Processor chain disclosure and sub-contractor obligations
- **nFADP Art. 63**: Personal liability documentation for governance decisions
- **CRS 2.0**: AI-influenced decision audit trail requirements
- **Portfolio construction AI**: Authorization chain documentation for continuous AI systems

The carrier is asking questions that activate disclosure obligations across all of these frameworks simultaneously. The managing director who signs the carrier form without pre-signing review is committing to obligations across multiple frameworks without having evaluated them.

### Why the Attestation Is Not Just an Underwriting Exercise

The carrier attestation is the practical forcing function that makes regulatory obligations concrete for the managing director. The theoretical obligations under FINMA, nFADP, and CRS 2.0 become present-tense exposure when the carrier asks about them on a form that requires the managing director's personal signature.

The carrier attestation is where the abstract regulatory cluster becomes a specific personal liability event. This is why pre-signing review is the critical service — the managing director who reviews what they're signing before they sign it can make an informed decision about whether to sign and what to document before signing.
