### Finding C300-P2-F1: Institutional Questionnaire Second-Generation Data Use as Discovered Competitive Exposure

**What it is:** Managing directors who responded to institutional AI governance questionnaires in 2024 are discovering — in C300 — that those responses are functioning as persistent comparative intelligence for 2026-2027 mandate decisions. They treated the questionnaire as a point-in-time disclosure event; their client treated it as competitive intelligence. The discovery creates a specific emotional state: the managing director cannot revise the historical response, cannot control how it's being interpreted, and now understands that every future response will be measured against a standard formed from responses they didn't know were formative.

**Why it matters:** The temporal asymmetry between the managing director's disclosure framing and the institutional client's intelligence gathering framing is a structural positioning gap. Every questionnaire response is now being constructed with implicit awareness that it will be compared, benchmarked, and used in decisions the boutique cannot predict. The documentation quality gap is now a competitive intelligence exposure. The response that satisfied a point-in-time disclosure requirement is now a market signal.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You responded to an institutional AI governance questionnaire in 2024. You treated it as a compliance exercise. Your client treated it as competitive intelligence. It's being used for decisions you're not in the room for. That's the exposure you're living with now — and it's the exposure you're creating if you don't build the next response differently."

**Distinct from C214–C299:** Named the specific discovery moment (managing director learning their 2024 response is active in 2026-2027 decisions) as a market signal, not just the competitive comparison mechanism itself.

### Finding C300-P2-F2: Carrier Attestation Pre-Signing Review Gap as Structural Medical Practitioner Analogue

**What it is:** The carrier attestation form requires the managing director's personal signature on undefined "adequate AI governance" standards. No Geneva market service offers pre-signing personal liability review for WM managing directors. Medical practitioners have explicitly developed this service — pre-signing review of carrier attestation forms by a qualified advisor who can assess personal liability exposure before the signature is applied. WM managing directors are structurally in the same exposure position — personal signature, undefined adequacy standard, carrier D&O gap on criminal defense documentation — without an equivalent service. The structural equivalence is itself the market insight: if medical practitioners needed this service, WM managing directors need it too.

**Why it matters:** The managing director signs carrier attestation forms without anyone having reviewed them against their specific exposure profile. The signature creates personal exposure under nFADP Art. 63 that carrier D&O insurance does not explicitly discharge. The documentation that would protect in an Art. 63 challenge is not the documentation the carrier requires — but no one is telling the managing director this. The medical practitioner analogue makes the service category legible: if the market solved this for doctors, it can solve it for WM managing directors.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Medical practitioners have a service: pre-signing review of carrier attestation forms. Before they sign, someone reviews what they're committing to and whether their signature can be defended. WM managing directors are in the same exposure position — personal signature, undefined standard, criminal liability — without that service. I built that service."

**Distinct from C214–C299:** Named the specific structural equivalence to medical practitioner market and the absence of an equivalent service for WM managing directors as a discrete market gap.

### Finding C300-P2-F3: Portfolio Construction AI Continuous Authorization Architecture as Named Category

**What it is:** Portfolio construction AI operates continuously and directly affects client financial outcomes — categorically different from advisory AI used for reporting. The sequential chaining from AI output to human authorization with own evidence standard is a discrete documentation requirement no Geneva market methodology addresses. Carrier third-generation attestation specifically probes "AI-influenced portfolio decisions" and requires documented human authorization — but the documentation framework for continuous authorization doesn't exist at boutique scale. The continuous nature of portfolio construction AI makes it architecturally incompatible with documentation frameworks designed for discrete decisions. You cannot apply a one-time authorization model to a continuous AI system.

**Why it matters:** No Geneva methodology for proportionate continuous authorization documentation exists — because this category has not been named. The naming is itself market differentiation: the ICP recognizes "portfolio construction AI authorization chain" as their specific problem, not as a generic AI governance category. Managing directors who hear this term immediately recognize it as naming their architecture problem. No other advisor in Geneva is naming this.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The AI that builds your portfolios is different from the AI that generates reports. It runs continuously. It makes decisions that directly affect client outcomes. The authorization chain from AI output to human approval — that's a discrete documentation requirement nobody in Geneva is addressing. I am."

**Distinct from C214–C299:** Named the continuous authorization architecture problem as a discrete category (not just "portfolio construction AI gap"), emphasizing the architectural incompatibility with discrete decision documentation frameworks.

### Finding C300-P2-F4: Carrier Third-Gen Questionnaire as De Facto nFADP Art. 9 Enforcement Instrument

**What it is:** The carrier's third-generation questionnaire now explicitly asks about AI vendor sub-processors, data termination protocols, and acquisition scenarios — questions that activate nFADP Art. 9 processor chain disclosure obligations the managing director has never evaluated. Years of click-through acceptance on vendor AI terms have been accumulating undisclosed Art. 9 obligations. The carrier is functioning as a de facto nFADP enforcement instrument through questionnaire interrogation — asking questions that trigger obligations under Swiss law that the managing director never knew existed. The effect is regulatory enforcement; the intent is underwriting risk assessment.

**Why it matters:** The managing director who learns about sub-processor disclosure obligations from their carrier's questionnaire learns it after the obligation has already been accumulating for years. The carrier's third-gen questions are now standard, not emerging — they represent the current state of carrier underwriting practice, not a future trend. If a client asks which AI systems process their data, the managing director cannot give a complete answer. They are in present-tense violation of obligations they did not know they had.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The carrier is asking about your AI vendor sub-processors. Those questions are activating nFADP Art. 9 obligations that have been accumulating through years of unevaluated click-through acceptance. The carrier doesn't intend to be an enforcement instrument — but that's what it's becoming. If you're answering those questions without knowing what obligations they're triggering, you're operating with undisclosed exposure."

**Distinct from C214–C299:** Confirmed carrier third-gen questions as standard (not emerging), explicitly named the carrier's function as de facto nFADP enforcement, and framed the obligation accumulation as years of unevaluated acceptance now activating.

### Finding C300-P2-F5: Four-Track Documentation Inconsistency as Active Exposure (Not Theoretical Risk)

**What it is:** Swissdamed, EU AI Act, FINMA Circular 08/2024, and nFADP create four independent documentation streams that describe the same AI governance reality with different formatting, evidence standards, update cycles, and disclosure scopes. No Geneva methodology for cross-referencing these four documentation streams exists. The coherence problem is structurally invisible — the firm doesn't know it's inconsistent until a challenge arrives that reviews multiple tracks simultaneously. In C300, this has moved from theoretical risk to active exposure: managing directors are reporting that they built documentation for each track independently without cross-reference review.

**Why it matters:** The documentation built for the carrier may contradict what was attested to FINMA. The institutional client questionnaire response may describe AI governance in terms that conflict with what was attested to the carrier. Cross-track inconsistency means a firm can be individually adequate across each track while being systematically inconsistent across all of them — and they won't know it until they're in a challenge that reviews multiple tracks simultaneously. No Geneva service manages cross-track coherence.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You built documentation for the carrier. You built documentation for FINMA. You built documentation for nFADP. You built documentation for the institutional questionnaire. Nobody checked whether they were consistent with each other. They may not be. And when a challenge reviews all four tracks at once — that's when you'll find out."

**Distinct from C214–C299:** Named the problem as active exposure (not theoretical risk), with managing directors now explicitly reporting they built tracks independently without cross-reference review.

### Finding C300-P2-F6: Swissdamed and EU AI Act Classification Paralysis as Proportionate Compliance Blocker

**What it is:** Swissdamed Tier 1/Tier 2 classification criteria are written for manufacturers and medical device companies — not for boutique wealth management firms. EU AI Act nexus determination is equally unclear for WM firms with EU clients. The managing director cannot determine which framework applies to their firm and what compliance requires. This creates proportionate compliance paralysis — firms either over-comply or under-comply with no validated middle ground. The classification determines what documentation is required; getting it wrong in either direction creates exposure. No Geneva-calibrated classification assessment methodology exists for boutique WM firms facing these deadlines.

**Why it matters:** Both Swissdamed and EU AI Act are weeks away. The managing director who cannot determine their classification cannot take proportionate compliance action. They are waiting — and the time is passing. The advisory practice's ability to provide Geneva-calibrated classification assessment for WM firms is a specific service that no competitor is offering.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Swissdamed is weeks away. EU AI Act is weeks away. You don't know if you're Tier 1 or Tier 2. You don't know if you're in scope for the EU AI Act. You can't take proportionate compliance action because you can't determine where you fit. I can help you make that determination — and build the documentation that matches your actual classification, not a guess."

**Distinct from C214–C299:** Named classification paralysis as active compliance blocker (not just uncertainty), explicitly framed both deadlines as weeks away with the managing director unable to take proportionate action.
