# Phase 04 Geneva Healthcare — ICP Language (Cycle 288)
## ICP: Independent Practitioners, Small Group Practices (2–10), Geneva + Vaud

---

## How the ICP Talks About AI Problems

### Primary Problem Framing: "It Arrived Before I Decided"

The dominant ICP framing across all cycles is **incremental accumulation without governance decision** — AI arrived through software updates, staff activation, embedded features in existing systems — not through a conscious adoption choice. This is the primary structural finding from C287 and it remains the foundation.

The language patterns below reflect the specific linguistic fingerprints of independent practitioners and small group practice directors in Geneva navigating this situation.

---

## ICP Language Patterns (English)

### On AI Arriving Without Decision

> "The last software update added it. I didn't ask for it, my medical assistant started using it and now I'm responsible for everything it does."

> "I've been using [EMR system] for six years. Three months ago it started suggesting diagnoses. I didn't decide to add AI. It just appeared."

> "My reception staff set up the chatbot. It was part of the package they sold us. I didn't evaluate it for confidentiality."

**Structural signal:** The physician frames themselves as passive recipient of AI functionality they did not authorize. The governance question ("did you decide to adopt this?") and the accountability question ("are you responsible for it?") are in direct conflict — they did not decide, but they are responsible.

### On Vendor Contracts and Professional Liability

> "I signed something. I don't know what I signed. The vendor said it was standard. My medical association said they couldn't advise on vendor contracts."

> "The contract says the vendor is not liable for clinical decisions. I am. But I don't know what the AI is doing inside the system."

> "My lawyer said to read the contract. The contract is 80 pages written for engineers. I'm a physician."

**Structural signal:** The physician understands at an intuitive level that signing vendor agreements creates personal liability exposure, but has no framework for evaluating what they are signing or what it means for their professional liability. The medical association (FMH) does not provide vendor contract review. No Geneva service offers pre-signature review for independent practices.

### On AI-Assisted Diagnosis Support and Liability

> "If the AI suggests a diagnosis and I'm wrong, did the AI make me wrong, or did I make the wrong decision with bad information?"

> "I use it as a check. But if I override it and I'm wrong, does that look like I ignored the AI? If I follow it and I'm wrong, does that look like I outsourced my judgment?"

> "There's no standard for what 'human oversight' means. I could read every AI output carefully and still miss something. Or I could glance at it and be overconfident."

**Structural signal:** The physician has correctly identified the attribution problem — when AI is involved in a clinical decision that goes wrong, the legal and professional responsibility allocation is structurally unclear. Swiss courts have not defined what adequate human oversight of AI-assisted diagnosis looks like. Neither has FMH.

### On Patient Confidentiality in AI Systems

> "The AI processes patient data on a server somewhere. The vendor says it's secure. I don't know where the data goes. I don't know who can access it."

> "I asked the vendor for a copy of their data processing agreement. They sent me a 50-page document. I don't have time to read it. My assistant doesn't either."

> "My patients trust me with their data. I don't know what I'm trusting the vendor with."

**Structural signal:** The physician understands they are accountable for patient data but has no practical method for verifying what happens to it inside AI vendor systems. The "ask the vendor" pathway produces documents that are structurally incomprehensible to independent practitioners.

### On Regulatory Change Without Notification

> "I heard something about Swissdamed. I don't know if it applies to me. I don't know what I need to do."

> "The rules are changing. Nobody tells me when they change. I find out when something goes wrong."

> "I called my insurance broker. He said they were updating the questionnaire. I don't know what that means for me."

**Structural signal:** The physician experiences regulatory change as something that happens to them, not something they participate in shaping. No Geneva infrastructure delivers notification to independent practices when AI governance obligations change.

### On Carrier Attestation as Operative Standard

> "My insurance broker sent me a form. He said I need to sign it to renew. He couldn't tell me what I'm signing."

> "The form asks about AI governance. I don't have any AI governance documentation. I signed it anyway."

> "I don't know what 'adequate AI governance' means. The insurance company is asking me to confirm I'm adequate. I don't know what that means."

**Structural signal:** The carrier questionnaire has become the de facto operative standard for AI governance adequacy in the Geneva market, without FMH validation or Geneva-calibrated interpretation. The physician signs because renewal depends on it, not because they understand what they are committing to.

## ICP Language Patterns (French)

### On AI Arriving Without Decision

> « La dernière mise à jour l'a ajouté. Je ne l'ai pas demandé, mon assistant médical a commencé à l'utiliser et maintenant je suis responsable de tout ce qu'elle fait. »

> « J'utilise [système DMS] depuis six ans. Il y a trois mois, il a commencé à suggérer des diagnostics. Je n'ai pas décidé d'ajouter de l'IA. C'est juste apparu. »

> « Mon personnel de réception a configured le chatbot. C'était inclus dans le forfait qu'ils nous ont vendu. Je ne l'ai pas évalué pour la confidentialité. »

**Signal structure:** Le médecin se perçoit comme destinataire passif d'une fonctionnalité IA qu'il n'a pas autorisée. La question de gouvernance (« avez-vous décidé d'adopter cela ? ») et la question de responsabilité (« êtes-vous responsable ? ») sont en conflit direct.

### On Vendor Contracts and Professional Liability

> « J'ai signé quelque chose. Je ne sais pas ce que j'ai signé. Le fournisseur a dit que c'était standard. Ma association médicale a dit qu'elle ne pouvait pas conseiller sur les contrats fournisseurs. »

> « Le contrat dit que le fournisseur n'est pas responsable des décisions cliniques. Moi, je le suis. Mais je ne sais pas ce que l'IA fait à l'intérieur du système. »

> « Mon avocat a dit de lire le contrat. Le contrat fait 80 pages écrites pour des ingénieurs. Je suis médecin. »

**Signal structure:** Le médecin comprend intuitivement que signer des accords avec les fournisseurs crée une exposition à la responsabilité personnelle, mais n'a pas de cadre pour évaluer ce qu'il signe ou ce que cela signifie pour sa responsabilité professionnelle.

### On AI-Assisted Diagnosis Support and Liability

> « Si l'IA suggère un diagnostic et que j'ai tort, est-ce que l'IA m'a rendu tort, ou est-ce que j'ai pris la mauvaise décision avec de mauvaises informations ? »

> « Je l'utilise comme vérification. Mais si je vais à l'encontre de sa recommandation et que j'ai tort, est-ce que ça ressemble à ignorer l'IA ? Si je la suis et que j'ai tort, est-ce que ça ressemble à une externalisation de mon jugement ? »

> « Il n'y a pas de standard pour ce que signifie la « surveillance humaine ». Je pourrais lire chaque sortie IA attentivement et quand même rater quelque chose. »

**Signal structure:** Le médecin a identifié correctement le problème d'attribution — quand l'IA est impliquée dans une décision clinique qui tourne mal, l'allocation de responsabilité juridique et professionnelle est structurellement unclear.

### On Patient Confidentiality in AI Systems

> « L'IA traite les données des patients sur un serveur quelque part. Le fournisseur dit que c'est sécurisé. Je ne sais pas où vont les données. Je ne sais pas qui peut y accéder. »

> « J'ai demandé au fournisseur une copie de leur accord de traitement des données. Ils m'ont envoyé un document de 50 pages. Je n'ai pas le temps de le lire. Mon assistant non plus. »

**Signal structure:** Le médecin comprend qu'il est responsable des données des patients mais n'a pas de méthode pratique pour vérifier ce qui se passe à l'intérieur des systèmes IA des fournisseurs.

### On Regulatory Change Without Notification

> « J'ai entendu quelque chose au sujet de Swissdamed. Je ne sais pas si cela s'applique à moi. Je ne sais pas ce que je dois faire. »

> « Les règles changent. Personne ne me dit quand elles changent. Je découvre quand quelque chose ne va pas. »

**Signal structure:** Le médecin vit le changement réglementaire comme quelque chose qui lui arrive, pas quelque chose auquel il participe.

### On Carrier Attestation as Operative Standard

> « Mon courtier d'assurance m'a envoyé un formulaire. Il a dit que je dois le signer pour renouveler. Il n'a pas pu me dire ce que je signe. »

> « Le formulaire demande sur la gouvernance IA. Je n'ai pas de documentation de gouvernance IA. Je l'ai signé quand même. »

**Signal structure:** Le questionnaire de l'assureur est devenu la norme opérationnelle de fait pour l'adéquation de la gouvernance IA sur le marché genevois, sans validation FMH ni interprétation adaptée à Genève.

## Key ICP Language Observations (Cycle 288)

1. **"It arrived" framing** — The passive voice ("it was added", "it appeared") is the dominant structural frame for how independent physicians describe their AI situation. This is not hesitation about adoption — it is description of a structural condition: AI governance responsibility without AI governance choice.

2. **Vendor contract comprehension failure** — Physicians accurately describe that vendor contracts transfer liability to them while leaving them without comprehension of what they signed. This is not a reading problem — it is a structural information asymmetry that no market service addresses.

3. **The human oversight ambiguity** — Physicians correctly identify that "human oversight" is an undefined standard in Swiss medical law. They have correctly diagnosed that they could be either over-trusting or under-trusting AI outputs with no calibration framework.

4. **Carrier attestation as personal liability on undefined standard** — Physicians are signing carrier AI attestation forms committing to an "adequacy" they cannot define, with personal liability consequences they have not evaluated, because the alternative is non-renewal.

5. **The notification vacuum** — Physicians experience AI governance regulatory change as something that happens to them through brokers and vendors, not through any direct notification from professional bodies or regulatory institutions.
