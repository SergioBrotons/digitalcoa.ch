## Market Signal 1: Swissdamed Registration Threshold Uncertainty Creating Compliance Paralysis

**Signal type:** Regulatory uncertainty → market paralysis

Independent practices cannot determine whether their AI tools require Swissdamed registration before the July 1, 2026 deadline. Swissmedic's classification criteria require technical understanding of SaMD risk categories that general practitioners do not possess and have no accessible pathway to obtain.

The practical consequence: practices with AI diagnostic imaging or AI symptom triage tools are operating under uncertainty about whether they face immediate July 1 obligations or December 31 grace period provisions, with no accessible classification pathway to resolve the uncertainty. This is not a knowledge gap — it is a structural access gap: the classification pathway exists in regulatory documentation written for medical device manufacturers, not for practicing physicians evaluating their existing tool portfolio.

**Geneva-specific amplification:** Independent practices in Geneva and Vaud cannot access Swissmedic classification guidance proportionate to their scale. The primary pathways — Swissmedic's published guidance documents, industry associations for medical device companies — are designed for manufacturers, not physician practices evaluating clinical AI tools they already use. This creates a specific compliance paralysis for the ICP that larger health systems do not experience.

**NET-NEW finding:** Swissdamed classification pathway inaccessibility at independent practice scale is a discrete market signal distinct from prior cycle Swissdamed deadline awareness (C287 Phase 04 Finding 1). Prior finding named the deadline; this finding identifies the classification access gap as the specific mechanism through which the deadline creates paralysis for independent practices specifically.

## Market Signal 2: FMH Physician Responsibility Guidance Absence Creates Accountability Vacuum

**Signal type:** Professional body signal → market uncertainty

FMH has published AI-related content on its website but has not issued operational guidance on what physician AI governance responsibility means in practice for independent practitioners. The gap between principle-level acknowledgment and operational guidance is structurally significant: physicians understand they are accountable for AI-assisted decisions but have no FMH-calibrated methodology for determining what adequate oversight looks like.

The practical consequence: independent practitioners are navigating personal professional liability exposure for AI-assisted diagnosis support without any Geneva-calibrated professional body guidance defining what the standard requires. FMH's published AI materials acknowledge the issue at the principle level without providing proportionate implementation methodology for 2–10 physician practices.

**Geneva-specific amplification:** No equivalent to the Swiss Medical Association's guidance exists for independent practitioners in Geneva and Vaud. The FMH is the national body; its guidance is calibrated for the full range of Swiss medical practice. Independent Geneva practices have no Geneva-canton-specific calibration of what FMH's general principles require at their specific scale and configuration.

**NET-NEW finding:** FMH guidance absence creating proportionate methodology vacuum at independent practice scale is a discrete market signal. Prior cycles addressed carrier attestation and nFADP obligations without isolating FMH guidance absence as a specific structural gap producing ungoverned physician accountability exposure. This finding names the specific mechanism: principle without methodology produces compliance theater at small-firm scale while accountability continues to accumulate.

## Market Signal 3: AI Medical Record Authorship Ambiguity as Emerging Evidence Problem

**Signal type:** Operational practice signal → legal exposure discovery

Independent practices using AI-assisted documentation tools are producing medical records where the authorship and analytical reasoning chain cannot be attributed to the physician with the clarity medical records require. AI-generated diagnostic suggestions embedded in records, AI-assisted documentation of clinical reasoning, AI-influenced assessment conclusions — all appearing in patient records under the physician's name and medical license.

The practical consequence: when patient records are reviewed in any context — Swissdamed incident reporting, professional liability claims, FMH disciplinary proceedings, insurance audits — the records present AI-influenced conclusions as if they were independently reached by the physician. No methodology exists for documenting the physician's independent analytical contribution versus AI-generated content within the same record.

**Geneva-specific amplification:** Swiss medical records have specific legal weight in professional liability proceedings. The FMH's position on medical record integrity assumes physician-authored content. AI-generated content within physician-maintained records creates a specific documentation gap that Swiss medical law has not addressed: the record is authoritative because it bears the physician's name, but the physician's analytical contribution to the content is not documented.

**NET-NEW finding:** AI medical record authorship ambiguity creating present-tense evidence exposure is distinct from prior cycle findings about AI-assisted diagnosis liability attribution. C287 Phase 04 Finding 3 addressed the decision-making liability question; this finding addresses the record-keeping evidence question that follows from the same structural condition. Physicians are accumulating undocumented AI influence in records that will function as evidence in future proceedings they cannot anticipate.

## Market Signal 4: Carrier AI Attestation Design Logic Produces Physician Compliance Theater

**Signal type:** Market structure signal → compliance failure mechanism

Carrier AI attestation questionnaires are designed by carrier underwriting teams to evaluate risk — not by clinical governance experts to produce adequate physician AI governance. The consequence is a structural mismatch: physicians responding to carrier questionnaires produce documentation that satisfies the carrier's risk evaluation model without addressing the actual AI governance gaps that expose the physician to professional liability.

The practical consequence: physicians who complete carrier attestation questionnaires believing they have addressed AI governance have typically produced documentation that serves carrier underwriting purposes while leaving the underlying governance gaps unaddressed. The documentation creates false adequacy — the physician has something on paper that the carrier accepts, while the actual accountability exposure remains.

**Geneva-specific amplification:** No Geneva market service offers pre-submission carrier questionnaire design review from the physician's professional liability perspective. The service gap means physicians are producing carrier-compliant documentation that does not serve their own accountability interest. The carrier receives what it designed the questionnaire to obtain; the physician receives renewal; the governance gap remains.

**NET-NEW finding:** Carrier questionnaire design logic mismatch creating physician compliance theater is distinct from prior cycle findings about carrier attestation as operative standard. C287 Phase 04 identified the carrier questionnaire as de facto operative standard; this finding identifies the specific mechanism: the standard was designed to solve carrier underwriting problems, not physician governance problems, producing documentation that satisfies the carrier's risk model while leaving the physician's accountability exposure unaddressed.

## Market Signal 5: Independent Practice AI Governance Compound Regulatory Burden with No Dedicated Compliance Pathway

**Signal type:** Regulatory burden signal → market gap

Independent practices face simultaneous AI governance obligations from multiple frameworks — Swissdamed registration requirements, nFADP patient data processing obligations, carrier attestation requirements, FMH professional standards — with no compliance pathway designed for their specific scale and configuration. Each framework operates independently: different authorities, different timelines, different documentation requirements, different consequences for non-compliance.

The practical consequence: a 3-physician Geneva practice navigating AI tool deployment is managing obligations that were designed for pharmaceutical companies (Swissdamed), data protection authorities (nFADP), insurance underwriters (carrier attestation), and national medical associations (FMH guidance) — none of which designed their requirements for 2–10 physician independent practices. The cumulative burden is not additive — it is multiplicative, because the same AI tool triggers obligations under multiple frameworks simultaneously, with no coordination mechanism.

**Geneva-specific amplification:** Independent practices in Geneva face additional cantonal obligations and institutional accountability expectations that compound the national framework burden. The compounding effect is not visible in any single framework's documentation requirements — it only becomes apparent when a practice attempts to manage its total AI governance portfolio across all applicable frameworks simultaneously.

**NET-NEW finding:** Compound multi-framework regulatory burden with no proportionate independent practice compliance pathway is distinct from prior cycle nFADP DPIA obligation findings. C287 Phase 04 identified the DPIA obligation as a discrete requirement; this finding identifies the compound burden of multiple simultaneous framework obligations creating a structural compliance architecture problem that no single framework's documentation requirements capture.

## Market Signal 6: Geneva Cantonal Institutional Caution Signal in Healthcare AI Deployment

**Signal type:** Institutional behavior signal → market formation indicator

Geneva cantonal health authorities and institutional healthcare actors have adopted a cautious posture toward AI deployment in clinical settings — slower adoption, more conservative internal governance requirements, extended evaluation periods — relative to the AI tool proliferation visible in the market. This institutional caution is a signal about risk assessment that independent practices operating at smaller scale do not have access to.

The practical consequence: when independent practices make AI governance decisions, they lack the institutional risk signal that larger Geneva healthcare organizations are implicitly generating through their own cautious adoption postures. The signal is visible to those inside Geneva's institutional healthcare ecosystem; it is not accessible to independent practitioners outside that ecosystem.

**Geneva-specific amplification:** CHUV and other Geneva institutional healthcare actors are actively navigating the same AI governance questions as independent practitioners — Swissdamed classification, nFADP obligations, FMH professional standards — but with significantly more resources, legal support, and institutional infrastructure. Their cautious postures are not published as guidance; they are observed through sustained institutional participation. Independent practices have no access to this signal through formal channels.

**NET-NEW finding:** Geneva institutional caution signal as inaccessible but operationally relevant market intelligence for independent practices is distinct from all prior cycle Phase 04 findings. Prior findings named regulatory gaps, market gaps, and governance absences; this finding identifies the specific mechanism through which institutional risk signals from better-resourced Geneva healthcare actors remain inaccessible to independent practices making autonomous AI governance decisions.
