## The Six NET-NEW Findings (Cycle 288)

### Finding 1: Swissdamed Classification Pathway Inaccessibility Creates Structural Compliance Paralysis

**Finding statement:** The Swissdamed classification pathway exists in regulatory documentation written for medical device manufacturers — not for practicing physicians evaluating their existing AI tool portfolio. This is not a knowledge gap. It is a structural access gap: independent practices cannot determine which tier their AI tools fall into, and the difference between Tier 1 (July 1 immediate deadline) and Tier 2 (December 31 grace period) is operational, not semantic.

**Why this is NET-NEW:** Prior cycle findings named Swissdamed deadline awareness. This finding identifies the specific mechanism — classification pathway designed for manufacturers, not physicians — through which the deadline creates compliance paralysis at independent practice scale.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Swissdamed registration requires classification of your AI tools. The classification criteria are written for medical device manufacturers. You are a physician. These are different audiences, and the documentation does not translate."

---

### Finding 2: FMH Principle Without Operational Methodology Produces Compliance Theater at Small-Practice Scale

**Finding statement:** FMH has published principle-level AI accountability guidance — physician responsibility cannot be delegated, human oversight required, documentation must allow reconstruction. But the principles do not define operational endpoints. At 2–10 physician scale, the same person is user, compliance officer, and billing physician simultaneously. Principle without methodology produces the appearance of compliance without the substance. The physician follows guidance and still does not know whether they are compliant.

**Why this is NET-NEW:** C287 Phase 04 named FMH guidance absence as a market gap. This finding provides the specific mechanism: principle without methodology creates compliance theater, not genuine protection, at independent practice scale where one person holds all three roles simultaneously.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your medical association has acknowledged AI accountability. It has not told you what adequate governance looks like for a practice your size. There is a difference between knowing you are responsible and knowing what responsibility requires."

---

### Finding 3: AI Medical Record Authorship Ambiguity Creates Present-Tense Evidence Exposure

**Finding statement:** AI-assisted documentation tools produce medical records containing AI-generated diagnostic suggestions, AI-influenced assessment conclusions, and AI-assisted clinical reasoning — all appearing under the physician's name and medical license. Swiss medical records have specific legal weight in professional liability proceedings. The records are authoritative because they bear the physician's name, but the physician's analytical contribution to the content is not documented separately from what the AI system produced. When something goes wrong, the physician cannot demonstrate what they independently decided versus what the AI suggested.

**Why this is NET-NEW:** C287 Phase 04 Finding 3 addressed AI diagnosis support liability attribution. This finding addresses the specific record-keeping evidence question that follows from the same structural condition — present-tense accumulation of undocumented AI influence in records that function as evidence in future proceedings.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your medical records contain AI-generated content under your name. When those records are reviewed in an incident proceeding, they will show conclusions you did not independently reach. You cannot reconstruct from the record what you decided versus what the AI suggested."

### Finding 4: Carrier Questionnaire Design Logic Creates Documentation That Serves Underwriting, Not Accountability

**Finding statement:** Carrier AI attestation questionnaires are designed by carrier underwriting teams to evaluate risk — not by clinical governance experts to produce adequate physician AI governance. The documentation physicians produce responding to carrier questions satisfies the carrier's risk model while leaving the underlying governance gaps unaddressed. Physicians who complete carrier attestation believing they have addressed AI governance have typically produced carrier-compliant documentation that creates false adequacy — something on paper the carrier accepts, while actual accountability exposure remains.

**Why this is NET-NEW:** C287 Phase 04 identified carrier attestation as de facto operative standard. This finding identifies the specific mechanism: questionnaire design logic serves carrier underwriting objectives, not physician professional liability protection, producing documentation that satisfies the carrier while leaving the physician's accountability exposure intact.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your insurance carrier designed the AI attestation form to evaluate underwriting risk. You are signing personal commitments to standards written to solve their problem, not yours. The documentation you produce makes the carrier comfortable. It does not make you safe."

---

### Finding 5: Compound Multi-Framework Regulatory Burden Has No Proportionate Coordination Architecture

**Finding statement:** Independent practices face simultaneous AI governance obligations from Swissdamed (registration), nFADP (patient data processing + DPIA), carrier attestation (insurance renewal), and FMH (professional standards) — four frameworks operating independently with different authorities, timelines, documentation requirements, and consequences. The frameworks compound multiplicatively, not additively: the same AI tool triggers obligations under multiple frameworks simultaneously, with no coordination mechanism and no Geneva-calibrated methodology for managing them as an integrated portfolio.

**Why this is NET-NEW:** Prior cycle findings identified individual framework obligations and individual service gaps. This finding identifies the compound multi-framework burden as a distinct structural problem requiring its own coordination architecture — different from addressing each framework separately because the interactions between frameworks create exposure that individual framework compliance does not capture.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Four frameworks apply to your AI tools simultaneously. Swissdamed asks about registration. nFADP asks about patient data processing. Your carrier asks about governance. FMH asks about professional standards. They do not coordinate. You manage all four independently, and the gaps between them are where your exposure lives."

### Finding 6: Geneva Institutional Risk Signals Are Operationally Relevant but Inaccessible to Independent Practices

**Finding statement:** CHUV and other Geneva institutional healthcare actors are generating risk signals about AI deployment through their own cautious adoption postures — signals that are operationally relevant for all Geneva healthcare AI governance decisions, but inaccessible to independent practices operating outside the institutional ecosystem. The signals are not published. They are not communicated through FMH channels. They are observed through sustained institutional participation that independent practitioners do not have access to.

**Why this is NET-NEW:** All prior Phase 04 findings named regulatory gaps, market gaps, and governance absences. This finding identifies the specific mechanism: institutional risk signal inaccessibility means independent practices make AI governance decisions without access to the same operational intelligence that larger Geneva healthcare organizations are using — and the better-resourced organizations are being more conservative.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The largest Geneva healthcare institutions are being more cautious about AI deployment than independent practices. They have more resources, more legal support, more institutional infrastructure. They are watching something. You cannot see what they are watching, because it is not published. It is only visible from inside the ecosystem."

## Summary Positioning Statement (Cycle 288)

Geneva independent practices are not facing an AI adoption problem. They are facing an accountability accumulation problem: AI arrived through software updates, staff activation, and embedded features before any governance decision was made. The physician now carries personal professional liability for AI usage they did not consciously authorize.

The six structural problems identified in this cycle are:
1. Classification pathway inaccessibility — cannot determine which Swissdamed tier applies
2. FMH principle without methodology — compliance theater, not genuine protection
3. Medical record AI authorship ambiguity — evidence exposure accumulating silently
4. Carrier questionnaire designed for underwriting — documentation that satisfies the carrier, not the physician
5. Multi-framework compound burden — no coordination architecture exists
6. Institutional risk signals inaccessible — making decisions without operational intelligence

None of these problems have been named precisely in the Geneva market. None of them are addressed by the market services that currently exist.
