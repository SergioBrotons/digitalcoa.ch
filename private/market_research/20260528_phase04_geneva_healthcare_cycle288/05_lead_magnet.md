## Core Positioning Statement

The Geneva independent practice AI accountability problem is not a technology adoption problem. It is an accountability accumulation problem: AI arrived before governance decisions were made, and the physician now carries personal professional liability for AI usage that was never consciously authorized.

This briefing addresses the specific structural conditions that exist in Geneva independent practices at this moment — conditions that are different from what larger health systems face, different from what the frameworks assume, and different from what any Geneva service has named precisely.

## The Six Structural Problems (Cycle 288)

### Problem 1: Swissdamed Classification Is Technically Accessible but Practically Inaccessible

**What I believed:** Swissdamed registration requirements are defined in published regulatory documents. I can read them and determine whether my AI tools require registration.

**What is actually true:** The classification pathway exists in documentation written for medical device manufacturers, not for practicing physicians evaluating their existing tool portfolio. The technical criteria for SaMD risk classification require understanding that independent practitioners do not have and have no accessible pathway to obtain.

**The specific consequence:** I cannot determine whether my AI diagnostic imaging tool requires July 1 registration or December 31 registration — and the difference is not a scheduling preference. For tools with serious incident obligations, July 1 is not a deadline I am approaching. It is a date that has already passed during which I was operating without registration.

**What this briefing provides:** Classification framework for independent practice AI tool portfolios — enabling me to determine which tools fall into which Swissdamed risk tier and what the specific compliance pathway is for each.

---

### Problem 2: FMH Published Guidance Has No Operational Definition for Independent Practices

**What I believed:** FMH has acknowledged physician AI accountability. Following FMH guidance protects me from professional liability.

**What is actually true:** FMH has published principle-level acknowledgment — physicians are accountable for AI-assisted decisions, human oversight is required, documentation must allow reconstruction of analytical contribution. But FMH has not published what these principles require at the level of specific clinical workflows, specific AI tool configurations, or specific practice scales. Principle without methodology produces compliance theater: I can follow the guidance and still not know whether I am compliant.

**The specific consequence:** When something goes wrong and FMH disciplinary proceedings evaluate my AI governance, they will apply standards that were defined after my decisions were made. The guidance that exists does not define the endpoint I am supposed to be aiming at.

**What this briefing provides:** FMH principle translation into proportionate operational methodology for 2–10 physician independent practices — what the principles require in practice, not just what they state in principle.

---

### Problem 3: My Medical Records Contain AI Content I Cannot Document as Mine

**What I believed:** Medical records I maintain are my documentation of clinical decisions. What is in the record reflects what I decided.

**What is actually true:** AI-assisted documentation tools generate content that becomes part of the medical record under my name and medical license — AI-generated diagnostic suggestions, AI-influenced assessment conclusions, AI-assisted clinical reasoning. When Swissdamed incident reporting, professional liability claims, or FMH disciplinary proceedings require me to demonstrate my analytical contribution, the records present AI-influenced conclusions as if they were independently reached.

**The specific consequence:** My medical records are authoritative because they bear my name. But the physician's analytical contribution to the content is not documented separately from what the AI system produced. I cannot reconstruct from the record what I independently decided versus what the AI suggested that I accepted.

**What this briefing provides:** Medical record AI authorship documentation methodology — how to document my independent analytical contribution in a way that satisfies Swiss medical record integrity requirements while using AI documentation tools.

---

### Problem 4: I Am Signing Carrier Attestation Committing to an Adequacy I Cannot Define

**What I believed:** The insurance carrier's AI attestation form asks whether I have adequate AI governance. If I sign, I have addressed the requirement.

**What is actually true:** The carrier designed the questionnaire to evaluate underwriting risk — not to produce adequate physician AI governance. The documentation I produce responding to carrier questions satisfies the carrier's risk model without addressing the governance gaps that expose me to professional liability. I sign the attestation, receive renewal, and remain in the same accountability exposure I was in before.

**The specific consequence:** I have signed personal commitments to adequacy standards that are undefined, that were written by carrier legal teams without Geneva medical input, and that create personal liability consequences I have not evaluated.

**What this briefing provides:** Carrier attestation design review from my professional liability perspective — what I am committing to when I sign, what the undefined adequacy standard may require of me, and what the documentation gap between carrier compliance and actual accountability protection actually is.

---

### Problem 5: I Am Subject to Four Simultaneous Framework Obligations with No Coordination Architecture

**What I believed:** AI governance compliance means addressing each framework's requirements. Complete the carrier questionnaire. Conduct the DPIA. Register the SaMD tools. Follow FMH guidance.

**What is actually true:** The frameworks operate independently — different authorities, different timelines, different documentation requirements, different consequences. My AI documentation tool simultaneously triggers Swissdamed classification obligations, nFADP Art.22 DPIA requirements, carrier attestation requirements, and FMH professional standards. No coordination mechanism exists. Satisfying one framework's documentation requirements may not affect my position under another, and may in some cases create tension between frameworks.

**The specific consequence:** I am managing four separate compliance tracks simultaneously without any methodology for understanding how they interact, how compliance under one affects exposure under another, or how to allocate limited governance resources across the four frameworks proportionally.

**What this briefing provides:** Multi-framework coordination architecture — assessment of how Swissdamed, nFADP, carrier attestation, and FMH obligations interact, and methodology for managing the four tracks as an integrated portfolio rather than separate tasks.

---

### Problem 6: The Geneva Institutions I Cannot Access Are Sending Signals I Cannot Receive

**What I believed:** If there were a serious AI governance problem in Geneva healthcare, I would hear about it through professional channels.

**What is actually true:** CHUV and other Geneva institutional healthcare actors are navigating the same AI governance questions I am — Swissdamed classification, nFADP obligations, FMH professional standards — with significantly more resources, legal support, and institutional infrastructure. They have adopted cautious postures toward AI deployment in clinical settings. These postures are visible to those inside Geneva's institutional healthcare ecosystem through sustained participation. They are not published. They are not sent to independent practitioners through FMH channels. I am making AI governance decisions without access to risk signals that larger Geneva healthcare organizations are using to guide their own decisions.

**The specific consequence:** My AI governance decisions are made in isolation from the institutional risk assessment that better-resourced Geneva healthcare actors are conducting in parallel. I do not know what they know. I cannot access what they are observing.

**What this briefing provides:** Geneva institutional risk signal intelligence — translated from sustained institutional ecosystem participation into actionable guidance for independent practices making autonomous AI governance decisions.
