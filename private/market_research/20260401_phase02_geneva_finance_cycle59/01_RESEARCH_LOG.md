## ⭐ NET-NEW Findings (Cycle 59 — Fresh Sources)

### 1. FINMA AI Survey: 50% of Swiss Financial Institutions Using AI — BigTech Dependency Increasing
**Source:** FINMA survey (Nov 2024 - Jan 2025), ~400 Swiss financial institutions, finma.ch

FINMA conducted a formal survey of ~400 Swiss financial institutions (banks, securities firms, insurance companies, fund managers). Key findings:
- ~50% already use AI; 25% plan to use within 3 years
- Average: 5 AI applications in use, 9 in development
- **91% using generative AI (chatbots like ChatGPT)**
- **BigTech provider dependency increasing** — most institutions use external providers, not in-house development
- Only 50% have an explicit AI strategy
- Top AI risk priorities: data quality, data protection, explainability
- **FINMA explicitly draws attention to outsourcing risks** (outsourcing to third-party providers = key operational risk)
- FINMA Guidance 08/2024: supervisory observations on AI governance published
- "Same business, same risks, same rules" — technology-neutral supervision principle confirmed

**WM implications (NET-NEW C59):** The BigTech dependency finding directly connects to Swiss tech platform mistrust. WM firms using US-hosted AI (BigTech cloud + chatbots) face compounded regulatory risk: FADP Art. 21/26 + FINMA outsourcing risk guidance. Geneva boutique WMs using US-hosted AI tools are exposed on both the data sovereignty axis AND the FINMA outsourcing oversight axis simultaneously.

### 2. EU AI Act August 2026 — Full Enforcement Creates Swiss WM Pressure
**Source:** Web search synthesis, EY Luxembourg, Oliver Wyman

EU AI Act high-risk AI system obligations fully enforced by **August 2026** for asset managers. Although Switzerland is not in the EU, Swiss WMs serving EU-based clients or operating EU-regulated subsidiaries face EU AI Act obligations. This creates a cross-border regulatory convergence:
- EU AI Act (August 2026) + FINMA AI guidance + Swiss Federal AI Ordinance (Jan 2025)
- Regulators signaling less tolerance for opaque "black box" AI decision-making in creditworthiness and financial services

**WM implications (NET-NEW C59):** Geneva WMs with EU client bases need AI governance that satisfies EU AI Act high-risk standards. The boutique WM that builds EU-compliant AI governance now positions itself ahead of larger institutions.

### 3. Agentic AI Emerging in Wealth Management — Liability Shift
**Source:** Web search synthesis (InvestSuite, Infront)

"Agentic AI" — AI systems capable of autonomously executing complex financial tasks (not just providing advice) — is emerging as a distinct category in 2026 wealth management. Examples: autonomous portfolio rebalancing, automated tax-loss harvesting, AI-driven trade execution within defined parameters.

**WM implications (NET-NEW C59):** Agentic AI fundamentally changes the liability equation. When AI autonomously acts (rather than advises), the WM firm's governance obligation intensifies. The Federal AI Ordinance's "human oversight" requirement becomes a direct operational requirement — not a theoretical one.

### 4. XAI (Explainable AI) as Competitive Differentiator for Swiss WM
**Source:** ZHAW blog (May 2025), web search synthesis

For HNW clients, trust and understanding of AI-driven decisions are paramount. Explainable AI (XAI) solutions provide clarity and confidence — and fulfill both client expectations and FINMA regulatory requirements. "AI models in action must be explainable toward auditors and regulators."

**WM implications (NET-NEW C59):** Swiss-hosted, sovereign AI infrastructure with explainability built in is a direct answer to both FINMA's risk priorities and HNW client expectations. This is a genuine competitive differentiator for Swiss Noxtua-type platforms.

### 5. Geneva SME AI Talent Gap — Governance Implementation Barrier
**Source:** Web search synthesis (Deloitte, Oliver Wyman)

A significant portion of Swiss SMEs (including boutique WMs) lack the in-house AI skills needed to implement and govern AI systems effectively. Modernizing IT and data infrastructure is cited as a critical barrier.

**WM implications (NET-NEW C59):** Boutique Geneva WMs face a double bind: they need AI governance expertise to manage regulatory risk, but they lack the internal resources to build it. This creates a market for external AI governance advisory services — and a vulnerable population of prospects who don't know they need help.
