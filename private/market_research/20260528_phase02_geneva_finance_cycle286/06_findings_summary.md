## Top 6 NET-NEW Findings (C286 vs Prior Cycles)

### Finding 1: Carrier Attestation Chain Confusion

**What changed**: AWS published a 2025 blog specifically highlighting its FINMA ISAE 3000 Type II attestation covering 183 services. Managing directors receive these massive attestation reports and have no idea what to do with them.

**The insight**: The carrier attestation proves the provider's controls. It does NOT prove the wealth manager's governance. The confusion between these two things is creating a documentation gap that institutional clients are now exploiting via AI governance questionnaires.

**The language ICP uses**: *"Je fais quoi avec le rapport ISAE 3000 de mon carrier ?"* ("What do I do with my carrier's ISAE 3000 report?")

---

### Finding 2: Institutional Client DDQ as the Definitive Trigger Moment

**What changed**: The institutional client AI governance questionnaire (DDQ) is no longer occasional — it has become the primary trigger event for Geneva SMB wealth managers to seek help.

**The insight**: Prior cycles identified FINMA inspection fear, nFADP compliance, and AI tool shadow use as entry points. C286 surfaces the institutional DDQ as the moment of reckoning — a specific, bounded, high-stakes event that forces action where abstract regulatory anxiety did not.

**The language ICP uses**: *"Un client institutionnel nous a envoyé un questionnaire sur l'IA de 40 pages. Je ne savais pas quoi répondre."*

---

### Finding 3: SRO AI Obligation Silence

**What changed**: All prior cycles noted FINMA 08/2024 as the primary regulatory signal. C286 confirms that SROs have issued zero AI governance guidance to their members.

**The insight**: Geneva SMB wealth managers who are SRO members are in a regulatory grey zone — FINMA expects AI governance, but their SRO has not told them what to do. This is a genuine policy failure at the SRO level.

**The language ICP uses**: *"Mon SRO n'a rien dit sur l'IA. Est-ce que c'est quand même ma responsabilité ?"*

---

### Finding 4: Art. 63 nFADP Criminal Liability

**What changed**: The revised nFADP includes Art. 63 establishing criminal liability for serious data protection violations. Geneva wealth managers are discovering this applies to AI contexts.

**The insight**: This is not a corporate fine. This is personal criminal liability for individual executives. For a managing director who thought "the worst case is a fine to the company," learning about personal criminal exposure changes the risk calculus entirely.

**The language ICP uses**: *"On m'a dit que le nFADP prévoit des sanctions pénales pour les dirigeants. C'est vrai ? Je peux être personnellement pénalisé ?"*

---

### Finding 5: AI Shadow Use Discovery as Crisis Moment

**What changed**: Prior cycles identified AI shadow use as a risk. C286 surfaces it as a crisis discovery moment — managing directors finding out their staff have been using ChatGPT with confidential client data.

**The insight**: The DDQ is external pressure. The shadow use discovery is an internal crisis. When a managing director discovers sensitive client data has been entered into an uncontrolled AI system, they face an immediate accountability moment.

**The language ICP uses**: *"Un gérant utilisait ChatGPT avec des données clients. Je l'ai découvert par accident. Qu'est-ce que je fais maintenant ?"*

---

### Finding 6: The 90-Day AI Governance Gap

**What changed**: All prior research confirmed the AI governance gap exists. C286 confirms the structure of what "starting from zero" actually means — and that meaningful progress is achievable in 90 days.

**The insight**: The 90-day roadmap (Inventory → Governance Framework → Documentation) is a real, actionable path. Geneva SMB wealth managers do not need enterprise budgets to make meaningful AI governance progress.

**The language ICP uses**: *"Par où on commence ? Je n'ai même pas d'inventaire de nos outils IA."*
