# 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

**Phase:** C311 Phase 03 | **ICP:** Small Consulting Boutiques, 2–50 employees, Geneva | **Date:** 2026-05-31

---

## Market Context

Geneva consulting boutiques occupy a specific position in the market: they serve mid-sized corporates, family offices, and institutional clients who are now conducting vendor due diligence that includes AI governance questionnaires. The consulting boutique's work product is methodology — the specific way the firm approaches a problem, structures analysis, and delivers recommendations. This makes methodology defensibility a core business concern.

The Geneva consulting boutique ICP faces a compound exposure: client questionnaires arriving without warning, sub-consultant AI use outside their visibility, proposal methodology partially AI-assisted but not documented, carrier attestations signed without understanding the commitments, and personal nFADP liability as the responsible person.

---

## Market Problem 1: The Client AI Questionnaire Is a Procurement Trigger, Not a Compliance Exercise

**The problem:** Geneva consulting boutiques are receiving AI governance questionnaires as part of client procurement and renewal processes — not as part of any proactive compliance initiative. The questionnaire arrives without warning. The managing director does not have time to prepare. They respond to it under deadline pressure, not knowing what they're committing to or what documentation they need to have to support their answers.

**The complaint pattern:**
- "This came with the contract renewal — I had two weeks to respond"
- "The client is asking about AI governance but I don't know what they actually want to see"
- "I had to fill in the form and I just guessed at some of the answers"
- "The client asked if we have a policy. We don't. I said we were 'developing one'"

**Why it matters for positioning:**
The client AI questionnaire is a competitive intelligence document — just like the institutional DDQ in Phase 02. The managing director who receives the questionnaire and reads it as a compliance form is already behind. The managing director who reads it as a window into what the client actually requires — before answering — has a significant advantage.

**Urgency signal:** Client procurement timelines are not synchronized with the consulting firm's compliance calendar. The questionnaire arrives when it arrives. The response is due in two weeks. There is no grace period.

---

## Market Problem 2: Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Creates Uncontrolled Exposure

**The problem:** Geneva consulting boutiques work with sub-consultants — specialists, associate firms, independent contractors — who produce work product that enters the client deliverable. Some sub-consultants are using AI tools in their work. The managing director has no systematic framework for knowing which sub-consultants use AI, which AI tools, on which client matters, with what review process.

**The complaint pattern:**
- "I have three sub-consultants I rely on — I don't know what AI tools they're using"
- "If the sub-consultant used AI on the client's work, is that my liability?"
- "I asked one of them and he said 'I use the same tools you do' — I don't know what that means"
- "My sub-consultant sent me a deliverable that was clearly AI-generated and I didn't know how to flag it"

**Why it matters for positioning:**
The authorization chain (identified in Phase 02 for WM) applies here differently: the managing director authorized the sub-consultant's engagement, not the AI tools the sub-consultant used. The sub-consultant's AI use is inside the chain of client responsibility but outside the managing director's direct control. The carrier attestation and the client questionnaire both ask about AI governance without specifying the sub-consultant delegation problem — but both hold the managing director responsible for the entire chain.

**The specific gap:**
No Geneva consulting boutique has a sub-consultant AI delegation framework. They have informal conversations. They have email threads. They do not have a documented, repeatable practice that creates accountability on both sides.

---

## Market Problem 3: Proposal Methodology Is Partially AI-Assisted and Not Documented

**The problem:** Consulting proposals are produced with AI tools — research, drafting, structure, analysis. The managing director cannot fully describe the methodology: what AI was used, at what stage, with what human review, producing what specific output. This is a problem when the client asks how the work was produced — and increasingly, clients are asking.

**The complaint pattern:**
- "The client asked me to explain my methodology and I realized I couldn't fully account for the AI component"
- "I used AI to structure the proposal but I'm not sure how to describe that in the document"
- "What happens if the client says 'show me how you produced this analysis' and part of it was AI-generated?"
- "I don't want to admit I used AI, but I also can't say I didn't"

**Why it matters for positioning:**
Methodology is the consulting firm's product. The client pays for the firm's specific way of solving the problem. If the methodology is partially AI-assisted and the firm cannot describe it clearly, the client has grounds to question the value of what was delivered. The consulting firm that can document its methodology — including the AI components — has a competitive advantage in every client conversation.

**The specific gap:**
Consulting methodology documentation is a distinct practice from IT documentation. The managing director needs a methodology documentation practice that is integrated into their workflow, not a compliance exercise appended after the fact.

---

## Market Problem 4: Carrier Attestation Signed Without Understanding the Commitment

**The problem:** The managing director signed a carrier attestation at renewal — likely including AI governance questions. They signed it without knowing what it requires, without knowing whether their documentation meets the standard, and without knowing what happens when the carrier asks for evidence of compliance.

**The complaint pattern:**
- "I signed something about AI at renewal — I don't think anyone reviewed it closely"
- "The carrier asked if we had documentation and I said yes, but I'm not sure what that means"
- "What happens if the carrier asks me to prove what I put on that form?"
- "I signed in my own name — is that actually a personal commitment?"

**Why it matters for positioning:**
The carrier attestation is a binding commitment. The managing director signed it personally. The gap between what was signed and what exists is the exposure. The pre-signing review — reading the form before signing and identifying exactly what documentation is required — is the highest-value intervention point.

**The specific gap:**
The managing director who signed the carrier attestation without reviewing it against their actual documentation state has an existing exposure. The retroactive reconstruction question — how to document what already happened — is distinct from the forward documentation question. Both need to be addressed.

---

## Market Problem 5: Personal nFADP Exposure Under Article 63 — Vague Awareness, No Specific Action

**The problem:** The managing director has heard that nFADP Article 63 creates personal liability for the "responsible person" — and suspects they are the responsible person. They have not connected the specific circumstances of their consulting practice to the specific triggering conditions of Article 63. They have vague anxiety without specific knowledge.

**The complaint pattern:**
- "I keep hearing about Article 63 — is that actually something I should worry about?"
- "If there's a data issue with the client's project, am I personally liable?"
- "I process personal data through AI tools in my consulting work — does that mean I'm responsible?"
- "I assumed this was a firm-level thing — but I signed in my own name"

**Why it matters for positioning:**
Article 63 applies to the managing director personally, not just the firm. The managing director's instinct that this matters is accurate. But they don't have the specific mechanism to understand what triggers the liability and what specific actions prevent it. The personal liability conversation — framed correctly, specifically, and with actionable resolution — is the conversation that creates real engagement.

---

## Market Problem 6: The Geneva Consulting Boutique Has No AI Governance Advisory Tier

**The problem:** The market for AI governance advisory in Geneva is structured for larger firms. The boutique consulting firm — 2 to 50 employees, managing director personally accountable — has no dedicated advisory tier. They are too small for the big consulting firms, too specialized for generalist advisors, and too busy running their practice to build the AI governance infrastructure themselves.

**The complaint pattern:**
- "I looked into AI governance services — everything I found was designed for banks or large firms"
- "I don't know who to call about this — the problem is too specific for a general consultant"
- "The firms that offer AI governance services don't understand how a boutique consulting firm works"
- "I'm figuring this out on my own because I don't have another option"

**Why it matters for positioning:**
The competitive gap is confirmed: no dedicated boutique-tier AI governance advisory for Geneva consulting firms (Phase 03, 7+ confirmations across multiple ICP cycles). The service that understands how a 5-person Geneva consulting boutique works — what AI tools they use, how they delegate to sub-consultants, what their client questionnaire exposure looks like — occupies a space that no competitor has claimed.

---

## Regulatory Urgency Stack (Phase 03 — Geneva Consulting)

1. **Client AI governance questionnaires** — already arriving as procurement/renewal triggers
2. **nFADP** — operative since September 1, 2023 (~3 years in effect); Article 63 personal liability established
3. **FINMA AI Guidance** — December 2024 baseline applies to supervised entities; consulting firms not directly supervised but client-side expectations converging
4. **EU AI Act** — August 2, 2026 (~15 months); extraterritorial for consulting firms with EU client nexus
5. **Carrier attestation** — signed and live; renewal cycle creates regular re-exposure points

---

## Key Market Signal: Client Questionnaires Are the Forcing Function

The Geneva consulting boutique is not waking up to AI governance proactively. They are being pushed into it by client questionnaires arriving as procurement and renewal triggers. This means the service design must be reactive — ready to respond to the client questionnaire before the managing director tries to answer it — and also proactive — building the documentation architecture before the next questionnaire arrives.

The pre-questionnaire review is the intervention point no one is offering.
