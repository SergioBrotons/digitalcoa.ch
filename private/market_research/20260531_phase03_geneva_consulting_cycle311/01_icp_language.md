# 01 — ICP Language: How Geneva Consulting Boutiques Talk About AI Problems

**Phase:** C311 Phase 03 | **ICP:** Small Consulting Boutiques, 2–50 employees, Geneva | **Date:** 2026-05-31

---

## ICP Definition

Small consulting boutiques in Geneva, 2–50 employees. Managing director or partner personally accountable for client commitments, sub-consultant oversight, and regulatory exposure. Clients are mid-sized corporates, family offices, and institutional entities that are now sending AI governance questionnaires as part of vendor due diligence.

**Primary concerns:**
- Client AI governance questionnaires (received, not yet answered)
- Proposal methodology defensibility (what happens when a client challenges how the work was done)
- Carrier attestation (what they signed, what it requires, what they don't have)
- Sub-consultant AI delegation (what happens when the sub-consultant uses AI on the client's work)
- Personal exposure under nFADP (Article 63 personal liability for the managing director)

---

## The Language They Use

### 1. "The client sent us an AI questionnaire"

**What this means in their world:**
The client — a mid-sized corporate, family office, or institutional counterparty — has sent a vendor due diligence questionnaire that includes AI governance questions. The managing director received it and does not know how to answer it. They did not expect to receive it. It arrived as part of a procurement or renewal process, not as a proactive governance exercise.

**Why this matters:**
This is their first encounter with AI governance as a client-facing accountability problem. They are not thinking about "AI governance" abstractly. They are thinking about the specific question on the specific form they need to return in two weeks.

**Language they use:**
- "We got an AI questionnaire from [client name] — I don't know what they're actually asking for"
- "The client wants to know if we use AI. We do, but I don't know what to put on the form"
- "This came with the contract renewal. I don't think anyone reviewed it before it landed on my desk"

---

### 2. "How do I know what my sub-consultant is actually doing with AI?"

**What this means in their world:**
The managing director delegates work to sub-consultants — specialists, associate firms, independent contractors. Some of these sub-consultants are using AI tools in their work. The managing director has no systematic visibility into what AI tools are being used, on which client matters, with what review process.

**Why this matters:**
They signed the carrier attestation. They signed the client questionnaire. They are personally responsible for the deliverables that went to the client. The sub-consultant's AI use is inside that chain of responsibility — but outside the managing director's knowledge or control.

**Language they use:**
- "I have three sub-consultants I work with — I don't know what tools they're using"
- "If the sub-consultant used AI on the client's work, am I responsible for that?"
- "How do I delegate to someone who uses AI without inheriting their AI decisions?"

---

### 3. "The proposal methodology — how do I defend it if the client questions it?"

**What this means in their world:**
The consulting proposal is built on methodology — the way the firm approaches the problem, structures the analysis, produces the deliverable. If AI tools were used in producing the methodology or the deliverable, the methodology defensibility question becomes acute: can the managing director explain exactly how the work was produced, who did what, and what the AI's role was?

**Why this matters:**
Consulting firms sell methodology as their product. The client's payment is partly for the firm's specific way of solving the problem. If the methodology is partially AI-assisted and the firm cannot describe the methodology clearly, the client has grounds to question the value of what was delivered.

**Language they use:**
- "If the client asks how we produced this analysis — what do I actually say?"
- "I used AI in the proposal work but I can't describe exactly what it did"
- "What happens when the client says 'explain your methodology' and part of it was AI-generated?"

---

### 4. "The carrier form — what did I actually sign?"

**What this means in their world:**
The managing director signed a carrier attestation form — probably as part of a professional liability renewal. The form asked about AI use, AI governance, and documentation. The managing director signed it and now does not know what commitments they actually made.

**Why this matters:**
The carrier form is the binding document. The managing director signed it personally. If the carrier asks for evidence of compliance, the form is the specification. The gap between what was signed and what exists is the exposure.

**Language they use:**
- "I signed something about AI at renewal — I don't know what it requires"
- "The carrier asked if we had AI governance documentation. We didn't. I signed anyway"
- "What happens if the carrier audits what I put on that form?"

---

### 5. "Personal liability under nFADP — is this actually about me?"

**What this means in their world:**
nFADP Article 63 establishes personal liability for the responsible person — typically the managing director or partner. The managing director has heard that Article 63 exists but has not connected it to their specific situation: they are processing personal data through AI tools in their consulting work, they have no nFADP compliance infrastructure, and they signed carrier attestations and client questionnaires without realizing they were the "responsible person."

**Why this matters:**
The personal liability framing — "this is about you, personally" — is the correct framing. The managing director's instinct that this matters is accurate. But they don't have the specific mechanism to understand what triggers the liability and what prevents it.

**Language they use:**
- "I keep hearing about Article 63 — is that actually something I should be worried about?"
- "If there's a data issue with the client's project, am I personally liable?"
- "I assumed this was a firm-level thing — is it actually my personal exposure?"

---

## The "Adequate" Problem (Cross-Phase Pattern)

The word "adequate" appears in carrier attestation forms and is undefined in all Swiss regulatory instruments. The Geneva consulting ICP has the same "adequate" problem as the legal and finance ICPs — they don't know what their documentation standard is supposed to be, they don't know what "adequate" looks like for a boutique consulting firm, and no one has told them.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "The word 'adequate' on your carrier form has never been defined for a Geneva consulting boutique. You need to be able to describe what your AI governance looks like specifically — in writing — before someone asks you to prove it."

## Language Patterns by ICP Concern

| Concern | How they phrase it | What they actually mean |
|---------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| Client AI questionnaire | "The client sent us a form" | I don't know how to answer it |
| Sub-consultant AI delegation | "I don't know what my sub-consultants are doing with AI" | I'm responsible for things I can't control |
| Methodology defensibility | "How do I explain how this work was produced?" | I used AI but I can't describe what it did |
| Carrier attestation | "I signed something about AI" | I don't know what it requires or if I comply |
| Personal nFADP liability | "Is Article 63 actually about me?" | I signed in my own name — am I exposed? |

---

## Synthesis: What This ICP Actually Needs

The Geneva consulting ICP needs:

1. **A way to read the client AI questionnaire before answering it** — and to understand what the questions actually commit them to
2. **A sub-consultant AI delegation framework** — something they can put in place with their sub-consultants that creates accountability without requiring 50 conversations about AI tools
3. **A methodology documentation practice** — a way to describe how work was produced that includes the AI component without being embarrassed about it
4. **A carrier form pre-review** — before they sign, a clear answer to what they are committing to and whether their documentation exists
5. **A personal nFADP exposure assessment** — a direct answer to whether Article 63 applies to them, in what specific circumstances, and what specific actions prevent the exposure
