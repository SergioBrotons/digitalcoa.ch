# 06 — Findings Summary: Key Insights and Ready-to-Use Language

**Phase:** C311 Phase 03 | **ICP:** Small Consulting Boutiques, 2–50 employees, Geneva | **Date:** 2026-05-31

## NET-NEW Findings (Distinct from C214–C311 Phase 01 + Phase 02)

Building on C311 Phase 01 (Legal — carrier form structural shift, OaF survey, junior associate AI surface, retroactive reconstruction, "adequate" word, personal liability) and Phase 02 (Finance — institutional DDQ as competitive intelligence, authorization chain compound liability, model drift live documentation obligation, institutional > FINMA standard, two-document false security, multi-mandate coherence), Phase 03 delivers 6 NET-NEW findings specific to the Geneva consulting boutique ICP.

### Finding 1: Client AI Questionnaire Pre-Review — The Forcing Function Before Compliance Becomes Active

**The insight:** The Geneva consulting boutique is not proactively building AI governance documentation. They are being pushed into it by client AI questionnaires that arrive as procurement and renewal triggers — with two-week deadlines and no preparation time. The questionnaire is the forcing function, not compliance intent. The service that reads the questionnaire before the managing director answers it — and identifies exactly what the questions commit them to — occupies the highest-value intervention point in the client relationship.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "The client didn't send you an AI governance questionnaire because they're curious. They sent it because their own regulators, their own board, their own risk committee requires them to verify that their vendors have AI governance practices. You have two weeks to respond. The question is whether you answer it with evidence or with guesses."

**Why it matters for positioning:** This is the same pre-signing intelligence model that worked in Phase 02 (institutional DDQ) — but applied to the consulting firm's client questionnaire. The managing director who reads the client questionnaire before answering has a significant advantage over the one who responds under deadline pressure. No competitor is offering this service.

---

### Finding 2: Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Is a Structural Gap — Not an Oversight Problem

**The insight:** The Geneva consulting boutique works with sub-consultants — specialists, associate firms, independent contractors. The managing director authorized the sub-consultant's engagement, not the AI tools they use. The sub-consultant's AI use is inside the chain of client responsibility but outside the managing director's direct control. This is not an oversight problem — it is a structural gap in how the consulting industry thinks about AI delegation. No Geneva consulting boutique has a documented, repeatable sub-consultant AI delegation framework.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "You authorized the sub-consultant's engagement. You didn't authorize the AI tools they used. But you're responsible for the deliverable they produced — including any AI component inside it. Your carrier asks about it. Your client questionnaire asks about it. Article 63 makes you responsible for it. And you have no systematic way to know what's happening."

**Why it matters for positioning:** The sub-consultant delegation framework is the operational gap that connects the managing director to the compound liability structure (Phase 02) in a new domain. The consulting firm that builds the framework — disclosure requirement, methodology documentation obligation, human review certification — creates accountability without requiring 50 conversations about AI tools. It is specific, defensible, and no competitor has built it for the Geneva consulting boutique.

---

### Finding 3: Proposal Methodology Defensibility Is a Competitive Differentiation — Not Just a Compliance Problem

**The insight:** The consulting firm's methodology is its product. The client pays for the firm's specific way of solving the problem. If the methodology is partially AI-assisted and the firm cannot describe it clearly, the client has grounds to question the value of what was delivered. The consulting firm that can document its AI-assisted methodology — by stage, with human review verification — has a competitive advantage in every client conversation that goes beyond compliance.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "Your methodology is your product. The client is paying for your specific way of solving the problem. If part of that methodology was AI-assisted and you can't describe it — that's a methodology credibility problem, not just a compliance problem. And when the client asks how you produced this analysis, and you can't fully answer — that's the moment they start questioning everything."

**Why it matters for positioning:** The methodology documentation service is not just a compliance service — it is a business development service. The firm that can clearly describe how its work was produced has an advantage in client conversations, proposal defense, and relationship continuity. This framing — business development, not just compliance — creates a different entry point for the managing director who is not yet in compliance panic mode.

---

### Finding 4: Retroactive Methodology Reconstruction — The Already-Exposed Consulting Firm

**The insight:** The consulting firm that has already produced AI-assisted deliverables without methodology documentation has an existing exposure. The client may have asked how the work was produced. The carrier may have asked for evidence. The managing director needs a credible retroactive reconstruction — not just a forward documentation practice. Retroactive methodology reconstruction is technically possible but legally fragile if not done correctly.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "Retroactive documentation is not the same as having documented at the time. If you've already produced AI-assisted deliverables without methodology documentation — the question isn't whether to do it. It's how to do it so it actually holds. You need to be able to say: this is what existed, this is what we did, this is how we verified it — and you need that to be credible under scrutiny."

**Why it matters for positioning:** The retroactive solution is distinct from the forward documentation solution — and both need to be available. The managing director who already has the exposure (has already responded to a client questionnaire, has already signed a carrier form) needs the retroactive path before the forward path. This is the same pattern identified in Phase 01 (Legal) — but applied to consulting methodology rather than carrier attestation.

---

### Finding 5: The Carrier Form Pre-Review — Before Signing, Not After

**The insight:** The managing director signed the carrier attestation at renewal — probably under deadline pressure, probably without reading the questions carefully, probably without knowing what "adequate" means for their firm. The carrier form is now asking for documentation infrastructure that doesn't exist. The pre-signing review — reading the form before signing and identifying exactly what the questions require, exactly what documentation exists, exactly what gaps need to be filled — is the intervention that prevents the exposure.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "The form you signed asked questions that require documentation you may not have. 'Adequate' has never been defined — not by your carrier, not by any regulator — for a Geneva consulting boutique. The pre-signing review tells you exactly what you committed to, exactly what you have, exactly what you need to build before the carrier asks for evidence."

**Why it matters for positioning:** The pre-signing carrier review is the same intervention point identified in Phase 01 (Legal) and Phase 02 (Finance). For the consulting boutique, it is specifically about: the AI governance policy, the sub-consultant delegation framework, the methodology documentation, and the personal Article 63 exposure assessment. The carrier form is the design specification — read it before you sign it.

---

### Finding 6: Personal nFADP Article 63 Exposure Is the Correct Framing — and It's the Conversation Starter

**The insight:** The managing director's first response to AI governance anxiety is personal: "I signed this in my own name." This instinct is accurate. Article 63 creates personal liability for the responsible person — and the managing director of a Geneva consulting boutique is almost certainly the responsible person. The solution must address the personal liability dimension directly, not redirect to firm-level risk.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "Article 63 applies to you personally. Not the firm. You. Every client deliverable that involved AI processing — every sub-consultant whose work entered your client's output — you've been responsible for it without documentation. That's the exposure. And it's personal."

**Why it matters for positioning:** The personal liability framing — "this is about you, personally" — is the conversation starter that creates real engagement. The managing director who has been vaguely anxious about "AI governance" suddenly has a concrete reason to act when they understand that Article 63 is personal, that the carrier form signature is personal, that the client questionnaire response is personal. This is the register that Phase 01 (Legal) found worked for attorneys — and it works equally for managing directors of consulting boutiques.

> "The client sent you an AI questionnaire. Before you try to answer it — let me read it. I'll tell you exactly what you're committing to, what evidence you need, and whether you have it."

### Sub-Consultant Delegation Problem
> "You authorized the sub-consultant. You didn't authorize the AI tools they used. But you're responsible for the deliverable — including any AI inside it. That's not an oversight. That's a structural gap in how the consulting industry thinks about delegation."

### Methodology Defensibility
> "Your methodology is your product. The client pays for your specific way of solving the problem. If you can't describe it — including the AI component — that's not just a compliance problem. That's a methodology credibility problem."

### "What is adequate" Resolution
> "Adequate means: for every AI system in your client work, you can document who authorized it, how you review it, how you monitor it, and how your sub-consultants' AI use is managed. That's the definition. Now the question is whether your documentation exists."

### Personal Article 63 Exposure
> "Article 63 is personal. Not the firm. You. Every AI processing decision in your client work — you've been the responsible person without knowing it. That's the exposure we need to address."

### Retroactive Solution (For Already-Exposed Firms)
> "If you've already answered the client questionnaire and guessed — or already signed the carrier form without documentation — we need to build the retroactive record differently than if you're preparing for a future questionnaire. Both are solvable. But they're not the same solution."

### Carrier Form Pre-Review
> "Before you sign your next carrier renewal — let me read the form. I'll tell you exactly what you're committing to, and exactly where your documentation stands against it."
