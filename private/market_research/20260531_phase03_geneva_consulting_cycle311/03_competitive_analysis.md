# 03 — Competitive Analysis: What Exists in Market vs. What's Missing

**Phase:** C311 Phase 03 | **ICP:** Small Consulting Boutiques, 2–50 employees, Geneva | **Date:** 2026-05-31

---

## Competitive Landscape Overview

The Geneva consulting boutique AI governance market has the same confirmed competitive gap as Phase 01 (Legal) and Phase 02 (Finance): no dedicated advisory tier for boutique-scale consulting firms. What exists in the market falls into three categories: technology enablers, large-firm consultancies, and generalist advisors. None of them serve the Geneva consulting boutique.

---

## Category 1: Technology Enablers (Not Competitors — Enablers)

Technology providers offer AI governance tools — compliance platforms, document management systems, DPIA automation. They are not advisors. They do not read client questionnaires, review carrier forms, or help the managing director understand what they signed.

**Named providers:**
- **Swiss Compliance AI** — GraphRAG for Swiss financial compliance
- **Modulos AI** — AI GRC solutions, enterprise-grade
- **OneTrust / TrustArc** — global data privacy platforms
- **Vanta / Drata** — automated compliance platforms (US-origin, growing EU presence)
- **Secureframe** — automated compliance (US-origin)

**Why they don't serve the Geneva consulting boutique:**
The Geneva consulting boutique — 2 to 50 employees, managing director personally accountable, client questionnaire trigger, sub-consultant delegation problem — does not need a compliance automation platform. They need an advisor who understands their specific situation and can produce the documentation they need to answer the client questionnaire and comply with the carrier attestation. Technology enablers sell tools; the managing director needs a person.

---

## Category 2: Large-Firm Consultancies (Not Direct Competitors — Out of Scope)

Large consultancies (Big Four, major strategy firms) offer AI governance advisory services. They serve large enterprises, financial institutions, and multinationals. Their engagement model, pricing, and delivery structure are designed for organizations with dedicated compliance teams — not for a 5-person Geneva boutique with a managing director who does everything.

**Named providers:**
- **Deloitte Switzerland** — AI governance, data privacy, regulatory compliance
- **EY Switzerland** — AI risk and compliance advisory
- **KPMG Switzerland** — digital risk and AI governance
- **PwC Switzerland** — AI governance and regulatory strategy

**Why they don't serve the Geneva consulting boutique:**
- Engagement minimums are designed for large enterprises
- Delivery teams are structured for enterprise compliance functions
- Pricing is calibrated for organizations with dedicated legal and compliance budgets
- The boutique consulting firm is not their ICP
- They do not understand how a consulting boutique works — methodology, sub-consultant delegation, client questionnaire response

---

## Category 3: Generalist Advisors (Not Direct Competitors — Wrong Tier)

Generalist business consultants and management advisors offer advice on business processes, technology adoption, and regulatory compliance. Some have added "AI governance" to their service description. None of them specialize in the Geneva consulting boutique's specific problem set.

**Why they don't serve the Geneva consulting boutique:**
- They do not understand the sub-consultant delegation problem
- They do not understand the proposal methodology defensibility question
- They do not understand the carrier attestation pre-review need
- They do not speak the managing director's language — personal liability, client questionnaire, methodology documentation
- They are not bilingual (EN + FR) in their Swiss market delivery

---

## The Unoccupied Position: Boutique AI Governance Advisory for Geneva Consulting

**What the market is missing:**

A dedicated advisory service for Geneva consulting boutiques — 2 to 50 employees, managing director personally accountable — that understands the specific problems:
1. Client AI governance questionnaire response (pre-review before answering)
2. Sub-consultant AI delegation framework (documented, repeatable)
3. Proposal methodology documentation (AI components included, defensible)
4. Carrier attestation pre-review (before signing, clear commitments)
5. Personal nFADP Article 63 exposure assessment (specific to the managing director's situation)
6. Retroactive documentation reconstruction (for firms already exposed)

**The specific positioning gap:**

The managing director of a Geneva consulting boutique needs an advisor who:
- Understands how a boutique consulting firm works (not a bank, not a large enterprise)
- Speaks French and English
- Has read the carrier form before the MD signs it
- Reads the client AI questionnaire as a design specification before the MD answers it
- Can produce the documentation the MD needs, not just advise on the framework
- Understands the sub-consultant delegation chain and how to document it
- Can connect the carrier attestation to the client questionnaire to the nFADP personal liability — as one integrated problem

**No one is doing this for Geneva consulting boutiques.**

## The Broker Channel (Confirmed from Phase 02)

The broker — insurance broker, business broker, M&A advisor — is the primary intelligence channel for the Geneva consulting boutique. The broker hears about client questionnaires before the managing director receives them. The broker knows which carriers are adding AI governance questions to their attestation forms. The broker is the early warning system.

**From Phase 02 finding:** Broker primary intelligence channel confirmed. The broker who flags the client questionnaire before it arrives — and refers the managing director to an advisor who can read it before it's answered — is the broker who keeps the client relationship intact.

**Phase 03 application:** The broker channel applies equally to consulting boutiques. The broker who refers a Geneva consulting boutique to a service that reads the client AI questionnaire before it's answered — and produces the documentation needed to respond credibly — is the broker who becomes the trusted referral partner.

## NET-NEW Competitive Findings for Phase 03

### Finding 1: Client AI Questionnaire Pre-Review — The Unoccupied Intervention Point

No competitor offers a pre-review service — reading the client questionnaire before the managing director answers it, identifying exactly what the questions commit them to, and producing the documentation needed to respond credibly. This is the highest-value intervention point. The managing director who receives the questionnaire and reads it as a design specification — before answering — has a significant advantage over the one who responds under deadline pressure.

### Finding 2: Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Framework — The Structural Gap

No consulting firm in Geneva has a documented, repeatable sub-consultant AI delegation framework. The problem is structural — the managing director authorized the sub-consultant's engagement, not the AI tools they use. The documentation gap is industry-wide, not firm-specific. The service that builds the framework for the firm — and trains the managing director to use it — occupies a position that no competitor has claimed.

### Finding 3: Methodology Documentation as Consulting Product — The Defensibility Differentiation

The consulting firm's methodology is its product. The client pays for the firm's specific way of solving the problem. The service that helps the firm document its methodology — including the AI components — creates a competitive differentiation that goes beyond compliance. The firm that can clearly describe how its work was produced has an advantage in every client conversation. This is not a compliance service — it is a business development service.

### Finding 4: Carrier Form Pre-Review Before Signing — The Before, Not After

The pre-signing carrier form review is the intervention point that no competitor offers. Every carrier attestation is signed under renewal deadline pressure. The managing director who reads the form before signing — and understands exactly what they are committing to, and whether their documentation exists — is the managing director who avoids the retroactive documentation problem. The service that reads the form and tells the MD exactly what it requires, and where they stand, is the service that prevents the exposure.

### Finding 5: Retroactive Methodology Reconstruction — The Already-Exposed Firm

The consulting firm that has already produced AI-assisted deliverables without methodology documentation has an existing exposure. The client may have asked how the work was produced. The carrier may have asked for evidence. The managing director needs a credible retroactive reconstruction — not just a forward documentation practice. This is a distinct service from the forward documentation service, and no competitor offers it specifically for consulting methodology.

### Finding 6: Bilingual EN + FR Consulting Boutique Positioning — The Language Gap

No competitor is positioned specifically for the Geneva consulting boutique in both English and French. The large-firm consultancies operate in English. The local advisors operate in French. The bilingual advisory service — built for the Geneva boutique, delivered in the managing director's language — fills a positioning gap that is not addressed by any competitor in the market.
