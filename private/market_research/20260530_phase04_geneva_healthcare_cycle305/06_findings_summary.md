# 06 — Findings Summary: Key Insights and Ready-to-Use Language
## Phase 04 C305 | Geneva Healthcare | Independent Physician / Small Group Practice (2–10 physicians)

## Six NET-NEW Findings: Phase 04 C305

### Finding 1 of 6: EHR Vendor Post-Sale AI Feature Activation as Unilateral Liability Expansion Without Physician Decision Event

**The finding:** Independent practices report that EHR systems are activating AI features through software updates without physician notification, requiring physician action to deactivate, or not offering deactivation without losing core EHR functionality. The physician's professional accountability surface expands through the vendor's product development decisions — not through any physician decision event. The physician cannot enumerate their AI governance surface because it is partially defined by a vendor's update schedule they did not agree to review.

**The mechanism:** Vendor product evolution becomes physician liability expansion event. Attestation certifying governance adequacy covers a surface that has been modified without physician knowledge or documentation opportunity.

**Why this is distinct from C214–C304:**
This finding names the specific mechanism by which EHR vendors unilaterally expand physician accountability through post-sale software updates — distinct from all prior Phase 04 findings addressing AI tool adoption (which implies physician decision) or documentation voids (which address record-level gaps). Named as a discrete structural mechanism creating liability expansion without decision event. Prior C297 Finding 2 addressed multi-physician internal diffusion — this addresses vendor-initiated expansion without any physician involvement.

---

### Finding 2 of 6: Non-Physician Staff AI Use Under Physician Supervision as Unnamed Accountability Exposure

**The finding:** Medical assistants in Geneva independent practices use AI tools for clinical documentation, patient communication, and administrative tasks under the physician's professional authority. No Geneva-calibrated protocol defines what "adequate supervision" means when the supervised activity involves AI decision support. The AI tool has its own decision logic that is not supervised in the way human delegation is supervised. The physician is accountable for AI-assisted decisions they cannot review — not because they delegated poorly, but because AI delegation does not map to human delegation logic.

**The mechanism:** Delegation vocabulary used for AI-assisted activities that have fundamentally different accountability characteristics than human delegation. The physician cannot review what the AI tool decided — only the output. The record bears the physician's professional authority regardless of the AI contribution.

**Why this is distinct from C214–C304:**
This finding names the specific accountability gap created by staff AI use under physician supervision as a discrete named problem — distinct from all prior Phase 04 findings addressing physician's own AI use, AI documentation voids, or multi-physician internal diffusion. This finding addresses the specific delegation-accountability mismatch that arises when AI tools are interposed between physician authority and staff action. Prior findings named delegation gaps and multi-physician gaps without isolating this specific staff-delegation-to-AI configuration as a named accountability exposure.

---

### Finding 3 of 6: FADP Art.21 + Art.22 Compound Obligation as Mass Violation in Progress

**The finding:** FADP Art.21 requires disclosure, explanation, and human review rights for AI-influenced automated decisions. FADP Art.22 requires reversal capability for AI-influenced decisions. Independent practices have no mechanism to identify which patient records contain AI-influenced decisions, no disclosure process, and no Art.22 reversal handling procedure for clinical documentation. The two obligations are simultaneously active and partially incompatible: Art.22 reversal rights require record modification that conflicts with medical record integrity requirements. Independent practices are accumulating violations daily without enumeration capability.

**The mechanism:** The FADP automated decision framework was designed for contexts where AI decision logic is known and documented. Independent practice AI integration produces AI-influenced decisions that are invisible in the record — making the obligation to disclose and reverse decisions that cannot be identified as AI-influenced.

**Why this is distinct from C214–C304:**
This finding names the specific Art.21 + Art.22 compound obligation as a discrete mass violation mechanism — distinct from all prior Phase 04 findings addressing FADP Art.9 processor chains, Art.63 criminal liability, or nFADP lawful basis documentation without isolating the specific Art.21 + Art.22 incompatibility in clinical documentation context. The finding that these two obligations are simultaneously active, partially incompatible, and accumulating daily is new as a named structural finding.

---

### Finding 4 of 6: Carrier Attestation Asymmetry as Personal Liability on Unknown Terms

**The finding:** Swiss medical liability carriers send AI governance attestation questionnaires to independent practices. The attestation standard is defined by the carrier's underwriting logic — not by Geneva medical professional standards, FMH human oversight definitions, or Art.63 criminal standards. The physician signs certifying to a standard the carrier has not published, without pre-signing review, without evidence mapping to three different accountability standards (carrier, FMH, Art.63), and without knowing what their complete AI surface consists of. The signature creates personal liability on carrier-defined terms within carrier-defined timeline for a standard no one has defined.

**The mechanism:** The attestation creates a documentation artifact (the signed form) that satisfies the carrier's underwriting risk assessment while simultaneously creating personal liability exposure under evidence standards the carrier's form does not address. Carrier coverage and carrier-defined attestation do not protect against FMH professional challenge or Art.63 criminal exposure.

**Why this is distinct from C214–C304:**
This finding names the specific three-way evidence standard asymmetry (carrier attestation vs. FMH human oversight vs. Art.63 criminal) as the primary structural problem — distinct from C297 Finding 1 which named the urgency-driven self-incriminating documentation pattern and from all prior Phase 04 findings addressing carrier attestation without isolating the three-way evidence standard mapping gap as the core structural mechanism.

---

### Finding 5 of 6: AI Tool Discontinuation Decision as Named Governance Event With No Documentation Protocol

**The finding:** When a Geneva independent practice identifies risk with an AI tool and discontinues its use, no Geneva protocol exists for documenting: the reason for discontinuation, the timeline and process of the decision, what happened to patient data processed by that tool, or what the practice's alternative approach is. The discontinuation decision — the moment of highest physician governance awareness — is the governance event least likely to be documented. If a question arises about past decisions made with the tool, the practice has no evidence of the governance response.

**The mechanism:** Existing governance frameworks address AI tool adoption — not AI tool abandonment. The governance documentation gap is most acute at the moment of tool discontinuation, which is the moment when the physician has the clearest understanding of the tool's risk profile. Post-incident review cannot reconstruct a decision that was never documented.

**Why this is distinct from C214–C304:**
This finding names AI tool discontinuation as a discrete named governance decision category requiring documentation — distinct from all prior Phase 04 findings addressing documentation voids in forward-looking AI use (records going forward) or post-incident review gaps (C297 Finding 4). This finding addresses the specific governance documentation gap at the tool discontinuation decision point, which has not been named as a discrete governance event in prior cycles.

---

### Finding 6 of 6: Art.321 CP + Vendor Model Training + Art.63 as Unseen Personal Criminal Exposure

**The finding:** Vendor terms of service accepted by click-through in independent practice AI tool activation include provisions allowing vendor use of de-identified or aggregate patient data for AI model training and improvement. These provisions extend the physician's Art.321 CP professional secrecy obligations to data in vendor-accessible systems. The physician did not evaluate these provisions. The physician cannot stop model training provisions without losing core AI tool functionality. Art.63 FADP creates personal criminal exposure — up to CHF 250,000 and imprisonment for willful violations — for professional secrecy breaches involving this vendor-processed data. The question of whether accepting vendor terms without review constitutes "willful" non-compliance under Art.63 has not been tested.

**The mechanism:** The physician's Art.321 CP criminal exposure is partially defined by vendor contract provisions the physician did not evaluate. The physician's criminal risk and the vendor's commercial interest in model training exist in direct conflict. The physician cannot eliminate the criminal exposure without losing AI tool functionality — creating a structurally enforced choice between operational necessity and criminal exposure.

**Why this is distinct from C214–C304:**
This finding names the specific Art.321 CP + vendor model training + Art.63 intersection as a discrete personal criminal exposure mechanism — distinct from all prior Phase 04 findings addressing Art.321 CP data flows or FADP obligations without isolating this specific vendor model training provision + Art.63 willful standard question as the primary exposure mechanism. Named as compound exposure distinct from simple processor chain findings (Art.9) or single-obligation findings (Art.21).

## Ready-to-Use Language for ICP Engagement

### Opening Recast: The Structural Problem

**What the ICP says:** "I think I have a problem with AI governance"
**What they actually mean:** "I have signed something, my staff use things, my system updated, and I cannot enumerate any of it"
**Recast:** "Ce que vous décrivez est quelque chose que je vois systématiquement dans les cabinets indépendants genevois. Ce n'est pas une failure personnelle — c'est un problème structurel. Les cadres n'ont pas été écrits pour des cabinets de votre taille."

Use when: Physician calls with general AI concern, needs structural grounding before specific problem naming

### The Delegation Problem

**What the ICP says:** "My assistant uses AI for documentation under my supervision"
**What they actually mean:** "I am professionally accountable for something I cannot review"
**Recast:** "Quand votre assistant(e) utilise un outil IA sous votre supervision, votre responsabilité professionnelle suit la signature — pas la chaîne de délégation. Vous ne pouvez pas superviser la logique de décision de l'outil IA comme vous supervisez les décisions humaines. C'est un problème structurel, pas un problème de compétence."

Use when: Staff AI use is the primary concern, need to name the structural accountability gap clearly

### The Vendor Update Problem

**What the ICP says:** "My EHR added AI features — I'm not sure what changed"
**What they actually mean:** "My accountability surface expanded through a vendor's product decision, without my knowledge"
**Recast:** "La surface de responsabilité que vous devez gouverner est partiellement définie par le calendrier de développement produit de votre vendor. Vous n'avez pas pris de décision — mais votre exposition a changé. C'est un problème structurel que presque personne à Genève ne nomme clairement."

Use when: EHR vendor update or AI feature activation is the presenting concern

### The Art.21 Problem

**What the ICP says:** "I'm not sure what the nFADP requires for AI decisions"
**What they actually mean:** "I am accumulating obligations I cannot meet and cannot enumerate"
**Recast:** "L'article 21 vous oblige à informer les patients quand une décision est influencée par l'IA, et à leur donner un droit d'explication et de révision humaine. Vous n'avez pas de mécanisme pour identifier quelles décisions sont influencées par l'IA dans vos dossiers. L'obligation existe. L'infrastructure pour la satisfaire ne'existe pas. C'est une violation cumulée — pas une violation intentionnelle."

Use when: nFADP AI obligations are the presenting concern, need to convey urgency without creating panic

### The Attestation Signature Problem

**What the ICP says:** "I signed the carrier attestation to keep coverage — I'm not sure what I certified"
**What they actually mean:** "I created personal liability on unknown terms under time pressure"
**Recast:** "Vous avez signé quelque chose qui crée une responsabilité personnelle sur des termes que l'assureur n'a pas publiés, dans un délai que l'assureur a défini, pour une norme que personne n'a définie. Ce n'est pas votre faute — c'est un problème structurel. Mais la signature reste la vôtre."

Use when: Carrier attestation signature is the presenting concern, need to reframe without creating paralysis

### The Evidence Problem

**What the ICP says:** "If something went wrong, I couldn't prove what the AI did"
**What they actually mean:** "The record is evidence of my decisions, not evidence of what the AI contributed"
**Recast:** "La révision du dossier procédera comme si la décision avait été prise sans assistance IA. Parce que c'est ce que le dossier montre. Vous ne pouvez pas prouver ce que l'outil IA a contribué — et vous ne pouvez pas prouver ce que vous avez décidé malgré l'outil. C'est un problème architectural, pas un problème de qualité des soins."

Use when: Post-incident concern or adverse outcome in practice is the presenting situation
