# 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Complaints, Failures
## Phase 04 C305 | Geneva Healthcare | Independent Physician / Small Group Practice (2–10 physicians)

---

## Active Market Signals: Geneva Healthcare AI Accountability C305

### Signal 1: EHR Vendor Post-Sale AI Feature Activation as Physician Liability Expansion Without Decision Event

**The signal:** Independent practices reporting that EHR systems are activating AI features through software updates without notification, requiring physician action to deactivate, or in some cases not offering deactivation without losing core EHR functionality. The physician's professional accountability surface expands through the vendor's product development decisions — not through any physician decision event.

**What this means operationally:** The physician's AI governance surface is partially defined by the vendor's update schedule. Attesting to "adequate AI governance" requires knowing what the vendor has added since the last review. Most practices have no update review process.

**Why this is intensifying:** AI feature integration is accelerating across all major Swiss EHR platforms. The window between feature activation and physician awareness is widening.

**Complaint verbatim (reconstructed):** *"Notre EHR a ajouté des fonctions IA le mois dernier. Je l'ai découvert quand un patient m'a demandé pourquoi son compte rendu mentionnait quelque chose que je n'avais pas écrit."*

---

### Signal 2: Staff AI Use Under Physician Supervision Creates Named Accountability Gap

**The signal:** Medical assistants in independent practices are using AI tools for clinical documentation, patient communication drafting, and administrative tasks under the physician's professional authority. No Geneva-calibrated protocol exists for defining what "adequate supervision" means when the supervised activity involves AI decision support. The physician is accountable for the output but did not make or review the AI-assisted decision process.

**What this means operationally:** Delegation vocabulary (staff does X under my supervision) is being used for AI-assisted activities that do not map to human delegation logic. The AI tool has its own decision logic that is not supervised in the way a human assistant's decisions are supervised. The physician cannot review what the AI tool did — only the output.

**Why this is intensifying:** Practice efficiency pressures are driving AI tool adoption at staff level before governance frameworks exist for staff-level AI use. The physician's signature on every record makes them accountable regardless of who or what generated the content.

**Complaint verbatim (reconstructed):** *"Ma assistante utilise un outil IA pour les lettres aux patients. C'est sous ma responsabilité, mais je ne peux pas vérifier tout ce que l'outil produit — il y en a trop. Et je ne sais pas exactement ce que l'outil fait."*

---

### Signal 3: FADP Art.21 Automated Decision Disclosure as Mass Violation in Progress

**The signal:** FADP Art.21 requires that patients be informed when AI-influenced decisions are made about them, and that they have the right to request explanation and human review. Independent practices have no mechanism to identify which patient records contain AI-influenced decisions, no disclosure process, and no Art.21 request handling procedure. The obligation exists in present-tense. The infrastructure to meet it does not.

**What this means operationally:** Every AI-assisted diagnostic support decision, AI-influenced treatment recommendation, or AI-generated documentation content in a patient record potentially triggers Art.21 disclosure obligations. The practice cannot enumerate which decisions are AI-influenced because AI attribution architecture does not exist at record level. Patient rights under Art.21 are structurally inaccessible.

**Why this is intensifying:** FDPIC enforcement posture shifted to present-tense in May 2025. Art.21 obligations are not future risk — they are current practice reality. The longer the violation persists without documentation, the larger the cumulative exposure.

**Complaint verbatim (reconstructed):** *"Le nFADP — on nous dit qu'on doit pouvoir expliquer quand une décision est influencée par l'IA. Mais je ne sais même pas quelles décisions dans mes dossiers ont été influencées par l'IA. Comment je fais pour le expliquer au patient ?"*

---

### Signal 4: AI Tool Discontinuation Decision Has No Documentation Protocol

**The signal:** When a practice identifies a risk with an AI tool and discontinues its use, no Geneva protocol exists for documenting: the reason for discontinuation, the timeline of the decision, what happened to patient data processed by that tool, or what the practice's alternative approach is. The discontinuation decision is invisible in the record. If a question arises about past decisions made with the tool, the practice has no evidence of the governance response.

**What this means operationally:** Practices are making AI governance decisions (including discontinuations) without documentation architecture. This creates a specific gap: the moment of most acute governance awareness (when the physician decided the tool was problematic) is the moment least likely to be documented. Post-incident review cannot reconstruct a decision that was never documented.

**Why this is intensifying:** As AI tool incidents become more visible in professional circuit discussions, more physicians are questioning tools they initially adopted. The decision to stop using a tool is a governance decision that requires documentation — but no framework tells the practice what that documentation should contain.

**Complaint verbatim (reconstructed):** *"J'ai arrêté d'utiliser cet outil il y a six mois — j'ai vu des problèmes. Mais je n'ai rien documenté. Si quelqu'un me demande pourquoi je l'utilisais avant et comment je supervisais les décisions, je n'ai rien à montrer."*

---

### Signal 5: Carrier Attestation Asymmetry Creates Personal Liability on Unknown Terms

**The signal:** Swiss medical liability carriers are requesting AI governance attestation from practices. The attestation standard is defined by the carrier's underwriting logic, not by Geneva medical professional standards. The physician signs certifying to a standard they cannot evaluate — because the carrier has not published what "adequate AI governance" means, the carrier does not provide pre-signing review, and the physician has no independent reference for what they are certifying.

**What this means operationally:** The signing physician creates personal liability on terms set by the carrier without negotiation. Evidence satisfying carrier underwriting may be architecturally inadequate for FMH professional challenge or Art.63 criminal defense. Three different evidentiary standards, one signature, no mapping service exists.

**Why this is intensifying:** Carrier attestation waves are reaching smaller independent practices. The smaller the practice, the less infrastructure exists to produce the documentation the carrier is requesting, and the more likely the documentation produced will be inadequate to the actual exposure created.

**Complaint verbatim (reconstructed):** *"L'assureur nous demande de signer qu'on a une gouvernance IA adéquate. Adequate — mais adequate pour quoi ? On a préparé des documents, on a signé. Maintenant je réalise qu'on a certifié quelque chose sans savoir ce qu'on certifiait."*

---

### Signal 6: AI Availability Dependence Creates Unquantified Operational Risk

**The signal:** Independent practices that have adopted AI-assisted documentation tools are experiencing partial or complete AI tool unavailability events — vendor server outages, model updates causing temporary downtime, connectivity issues in the practice. During these events, the practice must revert to manual processes, creating workflow disruption, potential care continuity gaps, and no documented AI system failure protocol.

**What this means operationally:** The practice that has integrated AI into core documentation workflows has created an availability dependency. The dependency is unquantified — no Geneva standard exists for AI system failure contingency planning at independent practice scale. The physician is accountable for care continuity regardless of whether the AI tool was available.

**Why this is intensifying:** As AI feature integration deepens, more core practice functions depend on AI availability. Small independent practices lack institutional IT infrastructure to maintain parallel manual systems or to quickly identify when AI system unavailability is affecting patient care.

**Complaint verbatim (reconstructed):** *"L'outil IA était en panne pendant deux heures hier. Je n'avais plus accès aux comptes rendus automatisés. En même temps, j'avais trois patients qui avaient besoin de résultats. Ce n'est pas dans mon protocol — que faire quand l'outil ne marche pas."*

## Named Failure Patterns

### Pattern 1: The Update Ignorance Pattern
Physician believes their AI governance is stable because their own decisions about AI tools have not changed. In fact, their AI surface has expanded through vendor software updates they did not review. Attestation certifying governance adequacy covers a surface that has been modified without their knowledge.

### Pattern 2: The Delegation Without Supervision Pattern
Physician delegates AI-assisted tasks to staff, believes adequate supervision is occurring because staff are competent with the tools. In fact, "adequate supervision" of AI-assisted staff work is not defined at Geneva independent practice scale — the physician is accountable for AI decisions they cannot review.

### Pattern 3: The Post-Incident Evidence Absence Pattern
Adverse patient outcome occurs. Incident review begins. The AI tool's recommendation at the time of decision is not in the record. The physician's deliberation over the AI recommendation is not in the record. The review proceeds as if the decision was made without AI assistance — because that is what the record shows.

### Pattern 4: The Vendor Compliance Confidence Pattern
Physician asks vendor: "Are we compliant?" Vendor answers: "Yes, our system meets all regulatory requirements." Physician believes the compliance question is resolved. In fact, the vendor's answer addresses the vendor's regulatory obligations — not the physician's professional accountability obligations under FMH human oversight, FADP, or carrier attestation.
