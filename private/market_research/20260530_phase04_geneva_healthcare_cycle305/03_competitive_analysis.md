# 03 — Competitive Analysis: What Exists vs. What's Missing
## Phase 04 C305 | Geneva Healthcare | Independent Physician / Small Group Practice (2–10 physicians)

---

## What Exists in the Geneva Healthcare AI Governance Market

### What Exists

**1. Vendor-Provided Governance Documentation**
- AI tool vendors and EHR providers supply compliance documentation designed for vendor regulatory positioning, not physician professional accountability
- Certification language in vendor documentation addresses vendor obligations under MedDO, FADP, and FMH requirements — it does not address what the physician must document about their own oversight decisions
- Vendor documentation creates false confidence that the compliance question has been addressed, while the physician's personal accountability exposure remains structurally unaddressed

**2. General AI Governance Frameworks**
- FMH publications define principle-level human oversight obligation without operational translation
- MedTech association guidelines are designed for institutional and manufacturing contexts
- No Geneva-calibrated governance framework exists for 2-10 physician outpatient practice from the physician's personal accountability starting point

**3. Carrier Attestation Forms**
- Swiss medical liability carriers sending AI attestation questionnaires
- Forms designed for underwriting risk assessment — not professional liability protection
- No pre-signing review service calibrated to physician personal accountability exposure

**4. Legal Counsel (General)**
- Data protection counsel advises on FADP obligations — separate from medical professional liability framing
- Medical liability counsel advises on professional accountability — separate from data protection framing
- No Geneva counsel offering integrated FADP + FMH + carrier attestation + Art.321 CP analysis from physician personal liability perspective

**5. Medical Association Publications**
- FMH and Ordre des Médecins de Genève principle-level AI guidance
- No operational implementation pathway for independent practice scale
- No Canton-specific Geneva guidance addressing the specific intersection of FMH, FADP, MedDO, and carrier attestation

**6. Swissdamed Registration Resources**
- Swissmedic provides registration database and manufacturer guidance
- No independent practice proportionate assessment service for determining whether AI tools meet Tier 1/Tier 2 thresholds
- Vendor community offering registration assistance — commercial interest in broad registration

### What's Missing (The White Space)

**1. Staff AI Delegation Supervision Protocol**
What it is: A documented framework for what "adequate physician supervision" means when medical assistants use AI tools under physician authority. Defines: which AI-assisted staff activities require physician review vs. which do not, how physician review of AI-assisted output is documented, what competency documentation staff need, and what the physician's accountability scope is for AI-assisted staff decisions.

Why it doesn't exist: All existing delegation frameworks address human delegation logic — the supervised person makes decisions the supervisor reviews. AI-assisted delegation does not map to this logic because the AI tool, not the staff member, makes the decision the physician is supervising. No Geneva body has defined this distinction.

Why it is urgent: Staff AI use is happening now, under physician accountability, without any supervision protocol. Every independent practice with staff using AI tools has this exposure.

**2. AI System Update Review Protocol**
What it is: A practice-level process for reviewing EHR and AI tool updates that activate new features or modify existing AI behavior. Defines: how updates are tracked, what triggers a governance review, who is responsible for update monitoring, and how update-induced governance changes are documented.

Why it doesn't exist: EHR vendors do not notify practices of AI feature activations through updates — practices discover changes through patient feedback or incidental observation. No independent practice has infrastructure for systematic AI update monitoring.

Why it is urgent: AI feature integration is accelerating. The physician's accountability surface is expanding through vendor product development decisions they did not make and may not know about.

**3. FADP Art.21 Disclosure Infrastructure for Independent Practice**
What it is: A lightweight patient disclosure and request-handling system for Art.21 automated decision obligations, proportionate to independent practice scale. Defines: how to identify AI-influenced decisions in records, what disclosure language satisfies Art.21, how to handle patient Art.21 requests, and how to document the practice's response.

Why it doesn't exist: Art.21 infrastructure is designed for contexts with dedicated compliance functions. Independent practices have no mechanism to enumerate AI-influenced decisions in their records, making Art.21 compliance structurally impossible without a documentation architecture that does not currently exist.

Why it is urgent: FDPIC enforcement is present-tense. Art.21 obligations exist now. The violation is accumulating daily.

**4. AI Tool Discontinuation Documentation Protocol**
What it is: A post-decision documentation format for when a practice identifies AI tool risk and discontinues use. Defines: what the discontinuation record must contain (timeline, rationale, data fate, alternative approach), how the discontinuation decision is attributed within the practice, and what follow-up actions are required.

Why it doesn't exist: AI tool discontinuation is not a named governance decision category. Existing governance frameworks address AI tool adoption — not AI tool abandonment. The governance moment of highest physician awareness (deciding to stop using a tool) is the least likely to be documented.

Why it is urgent: As AI tool incidents surface in professional circuit, more physicians are discontinuing tools — without documentation, creating a retroactive governance gap.

**5. Carrier Attestation Pre-Signing Review Service (Medical Practice Calibrated)**
What it is: A service that analyzes carrier AI attestation forms from physician professional liability perspective before signature. Reviews: what the attestation actually requires the physician to certify, what evidence gaps exist at the practice's current state, what the physician is exposed to by signing as-is, what documentation would reduce personal exposure.

Why it doesn't exist: Finance sector developed pre-signing carrier review as carrier attestation became active. Medical practice carrier attestation wave is 6-12 months behind. The service gap is temporal — the medical practice market is arriving at the same problem without the response infrastructure.

Why it is urgent: Medical liability carrier attestations are arriving at smaller independent practices now, with short response deadlines, producing urgency-driven signature without review.

**6. AI Availability Contingency Planning for Independent Practice**
What it is: A documented protocol for maintaining care continuity when AI-assisted systems become unavailable. Defines: which AI functions are critical vs. supplementary, what manual fallback procedures exist, how AI unavailability is identified and escalated, and how the practice documents care delivered during AI system unavailability.

Why it doesn't exist: Enterprise AI governance frameworks include business continuity planning — but these frameworks assume IT infrastructure and dedicated personnel that independent practices do not have. No Geneva-calibrated contingency planning template exists for small practice AI availability dependence.

Why it is urgent: As AI features become embedded in core documentation workflows, AI unavailability creates direct care continuity risk. The physician remains accountable for care continuity regardless of AI system state.

## Competitive Positioning Assessment

### Who Could Fill These Gaps

**EHR vendors:** Will not provide independent practice governance architecture because it would create liability for their own product decisions. Vendor update notifications to practices would create accountability for vendor product development choices.

**Medical device manufacturers:** Will not provide small-practice methodology because doing so would constrain their market positioning advantage and create liability for their classification obligations.

**General AI governance consultancies:** Not Geneva-calibrated, not medical-practice-calibrated. Enterprise-designed tools create intimidation rather than action at independent practice scale.

**Swiss medical associations:** Producing principle-level guidance without operational implementation pathway. No mechanism for sustained circuit-level intelligence gathering from independent practice segment.

**Legal counsel:** Operating in data protection and medical liability silos. No integrated FADP + FMH + carrier + Art.321 CP offering from physician personal liability perspective exists in Geneva market.

**Carriers:** Will not provide pre-signing review — creates conflict with underwriting function. Carrier's interest is in documenting AI governance for risk assessment, not in protecting physician professional accountability position.

### Structural Moat Assessment

The Geneva clinical AI accountability service space has a structural moat created by:

1. **Information asymmetry:** Most operationally relevant intelligence is formed in informal circuit — inaccessible through publication or general expertise
2. **Segment isolation:** The 2-10 physician independent practice segment is structurally underserved by enterprise-designed tools and frameworks
3. **Regulatory intersection complexity:** FADP + FMH + MedDO + carrier attestation + Art.321 CP requires Geneva-specific multi-framework intelligence most service providers cannot access or integrate
4. **Pricing proportionality:** Services must be priced at a point proportionate to independent practice budgets — creating a pricing ceiling that filters most professional service providers

### Service Entry Sequence

The market is arriving at the AI accountability problem in a specific sequence. Service entry should follow:

1. **Immediate (now):** Carrier pre-signing review — acute urgency, finite scope, clear value
2. **Near-term (Q3-Q4 2025):** Staff AI delegation supervision protocol — active exposure, no existing solution
3. **Near-term (Q3-Q4 2025):** Art.21 disclosure infrastructure — present-tense obligation, no existing solution
4. **Mid-term (2026):** Swissdamed classification assessment — deadline-driven urgency
5. **Ongoing:** AI governance maintenance — retainer structure, evolving regulatory requirements
