## Current Problems Observed in Geneva Healthcare AI Discourse

### 1. Staff Shadow AI as Primary Governance Failure Mode

**What We Hear:**
- "My medical assistant downloaded ChatGPT to help with correspondence."
- "I found out three months later that my receptionist had been using an AI scheduling tool I didn't know about."
- "My office manager signed up for an AI billing optimization tool. I only found out when the bill came."

**The Gap:**
Small practice physicians have no operational mechanism to know what their staff has adopted. Attribution of AI governance failures runs through staff behavior, not policy failure. The physician is accountable for the outcome, but not positioned to govern the input.

**Market Signal:**
Staff shadow AI creates a compound risk: the physician is personally accountable for clinical decisions made with AI assistance, but has no governance mechanism to know what tools are in use. This is structurally different from legal (staff attorney using AI) or finance (portfolio manager using AI) — in healthcare, the accountability chain runs through a single supervising physician whose license is the asset at risk.

---

### 2. nFADP Art. 5(c) Consent Architecture Insufficient

**What We Hear:**
- "The patient signed the general consent form. Doesn't that cover AI processing?"
- "We didn't change anything in our consent forms when we started using Dragon Medical. Should we have?"

**The Gap:**
Treatment consent and data processing consent are treated as the same instrument by small practice. The nFADP Art. 5(c) requirement for explicit consent for sensitive data processing — and the distinction from treatment consent — is understood by nearly no small practice physicians. This represents a structural compliance gap with no current market solution addressing it specifically.

**Market Signal:**
The consent architecture gap is unserved. No Geneva boutique advisory is offering practical consent form templates or training specifically calibrated for small medical practice. Swissmedic compliance, nFADP Art. 5(c) consent, and clinical consent are being treated as one question when they require three different answers.

### 3. Swissmedic swissdamed Registration — Compliance Theater

**What We Hear:**
- "Swissmedic sent us something about AI device registration. I forwarded it to my IT person. They filed it."
- "I don't know if our Dragon Medical version qualifies as a medical device. Nobody asked until I did."
- "The deadline is July 2026. I don't have anyone who can tell me if we're compliant. I assume we're fine."

**The Gap:**
Swissmedic swissdamed registration is being handled as an administrative filing exercise rather than a substantive compliance review. The question "does my AI tool qualify as a medical device requiring registration?" is not being asked by practices, and the information infrastructure to get a clear answer is inadequate for small practices.

**Market Signal:**
swissdamed is functioning as a compliance theater event: something is being filed, but no one is confident about the substantive determination. July 1, 2026 is a material deadline for any practice using AI tools for diagnosis, monitoring, or treatment support. The gap between "we filed something" and "we are compliant" is wide and unaddressed.

### 4. Carrier Attestation Cross-Contamination from Finance/Legal Verticals

**What We Hear (Derived from Cross-Vertical Pattern):**
Small Geneva medical practices are beginning to receive AI governance attestation requests from their professional liability insurance carriers. The same carrier pressure that triggered Phase 01 (legal), Phase 02 (finance), and Phase 03 (consulting) is arriving in healthcare with a lag.

**The Gap:**
Healthcare carrier attestation is newer and less structured than legal/finance verticals. Physician professional liability carriers are asking AI governance questions, but the questions are general (not healthcare-specific), and physicians are signing attestations without understanding what they have committed to.

**Market Signal:**
The carrier attestation wave that drove urgency in legal/finance is now arriving for healthcare. Medical practices receiving carrier attestation requests are experiencing the same "I signed something I didn't understand" moment that attorneys and wealth managers experienced 6–12 months earlier.

### 5. AI Diagnosis Support Liability — Medical Standard of Care Ambiguity

**What We Hear:**
- "If my AI tool suggests a diagnosis and I'm wrong, am I liable for following the AI's suggestion?"
- "My specialist colleague says AI diagnosis tools are below standard of care. I disagree. Where do I go to find out?"
- "If AI assistance becomes the standard of care and I wasn't using it, am I liable for that too?"

**The Gap:**
The medical standard of care for AI-assisted diagnosis is not defined. Unlike legal malpractice (standard of practice) or financial advice (best execution), medical standard of care evaluation requires comparison to what a reasonable physician would do in the same circumstances. AI tools have been available in clinical contexts for only a few years — there is no body of case law, FMH guidance, or cantonal precedent defining the standard of care for AI-assisted diagnosis in Geneva.

**Market Signal:**
The standard of care ambiguity creates a dual paralysis: physicians are uncertain whether using AI exposes them to liability (follow a wrong AI suggestion) AND whether not using AI exposes them to liability (failure to adopt available standard of care). No Geneva body has published guidance resolving this. This is the sharpest liability question in the healthcare vertical, and no one in the market is addressing it specifically.

### 6. The Geneva Health Forum 2026 Silence on Small Practice AI Governance

**What We Observe:**
Geneva Health Forum 2026 (May and November) will bring together international healthcare leaders. The dominant themes will center on hospital systems, large institution AI governance, and policy-level AI strategy. Small practice AI governance is not a conference theme.

**The Gap:**
The formal Geneva healthcare professional community (GHF, AMGe, specialist societies) is not addressing small practice AI governance in a practically actionable way. Physicians attending GHF 2026 will return to practices with elevated awareness of AI governance as an issue, but no community-produced guidance on what to do operationally.

**Market Signal:**
Every physician attending Geneva Health Forum 2026 returns more aware of AI governance as an issue and no better equipped to address it in their practice operationally. This creates a structural awareness-to-action gap that advisory services can close with targeted content distribution at or near the May 2026 forum dates.
