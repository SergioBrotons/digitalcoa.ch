## 6 NET-NEW Findings (Distinct from C214-C289 Phase 04)

### Finding 1: Carrier Attestation Arriving in Healthcare with Physician Liability Insurance as the Mechanism

**The pattern:** The carrier attestation wave that drove urgency in legal (SBA attestation), finance (FINMA/SRO attestation), and consulting (SRO/advisory attestation) is now arriving in healthcare through physician professional liability insurance carriers.

**What's new in cycle 291:** Healthcare carriers are issuing AI governance attestation forms as part of renewal cycles, 6-12 months after the equivalent legal/finance wave. Physicians are receiving these forms, signing them, and not knowing what they committed to. The language is general (not healthcare-specific), the "adequate" standard is undefined, and the physician's personal license is on the line.

**Why this is NET-NEW:** Prior healthcare Phase 04 cycles noted the market as "developing." This cycle the carrier forcing function is confirmed as operative in healthcare. The personal accountability framing (physician's license at stake from carrier attestation) is the healthcare-specific variant of the Pattern 1 finding.

**Implication:** DigitalCoa.ch has approximately 6-12 months of first-mover window before healthcare carrier attestation becomes a widely known pressure point in the physician community. The timing is analogous to legal (2024), finance (2024-2025), consulting (2025), healthcare (2025-2026).

### Finding 2: License-Centric Accountability Framing Distinct from Fine-Centric Framing

**The pattern:** Legal ICPs respond to bar membership threat (fine/credential). Finance ICPs respond to FINMA authorization threat. Healthcare ICPs respond to physician license threat — the most personally immediate professional asset.

**What's new in cycle 291:** The distinction between different accountability frame anchors is now mapped with specificity: the physician's medical license is the asset that cannot be replaced, cannot be transferred, and represents the physician's entire professional identity. Carrier attestation, Swissmedic obligations, and nFADP compliance all map against this single asset.

**Why this is NET-NEW:** Prior cycles noted physician accountability concerns as one of many. This cycle isolates physician license as the primary framing mechanism — the license is why physician AI governance is more emotionally activating than attorneys responding to bar discipline or wealth managers responding to FINMA review. The license is singular, irreplaceable, and professionally existential in a way that distinguishes the healthcare vertical from all other verticals.

**Implication:** All messaging for the healthcare vertical must be anchored to license protection and standard of care — not to regulatory fines, not to administrative burden. "This could affect your license" activates the healthcare ICP in a way that "this could result in an administrative fine" does not.

### Finding 3: Medical Standard of Care as Unresolved Dual Paralysis

**The pattern:** In healthcare, AI creates a unique paralysis: not using AI AND using AI both potentially expose the physician to liability. Using AI: physician followed wrong AI suggestion. Not using AI: physician failed to adopt available standard of care.

**What's new in cycle 291:** The dual paralysis is now identified as the most acute unresolved liability question in the healthcare vertical — distinct from legal (supervision of attorney using AI), finance (best execution standard), and consulting (SRO governance standard for advisory). No Geneva authority (FMH, cantonal medical board, Swissmedic) has published guidance defining the AI-assisted standard of care for practicing physicians. The question is genuinely open.

**Why this is NET-NEW:** Prior cycles noted physician liability concerns broadly. This cycle identifies the dual paralysis as structurally unique to healthcare: the standard of care question creates two simultaneous failure modes (AI-incorrect and AI-absent), where legal/finance/consulting have only one failure mode each (failure to supervise / best execution failure / inadequate governance framework).

**Implication:** Advisory positioning around this standard of care ambiguity is high-value: demonstrating awareness of the dual paralysis and offering a documented human oversight process as a practical protection is differentiated advisory content. This cannot be resolved by the advisor (the standard is genuinely undefined), but it can be managed practically.

### Finding 4: nFADP Art. 5(c) + Treatment Consent Structural Conflation as Unserved Gap

**The pattern:** Small medical practices believe their existing treatment consent forms satisfy nFADP data protection consent requirements for AI-assisted processing. They do not.

**What's new in cycle 291:** The structural conflation is now identified as a compliance gap that cannot be resolved without addressing three separate consent instruments simultaneously: treatment consent, nFADP data processing consent (Art. 5(c)), and Swissmedic device use consent (if applicable). No existing market solution addresses all three simultaneously.

**Why this is NET-NEW:** Prior cycles noted consent architecture as a gap. This cycle specifies the structural conflation (treatment consent ≠ nFADP data processing consent) as the operative compliance problem — not a knowledge gap (physicians don't know they should know) but a systematic gap (no one provides a three-consent-architecture solution for small practice). The solution requires integration of clinical consent architecture, nFADP data processing consent, and device-specific consent in one coherent framework.

**Implication:** The three-consent-architecture deliverable is a differentiated product component that no existing competitor provides. Integration of treatment consent + nFADP Art. 5(c) consent + Swissmedic device consent in one practical framework is a clear, nameable service offering.

### Finding 5: FMH Human Oversight Principle Has No Tuesday Translation

**The pattern:** FMH states that physicians bear ultimate accountability for AI-assisted clinical decisions and must maintain human oversight. These principles sound reasonable and are not implemented operationally. There is no published guidance on what human oversight looks like on a Tuesday.

**What's new in cycle 291:** FMH human oversight requirement is now identified as the healthcare-specific variant of the Phase 01/02/03 "the principle doesn't tell you what to do on a Tuesday" finding. The healthcare-specific version: FMH has stated human oversight obligations (no published Tuesday translation) at the same time medical standard of care for AI-assisted decisions is undefined.

**Why this is NET-NEW:** This finding connects the human oversight principle (FMH requirement) with the undefined standard of care (Finding 3) and the carrier attestation (Finding 1) — creating a compound exposure where physicians must maintain human oversight (FMH requirement) of AI-assisted decisions (undefined standard) and have documented governance (carrier attestation). The Tuesday translation for what human oversight means is the most practical deliverable in the healthcare vertical.

**Implication:** The human oversight documentation protocol — what to document, when, how much — is the single most practical offering for the healthcare ICP at this moment in time. It connects FMH compliance (stated but undefined), carrier attestation (documented governance attempt), and standard of care protection (documented physician review of AI output).

### Finding 6: GHF 2026 Awareness Amplification Without Operational Output Creates Content Positioning Opportunity

**The pattern:** Geneva Health Forum 2026 (May + November) will elevate AI governance awareness among attending physicians. The forum will NOT provide operational small practice AI governance methodology. Every attendee returns more aware without operational guidance.

**What's new in cycle 291:** GHF 2026 is now confirmed with specific dates (May 18-22, November 10-12). The forum is positioned as the awareness event that creates the ICP with a problem (elevated awareness) and a gap (no methodology). Pre-forum content building (April-May 2026 publication window) and post-forum content distribution (June-November 2026) are identified as the most efficient content placement strategy for healthcare vertical.

**Why this is NET-NEW:** GHF 2026 has been named in prior cycles as a positioning event. This cycle specifically identifies the awareness-without-operational-output gap as the content positioning window: post-forum content positioned as "what GHF 2026 should have told you about small practice AI governance" is a first-to-market content opportunity with a concrete timing window.

**Implication:** Content series timed for pre-GHF 2026 (April-May) and post-GHF 2026 (June-July and October-November) positions DigitalCoa.ch as the voice that fills the Geneva Health Forum operational guidance gap. This is a named, time-bound content strategy — not a generic "publish content consistently" recommendation.

## Ready-to-Use Language

### EN — Primary Frames (For Lead Magnet, Web Copy, Outreach)

**Entry Hook (Carrier Attestation Frame):**
"You signed your professional liability carrier attestation. Did you know what you were committing to? Most physicians didn't."

**nFADP Frame:**
"Your patient data is subject to nFADP Article 5(c) — the highest protection category in Swiss data protection law. If your AI tools process patient health data, your practice is a sensitive data processor with specific obligations most small practices haven't addressed."

**Swissmedic Frame:**
"Swissmedic requires registration of medical devices. If your AI tools qualify as medical devices under Swissmedic rules — and some widely-used clinical AI tools do — the July 1, 2026 deadline applies to your practice. Filing the Swissmedic communication is not the same as being compliant."

**Standard of Care Frame:**
"Two questions physicians should be able to answer: If AI assists your diagnosis and you're wrong, are you liable for following the AI's suggestion? And: If AI-assisted diagnosis becomes the standard of care and you weren't using AI, are you liable for that too? No Geneva authority has published guidance answering either question definitively. The honest advisory answer: document your human oversight process."

**FMH Frame:**
"FMH requires human oversight of AI-assisted clinical decisions. No Geneva publication has defined what human oversight looks like operationally — on a Tuesday, with four staff, managing twelve patients. This guide is that translation."

Aucune autorité genevoise n'a publié de directive répondant définitivement à l'une ou l'autre de ces questions."
