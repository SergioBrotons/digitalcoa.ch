## What Exists vs. What's Missing

### What Exists

1. **General AI governance frameworks** (ISO 42001, NIST AI RMF) — designed for enterprise; too abstract for small practice operationally
2. **nFADP compliance guidance** — general; not healthcare-specific in ways that address Art. 5(c) consent + Swissmedic simultaneously
3. **Swissmedic swissdamed documentation** — self-service information; requires technical understanding most small practices lack
4. **FMH continuing education** — general medical topics; AI governance not yet a CME category with operational content
5. **AI tool vendor documentation** — DPIA and DPA exist, but vendors don't proactively educate users on compliance obligations
6. **Legal firm data protection opinions** — nFADP compliance letters; not AI governance strategy
7. **Big 4 AI governance consulting** — enterprise-priced; not designed for CHF 300K–1.2M revenue practice

### What's Missing

1. **Physician-specific liability framing** — AI governance framed through license protection and standard of care evaluation, not general compliance
2. **nFADP Art. 5(c) + treatment consent architecture** — practical templates and training distinguishing treatment consent from data processing consent
3. **Swissmedic-device classification advisory** — independent determination of whether AI tools qualify as medical devices, with actionable registration guidance
4. **Carrier attestation preparation** — specifically for physician professional liability carriers; not general AI governance questionnaires
5. **Staff shadow AI governance policy** — practical policy templates for small practice addressing the operative failure mode
6. **FMH-adjacent AI governance positioning** — credible advisory with physician community legitimacy; FMH has not published operational guidance
7. **Geneva-cantonal AI governance for small practice** — practice-level AI governance addressing Geneva canton health inspectorate expectations

## Why No One Has Claimed This Gap

### Structural Reasons

**Reason 1: The Knowledge Intersection Is Rare**
The required knowledge spans: Swiss medical regulatory (Swissmedic, FMH), Swiss data protection (nFADP Art. 5(c) specific), AI governance methodology, Geneva cantonal health law, and physician practice economics. Most advisory providers have one or two of these, not all five. The intersection is occupied by no one.

**Reason 2: The Market Is Undefined by Buyers**
Small medical practices don't self-identify as "needing AI governance advisory." They experience the problem as: "my assistant downloaded something I don't understand," "Swissmedic sent a form," "I'm not sure what my consent forms should say." The problem is present; the category is unnamed. No competitor has named it for them in physician language.

**Reason 3: Pricing Architecture Absent**
The service doesn't have a market-established price. Legal firms have hourly rates. Big 4 have daily rates. Small medical practice doesn't know what to expect to pay for AI governance advisory. Advisory pricing must be established in the market, not borrowed from adjacent categories.

**Reason 4: FMH Institutional Gap**
FMH publishes medical standards, not operational AI governance guidance. This gap is structural — FMH's mandate is physician professional standards, not AI administration. The institutional gap creates the advisory opportunity, but advisory must earn credibility through FMH-adjacent positioning, not explicit FMH endorsement.

**Reason 5: Carrier Form Arrival Timing Has Lagged**
The carrier attestation wave arrived in legal first (2024), finance second (2024-2025), consulting third (2025), and is only now arriving in healthcare (2025-2026). The market force that created urgency in legal/finance/consulting is now becoming operative in healthcare. This timing lag means healthcare advisory demand is emerging now. The window is not closed.

The forcing function that created urgency in legal/finance/consulting is now becoming operative in healthcare. **3. swissdamed Deadline Is Concrete**
July 1, 2026 is a hard deadline. Medical practices using AI tools that qualify as medical devices must register. This creates a concrete compliance checkpoint that generates urgency without requiring carrier pressure.

**6. FMH Has Not Published Operational Guidance**
FMH's AI governance publications to date are principles without practice. No Geneva boutique has stepped into the gap with FMH-adjacent, physician-native AI governance advisory. FMH institutional vacuum creates first-mover positioning opportunity.

## Why AlpineAI / SwissGPT Health Is Not a Threat

AlpineAI (SwissGPT Health) is an AI tool vendor selling a product. Their positioning:
- Secure Swiss AI infrastructure for medical/psychological professionals
- Data security + efficiency
- Subscription model (CHF 99–299/month)

Why AlpineAI is not a competitor for AI governance advisory:
1. **Incentive structure mismatch**: Vendors want to sell tools; governance advisory is orthogonal to their revenue model.
2. **No independent liability coverage**: SwissGPT Health will not advise on compliance obligations it did not create.
3. **No physician-specific liability framing**: Their product is infrastructure; they do not address carrier attestation or standard of care.
4. **swissdamed conflict of interest**: As a vendor, AlpineAI has interest in its tools being classified as compliant. Independent advisory provides classification without vendor bias.
5. **Not trusted advisor in liability context**: When a physician faces a liability question, they need independent counsel, not a vendor.
