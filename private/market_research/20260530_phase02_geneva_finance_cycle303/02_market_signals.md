## Current Market Problems

### Problem 1: The Institutional Client Questionnaire is a Competitive Event Nobody Warned Them About

**What's happening:**
Boutique wealth management firms in Geneva are receiving institutional client AI governance questionnaires as standard vendor due diligence — not as occasional regulatory inquiry. These questionnaires arrive with 2–4 week deadlines, ask for specific documentation (AI tool inventory, authorization records, data access controls, decision attribution, vendor DPAs, nFADP Art. 5(c) compliance evidence), and are scored. The firm treating it as a one-off compliance disclosure is competing against firms treating it as a scored competitive evaluation.

**The complaint:**
"Our largest institutional client sent us a 40-point AI governance DDQ. We had to leave significant sections blank. We just found out that response is being used as live intelligence for their 2026-2027 mandate decisions — decisions about whether we stay in their portfolio."

**The structural failure:**
Firms are building questionnaire responses as compliance events. Institutional clients are using those responses as competitive intelligence. The asymmetry: firms don't know they're in a comparison until after the decision is made.

**Signal for DigitalCoa.ch:**
Positioning around institutional questionnaire readiness — helping firms prepare documentation that wins scored evaluations, not just satisfies disclosure requirements.

### Problem 2: Carrier Attestation Evidence Standard Has Crossed the Line from Attestation to Documentation

**What's happening:**
Insurance carriers have shifted from simple attestation ("we use adequate AI governance") to evidence-based documentation requirements. Form v4 requires "last three authorization records per AI tool" — not a policy, but specific records showing when each AI tool was authorized, who approved it, what data it accesses, and the sub-processor chain.

**The complaint:**
"I signed the carrier attestation. I didn't have anyone review it before I signed. The form asks about AI vendor due diligence, processor chains, and sub-contractor disclosures. I accepted vendor terms without evaluating any of this. If this triggers a claim, that's my personal signature on a document I didn't fully understand."

**The structural failure:**
Attestation ≠ compliance. The signature creates a record — but not a defense. Managing directors are discovering that the carrier's evidence request cannot be satisfied with the documentation they have.

**Signal for DigitalCoa.ch:**
Evidence production for carrier attestation — producing authorization records that satisfy Form v4 requirements, not just the attestation itself.

### Problem 3: Portfolio Construction AI Authorization Chain Is Nameless and Undocumented

**What's happening:**
Boutique wealth management firms use AI for portfolio construction — algorithmic rebalancing, automated risk profiling, AI-generated investment recommendations. These systems operate continuously and make decisions that directly affect client financial outcomes. The authorization chain from AI recommendation to human approval was never documented. Retroactive reconstruction is structurally impossible.

**The complaint:**
"L'IA qui construit nos portfolios est différente de l'IA qui génère des rapports. L'IA portfolio prend des décisions qui affectent directement les résultats clients. Quand l'assureur demande sur les 'décisions de portfolio influencées par l'IA,' je ne sais pas quelle preuve il cherche."

**The structural failure:**
"Portfolio construction AI" is a discrete accountability category that nobody names. The ICP understands this distinction intuitively but has no vocabulary for it and no service addressing it. The gap is unnamed — which makes it invisible to firms that should be addressing it.

**Signal for DigitalCoa.ch:**
Naming and documenting portfolio construction AI authorization chains — the category nobody has addressed in the Geneva market.

### Problem 4: Vendor Data Processing Architecture Opacity Creates nFADP Art. 9 Exposure Nobody Has Measured

**What's happening:**
The managing director accepted vendor terms for three to five AI tools across portfolio analytics, client reporting, and CRM — each with sub-processor chains listed in appendices that were never reviewed. nFADP Art. 9 obligations arise from the nature of data processing activity, not from whether the MD evaluated the terms. The exposure has been accumulating through years of unevaluated click-through acceptance.

**The complaint:**
"L'IA d'analyse de portfolio accède aux données clients. J'ai accepté les conditions du vendor sans jamais les lire. Les sous-traitants du vendor IA sont listés dans une annexe que je n'ai jamais consultée. Ça fait partie de notre exposition Art. 9 nFADP."

**The structural failure:**
The exposure is present-tense and undisclosed. The firm cannot answer: "Which AI systems process client data?" with a complete answer. The carrier's third-generation questionnaire now asks about sub-processor chains — activating disclosure obligations that have been accumulating silently.

**Signal for DigitalCoa.ch:**
Vendor processor chain mapping — the specific documentation that answers carrier attestation requirements and nFADP Art. 9 obligations simultaneously.

### Problem 5: Four-Track Regulatory Cluster With No Cross-Reference Methodology

**What's happening:**
Swissdamed (weeks), EU AI Act (weeks), FINMA Circular 08/2024 (17 months), nFADP (operational), CRS 2.0 (5 months live) — all creating obligations simultaneously with no Geneva-calibrated methodology for managing them as an integrated portfolio. The boutique manages four documentation streams that describe the same AI governance reality with different formatting, evidence standards, update cycles, and named responsible persons.

**The complaint:**
"On ne sait pas si on est Tier 1 ou Tier 2 (Swissdamed). On ne sait pas si on a un nexus EU (EU AI Act). La Circulaire FINMA n'a pas de checklist. L'OSR n'a rien dit. Tout arrive en même temps et il n'y a pas de méthodologie pour gérer ça comme un portfolio."

**The structural failure:**
Documentation may be internally inconsistent despite individually satisfying each track. A single AI governance event (e.g., vendor contract signature) creates obligations under multiple frameworks simultaneously — but the firm has no cross-reference architecture. No Geneva service addresses cross-track coherence management.

**Signal for DigitalCoa.ch:**
Cross-track documentation coherence — the methodology for managing parallel accountability tracks without duplicating effort or creating internal inconsistency.

---

### Problem 6: The Evidence Gap Between Policy and Operational Documentation Is Not Abstract

**What's happening:**
Boutique wealth management firms have AI governance policies. They do not have authorization records, decision attribution documentation, vendor due diligence files, or evidence of human oversight. The gap is not abstract — it's the difference between "we have a policy that says humans approve AI decisions" and "here are the records showing who approved which AI system, when, with what data access, and with what evidence."

**The complaint:**
"On a une politique IA. Elle ne documente rien de ce qui existe vraiment. Le carrier demande des records — pas un policy document. Je peux expliquer ce qu'on fait. Je ne peux pas prouver quand c'était autorisé et par qui."

**The structural failure:**
The policy documents the intent; the evidence documents the reality. The ICP understands this distinction and can articulate it — but has no pathway to producing evidence from their existing policy documentation. The gap is specific, operational, and solvable.

**Signal for DigitalCoa.ch:**
Evidence production methodology — transforming policy documentation into operational authorization records, without rebuilding the policy from scratch.
