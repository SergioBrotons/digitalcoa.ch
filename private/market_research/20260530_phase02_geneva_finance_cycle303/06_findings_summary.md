## Key Insights

### NET-NEW Findings (C303 Phase 02 — Finance Vertical)

**1. Institutional Client AI Governance Questionnaires Are Competitive Events Before They Are Compliance Events**
Corporate clients, family offices, endowments, and institutional investors are sending detailed AI governance questionnaires to Geneva wealth managers as standard vendor due diligence. These questionnaires have 2–4 week response deadlines, ask for specific documentation (AI tool inventory, authorization records, data access controls, decision attribution, vendor DPAs, nFADP Art. 5(c) compliance evidence), and are scored. The competitive dimension: firms are being compared across their portfolio without knowing it. A firm responding to a questionnaire as a compliance event is competing against firms treating it as a scored evaluation. The mandate decision for 2026-2027 is being made from documentation that exists now — including responses built in 2024 that are functioning as live competitive intelligence.

**2. Evidence vs. Policy Is the Specific Operational Gap the Finance ICP Can Name but Cannot Address**
The finance ICP has an AI governance policy. They do not have authorization records. The gap is not abstract: policy documents describe what should exist; evidence shows what exists, when it was authorized, by whom, and with what data access. The ICP can articulate this distinction ("le carrier demande des records — pas un policy document") but has no pathway to producing evidence from existing policy documentation. This is the specific, operational, solvable problem that no Geneva market service addresses at boutique WM scale.

**3. Portfolio Construction AI Is a Discrete Accountability Category Nobody Names**
Boutique wealth management firms use AI for portfolio construction — algorithmic rebalancing, automated risk profiling, AI-generated investment recommendations. These systems operate continuously and make decisions that directly affect client financial outcomes. The authorization chain from AI recommendation to human approval was never documented. Retroactive reconstruction is structurally impossible — systems were never built to capture this data. The ICP names this category ("l'IA de construction de portfolio, c'est une catégorie de responsabilité différente") but has no service addressing it. This is both a documentation gap and a market positioning opportunity: naming the category is itself differentiation in a market where no competitor has named it.

**4. Carrier Attestation Form v4 Has Crossed from Attestation to Evidence Documentation**
Insurance carriers have shifted from simple attestation ("we use adequate AI governance") to evidence-based documentation requirements. Form v4 requires "last three authorization records per AI tool" — not a policy, but specific records showing when each AI tool was authorized, who approved it, what data it accesses, and the sub-processor chain. Managing directors discovering that the carrier's evidence request cannot be satisfied with their existing documentation are seeking evidence management solutions. The carrier is functioning as a de facto enforcement agent — parallel to how bar associations function in the legal vertical. The gap is specific: evidence production, not policy documentation.

**5. Cross-Track Documentation Coherence Is an Unnamed Service Category**
Swissdamed (weeks), EU AI Act (weeks), FINMA Circular 08/2024 (17 months), nFADP (operational), CRS 2.0 (5 months live) — all creating obligations simultaneously with no Geneva-calibrated methodology for managing them as an integrated portfolio. The boutique manages four documentation streams that describe the same AI governance reality with different formatting, evidence standards, update cycles, and named responsible persons. No cross-reference architecture exists. Documentation may be internally inconsistent despite individually satisfying each track. No Geneva service addresses cross-track coherence management. This is a discrete service category — unnamed in the Geneva market — with clear addressable demand.

**6. Pre-Signing Personal Liability Review for Carrier Attestation Has No Market Equivalent at Boutique WM Scale**
The carrier attestation requires the managing director's personal signature on "adequate AI governance" — an undefined standard designed by underwriting logic, not professional liability architecture. No Geneva market service offers pre-signing personal liability review for WM managing directors. The signature creates personal exposure under nFADP Art. 63 that the carrier's insurance product does not discharge. Medical practitioners have pre-signing review for carrier attestation. Wealth management managing directors are structurally in the same exposure position without an equivalent service. This is a specific service category with no current competitor in the Geneva boutique WM market.

## Ready-to-Use Language

### For the Evidence Gap
**What the ICP needs:** Authorization records, decision attribution files, vendor processor chain documentation — not another framework document.

**What to say:** "You have a policy. You don't have records. The carrier doesn't ask for a policy — it asks for evidence. That's the gap."

### For the Institutional Questionnaire Problem
**What the ICP needs:** A response file organized by question, with specific documentation attached — not a framework.

**What to say:** "Your 2024 response is being used as live intelligence for 2026 mandate decisions. You're being measured against firms that filled in the sections you couldn't answer. That's the competitive window."

### For the Personal Liability Problem
**What the ICP needs:** Documentation that shows Art. 63 due diligence — oversight, controls, and accountability.

**What to say:** "The insurance product responds to claims. Art. 63 personal liability is criminal. The evidence of due diligence that protects you personally is specific documentation, not a policy document."

### For the Portfolio Construction AI Problem
**What the ICP needs:** Authorization chain documentation for continuous AI decision systems.

**What to say:** "Portfolio construction AI operates continuously. It's not a one-time authorization. The authorization chain for continuous AI systems is a specific documentation problem — and nobody in the Geneva market has named it or solved it."

### For the Carrier Attestation Problem
**What the ICP needs:** Authorization records that satisfy Form v4 requirements — last three records per AI tool.

**What to say:** "The carrier asks for records, not attestation. Form v4 requires specific approval records with specific content. If you don't have them, you're not in compliance — even if you signed the attestation."

### For the Cross-Track Coherence Problem
**What the ICP needs:** A methodology that produces documentation satisfying all four tracks simultaneously.

**What to say:** "Swissdamed, EU AI Act, FINMA, nFADP — they all ask about the same AI governance reality with different formatting and evidence standards. You can't build four separate documentation streams without creating inconsistency. You need one documentation architecture that satisfies all four."

**The market offers frameworks. We produce evidence.**

The distinction: most advisory firms offer a framework — a document that describes what should exist. What the finance ICP actually needs is evidence — documentation that shows what exists, when it was authorized, who approved it, and how decisions were made.

The institutional client questionnaire doesn't ask for a framework. It asks for records. The carrier form doesn't ask for an attestation. It asks for evidence. The managing director under Art. 63 doesn't need a policy. They need proof of oversight.

DigitalCoa.ch is positioned to produce evidence, not just frameworks.
