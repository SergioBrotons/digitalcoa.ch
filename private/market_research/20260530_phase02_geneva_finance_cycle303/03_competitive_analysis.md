# 03 — Competitive Analysis: What Exists vs. What's Missing
## Phase 02 | Cycle C303 | Geneva Finance / Boutique Wealth Management

---

## Current Market Landscape

### What Exists

#### 1. Big 4 Advisory — Framework Producers
**What they offer:** AI governance frameworks, risk assessments, policy documents, regulatory gap analyses for financial institutions.

**Where they operate:** Large institutions (systemically important financial institutions, banks, insurance groups). Minimum engagement sizes that make them inaccessible to boutique WM firms (50–200 employees, CHF 500M–5B AUM).

**The gap:** Big 4 produce frameworks — documents that describe what should exist. They do not produce evidence — documentation that shows what exists, when it was authorized, and with what records. Their deliverable is a framework document, not an evidence file. The finance ICP needs evidence to satisfy carrier attestation Form v4 and institutional client questionnaires — not a framework.

**The proportionality failure:** Big 4 engagement minimums are designed for institutional scale. A CHF 2B AUM boutique wealth management firm cannot justify the minimum engagement fee for a Big 4 framework project.

---

#### 2. General Management Consultants — Generic Frameworks
**What they offer:** AI governance frameworks, ISO 42001 certification prep, technology risk assessments.

**Where they operate:** Cross-industry, including financial services. Generalist approach.

**The gap:** General management consultants understand AI governance in the abstract but lack Swiss regulatory specificity. The finance ICP needs someone who understands FINMA 08/2024, nFADP Art. 5(c) and Art. 63, Swissdamed Tier classification, CRS 2.0 AI obligations, and the carrier attestation evidence standard simultaneously. General consultants cannot speak to the Swiss-specific regulatory cluster with precision.

**The proportionality failure:** Consultants offer standardized methodologies designed for broad applicability. The boutique WM firm needs a methodology calibrated to its specific regulatory cluster, firm size, and client base.

---

#### 3. Legal Firms — Regulatory Counsel
**What they offer:** Legal opinions on AI governance obligations, regulatory interpretation, nFADP compliance counsel, contract review for AI vendor agreements.

**Where they operate:** Legal compliance, contract review, regulatory interpretation. Limited to legal dimensions.

**The gap:** Legal firms provide legal interpretation — what the obligations are under Swiss law. They do not provide operational documentation — how to produce the authorization records, decision attribution files, and evidence packages that satisfy carrier attestation and institutional questionnaires. The gap between legal opinion and operational evidence is not addressed by legal counsel.

**The specificity failure:** Legal counsel understands nFADP Art. 63 personal liability in the abstract but does not produce the documentation that would serve as evidence of due diligence if a claim arises.

---

#### 4. Compliance Software Vendors — Documentation Platforms
**What they offer:** Compliance management platforms, policy management systems, audit trail software, regulatory tracking tools.

**Where they operate:** Enterprise compliance management, document retention, audit workflows.

**The gap:** Compliance software platforms are documentation tools — they manage the storage and retrieval of documents the firm produces. They do not produce the underlying documentation. A boutique WM firm without authorization records cannot produce them by buying a compliance software platform. The software manages what exists; it does not create what doesn't exist.

**The documentation failure:** The platform assumes the firm has documentation to manage. The finance ICP does not have the underlying authorization records, decision attribution files, and evidence packages. Buying platform access does not solve the documentation gap.

---

#### 5. Cybersecurity Firms — Vendor Security Assessments
**What they offer:** Vendor security assessments, due diligence questionnaires, AI tool security evaluations, data processing reviews.

**Where they operate:** Vendor security, technology risk, third-party risk management.

**The gap:** Cybersecurity firms assess vendor security posture — whether the vendor's systems are secure. They do not assess vendor contractual compliance — whether the vendor's data processing terms create obligations under nFADP Art. 9 and carrier attestation requirements. The security assessment and the contractual compliance assessment are different exercises. A vendor that passes a security assessment may still have data processing terms that create undisclosed exposure.

**The narrow scope failure:** Cybersecurity firms address the vendor security question, not the vendor data processing compliance question. The finance ICP needs both — and the gap between them is unaddressed.

---

### What's Missing

#### Missing 1: Evidence Production for Carrier Attestation — The Core Gap
**What is missing:** A service that produces the authorization records, vendor due diligence files, sub-processor chain documentation, and evidence packages that satisfy insurance carrier Form v4 requirements.

**Why it doesn't exist:** Carrier attestation evidence production requires understanding both the carrier's evidence standard and the boutique WM firm's operational reality. No Geneva market service addresses the intersection. Legal counsel addresses the regulatory interpretation; they don't produce the records. Compliance software manages documents; they don't create them. Big 4 addresses large institutions; the boutique WM firm falls below their minimum engagement threshold.

**The addressable gap:** A service that produces carrier attestation evidence packages for boutique WM firms at a price point and complexity level proportional to the firm's size and regulatory reality.

---

#### Missing 2: Portfolio Construction AI Authorization Chain Documentation — The Named Gap Nobody Addresses
**What is missing:** A service that names, documents, and produces evidence for the portfolio construction AI authorization chain — the discrete accountability category that the finance ICP identifies but cannot address.

**Why it doesn't exist:** Portfolio construction AI authorization documentation requires understanding both the technical architecture of algorithmic portfolio management systems and the regulatory accountability requirements under FINMA, nFADP, and carrier attestation. No Geneva market service addresses this specific intersection. General consultants don't name the category; cybersecurity firms don't address it; legal counsel doesn't produce the documentation.

**The addressable gap:** A service that names the portfolio construction AI authorization chain as a discrete accountability category and produces the documentation that satisfies carrier attestation and institutional client questionnaire requirements for this specific use case.

---

#### Missing 3: Cross-Track Documentation Coherence Management — The unnamed regulatory coordination problem
**What is missing:** A methodology for managing four parallel documentation tracks (Swissdamed, EU AI Act, FINMA 08/2024, nFADP) as an integrated portfolio — producing coherent documentation that satisfies all four tracks without duplicating effort or creating internal inconsistency.

**Why it doesn't exist:** Cross-track documentation coherence requires understanding the evidence standards, update cycles, and responsible person designations for each track — and designing documentation that satisfies all four simultaneously. No Geneva market service addresses this regulatory coordination problem for boutique firms. Each regulatory consultant addresses their specific track; no coordination service exists.

**The addressable gap:** A methodology that treats the regulatory cluster as an integrated portfolio and produces documentation with cross-track coherence — where a single AI governance event produces documentation that satisfies multiple tracks simultaneously.

---

#### Missing 4: Pre-Signing Personal Liability Review for Carrier Attestation — The Personal Signature Problem
**What is missing:** A service that reviews carrier attestation forms for personal liability exposure before the managing director signs — analogous to the pre-signing review service that exists for medical practitioners.

**Why it doesn't exist:** The carrier attestation requires the managing director's personal signature on "adequate AI governance" — an undefined standard designed by underwriting logic, not professional liability architecture. No Geneva market service offers pre-signing personal liability review for WM managing directors. The signature creates personal exposure under nFADP Art. 63 that the carrier's insurance product does not discharge.

**The addressable gap:** A pre-signing review service that analyzes carrier attestation forms for personal liability exposure, identifies the specific documentation the managing director needs before signing, and produces that documentation — protecting the personal signature with evidence of due diligence.

---

#### Missing 5: Institutional Client Questionnaire Readiness — The Competitive Positioning Problem
**What is missing:** A service that helps boutique WM firms prepare for institutional client AI governance questionnaires as competitive evaluations — not just compliance disclosures.

**Why it doesn't exist:** The institutional client questionnaire is understood as a compliance event by most firms. The competitive dimension — the scored evaluation against other managers in the client's portfolio — is invisible to firms that haven't discovered it. No service helps boutique WM firms prepare documentation that wins scored evaluations, not just satisfies disclosure requirements.

**The addressable gap:** A service that positions the firm to win institutional mandates by producing AI governance documentation that scores competitively — not just adequately.

---

#### Missing 6: nFADP Art. 63 Personal Liability Documentation — The Personal Exposure Problem
**What is missing:** A service that produces the specific documentation the managing director needs to demonstrate personal due diligence under nFADP Art. 63 — evidence that protects personal assets, not just institutional liability.

**Why it doesn't exist:** nFADP Art. 63 personal liability is understood as an abstract regulatory risk. The specific documentation that would serve as evidence of due diligence if a claim arises is not defined, named, or systematized for the finance ICP. Legal counsel addresses the legal interpretation; they don't produce the operational evidence package.

**The addressable gap:** A service that names the specific documentation requirements for personal liability protection under Art. 63 — and produces that documentation for the managing director who needs it before an incident occurs, not after.
