## Six NET-NEW Findings (Distinct from C214–C299)

### Finding C300-P3-F1: Client-Contracted AI Governance Questionnaires as Engagement Contract Trigger

**What it is:** Geneva consulting boutiques are increasingly receiving engagement contracts that include AI governance disclosure requirements imposed by clients — not regulators, not law, but contractual counterparties. The client sends the contract with the AI clause embedded; the consulting firm has no negotiating position because refusing the clause means losing the engagement. The consulting firm signs without understanding what they've committed to, without any internal framework to respond, and without documentation of their actual AI governance state.

**Why it matters:** This is a structural market change — AI governance is being imposed through the commercial contract, not through regulatory requirement. The consulting firm that cannot respond to a client AI clause cannot compete for mandates in C300. The firm that can respond has a competitive advantage — because the client sending these contracts is encountering the same gap on the other side, and they will value a consulting counterparty who has a methodology.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your clients are including AI clauses in your engagement contracts. You're not sure what you're supposed to say. You're not sure what documentation you need. You signed — and now you're hoping it doesn't come up. I can tell you what you actually committed to, and I can build the documentation that makes that commitment defensible."

**Distinct from C214–C299:** Named the specific mechanism — client-contracted requirement as the forcing function, not carrier, regulator, or internal initiative. This is the consulting ICP's primary engagement trigger, distinct from legal (bar guidance) and finance (institutional client questionnaire) triggers.

### Finding C300-P3-F2: Proposal Methodology Defensibly Crisis as Existential Threat to Consulting Value

**What it is:** The consulting firm's value is their methodology and intellectual capital. In C300, managing directors are discovering that they cannot reconstruct what role AI played in the proposals and deliverables they've produced — and clients are starting to ask. When a client asks "how did you produce this recommendation?" the consulting firm has no documented answer. They used AI tools in the engagement but have no methodology provenance record. The recommendation stands on the firm's intellectual authority — and that authority is undermined when they cannot demonstrate the integrity of the work product.

**Why it matters:** This is not a compliance problem. This is an existential problem for the consulting business model. If a consulting firm cannot demonstrate the integrity of their methodology, they cannot defend their recommendations, cannot respond to client challenges, and cannot produce evidence in a professional liability claim. The methodology is the product — and it's undocumented when AI is used.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The client asked: 'how did you produce this analysis?' You can't say you used ChatGPT. You can't pretend everything came from your brain. And you have no documented answer. That's the problem. I can give you a methodology provenance system — a way to document AI's role in each deliverable without undermining your intellectual authority."

**Distinct from C214–C299:** Named methodology defensibility as the consulting firm's core value problem — not as a compliance gap, but as a threat to the fundamental business model of consulting. Legal (Phase 01) and finance (Phase 02) have different core values (client confidentiality, portfolio integrity); consulting's core value is methodology and intellectual capital, which AI use without documentation directly undermines.

### Finding C300-P3-F3: Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Chain as Unmonitored Structural Exposure

**What it is:** Geneva consulting boutiques routinely delegate portions of client engagements to sub-consultants — other boutiques, independent specialists, niche experts. In C300, those sub-consultants are using AI tools. The delegating firm has no visibility into what tools, what data, what methodology, what client data is being processed. The engagement contract has no AI delegation clause. The sub-consultant has no obligation to disclose their AI tool use. The managing director signed the client contract with the AI clause — and has no way to close the liability chain through the sub-consultant's AI use.

**Why it matters:** The liability chain is structurally open and undocumented. When something goes wrong — the sub-consultant's AI-assisted analysis causes a bad client outcome — the managing director cannot produce documentation showing where responsibility sits, what AI was used, what data was processed. The chain was never structured. The managing director is personally exposed because their signature is on the engagement, but they never required the sub-consultant to represent anything about AI governance.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You're working with sub-consultants. They're using AI tools. If AI from your sub-consultant's work causes a problem at the client — it's your problem. You don't know where the chain ends because you never closed it. I can show you how to structure the sub-consultant AI delegation clause — and what your sub-consultants need to represent about their AI governance."

**Distinct from C214–C299:** Named the sub-consultant delegation chain as a discrete structural exposure for consulting firms — distinct from legal (internal law firm operations) and finance (portfolio construction AI) which don't involve the same sub-delegation model. The consulting firm's delegation architecture creates a specific AI governance problem that neither legal nor finance ICPs face.

### Finding C300-P3-F4: Engagement Letter AI Silence as Structural Contractual Protection Gap

**What it is:** Geneva consulting engagement letters (lettres de mission) do not contain AI-related clauses. They do not specify: what AI tools may be used in the engagement, how client data is processed through AI systems, what happens to client data when the engagement ends, how AI-assisted work product should be interpreted, who bears risk when AI-assisted analysis produces an incorrect recommendation. The engagement letter — the foundational document of the client relationship — is silent on AI. When something goes wrong, there is no contractual framework for allocating responsibility for AI-assisted work.

**Why it matters:** The engagement letter is the firm's primary contractual protection mechanism. Its silence on AI means the firm has: no liability limitation for AI-assisted work product, no data processing clause covering AI system access to client data, no disclosure language for clients about AI's role in the deliverable, no sub-consultant AI clause. When a client challenges an AI-assisted recommendation, the firm has no contractual defense.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your engagement letters don't mention AI. That means when something goes wrong, you have no contractual protection for AI-assisted work. And you have no liability limitation. I can give you a ready-to-use AI clause for consulting engagement letters — calibrated for Geneva practice, ready for your next engagement."

**Distinct from C214–C299:** Named the engagement letter AI silence as a discrete contractual protection gap for consulting firms. Legal ICP (Phase 01) faces engagement letter gaps but in the context of client confidentiality; the consulting ICP faces it in the context of methodology defensibility, client data processing, and sub-consultant delegation.

### Finding C300-P3-F5: Carrier Attestation Pre-Signing Review as Consulting-Specific Medical Practitioner Analogue

**What it is:** Professional liability carriers serving Geneva consulting boutiques are including AI attestations in renewal documents — asking the managing director to confirm adequate AI governance for client engagements. The attestation specifically covers: AI use in client delivery, client data processing through AI tools, sub-consultant AI delegation, methodology documentation. The managing director signs personally. No documentation is required at signing. No one has reviewed what the attestation actually requires the firm to have — or what personal exposure the managing director is accepting by signing.

**Why it matters:** The medical practitioner analogue from Phase 02 (finance) applies here: medical practitioners needed and developed a pre-signing review service — someone reviews the carrier attestation form before the signature is applied, assesses personal liability exposure, and advises on what documentation must exist before signing. Consulting managing directors face the same exposure — personal signature, undefined adequacy standard, carrier audit after claim — without that service existing. The attestation covers client delivery AI, which is precisely where the consulting ICP's exposure lives.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Medical practitioners have a service: pre-signing review of carrier attestation forms. Before they sign, someone reviews what they're committing to and whether their signature can be defended. Consulting managing directors are in the same exposure position — personal signature, undefined standard, client delivery AI exposure — without that service. I built that service for consulting firms."

**Distinct from C214–C299:** Named the carrier attestation pre-signing review gap as a consulting-specific service, distinct from the finance (Phase 02) version which focused on portfolio management AI. The consulting version covers client engagement methodology, sub-consultant delegation, and proposal defensibility — the specific exposure profile for consulting boutiques.

### Finding C300-P3-F6: Swissdamed/EU AI Act Classification Paralysis as Proportionate Compliance Blocker for Consulting Firms

**What it is:** Swissdamed and EU AI Act create compliance obligations for AI system users in Switzerland. Geneva consulting boutiques using AI in client engagements cannot determine: (a) whether Swissdamed applies to their AI use in client service delivery, (b) whether they are users of a Tier 1 or Tier 2 AI system under Swissdamed, (c) whether they are a high-risk AI system operator under EU AI Act if they serve EU clients, (d) what proportionate documentation is required for their classification. The classification determines what compliance requires — and the consulting firm cannot determine their classification. This creates proportionate compliance paralysis: firms either over-comply or under-comply with no validated middle ground.

**Why it matters:** Both Swissdamed (July 1, 2026) and EU AI Act (implementing) are imminent. The consulting firm that cannot determine their classification cannot take proportionate compliance action. The firm's AI use in client engagements — the core of their service delivery — is the subject of the classification question, and the firm has no guidance on how to answer it. No Geneva-calibrated classification assessment methodology exists for consulting boutiques facing these deadlines.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Swissdamed is weeks away. EU AI Act is weeks away. You use AI in your client engagements — you don't know if either framework applies to your firm, or what it would require if it did. The classification criteria were not written for consulting boutiques. I can help you make that determination — and build the documentation that matches your actual classification, not a guess."

**Distinct from C214–C299:** Named classification paralysis as active compliance blocker for consulting firms — distinct from Phase 02 (finance) which focused on portfolio management AI classification and Phase 01 (legal) which focused on internal law firm AI classification. The consulting version addresses client engagement AI delivery classification, which has different triggers and different documentation requirements.

### Body Copy Principles (C300 Phase 03)

**What works with the C300 Phase 03 ICP:**
- **Client-contractual framing** — the forcing function is the engagement contract, not a regulator; this changes the urgency from compliance to competition
- **Methodology as the product** — the consulting firm's methodology is their value; AI use without documentation undermines it
- **Chain-of-responsibility framing** — sub-consultant AI delegation is a liability chain the managing director cannot close without structure
- **Engagement letter as protection mechanism** — the letter of mission is the firm's primary contractual defense; its silence on AI is a structural gap
- **Pre-signing review as the critical moment** — the carrier signature is the forcing function; everything before it is preparation
- **Classification as active blocker** — both deadlines create proportionate compliance paralysis; naming the specific problem is the first step

**What does NOT work with the C300 Phase 03 ICP:**
- Generic AI governance framing ("you need an AI policy")
- Enterprise framework language ("NIST controls", "ISO 42001")
- Compliance template approach ("here's a policy template")
- Regulatory compliance framing when the immediate pressure is contractual
- Big 4 comparison language ("we're like Big 4 but smaller")
- English-only content for Geneva-first audience
