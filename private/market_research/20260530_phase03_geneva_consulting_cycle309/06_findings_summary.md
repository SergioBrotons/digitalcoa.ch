## The 6 NET-NEW Findings

### Finding 01: Client RFP AI Scoring Has Become the Primary Commercial Gate — And Boutique Documentation Hasn't Caught Up

**The finding:** The client RFP AI governance scoring matrix is now the primary activating pressure for Geneva consulting boutique AI governance action. The institutional or corporate client uses a scoring matrix that evaluates specific evidence — not general policy statements. The boutique that responds with a general AI governance policy scores zero or near-zero on the AI governance dimension. The commercial consequence is immediate: shortlist elimination, mandate loss, competitive disadvantage.

**The mechanism:** Client procurement function sends RFP → scoring matrix includes AI governance criteria → boutique responds with general policy → client scores against specific evidence criteria → boutique fails scoring threshold → mandate lost to competitor who could produce specific evidence.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your RFP response scored zero on AI governance. Not because you don't have a policy — because your policy doesn't answer what the scoring matrix measures. I rebuild the response to match what the client actually scores."

**The C309 unique insight:** Phase 01 (legal) was carrier-driven. Phase 02 (finance) was institutional DDQ-driven. Phase 03 (consulting) is client RFP-driven — the client is the primary forcing function, not the regulator or the carrier. The managing partner responds to commercial pressure, not regulatory pressure, first.

---

### Finding 02: Proposal Methodology Defensibility Has Two Dimensions — Disclosure and Traceability — And Boutique Firms Are Missing Both

**The finding:** Proposal methodology defensibility for AI-assisted consulting work has two distinct dimensions that are often conflated. Disclosure (did the client know AI was used) is necessary but not sufficient. Traceability (can you walk through the AI-assisted logic chain with precision and identify where errors could have occurred) is what professional liability actually turns on when a client challenges a recommendation. Most boutiques have neither dimension covered in their proposal documentation.

**The mechanism:** Client challenges recommendation → asks about methodology → managing partner cannot walk through AI-assisted analysis with precision → client infers methodology is not systematic → trust deteriorates → engagement at risk OR professional liability claim viable.

**Ready-to-use language:** "There's a difference between 'we used AI' and 'we can defend how we used AI.' The first is a disclosure question. The second is what your professional liability turns on. I build the methodology documentation that covers both."

**The C309 unique insight:** The consulting boutique's primary product is the proposal. The methodology is the product. If the methodology is AI-assisted and cannot be defended, the entire value proposition is compromised. This is more immediate than in law firms (where the primary product is legal advice) or wealth management (where the primary product is portfolio performance). The proposal quality IS the consulting firm's product.

### Finding 03: Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Creates an Invisible Accountability Gap — Carrier Attestation Signed, No Monitoring System Exists

**The finding:** Geneva consulting boutiques engage 1–3 sub-consultants on larger mandates. Sub-consultant AI tool use is not systematically documented. The managing partner reviews AI-assisted sub-consultant output, incorporates it into client deliverables, and signs off — without a record of what AI tools were used, what data was processed, or what chain of authorization exists. The carrier attestation signed ("I monitor AI use in the firm") is a misrepresentation in the absence of any sub-consultant AI monitoring system.

**The mechanism:** Sub-consultant uses AI on client matter → managing partner incorporates output into deliverable → client asks methodology question → managing partner hedges → client infers weak methodology control → carrier attestation signed without sub-consultant monitoring documentation → coverage risk if claim arises.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You're signing the carrier attestation that says you monitor AI use. The sub-consultant is using AI on your client matter. You have no monitoring system. That's the gap. I'll build the delegation register that closes it — something your firm can actually maintain, not an enterprise governance platform."

**The C309 unique insight:** Sub-consultant delegation is structurally different from associate supervision (legal) or portfolio AI shadow systems (finance). In a consulting boutique, the sub-consultant is often more AI-fluent than the managing partner, operates independently on specialized expertise, and is engaged specifically for their domain knowledge — not for their AI governance sophistication. The accountability chain must accommodate this.

### Finding 04: Personal nFADP Article 52 Exposure Is Activating Through Broker Conversations — Not Regulatory Channels

**The finding:** Managing partners are learning about their personal nFADP Article 52 exposure through broker conversations, not FDPIC guidance. The broker community is asking about personal nFADP coverage in routine insurance reviews. The managing partner investigates, discovers administrative fines are explicitly excluded from standard professional liability insurance, and realizes they are personally exposed for up to CHF 250,000 for intentional violations. The discovery pathway is peer-to-peer and broker-to-client, not regulatory communication.

**The mechanism:** Broker reviews coverage → asks about personal nFADP exposure → managing partner checks policy → finds exclusion for administrative fines → manages partner investigates nFADP obligations → discovers Article 52 personal liability → searches for proportionate documentation standard → finds none → calls service provider.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your broker asked about personal nFADP coverage. You checked. Administrative fines are excluded from your policy. Article 52 makes you personally responsible as managing partner. The documentation that protects you isn't a compliance document — it's evidence of specific authorization decisions you made, as managing partner, for specific AI systems, on specific client data. I build that evidence."

**The C309 unique insight:** The Phase 03 personal liability activation pathway is different from Phase 01 (carrier attestation) and Phase 02 (FINMA examination). The broker is the primary activation channel — the broker identifies the gap in the insurance conversation, and the managing partner discovers the exposure from the policy exclusion, not from regulatory guidance.

### Finding 05: Bilingual EN/FR Documentation Is a Design Requirement, Not a Translation Task — And the Market Has Neither

**The finding:** Geneva consulting boutiques serve both English-speaking and French-speaking clients. Carrier attestation forms arrive in French. Client RFPs arrive in English. Managing partners need to produce documentation in both languages — and most AI governance resources are English-only. The boutique that produces translated documents has two problems: translation is not the same as native bilingual documentation design, and neither the French carrier form requirements nor the English-language client methodology documentation is adequately satisfied by a translated policy.

**The mechanism:** Boutique produces English-only AI governance documentation → carrier form arrives in French → boutique translates policy for carrier response → French translation doesn't match carrier form vocabulary expectations → evidence gap persists. Boutique serves English-speaking clients → client asks for methodology documentation → English-only documentation insufficient → evidence gap in both directions.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The carrier form is in French. Your English-speaking clients want English-language methodology documentation. Translation doesn't solve this — you need a documentation system designed to work in both. I build bilingual AI governance documentation as standard. Not translated. Designed."

**The C309 unique insight:** The bilingual requirement is a design constraint, not a localization task. Enterprise platforms are English-only. Local Swiss providers offer French-only guidance. No one has built a documentation system designed natively for the Geneva consulting boutique's dual-language client and carrier context. This is a specific competitive moat: the service that builds native bilingual documentation as standard has an advantage no competitor has addressed.

### Finding 06: The Proportionate AI Governance Service Design Gap Is Preventing Action — Not Knowledge

**The finding:** Geneva consulting boutiques know they have an AI governance problem. They have attempted to find solutions. The solutions they find are enterprise AI governance platforms (require compliance departments), generic policy templates (don't address consulting methodology), and general AI governance training (produce awareness, not documentation). None are designed for a 5–50 person consulting firm where the managing partner is simultaneously the compliance function, service lead, and BD lead. The service design gap is preventing action, not knowledge.

**The mechanism:** Boutique evaluates options → enterprise platform requires infrastructure they don't have → generic template doesn't address specific problems → training doesn't produce usable documentation → managing partner concludes "nothing is designed for my firm" → action delayed → documentation debt compounds → next RFP cycle arrives without evidence → commercial consequence confirmed.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Nothing you've found is designed for a 12-person consulting firm. Enterprise platforms, generic templates, general training — none of it fits your operational reality. I design the service around how a small Geneva consulting boutique actually works. Proportionate. Bilingual. Actionable."

**The C309 unique insight:** The proportionate service design gap is the reason the C309 consulting ICP has not acted despite knowing about the problem. The ICP is not in discovery mode ("do I have a problem?"). The ICP is in frustrated action mode ("I know I need to do something, nothing is designed for me"). Converting this ICP requires showing you understand their operational reality specifically — not generic AI governance principles.

## The C309 Phase 03 Core Insight

**The market has moved from "can I keep the mandate?" (Phase 02) to "can I win the next one?" (Phase 03)**

Phase 01 (legal): "Do I have a problem?" — discovery mode
Phase 02 (finance): "Can I keep the mandate?" — retention mode
Phase 03 (consulting): "Can I win the next mandate?" — acquisition mode

The consulting boutique ICP is activated by client RFP AI scoring. The client is the forcing function. The RFP scoring matrix is the specification. The evidence gap is costing mandates. The solution is evidence architecture built to the client's scoring criteria, not to a generic AI governance framework.

**The Phase 03 unique contribution:**
The consulting boutique's primary product is the proposal. The methodology IS the product. When AI enters the proposal and cannot be defended, the boutique's core value proposition is compromised. This is more immediate than regulatory compliance or carrier attestation — it's commercial survival in the pitch.

The three activating pressures by vertical:

| Vertical | Primary Activation | Forcing Function |
|---|---|---|
| Legal (Phase 01) | Carrier renewal | Regulatory deadline |
| Finance (Phase 02) | Institutional DDQ | Commercial gatekeeper |
| Consulting (Phase 03) | Client RFP scoring | Commercial acquisition |

All three lead to the same documentation need: evidence of human authorization for specific AI-assisted decisions. But the activation pathway differs by vertical — and understanding which pathway the ICP is on determines the entry point for the conversation.
