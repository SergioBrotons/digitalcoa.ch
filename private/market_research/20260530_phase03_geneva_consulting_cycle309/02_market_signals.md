## The 6 NET-NEW Findings — C309 Phase 03

### Finding 01: Client RFP AI Scoring Has Evolved from Yes/No to Specific Evidence Requests — And Boutique Responses Haven't

**Signal type:** Active commercial consequence, observed market failure

**What the market is doing:** Institutional and mid-size corporate clients are now sending RFPs that include AI governance scoring matrices. The matrices ask for specific evidence: vendor contracts for AI tools used in the engagement, supervision protocols for AI use, error identification procedures, documentation of AI tool authorization. This is not "do you have an AI policy" — it is "show us your actual AI practice for this specific engagement."

**What boutique consulting firms are doing:** Most are responding with their firm-level AI governance policy — a general statement that answers the wrong question. The score is zero or near-zero on the AI governance dimension because the evidence format doesn't match what the scoring matrix actually measures.

**What is failing:** The documentation-practice mismatch. The boutique has some form of AI governance policy (written after the initial carrier attestation wave). That policy is designed to answer "does this firm have AI governance" — it is not designed to answer "can this firm produce specific evidence of AI supervision per engagement." The RFP scoring matrix measures the latter.

**Commercial consequence:** Mandate loss, shortlist elimination, competitive disadvantage in pitches where AI-savvy competitors can produce the evidence.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your RFP response scored zero on AI governance. Not because you don't have a policy — because your policy doesn't answer what the scoring matrix measures. I rebuild the response to match what the client actually scores."

### Finding 02: The Proposal Methodology Defensibility Gap Has Two Dimensions — Disclosure and Traceability — And Boutique Firms Are Missing Both

**Signal type:** Active client relationship friction, professional liability concern

**The two dimensions:**

**Dimension 1 — Disclosure:** Did the client know AI was used in the proposal? Most boutiques have not explicitly disclosed AI use in proposals. Some add disclaimers after the fact. The disclaimer is not the same as documented client consent.

**Dimension 2 — Traceability:** Can the managing director walk through the AI-assisted logic chain with precision, identify where errors could have occurred, and correct them? This is what professional liability actually turns on if a client challenges a recommendation. Most cannot do this.

**The failure mode:** Client challenges recommendation → asks about methodology → managing director hedges or gives vague answer → client infers methodology is not systematic → trust deteriorates → engagement at risk.

**Observed market behavior:** Managing directors adding disclaimers to proposals without understanding that a disclaimer is not the same as a defensible methodology. The disclaimer protects against "you didn't tell me" but not against "your methodology was flawed."

**The consulting specific problem:** The proposal is the primary deliverable. The quality of the methodology is the product. If the methodology is AI-assisted and cannot be defended, the entire value proposition is compromised.

**Ready-to-use language:** "There's a difference between 'we used AI' and 'we can defend how we used AI.' The first is a disclosure question. The second is what your professional liability turns on. I build the methodology documentation that covers both."

### Finding 03: Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Creates an Invisible Accountability Gap That Surfaces Only in Client Meetings

**Signal type:** Client relationship friction, carrier attestation misrepresentation risk

**The mechanism:** Managing partner engages 1–3 sub-consultants on larger mandates. Sub-consultant uses AI for research, analysis, slide preparation. Managing partner reviews output, incorporates into client deliverable, signs off. No record of what AI tools were used, what data was processed, what chain of authorization exists. Carrier attestation signed: "I monitor AI use in the firm." In practice, no monitoring system exists for sub-consultant AI use.

**When it surfaces:** Client asks about methodology → managing partner cannot walk through the AI-assisted analysis with precision → client infers weak methodology control → carrier has grounds to question attestation accuracy if this becomes a claim.

**The boutique-specific exposure:** Sub-consultants are often engaged for their specialized expertise — not their AI governance sophistication. The managing partner is accountable for the output but has no visibility into the AI tools used to produce it.

**Observed market behavior:** Boutique managing directors reporting specific incidents where sub-consultant AI use was questioned by clients and they had no documentation to show. Some describing it as "embarrassing" — indicating it has happened in pitch or client meeting contexts.

**The documentation need:** Lightweight delegation register — not an enterprise governance platform. Something a 5-person firm can actually maintain: sub-consultant name, engagement, AI tools used, data processed, authorization chain.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You're signing the carrier attestation. The attestation says you monitor AI use. The sub-consultant is using AI on your client matter. You have no monitoring system. That's the gap."

### Finding 04: Personal nFADP Article 52 Exposure Is Activating Through Broker Conversations — Not Regulatory Channels

**Signal type:** Discovery pathway, peer activation, insurance coverage gap

**The observation:** Managing partners are learning about their personal nFADP exposure through broker conversations, not FDPIC guidance. The broker community is asking about personal nFADP coverage in routine insurance reviews. The managing partner investigates, discovers administrative fines are explicitly excluded from standard professional liability coverage, and realizes they are personally exposed.

**The legal basis:** nFADP Article 52 creates personal liability for natural persons — managing partners are natural persons. Administrative fines up to CHF 250,000 for intentional violations. The fine is not covered by professional liability insurance (which covers third-party claims, not administrative penalties).

**The discovery pathway:**
1. Broker reviews coverage → asks about personal nFADP exposure
2. Managing partner checks policy → finds exclusion for administrative fines
3. Managing partner investigates nFADP obligations → discovers Article 52 personal liability
4. Managing partner searches for documentation standard → finds none proportionate to boutique consulting scale

**The paradox:** The managing partner who investigates is more exposed than the one who doesn't — because the investigation reveals the gap the carrier doesn't ask about. The "I didn't know" defense doesn't work once you've been told.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your broker asked about personal nFADP coverage. You checked. Administrative fines are excluded from your policy. Article 52 makes you personally responsible. The documentation that protects you isn't a compliance document — it's evidence of due diligence in your specific AI decisions. I build that evidence."

### Finding 05: The Bilingual EN/FR Requirement Is a Documentation Design Problem, Not a Translation Problem

**Signal type:** Operational barrier, service design gap

**The observation:** Geneva consulting boutiques serve both English-speaking and French-speaking clients. Carrier attestation forms arrive in French. Client RFPs arrive in English. The managing director needs to respond in both languages. Most AI governance resources are English-only. The boutiques that do produce documentation produce it in English only — and then face problems with French carrier forms and French-speaking clients.

**The failure:** Translation is not the solution. A translated policy is not the same as a bilingual documentation system designed to work in both languages simultaneously. The carrier form expects specific French vocabulary. The English-language client expects English-language methodology documentation. A translation of a policy does not satisfy either requirement.

**The service design gap:** No provider has designed an AI governance documentation system for the Geneva consulting boutique that works natively in EN/FR. Enterprise platforms are English-only. Local providers offer French-language guidance that doesn't address the English-language client context. The bilingual requirement is unsolved.

**What boutiques are doing:** Some use English-language policies with French disclaimers — neither sufficient for the carrier form questions nor for the client-facing methodology documentation. Some hire translators for carrier forms — a reactive, expensive, non-strategic approach.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The carrier form is in French. Your English-speaking clients want English-language methodology documentation. Translation doesn't solve this — you need a documentation system designed to work in both. I build bilingual AI governance documentation as standard."

### Finding 06: The Proportionate AI Governance Service Design Gap Is Preventing Action — Not Knowledge

**Signal type:** Market failure, service design gap, action paralysis

**The observation:** Geneva consulting boutiques know they have an AI governance problem. They have attempted to find solutions. The solutions they find are: (1) enterprise AI governance platforms requiring compliance departments, (2) generic policy templates not designed for consulting deliverables context, (3) general AI governance training not addressing specific documentation needs. None are designed for a 5–30 person consulting firm where the managing partner is simultaneously the compliance function, the service lead, and the business development lead.

**The failure mode:** Boutique evaluates options → enterprise platform requires infrastructure they don't have → generic template doesn't address their specific problems → training doesn't produce documentation they can use → managing partner concludes "nothing is designed for my firm" → no action taken → documentation gap persists.

**The specific service design gap:** No one has offered a service architecture that matches the consulting boutique's operational reality: client RFP AI response sprints, carrier attestation accuracy review, sub-consultant delegation framework, proposal methodology defensibility package, personal nFADP exposure assessment, proportionate ongoing retainer. The boutique needs a sequenced, proportionate response — not a comprehensive framework.

**The activation problem:** The managing partner who knows they need to act but cannot find a proportionate service will delay action. The delay compounds the documentation debt. The market needs a service designed for their specific scale and specific problems.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Nothing you've found is designed for a 12-person consulting firm. Enterprise platforms, generic templates, general training — none of it fits your operational reality. I design the service around how a small Geneva consulting boutique actually works. Proportionate. Bilingual. Actionable."

## What Phase 03 Adds to the C309 Core Insight

**Phase 01 established:** The market moved from "do I have a problem?" to "why didn't my solution work?" — implementation failure mode.

**Phase 02 established:** The market moved from "why didn't my solution work?" to "can I keep the mandate?" — commercial consequence mode.

**Phase 03 reveals:** The consulting boutique is facing a client-driven commercial forcing function (RFP AI scoring) that operates independently of regulatory deadlines. The managing director's mandate survival depends on producing AI governance evidence in a format the client can score. The documentation gap is not just a liability problem — it is a competitive positioning problem. The boutique that cannot produce AI governance evidence loses mandates. The boutique that can, wins them.

The Phase 03 insight: **The client RFP is the forcing function for consulting boutiques, as the institutional DDQ is for wealth managers and carrier renewal is for law firms.** Each vertical has a different primary activating pressure, but all lead to the same documentation need: evidence of human authorization for specific AI-assisted decisions.
