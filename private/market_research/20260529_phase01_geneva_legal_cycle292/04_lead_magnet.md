# The Geneva Small Firm AI Accountability Briefing
**6 Accountability Gaps Every 2–5 Attorney Geneva Firm Needs to Name Before Their Carrier Asks**
*Market Research — C292 Phase 01 | Geneva Legal ICP | May 2026*

---

## Why This Briefing Exists

If you are a Geneva-based law firm with 2 to 5 attorneys, and your professional liability carrier has begun asking questions about AI, you are not imagining the shift. What has changed is that the accountability questions are no longer hypothetical — and the gaps in your AI governance are no longer invisible to carriers, regulators, or clients.

This briefing names six specific accountability gaps that research for C292 Phase 01 identified as distinct to small Geneva firms in 2026. These are not generic AI risk warnings. These are named gaps that have a specific structure, a specific risk profile, and a specific window before they become unavoidable in carrier attestation, regulatory review, or client due diligence.

---

## Gap 1: The Art. 167a CPC Accountability Split You Haven't Named

**What happened:** On January 1, 2025, the Swiss Code of Civil Procedure was amended to create a new right for in-house counsel — a formal privilege to refuse cooperation in civil proceedings (Art. 167a CPC). This is not the same as Art. 321 CP professional secrecy. It is a new procedural right with different scope and different conditions.

**Why it creates a gap:** If your small firm operates with a mix of external-facing client work and in-house-type functions — as most 2–5 attorney firms do — your AI tool usage is governed by two different accountability tracks that have not been reconciled. External-facing AI usage falls under Art. 321 CP. In-house functions fall under Art. 167a CPC refusal rights. The same AI tool, used by the same attorney for different functions, may carry different professional accountability implications. No governance framework has mapped this split for small firms.

**What to do:** Audit which AI tools are used in which function within your practice. Document the accountability track applicable to each tool. If you are using AI tools across both external client matters and internal firm functions, name the split explicitly in your AI governance documentation.

## Gap 2: The FDPIC Is Not Waiting — Enforcement Is Live

**What happened:** In May 2025, the Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner (FDPIC) confirmed that existing Swiss data protection law applies directly to AI systems. There is no regulatory gap. There is no grace period. Enforcement is live under existing nFADP provisions, including criminal sanctions against responsible individuals.

**Why it creates a gap:** Most small Geneva firms are still operating under the assumption that AI governance can be deferred until AI-specific Swiss rules arrive. The May 2025 FDPIC signal changes that. If you are using cloud AI tools with US processing, EU data flows, or cross-border data transfers, your current AI usage may already be subject to existing enforcement risk. The FDPIC can initiate investigations independently — not only in response to complaints.

**What to do:** Treat the FDPIC May 2025 signal as a live enforcement posture, not a future risk. Review your current AI tool stack for cross-border processing. Verify that your data processing agreements with AI vendors meet existing nFADP Art. 9 requirements. Document this review.

## Gap 3: "Swiss Hosting" Is Not the Same as Professional Secrecy Compliance

**What happened:** A persistent procurement assumption in small Geneva firms is that AI tools marketed as "Swiss hosted" satisfy professional secrecy requirements under Art. 321 CP. This assumption conflates data residency (where data is stored) with processing location (where computation occurs). A tool with Swiss-based storage but US-based API processing, or processing through third-party infrastructure in undetermined jurisdictions, does not satisfy Art. 321 CP — regardless of where the data sits at rest.

**Why it creates a gap:** AI tool vendors market on data residency because that is the visible, verifiable attribute. Processing location is invisible in most vendor contracts and often undocumented even in data processing agreements. Small firms purchasing on the Swiss signal are not detecting the processing-location gap — and carriers asking about AI are beginning to ask the processing-location question.

**What to do:** For each AI tool in your stack, request documentation of where processing occurs — not just where data is stored. If your primary AI tools route processing through US infrastructure, you have a named Art. 321 CP exposure that is not resolved by the Swiss hosting label.

## Gap 4: The Geneva Bar Has Not Issued AI Guidance Specific to This Canton

**What happened:** The Swiss Bar Association (SBA/SAV) published AI guidelines in June 2024 (Anwaltsrevue 9/2024). These national guidelines provide a reference framework. They are not sufficient for firm-level AI governance decisions. The Ordre des Avocats de Genève has not issued canton-specific AI guidance. Geneva practitioners are operating under national guidelines that are too general to drive specific firm decisions, with no Geneva-level supplement.

**Why it creates a gap:** The national SBA framework does not account for Geneva-specific practice dynamics: French-language client expectations, cantonal court procedure specifics, and the particular profile of Geneva-based international clientele with cross-border data expectations. A small Geneva firm relying on SBA guidelines alone is effectively operating without jurisdictionally specific accountability framing. Carriers and clients asking about AI governance will ask what specific Geneva-specific standards the firm applies.

**What to do:** Do not rely on SBA guidelines as your sole AI governance reference for Geneva-specific accountability. Develop internal AI governance documentation that addresses Geneva-specific practice context. This is not required by any current rule — but it is what carrier attestation questions and client due diligence are beginning to imply is the standard.

## Gap 5: The nFADP Criminal Track Is Not the Same as an Administrative Fine

**What happened:** Under nFADP Art. 60–66, violations carry criminal sanctions against responsible individuals, with fines up to CHF 250,000. This is commonly conflated with administrative fine frameworks. The criminal liability track under Art. 63 operates on different evidentiary standards (personal guilt, intent or negligence) and different enforcement logic (criminal prosecution by cantonal prosecutors, not administrative sanction by FDPIC).

**Why it creates a gap:** Small Geneva firms treat nFADP exposure as a regulatory compliance matter. The personal criminal liability track is a structurally different exposure — with different risk characteristics, different consequences, and different defensive requirements. AI tool usage in client matters that results in a data breach or unauthorized disclosure does not simply trigger a fine; it can trigger personal criminal exposure for the responsible attorney.

**What to do:** Name the criminal liability track as a distinct risk category in your AI governance documentation. Assess which AI tools, if misused in a client matter, could trigger personal criminal exposure under Art. 63. This is not the same risk assessment as your nFADP compliance review.

## Gap 6: Your EU Clients Are Beginning to Ask About Your AI Literacy

**What happened:** Under the EU AI Act, organizations using AI systems must ensure staff have appropriate AI literacy (Art. 4, applicable since February 1, 2025). For Geneva small firms with EU client relationships or EU data flows, this obligation applies extraterritorially. It is not hypothetical. It is not deferred. And it has no Swiss equivalent — Swiss regulators have not adopted an AI literacy standard.

**Why it creates a gap:** Geneva firms with EU nexus face a live compliance obligation under EU law that is invisible to Swiss authorities, unacknowledged in standard Swiss firm practice, and potentially material in EU client relationships. EU-based clients and counterparties are beginning to include AI literacy assessments in vendor due diligence. A Geneva firm that cannot document staff AI literacy may face EU client relationship consequences — with no Swiss regulatory body having issued guidance on what the obligation requires.

**What to do:** If you have EU client relationships or process data for EU-based matters, conduct a baseline AI literacy assessment. Document it. This is not currently a Swiss regulatory requirement — but it is an EU legal obligation with potential commercial consequences for your EU client relationships.

## What to Do With This Briefing

These six gaps are not comprehensive. They are named because they are specific to the Geneva small firm context, distinct from enterprise firm dynamics, and actionable without enterprise-scale governance infrastructure.

Each gap has a structure: a triggering condition, a named risk, and a documentation response. The documentation response does not require a compliance department. It requires a partner who is willing to name the gap, document the firm's position, and maintain the documentation as AI usage evolves.

If you are the partner responsible for AI decisions in a 2–5 attorney Geneva firm, this briefing is your accountability reference for the questions your carrier is beginning to ask.
