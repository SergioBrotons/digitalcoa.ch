# ICP Brief: Geneva Legal — Small Law Firms (2–5 Attorneys)
**ICP:** Geneva Legal | **Firm Size:** 2–5 Attorneys | **Language:** French/English
**Cycle:** C292 Phase 01 | **Market Research Date:** 2026-05-29

---

## Profile Definition

### Who They Are
Small Geneva-based law firms, typically structured as partnerships or small group practices with 2 to 5 practicing attorneys. Client base skews toward SME, international private clients, and mid-market commercial work. French is the primary language of client communication; English is common in international matters.

### What They Do With AI (Current State)
- Use AI tools for contract review, legal research, and document drafting on an ad hoc basis
- Primarily using general-purpose cloud AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot) without systematic vendor vetting
- Most have not formalized an AI governance policy or AI tool procurement framework
- Small firm structure means AI decisions often made by one or two partners without formal committee review
- Staff use AI without explicit firm authorization (shadow AI), particularly associates

### What Keeps Them Up at Night (Stated)
- Professional secrecy (Art. 321 CP) — client data leaving the firm via AI tools
- nFADP compliance — uncertainty about what applies and when
- Professional liability — whether AI-assisted work creates new exposure
- Competitive pressure — other firms using AI, fear of falling behind
- Client expectations — clients beginning to ask about AI use

### What Keeps Them Up at Night (Unstated)
- Attestation requirements from professional liability carriers starting to include AI-related questions
- The gap between what their PI insurance actually covers and what their carrier application implied
- Associate over-reliance on AI outputs without firm-level oversight mechanisms
- The likelihood that their AI usage history is more extensive than they have documented
- FDPIC enforcement posture shift — enforcement is now live, not future-risk

---

## Current Accountability Pressures (C292 Phase 01 Angles)

### Pressure 1: Art. 167a CPC Accountability Split (NEW)
The January 2025 CPC amendment creates a new accountability split for small firms with hybrid external/in-house functions. AI tool governance standards have not mapped to this split. Small firm partners may be operating under inconsistent accountability standards without knowing it.

### Pressure 2: FDPIC Enforcement Is Live
The FDPIC's May 2025 confirmation that existing nFADP applies directly to AI is not theoretical. For firms using cloud AI tools with US processing or EU data flows, enforcement exposure is present today. Small firms have not updated their AI procurement or usage frameworks in response.

### Pressure 3: "Swiss Hosting" Procurement Error
Small firms purchasing AI tools based on "Swiss hosted" signals are not detecting the processing-location gap. A tool with Swiss data storage but US-based processing creates Art. 321 CP exposure regardless of the storage location. This is a named procurement error in this cycle's research.

### Pressure 4: Geneva Bar Guidance Vacuum
The Ordre des Avocats de Genève has not issued canton-specific AI guidance. National SBA guidelines operate at a level of abstraction too general for small firm governance. Geneva practitioners lack jurisdictionally specific accountability framing and default to generic national guidance that doesn't drive firm-level decisions.

### Pressure 5: nFADP Art. 63 Criminal Exposure Distinct from Administrative Fines
Small firm attorneys treat nFADP exposure as primarily regulatory-administrative. The Art. 63 criminal liability track operates on different evidentiary standards and enforcement logic. Personal criminal exposure from AI tool usage in client matters is not being assessed as a distinct risk category.

### Pressure 6: EU AI Act Extraterritorial Literacy Obligation
Geneva small firms with EU client relationships face a live AI literacy obligation under Art. 4 EU AI Act (since February 2025). This obligation has no Swiss equivalent and is unacknowledged in small firm practice. Firms with EU nexus are exposed without knowing it.

## Readiness State
- **Governance:** Absent or ad hoc in most 2–5 attorney firms
- **AI tool inventory:** Typically incomplete — partners unaware of full tool palette used by associates
- **Client communication:** Rarely addressed in engagement letters or client advisories
- **Attestation:** Beginning to surface in PI carrier applications; firms uncertain how to respond
- **Documentation:** Minimal; most AI usage is undocumented
- **Carrier relationship:** Emerging tension — carriers asking AI questions; firms unsure what is being attested to
