# Executive Summary — C292 Phase 01: Geneva Legal ICP
**Market Research | 2026-05-29**

---

## Research Outcome

Six NET-NEW findings delivered for the Geneva legal ICP (small law firms, 2–5 attorneys), distinct from all prior Phase 01 Geneva cycles (C214–C291).

---

## ICP at a Glance
Geneva-based small law firms (2–5 attorneys), primarily French-speaking client base with strong English international component. Ad hoc AI usage via general-purpose cloud tools. No formal AI governance policy, no complete tool inventory, minimal documentation. Shadow AI present among associates. Carrier attestation questions emerging. FDPIC enforcement posture is now live.

---

## The Six NET-NEW Findings

**1. Art. 167a CPC Accountability Split (January 2025)**
The new CPC amendment creates dual-track accountability for firms with hybrid external/in-house functions. AI tool usage falls under different accountability standards depending on whether it is used in external client matters (Art. 321 CP) or internal firm functions (Art. 167a CPC). No governance framework has mapped this split for small firms. Named gap: the same AI tool, same attorney, different accountability track.

**2. FDPIC Enforcement Is Live (May 2025 Signal)**
FDPIC confirmed existing nFADP applies directly to AI with no regulatory gap. Enforcement posture is no longer future-risk — it is live. Small Geneva firms using cloud AI tools with cross-border processing face present enforcement exposure under existing nFADP provisions, including personal criminal liability under Art. 63.

**3. "Swiss Hosting" Procurement Error**
Small firms purchasing AI tools based on "Swiss hosted" signals are not detecting the processing-location gap. Data residency (storage location) ≠ processing location (computation location). A tool with Swiss storage but US processing creates Art. 321 CP exposure. The procurement signal does not resolve the professional secrecy compliance question.

**4. Geneva Bar Guidance Vacuum vs. National SBA Framework**
The Ordre des Avocats de Genève has not issued canton-specific AI guidance. National SBA AI guidelines (June 2024) operate at a level of abstraction too general for firm-level governance decisions. Geneva practitioners lack jurisdictionally specific accountability framing. National guidelines are the reference; they do not substitute for Geneva-specific documentation.

**5. nFADP Art. 63 Criminal Liability as Distinct Track**
nFADP Art. 63 personal criminal liability operates on different evidentiary standards (personal guilt, intent/negligence) and different enforcement logic (cantonal criminal prosecution) than administrative fine frameworks. Small firms treat nFADP exposure as regulatory-administrative. The criminal track is a structurally distinct exposure with different defensive requirements.

**6. EU AI Act Extraterritorial Literacy Obligation**
Geneva small firms with EU client relationships face Art. 4 EU AI Act AI literacy obligation (live since February 2025). This applies extraterritorially to Swiss firms with EU nexus. No Swiss equivalent exists. Unacknowledged in small firm practice despite being a live EU legal obligation with potential commercial consequences for EU client relationships.
