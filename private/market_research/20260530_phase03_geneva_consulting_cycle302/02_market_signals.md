# 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Complaints, Failures

## Phase 03 — C302 | Geneva Consulting (2–50 employees)

---

## 1. Client AI Governance Questionnaires — The New Primary Conversion Trigger

**Signal:** Corporate clients, institutional investors, family offices, and public sector entities are now sending detailed AI governance questionnaires to their consulting vendors as part of RFP due diligence and ongoing vendor oversight.

**What the ICP reports:**
- Questionnaires arrive with 2–4 week deadlines
- Questions ask for AI authorization records, decision attribution logs, vendor AI documentation
- Scoring criteria now include AI governance as a weighted section — not just a checkbox
- Consultants don't know what records they should have, what format they should be in, or how to respond
- Some ICPs have received the same questionnaire from multiple clients — inconsistency in what clients ask amplifies confusion
- A "no" or "incomplete" response risks disqualification from future RFPs

**Failure mode:** The ICP has no documentation system. They try to reconstruct retrospectively. They produce a vague policy document. The client sees through it. Trust is damaged.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva institutional clients (banks, pharma, commodity trading firms) are more advanced in their own AI governance posture. They expect consulting vendors to have equivalent sophistication. The expectation gap is wide and visible.

---

## 2. Carrier Attestation — The Evidence Gap

**Signal:** Insurance carriers serving the professional services sector have shifted from attestation-based to evidence-based documentation requirements (carrier form v4 equivalent). The request for "last three authorization records" is landing in consulting firm inboxes.

**What the ICP reports:**
- *"I signed the attestation months ago. I didn't know it meant I'd need to produce records."*
- *"We don't have authorization records. We have email threads."*
- *"The carrier is asking for something we never captured. How do we reconstruct it?"*
- *"Is this the same standard as what the bank asked for? Are these connected?"*
- Confusion about whether carrier attestation and client questionnaires are related or separate obligations

**Failure mode:** The ICP discovers the gap only when asked to produce. Retroactive reconstruction fails. The carrier form becomes a liability rather than a protection.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva consulting firms often carry professional liability insurance with carriers who are themselves navigating AI governance standards. The carrier is also learning — creating additional uncertainty about what constitutes acceptable evidence.

---

## 3. Proposal Methodology Defensibility — The Moment of Truth

**Signal:** Consulting proposals are increasingly challenged by clients — both at RFP stage and retrospectively when outcomes are reviewed. AI-assisted methodology is no longer accepted as a disclosure; clients want to understand the reasoning chain.

**What the ICP reports:**
- *"I said 'AI-assisted analysis' in the proposal. The client asked what that means specifically."*
- *"If the recommendation turns out wrong, I need to show the decision process. I can't."*
- *"Junior staff use AI tools I don't fully understand. I'm accountable for the output."*
- *"How do I document the AI's role in the analysis without making it sound like I outsourced the thinking?"*
- *"The client wants to know: was it AI-generated, AI-assisted, or AI-reviewed? These are different. I treat them the same."*

**Failure mode:** Methodology becomes indefensible when challenged. The ICP either over-discloses (sounds like they outsourced judgment) or under-discloses (sounds evasive). Neither position is comfortable.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva consulting boutiques often win mandates on intellectual authority and personal judgment. If the methodology is perceived as AI-mediated, the value proposition shifts. This creates resistance to transparency about AI use — a dangerous posture given client expectations.

---

## 4. Sub-Consultant AI Delegation — The Hidden Accountability Chain

**Signal:** Small consulting boutiques regularly engage sub-consultants for specialized work. The proliferation of AI tools among sub-consultants creates an undocumented delegation chain.

**What the ICP reports:**
- *"I bring in a specialist for a specific workstream. They use AI tools. Am I responsible?"*
- *"My client holds me accountable for the deliverable. They don't care who or what produced it."*
- *"I can't audit every sub-consultant's tool stack and AI usage."*
- *"The delegation chain — who documents what — nobody's defined this."*
- *"If the sub-consultant's AI use creates a data protection issue, does that flow back to me?"*

**Failure mode:** The ICP has no delegation documentation framework. When clients ask about AI governance in the supply chain, the ICP either doesn't know or provides vague reassurances. This is increasingly insufficient.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva's concentration of specialized boutique consultants means sub-consultant relationships are common and often cross-border. A Geneva boutique may engage a specialist in Zurich, Paris, or London. The AI governance question becomes an international supply chain question.

---

## 5. Personal Liability Under nFADP Article 63 — The Wake-Up Call

**Signal:** Article 63 of Switzerland's nFADP creates personal liability for the designated "responsible person." For small consulting boutiques, this is almost always the managing director or partner.

**What the ICP reports:**
- *"I am the responsible person. What does that actually mean for me personally?"*
- *"My firm's data protection obligations — where does AI fit in?"*
- *"We process sensitive client data. If an AI vendor mishandles it, am I personally exposed?"*
- *"Article 63 — is this like GDPR's Article 82? What's the exposure?"*
- *"Personal assets — what are we actually talking about here?"*

**Failure mode:** The ICP discovers the personal liability dimension only when something goes wrong — a vendor data incident, a client complaint, a regulatory inquiry. At that point, documentation gaps are already present.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva consulting boutiques handle highly sensitive client information — business strategy, M&A target profiles, organizational restructuring data, investor information. This is among the most sensitive categories of personal and commercial data. The exposure is not theoretical.

---

## 6. The Geneva Professional Standard — Expectation Gap

**Signal:** Geneva functions as a quality threshold marker. Clients and peers expect Geneva-based professionals to operate at a certain standard. AI governance is becoming a visible dimension of that standard.

**What the ICP reports:**
- *"Geneva standard — what does that mean for AI governance specifically?"*
- *"My peer firms — are they further ahead than me? I have no way to know."*
- *"ODAGE event acknowledged the problem. Nobody provided a solution I could actually implement."*
- *"I went to a conference. Everyone agreed it's a problem. Nobody showed me a tool."*
- *"The frameworks I've seen are for large enterprises. I'm a 12-person firm."*

**Failure mode:** The ICP experiences isolation — they know there's a gap, they attend events that confirm the gap, but no one provides an operationally accessible solution. This generates both anxiety and skepticism about vendors.

**The Geneva-specific dimension:** Geneva professional culture values discretion and competence. Admitting uncertainty publicly is uncomfortable. The ICP often experiences the gap privately before seeking help — which means by the time they engage, the urgency is already high.

---

## 7. Competitive Disqualification Risk

**Signal:** AI governance is becoming a pre-qualification criterion, not just a differentiator.

**What the ICP reports:**
- *"I lost a mandate last quarter. The client said my AI governance documentation was insufficient."*
- *"The RFP now scores AI governance. I didn't know that was a scored criterion."*
- *"My client's compliance team is asking questions I can't answer. They're escalating to my peer firms."*
- *"A competitor with better documentation won a mandate I was shortlisted for."*

**Failure mode:** The ICP loses competitive positioning not on expertise but on documentation posture. This is experienced as deeply unfair — they have the expertise, they just can't prove it in the required format.

---

## 8. Documentation Debt — The Structural Accumulation

**Signal:** Documentation debt is universal and structural. Every client engagement, every AI tool adoption, every sub-consultant engagement adds to the debt. The ICP is not ignoring the problem — they are drowning in it.

**What the ICP reports:**
- *"Where do I even start? We have five years of accumulated AI use."*
- *"Every time I think we're getting on top of it, there's another tool adoption."*
- *"The documentation I need to produce — it doesn't exist in any system I have."*
- *"I know what I should be doing. I don't have the system to do it."*
- *"Junior staff adopt tools I don't know about. By the time I hear about them, there's already no record."*

**Failure mode:** The ICP either ignores the accumulating debt (until a triggering event) or attempts a retroactive reconstruction that fails structurally. Both paths lead to the same exposure.
