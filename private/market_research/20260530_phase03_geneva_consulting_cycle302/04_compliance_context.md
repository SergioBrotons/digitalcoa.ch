# 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Switzerland Regulatory Landscape

## Phase 03 — C302 | Geneva Consulting (2–50 employees)

---

## 1. Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) — The Core Obligation

**Status:** In force since2023, with ongoing interpretation development

**Key Articles for Geneva Consulting ICP:**

### Article 63 — Personal Liability (Critical)
- Creates personal liability for the designated "responsible person"
- For small consulting firms, this is almost always the managing director or partner
- Personal liability means personal assets can be at risk in case of breach
- The "responsible person" designation is not optional — it is a legal requirement
- **What it means in practice:** If an AI vendor used by the firm mishandles personal data, the responsible person can face personal liability — not just the firm
- **Geneva consulting dimension:** These firms process highly sensitive client data — business strategy, M&A information, organizational restructuring, investor data. This is among the most sensitive personal data categories.

### Article 51 — Automated Decision Processing
- Requires transparency when automated systems make or materially influence decisions
- Applies to AI systems used in client service delivery
- Documentation requirements: what decision, what data, what logic, what human oversight
- **Geneva consulting dimension:** Consulting recommendations are increasingly AI-assisted or AI-generated. The line between "assisted" and "materially influenced" is unclear. Documentation is the safe harbor.

### Article 6 — Lawfulness of Processing
- Personal data may only be processed lawfully
- AI tool use = processing personal data (client information processed through AI vendor systems)
- Requires legal basis for each processing activity
- **Geneva consulting dimension:** Most AI tools used by consulting firms process client data through third-party servers. The legal basis for this processing must be established and documented.

### Article 12 — Transparency Obligations
- Data subjects (clients) have the right to know when AI is used in processing their data
- Consent or legitimate interest must be documented
- **Geneva consulting dimension:** Clients increasingly ask directly. Silence or vague disclosure is not compliant.

## 3. Insurance Carrier Requirements — Professional Liability

### Carrier Form v4 Evidence Standard
- Shifted from attestation to evidence-based documentation
- "Last three authorization records" — active requirement
- **Geneva consulting dimension:** Professional liability insurance is mandatory for many consulting practices. Carrier requirements function as de facto practice standards.

### What Carriers Are Now Asking For:
- Evidence of AI tool authorization before use
- Records of which AI systems were used in client engagements
- Documentation of human oversight in AI-assisted decisions
- Decision attribution logs — which AI influenced which client deliverable
- Sub-consultant AI delegation documentation

### The Carrier as De Facto Enforcement Agent
- Carriers are not just insurance providers — they are information collectors
- The data carriers collect becomes evidence in regulatory proceedings
- Attestation without evidence = false documentation = additional liability
- **Critical insight:** The carrier form is not a compliance exercise — it is a legal document with personal liability implications

---

## 4. Client AI Governance Questionnaires — The Compliance Multiplier

### Institutional Client Due Diligence
- Corporate clients, family offices, endowments, institutional investors
- AI governance as scored RFP criterion — not just a checkbox
- 2–4 week response deadlines
- **Geneva dimension:** Geneva institutional clients (banks, pharma, commodity trading) are among the most sophisticated in AI governance globally. They set high standards for vendor compliance.

### Common Questionnaire Elements:
- AI tool inventory (what tools, what purposes)
- Authorization records (before use)
- Decision attribution documentation
- Human oversight procedures
- Sub-consultant AI governance
- Data incident history
- Evidence of compliance with Swiss law

### Compliance Gap:
- The ICP has no system to respond comprehensively
- Partial responses are visible — they signal insufficient documentation
- **The documentation debt becomes a competitive liability**

## 5. EU AI Act — Timeline and Geneva Impact

**Status:** Entering force August 2026 (~14 months away)

**Geneva consulting implications:**
- Switzerland is not EU, but Geneva consulting firms serve EU clients
- EU-based clients will demand EU AI Act compliance from vendors
- The EU AI Act creates a de facto standard even for non-EU Swiss firms
- High-risk AI system use in professional services will require conformity assessment
- **Timeline:** ~14 months to prepare — not urgent, but must be on the planning horizon

**Geneva-specific consideration:**
- Many Geneva consulting firms have EU client bases
- EU AI Act compliance will become a client questionnaire topic
- The documentation standard required by EU AI Act is higher than current Swiss requirements
- First-mover advantage: firms that document to EU AI Act standard now will be ahead when enforcement begins

- The consultation draft will clarify Switzerland's AI governance direction

## 7. Cross-Border Dimensions — Geneva Specific

### Sub-Consultant Network (International)
- Geneva consulting firms regularly engage sub-consultants across borders
- Cross-border AI tool use = cross-border data processing
- EU AI Act, Swiss nFADP, and potentially other national laws may apply simultaneously
- **The delegation chain becomes an international compliance question**

### Data Residency Considerations
- Client data processed through AI tools may be stored internationally
- Swiss data protection law expectations may conflict with US-based AI vendor data practices
- Documentation of data flows is increasingly required by clients and carriers

## 9. The Documentation Imperative

**The compliance environment is not theoretical — it is operational.**

The Geneva consulting ICP faces a stack of overlapping compliance requirements:
1. **Client questionnaires** (immediate, economic consequence)
2. **Carrier attestation** (immediate, personal liability consequence)
3. **nFADP Article 63** (structural, personal asset consequence)
4. **EU AI Act** (emerging, client mandate consequence)
5. **Professional standard** (psychological, reputation consequence)

**The common thread:** Every compliance requirement requires documentation the ICP does not have and cannot retroactively create.

**The solution must be:**
- Swiss-specific (nFADP mapped)
- Carrier form v4 compatible
- Client questionnaire responsive
- Personally relevant (Art. 63 translated to action)
- Operationally accessible for 2–50 person firm
- Geneva professional standard aligned
