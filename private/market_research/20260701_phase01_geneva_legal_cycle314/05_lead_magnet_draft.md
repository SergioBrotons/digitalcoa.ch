**What Changed**

The EU AI Act goes live in late August 2026. That's roughly thirty days from now. It is the first external regulatory instrument that defines AI governance for legal services as a high-risk use case. It applies extraterritorially. It defines "deployer" by use, not by location.

A Geneva firm using AI to draft a contract for an EU corporate client, to advise an EU individual on cross-border inheritance, or to research EU case law for a litigation matter — is a deployer under the EU AI Act. The Act's obligations apply.

My client didn't know this. The Geneva managing partner on the other end of that email didn't know this. Most Geneva boutique law firms don't know this.

The question isn't whether the EU AI Act applies to Geneva firms. The question is whether Geneva firms will be ready when their EU clients ask — and EU clients are now asking.

**The Email That Changed the Conversation**

The email was brief. Standard inquiry from an EU corporate client's compliance team.

*"We are reviewing our external counsel panel for EU AI Act readiness. Please confirm whether your firm has documented compliance with Articles 4, 14, and 50 of the EU AI Act, and whether you maintain a risk management system under Article 9 and logging under Article 12 for AI-assisted legal work on our matters."*

Six references to specific EU AI Act articles. Zero ambiguity. The EU client is doing its own EU AI Act compliance review. The Geneva firm is on the list. The Geneva firm cannot answer.
