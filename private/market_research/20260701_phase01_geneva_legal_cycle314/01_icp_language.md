### The Language They Use (C314 — July 2026 register)

**"We have one client matter that crosses into France. Does that mean the EU AI Act applies to my firm?"**
Cross-border applicability uncertainty. The Geneva firm with even a single EU-jurisdiction matter is asking whether the EU AI Act's high-risk provisions apply. The Article 6(3) ancillary exception is the legal test. The test is not well-formed for boutique legal services. The firm's entire AI governance architecture is conditional on the answer. The answer is conditional on facts the firm does not currently document.

**"The EU AI Act requires logging. Article 321 prohibits logging. Which one wins?"**
Article 321 / EU AI Act logging conflict. The Geneva firm that recognizes both regimes is discovering they issue contradictory instructions for the same data flow. Article 321 prohibits the retention of attorney-client communication metadata (it constitutes professional secrecy). EU AI Act Article 12 requires logging of high-risk AI system operations. The firm cannot simultaneously prohibit and require the same logging. The conflict is unnamed in vendor contracts, unaddressed by bar guidance, and unreconciled by carrier attestations. The managing partner who has noticed this conflict has no service to call. The managing partner who has not noticed is signing attestations that may be impossible to satisfy.

**"If I used AI to draft a brief and the AI fabricated a citation, am I sanctioned personally?"**
AI hallucination personal liability. Court sanctions for AI-fabricated citations are now documented in multiple jurisdictions — including the most-cited 2023 U.S. case (Mata v. Avianca), subsequent U.K. and Australian cases, and emerging Swiss doctrine through cantonal bar discipline. The Geneva attorney who submits AI-drafted content without verifying every citation faces personal liability under three independent regimes: bar discipline (failure to supervise), carrier malpractice (failure to verify work product), and client malpractice (delivering fabricated authority). The C314 ICP does not currently have a verification protocol. The verification protocol is not in their authorization documentation. The carrier attestation asks whether they have one. They don't.

**"Did I waive privilege by feeding the client's email into the AI?"**
Privilege waiver through AI ingestion. The act of feeding client content into a third-party AI vendor may constitute third-party disclosure — destroying attorney-client privilege. The vendor's terms of service may include a license to retain, review, or use the content. The vendor's sub-processors may include human reviewers (for quality assurance, abuse detection, fine-tuning). The act of typing client content into the AI tool interface is the moment of disclosure. The Geneva attorney who has been feeding client emails into AI tools to draft responses has potentially waived privilege on every matter they touched. The exposure is not theoretical. The exposure accumulates with every AI prompt.

**"Is my AI tool trained on other firms' privileged content?"**
Cross-matter contamination via AI training data. Legal AI tools (Harvey, Thomson Reuters CoCounsel, Lexis+ AI, Westlaw AI, Spellbook) are trained on legal corpora. Those corpora include content from other firms' privileged matters. The AI outputs blend training data across firms. The Geneva firm using these tools is exposed to AI outputs that contain semantic residue from other firms' work product. The contamination is not visible to the user. The contamination is documented in model behavior research. The carrier has not yet asked. The bar has not yet noticed. The EU AI Act requires training data documentation for high-risk systems. The boutique vendor the firm is using does not provide that documentation. The contamination is the firm's exposure without the firm knowing.

**"My bar guidance, my carrier attestation, and the EU AI Act don't say the same things. What do I do?"**
Three-regime reconciliation failure. The Geneva boutique now operates under three separate regulatory regimes: bar (Ordre des avocats de Genève — OaF and FSA cantonal directives), carrier (professional liability attestation), and EU AI Act (when applicable). None of the three regimes speak to each other. Each imposes obligations on AI use that do not align with the others. The bar requires supervision. The carrier requires documentation. The EU AI Act requires risk management, logging, transparency, human oversight, accuracy, robustness, cybersecurity. The managing partner must reconcile these regimes manually. No current service delivers the three-regime reconciliation memo. The first service to publish it owns the category.
