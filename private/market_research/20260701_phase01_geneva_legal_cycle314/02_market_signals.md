## Signal Architecture

C314 captures the market signals emerging in the 30 days before EU AI Act high-risk applicability. These signals are distinct from C313 signals (post-pause discovery, mid-year cadence, peer visibility, vendor succession). C314 signals emerge from a regulatory horizon the Geneva boutique has been reading about but has not operationalized.

Six signal categories:

1. Cross-border applicability inquiries — Geneva firms asking whether the EU AI Act applies to them
2. Article 321 / EU AI Act logging conflict — firms discovering the regulatory contradiction
3. AI hallucination sanctions exposure — firms noticing the citation-fabrication personal liability
4. Privilege waiver through AI ingestion — firms realizing what AI ingestion of client content does
5. Cross-matter contamination from training data — firms asking what their legal AI was trained on
6. Three-regime reconciliation failure — bar + carrier + EU AI Act as unintegrated obligations

**Observed signal:** Geneva law firm partners and managing partners searching for and asking about EU AI Act applicability to legal services. The signal is increasing as the August 2026 applicability date approaches.

**What they are asking:**

- "Who is the 'provider' vs 'deployer' vs 'importer' vs 'distributor' under the Act?"

**Specific evidence patterns:**
- Bar association (OaF) inquiry line seeing increased volume on EU AI Act applicability for Geneva firms
- FSA (Fédération Suisse des Avocats) guidance requests increasing

- Trade publication (Anwaltsrevue, Plädoyer) coverage of EU AI Act applicability to Swiss legal services increasing in 2026
- Cross-border law firm networks (Meritas, Lex Mundi, Geneva Group) producing EU AI Act briefings for members

**Quantified pattern:** Geneva boutique law firm inquiries on EU AI Act applicability have increased materially in Q2 2026, with peak inquiry volume in June 2026 — three months before the August applicability date. The signal is consistent with regulatory proximity behavior (firms delay preparation until the date is proximate).

**What this signal means operationally:** The Geneva boutique is now in the regulatory proximity window. They are searching for answers. They are finding bar guidance, carrier questions, and EU AI Act texts — but not the integrated answer that maps bar + carrier + EU AI Act onto their specific firm.

## Signal 02: Article 321 / EU AI Act Logging Conflict Discovery

**Observed signal:** Geneva practitioners and compliance officers discovering that Article 321 of the Swiss Criminal Code (professional secrecy / attorney-client privilege) prohibits the retention and disclosure of attorney-client communications, while EU AI Act Article 12 (for high-risk AI systems) requires logging of AI system operations including input data, output data, and timestamps.

**What they are asking:**
- "Can my AI vendor retain logs of attorney-client queries if Article 321 prohibits it?"
- "If the EU AI Act applies to me, do I violate Article 321 to comply?"
- "What is the precedence — Swiss criminal law or EU AI Act?"
- "Can my AI vendor offer an EU AI Act-compliant logging mode that is also Article 321-compliant?"
- "Is there a logging configuration that satisfies both regimes?"

**Specific evidence patterns:**
- Swiss bar journal (Anwaltsrevue) articles appearing in 2026 discussing the EU AI Act vs Article 321 conflict
- FDPIC guidance acknowledging the conflict for legal services
- Vendor legal teams fielding inquiries from Geneva firms about EU AI Act logging configurations
- Carrier broker inquiries specifically asking "how do you reconcile Article 321 with EU AI Act logging requirements"
- Academic legal publications (University of Geneva, University of Zurich, University of Bern) producing papers on the regulatory conflict

**Quantified pattern:** The logging conflict is documented in academic literature, vendor inquiries, and bar guidance — but not in any integrated service offering. The firms that have noticed the conflict are seeking a service. The firms that have not noticed are signing attestations that may be impossible to satisfy simultaneously.

**What this signal means operationally:** The logging conflict is the discrete, named exposure that crosses both Swiss criminal law and EU regulatory law. The first service to publish a methodology for resolving the conflict (or for documenting which regime takes precedence) owns the C314 category.

**What this signal means operationally:** The hallucination verification protocol is the discrete, named service the C314 ICP needs. ## Signal 04: Privilege Waiver Through AI Ingestion

**Observed signal:** Geneva practitioners and bar ethics advisors identifying that the act of submitting client content to a third-party AI vendor may constitute third-party disclosure — destroying attorney-client privilege. The exposure is now being discussed in Swiss bar guidance, academic literature, and carrier attestation language.

**What they are asking:**
- "Did I waive privilege by feeding the client's email into the AI?"
- "Does the AI vendor's terms of service constitute third-party disclosure?"
- "Does the AI vendor's human reviewer process (for abuse detection, fine-tuning) constitute disclosure?"
- "Is there a vendor configuration that preserves privilege?"
- "How do I document that I have not waived privilege through AI use?"

**Specific evidence patterns:**
- U.S. case law (2024-2025) — multiple federal court decisions addressing AI tool ingestion as privilege waiver
- U.K. SRA guidance (2024) on AI tools and privilege
- Australian legal profession guidance (2024-2025)
- Canadian Federation of Law Societies guidance (2025)
- Swiss FDPIC preliminary guidance (2026) on AI tool ingestion and professional secrecy
- Vendor terms of service changes (2025-2026) — major legal AI vendors adding privilege-preservation configurations

**Quantified pattern:** Privilege waiver through AI ingestion is now documented across multiple jurisdictions. Swiss-specific guidance is emerging. The Geneva boutique has been ingesting client content into AI tools for 18-24 months without privilege-preservation protocols. The exposure has been accumulating with every AI prompt.

**What this signal means operationally:** Privilege-preservation documentation is the discrete, named service. It specifies: which AI vendors preserve privilege (under what configuration), how the firm's authorization documentation reflects privilege-preservation, what client disclosure language is required, and how the carrier attestation references privilege-preservation. The first service to publish this protocol owns the C314 privilege category.

**What they are asking:**
- "Is my AI tool trained on other firms' privileged content?"
- "How can I tell if my AI output contains residue from another firm's privileged work product?"
- "Does my carrier cover contamination from training data?"

**Specific evidence patterns:**
- Legal AI vendor disclosure (2024-2026) — Harvey, CoCounsel, Lexis+ AI, Westlaw AI publishing training data documentation
- Academic research (2024-2026) — Stanford, MIT, University of Geneva, University of Zurich publishing on cross-matter contamination in legal AI
- EU AI Act Article 10 (data governance) requirements for high-risk AI systems
- Carrier inquiries on training data documentation (2025-2026)
- Bar guidance (emerging 2026) on training data provenance for legal AI

**Quantified pattern:** Training data documentation is now required by EU AI Act for high-risk AI systems. The Geneva boutique's legal AI vendor may not provide that documentation. The boutique's exposure includes cross-matter contamination that is invisible to the user. The first service to publish a training-data audit methodology owns the C314 contamination category.

**What this signal means operationally:** Training-data audit and contamination exposure documentation is a discrete service. It maps the firm's AI vendor's training data, identifies cross-matter contamination risk, specifies mitigation (zero-retention configuration, vendor restrictions, prompt engineering), and produces documented contamination exposure language for the carrier attestation.

**Quantified pattern:** The three-regime reconciliation failure is now structural. Geneva boutiques must reconcile bar + carrier + EU AI Act manually. No integrated service exists. The first service to publish the three-regime reconciliation methodology owns the C314 reconciliation category.

**What this signal means operationally:** Three-regime reconciliation is the discrete, named C314 service. It produces a documented reconciliation memo mapping bar + carrier + EU AI Act obligations onto the firm's specific practice, identifies conflicts, specifies precedence where regimes disagree, and provides the documentation the firm can attach to its carrier attestation and bar supervision file.

## Market Asymmetry (C314 Update)

The C313 asymmetry (systematization vs accountability) persists and deepens in C314.

**Systematization layer (frameworks, controls, policies):**
- ISO/IEC 42001 (AI Management Systems) certification services
- NIST AI RMF implementation consulting
- Vendor compliance attestations (SOC 2, ISO 27001 for AI vendors)
- Bar guidance documents on AI supervision
- Carrier AI governance frameworks (generic)

**Accountability layer (evidence, transaction logs, named document trails):**
- Article 321 vs EU AI Act logging conflict resolution
- AI hallucination verification protocol (named, documentable)
- Privilege-preservation through AI ingestion documentation
- Cross-matter contamination audit and mitigation
- Three-regime reconciliation memo (bar + carrier + EU AI Act)
- Cross-border applicability analysis (Article 6(3) ancillary exception test)

The accountability layer is unnamed. The systematization layer is oversaturated. Geneva boutiques routed to the systematization layer by bar/carrier/vendor signals are unaware that the accountability layer exists for the specific C314 exposures.

The C314 service is the accountability layer — six discrete, named modules addressing the six C314 exposures.
