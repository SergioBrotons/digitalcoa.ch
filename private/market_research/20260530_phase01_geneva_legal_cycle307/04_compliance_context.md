# Compliance Context: Geneva Legal — C307 Phase 01

## Geneva/Switzerland Specific Regulatory — C307

---

## Regulatory Landscape Overview

### Primary Regulatory Framework (C307)

**1. Article 321 CC — Professional Secrecy (Swiss Criminal Code)**
- Article 321 CC criminalizes disclosure of confidential information obtained in professional capacity
- Applies to attorneys, physicians, clergy, and designated professionals
- AI vendor access to client information processed through AI tools may constitute "disclosure" under Article 321 CC
- Vendor contracts that grant data access for model training or system improvement create potential Article 321 CC exposure
- No Geneva-specific guidance on AI vendor Article 321 CC compliance published by OFL or FDPIC

**2. nFADP (Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection) — Active Obligation**
- In force since 2014, revised nFADP in force September 2023
- Applies to any processing of personal data of Swiss residents
- AI tool use in client matters = processing personal data under nFADP
- Data Processing Agreements required with AI vendors processing client personal data
- DPA review obligation: every AI vendor in use requires a compliant DPA assessment
- FDPIC enforcement: FDPIC building AI enforcement portfolio across sectors; law firms not yet targeted but gap is timing signal, not exemption

**3. EU AI Act — Nexus-Based Exposure**
- EU AI Act enters into force August 2, 2026 (published in EU Official Journal)
- Applies to AI systems placed in service or used in the EU, or whose outputs are used in the EU
- Geneva firms with EU client matters or EU-intended AI outputs have EU AI Act nexus
- General-purpose AI (GPAI) providers: new obligations for foundation model providers cascade to users
- No Geneva-specific EU AI Act guidance for small law firms published by OFL or cantonal bar

**4. Ordre des Avocats de Genève (OFL) — No AI Guidance Published**
- OFL has not published AI governance guidance for Geneva attorneys
- No FAQ, no template, no training requirement, no advisory opinion on AI use
- OFL disciplinary framework applies to AI use but no specific AI-related disciplinary standard defined
- The guidance vacuum is a structural market opportunity — no official anchor exists for Geneva attorneys

**5. Carrier Attestation Forms — Insurance Carrier AI Governance Requirements**
- Carrier attestation forms require managing partner personal signature
- Forms commit the signatory to undefined "adequate" AI governance standards
- Evidence requirements: carriers asking for documentation of actual AI use, not policy statements
- The personal signature creates personal liability exposure — not firm liability, managing partner liability
- Broker facilitation of signature: broker presents form as routine, does not explain personal liability implications

- No law firm enforcement case found — gap is timing signal, not exemption

- Geneva firms with EU client matters: EU AI Act creates specific documentation requirements
- No Geneva-specific EU AI Act implementation guidance for small law firms — guidance vacuum continues

### Federal Council AI Action Plan (C307 Update)

## Multi-Authority Documentation Requirements (C307)

### Authority 1: Insurance Carrier — Attestation Evidence
- **What they want:** Evidence of actual AI governance practice — tool inventories, use logs, supervision documentation
- **What they ask for:** Documentation of what the firm actually did, not what the policy says should happen
- **Form:** Self-reported attestation with carrier right to request supporting documentation at renewal
- **Documentation type:** Evidence architecture (records of actual use)

### Authority 2: Ordre des Avocats de Genève (OFL) — Disciplinary Documentation
- **What they want:** Demonstration of professional supervision and competence maintenance
- **What they ask for:** Documentation of actual supervision process for AI-assisted work
- **Form:** Disciplinary proceedings if client complaint or bar investigation initiated
- **Documentation type:** Supervision framework documentation

### Authority 3: FDPIC — Data Processing Compliance
- **What they want:** Evidence of nFADP compliance for AI processing of client personal data
- **What they ask for:** Data Processing Agreement compliance, data subject rights implementation
- **Form:** FDPIC investigation triggered by complaint or sectoral audit
- **Documentation type:** nFADP compliance documentation for AI vendor relationships

### Authority 4: Cantonal Court — Litigation Discovery
- **What they want:** Disclosure of AI use in specific client matters in litigation
- **What they ask for:** Matter-specific AI use disclosure, potentially including vendor identities and data processed
- **Form:** Discovery request or mandatory disclosure in civil proceedings
- **Documentation type:** Matter-level AI use documentation

### Authority 5: FINMA — Financial Services Matters
- **What they want:** AI governance documentation for firms handling financial services matters
- **What they ask for:** Documentation of AI use in FINMA-regulated contexts
- **Form:** FINMA audit or investigation
- **Documentation type:** AI governance documentation for regulated financial services context

## Documentation Cross-Mapping Problem (C307)

### The Core Problem
Each authority asks for different documentation in different forms for different purposes. A documentation system built to satisfy one authority may:
- Create a record that could be used against the firm in another authority's proceeding
- Not satisfy another authority's specific documentation requirements
- Be structured in a way that waives privilege when disclosed to the wrong authority

### Cross-Authority Documentation Design Requirements
- **Carrier attestation:** Requires documented evidence of actual AI use across all matters
- **Ordre disciplinary:** Requires documented supervision process with privilege protection
- **FDPIC nFADP:** Requires DPA compliance documentation for each AI vendor
- **Cantonal court discovery:** Requires matter-level AI use disclosure, potentially privileged
- **FINMA:** Requires AI governance documentation in financial services context

### The Missing Service
No Geneva service maps which documentation simultaneously satisfies multiple authority demands. The managing partner needs one evidence system that protects across carrier, Ordre, FDPIC, cantonal court, and FINMA — without creating cross-exposure between authorities.

## Geneva-Specific Professional Context (C307)

### Geneva Bar (OFL) — Structural Guidance Vacuum
- OFL has not published AI governance guidance — confirmed across all cycles
- No FAQ, no template, no training requirement, no advisory opinion on AI use
- The guidance vacuum means: no official standard exists to point to, no official answer to "what does adequate look like for Geneva attorneys"
- Managing partners are navigating this vacuum without an official anchor

### Canton of Geneva Professional Environment
- Geneva is the largest legal market in Switzerland by attorney count
- Significant bilingual (French/English) professional client base
- EU nexus: Geneva firms frequently handle EU client matters or EU-intended AI outputs
- International Geneva: significant expat/English-speaking client population with higher AI risk awareness
- Small firm structure: 2–5 attorney firms represent majority of Geneva bar practice

### Personal Liability Context
- Geneva attorneys: personal liability exposure for professional conduct
- Carrier attestation signature: personal signature, not firm signature
- Article 321 CC: personal criminal exposure for privilege breach
- Ordre disciplinary: personal professional license exposure
- The managing partner's personal liability is the operative exposure — firm liability structures do not eliminate personal exposure

## C307 Compliance Context Summary

**Active regulatory obligations:**
1. Article 321 CC — professional secrecy, AI vendor access creates potential breach exposure
2. nFADP — active, AI tool use in client matters = data processing requiring DPA compliance
3. EU AI Act — nexus-based, August 2, 2026 enforcement start, EU client matters create exposure
4. OFL — no AI guidance published, guidance vacuum creates uncertainty
5. Carrier attestation — personal signature required, undefined "adequate" standard, evidence requirements

**Authority documentation requirements:**
- Carrier: evidence of actual AI use (records, not policies)
- Ordre: supervision process documentation (privilege-protected)
- FDPIC: nFADP DPA compliance documentation
- Cantonal court: matter-level AI use disclosure
- FINMA: AI governance in financial services context

**Key regulatory gap:** No cross-authority documentation mapping service exists for Geneva small firms. The documentation architecture that satisfies all relevant authorities simultaneously — without creating cross-exposure — is the missing service.
