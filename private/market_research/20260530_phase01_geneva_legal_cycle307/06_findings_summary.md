# Findings Summary: Geneva Legal — C307 Phase 01

## 6 NET-NEW Findings — Distinct from All Prior Cycles C214–C306

### Finding 01: The Accountability Inversion Creates a Structural Supervision Gap with Personal Liability Consequences

**The finding:** The person with personal liability (the managing partner) has less knowledge about the AI tools in use than the people whose work they supervise (associates, assistants). This is not a training gap — it is a structural governance problem. The managing partner cannot evaluate AI output substantively, cannot monitor every AI use decision, but is personally accountable for every output that bears their signature. The delegation of task and the delegation of accountability do not align — and no supervision framework exists in the market for small Geneva firms that addresses this specific misalignment.

**Mechanism:** Associates bring AI tools → managing partner cannot evaluate AI output → managing partner signs off on AI-assisted work without substantive review → personal liability for output they cannot evaluate → no supervision framework exists to close this gap.

**Implication:** AI supervision architecture design is a discrete service — distinct from training, distinct from policy templates, distinct from general AI governance frameworks. The output is a documented supervision framework that defines delegation limits, review requirements, documentation obligations, and error-correction procedures for a 2–5 attorney firm operating with resource constraints.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You can't stop delegating to AI. You can't evaluate every output yourself. But you can design a supervision architecture that makes your delegation defensible — and keeps the liability from falling entirely on you."

### Finding 02: Attorney-Client Privilege Exposure via AI Vendor Access is Structural, Not Theoretical

**The finding:** Standard AI vendor contracts — Data Processing Agreements, Terms of Service — grant vendors access to submitted data for model training, system improvement, or maintenance purposes. For attorneys processing privileged client communications through AI tools, this contract language creates a potential privilege waiver event: allowing a third-party vendor to access privileged communications may constitute disclosure under Article 321 CC. No Geneva small firm has evaluated this exposure for their specific vendor stack. The exposure is structural — embedded in the vendor contracts — not hypothetical.

**Mechanism:** Vendor contract accepted without review → vendor has access to client communications processed through AI tool → privilege waiver may have occurred → FDPIC investigation or client complaint triggers disclosure of vendor relationship → exposure confirmed retroactively.

**Implication:** Vendor contract privilege review is a discrete bounded service. The output is a privilege exposure assessment for each AI vendor in the firm's stack, with specific recommendations for contract modification or vendor replacement where privilege exposure is unmanageable. This is distinct from general vendor due diligence — the privilege angle is specific to the legal profession's Article 321 CC obligations.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The vendor contract you didn't read may have created a privilege exposure without your knowledge. I can review every AI vendor contract in your stack against your Article 321 CC obligations and tell you which ones create exposure — and what to do about it."

### Finding 03: The Carrier-Ordo Documentation Conflict Creates Paralysis, Not Compliance

**The finding:** Managing partners who build documentation in response to carrier attestation requests are encountering a specific problem: the documentation they build for the carrier creates a documented record. If that record is incomplete or inconsistent with actual practice, it becomes a liability in an Ordre disciplinary proceeding. Managing partners are caught between: build documentation (and create a record that could be used against you) vs. build no documentation (and have nothing to show). Neither option feels safe. The documentation conflict is creating decision paralysis — not compliance action.

**Mechanism:** Documentation built for carrier → carrier asks for evidence → documentation presented → creates record → Ordre disciplinary proceeding initiated → record used against firm → documentation intended to satisfy carrier created Ordre exposure.

**Implication:** Cross-authority documentation architecture is a discrete service. The key is documentation sequencing and privilege structuring: which documents are created, which are held privileged, which are disclosed to which authority, and in what order. The service maps which records satisfy which authority's demand and designs a single documentation system that works across all of them without creating cross-exposure.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You're caught between the carrier wanting your records and the Ordre potentially using those same records. I can design the documentation so that the carrier gets what it needs — and the Ordre gets what it needs — without one making the other worse."

### Finding 04: Bilingual Client Communication Bifurcation Creates Separate Exposure Moments per Language

**The finding:** Geneva small firms serve both French-speaking and English-speaking clients. The two populations have different implicit expectations about AI governance disclosure. French clients assume the attorney knows the local Geneva standard and do not probe AI questions directly. English-speaking clients — often more informed about AI risks from international media — ask specific questions about data use, AI involvement in their case, and the firm's AI governance policy. The firm cannot give a consistent answer in either language because they have no defined AI governance standard to communicate, and the bilingual communication burden is not addressed by any available service.

**Mechanism:** Client intake → French client expects Geneva-standard disclosure → no Geneva-defined standard exists → vague answer given → French client accepts vague answer → English client asks specific AI questions → no calibrated response exists → exposure in both linguistic contexts without documentation in either.

**Implication:** Bilingual client-facing AI governance communication is a discrete service. Engagement letter clauses, intake disclosures, client communication templates — all calibrated for Geneva's specific bilingual context with different implicit client expectations in each language. Not translation — culturally adapted communication that addresses the different risk awareness levels and disclosure expectations in each linguistic context.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your French clients assume you know the Geneva standard. Your English clients expect you to have a policy. Neither has been defined, and you don't have language for either. I can give you both — in both languages."

### Finding 05: The Multi-Standard Confusion is an Operational Friction That Accelerates Client Relationship Risk

**The finding:** Geneva small firms are not just serving French and English clients — they are operating in a multi-standard regulatory environment where the applicable standard is undefined and varies by context. nFADP applies to all AI processing of Swiss resident personal data. EU AI Act applies to EU client matters or EU-intended outputs. OFL has not defined what "adequate" AI governance means for Geneva attorneys. The managing partner is operating without a defined standard in any context — and each client's implicit expectation of what the standard should be is different. The multi-standard confusion is not just an internal compliance problem — it is an external client relationship problem that compounds with every client interaction.

**Mechanism:** No Geneva-defined AI governance standard → French clients assume local standard (undefined) → English clients expect international standard (undefined) → managing partner cannot communicate consistent standard → client relationship friction → client questions unanswered → exposure in client relationship and regulatory context simultaneously.

**Implication:** Standard definition is a prerequisite to communication. The service must first help the firm define what their AI governance standard is (given their specific tool use, client base, and regulatory exposure) — then help them communicate it in both languages. The standard definition and the communication framework are sequential, not simultaneous.

**Ready-to-use language:** "There's no Geneva-defined AI governance standard. Your French clients assume you know it. Your English clients expect you to have one. The multi-standard confusion is eating away at client relationships — and you can't communicate a standard you haven't defined."

### Finding 06: The Active Exposure Recognition is the Decisive Trigger — The ICP Now Knows They Need to Act, Not Just Worry

**The finding:** The Geneva legal ICP has moved from "am I exposed?" to "what do I do now?" This is the decisive trigger for service adoption. The managing partner has encountered: the carrier attestation signature, the realization that the signature was personal, the discovery that AI tools are in use without documentation, the growing client questions about AI, and the growing awareness that the Ordre has not provided guidance — and that the absence of guidance is not the same as permission. The ICP now knows the problem is real. The question is what to do next, in what order, and with whom. The service that converts this intent into structured action gets the engagement.

**Mechanism:** Carrier signature → personal liability recognized → tool sprawl identified → privilege exposure suspected → multi-regulator uncertainty understood → Ordre silence noted → active exposure confirmed → decision to act made → service adoption triggered.

**Implication:** The lead magnet and the briefing session are positioned for exactly this state. The ICP who arrives at the briefing is already past the decision to seek help. The service's job is to convert that intent into a structured engagement — by demonstrating that the problem is understood, that the approach is specific to their situation, and that the path forward is actionable. The call comes when they realize they need to act — not when they learn about the problem.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You've already figured out that this is real. You signed something. You know the tools are in use. You know the gap is there. Now the question is what to do about it — and in what order. That's what I do."
