## Confirmed Competitive Landscape (C307)

### Large Firm Advisors (Not Competitors for ICP)
- **CMS Switzerland:** Large firm, enterprise pricing, international framework — not for2–5 attorney Geneva firms
- **Schellenberg Wittmer:** Large firm, Zollikon/Zurich-based, not Geneva-focused at small firm scale
- **White & Case / A&O Shearman:** International, large transaction focus — structural mismatch for Geneva SMB legal market
- **Niederer Kraft Frey:** M&A and litigation focus — not AI governance advisory for small firms
- **Khaitan:** India-connected international — not Geneva-focused

**Status:** Structural non-competitors for the ICP. Large firm advisors serve large firm problems at large firm economics. The 2–5 attorney Geneva firm is not their addressable market.

### OFL (Ordre des Avocats de Genève) — Structural Non-Provider
- **Status:** No AI governance guidance published for Geneva attorneys (confirmed across all cycles)
- **OFL position:** No formal guidance, no FAQ, no template, no training requirement for AI use
- **Market implication:** The guidance vacuum from OFL is a structural opportunity — the managing partner has no official anchor to point to, which means any structured guidance from a credible Geneva advisor fills an unmet need

### ANBLES (Association neuchâteloise des lawyers)
- **Status:** Feb 2026 report published — awareness signal, not formal guidance
- **Content:** General AI awareness for legal practice, not Geneva-specific compliance framework
- **Market implication:** Not a competitor service — reference point for client awareness levels

### Insurance Brokers — Structural Relationship, Not Compliance Advisory
- **Role:** Present carrier attestation forms, facilitate signature, relationship-based
- **Gap:** Do not explain carrier form implications, do not provide AI governance framework, E&O coverage unclear for form explanation
- **Market implication:** Broker relationship is the access point for the ICP, not the service provider for AI governance

### Self-Service Platforms (International) — Not Geneva-Specific
- **Templates and frameworks:** International AI governance templates available (ISACA, NIST, ISO frameworks)
- **Gap:** Not Geneva-specific, not attorney-specific, not calibrated for Swiss Article 321 CC professional secrecy obligations, not bilingual
- **Market implication:** The ICP who tries self-service returns to the market for human-guided Geneva-specific advisory

### DigitalCoa / Sergio Brotons — Addressable Position
- **Position:** Boutique Geneva-specific AI governance advisory for small firms
- **Competitive differentiation:** Geneva-canton specific, attorney-client privilege focused, Article 321 CC expertise, bilingual EN+FR, carrier-attestation-to-evidence architecture, managing partner personal liability lens
- **Service fit:** Exactly the ICP's problem set — accountability inversion, privilege exposure, multi-authority documentation, bilingual client communication
- **Competitive status:** No Geneva-specific boutique competitor confirmed — structural white space

## What's Missing (White Spaces) — C307

### Missing 01: Geneva-Specific AI Governance for Small Law Firms

**What's missing:** A Geneva-canton-specific AI governance advisory service designed for 2–5 attorney firms — not adapted from large firm frameworks, not international templates, not generic compliance checklists.

**Why it hasn't been filled:** Large firm advisors don't serve small firms. OFL hasn't provided guidance. Self-service platforms aren't Geneva-specific. The service requires canton-specific regulatory knowledge (Article 321 CC, nFADP, EU AI Act nexus), attorney-client privilege expertise, and small firm operational reality understanding.

**Entry condition:** A credible Geneva-based advisor with this specific combination of expertise can fill this space. The ICP is actively looking — they just don't know who to call.

---

### Missing 02: Attorney-Client Privilege AI Exposure Review

**What's missing:** A service that specifically evaluates AI vendor contracts against attorney-client privilege obligations — identifying which vendor relationships create privilege exposure, which contracts need modification, and which vendors need to be replaced or restricted.

**Why it hasn't been filled:** General vendor due diligence services don't understand Article 321 CC. Large firm tech law practices don't serve small firms. The privilege angle is specific to the legal profession's ethical obligations and requires both legal ethics expertise and AI vendor contract knowledge.

**Entry condition:** The service requires a specific combination: Swiss legal ethics (Article 321 CC), AI vendor contract review capability, and the ability to communicate findings in Geneva-canton professional context. No competitor offers this combination for small Geneva firms.

---

### Missing 03: Cross-Authority Documentation Architecture (Carrier + Ordre + Litigation)

**What's missing:** A documentation design service that maps which documentation simultaneously satisfies carrier attestation requirements, Ordre disciplinary documentation needs, and litigation discovery demands — and designs a single evidence architecture that works across all three authorities without creating cross-exposure.

**Why it hasn't been filled:** The problem requires understanding all three authority contexts and their documentation requirements. No Geneva service addresses this cross-mapping. General compliance advisors don't understand Ordre disciplinary documentation requirements. Large firm attorneys don't serve small firms for this type of engagement.

**Entry condition:** The service requires deep knowledge of carrier attestation evidence requirements, Ordre disciplinary documentation standards, and cantonal litigation discovery procedures — combined with the ability to design a unified documentation system. No competitor offers this for small Geneva firms.

---

### Missing 04: Bilingual Client-Facing AI Governance Communication

**What's missing:** A bilingual (EN+FR) client communication framework for Geneva small firms — engagement letter clauses, intake disclosures, and client communication templates calibrated for the different implicit expectations French-speaking and English-speaking clients bring to AI governance questions.

**Why it hasn't been filled:** Translation services don't understand Geneva legal professional context. Large firm client communication templates aren't designed for small firm bilingual reality. The service requires both linguistic capability and Geneva legal professional knowledge.

**Entry condition:** The service requires bilingual legal professional communication capability — not just translation, but culturally adapted communication for Geneva's specific bilingual professional context. No competitor offers this for small Geneva firms.

---

### Missing 05: AI Supervision Architecture for Small Law Firms

**What's missing:** A supervision framework designed for small law firms that allows managing partners to delegate AI-assisted work to associates while maintaining defensible oversight — without requiring the partner to become technically proficient in AI tools.

**Why it hasn't been filled:** General AI governance frameworks are designed for enterprises with compliance departments. The small firm supervision problem — where one person is accountable for work they cannot substantively evaluate — is not addressed by any available framework. The service requires understanding both legal professional supervision obligations and AI tool operation at a practical level.

**Entry condition:** The service requires combining legal supervision obligation knowledge with practical AI tool understanding — and the ability to design a supervision framework that is operationally practical for a 2–5 attorney firm. No competitor offers this for Geneva small law firms.

---

### Missing 06: Post-Attestation Evidence Reconstruction

**What's missing:** A service for managing partners who have already signed carrier attestation forms and now need to document what AI use actually occurred before any documentation system existed — producing defensible evidence of past AI use without fabrication, and building forward-looking evidence architecture.

**Why it hasn't been filled:** The service requires specific framing: reconstructing what actually happened (not claiming the past was different from what it was), producing defensible backward-looking evidence, and building forward-looking evidence architecture. General compliance advisors don't understand the ethical constraints on evidence reconstruction. Large firm practices don't serve small firms for this type of engagement.

**Entry condition:** The service requires the ability to conduct a structured retrospective of AI use (interviews, system logs, matter review), produce documented evidence of past use within ethical constraints, and design a forward-looking evidence architecture. No competitor offers this for Geneva small firms.

## Competitive Response Scenarios

### Scenario A: OFL Publishes AI Guidance
- **Probability:** Low in near term (6–12 months)
- **Impact:** Would provide official anchor but not eliminate service need — guidance is baseline, not implementation
- **Response:** Position as implementation partner for OFL guidance — firms still need help applying any OFL guidance to their specific situation

### Scenario B: Large Firm Launches Small Firm AI Governance Service
- **Probability:** Low — large firm economics don't support small firm engagement
- **Impact:** Would create confusion about market positioning
- **Response:** Emphasize boutique specificity, Geneva-canton focus, and personal liability lens — large firm framing won't resonate with 2–5 attorney managing partners

### Scenario C: Broker Launches AI Governance Education
- **Probability:** Medium — brokers are being asked AI questions by their clients
- **Impact:** Would change the initial conversation but not eliminate service need — brokers don't provide implementation
- **Response:** Position as the implementation partner for broker-referred clients — brokers can refer but not implement

### Scenario D: International Self-Service Platform Launches Geneva Content
- **Probability:** Medium — international platforms expanding EU/Switzerland coverage
- **Impact:** Would provide cheaper alternative for self-directed firms
- **Response:** Emphasize human-guided, Geneva-specific, attorney-privilege-focused service vs. generic platform — the ICP who wants generic templates was never the addressable client

## C307 Competitive Summary

**Structural white space confirmed:** No Geneva-specific boutique AI governance competitor for small firms — consistent with all prior cycles.

**Six white spaces identified:**
1. Geneva-specific AI governance for small law firms
2. Attorney-client privilege AI exposure review
3. Cross-authority documentation architecture (carrier + Ordre + litigation)
4. Bilingual client-facing AI governance communication
5. AI supervision architecture for small law firms
6. Post-attestation evidence reconstruction

**Competitive positioning:** The category is unnamed. The naming is the primary competitive act. Sergio Brotons / DigitalCoa is positioned to name and fill the category — Geneva AI governance for small boutique firms, with personal liability focus, attorney-client privilege lens, and bilingual capability.
