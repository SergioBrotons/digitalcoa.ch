# Phase 02 ICP Language — Geneva Finance / Boutique Wealth Management
## Cycle 299 (2026-05-30)

## Addressable Profile
Boutique wealth management firms and multi-family offices in Geneva, 50–200 employees, managing CHF 500M–5B AUM. Managing director or partner personally accountable. Serving UHNW and institutional clients internationally. CIO or Managing Director owns compliance decisions and bears personal liability.

## The ICP's Language: How They Talk About AI Problems

### Framing 1: "The Institutional Client Questionnaire Hit Before I Knew the Rules"
The managing director receives an AI governance questionnaire from an institutional client and feels the dual pressure of client retention and personal exposure simultaneously. The language they use:

- "Our largest institutional client sent us a 40-point AI governance DDQ. We had to leave significant sections blank."
- "The questionnaire asks about model documentation, human authorization chains, and algorithmic explanation. We don't have any of that."
- "I found out we're being compared against other managers in their portfolio. We don't know how we compare."
- "We responded to the questionnaire in 2024. We just found out that response is being used as live intelligence for their 2026 selection decisions."

### Framing 2: "Carrier Attestation Personal Signature Without Knowing What I'm Certifying"
The carrier attestation requires a personal signature from the managing director. The language they use:

- "The carrier form requires my personal signature on 'adequate AI governance.' What does 'adequate' mean?"
- "I signed the carrier attestation. I didn't have anyone review it before I signed."
- "The form asks about AI vendor due diligence, processor chains, and sub-contractor disclosures. I accepted vendor terms without evaluating any of this."
- "If this triggers a claim, that's my personal signature on a document I didn't fully understand."

### Framing 3: "The Regulatory Cluster Arriving Simultaneously With No Coordination"
Multiple regulatory frameworks are arriving at the same time with no Geneva-calibrated methodology for handling them as an integrated portfolio:

- "Swissdamed registration deadline is in weeks. We don't know if we qualify as Tier 1 or Tier 2."
- "EU AI Act is weeks away. We have EU clients — does that mean we're in scope?"
- "FINMA guidance from December 2024 has no implementation checklist. We're still guessing."
- "CRS 2.0 has been live for months. The AI governance obligations under it weren't in our original AML framework."
- "The SRO has said nothing. Not one word about what they expect from us on AI."

### Framing 4: "I Am the Compliance Function — There Is No Separate Team"
The boutique WM managing director is structurally responsible for AI governance without institutional support:

- "We are 80 people. We don't have a Chief Compliance Officer. I am the compliance function."
- "Big 4 firms quoted us engagement minimums we can't justify. General consultants don't understand Swiss regulatory specificity."
- "Every decision about AI is my decision. The documentation of that decision is also my responsibility."
- "I need to build an AI governance framework from scratch that satisfies FINMA, carriers, institutional clients, and nFADP simultaneously."

### Framing 5: "Portfolio Construction AI Is Different From Advisory AI — Nobody Names This"
The managing director distinguishes between AI used for client advisory and AI used for portfolio construction — and feels the portfolio construction AI accountability is more acute:

- "The AI that builds our portfolios is different from the AI that generates reports. The portfolio AI makes decisions that directly affect client outcomes."
- "When the carrier asks about 'AI-influenced portfolio decisions,' I don't know what evidence they're looking for."
- "The authorization chain from AI recommendation to human approval in portfolio construction — we never documented that."
- "Portfolio construction AI runs continuously. It's not a one-time decision. I don't know how to document continuous authorization."

### Framing 6: "Client Data Being Processed by AI Systems I Can't Describe"
Client data is flowing through AI systems the managing director cannot fully enumerate or explain:

- "Our portfolio analytics AI accesses client data. I accepted the vendor's data processing terms without reading them."
- "The AI vendor's sub-processors are listed in an appendix I never reviewed. That's part of our nFADP Art. 9 exposure."
- "If a client asks me which AI systems process their data, I can't give them a complete answer."
- "We're using three AI tools simultaneously. Each one processes client data. I don't have a unified view of the surface."

## NET-NEW Finding C299-P2-F1
**Portfolio construction AI as a discrete accountability architecture with no Geneva-calibrated methodology:** The managing director distinguishes between AI used for advisory/reporting and AI used for portfolio construction — and recognizes that portfolio construction AI creates higher-stakes accountability because it operates continuously and directly affects client financial outcomes. The authorization chain from AI output to human approval in portfolio construction is a specific documentation gap that no Geneva market service addresses. Carrier third-generation attestation specifically probes this. The naming distinction ("portfolio construction AI authorization chain" vs. general "AI governance") is itself a market differentiation — ICPs recognize it immediately as specific to their problem.

## NET-NEW Finding C299-P2-F2
**Cross-firm comparison visibility asymmetry as competitive exposure invisible to the measured firm:** Institutional clients are running simultaneous AI governance comparisons across their manager roster without notifying the managers being compared. The boutique responds to a questionnaire as a single-point event, unaware that their response is being benchmarked against 8-12 other managers simultaneously. The aggregate of responses is forming a de facto market standard. The boutique that responds thoughtfully and the boutique that leaves sections blank are both being measured — but only one knows they're being measured. This asymmetry is a specific positioning opportunity: firms that understand they're in a comparison dynamic can prepare differently than firms treating each questionnaire as an isolated event.

## NET-NEW Finding C299-P2-F3
**Carrier attestation as personal liability instrument with no pre-signing review market in Geneva for WM:** The carrier attestation requires the managing director's personal signature on "adequate AI governance" — an undefined standard designed by underwriting logic, not professional liability architecture. No Geneva market service offers pre-signing personal liability review for WM managing directors. The signature creates personal exposure under nFADP Art. 63 that the carrier's insurance product does not discharge. The market has developed pre-signing review for medical practitioners; WM managing directors are structurally in the same exposure position without an equivalent service. This is a specific service category with no current competitor.

## NET-NEW Finding C299-P2-F4
**Vendor data processing architecture opacity as nFADP Art. 9 processor chain exposure the MD cannot enumerate:** The managing director accepted vendor terms for three to five AI tools across portfolio analytics, client reporting, and CRM — each with sub-processor chains the MD has never reviewed. nFADP Art. 9 obligations arise from the nature of data processing activity, not from whether the MD evaluated the terms. The carrier's third-generation questionnaire now asks about sub-processor chains — activating disclosure obligations that have been accumulating through years of unevaluated click-through acceptance. The exposure is present-tense and undisclosed. No Geneva service maps the aggregate vendor data processing architecture at boutique WM scale.

## NET-NEW Finding C299-P2-F6
**Institutional client questionnaire second-generation data use creating undisclosed temporal exposure:** The managing director responded to an institutional AI governance questionnaire in 2024, treating it as a point-in-time disclosure obligation. This temporal dimension of institutional questionnaire response is a specific undisclosed exposure that compounds with every questionnaire cycle.
