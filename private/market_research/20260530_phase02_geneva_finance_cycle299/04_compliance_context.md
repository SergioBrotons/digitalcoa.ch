### FINMA Circular 08/2024: The Guidance Without the Checklist

FINMA published Circular 08/2024 in December 2024, establishing that supervised institutions must ensure adequate AI governance. The circular defines expectations at institutional level without providing a proportionate implementation checklist for boutique WM firms.

**What the circular requires:**
- Documented AI inventory
- Defined governance framework for AI tool deployment
- Human oversight mechanisms
- Risk management integration
- Vendor due diligence

**What the circular does not provide:**
- Proportionate methodology for 50-200 employee firms without dedicated compliance functions
- Specific evidence standards for "adequate" governance
- Checklist or scoring mechanism for self-assessment
- Geneva-calibrated implementation guidance

**The boutique WM problem:**
The managing director knows FINMA expects AI governance. They do not know what "adequate" means for their specific firm. They have no checklist to follow. They have no Geneva-calibrated guidance document. They are guessing — and guessing wrong creates enforcement exposure.

**SRO silence amplification:**
The SRO has published no AI governance guidance. The managing director's industry body — the organization that is supposed to translate FINMA expectations into practical member guidance — has said nothing. This structural vacuum amplifies the FINMA guidance gap. The boutique is not only without FINMA guidance; they are without their primary institutional translation mechanism.

### nFADP: Personal Liability That the Carrier Doesn't Discharge

The Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) introduced Article 63 with personal criminal liability for compliance failures. For the boutique WM managing director, this creates a specific exposure that carrier D&O insurance does not explicitly address.

**Art. 63 Personal Liability Exposure:**
- Managing director can be held personally criminally liable for nFADP compliance failures
- The liability is personal — attached to the individual, not just the firm
- Carrier D&O insurance covers financial exposure, not governance documentation adequacy
- The documentation that would protect against Art. 63 liability is not the same documentation the carrier requires

**The Documentation Architecture Incompatibility:**
The carrier's attestation form is designed for underwriting risk evaluation — it measures whether the firm has documentation that satisfies the carrier's risk assessment. This is not the same as documentation that would satisfy nFADP Art. 63 criminal defense requirements. Criminal defense requires:
- Contemporaneous reasoning documentation
- Decision authorization chain
- Chain of custody for AI-influenced decisions
- Evidence of personal engagement with governance decisions

Carrier attestation documentation may satisfy the carrier while leaving the managing director's criminal defense exposure undocumented. This architecture incompatibility is unnamed in the Geneva market.

**Art. 9 Processor Obligations:**
nFADP Art. 9 creates data processor obligations that arise from the nature of data processing activity — not from whether the managing director evaluated the vendor terms. Years of click-through acceptance have been accumulating undisclosed Art. 9 processor chain obligations. The carrier's third-generation questionnaire now asks about sub-processors — activating disclosure obligations the managing director has never evaluated.

### Swissdamed: The Classification Problem Boutiques Can't Solve

Swissdamed registration deadlines are weeks away. The classification framework (Tier 1 vs. Tier 2) is written for manufacturers and medical device companies — not for boutique wealth management firms.

**The Classification Problem:**
- Tier 1/Tier 2 criteria have operational consequences for AI system registration
- Boutique WM firms don't know if they fall under Tier 1 or Tier 2
- No Geneva-calibrated classification assessment methodology exists for WM firms
- The registration deadline creates urgency without proportionate compliance pathway

**Why This Matters for WM Specifically:**
The classification determines what AI governance documentation is required. Getting it wrong in either direction creates exposure — over-compliance wastes resources, under-compliance creates regulatory risk. The boutique WM managing director has no Geneva-calibrated reference for making this determination.

---

### EU AI Act: The Nexus Question Boutiques Can't Answer

The EU AI Act is live, with high-risk provisions and AI literacy obligations activating. Boutique WM firms with EU clients don't know if they're in scope — and if they are, what compliance requires.

**The Nexus Problem:**
- EU AI Act applies to systems used in the EU and to firms with EU nexus
- Having EU clients may constitute sufficient nexus for the Act to apply
- "AI literacy" documentation obligation is present-tense for firms in scope
- No Geneva-calibrated assessment methodology exists for boutique WM EU nexus

**What "AI Literacy" Actually Requires:**
Art. 4 AI literacy requires that personnel using AI systems have appropriate knowledge and competence. For the boutique WM managing director, this means:
- Understanding what the firm's AI tools actually do
- Documenting that understanding
- Being able to demonstrate competence if challenged

This is not a training certificate. This is a demonstrated competency standard. The documentation required to satisfy it does not exist in boutique WM firms because the standard has never been operationalized for this context.

### CRS 2.0: The AI Governance Obligation Nobody Integrated

CRS 2.0 has been live since January 2026. The standard requires AI governance obligations that were not in the original AML framework. Boutique WM firms built their CRS compliance programs before AI became a core operational component — the AI governance obligations under CRS 2.0 have not been integrated into existing compliance frameworks.

**The Integration Problem:**
- AI governance obligations under CRS 2.0 exist separately from existing AML documentation
- No cross-reference methodology for managing CRS 2.0 AI obligations alongside other accountability tracks
- Firms may be CRS 2.0 non-compliant without knowing it because they never integrated the new requirements
- The documentation gap is structural — it exists in the framework, not just in the firm's operations

### Carrier Attestation: The Convergence Point

The carrier attestation is the point where multiple regulatory obligations converge on the managing director's personal signature. The carrier's third-generation questionnaire is now probing:
- AI vendor sub-processors and data termination protocols
- Portfolio construction AI authorization chains
- nFADP Art. 9 processor chain disclosure
- Human authorization evidence for AI-influenced portfolio decisions

This convergence means the carrier questionnaire is functioning as a de facto regulatory enforcement instrument — asking questions that activate obligations under FINMA, nFADP, and CRS 2.0 simultaneously. The managing director who signs the carrier form without pre-signing review is committing to obligations across multiple frameworks without having evaluated them.

## Key Compliance Context Findings

### Finding: The Documentation Architecture Incompatibility Problem
Carrier attestation documentation built for underwriting risk evaluation is architecturally inadequate for nFADP Art. 63 criminal defense. The managing director may have documentation that satisfies the carrier while leaving personal criminal exposure undocumented. This incompatibility is unnamed in the Geneva market. No Geneva service addresses this dual-track documentation architecture problem.

### Finding: The Regulatory Cluster Without Cross-Reference Methodology
Four independent documentation systems (FINMA, institutional questionnaires, nFADP, carrier attestation) describe the same AI governance reality with different formatting, evidence standards, update cycles, and disclosure scopes. No Geneva methodology exists for cross-referencing these four documentation streams. Firms may be internally inconsistent across tracks without knowing it. This is a discrete service category — cross-track coherence management — unnamed in the market.

### Finding: The Classification Problem Creating Compliance Paralysis
Swissdamed Tier 1/Tier 2 classification criteria are not calibrated for boutique WM firms. The EU AI Act nexus question is unanswered. The managing director cannot determine which framework applies to their firm and what compliance requires. This creates proportionate compliance paralysis — firms either over-comply or under-comply with no validated middle ground.

### Finding: SRO Silence Operating as Structural Accountability Vacuum
The SRO's structural silence on AI governance is not the same as FINMA silence. The managing director expects their SRO to provide practical operational guidance — and the SRO has not. This vacuum creates a specific pain point: even the managing director's own industry body has abandoned them on AI governance. The advisory practice's positioning as understanding "what the SRO should be saying even if it isn't saying it" directly addresses this pain point.
