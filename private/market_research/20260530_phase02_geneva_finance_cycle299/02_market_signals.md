# Phase 02 Market Signals — Geneva Finance / Boutique Wealth Management
## Cycle 299 (2026-05-30)

## Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

### Signal 1: The Regulatory Cluster Pressure — Multiple Timelines Collapsing Simultaneously
The managing director of a Geneva boutique WM firm is experiencing a specific pressure pattern: multiple regulatory frameworks are arriving at the same time with no coordination mechanism and no Geneva-calibrated methodology for handling them as an integrated portfolio.

**What the market is signaling:**
- Swissdamed registration deadline: weeks away, Tier 1/Tier 2 classification criteria written for manufacturers, boutique WM firms don't know where they fall
- EU AI Act: weeks away, firms with EU nexus don't know if they're in scope or what compliance requires
- FINMA Circular 08/2024: 17 months old, no implementation checklist, firms still guessing what "adequate" means
- CRS 2.0: 5 months live, AI governance obligations not in original AML framework
- nFADP: operational, Art. 63 personal liability active, no Geneva protocol for WM-specific implementation
- SRO: structural silence, not just delayed — no word on what they expect from members on AI governance

**The specific failure pattern:**
The managing director is not experiencing these as separate problems. They are experiencing them as a single compound pressure — "every regulator is asking about AI at the same time and I have no framework to answer any of them." The four-track documentation problem (FINMA, institutional questionnaires, nFADP, carrier attestation) has no cross-reference methodology. Firms may be internally inconsistent across tracks without knowing it.

### Signal 2: Carrier Attestation as Personal Liability Instrument — Undefined Standard, Personal Signature, No Review
The carrier attestation is being experienced as a personal liability event, not a compliance documentation exercise. Managing directors are signing attestation forms designed by underwriting logic, committing to undefined adequacy standards, without pre-signing review.

**What the market is signaling:**
- Carrier forms require personal signature from managing director or partner
- The "adequate AI governance" standard is undefined — the carrier cannot tell the signatory what adequate means
- No Geneva market service offers pre-signing personal liability review for WM managing directors
- Medical practitioners have developed pre-signing review; WM managing directors are structurally equivalent without an equivalent service
- The signature creates personal exposure under nFADP Art. 63 that carrier D&O insurance does not explicitly discharge
- Carrier's third-generation questionnaire now probes AI vendor sub-processors and data termination protocols — activating nFADP Art. 9 obligations the managing director has never evaluated

**The specific failure pattern:**
The managing director signed the carrier attestation. They did not have anyone review it before signing. They are now realizing that their personal signature created commitments they cannot evidence compliance with. If a challenge occurs, they have documentation they signed without the documentation that would support the signature.

### Signal 3: Institutional Client Questionnaire Response Functioning as Persistent Comparative Intelligence
The managing director responded to an institutional AI governance questionnaire in 2024 as a point-in-time disclosure obligation. That response is now functioning as live comparative intelligence for 2026-2027 mandate decisions being made by institutional clients across their manager roster.

**What the market is signaling:**
- Institutional clients are running simultaneous multi-firm comparisons across 8-12 managers in their portfolio
- The comparison is benchmarking documentation quality against governance quality — firms with sophisticated documentation are advantaged regardless of actual governance architecture
- Boutique firms are discovering this comparison is happening without being notified
- 2024 responses are being used for decisions the boutique never anticipated when responding
- The boutique that responded thoughtfully and the boutique that left sections blank are both being measured — only one knows they're being measured

**The specific failure pattern:**
The boutique cannot revise historical responses and cannot control how they're being interpreted. The documentation quality gap is now a competitive intelligence exposure. The response that was built to satisfy a point-in-time disclosure requirement is now a persistent market signal.

### Signal 4: Portfolio Construction AI Authorization Chain Documentation Gap
The portfolio construction AI authorization chain — from AI output to human approval moment — is a specific documentation requirement that no Geneva market methodology addresses. The continuous nature of portfolio construction AI makes it architecturally incompatible with documentation frameworks designed for discrete decisions.

**What the market is signaling:**
- Portfolio construction AI operates continuously, not as a one-time decision
- Carrier third-generation attestation specifically probes "AI-influenced portfolio decisions" and requires documented human authorization
- The sequential chaining from AI output to human authorization with evidence of each step is a specific documentation category
- No current AI governance framework at boutique WM scale addresses chaining documentation as a discrete requirement
- Boutiques are being asked to document a sequential integrity requirement they never built a record for

**The specific failure pattern:**
The managing director cannot produce evidence of the authorization chain for portfolio construction AI. They know the AI is making recommendations. They know humans are approving. They have no record of the specific moments, conditions, and authorization evidence for those approvals. When the carrier asks, they have nothing to show.

**What the market is signaling:**

- The carrier's third-generation questionnaire asks about sub-processor chains — now the managing director has to disclose processor chains they don't know they have
- Years of unevaluated click-through acceptance have been accumulating undisclosed Art. 9 obligations
- No Geneva service maps the aggregate vendor data processing architecture at boutique WM scale

**The specific failure pattern:**
The managing director accepted vendor terms for multiple AI tools without reviewing data processing clauses. The vendor's sub-processors are listed in appendices never reviewed. The Art. 9 processor chain is active and undisclosed. The carrier questionnaire is now probing exactly this.

### Signal 6: The "Too Small for Big 4, Too Specific for Generalist Consultant" Structural Gap Confirmed
Geneva boutique WM firms (50-200 employees) fall between two market failures: too small for Big 4 engagement minimums, and too specific in Swiss regulatory AI governance needs for general boutique consulting firms. This gap has been confirmed across multiple cycles and remains unnamed in the market.

**What the market is signaling:**
- Big 4 firms quote engagement minimums boutique WM firms cannot justify
- Generalist consultants do not understand Swiss regulatory specificity — FINMA, SRO, nFADP, AMLA, LETA, CRS 2.0
- No existing market service addresses the managing director's specific accountability problem at proportionate scale
- The structural gap explains why no competitor has emerged across 7+ cycles of research
- The ICP explicitly names this gap as their experience: "there's no one who understands boutique scale and Swiss regulatory specificity simultaneously"

**The specific failure pattern:**
The managing director has no market option that fits their profile. They either over-pays for Big 4 engagement they can't justify, or works with generalist consultants who don't understand their problem. The market gap is structural, not product-line — no existing player is positioned to fill it.
