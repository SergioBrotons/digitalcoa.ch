## Net New Findings (C288 Phase 01) — 6 Distinct from C214–C287

### Finding 1: Carrier Attestation Is the Primary Trigger Event

**Insight:** The single most actionable trigger for AI governance urgency among small Geneva law firms is the RC pro carrier renewal attestation form. This is more immediate and concrete than philosophical concerns about the AI Convention, bar guidance gaps, or professional media discourse.

**Why it's new:** Prior market research may have identified carrier attestation as a concern. The specific finding here is that it's functioning as a primary trigger event — the thing that converts abstract AI governance anxiety into concrete action within a defined timeline (the policy renewal deadline).

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "Your carrier is asking questions about AI governance. Here's what you actually need to answer."
- "The attestation form is the deadline that makes AI governance real. We help you meet it."
- "Don't let your policy renewal be the first time you confront your AI governance gaps."

### Finding 2: The DPA Gap Is the Compliance Core

**Insight:** The absence of signed Data Processing Agreements with AI vendors is the most widespread and immediately actionable compliance gap among small Geneva firms. Every other governance obligation — sub-processing restrictions, training data controls, data deletion — flows from the existence and quality of DPAs.

**Why it's new:** Prior research may have noted DPA requirements generally. The specific finding is the absence of DPAs as the central compliance gap that, if fixed, resolves multiple downstream problems simultaneously.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "If you don't have a DPA with your AI vendor, you don't have AI governance. You just have AI in use."
- "The DPA is where AI governance starts. Everything else — training data opt-out, sub-processing controls, deletion rights — lives inside it."
- "Your carrier wants to see DPAs. Most small Geneva firms don't have them. That's the gap."

### Finding 3: Training Data Is the Professional Secrecy Flash Point

**Insight:** Among all AI vendor risks, the use of client data for model training is the flash point for professional secrecy (BGFA Art. 13) concerns. Attorneys understand this instinctively — the idea that their client matter inputs are being used to train a commercial model feels like a violation, even if they can't articulate the exact legal mechanism.

**Why it's new:** The training data problem has been discussed in general AI compliance contexts. The specific finding for Geneva small firms is that it functions as the emotional core of the professional secrecy concern — it's the thing that makes AI feel dangerous to attorneys who might otherwise be comfortable with productivity tools.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "Client data in, model improvements out — that's the training data problem. It's not hypothetical. It's happening right now with most commercial AI tools by default."
- "If your AI vendor is using your client inputs to train their model, you've arguably disclosed your client's matter to a third party. That's a professional secrecy question, not just a data protection question."
- "Before you use any AI tool on a client matter, ask one question: does this vendor use my inputs for training? If you don't know the answer, you don't know what you're doing to your client's confidentiality."

### Finding 4: Scale-Specific Governance Is the Market Gap

**Insight:** No existing provider offers a governance framework specifically designed for the resource constraints of a 2–5 attorney practice. All available frameworks — SBA guidelines, generic compliance templates, international legal tech documentation — assume compliance infrastructure that doesn't exist in small firms.

**Why it's new:** This is a structural market observation, not a legal research finding. The specific insight is that the gap is not just "there's no guidance" but "there's no guidance calibrated to how a small firm actually operates." The solution needs to be operable by a managing partner without a compliance team, in a realistic timeframe, at a realistic cost.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "Enterprise compliance frameworks for Big Law don't translate to boutique practice. We build governance that works at your scale."
- "Three attorneys. Limited time. No compliance team. That's the starting point we design for."
- "If your governance framework requires a dedicated compliance officer to maintain, it's not a governance framework — it's a wish."

### Finding 5: The AI Register Solves the Documentation Problem

**Insight:** The AI tool register — a simple documented inventory of AI tools in use, what they access, where data flows, and what safeguards are in place — is simultaneously the most practical and the most undervalued governance tool for small Geneva firms. It solves the carrier attestation documentation problem, creates the foundation for periodic review, and forces systematic awareness of AI tool usage that most firms currently lack.

**Why it's new:** The AI register concept has not been presented to this market as a specific, discrete, immediately implementable artifact. It is novel and immediately actionable.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "Your carrier wants to know which AI tools you're using. Do you know? Actually know — not guess, not hope, know?"
- "The AI register is the document that proves you have governance. Without it, you're attesting to nothing you can actually show."
- "Start with the register. One table. Annual review. That's the minimum viable governance in practice, not just in theory."

### Finding 6: Geneva-Cantonal Positioning Has Differentiation Value

**Insight:** The absence of Geneva-specific (SVA) operational AI governance guidance creates a credible positioning opportunity for a Geneva-caliber resource. Attorneys in Geneva identify as Geneva practitioners; a resource that speaks to their specific context — not just national SBA principles translated into French — will have higher resonance and credibility.

**Why it's new:** While SBA national guidelines exist, the specific finding is that the cantonal dimension is load-bearing for this ICP. A Geneva-specific resource would be differentiated even from high-quality French-language national content, because it acknowledges the cantonal professional identity.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "National guidelines. Geneva practice. We translate the SBA principles into what they actually mean for your three-attorney firm in Geneva."
- "The SVA hasn't published a Geneva-specific AI governance guide. Until now."
- "Geneva law firms deserve guidance that starts with their context, not a generic template adapted downward."
