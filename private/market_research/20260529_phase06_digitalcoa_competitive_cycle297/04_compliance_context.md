## Framework 1: SAV AI Guidelines — The Accountability Standard Without Implementation

### What the SAV Published
The Swiss Federal Bar (Schweizerische Anwaltsverband / SAV) published AI guidelines for attorneys using AI in legal practice. The guidelines establish that attorneys remain personally accountable for AI-assisted work product, must conduct adequate human review, and must be able to demonstrate the adequacy of their AI governance controls.

### The Operational Problem
The SAV guidelines are conceptually correct and operationally undeliverable for a 2–5 attorney Geneva boutique. They state the principle ("you are accountable") without providing the decision tree ("here is how to implement that accountability at boutique scale"). The guidelines do not define:
- What "adequate human review" means operationally for a specific AI-assisted legal memo
- What documentation evidence of adequate review looks like
- How a boutique firm with no IT department implements the SAV standard
- What the human review record must contain to satisfy the accountability requirement

### The GAP DigitalCoa.ch Fills
The SAV implementation gap is the discrete service product: SAV-aligned AI governance architecture for boutique-scale Geneva firms. The implementation is not a compliance checklist — it is a decision-tree guide for discharging SAV accountability obligations on a Tuesday with two attorneys and no IT department.

### Regulatory Trajectory
The SAV is expected to update AI guidelines as carrier attestation questions become more specific and as AI tool use in legal practice intensifies. The next iteration of SAV guidance will likely become more prescriptive about what adequate human review documentation must contain. Firms that have built Human Decision Record architecture now will be ahead of the next SAV iteration.

## Framework 2: FINMA AI Guidance — The Supervisory Expectation Without Operational Pathway

### What FINMA Has Communicated
FINMA has communicated supervisory expectations for AI use by supervised institutions (banks, insurance intermediaries, asset managers) without publishing formal binding regulations. The expectations include: human authorization for AI-influenced decisions, documentation of AI governance framework, and evidence of board-level AI oversight.

### The Operational Problem
FINMA's AI guidance is non-binding supervisory expectation, not formal regulation — which means the enforcement mechanism is indirect (through carrier examination, not direct regulatory action). For Geneva wealth management boutiques, the carrier is the enforcement instrument: carrier attestation instruments ask about AI governance adequacy, and the answers become evidence in regulatory examinations.

### The GAP DigitalCoa.ch Fills
The FINMA-guidance-to-carrier-attestation translation is a discrete service: mapping FINMA supervisory expectations to third-generation carrier attestation questions. The ICP who understands what FINMA actually expects can produce carrier attestation evidence that is calibrated to both the supervisory standard and the carrier instrument.

### Regulatory Trajectory
FINMA is expected to formalize AI guidance into more prescriptive requirements as the EU AI Act implementation progresses and Swiss AI governance expectations become more defined. Geneva wealth management boutiques using AI in portfolio construction and risk modeling will face increasing FINMA scrutiny. The advisory practice should anticipate FINMA guidance formalization as a medium-term market driver.

## Framework 3: FMH AI Competency Standards — Implied Obligation Without Definition

### What the FMH Has Communicated
The Swiss Medical Association (Foederatio Medicorum Helveticorum / FMH) has communicated implied expectations that physicians maintain competency to oversee AI tools used in clinical practice. The expectation appears in FMH guidelines on medical device use, professional liability standards, and carrier requirements for AI-assisted clinical documentation.

### The Operational Problem
The FMH has never operationally defined what "AI competency" means for a physician. Vendor training certificates demonstrate attendance, not competency. No Geneva medical body has published AI competency requirements proportionate to independent practice scale. The implied obligation exists without a defined standard, measurement methodology, or compliance pathway.

### The GAP DigitalCoa.ch Fills
The FMH AI competency gap is a discrete advisory product: AI competency framework design for independent physician practices. The framework does not need to be FMH-approved (because no approval pathway exists) — it needs to be defensible to a carrier challenging AI competency documentation. The product is: structured interview, competency framework document, evidence architecture design.

### Regulatory Trajectory
The FMH is under pressure to formalize AI competency standards as AI diagnostic tools proliferate in outpatient practice. The next 12–18 months are the window before FMH formalizes requirements. Practices that have built competency frameworks proactively will be ahead of the formalization.

## Framework 4: nFADP Art.9 — The Processor Obligation Without Classification Pathway

### What nFADP Art.9 Requires
The revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) Article 9 establishes enhanced obligations for processing sensitive personal data, including health data. When a firm uses an AI vendor to process client data, the vendor is a data processor (or sub-processor) requiring a compliant Data Processing Agreement under Art.9.

### The Operational Problem
The AI vendor classification paradox (Phase 05, Finding 02) makes nFADP Art.9 compliance impossible to complete. Before executing a compliant DPA, the firm must determine the vendor's legal role. AI vendors occupy a role no existing framework was designed to assign. No Geneva authority has published AI vendor classification guidance. The compliance work stops at the entry point.

### The GAP DigitalCoa.ch Fills
The AI vendor classification service is the entry point to nFADP compliance work. It does not exist in the Geneva market. The service is: structured classification interview, role determination documentation, DPA architecture recommendation. The product resolves the classification paradox for the specific vendor environment the ICP operates in.

### Regulatory Trajectory
The nFADP is in early enforcement phase. The Federal Data Protection Commissioner (EDÖB) has not published AI-specific nFADP guidance. As enforcement cases accumulate and AI vendor terms evolve, the classification problem will become more acute. The first actor to publish a Geneva-calibrated AI vendor classification methodology occupies a significant market position.

## Framework 5: MedDO / Swissdamed — Device Classification Without Proportionate Methodology

### What MedDO Requires
The Medical Devices Ordinance (MedDO) implements Swissdamed — the Swiss medical devices database — requiring mandatory registration of medical devices placed on the Swiss market from 1 July 2026. AI tools used in clinical practice that qualify as medical devices must be registered.

### The Operational Problem
The Tier 1/Tier 2 classification criteria in MedDO are written for manufacturers placing devices on the market, not for outpatient independent practices using AI tools. An independent physician practice using an AI diagnostic support tool faces a classification question with no proportionate methodology: the criteria assume a manufacturer perspective, not a clinical user perspective. The practice cannot determine its registration obligation.

### The GAP DigitalCoa.ch Fills
The Swissdamed classification assessment is a discrete product for the 30-day window before the 1 July 2026 deadline: structured classification interview, determination of registration obligation, and registration pathway if required. This product does not exist in the Geneva market. The healthcare ICP is currently receiving guidance from AI vendors who have a commercial interest in broad registration.

### Regulatory Trajectory
Swissdamed enforcement begins 1 July 2026. Independent practices that have not resolved their classification status will face regulatory exposure. The classification assessment product has a 30-day acute market window. After the deadline, the product shifts to ongoing compliance maintenance.

## Framework 6: Professional Liability Carrier Attestation Evolution

### What Carriers Are Doing
Professional liability carriers (professional indemnity, D&O) have iterated their AI governance attestation instruments through three generations:
- **First generation:** Tool inventory, policy existence, acknowledgment of AI use
- **Second generation:** Client questionnaire response review, processor chain documentation, nFADP compliance evidence
- **Third generation:** Authorization chain evidence, Human Decision Record existence, cross-client commitment consistency

### The Operational Problem
The third-generation attestation is arriving now and asks for evidence that documentation improvements alone cannot produce. The authorization chain — which AI recommendation, which named human, at which moment, before which decision — requires a structural documentation architecture the boutique does not have.

### The GAP DigitalCoa.ch Fills
The pre-signing carrier attestation review is the highest-conversion entry point for the advisory practice. The ICP arrives with the carrier form in hand, the signature deadline approaching, and the personal liability crystallization moment. The 90-minute briefing is calibrated precisely for this moment.

### Regulatory Trajectory
Carrier attestation evolution will continue as AI tool use intensifies and as carrier claims experience with AI-related incidents accumulates. The third-generation attestation is not the final iteration. The advisory practice should track fourth-generation attestation evolution and update the briefing content accordingly.

## Framework 7: Institutional Client AI Governance Questionnaire Sophistication

### What Institutional Clients Are Doing
Family offices, pension funds, corporate boards, and institutional investors are circulating increasingly sophisticated AI governance questionnaires to their service providers (asset managers, consultants, advisors). The second-generation questionnaires in circulation now include:
- Authorization chain documentation requests
- Cross-mandate AI governance consistency questions
- Sub-consultant AI vendor disclosure requirements
- AI model version documentation for mandate-period deliverables

### The Operational Problem
The institutional questionnaire is functioning as a vendor selection instrument, not just a compliance audit. Responses are being used for comparative evaluation across manager rosters. A boutique's 2024–2025 first-generation questionnaire responses are now live comparative intelligence for 2026–2027 mandate decisions. The ICP who responded with vendor-downloaded templates has performed informality. The ICP who responds with structured, substantively complete responses performs seriousness and governance sophistication.

### The GAP DigitalCoa.ch Fills
The institutional questionnaire response architecture is a discrete product: triage framework (which questions require substantive response, which are checkboxes), cross-client consistency analysis, and response drafting for complex questions. The product positions the boutique as a preferred vendor candidate in institutional selection processes.

### Regulatory Trajectory
Institutional AI governance questionnaires will continue to increase in sophistication as institutional clients accumulate claims experience with AI-related failures. The questionnaire evolution is faster than Geneva regulatory evolution — the institutional client is often ahead of the Geneva authority in AI governance sophistication.
