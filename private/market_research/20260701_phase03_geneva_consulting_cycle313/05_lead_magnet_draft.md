**What Changed**

Three things happened during what I now realize wasn't a pause at all.

*First:* **the client is now offering an AI tool to help me fill in the RFP questionnaire.** They call it a convenience. I enter my proposal methodology, my sub-consultant engagement terms, my prior deliverable samples, my AI tool inventory — exactly the categories the client questionnaire asks about. My RFP response now passes through a vendor the client's own due diligence has not reviewed. nFADP Art.52 is my personal exposure — and the tool creates that exposure. And the response looks generic — every consulting boutique using the same tool produces indistinguishable language. The client's procurement committee reads past the generic response. My specific proposal methodology is invisible.

*Second:* **the client is now sending mid-engagement follow-up questionnaires.** Not just at engagement renewal. They sent a structured mid-engagement follow-up in June asking what changed in my AI governance in the last 90 days. I signed the engagement letter in November. My proposal methodology documentation is seven months old. The client is asking about freshness my documentation cycle isn't built for.

*Third:* **the June Geneva consulting conference produced public peer proposal benchmarks.** I saw what three named Geneva consulting boutiques declared in their client RFP responses. Their language was specific, defensible, anchored to their actual methodology. Mine was generic — because I used the RFP form-completion tool the client offered. The gap is now public. I'm now visibly grouped with other form-completion vendor users, not with the boutiques whose proposals were anchored to their actual methodology.

La méthodologie client qui était censée être confidentielle est maintenant traitée par un successeur qui n'a pas hérité de vos obligations de confidentialité.
