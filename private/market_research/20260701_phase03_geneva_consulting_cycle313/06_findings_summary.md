## The Six NET-NEW Findings — Detailed

### Finding 01: RFP Form-Completion Vector — The Client Proposal Response Loop

**The finding:** The client is now offering (directly or through third-party RFP-completion vendors like Loopio, Responsive, RFPIO, or AI-native proposal-assistance tools) AI-assisted questionnaire response tools. The Geneva boutique consulting senior partner is using the tool to draft responses to the client RFP AI governance questionnaire by entering proposal methodology, sub-consultant engagement terms, prior deliverable samples, AI tool inventory — exactly the categories of information the client questionnaire is asking about. The RFP-completion tool processes this through its own AI vendor's servers. The RFP response is now being produced through a vendor the client's own due diligence has not reviewed, with data flows the senior partner's nFADP Art.52 review has not addressed. The personal exposure loop is closed at the client RFP level. The response is generic — every consulting boutique using the same tool produces indistinguishable language. The client's procurement committee reads past the generic response to find the senior partner's specific proposal methodology evidence. The consulting boutique that submitted the form-completion response has no specific evidence behind the language. The proposal is lost.

**Mechanism:** Client offers AI questionnaire-completion tool → consulting boutique enters proposal methodology → RFP-completion vendor processes client methodology through its AI → response generated → response submitted → RFP response now incorporates content processed through a separately-disclosed vendor → nFADP Art.52 personal exposure vector is doubled → response is generic because every consulting boutique using the same tool produces similar language → client procurement committee reads past generic language → consulting boutique's specific proposal methodology is invisible → proposal lost.

**Implication:** RFP form-completion vendor review is a discrete, named C313 service. It audits which questionnaire-completion tool the consulting boutique uses, what client methodology that tool processes, whether its terms create nFADP Art.52 personal exposure, and what the alternative is (answering the RFP directly with the consulting boutique's own proposal methodology evidence architecture). The service also addresses the generic-response trap — the form-completion tool produces a response that looks like every other consulting boutique's response. The consulting boutique needs a specific proposal methodology documentation architecture to anchor the response. The first Geneva boutique consulting service to publish the RFP form-completion vendor review owns the C313 client RFP category.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The AI tool you used to complete the client RFP creates the same nFADP Art.52 personal exposure the questionnaire is asking about. And your response now looks like everyone else's. I'm showing you how to break the loop and produce a response anchored to your specific proposal methodology."

### Finding 02: Mid-Engagement Follow-Up Questionnaire Cadence — Documentation Truncation Between Proposal and Renewal

**The finding:** Mid-engagement client follow-up questionnaires are now structural in Geneva boutique consulting client relationships. Clients that signed engagement letters in Q4 2025 are now sending mid-engagement follow-ups at engagement month three, month six, month nine — asking specifically "what changed in your AI governance in the last 90 days" and requesting updated proposal methodology documentation, sub-consultant engagement disclosure, vendor processor chain updates, and AI tool inventory refreshes. The consulting boutique's documentation is dated within days of the prior engagement letter signature. The mid-engagement follow-up asks about freshness the consulting boutique's documentation cycle isn't built for. Documentation aged within 90 days is the new freshness standard. Consulting boutiques treating engagement documentation as static between proposal and renewal are now visibly behind at every mid-engagement follow-up moment. The gap widens with every client follow-up cycle. The engagement renewal is at risk at each follow-up moment.

**Mechanism:** Consulting boutique submits proposal and engagement letter (e.g., November 2025) → client sends mid-engagement follow-up at month three, month six, month nine (e.g., June 2026) → consulting boutique's documentation is 6-9 months old → follow-up asks "what changed in last 90 days" → consulting boutique has no documentation of changes in last 90 days → client procurement committee checks documentation cadence as part of engagement renewal assessment → annual engagement documentation cycle structurally behind mid-engagement follow-up cadence → engagement renewal at risk.

**Implication:** Mid-engagement documentation refresh is a discrete service. It produces client engagement documentation aged within days, refreshed quarterly, updated at sub-consultant engagement moments and vendor change moments, designed for the mid-engagement follow-up request. This is the documentation cycle the C313 boutique consulting senior partner needs and no current service delivers. The first Geneva boutique consulting service to publish a mid-engagement follow-up documentation refresh component owns the C313 mid-engagement category.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your client is now asking mid-engagement follow-ups — at month three, six, nine. Your engagement documentation was written at proposal signature. You're behind at every follow-up. I'm refreshing the proposal methodology documentation at the cadence your client actually asks."

### Finding 03: New Sub-Consultant Authorization Discontinuity — The Permanent AI Delegation Gap

**The finding:** The Geneva boutique consulting senior partner who engages a sub-consultant creates the most structurally permanent authorization chain gap in the consulting market. Sub-consultants are external contractors, not employees. The senior partner authorizes the sub-consultant's engagement through the engagement letter signature. The senior partner does NOT authorize the sub-consultant's AI tools. The sub-consultant uses AI on the client deliverable — drafting, analysis, research, modeling, methodology development — without the consulting boutique's authorization or awareness. When the deliverable goes to the client under the consulting boutique's name, the senior partner is personally accountable under nFADP Art.52 for AI use they didn't authorize, didn't know about, and can't supervise. The handover window is permanent — sub-consultant engagement has no employment-cycle boundary and no mandate-cycle boundary. The sub-consultant's tools are not in the consulting boutique's AI inventory. The sub-consultant's data flows are not in the consulting boutique's nFADP ROPA. The sub-consultant signs nothing acknowledging Art.52 exposure. nFADP Art.52 personal exposure begins when client methodology flows through AI tools the senior partner didn't authorize, not when contracts are reviewed.

The handover window also includes sub-consultant disengagements. When the engagement ends, the sub-consultant's AI tools are deactivated (sometimes), but the AI-generated client deliverable work product (drafts, analyses, models, methodology artifacts) remains in the sub-consultant's systems and in the consulting boutique's deliverable archive. nFADP Art.52 obligations continue for retained work product. The senior partner's authorization documentation does not reflect the deactivation or the retained work product.

**Mechanism:** Consulting boutique engages sub-consultant (e.g., May 2026) → sub-consultant activates their own AI tools on client methodology → senior partner unaware of specific tool use → client deliverable produced under consulting boutique's name → sub-consultant AI use invisible to client → client mid-engagement follow-up asks "what AI tools were used on this engagement, including by sub-consultants" → consulting boutique cannot produce documentation for sub-consultant AI use → engagement renewal at risk → sub-consultant disengaged at engagement end → AI-generated deliverable remains in consulting boutique's archive → senior partner's Art.52 obligations for that work product continue.

**Implication:** Sub-consultant authorization chain documentation tied to sub-consultant engagement cadence — not annual engagement cadence. The service produces authorization chain documentation refreshed at the rate of sub-consultant engagement book change, with documented sub-consultant AI tool disclosure, sub-consultant data processing agreement terms, sub-consultant supervision architecture, and documented handover for both sub-consultant engagements and disengagements. This is the discrete, named service the C313 boutique consulting senior partner needs and no competitor has built. The service is structurally parallel to the C313 Phase 01 summer associate authorization handover documentation (legal, employment-cycle boundary) and the C313 Phase 02 new institutional mandate authorization discontinuity (finance, mandate-cycle boundary), but applied to the consulting sub-consultant context where the handover window is permanent (no employment-cycle or mandate-cycle boundary).

**Ready-to-use language:** "You authorized your sub-consultant's engagement. You didn't authorize their AI tools. The deliverable goes to the client under your name. nFADP Art.52 is your personal exposure. The handover window is permanent — sub-consultants don't have employment cycles. I'm documenting the sub-consultant AI authorization chain before your client's mid-engagement follow-up asks."

### Finding 04: Proposal Management Vendor Data Residency as False Reassurance — The Sub-Processor Lens Applied to Client Methodology Confidentiality

**The finding:** The Geneva boutique consulting firm that selected a "Swiss-hosted" proposal management AI vendor on client methodology confidentiality and nFADP Art.52 grounds has a documented misconception. Article 52 is not satisfied by data residency alone, and client contractual confidentiality is not preserved by residency claims alone. The Swiss-hosted proposal management vendor's sub-processors (logging, telemetry, authentication, monitoring, parent-company cloud infrastructure) operate extraterritorially. The metadata — query classifications, client methodology identifiers, proposal draft parameters, AI conversation history with senior partners, sub-consultant coordination patterns — routes through infrastructure that doesn't honor Swiss borders. The vendor's parent-company cloud infrastructure (when non-Swiss, which is the common case for proposal management AI vendors marketed as "Swiss-hosted") inherits CLOUD Act extraterritorial access. The consulting boutique's nFADP Art.52 obligation reads through the marketing. The client's contractual confidentiality obligations read through the marketing.

**Mechanism:** Consulting boutique selects Swiss-hosted proposal management vendor on Art.52 and client confidentiality grounds → vendor's terms disclose sub-processors (extraterritorially) → client methodology metadata routes through parent-company infrastructure → consulting boutique believes Art.52 satisfied and client confidentiality preserved → exposure persists → client RFP asks vendor contract review → vendor disclosure reveals sub-processor chain → Art.52 exposure visible and client confidentiality exposure visible → consulting boutique's documented misconception is documented → client mid-engagement follow-up flags it → engagement renewal at risk.

**Implication:** Swiss-hosted proposal management vendor sub-processor audit is a discrete service. It maps the vendor's sub-processor chain (logging, telemetry, auth, monitoring, parent-company cloud) against nFADP Art.52 requirements and client contractual confidentiality obligations, identifies which sub-processors create extraterritorial exposure, and produces a sub-processor audit memo the consulting boutique can attach to its client RFP response. The service is bounded, named, and triggerable at any vendor review or new vendor selection moment.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your Swiss-hosted proposal management vendor still routes client methodology metadata through U.S. cloud infrastructure. Article 52 reads through the marketing. Client confidentiality reads through the marketing. I'm auditing the sub-processors your vendor didn't disclose at the front of the contract."

### Finding 05: Peer Proposal Methodology Visibility — The Geneva Consulting Conference Benchmark

**The finding:** The June 2026 Geneva consulting conference season (Geneva Strategy Network summer sessions, Geneva Management Consulting Council roundtables, Swiss Consulting Association workshops, IMC Switzerland events) produced public disclosures of named-firm proposal methodology patterns (with firm consent). The boutique consulting senior partner now has visibility into what specific peer firms declared in their client RFP responses and methodology descriptions — not inference from peer-group averages, but direct reading of named-firm proposal language. They are discovering their proposal was less specific, less defensible, less well-formed than what named peers produced. The senior partner is also discovering that several named peers' proposals look generic — they used the same RFP form-completion vendor tool. The consulting boutique that submitted a generic proposal is now visibly indistinguishable from the form-completion vendor user group. The comparison trap has moved from peer inference to direct comparison. The gap is visible in writing. The consulting boutique carries the visibility whether they want it or not.

**Mechanism:** Geneva consulting conference (June 2026) → named firms consent to disclosure of proposal methodology patterns → public summary published → clients, peers, and procurement consultants read each other's proposals → boutique consulting senior partner reads peer A's proposal (specific, defensible, anchored to actual methodology evidence) → senior partner reads peer B's proposal (generic, indistinguishable, likely form-completion vendor output) → senior partner reads their own proposal (generic because they used the form-completion vendor) → senior partner realizes they are in peer B's category, not peer A's → gap visible in writing → client procurement committee reads the same peer benchmarks → engagement renewal lost.

**Implication:** Peer-proposal gap analysis is a discrete service. It maps the consulting boutique's current proposal against the public benchmark, identifies specific gaps in specificity, defensibility, and formation, and produces a documented comparison the consulting boutique can use to update their proposal position before the next client RFP cycle. The service also addresses the form-completion vendor trap — it identifies whether the consulting boutique's proposal is anchored to specific proposal methodology evidence or generated through a form-completion tool (and therefore indistinguishable from other form-completion vendor users). The service is peer-benchmarked, documented, and bounded — a discrete, named C313 offering for boutique consulting.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You saw what your peer consulting boutiques declared at the Geneva consulting conference. The benchmark is now public. I'm mapping the gap between your proposal and theirs — and showing you whether your proposal is anchored to your specific methodology or generated by the same form-completion vendor the other generic proposals came from."

### Finding 06: Proposal Management Vendor Succession / Data Portability — Client Methodology Continuity Gap

**The finding:** The proposal management AI vendor may go bankrupt, get acquired by a larger player, terminate the service, or pivot the offering. When this happens, the consulting boutique's client methodology data — proposal templates, capability commitments, methodology descriptions, prior client deliverables, sub-consultant coordination patterns, AI-assisted proposal drafts in progress — transfers with the vendor's asset sale to a new controller who did not negotiate the senior partner's nFADP Art.52 obligations or the senior partner's contractual confidentiality obligations to clients. The senior partner is now in privity with a new data controller without their consent. The clients whose methodology is being processed did not consent to the new controller either. nFADP Art.52 doesn't terminate on vendor insolvency — the obligation transfers to the legal entity that holds it (the consulting boutique), and the senior partner becomes personally accountable for the client methodology's protection downstream of the asset transfer. The senior partner's contractual obligations to clients (data processing agreements, confidentiality commitments, professional secrecy terms) survive vendor exit. The clients' methodology is being processed by a successor who didn't inherit any of these obligations. This exposure is unnamed in vendor contracts, unspecified in client RFP responses, and unaddressed by any current service.

**Mechanism:** Proposal management vendor files for bankruptcy / signs asset purchase / terminates service → client methodology transfers with asset sale to new controller → new controller did not inherit consulting boutique's Art.52 obligations or contractual confidentiality obligations to clients → clients' proposal methodology, prior deliverables, AI-assisted draft history is now being processed by a successor without consent → Art.52 obligation transfers to senior partner (still personally accountable) → senior partner is professionally responsible for client methodology protection the successor isn't providing → client learns of vendor exit → client mid-engagement follow-up asks about vendor succession → consulting boutique's response now includes vendor-exit exposure the boutique cannot satisfy → engagement renewal lost → client mandate lost.

**Implication:** Proposal management vendor succession / data portability planning is a discrete service. It includes: pre-vendor-selection succession terms in vendor contracts (what happens on insolvency, acquisition, termination — including client methodology export obligations, transition period, controller-change disclosure to clients); mid-vendor-relationship succession monitoring (vendor financial signals, acquisition signals, service-termination signals); post-vendor-exit response (data portability execution, controller-change documentation, successor-controller audit, client notification architecture). The service is novel, discrete, and triggerable at any vendor exit signal.

**Ready-to-use language:** "When your proposal management AI vendor goes bankrupt or gets acquired, your client methodology transfers to the buyer. nFADP Art.52 doesn't transfer with it. Your contractual confidentiality obligations to clients don't transfer with it. Your obligations stay. I'm building the succession terms into your vendor contracts now, before the exit happens."

## C313 Core Synthesis

**The market has moved from "I already signed. Now what?" to "I thought the proposal was confidential. It isn't anymore."**

C312 captured the structural failure mode for boutique consulting: the client RFP created a documented standard at submission, and the evidence to support that standard (proposal methodology defensibility, sub-consultant delegation documentation, engagement letter AI clause architecture, retroactive methodology reconstruction, nFADP Art.52 personal exposure framing) was never built.

C313 adds six new structural conditions specific to the client RFP and engagement documentation context:

1. **RFP form-completion loop** — the AI used to answer the client RFP creates the nFADP Art.52 personal exposure the questionnaire asks about, and produces a generic response indistinguishable from other form-completion vendor users
2. **Mid-engagement follow-up questionnaire cadence** — clients now ask at month three, six, nine; engagement-letter-anchored documentation cycle; cadence gap widens at every follow-up
3. **Sub-consultant authorization discontinuity** — sub-consultant engagements create authorization chain handover gaps that are permanent (no employment-cycle or mandate-cycle boundary)
4. **Proposal management residency assumption** — Swiss hosting doesn't satisfy nFADP Art.52 personal obligations or client contractual confidentiality for proposal management vendors
5. **Peer proposal methodology visibility** — the comparison is now in writing at Geneva consulting conferences
6. **Proposal management vendor succession** — nFADP Art.52 personal obligations and client contractual confidentiality obligations survive vendor exit

The C313 service for Geneva boutique consulting is the C312 service plus six new modules. Same architecture. Twelve white spaces. Twelve liability vectors. Six deliverable trigger events (plus the C312 triggers).
