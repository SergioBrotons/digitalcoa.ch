## 6 NET-NEW Market Signals (C308 Phase 03)

### Signal 1: Client RFP AI Scoring Criteria Are Escalating Beyond Initial Screening

**The observation:** The client AI governance criteria embedded in RFPs have escalated in sophistication over the past 12 months. Initially, the criteria were simple yes/no questions about whether the firm had an AI policy. Now, institutional clients and large corporate procurement functions are asking for specific evidence: how AI tools are authorized, what supervision process exists, how errors are identified, what records are maintained per engagement.

**The complaint:** *"The first time they asked about AI governance, I wrote one sentence in our proposal. This time, they want a separate document with specific sections. I don't have that document."*

**The commercial consequence:** Boutique firms that cannot produce the specific evidence are being moved to a secondary shortlist — not necessarily excluded immediately, but disadvantaged relative to firms that can respond with documentation. The competitive disadvantage is cumulative: each lost opportunity compounds the firm's visibility in a market where institutional clients talk to each other.

**The trigger for engagement:** The managing partner receives a proposal rejection or a note from a procurement contact that mentions "AI governance documentation" — and the commercial consequence becomes concrete and personal.

**Service response:** Client RFP AI Response Sprint (3–5 day engagement to produce a client-specific AI governance response document that documents actual practice against the client's specific criteria).

### Signal 2: Carrier Attestation Renewal Is Producing More Specific Questions Than Initial Attestation

**The observation:** The carrier attestation forms arriving at renewal are more specific than the initial attestation forms from 18–24 months ago. Carriers have refined their questions based on claims experience and regulatory guidance. The renewal attestation now asks about sub-consultant AI use, client data in AI systems, proposal methodology documentation, and evidence of human authorization for AI-assisted recommendations.

**The complaint:** *"I signed the same attestation two years ago. This one has more questions. Some of them I don't know how to answer. And now I know more — which means if I sign something inaccurate, the misrepresentation risk is higher."*

**The failure mode:** The managing partner who was less informed at initial attestation is now more exposed at renewal — they understand the questions better, they understand the stakes better, and they have a clearer picture of the gap between what they certified and what they actually do. More knowledge paradoxically increases exposure if the attestation is not corrected.

**The trigger for engagement:** Renewal notice arrives with revised attestation form. The managing partner recognizes the stakes and looks for help before signing.

**Service response:** Carrier Attestation Accuracy Review (2–3 day engagement to compare what was signed against actual practice, identify misrepresentation risk, and produce corrected documentation before renewal submission).

### Signal 3: Sub-Consultant AI Use Is Creating Accountability Gaps in Client-Facing Deliverables

**The observation:** Geneva consulting boutiques routinely engage 1–3 sub-consultants on larger mandates. The sub-consultant's AI tool use is not systematically documented. The managing partner reviews the output, incorporates it into client deliverables, and signs off — without a record of what AI tools were used, how they were prompted, or what data informed the analysis. When clients ask detailed methodology questions, the managing partner cannot provide the precision the client expects.

**The complaint:** *"My sub-consultant used AI to prepare the market analysis section of the proposal. The client asked me to walk through the methodology in our meeting. I hedged — I said the analysis was thorough and based on multiple sources. The client asked a follow-up question I couldn't answer precisely. I saw the doubt in their expression."*

**The failure mode:** The trust relationship deteriorates not because the work was wrong, but because the methodology cannot be defended with precision. The client infers that the firm does not have systematic control over its work product — and the inference is correct.

**The trigger for engagement:** A specific client interaction where the managing partner felt exposed — not a general awareness of the problem, but a specific moment where the accountability gap was visible.

**Service response:** Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Framework (1-day workshop + documentation production: delegation register, use log, client disclosure protocol, bilingual EN/FR).

### Signal 4: Pitch Meeting Methodology Questions Are More Sophisticated Than Expected

**The observation:** Geneva consulting boutiques are encountering more sophisticated questions during pitch meetings. Procurement teams and client compliance functions are asking about proposal methodology with specific questions about AI use, error identification processes, supervision documentation, and client disclosure protocols. The questions are more specific than the firm's proposal documentation anticipated.

**The complaint:** *"I prepared our methodology section. I included a sentence about using AI appropriately. The client's procurement person asked me three follow-up questions I hadn't anticipated: What AI tools specifically? How do you supervise the output? How do you identify when the AI has made an error? I had good answers in my head but not in a form I could present confidently."*

**The failure mode:** The managing partner who hedges in a pitch meeting is perceived as not having systematic methodology — even if the underlying practice is reasonable. The pitch outcome is influenced by the quality of the methodology documentation, not just the quality of the underlying work.

**The trigger for engagement:** A specific pitch meeting where the methodology questions created discomfort — and the outcome is unknown but the managing partner suspects the hedging was noticed.

**Service response:** Proposal Methodology Defensibility Package (2-day engagement: methodology documentation template, disclosure checklist, defensibility review process, bilingual EN/FR).

### Signal 5: Personal nFADP Coverage Is Being Discussed in Managing Partner Peer Networks

**The observation:** Peer conversations among Geneva managing partners are increasingly including the question of personal nFADP coverage. The broker community is beginning to raise this explicitly — not just "do you have professional liability insurance" but "do you have coverage for personal FDPIC fines under Article 52." The peer network effect is spreading the awareness faster than regulatory communications do.

**The complaint:** *"At a dinner with three other managing partners, two of them mentioned that their brokers had asked about personal nFADP coverage. One of them had looked into it and found that his professional liability policy explicitly excludes administrative fines. He'd been assuming he was covered."*

**The failure mode:** The managing partner discovers the coverage gap only when a fine is imposed — and by then, the documentation that would demonstrate due diligence is absent. The personal asset exposure is live regardless of insurance coverage. Documentation is not just insurance gap-fill — it is evidence of due diligence that supports a defense against personal liability.

**The trigger for engagement:** A peer conversation where the coverage question arose — or a broker conversation where personal nFADP exposure was raised explicitly.

**Service response:** Personal nFADP Article 52 Exposure Assessment (1-day assessment: personal liability exposure report, documentation adequacy assessment, personal asset protection strategy).

### Signal 6: The "Proportionate AI Governance" Market Gap Is Creating Competitive Opportunity

**The observation:** The AI governance advisory market has a structural gap at the small Geneva consulting boutique tier. Enterprise-grade platforms, comprehensive frameworks, and multi-jurisdiction compliance tools are designed for organizations with dedicated compliance functions. A 5-person Geneva consulting boutique cannot implement these tools — and doesn't need most of what they offer.

**The complaint:** *"I spent two days evaluating AI governance platforms. Every single one assumed I had a compliance team. I'm the managing partner, the compliance function, the BD lead, and the father of two kids. I don't have a compliance team. I need something that works for my actual situation."*

**The market gap:** There is no established advisory service in Geneva specifically designed for small consulting boutiques facing the specific combination of pressures that Phase 03 ICPs face: client RFP AI gates, carrier attestation accuracy, sub-consultant delegation documentation, proposal methodology defensibility, and personal nFADP exposure. The existing options are either enterprise-grade tools the boutique can't use, or generic AI policy templates that don't address the boutique's specific risk profile.

**The trigger for engagement:** The managing partner has looked at available options and found them either inaccessible (enterprise platforms) or inadequate (generic templates) — and the search has produced a clear understanding of what is missing.

**Service response:** Proportionate AI Governance Retainer (monthly retainer, 4–6 hours/month, priority access, bilingual EN/FR, Geneva-based — covers all Phase 03 service components on an ongoing basis).

## Key Market Insight (C308 Phase 03)

The consulting boutique ICP is in **execution mode**, not discovery mode. They know AI governance matters. They've received the questionnaires and attestations. The question is no longer "do I have a problem" but "what do I do first, in what order."

The advisory response must be:
1. **Sequenced** — the ICP needs a prioritized action sequence, not a comprehensive framework
2. **Proportionate** — designed for 2–50 person firms, not scaled-down enterprise frameworks
3. **Operationally concrete** — specific documentation tools, not abstract principles
4. **Commercially urgent** — client RFP gates and carrier attestation deadlines create immediate pressure
5. **Bilingual** — Geneva market requires EN + FR capability without translation overhead

The C308 Phase 03 insight deepens the Phase 01-02 core synthesis: the Geneva accountability market requires **one evidence architecture with multiple regulatory and commercial overlays**, not four separate documentation exercises. For consulting boutiques, the four overlays are:
1. Client RFP scoring criteria (commercial gate)
2. Carrier attestation accuracy (insurance consequence)
3. Proposal methodology defensibility (professional liability)
4. Personal nFADP Article 52 exposure (personal asset protection)
