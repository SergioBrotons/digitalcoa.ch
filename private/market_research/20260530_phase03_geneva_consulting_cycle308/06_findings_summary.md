## The 6 NET-NEW Findings

### Finding 01: Client RFP AI Scoring Criteria Are Escalating Beyond Initial Screening Questions

**The finding:** Client AI governance criteria in RFPs have evolved from simple yes/no questions to specific evidence requests. Institutional clients and large corporate procurement functions now ask for: how AI tools are authorized, what supervision process exists, how errors are identified, what records are maintained per engagement. The sophistication level has increased faster than most Geneva consulting boutiques anticipated.

**Mechanism:** Client procurement function → RFP includes AI governance scoring matrix → boutique responds with generic policy statement → client scores against specific evidence criteria → boutique moves to secondary shortlist → commercial consequence concrete and immediate.

**Implication:** The client RFP AI gate is not a one-time documentation exercise — it requires a documentation architecture that can produce specific evidence against each client's specific criteria. The boutique that can respond precisely wins mandates; the boutique that cannot loses them.

**Ready-to-use language:** *"Your client sent you an RFP with AI governance scoring criteria. You have two weeks to respond. We help you document your actual practice against their specific criteria — and produce a response that is both accurate and competitive."*

### Finding 02: Carrier Attestation Renewal Produces More Specific Questions Than Initial Attestation — And More Informed ICP Means Higher Misrepresentation Risk

**The finding:** The carrier attestation forms arriving at renewal are more specific than initial attestation forms from 18–24 months ago. Carriers have refined their questions based on claims experience and regulatory guidance. The renewal ICP is paradoxically more exposed than the initial attestation ICP: more informed, more aware of the stakes, clearer about the gap between what was signed and what exists.

**Mechanism:** Initial attestation signed → ICP processes personally over time → peer conversations at bar events → ICP investigates → renewal arrives with more specific questions → ICP recognizes gap → signs anyway or delays → carrier has grounds for coverage challenge if claim arises.

**Implication:** The renewal window is the highest-value intervention point for the carrier attestation risk. The service that identifies misrepresentation risk before the ICP signs — and produces the documentation that closes the gap — prevents the coverage challenge from being viable.

**Ready-to-use language:** *"The renewal attestation is riskier than the initial attestation — you know more now. The gap between what you certified and what you actually do is where the misrepresentation risk lives. Three months before renewal is when firms call me."*

### Finding 03: Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Creates an Invisible Accountability Gap in Client-Facing Deliverables

**The finding:** Geneva consulting boutiques routinely engage 1–3 sub-consultants on larger mandates. The sub-consultant's AI tool use is not systematically documented. The managing partner reviews the output, incorporates it into client deliverables, and signs off — without a record of what AI tools were used, how they were prompted, or what data informed the analysis. When clients ask methodology questions, the managing partner cannot provide precision.

**Mechanism:** Sub-consultant uses AI → managing partner reviews output → output incorporated into client deliverable → managing partner signs off → client asks methodology question → managing partner hedges → client infers inadequate methodology control → trust relationship deteriorates → engagement at risk.

**Implication:** The sub-consultant accountability gap is invisible until it surfaces in a client interaction. The service need is a lightweight delegation register and use log designed for boutique scale — not an enterprise governance platform. The documentation prevents the client interaction problem, not just the liability problem.

**Ready-to-use language:** *"You're accountable for deliverables that incorporate AI you didn't directly control. Your sub-consultant uses AI for research and presentation. You sign the carrier attestation confirming you monitor AI use — but you don't have a monitoring system. I build the documentation system that makes the accountability chain visible."*

### Finding 04: Proposal Methodology Defensibility Has Two Dimensions — Disclosure and Traceability — And Boutique Firms Have Neither

**The finding:** Proposal methodology defensibility has two distinct dimensions that the ICP often conflates. Disclosure (did you tell the client you used AI) is necessary but not sufficient. Traceability (can you walk through the logic of the AI-assisted analysis with precision and identify errors) is equally important and is what professional liability actually turns on. Most boutiques have neither dimension covered in their proposal documentation.

**Mechanism:** Client challenges recommendation → asks about methodology → managing partner cannot walk through AI-assisted analysis with precision → client infers methodology is not systematic → professional liability exposure confirmed → carrier may challenge whether methodology documentation existed at time of recommendation.

**Implication:** The documentation that serves proposal methodology defensibility must address both dimensions. The service deliverable is a methodology documentation template that captures disclosure evidence, traceability chain, and error identification process for each AI-assisted component of a proposal.

**Ready-to-use language:** *"If a client challenges your proposal and you've used AI to build it, can you defend it? Not just 'did you disclose the AI use' — but 'can you walk through the logic and correct errors?' Methodology defensibility package: documentation that is both disclosure-ready and defensible under professional liability standards."*

### Finding 05: Personal nFADP Article 52 Exposure Is Activating Through Broker Conversations, Not Regulatory Communications

**The finding:** Managing partners in Geneva consulting boutiques are learning about their personal nFADP exposure through broker conversations, not FDPIC communications. The broker community is beginning to ask explicitly about personal nFADP coverage — and the managing partner who investigates discovers that professional liability insurance explicitly excludes administrative fines. The discovery is happening peer-to-peer and broker-to-client, not through regulatory channels.

**Mechanism:** Broker raises personal nFADP coverage question → managing partner investigates → discovers insurance exclusion for administrative fines → realizes personal assets exposed for up to CHF 250,000 → searches for documentation that demonstrates due diligence → finds no proportionate guidance for boutique scale.

**Implication:** The peer network and broker referral channel is more effective than regulatory communications for activating the personal liability motivation. The service that addresses this explicitly — and produces the documentation that constitutes evidence of due diligence — converts through broker referrals and peer recommendations.

**Ready-to-use language:** *"Article 52 makes you personally responsible as managing partner. Administrative fines up to CHF 250,000 for intentional violations — not covered by your professional liability insurance. The documentation that protects you isn't a compliance policy. It's evidence that you authorized the specific AI decisions and supervised the output. I can give you that evidence."*

### Finding 06: The "Proportionate AI Governance" Market Gap Is a Service Design Problem, Not a Product Gap

**The finding:** The AI governance market has frameworks, tools, and analysis — but no proportionate service design for small Geneva consulting boutiques. Enterprise-grade platforms, comprehensive frameworks, and multi-jurisdiction compliance tools are designed for organizations with dedicated compliance functions. A 5-person Geneva boutique cannot implement these tools — and doesn't need most of what they offer. The gap is not that the boutique can't find a product — it's that no one has designed a service around their actual operational reality.

**Mechanism:** Boutique evaluates AI governance options → enterprise platforms require compliance departments → generic templates don't address specific risk profile → managing partner concludes "nothing is designed for me" → no solution adopted → documentation gap persists.

**Implication:** The service design opportunity is to build the specific service architecture that fits the consulting boutique's operational reality: client RFP AI response sprint, carrier attestation accuracy review, sub-consultant delegation framework, proposal methodology defensibility package, personal nFADP exposure assessment, proportionate ongoing retainer. Not four products — one architecture with four overlays.

**Ready-to-use language:** *"I work with Geneva consulting boutiques on AI governance. Not enterprise platforms — specific documentation for specific problems: client RFP responses, carrier attestations, sub-consultant delegation chains, proposal methodology. Designed for a 5-person firm where you're the managing partner, the compliance function, and the BD lead."*

Building on the Phase 01-02 synthesis ("evidence of human authorization for specific AI-assisted decisions"), C308 Phase 03 reveals that the consulting boutique ICP is distinguished by **commercial gate pressure operating independently of regulatory deadlines** combined with **personal liability exposure at the managing partner level**.

The client RFP AI gate is a present commercial consequence, not a future regulatory risk. The carrier attestation renewal is an active misrepresentation risk, not a hypothetical documentation gap. The sub-consultant accountability gap is an operationally invisible exposure, not a visible process failure.

The advisory response must match this:
- Sequenced (not comprehensive)
- Proportionate (not enterprise-grade)
- Operationally concrete (not abstractly strategic)
