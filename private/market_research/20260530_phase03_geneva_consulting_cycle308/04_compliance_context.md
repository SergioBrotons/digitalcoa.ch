# Phase 03 Compliance Context — Geneva Consulting Boutique
## C308 Phase 03 (2026-05-30)

## Regulatory Landscape for Geneva Consulting Boutiques

### Overview

Geneva consulting boutiques face a distinct regulatory environment that differs from legal firms (Phase 01) and wealth management firms (Phase 02). The primary regulatory pressures are:
- nFADP (Swiss Federal Data Protection Act) — personal liability under Article 52
- Client contractual AI governance requirements — RFP scoring criteria
- Carrier insurance attestation requirements — misrepresentation risk
- Professional liability standards — proposal methodology defensibility
- EU AI Act cascade — GPAI obligations flowing to consulting firms serving EU clients

---

## nFADP — Article 52 Personal Liability (Managing Partner Level)

### The Regulatory Provision

Article 52 of the Swiss Federal Data Protection Act (nFADP) establishes **direct personal liability** for natural persons who are responsible for data processing. Managing directors and partners of small consulting boutiques fall within the scope of Article 52 as natural persons responsible for the firm's data processing operations.

The personal liability threshold is reached when:
1. The managing partner determines the purposes and means of processing (operational control)
2. Client data is processed using AI tools without adequate documentation of authorization
3. The documentation gap constitutes a violation of nFADP obligations

### Maximum Exposure

Administrative fines up to **CHF 250,000** for intentional violations. For negligent violations, fines up to CHF 125,000. These fines are imposed directly on the responsible natural person — not on the firm. Professional liability insurance covers client claims; it does not cover administrative fines imposed personally by the FDPIC.

### Geneva Consulting Boutique Specific Exposure

Small Geneva consulting boutiques typically:
- Handle client PII in the context of consulting engagements (contact details, project data, strategic information)
- Use AI tools for research, analysis, and presentation preparation
- Engage sub-consultants who use their own AI tools on client matters
- Have no systematic documentation of AI tool authorization, data flows, or human supervision

The combination of client PII handling, distributed AI use, and no systematic documentation creates a specific Article 52 exposure profile for managing partners. The exposure is active — the documentation gap exists now and creates ongoing risk.

### The Personal Asset Protection Logic

Managing partners are beginning to understand that documentation practices today are evidence of due diligence tomorrow. The documentation that protects against personal liability is not a compliance policy — it is evidence that the managing partner authorized specific AI tools for specific purposes, supervised the output, and maintained records of the authorization chain.

This is the same evidence architecture that serves carrier attestation accuracy and client RFP documentation — but the nFADP framing is distinct because it activates the "personal asset protection" motivation rather than the "insurance coverage" motivation.

## Client Contractual AI Governance Requirements

### The Commercial Gate

Large corporate clients and institutional buyers have embedded AI governance criteria into RFP scoring matrices. This is not a regulatory requirement — it is a contractual/ commercial requirement that functions as a competitive gate.

The legal basis for client AI governance requirements varies:
- EU AI Act obligations flowing through supply chain (companies subject to EU AI Act extend obligations to vendors)
- Client internal compliance requirements (larger organizations with compliance functions impose their standards on vendors)
- Sector-specific obligations (financial services, healthcare, professional services have higher AI governance expectations)

### Geneva-Specific Dynamics

Geneva consulting boutiques serve a mix of:
- Swiss corporate clients (subject to nFADP, potentially EU AI Act if serving EU markets)
- International corporate clients (subject to EU AI Act if serving EU markets, GDPR if serving EU data subjects)
- Institutional clients (family offices, pension funds, foundations with their own AI governance requirements)

The client base diversity means the consulting boutique may face AI governance requirements from multiple directions simultaneously — and may not have a clear understanding of which client's requirements apply to which engagement.

### The Documentation Requirement

Client AI governance requirements typically ask for:
1. AI tool inventory — what AI tools does the firm use
2. Authorization process — how are AI tools authorized for client work
3. Supervision documentation — how does human supervision work for AI-assisted work
4. Error identification — how are AI errors identified and corrected
5. Client disclosure — what does the client know about AI use in their engagement

The consulting boutique that cannot produce this documentation for a specific client engagement is at a competitive disadvantage in the RFP process — regardless of the quality of their underlying consulting work.

## Carrier Insurance Attestation Requirements

### The Attestation Evolution

Carrier attestation forms have evolved from simple yes/no questions about AI policy existence to specific questions about:
- Sub-consultant AI use monitoring
- Client data in AI systems
- Proposal methodology documentation
- Evidence of human authorization for AI-assisted recommendations

The evolution reflects carrier learning from claims experience and regulatory guidance development. The managing partner who signed an initial attestation 18–24 months ago is now facing a more specific renewal attestation with questions that require actual documentation — not just a policy statement.

### Misrepresentation Risk

The carrier attestation is a legal document. Signing an attestation that misrepresents actual practice creates misrepresentation risk — the carrier can deny coverage or reduce payout if the attestation did not reflect actual practice at the time of signing.

For Geneva consulting boutiques, the specific misrepresentation risk is:
- Attesting to AI governance policies that don't exist in documented form
- Attesting to sub-consultant monitoring procedures that aren't systematically documented
- Attesting to client data protection practices that don't account for AI vendor data flows

### The Renewal Window

The insurance renewal window is a distinct trigger from the initial attestation. At renewal, the managing partner has had time to investigate, has talked to peers, and arrives with more specific questions and a peer-informed sense of urgency. The renewal ICP is a different buyer than the initial attestation ICP — they need more technical language, specific documentation demands, and a clear action sequence.

## Professional Liability Standards

### Proposal Methodology Defensibility

Professional liability exposure for Geneva consulting boutiques has two dimensions:

**Dimension 1: Disclosure**
- Did the firm disclose AI use in client proposals or engagement letters
- Is the disclosure adequate given the client's reasonable expectations
- Does the engagement documentation address AI-assisted work

**Dimension 2: Traceability**
- Can the firm walk through the logic of AI-assisted analysis with precision
- Can errors in AI-assisted work be identified and corrected
- Does the firm have a defensible methodology for AI-assisted recommendations

Both dimensions are relevant to professional liability — disclosure alone does not protect against claims arising from flawed AI-assisted methodology. Traceability requires documentation that most boutiques don't have.

### The Geneva Civil Procedure Context

C308 Phase 01 identified that Geneva civil procedure has no AI disclosure protocol. This applies to consulting boutiques as well as law firms — when a consulting engagement leads to litigation, the court may ask about AI use in the engagement. No Geneva court has issued guidance on what AI disclosure is required or how it should be documented.

The absence of guidance shifts all risk to the managing partner who has not prepared — the documentation gap may itself be treated as evidence of inadequate governance if a dispute arises.

## EU AI Act Cascade

### The GPAI Obligation Flow

The EU AI Act's General-Purpose AI (GPAI) provisions create obligations for GPAI model providers that cascade to downstream users — including consulting firms that use GPAI tools in client engagements. The cascade effect means that consulting boutiques serving EU clients are within the scope of EU AI Act obligations indirectly — through their clients' obligations.

Specifically:
- If a consulting boutique's client is subject to EU AI Act (any EU-incorporated company or non-EU company serving EU markets), the client's GPAI obligations extend to their vendors, including consulting boutiques
- The client may require consulting boutiques to demonstrate GPAI tool compliance as a supply chain requirement
- The client may flow specific AI governance documentation requirements to the consulting boutique through RFP or contract terms

### The Documentation Implication

The EU AI Act cascade means that Geneva consulting boutiques serving EU clients may face AI governance requirements that are technically derived from EU regulation — even if the boutique is not directly subject to EU AI Act. The documentation requirement is the same regardless of the legal basis — evidence of human authorization for specific AI-assisted decisions.

## Compliance Documentation Requirements (Summary)

The Geneva consulting boutique needs documentation that serves multiple simultaneous requirements:

1. **nFADP Article 52 defense** — evidence of personal due diligence for managing partner
2. **Client RFP response** — evidence of AI governance maturity against specific client criteria
3. **Carrier attestation accuracy** — evidence that matches attestation language
4. **Professional liability protection** — methodology documentation for defensibility
5. **EU AI Act cascade compliance** — documentation for EU client supply chain requirements

These five requirements are served by **one coherent evidence architecture** — not five separate documentation exercises. The architecture is:
- Per-engagement documentation (sub-consultant delegation, client disclosure, methodology)
- Per-firm authorization framework (AI tool authorization, vendor management, supervision process)
- Personal due diligence record (managing partner-level documentation of authorization decisions)

## Compliance Context Summary

The Geneva consulting boutique operates in a regulatory environment where:
- Personal liability is active and uninsurable through standard professional liability policies
- Commercial gates are operating independently of regulatory deadlines
- Professional liability exposure is visible in pitch meetings before it is visible in claims
- EU AI Act obligations cascade through client relationships without direct regulatory contact
- No Geneva-specific guidance exists for AI disclosure in consulting engagements

The documentation that serves these requirements is proportionate, engagement-specific, and maintained at both the engagement level and the managing partner level. The evidence architecture is the same structure that serves legal (Phase 01) and finance (Phase 02) — but the specific documentation elements are calibrated to consulting boutique operations.
