# Phase 01 Geneva Legal — ICP (Cycle 7)
## ICP: Geneva Small Law Firm AI Governance Advisory
*Cycle 7 version — FDPIC enforcement gap documented with positive evidence*

---

## ICP Summary

**Ideal Client Profile:** Managing partners or senior equity attorneys at 2–5 attorney Geneva firms, currently using (or considering) AI tools for client matters, with no formal AI governance policy, facing nFADP obligations and upcoming EU AI Act exposure.

**Window:** ~12–18 months before FDJP consultation + market activation narrow the field.
**Key sharpening (Cycle 7):** FDPIC enforcement gap — law firms NOT yet targeted, but enforcement portfolio being built across other sectors. The gap is a timing signal.

---

## Firmographic Profile

### Target Firm Characteristics
- **Size:** 2–5 attorneys (small, manageable for boutique advisory)
- **Location:** Geneva canton, ideally central Geneva or canton periphery
- **Practice areas:** SMB-facing: employment, real estate, family, contracts, commercial — not large corporate
- **AI adoption posture:** Unofficial use (personal accounts, no firm policy) — the dominant pattern
- **nFADP exposure:** Active — processing client personal data via AI tools
- **EU AI Act exposure:** Variable — any EU client matters or EU-intended AI outputs create nexus
- **Regulatory awareness:** Low-to-medium — aware nFADP exists, uncertain about obligations
- **Decision-maker:** Managing partner (equity) — controls budget, practice decisions, risk posture
- **Pain trigger:** Regulatory deadline + enforcement signal (not abstract compliance advice)
- **Buying trigger (Cycle 7 update):** FDPIC enforcement gap closing — when FDPIC opens first law firm case, demand will spike

### Addressable Market
- Geneva bar (OFL): approximately 1,200–1,500 registered attorneys
- 2–5 attorney firms: estimated 60–70% of Geneva bar = ~720–1,050 small firms/practices
- Of these: ~40–60% likely using AI tools unofficially = ~290–630 firms with active nFADP exposure
- EU nexus subset: ~30–40% of these = ~90–250 firms with dual nFADP + EU AI Act exposure

### Not a Fit
- Large firms (10+ attorneys) — already have compliance infrastructure; different buying process
- Large corporate firms — already using large firm advisors (CMS, Schellenberg Wittmer, White & Case)
- Firms with formal AI governance policies already in place — small minority
- Attorneys with no AI use whatsoever — no immediate pain trigger

---

## Decision-Maker Profile

### Managing Partner / Senior Attorney
**Demographics:** 40–60 years old, established practice, Geneva-based, likely equity partner

**Psychographics:**
- Values professional reputation and client trust above all
- Risk-averse by professional training (bar obligations)
- Time-poor — billable hour pressure leaves little room for compliance research
- Pragmatic — wants actionable guidance, not academic frameworks
- Skeptical of large firm pricing, open to boutique advisory relationships

**Pain points:**
- Uncertainty about what nFADP obligations actually mean for their practice
- Unofficial AI use by associates and assistants — unmonitored, ungoverned
- Awareness that EU AI Act creates obligations but not knowing scope
- No Geneva-specific AI guidance from OFL — no official compliance anchor
- Feeling that AI governance is a large-firm concern — until now

**Buying trigger (Cycle 7):** FDPIC enforcement attention turning toward AI — even if no Geneva law firm case yet, the pattern is visible across other sectors. The gap is the time to act.

**Decision process:** Fast — single decision-maker, no procurement process, relationship-driven
