# Competitive Analysis: Geneva Consulting — C305 Phase 03

## What Exists in the Market vs. What's Missing

---

### What Exists

#### 1. General AI Governance Frameworks
**What:** ISO 42001, NIST AI RMF, EU AI Act compliance frameworks
**Gap:** These frameworks are designed for large enterprises deploying AI systems. They are not calibrated for 2–50 person consulting boutiques. They do not address the specific accountability architecture a managing director needs for carrier attestation or client AI governance questionnaires.

**What consulting firms experience:** "We looked at ISO 42001. It's for companies building AI systems, not companies using them. It doesn't tell me what documentation I need for a client RFP response."

---

#### 2. Legal AI Tools and Research Platforms
**What:** Harvey, Casetext, Lexis+ AI, Westlaw Edge AI
**Gap:** These address legal research and document drafting. They do not address consulting methodology documentation, sub-consultant delegation, or carrier attestation evidence. They are tool-specific, not practice-wide.

**What consulting firms experience:** "These tools help with legal research. They don't help me document how I used them or what my oversight of them looks like."

---

#### 3. Generic AI Policy Templates
**What:** Policy templates available from insurers, industry associations, and consultants
**Gap:** Templates describe what an AI policy should contain. They do not produce evidence of oversight capability. They do not address consulting-specific methodology documentation. They do not create defensible client questionnaire responses.

**What consulting firms experience:** "We have an AI policy. The carrier still asks us to attest to things our policy doesn't address."

---

#### 4. Professional Liability Carriers — AI Attestation Forms
**What:** Carrier-specific attestation forms asking about AI governance
**Gap:** The forms do not define "adequate." They do not provide a methodology for producing the evidence they assume exists. The form completion is not the same as evidence production.

**What consulting firms experience:** "We completed the carrier form. I don't know if what we wrote is adequate or what evidence the carrier actually wants."

---

#### 5. Consulting Industry Associations — AI Guidance
**What:** Various consulting industry bodies publishing AI guidance notes
**Gap:** The guidance is high-level and aspirational. It does not provide Geneva-specific regulatory context, firm-size-specific implementation, or methodology documentation standards. It does not address carrier attestation evidence requirements or client AI governance questionnaire response frameworks.

**What consulting firms experience:** "The association guidance is useful at a board level. It doesn't tell me what to do on Monday morning."

---

### What's Missing (The Positioning Opportunities)

#### Missing 01 — Consulting Methodology Provenance Documentation Standard
**The gap:** No one has defined what "documenting AI-assisted consulting methodology" means in practice. No standard exists for: which AI tools used, what data input, what methodology applied, what AI output was produced, how output was reviewed, what human judgment was applied to validate. When a client challenges a recommendation, the consulting firm has no provenance record.

**Why it doesn't exist:** It requires understanding both AI tool architecture and consulting methodology — a cross-domain expertise that neither AI vendors nor traditional consulting advisors have developed.

**Positioning opportunity:** A consulting-specific AI methodology provenance documentation standard. Distinct from legal AI documentation approaches. Specific to the consulting firm's core asset — the methodology.

---

#### Missing 02 — Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Authorization Framework
**The gap:** No framework exists for managing directors to authorize, scope, and monitor sub-consultant AI use. The existing alternatives are either "don't use sub-consultants" (operationally impossible) or "trust them completely" (creates unmitigated accountability exposure). The middle ground — documented authorization with defined scope and monitoring — does not exist as a service.

**Why it doesn't exist:** Sub-consultant AI delegation is a structural feature of boutique consulting. The accountability gap has been present for years. The market has not yet recognized it as a discrete service.

**Positioning opportunity:** A sub-consultant AI delegation framework producing: delegation authorization records, AI tool scope definitions, oversight checkpoints, and accountability documentation. Enables effective sub-consultant use while maintaining accountability visibility.

---

#### Missing 03 — Client AI Governance Questionnaire Response Methodology
**The gap:** Firms receive client AI governance questionnaires and respond without a methodology — producing ad hoc responses that become documented evidence of their governance state. There is no framework for: determining what to disclose, what level of detail is appropriate, what can be shared without revealing proprietary methodology, how to document responses defensibly.

**Why it doesn't exist:** The client AI governance questionnaire is a new phenomenon. Firms have not had time to develop a response methodology. The market has not yet offered one.

**Positioning opportunity:** A structured client questionnaire response methodology producing defensible documentation without revealing proprietary methodology. The response becomes evidence of governance quality, not governance inadequacy.

---

#### Missing 04 — Consulting-Specific Carrier Attestation Evidence Production
**The gap:** Carriers offer attestation forms without evidence production guidance. The consulting firm completes the form without producing the evidence the form architecture assumes exists. The evidence production is not the same as the form completion. The gap between form and evidence is not addressed by any carrier or advisor.

**Why it doesn't exist:** Carriers have an interest in form completion (distribution) and evidence availability (claims defense). The gap between attestation and evidence production is structurally unresolved because carriers do not want firms to know exactly what evidence would be required — that knowledge would reveal the full extent of the exposure.

**Positioning opportunity:** Evidence production methodology specifically designed for consulting firm carrier attestation architecture. Producing the evidence record that the attestation form assumes and the carrier will eventually request.

---

#### Missing 05 — Geneva Consulting nFADP Art. 63 Personal Structuring Review
**The gap:** nFADP Article 63 personal criminal liability structuring for managing directors of consulting firms. The existing legal market addresses nFADP at the corporate level (data protection officer frameworks, privacy policies, consent mechanisms). It does not address the specific personal criminal liability exposure of a managing director who has signed carrier attestations and made representations about AI oversight capability.

**Why it doesn't exist:** nFADP enforcement is new. Personal criminal liability under Article 63 is recent. The consulting firm context — personal signature on carrier attestations, methodology defensibility obligations, sub-consultant delegation — creates a specific configuration of exposure that general nFADP legal advice does not address.

**Positioning opportunity:** A personal liability structuring review for managing directors of consulting firms. Distinct from corporate nFADP compliance. Serving the managing director's personal asset and license protection interest.

---

#### Missing 06 — Proposal AI Transparency Framework for Competitive Advantage
**The gap:** No framework exists for consulting firms to proactively disclose AI use in proposals in a way that builds client trust rather than undermining confidence. Firms either hide AI use (creating documentation gaps and trust risks) or disclose it vaguely (creating uncertainty about methodology). The proactive, structured disclosure of AI use as a competitive differentiator does not exist.

**Why it doesn't exist:** The instinct in consulting is to protect methodology from disclosure. AI use is seen as something to minimize rather than something to explain. The competitive advantage of proactive AI transparency has not been articulated or packaged.

**Positioning opportunity:** A proposal AI transparency framework that enables consulting firms to describe their AI use in client proposals with appropriate detail — building trust through transparency while protecting proprietary methodology. The firm that explains its AI use credibly earns more client trust than the firm that conceals it.

## First-Mover Status

The following opportunities are still open first-mover positions:

1. **Consulting methodology provenance documentation standard** — no competitor has defined this
2. **Sub-consultant AI delegation authorization framework** — no competitor offers this as a discrete service
3. **Client AI governance questionnaire response methodology** — no competitor has packaged this as a service
4. **Carrier attestation evidence production for consulting firms** — no competitor has separated form completion from evidence production
5. **nFADP Art. 63 personal structuring for consulting managing directors** — no competitor has addressed this specifically
6. **Proposal AI transparency framework** — no competitor has framed proactive AI disclosure as competitive advantage

The Geneva consulting boutique ICP is underserved by 3–5 years relative to the legal and financial ICPs. The first-mover window is open and actively closing as client AI governance questionnaires become standard procurement practice.
