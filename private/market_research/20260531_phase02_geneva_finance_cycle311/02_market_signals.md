## The Core Market Signals — C311 Phase 02

### Signal 01: The Institutional DDQ Has Become a Competitive Gate — Not a Compliance Exercise

**What is being reported:**
The institutional client sends an AI governance DDQ as part of standard mandate review. The DDQ asks for evidence of human authorization for AI-assisted portfolio decisions. The managing director cannot produce this documentation. The institutional client's internal review notes the gap. The MD does not know what the client's internal review will conclude — but knows it is being noted. The DDQ is being treated as a material signal about the quality of the manager's governance, not merely a compliance checkbox.

**The specific failure mode:**
The boutique WM firm has been treating the institutional DDQ as a documentation exercise to complete. The institutional client is treating it as a signal about the quality of governance. These are different frames. The firm that completes the DDQ without evidence of authorization has confirmed to the client that authorization was not documented. The inability to answer is more visible than the answer would have been.

**What this means for the market:**
The institutional DDQ is the competitive intelligence document. The boutique WM firm that can read the DDQ as a design specification — and produce evidence that answers it — has a mandate retention advantage. The firm that cannot is not just non-compliant — they are visibly non-governed to their institutional client. The mandate is the commercial consequence; the DDQ is the mechanism that makes governance quality visible.

### Signal 02: The Authorization Chain Is Structurally Compound — Vendor Implementation Plus Associate Activation

**What is being reported:**
The managing director selected and authorized a portfolio management system. The system has an AI module. The AI module was implemented by the vendor and activated by an associate. Neither the vendor implementation nor the associate activation was reviewed or authorized by the managing director. The AI module has been generating recommendations that go to clients with the MD's name attached. The MD has been personally attesting to AI governance adequacy without having authorized the AI module that is generating the recommendations.

**The specific failure mode:**
The authorization chain runs through four steps: MD selects system → vendor implements AI module → associate activates module → recommendations go to clients. Steps 2 and 3 are undocumented. The MD signed the carrier attestation and the DDQ response covering AI governance — including AI use they did not know was happening and did not authorize. The authorization chain compound problem means the MD's personal liability is layered: they are accountable for AI use they didn't know about AND for AI use that was implemented and activated by others without their review.

**What this means for the market:**
The compound authorization chain is the defining liability structure for boutique WM. The MD cannot delegate their way out of it — the liability flows to them personally regardless of how the AI was activated. The documentation solution must capture the entire chain, not just the MD's authorization decision. This is architecturally different from the legal ICP problem (associate personal device use) because here the liability chain runs through a vendor and an associate simultaneously, both outside the MD's direct control.

### Signal 03: Model Drift Is Now a Live Documentation Obligation — Not a Theoretical Concern

**What is being reported:**
The boutique WM firm has been running AI-assisted portfolio construction for 12–24 months. The AI model's recommendation patterns have changed over this period. The managing director has not been monitoring for model drift. The institutional DDQ now asks for evidence of AI performance monitoring. The MD has no record of AI model performance review. The AI model's recommendations over the past 18 months — which affected client portfolios — were generated by a model that changed over time without documented human review.

**The specific failure mode:**
Model drift is not a one-time event — it is continuous. The boutique WM firm running AI-assisted portfolio construction has an obligation — under FINMA human oversight requirements and under nFADP Article 63 — to continuously monitor AI model performance and document human review of model drift. The documentation does not exist. The model has drifted. The client has received AI-influenced recommendations from a model that changed without documented human oversight. The retroactive gap is not just the initial authorization — it is the continuous operation of the AI system over the entire period.

**What this means for the market:**
Model drift monitoring is the operationalization gap that follows the authorization chain problem. The MD who now knows they need to document authorization also needs to document model performance review continuously. These are two sequential documentation obligations — both currently absent. The institutional DDQ is beginning to make the model drift documentation obligation visible. The boutique WM firm that has no documentation of AI performance review over the past 18 months has a specific, named exposure — not a vague governance gap.

### Signal 04: The Institutional Client Sets a Higher Standard Than FINMA — Competitive Gate Above Regulatory Floor

**What is being reported:**
The institutional client's AI governance questions are more specific and more demanding than anything FINMA has asked directly at boutique WM scale. The institutional DDQ asks for evidence of human authorization, model performance monitoring, data lineage documentation, and vendor AI governance review — none of which FINMA has directly audited at boutique WM level. The institutional client is setting a standard that exceeds current regulatory requirements in specific operational respects. The boutique WM firm that aligns to FINMA principles is not aligned to what their institutional clients actually require.

**The specific failure mode:**
The boutique WM firm has been preparing for FINMA requirements — which are principles-based and undefined at boutique scale — while the institutional client has been preparing a more specific operational standard. The firm is further from meeting the institutional client's standard than they are from meeting FINMA's standard. The competitive gate (institutional DDQ) is set above the regulatory floor (FINMA guidance). Preparing for FINMA alone does not prepare the firm for institutional client scrutiny.

**What this means for the market:**
The market is experiencing a standard-setting function shift. FINMA set the floor. Institutional clients are setting the operational standard. The service must read institutional DDQs as the primary design specification — not FINMA guidance. The DDQ is not a compliance exercise — it is competitive intelligence. The boutique WM firm that reads the DDQ before the institutional client sends it (through broker network intelligence) has a significant advantage over the firm that waits for the form to arrive.

### Signal 05: Carrier Attestation and Institutional DDQ Are Different Documents — Not Interchangeable

**What is being reported:**
The boutique WM firm has completed the carrier's AI attestation form — a firm-level attestation about AI governance policy and vendor management — and believes they are adequately prepared for AI governance scrutiny. The institutional client sends a separate AI governance DDQ with materially different questions. The firm discovers they have answered one document but not the other. The two documents do not cover the same ground. The firm has been treating them as equivalent.

**The specific failure mode:**
The carrier attestation asks: "Do you have an AI governance policy?" The institutional DDQ asks: "Provide evidence of human authorization for AI-assisted portfolio construction decisions in your management of our assets." These are different question types requiring different documentation architectures. The firm-level policy cannot answer the matter-level question. The firm that completed the carrier attestation without reviewing the institutional DDQ discovers the documentation gap only when the DDQ arrives — at which point the mandate is at risk.

**What this means for the market:**
The two-document problem is creating false security. The managing director who completed the carrier attestation believes they are prepared. The institutional DDQ reveals they are not. Pre-signing review of both documents — before the MD signs either — is the intervention point that prevents this failure mode. The service that reads both documents and identifies the specific documentation gaps before the MD signs is the service that provides the most value.

### Signal 06: Multi-Mandate Coherence Is Now a Visible Problem — Not a Theoretical One

**What is being reported:**
The boutique WM firm has multiple institutional clients. Each institutional client sends a version of the AI governance DDQ. The versions differ in their specific questions. The firm has been responding to each DDQ separately without a coherent underlying documentation architecture. The result: inconsistent responses across clients, evidence of different documentation levels for different mandates, and — in some cases — conflicting statements about AI use across different institutional client DDQ responses.

**The specific failure mode:**
The WM firm managing multiple institutional mandates has been producing client-specific AI governance documentation without a coherent underlying architecture. When Client A asks about the same AI system as Client B, the responses differ because they were produced at different times, by different associates, without reference to a common documentation standard. The inconsistency is now becoming visible as institutional clients share AI governance expectations with each other through industry associations and peer networks. The multi-mandate firm with incoherent AI governance documentation has a structural problem — not a documentation completion problem.

**What this means for the market:**
The multi-mandate coherence problem is specific to boutique WM and does not appear in the legal ICP. A law firm's clients do not compare AI governance documentation. Institutional WM clients do. The coherence requirement is a design constraint — the documentation architecture must produce consistent evidence across all mandates, not separate documentation exercises for each client. The service that builds one coherent evidence architecture across all mandates is the service that resolves the multi-mandate problem permanently.

### Signal 07: The Broker Channel Is the Primary Early Warning System — Not a Sales Channel

**What is being reported:**
The broker — the intermediary between the boutique WM firm and the institutional client — is the first to know that the institutional client has sent or will send an AI governance DDQ. The broker flags the DDQ to the managing director as a matter of course. The MD receives the DDQ after the broker has already contextualized it. The broker's signal is the earliest possible warning. The broker is not yet a sales channel for AI governance services — they are an intelligence channel.

**The specific failure mode:**
The broker knows the DDQ is coming before the MD does. The broker has contextualized the DDQ for the MD in casual conversation — "the client is asking AI questions now as part of standard due diligence" — without offering a solution. The MD receives the signal and decides to handle it internally or delay. The broker's early warning is not connected to a service response.

**What this means for the market:**
The broker channel is the primary intelligence source and a secondary sales channel. The service that works the broker network to provide a response when the broker flags the DDQ — "your client is asking AI questions, here's what to send and how to prepare" — is the service that the broker recommends. The broker who has a ready answer when they flag the DDQ becomes a referral source. The service that has built broker relationships as the primary intelligence channel has the earliest possible intervention point.

### Signal 08: The Boutique WM Documentation Debt Has Three Distinct Layers

**What is being reported:**
The boutique WM firm has documentation debt in three distinct layers that have accumulated simultaneously:
1. **Initial authorization** — the AI system was activated without documented authorization from the MD
2. **Continuous operation** — the AI system has been running continuously for 12–24 months without documented performance review
3. **Client-specific responses** — the DDQ responses have been produced without coherent underlying architecture

**The specific failure mode:**
Linear documentation solutions address Layer 1 only. The documentation debt is not linear — it compounds across three layers simultaneously. The firm that addresses authorization only (policy document) does not address operational monitoring (Layer 2) or multi-mandate coherence (Layer 3). The documentation debt requires a three-layer architecture, not a single policy document.

**What this means for the market:**
The three-layer documentation debt structure is the reason linear solutions fail. The market needs an evidence architecture that addresses all three layers simultaneously. The service that can diagnose which layers are missing and build a coherent response across all three is the service that provides the complete solution.
