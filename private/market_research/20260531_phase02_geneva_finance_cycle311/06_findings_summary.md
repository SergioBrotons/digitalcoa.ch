## NET-NEW Findings (Distinct from C214–C311 Phase 01)

### Finding 1: Institutional DDQ Is a Competitive Intelligence Document — Not a Compliance Exercise

**The insight:** The institutional client sends an AI governance DDQ as a competitive gate, not a compliance checkbox. The MD who reads the DDQ as intelligence — before responding — has a significant advantage over the MD who treats it as a form to complete.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "The DDQ is not a compliance form. It is a window into what your institutional client actually requires — which is consistently more specific than what FINMA has asked. Read it before you have to answer it."

**Why it matters for positioning:** The competitive intelligence function is unoccupied. No competitor reads the DDQ as market intelligence. The service that reads the DDQ before the MD has to respond — and identifies the specific documentation gaps before the mandate is at risk — is the service that the broker recommends.

---

### Finding 2: Authorization Chain Compound Liability — Vendor + Associate, Both Undocumented

**The insight:** The authorization chain runs through two undocumented steps: vendor implementation of the AI module, and associate activation. The managing director authorized the portfolio system — not the AI module within it. Both undocumented steps create personal liability for the MD who signed the attestation.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "You selected the portfolio system. The vendor implemented the AI module during setup. Your associate activated it. You didn't authorize the AI module. You are personally liable for all three steps — including the two you didn't document."

**Why it matters for positioning:** This is the compound liability structure unique to WM. The legal ICP has associate personal device problem. The WM ICP has vendor + associate compound problem — both outside the MD's direct control, both creating documented personal liability. The documentation solution must capture the entire chain, not just the MD's authorization decision.

---

### Finding 3: Model Drift Is a Live Documentation Obligation — Not a Theoretical Concern

**The insight:** The institutional DDQ now asks for evidence of AI performance monitoring. The boutique WM firm has been running AI-assisted portfolio construction for 12–24 months. The AI model's recommendation patterns have changed. The MD has no record of performance review. The client has received AI-influenced recommendations from a model that changed without documented human oversight.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "The AI model running your portfolio has been changing for 18 months. You have no documentation of what it was doing, when it changed, or what you did about it. The institutional client is now asking for evidence of monitoring. That's the exposure."

**Why it matters for positioning:** Model drift monitoring is the operationalization gap that follows the authorization chain problem. It is not a forward-only obligation — it is a retroactive one. The documentation debt includes the continuous operation of the AI system, not just its initial activation.

---

### Finding 4: The Institutional Client Sets a Higher Standard Than FINMA — and This Is the Competitive Gate

**The insight:** FINMA set the floor. Institutional clients are setting the operational standard. The boutique WM firm that aligns to FINMA principles is not aligned to what their institutional clients actually require. The competitive gate (DDQ) is set above the regulatory floor (FINMA).

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "FINMA gave you principles. Your institutional client gave you questions. The questions are more specific than the principles. Meeting FINMA's standard does not mean you're meeting your client's standard — and your client's standard is the one that keeps the mandate."

**Why it matters for positioning:** The standard-setting function has shifted. The service must read institutional DDQs as the primary design specification — not FINMA guidance. The DDQ is competitive intelligence, not a compliance exercise.

---

### Finding 5: Two Documents, Two Different Standards — and the MD Has Been Treating Them as One

**The insight:** The carrier attestation and the institutional DDQ are different documents requiring different documentation architectures. The firm-level policy cannot answer the matter-level question. The MD who completed the carrier attestation without reviewing the institutional DDQ discovers the gap only when the DDQ arrives — at which point the mandate is at risk.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "You signed the carrier attestation. You also signed the DDQ response. Are those the same thing? No. The carrier asks: 'Do you have a policy?' The institutional client asks: 'Prove you authorized the AI system on our mandate.' These are not the same question."

**Why it matters for positioning:** The two-document problem is creating false security. Pre-signing review of both documents — before the MD signs either — is the highest-value intervention point. The service that reads both and identifies the specific documentation gaps is the service that provides the most value.

---

### Finding 6: Multi-Mandate Coherence Is Now a Visible Structural Problem — Not a Documentation Gap

**The insight:** The WM firm with multiple institutional mandates has been producing client-specific AI governance documentation without a coherent underlying architecture. The results are inconsistent responses across clients — and this inconsistency is becoming visible as institutional clients share AI governance expectations with each other through industry networks.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "You're giving different answers to different clients about the same AI system. The institutional clients are comparing notes. Your documentation incoherence is becoming visible — and it is being interpreted as governance quality."

**Why it matters for positioning:** Multi-mandate coherence is a design constraint, not a documentation completion task. One evidence architecture must produce consistent evidence across all mandates — not separate documentation exercises for each client. The service that builds the coherent architecture resolves the problem permanently.
