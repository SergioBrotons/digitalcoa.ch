## The Competitive Landscape

### Layer 1: General AI Governance Advisors

**What they offer:**
- AI governance frameworks
- Policy document templates
- Compliance gap assessments
- Training programs

**What's missing for the boutique WM ICP:**
- No carrier form reading as design specification
- No institutional DDQ as competitive intelligence document
- No retroactive reconstruction methodology
- No bilingual EN/FR documentation as standard
- No personal liability framing for managing director
- No broker channel strategy
- Wrong scale (enterprise frameworks for SMB problems)

**Why they fail for the boutique WM ICP:**
The general advisor offers a framework. The managing director needs evidence. The framework does not address the specific documentation gaps that the institutional DDQ exposes. The general advisor does not read the carrier form or the institutional DDQ before recommending a solution. The boutique WM firm that engages a general AI governance advisor produces a policy document — not evidence of human authorization for specific AI-assisted decisions.

### Layer 3: Big Four and Mid-Tier Accounting Firms

**What they offer:**

But they do not yet have the methodology for retroactive reconstruction or carrier form pre-signing review.

### Layer 5: Technology Vendors (Portfolio Management Systems)

**What they offer:**

**What's missing for the boutique WM ICP:**
- No authorization chain documentation
- No liability allocation for AI-influenced investment decisions
- No carrier form review
- No institutional DDQ response preparation
- No ongoing monitoring documentation
- No vendor contract terms that protect the MD from AI governance liability

**Why they fail for the boutique WM ICP:**
The technology vendor benefits from AI adoption — more AI use means more system utilization. The vendor has no incentive to document the authorization chain that would expose the MD's liability. The vendor contract is typically silent on AI governance documentation obligations and record production for regulatory or client scrutiny. The vendor is the source of the problem (AI module activation) and simultaneously not a solution to the documentation obligation.

## The Six White Spaces (What's Missing in the Market)

### White Space 1: Pre-Signing Carrier Form Review

**The opportunity:**
No competitor offers pre-signing review of carrier AI attestation forms as a discrete service. The market offers frameworks after the form is signed. No one reads the form before the MD signs it and tells them exactly what they are committing to.

**The service design:**
- Receive the carrier form before renewal
- Read every question as a design specification for documentation
- Identify exactly what documentation is required and where it doesn't exist
- Deliver a two-page memo: "Here's what the form requires, here's where your documentation stands, here's what you need before you sign"
- Price: CHF 1,100–1,650 (bounded, specific, pre-signing)

**Why it cannot be commoditized:**
The carrier form changes every renewal cycle. Each iteration asks for more specific evidence. The service requires ongoing reading of each new form version. The competitor who builds this as a standard service owns the highest-value intervention point in the market — before the MD signs, before the documentation commitment is made.

---

### White Space 2: Institutional DDQ as Competitive Intelligence Document

**The opportunity:**
No competitor reads the institutional DDQ as a competitive intelligence document. The market treats it as a compliance exercise to complete. The intelligence opportunity: the DDQ reveals what the institutional client actually requires, which is consistently more specific than FINMA guidance.

**The service design:**
- Build a broker intelligence network (broker is primary early warning for DDQ arrival)
- Read the DDQ before the MD has to respond
- Identify documentation gaps against the DDQ's specific questions
- Produce evidence that answers the DDQ before the deadline
- Price: CHF 2,200 for DDQ response preparation + evidence production

**Why it cannot be commoditized:**
Reading the DDQ as intelligence requires understanding the institutional client's standard-setting function — which is not publicly documented. The service that has built broker relationships and has a library of institutional DDQ versions across clients has a proprietary intelligence advantage. The competitor that treats the DDQ as a compliance form cannot replicate the intelligence function.

---

### White Space 3: Retroactive Reconstruction for Boutique WM

**The opportunity:**
No competitor offers retroactive reconstruction methodology for boutique WM documentation debt. The market offers forward-looking frameworks. The majority of the problem is retrospective — 12–24 months of undocumented AI use that must be addressed before the next institutional DDQ response.

**The service design:**
- Diagnose the three-layer documentation debt structure (authorization, continuous operation, multi-mandate coherence)
- Establish what can and cannot be reconstructed credibly
- Produce a reconstruction methodology memo describing what happened, when, and what evidence exists
- Build forward documentation architecture that connects to the reconstruction
- Price: CHF 3,300–5,500 depending on documentation debt depth

**Why it cannot be commoditized:**
Retroactive reconstruction requires judgment about what can be credibly documented versus what cannot. The methodology is not a template — it is a diagnostic process that produces a specific credibly documentable history. The competitor that offers a template cannot replicate the judgment required to determine what is credibly reconstructable versus what is not.

---

### White Space 4: Broker Channel as Primary Acquisition Path

**The opportunity:**
No competitor has built a broker referral program for AI accountability services. The broker is the primary early warning system — they know when the institutional client sends a DDQ before the MD does. The broker is currently an intelligence channel with no service to refer to.

**The service design:**
- Build broker relationships as the primary intelligence network
- Provide brokers with a specific, named service to refer when they flag the DDQ
- Provide brokers with a briefing on what the institutional DDQ requires so they can contextualize for the MD
- Create a referral relationship that makes the broker the trusted advisor on AI governance questions
- Price: referral fee or structured partnership

**Why it cannot be commoditized:**
The broker relationship is built on trust and repeated intelligence sharing. The competitor that builds broker relationships owns the earliest possible intervention point. The broker who has a trusted referral for AI governance questions becomes a permanent channel — not a one-time referral. Building broker relationships takes sustained intelligence sharing, not a marketing campaign.

---

### White Space 5: Bilingual EN/FR Documentation as Standard Product

**The opportunity:**
No competitor offers bilingual EN/FR AI governance documentation as a standard product in the Swiss market. English-only documentation is the market norm. Geneva requires EN/FR — the carrier form is in English, the regulatory proceedings are in French, the institutional client communications are often in English, the staff conversations are in French.

**The service design:**
- Produce all documentation in bilingual EN/FR as standard
- Design documentation architecture that operates in both languages simultaneously
- Build the evidence system so that French regulatory scrutiny and English institutional client scrutiny are both addressed
- Price: included in evidence architecture service (not a premium)

**Why it cannot be commoditized:**
Bilingual documentation is a design requirement, not a translation task. The documentation architecture must be built to function in both languages simultaneously — not translated after the fact. The competitor that translates English documentation into French produces a different document, not a bilingual one. The service that designs for bilingual from the beginning has a structural advantage that cannot be replicated by adding a translation step.

---

### White Space 6: Multi-Mandate Coherence Documentation Architecture

**The opportunity:**
No competitor offers a documentation architecture designed for multi-mandate WM firms that produces consistent evidence across all institutional client relationships. The market treats each institutional DDQ response as a separate exercise.

**The service design:**
- Build one underlying evidence architecture for the WM firm
- Produce consistent documentation across all mandates
- Ensure that Client A and Client B receive coherent evidence about the same AI systems
- Design the architecture so that new mandates integrate into the existing system
- Price: included in evidence architecture service

**Why it cannot be commoditized:**
Multi-mandate coherence requires architectural design, not documentation completion. The evidence architecture must be built so that each new institutional DDQ response references the same underlying documentation system. The competitor that produces separate documentation for each client cannot replicate the coherence function — they are producing documents, not a system.

## Competitive Response Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Big Four Launches SMB AI Governance Practice
- **Threat level:** Low near-term (pricing model mismatch, SMB delivery infrastructure absent)
- **Response:** Emphasize personal liability focus, broker channel, proportionate service design
- **Key differentiator:** The Big Four will offer frameworks; Sergio produces evidence

### Scenario 2: Mid-Tier Accounting Firm Adds AI Governance Specialist
- **Threat level:** Medium near-term (existing client relationships, one hire, SMB pricing capacity)
- **Response:** Build broker channel exclusivity, carrier form pre-signing review as specialized capability
- **Key differentiator:** The accounting firm does not have retroactive reconstruction methodology or institutional DDQ intelligence

### Scenario 3: Legal Firm Adds AI Documentation Practice
- **Threat level:** Low near-term (legal practice culture, no documentation architecture expertise)
- **Response:** Emphasize documentation architecture versus legal opinion, bilingual capacity, ongoing monitoring
- **Key differentiator:** The legal firm advises on liability; Sergio produces evidence that prevents liability from arising

### Scenario 4: Technology Vendor Adds AI Governance Documentation
- **Threat level:** Low (vendor conflict of interest, no incentive to document MD liability)
- **Response:** Emphasize vendor contract review, independent position, carrier form reading
- **Key differentiator:** The vendor benefits from AI adoption; Sergio protects the MD from AI liability
