# Phase 02 — Geneva Finance (Boutique Wealth Management) — Market Signals
## C302 Cycle | 2026-05-30 | Operator Agent

## Market Signals

### NET-NEW Findings (C302 Phase 02)

####1. Institutional Client AI Governance Questionnaires — New Primary Trigger
**Signal:** Corporate clients, family offices, endowments, and institutional investors are now sending detailed AI governance questionnaires to Geneva wealth managers as part of standard vendor due diligence.

**Evidence:**
- Institutional mandates (RFPs) now include AI governance as a scored evaluation criterion
- Questionnaires ask about: AI tool inventory, authorization records, data access controls, decision attribution, vendor DPAs, nFADP compliance
- 2-week response deadlines create time-bounded urgency
- Wealth managers receiving these questionnaires have no standardized response framework

**Implication:** Institutional client pressure is now the primary conversion trigger for finance ICP, replacing abstract regulatory concern. The trigger is external and time-bounded — harder to ignore than regulatory deadlines.

**Language pattern:** "We got a 40-question AI questionnaire from our biggest client. We had 2 weeks."

---

#### 2. Carrier Attestation v4 — Evidence Standard Shift (Finance Vertical)
**Signal:** Insurance carriers serving wealth management firms have shifted from simple attestation to evidence-based documentation requirements. Form v4 asks for "last three authorization records" per AI tool.

**Evidence:**
- Carrier forms now require: when each AI tool was authorized, who approved it, what client data it accesses, sub-processor chain
- Documentation debt is now a carrier underwriting issue, not just a compliance issue
- Wealth managers realizing that attestation ≠ compliance

**Implication:** Carriers are functioning as de facto enforcement agents. The shift to evidence standard mirrors what C302 Phase 01 found in the legal vertical — but in finance, the enforcement vector is insurance carriers, not bar associations.

**Finance-specific dimension:** Wealth managers need AI governance documentation for two separate accountability frames — regulatory (FINMA/nFADP) and insurance (carrier attestation). These require different documentation formats but share the same underlying evidence gap.

---

#### 3. Institutional Mandate AI Scoring — Competitive Pressure Active
**Signal:** Geneva wealth managers competing for institutional mandates (CHF 100M–500M+) are being scored on AI governance readiness in RFP evaluations.

**Evidence:**
- AI governance frameworks listed as scored criteria in institutional RFPs
- Wealth managers without documented frameworks at competitive disadvantage
- RFP deadlines create time-bounded conversion windows

**Implication:** The competitive dimension adds urgency beyond regulatory compliance. A firm without AI governance documentation doesn't just face regulatory risk — it loses institutional business to a competitor who has it.

**NET-NEW finding:** This is distinct from legal vertical. In legal, the enforcement is court sanctions and bar disciplinary events. In finance, the enforcement is market access (winning/losing institutional mandates).

---

#### 4. Decision Attribution Gap — Finance-Specific Consequence
**Signal:** Wealth managers cannot reconstruct which AI system made or significantly influenced which client investment decision. This is the named primary exposure for the finance ICP.

**Evidence:**
- AI-driven portfolio rebalancing without human review
- Algorithmic suitability scoring for financial products
- Automated risk profiling determining advisory approach
- AI-generated investment recommendations without documented human approval

**Why it's finance-specific:**
- Investment decisions have direct financial consequences for clients
- A client who suffers loss can challenge: was this decision made by a human or an AI?
- The managing director cannot answer this question
- Retroactive reconstruction is structurally impossible — systems were never built to capture this

**Implication:** The decision attribution gap is both a regulatory exposure (FINMA suitability requirements) and a legal exposure (client liability). It is the highest-priority named problem for the finance ICP.

---

#### 5. nFADP Art. 63 Personal Liability — Managing Director Wake-Up Call
**Signal:** Managing directors and partners at boutique wealth management firms are waking up to personal liability under nFADP Article 63 for AI-related data breaches.

**Evidence:**
- Art. 63 establishes personal liability for the responsible person (typically managing director or partner)
- FDPIC May 8, 2025 explicit confirmation: nFADP applies directly to AI-based data processing
- Wealth managers increasingly aware that personal assets are at risk

**Finance-specific dimension:**
- Wealth management firms process highly sensitive client data (financial position, net worth, family circumstances)
- AI tools processing this data create amplified breach risk
- A data breach involving AI tool vendor data sharing could trigger Art. 63 personal liability

**Implication:** Personal liability is the emotional trigger that converts abstract concern into action. The managing director needs a solution that protects them personally, not just the firm institutionally.

---

#### 6. 50–200 Employee Boutique — The Compliance Gap Between Small and Large
**Signal:** Mid-size Geneva wealth management firms (50–200 employees) face the same AI governance obligations as large institutions, but without large-institution compliance resources.

**Evidence:**
- FINMA 08/2024 applies to all supervised institutions regardless of size
- nFADP Art. 5(c) and Art. 26 apply to all firms using AI tools
- Institutional clients apply the same due diligence standards to boutiques as to large banks
- Big 4 / large law firm advisory fees are structured for large institutions, not 50–200-person boutiques

**The gap:** Boutique wealth managers need institutional-grade AI governance documentation at a price point and complexity level designed for their firm size. No one is serving this gap specifically.

**Implication:** This is the addressable market gap — a boutique advisory firm offering AI governance services to mid-size Geneva wealth managers (50–200 employees) at an appropriate engagement scale.
