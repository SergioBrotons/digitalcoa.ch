## ICP: Geneva Boutique Wealth Manager (50–200 Employees)

### Vertical Definition
Boutique wealth management firms and independent asset managers,50–200 employees, Geneva-based. Managing director or partner personally accountable. Serving high-net-worth individuals, families, and institutional clients. Using AI tools for portfolio analysis, client communication, risk modeling, and administrative automation.

### Firm Profile

| Attribute | Typical Profile |
|---|---|
| **Employees** | 50–200 |
| **AUM** | CHF 500M–5B+ |
| **Client type** | HNWI, UHNWI, family offices, institutional clients |
| **Geneva presence** | Rue du Rhône, Cours de Rive, La Praille, Chêne-Bougeries |
| **Technology stack** | Bloomberg, Charles River / Aladdin (institutional grade), AI SaaS |
| **AI adoption** | 91% using external AI SaaS tools (FINMA 08/2024 signal) |
| **Regulatory framework** | FINMA Circ. 2018/3, FINMA 08/2024, FINMA 2025/02, nFADP, FinSA |
| **Key decision-maker** | Managing Director, Partner, CEO, CIO |
| **Personal exposure** | nFADP Art. 63 personal liability for managing director/partner |

### Addressable Firm Types

1. **Mid-size boutique WM** — CHF 500M–2B AUM, 50–100 employees, independent
2. **Multi-family offices** — CHF 1B–5B AUM, 50–150 employees, Geneva-based
3. **Independent asset managers (IAMs)** — affiliated with AOOS or independent,50–200 employees
4. **Wealth planning and advisory** — estate planning, tax advisory, 50–100 employees

### ICP Pain Points (Finance-Specific)

1. **Institutional client AI governance questionnaires** — corporate clients, family offices, and institutional investors sending detailed AI due diligence questionnaires before engaging a wealth manager
2. **Carrier attestation requirements** — insurance carriers requiring AI governance evidence before binding coverage or renewing policies
3. **FINMA AI governance expectations** — FINMA 08/2024 operative since December 2024; supervisory scrutiny increasing
4. **nFADP Art. 63 personal liability** — managing director or partner personally liable for AI-related data breaches
5. **Decision attribution gap** — cannot reconstruct which AI system made or influenced which client decision
6. **Institutional client due diligence** — increasingly required before winning mandates

### How the ICP Talks About AI Problems

**Framing A: "We got an AI questionnaire from our biggest client"**
*"Our largest client sent us a 40-question AI governance questionnaire as part of their vendor due diligence. We had2 weeks to respond. We didn't know what half the questions meant. We had to say 'we're working on it.'"*

- This is the primary trigger for action
- Institutional clients (corporate treasuries, family offices, endowments) now include AI governance in standard due diligence
- Time-bounded response requirement creates urgency
- No internal resource to answer the questions accurately

**Framing B: "Our carrier is asking for evidence, not attestations"**
*"Last year the carrier attestation was a checkbox. This year they want supporting documentation — when was each AI tool authorized, who approved it, what data does it access. We can't produce that."*

- Carrier form v4 has shifted from attestation to evidence standard
- Insurance carriers are now the enforcement vector for AI governance
- Wealth managers are realizing attestation ≠ compliance

**Framing C: "I'm personally liable for something I don't understand"**
*"Under nFADP Article 63, I'm personally responsible. But I don't know what AI tools my team is using, what data they access, or who approved them. The junior people set this up."*

- Personal liability under nFADP Art. 63 is the wake-up call
- Accountability gap between senior decision-maker and technical implementation
- No one has bridged this gap with a tool or framework designed for their situation

**Framing D: "We need to win this institutional mandate"**
*"We're competing for a CHF 200M mandate. The RFP includes AI governance as a scored criterion. We don't have a documented AI governance framework. Our competitor does."*

- Institutional mandates now include AI governance scoring
- Competitive disadvantage for firms without documented frameworks
- Time-bounded opportunity (RFP deadline creates urgency)

**Framing E: "We can't reconstruct our own decisions"**
*"If a client challenges an investment decision, we need to show who made it — human or AI. We can't. Our systems were never designed to capture this."*

- Decision attribution is the named primary exposure
- Historical documentation debt is structural, not procedural
- Retroactive reconstruction is impossible

## Language Markers (How They Speak)

**What they say they need:**
- "AI governance framework"
- "Compliance with FINMA AI guidance"
- "nFADP compliance for AI tools"
- "Evidence of AI controls"

**What they actually mean:**
- A document that satisfies institutional client questionnaires
- Proof they can show to carriers or auditors
- Something that protects the managing director personally
- A competitive answer to the RFP scoring criteria

**Words they use naturally:**
- Attestation, due diligence, questionnaire, evidence, documentation
- FINMA, nFADP, Article 63
- Portfolio management, risk, compliance
- Vendor, data processing agreement, sub-processor

**Words they avoid:**
- AI governance (too abstract until they have the problem)
- DPIA (too technical, compliance-team language)
- Explainability, model risk (academic, not practitioner language)

**Emotional state when they call:**
- Alarmed by a specific event (client questionnaire, carrier form, RFP)
- Confused about what "compliant" actually means
- Concerned about personal liability
- Time-pressured (2-week deadline from institutional client)
