## Key Insights

### NET-NEW Findings (C302 Phase 02 — Finance Vertical)

**1. Institutional Client AI Governance Questionnaires — New Primary Conversion Trigger**
Corporate clients, family offices, endowments, and institutional investors are now sending detailed AI governance questionnaires to Geneva wealth managers as part of standard vendor due diligence. These questionnaires have2–4 week response deadlines and ask about AI tool inventory, authorization records, data access controls, decision attribution, vendor DPAs, and nFADP compliance. Institutional mandates (RFPs) now include AI governance as a scored evaluation criterion. The competitive dimension creates urgency independent of regulatory enforcement — a firm without documented AI governance loses institutional mandates to a competitor who has it.

**2. Carrier Attestation Form v4 — Evidence Standard Active in Finance Vertical**
Insurance carriers have shifted from simple attestation to evidence-based documentation requirements. Form v4 asks for "last three authorization records" per AI tool, including when each was authorized, who approved it, what data it accesses, and sub-processor chain. Wealth managers realizing that attestation ≠ compliance are seeking evidence management solutions. The carrier is functioning as a de facto enforcement agent — parallel to how bar associations function in the legal vertical.

**3. Decision Attribution Gap — Named Primary Exposure for Finance ICP**
The inability to reconstruct which AI system made or influenced which client investment decision is the named primary exposure for wealth management firms. AI-driven portfolio rebalancing, algorithmic suitability scoring, automated risk profiling, and AI-generated investment recommendations all create decision attribution requirements. A client who suffers loss can challenge: was this human or AI? The managing director must be able to answer. Retroactive reconstruction is structurally impossible — systems were never built to capture this data.

**4. nFADP Art. 63 Personal Liability — The Managing Director Wake-Up Call**
Managing directors and partners at boutique wealth management firms face personal liability under nFADP Art. 63 for AI-related data breaches. The "responsible person" designation means personal assets at risk, not just institutional liability. Wealth management firms process the most sensitive personal data category: financial position, net worth, family circumstances, inheritance plans. A breach involving AI tool vendor data sharing is the highest-risk scenario. The emotional trigger is personal, not corporate — converting abstract concern into action faster than regulatory awareness alone.

**5. 50–200 Employee Boutique — The Compliance Gap Between Small and Large**
Mid-size Geneva wealth management firms (50–200 employees, CHF 500M–5B AUM) face the same AI governance obligations as large institutions but without large-institution compliance resources. They face institutional client due diligence, carrier attestation evidence requirements, FINMA 08/2024 operative obligations, and nFADP Art. 63 personal liability — at a firm size that cannot absorb big4 engagement models. This is the addressable market gap: institutional-grade AI governance documentation at a price point and complexity level designed for mid-size boutique firms.

**6. Finance vs. Legal Vertical — Distinct Enforcement Vectors**
The finance ICP differs from the legal ICP in its enforcement vectors. Legal vertical enforcement is court sanctions and bar disciplinary events (C302 Phase 01). Finance vertical enforcement is institutional client RFP scoring and carrier attestation requirements. Both verticals share the evidence reconstruction problem, the junior-senior fluency inversion, and the vendor contract trap. But the conversion trigger and the urgency framing are distinct: legal ICP acts before peer disciplinary events; finance ICP acts before losing an institutional mandate or failing a carrier attestation.

## Positioning Statement

**The market offers frameworks. We produce evidence.**

The distinction: most advisory firms offer a framework — a document that describes what should exist. What the finance ICP actually needs is evidence — documentation that shows what exists, when it was authorized, who approved it, and how decisions were made.

The institutional client questionnaire doesn't ask for a framework. It asks for records. The carrier form doesn't ask for an attestation. It asks for evidence. The managing director under Art. 63 doesn't need a policy. They need proof of oversight.
