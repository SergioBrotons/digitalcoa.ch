# The Boutique Wealth Manager's AI Governance Readiness Checklist
## Self-Assessment Edition — Finance Vertical

*Originally developed for legal. Adapted for wealth management firms facing institutional client AI governance questionnaires, carrier attestation requirements, and FINMA AI governance obligations.*

---

## How I Think About This Problem

I've spent years watching financial institutions deal with regulatory change. The pattern is always the same: a new obligation arrives, firms scramble to understand what it means, and the ones who move fastest with the clearest documentation fare better than those who wait for clarity that never comes.

AI governance is different. The obligation isn't coming — it's already here. FINMA Guidance 08/2024 has been operative since December 2024. The question isn't whether you need an AI governance framework. It's whether you can produce one when an institutional client asks, a carrier requires evidence, or FINMA opens a supervisory review.

Most boutique wealth managers I've spoken with are in the same position: AI tools are in use, the team is comfortable with them, but no one has documented when each tool was authorized, who approved it, what data it accesses, or how decisions made or influenced by AI are attributed to a human.

That documentation gap is the problem. And it's more urgent than most firms realize.

---

## Section 1: Your AI Tool Inventory

*Are you tracking every AI tool that touches client data?*

An institutional client AI governance questionnaire will ask you to list every AI system in use. Most firms can't produce this list accurately. The AI tools were adopted incrementally — a portfolio analytics add-on, a CRM with AI scoring, an AI meeting summary tool — without a central record of what exists.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Charles River / Aladdin / Bloomberg AI components (if applicable)
- [ ] InvestGlass AI / Additiv / Altoo / Neurons Lab / Nevis (if applicable)
- [ ] CRM with AI-driven client scoring or segmentation
- [ ] Portfolio analytics with AI-powered risk modeling
- [ ] Automated rebalancing tools
- [ ] AI meeting summaries or client email generation tools
- [ ] Third-party AI APIs integrated into your workflows
- [ ] Document management with AI search

**The institutional client question:** "Please provide your complete AI tool inventory, including date of first deployment, authorization records, and data access scope for each system."

If you cannot produce this list with confidence, you have an inventory gap.

---

## Section 2: Automated Decision-Making — Art. 5(c) Exposure

*Is any AI system making or significantly influencing decisions with legal effect on your clients?*

Under nFADP Article 5(c), when an AI system makes or significantly influences a decision with legal effect on a client, specific obligations apply: the client must be informed, and they have the right to request human review.

In a wealth management context, this covers:
- AI-driven portfolio rebalancing without human review
- Algorithmic suitability scoring for financial products
- Automated risk profiling determining advisory approach
- AI-generated investment recommendations without documented human approval

**Checklist:**
- [ ] AI-driven portfolio rebalancing (fully automated or threshold-based)
- [ ] Algorithmic suitability scoring for financial products
- [ ] Automated risk profiling determining advisory approach
- [ ] AI-generated investment recommendations without human review
- [ ] AI-driven client segmentation affecting service levels or fees

**If YES to any:** nFADP Art. 5(c) obligations apply. You need client notification documentation and a documented process for human review upon request.

**The carrier attestation question:** "For each AI system that makes or significantly influences decisions with legal effect on clients, please provide: client notification records, human review process documentation, and evidence of authorization."

---

## Section 3: Vendor Due Diligence — Art. 26 ADMD + FINMA 08/2024

*Do you have signed Data Processing Agreements with all AI vendors?*

Before deploying AI tools that process client data, nFADP Art. 26 requires you to inform clients about the AI tool's nature and purpose, document the legal basis for data processing, and ensure vendor DPAs are in place.

FINMA Guidance 08/2024 requires the same documentation as part of your AI governance framework: risk classification, accountability definition, and incident reporting for each vendor.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Data Processing Agreement signed with each AI SaaS vendor
- [ ] Sub-processor restrictions documented (vendor's vendors)
- [ ] Breach notification procedures defined in vendor contracts
- [ ] Data retention and deletion terms agreed
- [ ] AI vendor risk classified in your AI inventory (FINMA 08/2024 requirement)
- [ ] Material AI incident reporting process documented

**The institutional client question:** "Please provide signed DPAs with all AI vendors processing client data, including sub-processor chain documentation."

**The carrier attestation question:** "Please provide evidence of vendor due diligence for all AI tools, including DPA terms, breach notification procedures, and data retention documentation."

---

## Section 4: Decision Attribution — Can You Reconstruct Who Made Each Decision?

*Can you show, for any client investment decision, whether it was made by a human or an AI — and who was responsible?*

This is the primary exposure for wealth management firms. If a client suffers loss and challenges the decision process, the managing director must be able to answer: was this human or AI? Who approved it?

Most firms cannot answer this question. Their systems were never built to capture decision attribution.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Authorization records maintained for each AI tool deployment
- [ ] Human oversight documented for AI-influenced decisions
- [ ] Decision log or audit trail for AI-assisted portfolio decisions
- [ ] Accountability assigned per AI application (FINMA 08/2024 requirement)
- [ ] Process for documenting when human judgment overrides AI recommendation

**The institutional client question:** "Please describe your decision attribution framework. For any client portfolio decision, can you identify: who made the decision, whether AI was involved, and who bears accountability?"

**If you cannot produce this:** You have a decision attribution gap. This is the highest-priority documentation gap for wealth management firms.

---

## Section 5: Institutional Client AI Governance Questionnaires

*Have you received an AI governance questionnaire from an institutional client? Are you prepared to respond?*

Institutional clients — corporate treasuries, family offices, endowments, institutional investors — are now sending detailed AI governance questionnaires to wealth managers as part of standard vendor due diligence. These questionnaires ask about AI tool inventory, authorization records, data access controls, decision attribution, vendor DPAs, and nFADP compliance.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Institutional client AI governance questionnaire received (or expected)
- [ ] Response framework drafted or in development
- [ ] AI tool inventory ready to submit
- [ ] Authorization records available for each AI tool
- [ ] Vendor DPA documentation ready to submit
- [ ] Decision attribution framework documented
- [ ] nFADP Art. 5(c) compliance evidence prepared
- [ ] Response deadline mapped (typically 2–4 weeks)

**The urgency:** Institutional mandates now include AI governance as a scored RFP criterion. A firm without documented AI governance loses mandates to a competitor who has it.

---

## Section 6: Personal Liability — nFADP Art. 63

*Are you personally exposed under nFADP Article 63?*

As managing director or partner, you are likely the "responsible person" under nFADP Art. 63. Personal liability for AI-related data breaches means your personal assets are at risk — not just the firm's institutional liability.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Art. 63 responsible person role understood and documented
- [ ] AI governance framework protects personal liability exposure
- [ ] Vendor AI tool risk assessed for personal liability implications
- [ ] AI incident response plan documented (protects you personally)
- [ ] Evidence of personal oversight documented (shows due diligence)

**The question I hear most:** "I'm personally liable for something I don't fully understand. The junior people set up the AI tools. I need to know what's in place, who approved it, and what could go wrong — before something does."

## What This Means for You

**If you scored 0–2:** Early stages. AI tools likely in use informally. Regulatory exposure exists but not yet formalized. The institutional client questionnaire or carrier attestation is likely your first wake-up call.

**If you scored 3–5:** Developing. Core AI governance structures forming. Key gaps likely in vendor DPAs, decision attribution, and Art. 5(c) client notification. These are the highest-priority gaps to close.

**If you scored 6+:** Advanced. Formal AI governance in place. The institutional client questionnaire is manageable. The 2026 regulatory wave (AMLA/LETA/CARF) is the next horizon.

## The Underlying Problem

The documentation gap isn't a compliance problem. It's an evidence problem.

Compliance means you have a framework. Evidence means you can show when each AI tool was authorized, who approved it, what data it accesses, and how decisions were made.

The market offers frameworks. What firms actually need is evidence.

If you're facing an institutional client AI governance questionnaire, a carrier attestation with evidence requirements, or a personal liability exposure you can't document — the first step is a clear picture of what exists.
