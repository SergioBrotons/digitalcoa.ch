## Competitive Analysis (C312 Phase 04)

### The Competitive Landscape for Geneva Healthcare AI Accountability

The Geneva healthcare vertical (2–10 physicians, outpatient primary care and specialist practices) has a distinct competitive landscape from legal, finance, and consulting. The healthcare competitive map has five layers:

1. **Medical association guidance** (FMH, AMGe) — publishes principles, not protocols
2. **Swissmedic** — regulates devices, not operator practice management
3. **Insurance brokers** — complete carrier attestation forms, no evidence architecture
4. **Medical technology vendors** — certify tools, not physician practice management
5. **General AI governance consultants** — frameworks, not physician-personal evidence

None of these five layers address the C312 healthcare evidence architecture components.

---

### Layer 1: Medical Association Guidance (FMH, AMGe)

**What they offer:** FMH publishes the principle of human oversight without operational protocol. AMGe publishes continuing education content on AI tools. Neither addresses physician-personal evidence architecture.

**The gap:** FMH's human oversight principle is stated. The operational protocol is absent. AMGe's CME content covers AI tool adoption, not evidence documentation. Neither gives the physician a documentation system they can present to a carrier or Swissmedic inspector.

**C312 NET-NEW gap:** FMH and AMGe have not published specialty-specific AI authorization chain documentation guidelines. The carrier form now asks for this. No professional association has issued guidance on what specialty-specific AI authorization documentation looks like in practice.

**C312 NET-NEW gap:** FMH and AMGe have not addressed the patient consent architecture for AI-assisted diagnostic decisions. No Geneva medical association has published consent form language or response protocols for the patient question "was AI involved in my diagnosis?"

---

### Layer 2: Swissmedic

**What they offer:** Swissmedic regulates medical devices, including AI-enabled diagnostic tools. Vendors obtain Swissmedic certification for their tools. Swissmedic defines operator obligations for physicians who use certified devices.

**The gap:** Swissmedic does not publish operator classification opinions for individual physicians. Swissmedic does not audit small practice EMR-AI data flows. Swissmedic does not provide evidence architecture guidance for the physician's own documentation system.

**C312 NET-NEW gap:** Swissmedic operator classification is now a multi-dimensional question — physician's use of the tool, tool certification status, and data flow architecture. Swissmedic has not resolved how data flow architecture affects operator classification at the federal level. No competitor has published an evidence-based position on this.

**C312 NET-NEW gap:** Swissmedic does not provide AI incident documentation formats for primary care. Swissdamed has mandatory reporting for medical device incidents. No Swissmedic guidance addresses AI-assisted primary care incidents specifically.

---

### Layer 3: Insurance Brokers

**What they offer:** Insurance brokers complete carrier attestation forms for physicians. They have carrier relationships, understand form architecture, and complete forms on behalf of practices.

**The gap:** Brokers complete forms. They do not review evidence before the physician signs. They do not identify documentation gaps in the physician's evidence system. They do not provide retroactive reconstruction for 2023-2024 undocumented AI deployment. They are administrative form-fillers, not evidence architects.

**C312 NET-NEW gap:** Brokers do not address the medical liability insurance coverage question. When physicians ask brokers whether AI-influenced diagnostic decisions are covered, brokers refer back to the insurer — and do not follow up with written confirmation. No broker has created a medical liability coverage confirmation service for AI-influenced decisions.

**C312 NET-NEW gap:** Brokers do not address EMR-AI data flow documentation. The question "where does patient data go after it leaves your HIN-protected EMR?" is a technical question that requires a data flow audit, not a form completion exercise.

---

### Layer 4: Medical Technology Vendors

**What they offer:** AI diagnostic tool vendors provide Swissmedic-certified tools, technical implementation support, and some vendor-side documentation (certification, data processing agreements).

**The gap:** Vendors certify their tools. They do not certify the physician's practice management documentation. Vendor certification does not answer whether the physician has met FMH human oversight obligations. Vendor data processing agreements do not provide evidence architecture for the physician's clinical reasoning chain.

**C312 NET-NEW gap:** Vendors do not provide specialty-specific authorization chain documentation frameworks. When a physician asks: "Does your certification mean I've met my FMH human oversight obligations?" vendors correctly respond: "That's a separate question for you to answer." No vendor has built a physician-facing documentation system that bridges vendor certification and FMH practice obligations.

**C312 NET-NEW gap:** Vendor data processing agreements do not address the EMR-AI data flow architecture question. Vendor agreements describe the vendor's data processing. They do not describe the routing path from the HIN-protected EMR to the vendor's processing servers — or document where that routing occurs relative to the HIN corridor.

---

### Layer 5: General AI Governance Consultants

**What they offer:** General AI governance consultants provide frameworks for AI deployment, compliance documentation, and training. Some have healthcare practices.

**The gap:** Frameworks are not evidence. Healthcare-specific frameworks (where they exist) address organizational AI governance, not physician-personal accountability. The consultant delivers a policy. The physician still needs evidence.

**C312 NET-NEW gap:** No AI governance consultant has addressed the patient consent architecture for AI-assisted diagnostic decisions as a physician-personal evidence component. This is distinct from organizational consent policies. The physician needs a patient-facing response protocol, documented in their own clinical system, that evidences appropriate disclosure.

**C312 NET-NEW gap:** No AI governance consultant has addressed the medical liability insurance coverage confirmation exercise. This is a specific documentation service — obtaining written confirmation from the insurer — that no consultant has built as a standard offering.

## Competitive White Spaces (C312 Phase 04)

### White Space 1: Physician-Personal Evidence Architecture for AI Diagnostic Tool Authorization

**Description:** No competitor offers a specialty-specific AI diagnostic tool authorization chain documentation system for the individual physician. All existing offerings address organizational authorization (practice-level) or tool certification (vendor-level). The physician's authorization chain — which tools, which functions, within which FMH specialty scope, authorized by whom — is unaddressed.

**Why It's Unoccupied:** Requires both FMH specialty scope knowledge and AI diagnostic tool architecture understanding. No competitor has both.

**Competitive Moat:** This is physician-personal documentation, not organizational policy. The physician owns it. It cannot be delegated to a vendor or a broker.

---

### White Space 2: EMR-AI Data Flow Documentation and Audit

**Description:** No competitor offers an EMR-AI data flow audit service for Geneva small practices. The service maps patient data routing from HIN-protected EMR to AI processing servers, documents the processing environment, and produces a data flow diagram for carrier attestation.

**Why It's Unoccupied:** Requires technical EMR and AI tool architecture knowledge, combined with HIN corridor understanding. No Geneva-based service combines these.

**Competitive Moat:** The data flow diagram becomes part of the evidence architecture. It is specific to the practice's actual EMR-AI configuration.

---

### White Space 3: Patient Consent Architecture for AI-Assisted Diagnostic Decisions

**Description:** No competitor offers a patient consent architecture specifically for AI-assisted diagnostic decisions in primary care. The service includes: consent form language update, staff response protocol for patient questions, documentation approach in EMR, and nFADP Article 63 exposure assessment.

**Why It's Unoccupied:** Requires nFADP knowledge, FMH consent standards, Swissmedic disclosure requirements, and clinical workflow integration. No competitor has built this as a healthcare-specific service component.

**Competitive Moat:** The consent architecture is practice-specific and physician-personal. It integrates with existing consent forms and existing clinical workflows.

---

### White Space 4: Medical Liability Insurance Coverage Confirmation Service

**Description:** No competitor offers a medical liability coverage confirmation exercise for AI-influenced diagnostic decisions. The service: drafts coverage confirmation request letter, manages insurer response, obtains written confirmation or identifies coverage gap, documents result in evidence architecture.

**Why It's Unoccupied:** Requires medical liability insurance knowledge and physician practice documentation. No Geneva service combines these. Brokers handle insurance placement, not coverage confirmation for specific risk scenarios.

**Competitive Moat:** Written coverage confirmation is a specific, addressable deliverable. It is the physician's document — it belongs to their evidence architecture.

---

### White Space 5: Patient-Facing nFADP Response Protocol for AI Involvement Questions

**Description:** No competitor offers a documented response protocol for when patients ask physicians directly whether AI was involved in their diagnosis. The service produces: a response script for physicians, staff training on documented response, EMR documentation approach for the response, and nFADP Article 63 exposure assessment for the response.

**Why It's Unoccupied:** Requires nFADP knowledge, clinical workflow integration, and physician communication design. No competitor has built this as a specific service component for primary care.

**Competitive Moat:** The response protocol becomes part of the evidence architecture. Documented physician responses to patient questions evidence appropriate disclosure.

## Why Healthcare Is Structurally Different From Other Verticals

The Geneva healthcare vertical has a distinct competitive structure because the accountability architecture is physician-personal in a way that legal, finance, and consulting are not:

- **Legal:** Accountability is firm-level. The managing partner is accountable, but the attestation is practice-level.
- **Finance:** Accountability is firm-level. The managing director is accountable, but the attestation is at the mandate level.
- **Consulting:** Accountability is firm-level. The managing director is accountable, but the attestation is at the client engagement level.
- **Healthcare:** Accountability is physician-personal. The carrier form asks for the physician's name and license number personally. Swissmedic asks about the physician's specific use. Patients ask the physician directly. The evidence architecture must be physician-personal throughout.

This structural difference means that generic AI governance frameworks — designed for organizational accountability — fail at the physician-personal level. No competitor has built a physician-personal evidence architecture for Geneva healthcare.

4. **Medical liability insurance coverage confirmation** — no competitor manages the coverage confirmation exercise with the insurer
5. **Patient-facing nFADP response protocol** — no competitor offers a documented response protocol for patient AI questions

All five white spaces are physician-personal, not organizational. All five require integration with existing clinical workflows. All five are distinct from general AI governance frameworks.

The healthcare evidence architecture is the only service that addresses all five simultaneously.
