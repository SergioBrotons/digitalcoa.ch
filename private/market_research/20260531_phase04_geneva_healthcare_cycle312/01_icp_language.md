## How Physicians Talk About AI Problems (C312)

### Primary ICP Language Pattern

**The Core Frame (C312):** "Five things are converging on me simultaneously — and they all lead back to my personal accountability. My carrier form has my name on it personally. My medical assistant is using AI on patient data I didn't specifically authorize. My EMR routes patient data to AI processing servers outside the HIN corridor. My patients are starting to ask whether AI was involved in their diagnosis — and I have no documented answer. And if something goes wrong, I'm not sure my medical liability insurance covers an AI-influenced decision. These are not separate problems. They're one problem with five pressure points."

This is distinct from C311. In C311, the primary ICP anxiety was "multiple named accountability vectors are now active simultaneously — medical assistant delegation, OaF survey, clinical reasoning chain, Swissmedic operator classification." In C312, the frame has shifted to: "the consequences of these gaps are now touching each other — the AI tool I deployed routes data outside the HIN corridor, my patients are asking about AI involvement, and if an incident occurs, I'm not sure my insurance covers it."

### The Five Converging Pressures (C312 Frame)

C312 identifies five pressures that are distinct from prior cycle findings:

1. **AI diagnostic tool authorization chain within specialty scope — now a carrier attestation question**
   - Physician language: "When I deployed the AI diagnostic tool, I authorized it for the practice. But the carrier form now asks: which specific AI tools did you authorize for which specific diagnostic functions, within which FMH specialty scope? I authorized it for general use. I didn't document which functions, which scope, which physician. The authorization was not specialty-specific."
   - This is distinct from C311: the medical assistant delegation gap (C311 Finding 1) was about staff AI use. The authorization chain gap (C312 Finding 1) is about the physician's own AI tool deployment — specifically whether it was authorized within the physician's documented FMH specialty scope. These are different accountability vectors with different documentation requirements.

2. **EMR-AI interoperability data flow gap — HIN corridor covers transport, not processing**
   - Physician language: "My HIN connection is secure. My EMR is HIN-compliant. But when patient data goes from my EMR to the AI diagnostic tool for processing, it routes through servers outside the HIN corridor. HIN covers transport security. It doesn't cover where the data goes for processing. I didn't know this. My carrier didn't ask me this before. They're starting to ask now."
   - This is distinct from C306-C307 HIN findings: prior cycles identified that HIN covers transport security, not AI tool certification. C312 identifies the specific data flow gap — the routing path from HIN-protected EMR to external AI processing servers — as a distinct documentation requirement that carriers are beginning to interrogate.

3. **Patient consent architecture for AI-assisted diagnostic decisions — no documented framework**
   - Physician language: "My patient consent form covers human interpretation of diagnostic data. A patient asked me last week: 'Did you use AI to interpret my results?' I didn't have a documented answer. I gave an ad hoc response. That's now a documented patient conversation — and I have no idea what I said."
   - This is distinct from C310 Finding 3 (nFADP data minimization conflict) and C311 Finding 3 (clinical reasoning chain): C310 addressed the physician-Swissmedic/carrier axis. C311 addressed the physician-clinical documentation axis. C312 addresses the physician-patient axis — specifically, the documented consent architecture for AI-assisted diagnostic decisions.

4. **Medical liability insurance coverage ambiguity for AI-influenced diagnostic decisions**
   - Physician language: "I called my medical liability insurer to ask: if an AI-influenced diagnostic decision leads to a patient injury claim, am I covered? They said: 'That's a question for the underwriter. We don't have a published position.' I need that answer in writing before an incident, not after."
   - This is distinct from all prior cycles: no prior C-cycle identified medical liability insurance coverage ambiguity for AI-influenced decisions as a distinct market signal. This is new in C312 — the question is now being asked directly to insurers, and the answer is "we don't have a published position."

5. **Patient-facing nFADP Article 63 activation — conversations, not just forms**
   - Physician language: "A patient asked me: 'Was AI involved in my diagnosis?' I said yes. She asked: 'Did you tell me that before?' I said no. She said: 'Does that affect my rights under nFADP?' I didn't have a documented answer. That conversation happened. It's in the patient's record now. And it's evidence of something I'm not sure I handled correctly."
   - This is distinct from all prior cycles: nFADP Article 63 personal liability was identified in C309 and confirmed in C310-C311 as activating through carrier forms and Swissmedic inquiries. C312 identifies the patient conversation itself as an activation point — a physician's response to a patient question about AI involvement in their diagnosis creates a documented record that can evidence either adequate disclosure or inadequate consent architecture.

### The Physician-to-Physician Channel (C312)

Physicians learn about AI accountability failures through peer networks — and the nature of the peer conversation has shifted:

- **How physicians hear about problems (C312):** "A colleague told me: 'I had a patient ask me directly whether AI was involved in their diagnosis. I said yes. The patient asked whether that affected their rights. I didn't know what to say. Now the patient has a note in their record about this conversation — and I don't know what I said or what I should have said.'"
- **How physicians share solutions:** AMGe WhatsApp groups, Geneva physician peer networks — specific incidents discussed by name, not abstract principles.
- **Trust formation:** "I trust what my colleague tells me from their actual experience — what they were asked, what happened, what they wish they'd known."
- **Decision trigger:** "When a colleague tells me their medical liability insurer said they don't have a published position on AI-influenced decisions — and they're treating that as a green light — I go home and check my own situation."

### The Peer Question Sequence (C312)

1. "Does your carrier form ask you to document which specific AI tools you authorized for which specific diagnostic functions within your FMH specialty scope — or did you authorize AI for general use?"
2. "Does your EMR route patient data to AI processing servers outside the HIN corridor? Do you know where the data goes after it leaves your EMR?"
3. "Has a patient asked you directly: was AI involved in my diagnosis? What did you say? Is that documented?"
4. "Have you asked your medical liability insurer: am I covered if an AI-influenced diagnostic decision leads to a patient injury claim? What did they say?"
5. "Does your patient consent form include any language about AI-assisted diagnostic decisions? If a patient asks, what does your form say they consented to?"
6. "If a patient asks: does that affect my rights under nFADP — do you have a documented answer prepared for that question?"

### The Evidence Language (C312)

Physicians in C312 do not respond to "AI governance framework." They respond to:

- "An AI diagnostic tool authorization chain — which tools, which functions, within which FMH specialty scope"
- "A data flow diagram showing where patient data goes after it leaves your HIN-protected EMR"
- "A patient consent architecture for AI-assisted diagnostic decisions — documented, staff-ready, patient-facing"
- "Written confirmation from your medical liability insurer about AI-influenced diagnostic coverage"
- "A documented response protocol for when patients ask whether AI was involved in their diagnosis"
- "Evidence that your nFADP Article 63 personal exposure is contained — not just through carrier forms, but through patient interactions"

### Decision Trigger Sequence (C312)

1. **EMR-AI data flow question surfaces:** Physician learns that patient data routes from HIN-protected EMR to external AI processing servers — not HIN-covered. This is the first point where the security assumption breaks.
2. **Patient asks about AI directly:** Patient asks physician whether AI was involved in their diagnosis. Physician gives ad hoc response. That response is now in the patient's record.
3. **Medical liability insurer question asked:** Physician calls insurer to ask about AI coverage. Receives non-committal answer. Physician realizes they need written confirmation — and doesn't have it.
4. **Carrier form asks about specialty-specific authorization:** Carrier form asks which AI tools were authorized for which diagnostic functions within FMH specialty scope. Physician authorized for general use — not specialty-specific.
5. **Patient asks about nFADP rights:** Patient asks physician whether AI involvement affects their nFADP rights. Physician has no documented response protocol.
6. **Medical society discussion:** AMGe event where peers compare notes — carrier forms, patient conversations, insurance questions, HIN corridor gaps.

## C312 Phase 04 ICP Language — Core Insight

The C312 healthcare ICP is managing five pressure points that are now touching each other — not operating in parallel:

- The AI tool deployment authorization chain (C312 Finding 1) connects to the carrier attestation question about specialty scope
- The EMR-AI data flow gap (C312 Finding 2) connects to the HIN security assumption physicians have been operating on
- The patient consent architecture gap (C312 Finding 3) connects to the nFADP Article 63 personal liability through documented patient conversations
- The medical liability insurance ambiguity (C312 Finding 4) connects to the efficiency-liability paradox — physicians adopted AI to be more efficient, but may have uninsured exposure
- The patient-facing nFADP activation (C312 Finding 5) connects to all of the above through documented physician responses in patient records

These are not five separate problems. They are one problem with five pressure points — and the pressure points are now beginning to interact.

Positioning anchor: "For physicians, evidence is personal. And your license is on the line."

The C312 healthcare evidence architecture: specialty-specific authorization chain documentation + EMR-AI data flow audit + patient consent architecture for AI-assisted decisions + medical liability insurance written confirmation + patient-facing nFADP response protocol.
