## Competitive Landscape Map

### Tier 1: Global Professional Services Firms (Big 4)

**Players:** KPMG, PwC (Strategy&, Switzerland office), Deloitte, EY
**Target:** Large enterprises, financial institutions, multinationals
**Pricing:** Minimum CHF 50,000–100,000+ for AI governance engagements
**What they say:** "AI governance framework implementation," "Regulatory compliance alignment (EU AI Act, FINMA, nFADP)," "AI risk management," "Governance, risk, and compliance integration"
**What they miss:** These firms serve enterprise-scale organizations with dedicated compliance departments. Their frameworks are designed for organizations that can implement ISO 42001, NIST AI RMF, or custom governance architectures over 6–12 month timelines. They do not serve Geneva small professional firms — not because they don't want to, but because the economics don't work. A 10-person Geneva law firm cannot authorize a CHF 50,000+ engagement, and a Big 4 engagement designed for an enterprise cannot be compressed to serve a small firm.

**The gap:** The structural exclusion of the Geneva small firm segment from Big 4 services creates the primary competitive opening. DigitalCoa serves exactly the segment that Big 4 economics exclude.

**White space for DigitalCoa:** "The market offers frameworks for enterprises. We build evidence systems for Geneva professional firms."

---

### Tier 2: AI Governance Software Platforms

**Players:** Workiva, ServiceNow GRC, AI governance-specific modules (Vcom, MetricStream), standardless AI governance tooling
**Target:** Enterprises with compliance departments
**Pricing:** Annual licenses at CHF 10,000–100,000+; implementation timelines 3–9 months; requires dedicated operational staff
**What they say:** "AI governance in one platform," "Automated evidence collection," "Regulatory compliance tracking," "Audit-ready documentation"
**What they miss:** Platforms assume a compliance department. They require operational capacity the firm doesn't have. A 12-person Geneva law firm cannot dedicate a full-time person to running a governance platform. The platform creates operational overhead that exceeds the firm's capacity — not a solution, a new problem.

**The gap:** Small professional firms need human-delivered advisory, not software platforms. The evidence capture system must be operational within the firm's actual capacity — not require a dedicated compliance officer to run.

**White space for DigitalCoa:** "We deliver the evidence system — you don't need to run software or hire a compliance department."

---

### Tier 3: Swiss Cyber Security Providers

**Players:** Swiss Cyber Institute (SCI), Compass Security, infoGuard, other Swiss cybersecurity firms
**Target:** Organizations prioritizing data security and cyber resilience
**What they say:** "AI security governance," "Data protection for AI systems," "Cybersecurity for AI deployments," "AI incident response"
**What they miss:** Cybersecurity and AI governance are adjacent but distinct. Cybersecurity addresses data breach prevention, system security, and technical controls. AI governance addresses accountability, evidence of authorization decisions, and decision attribution. A firm with excellent cybersecurity posture can still have no AI governance evidence system — because security and accountability are different problems.

**Why it matters for DigitalCoa:** An ICP who attended Swiss Cyber Institute AI governance training received security-focused content. They still cannot answer their carrier attestation form, because carrier auditors are testing evidence of authorization decisions — not security controls. The ICP paid for training and received the wrong product.

**White space for DigitalCoa:** "Cybersecurity and AI governance are different problems. We solve the accountability problem — not the security problem."

### Tier 4: GDPR / nFADP Compliance Consultants

**Players:** Swiss data protection consultants (general practitioners), international GDPR advisory firms with Swiss offices, legal data protection boutiques
**Target:** All organizations with Swiss or EU data protection obligations
**What they say:** "nFADP compliance," "Data protection officer services," "Privacy by design," "Data subject rights implementation," "AI data processing compliance"
**What they miss:** GDPR consultants solve the data protection problem — consent, privacy notices, data subject rights, processing records. AI governance evidence systems address authorization records, decision attribution, AI system inventory, and carrier attestation readiness. These are related but distinct. An ICP who hired a GDPR consultant has a complete data protection framework and still cannot answer their carrier attestation form.

**The gap:** AI governance evidence is a distinct service category from GDPR compliance. A GDPR consultant who offers "AI governance" as an add-on module is not an AI governance specialist — they're a data protection consultant applying familiar frameworks to a new problem.

**White space for DigitalCoa:** "nFADP compliance and AI governance evidence are different problems. We specialize in the evidence system your carrier is actually testing."

## What No Competitor Has Claimed — The White Space

### White Space 1: "Evidence" as the Positioning Term

**What it is:** No competitor positions around "evidence." Every competitor uses frameworks, compliance, risk, security, governance, policies. None say "evidence."

**Why it matters:** ICP language research reveals that "evidence" is the word ICPs use when describing what they need — not "compliance," not "governance," not "framework." The market has been searching for evidence and finding frameworks.

**The opening:** "The market offers frameworks. We offer evidence." owns the white space no competitor has claimed. The phrase is:
- ICP-resonant (they use the word "evidence")
- Competitor-differentiating (no one says it)
- Operationally specific (evidence is what carrier forms test for)
- Emotionally neutral but practically urgent (evidence, not fear)

**This is the primary positioning statement.**

---

### White Space 2: The Geneva Standard Definition

**What it is:** No one has defined what "Geneva-standard AI governance documentation" means in operational terms. The phrase is referenced but empty of content.

**Why it matters:** ICP wants to claim Geneva-standard compliance but has no reference document. The first entity to define the standard becomes the authority — every subsequent reference to "Geneva standard" will reference whoever defined it first.

**The opening:** Define the standard through client work and publication. The definition becomes the reference document that professional bodies and carriers recognize. Ownership of the phrase "Geneva-standard AI governance documentation" creates a long-term brand moat.

---

### White Space 3: The Vendor Contract Trap as a Named Service

**What it is:** No competitor addresses the vendor contract trap — historical data exposure that cannot be unwound by switching vendors. Everyone advises on how to avoid it in new contracts. None offer the forward-looking evidence system for firms already trapped.

**Why it matters:** A significant portion of the ICP market is trapped. They cannot switch vendors and resolve historical exposure. They can only build a forward-looking evidence system. No competitor has named this as a service category — leaving trapped firms with no pathway.

**The opening:** Name the service explicitly. "Vendor contract trap resolution" or "historical AI data exposure management." Firms who understand they are trapped will recognize themselves and seek the named service.

---

### White Space 4: Vertical-Specific Reference Portfolio

**What it is:** No competitor has built a vertical-specific reference portfolio for Geneva professional firms (legal, finance, consulting, healthcare). Every competitor offers horizontal frameworks that apply across verticals.

**Why it matters:** ICP who is evaluating an advisor wants to see someone who has done this for a firm like theirs. "We've worked with other Geneva law firms" is more powerful than "we have AI governance expertise." The reference portfolio is the proof that vertical specificity exists.

**The opening:** Build reference clients in each vertical. Document outcomes (with permission). Publish case frameworks. The reference portfolio compounds over time — a competitor who enters in 2027 must build from scratch, while DigitalCoa's portfolio in 2026 creates a 2–3 year competitive advantage.

---

### White Space 5: Personal Liability as the Primary Messaging Frame

**What it is:** No competitor uses personal liability as the primary emotional frame. All competitors frame AI governance as a business compliance problem. None say "your name, your liability."

**Why it matters:** nFADP Art. 63 creates named personal liability for managing directors and responsible individuals. The ICP who is the named responsible person feels this personally — not as a business risk, as a personal risk. No competitor speaks to that feeling.

**The opening:** The personal liability frame converts abstract compliance concern to urgent personal action. "Your name. Your liability. Your evidence system." is the emotional trigger that competitor messaging cannot create because they frame AI governance as a business problem, not a personal one.
