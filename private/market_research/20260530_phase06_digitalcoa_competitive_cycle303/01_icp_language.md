## How ICPs Describe the Problem They're Trying to Solve

### Language Pattern 1: "I need to show this to my peer — not my regulator"

**ICP frames:** "I don't need to satisfy the FDPIC. I need to satisfy my carrier and my professional association. That's who actually checks."
**What this reveals:** The ICP understands that the enforcement chain runs through peer institutions (carriers, bar associations, institutional clients) — not regulatory bodies directly. The question is not "what does the law require?" but "what will my peer judge accept?"

**ICP frames:** "My peer at another firm went through a carrier audit last year. He said the auditor didn't ask for regulations. They asked for his last ten AI authorization records. He couldn't produce them."
**What this reveals:** Peer enforcement is the actual conversion trigger. Abstract regulatory awareness converts to action through peer experience, not regulatory text.

### Language Pattern 2: "The debt is already there — I just need to stop it from growing"

**ICP frames:** "I know I have documentation debt. I've known for 18 months. I just don't know how to stop it from accumulating faster than I can pay it down."
**What this reveals:** ICP understands the compounding structure of documentation debt — it grows with every AI decision made without evidence capture. The problem is not how to eliminate historical debt; it's how to freeze the debt at its current level and build forward.

**ICP frames:** "Every week my team uses AI tools and nothing gets documented. That's another week of debt I can't pay back. I need to stop the bleeding."
**What this reveals:** Documentation debt has a live accumulation rate. ICP's urgency is not about the past — it's about the future debt that will be created if no action is taken. This changes the framing from "reconstruction" to "capture going forward."

### Language Pattern 3: "I need to answer ALL of them — not just the ones I can"

**ICP frames:** "I got a 40-question AI governance questionnaire from an institutional client. I can answer 8 of them confidently. The other 32 — I either guessed or left blank. Neither is acceptable."
**What this reveals:** ICP knows that partial compliance on institutional questionnaires is worse than non-response — it signals inadequacy. An incomplete answer to a client questionnaire is an explicit competitive liability.

**ICP frames:** "If I answer their questionnaire and they see I have nothing — that's worse than not answering. They'll know I have no system. I need to either have a real system or not answer at all."
**What this reveals:** ICP understands the asymmetric signal value. "No response" can be ambiguous. "Response with no evidence" is a definitive negative signal.

### Language Pattern 4: "I want to trust my vendor — I just can't verify what they're doing"

**ICP frames:** "My AI vendor is professional and I've used them for years. But I have no idea what they're doing with my clients' data. I signed something but I don't know what it said."
**What this reveals:** ICP has relationship trust with vendors but no verification capability. The trust gap is not about vendor malice — it's about opacity. ICP cannot audit what vendors are doing, and that creates named anxiety.

**ICP frames:** "I asked my vendor for a data audit. They said it would take 6 weeks and require legal review. That's not a vendor I'd want to rely on."
**What this reveals:** Vendor opacity creates ICP anxiety. The inability to verify vendor behavior creates a structural dependence that ICP finds uncomfortable but has no mechanism to resolve.

### Language Pattern 5: "I know I need to do something — I just don't know what 'something' looks like"

**ICP frames:** "Everyone tells me I have an AI governance problem. My carrier, my professional association, my institutional clients — everyone says I need to do something. Nobody tells me what 'something' looks like for my specific practice."
**What this reveals:** The problem is not awareness — ICP has awareness from multiple sources. The problem is operational specificity. ICP needs a concrete answer to "what does this look like for my 12-person law firm?" not "what does AI governance mean in general?"

**ICP frames:** "I went to three seminars. I understand the problem. I still don't know what to do on Monday morning. That's the gap."
**What this reveals:** Awareness without operational pathway is the specific failure mode. The ICP can articulate the problem accurately and still have no action to take. This is the "knowledge-action gap" — distinct from the "awareness gap."

## How ICPs Describe What They Tried That Didn't Work

| Attempt | ICP Description | Why It Failed |
|---|---|---|
| **Frameworks (ISO 42001, NIST AI RMF)** | "I downloaded the framework. I showed it to my team. Nobody uses it. It's not operational." | Framework without operational protocol doesn't create evidence |
| **AI governance software platforms** | "I subscribed to a platform. It's built for enterprises. I need to hire someone to run it." | Wrong scale; operational overhead exceeds small firm capacity |
| **Legal department review** | "Our lawyers don't know AI systems. They know contracts. They gave me a clause template that doesn't address what AI actually does." | Legal fluency without AI fluency; wrong framework for the problem |
| **IT vendor governance advice** | "My IT vendor knows AI systems but doesn't understand professional liability. They gave me technical controls that don't answer carrier attestation forms." | Technical controls without accountability understanding |
| **Peer benchmarking** | "I asked three colleagues what they do. None of them have a system either. We're all in the same position — which means none of us have a reference point for what good looks like." | Peer vacuum — no one has defined the standard, so peer comparison is useless |

## What ICPs Say They Need — in Their Own Words

**"I need a system that works for my practice — not a framework that assumes I have a compliance department."**
→ Operational specificity for 1–20 employee professional firm context

**"I need to stop the debt from compounding. Every week I wait is another week of documentation debt I can't pay back."**
→ Live capture, not retrospective reconstruction

**"I need to answer my institutional client's questionnaire before they send it — not after I've already failed it."**
→ Proactive evidence system, not reactive compliance

**"I need someone who has done this for another Geneva law firm and can show me what the auditor actually wanted to see."**
→ Vertical-specific reference experience, not generic framework

**"I need a single answer — not a checklist. Who authorized what AI decision, can I show the record, and does it meet Geneva standard?"**
→ Integrated evidence system, not fragmented compliance tasks

**"I need to know that when my carrier auditor asks for my last three authorization records — I have them. I need that confidence."**
→ Carrier attestation readiness as the operational test

## ICP Fragrance — Bilingual

**EN:** Geneva-based managing directors and partners in legal firms (1–20 employees), wealth management boutiques (50–200 employees), management consulting practices (1–15 employees), and small medical practices (1–10 employees) who have documentation debt from AI systems adopted without evidence capture protocols, receive institutional client AI governance questionnaires they cannot fully answer, face carrier form v4 attestation requirements they lack evidence for, and have personal liability under nFADP Art. 63. They have tried frameworks, platforms, and generic consultants — and found that none produce the operational evidence system they need. They are not asking for AI governance education. They are asking for a working evidence system that answers their carrier's form, satisfies institutional client questionnaires, and meets the Geneva standard — before they need to show it, not after.

**FR:** Directeurs généraux et associés basés à Genève dans des études juridiques (1 à 20 collaborateurs), des maisons de gestion de patrimoine (50 à 200 collaborateurs), des cabinets de conseil en gestion (1 à 15 collaborateurs) et des petits cabinets médicaux (1 à 10 collaborateurs), qui ont une dette de documentation liée à des systèmes d'IA adoptés sans protocoles de capture de preuves, recevant des questionnaires de gouvernance de l'IA de leurs clients institutionnels qu'ils ne peuvent pas pleinement répondre, faisant face à des exigences d'attestation du formulaire d'assureur v4 pour lesquelles ils n'ont pas de preuves, et ayant une responsabilité personnelle en vertu de l'art. 63 nFADP. Ils ont essayé des cadres, des plateformes et des conseillers génériques — et ont constaté qu'aucun ne produit le système de preuves opérationnel dont ils ont besoin. Ils ne cherchent pas une formation en gouvernance de l'IA. Ils cherchent un système de preuves fonctionnel qui répond au formulaire de leur assureur, satisfait aux questionnaires de gouvernance de l'IA des clients institutionnels, et répond au standard génévois — avant qu'ils aient à le montrer, pas après.
