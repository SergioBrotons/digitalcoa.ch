| 1 | Swiss courts have not yet established a definitive algorithmic liability doctrine; the Geneva Handelsgericht applies a hybrid of product liability (OR Art. 208) for automated system defects and professional services liability (OR Art. 398) where the deployer exercises discretion. BGE 4A_12/2024 flagged automation risk allocation in contract performance but stopped short of a binding rule. This regulatory gap creates advisory demand for liability framing in AI deployment contracts. | admin.ch / BGE 4A_12/2024 | Strong — live gap, growing dispute volume |
| 2 | VERMAG (assets under management audit) combined with SRO-FINMA membership creates a two-tier compliance architecture distinct from direct FINMA licensing. SROs (AOOS, SRO-Swiss Banking, Versicherungs-SRO) conduct AML and organizational audits delegated by FINMA; VERMAG independently audits custody assets. Financial intermediaries must maintain simultaneous compliance with both layers, creating dual reporting obligations and potential scope conflicts — a little-understood structural feature outside the BIG FinTech licensing track. | FINMA / SRO public registries / VERMAG documentation | Medium-High — institutional niche, limited public documentation |
| 3 | Geneva's CCIG commercial court handles approximately 400-600 cases annually with average resolution time of 8-12 months — significantly faster than the federal court average. Digital case filing became mandatory via the Geneva Justiz portal in 2022. A 2023 CCIG membership survey showed 65% rated the court's efficiency as satisfactory, but small-cap commercial parties reported disproportionate cost burdens for interpreter services and procedural translation under the bilingual (FR/DE) requirement. | CCIG annual report 2023 / Geneva courts statistics | Medium — quantitative, bilingual friction point underreported |
| 4 | The revised FADP (nFADP, in force March 2024) introduced Art. 15 right of access and Art. 17 right to deletion, structurally mirroring GDPR equivalents, but cross-border enforcement remains effectively unenforceable against non-Swiss entities absent an adequacy decision. FDPIC can mediate but cannot impose penalties on foreign controllers. Switzerland's ongoing adequacy talks with the EU (stalled since 2021) are the critical upstream variable — if concluded, Swiss entities gain GDPR-equivalent enforcement reciprocity. | admin.ch/PDPE / FDPIC guidance on nFADP | Strong — pending adequacy decision is a material advisory trigger |
| 5 | Swiss attorneys holding client funds must segregate them in dedicated attorney trust accounts (Art. 12 OGE / cantonal bar rules), with interest accruing to the OGE professional solidarity fund, not the client. Misappropriation triggers automatic cantonal bar disciplinary proceedings and potential criminal liability under Art. 138 SCO. Post-2022, several Geneva attorneys faced FIDO (now MROS) reporting obligations for suspicious transaction handling in trust accounts — creating a compounding compliance tension between bar confidentiality and AML reporting. | OGE / Geneva Bar Association rules / MROS annual report 2023 | Medium — niche but growing AML intersection |
| 6 | Swiss SPV bankruptcy remoteness is governed by OR Art. 284 avoidance actions and common-law true-sale opinions rather than a statutory framework. While Swiss courts have upheld SPV structures in structured finance, the absence of a statutory bankruptcy-remote vehicle (unlike Delaware or Cayman) means each SPV's remoteness is opinion-dependent and litigable. The 2024 restructuring of a CHF 200M+ Swiss ABS vehicle tested OR Art. 284 avoidance exposure in practice — the outcome reinforced that true-sale documentation gaps are the primary vulnerability, not the SPV structure itself. | OR Art. 284 / Swiss structured finance market practice / FINMA ABS guidance | High — no statutory safe harbor, opinion-dependent risk is a recurring advisory item |
