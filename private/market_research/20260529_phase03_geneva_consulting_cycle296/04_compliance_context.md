# 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Switzerland Regulatory Landscape

## Phase 03 | C296 | Geneva Consulting ICP

---

## Overview

Switzerland has adopted a sector-specific approach to AI regulation rather than a comprehensive AI act. For Geneva-based consulting boutiques, the relevant regulatory landscape spans Swiss domestic law, financial sector guidance, and the extraterritorial reach of the EU AI Act. Personal liability for managing directors and partners adds an individual dimension that corporate compliance frameworks do not address.

---

## 1. Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) — Revised Edition

**Status:** Revised nFADP entered into force on 1 September 2023, with a transition period until 1 September 2024. Full enforcement is now active.

**Relevance to consulting boutiques:**
When a consulting firm uses AI tools in client engagements, client data may be processed by AI systems. Under nFADP, this constitutes data processing requiring:
- A lawful basis (typically consent or legitimate interest, or for contractual purposes)
- Adequate transparency (clients must be informed that their data will be processed by AI tools)
- Data processing agreements with AI tool vendors where applicable
- Assessment of whether cross-border data transfers occur (AI vendors operating outside Switzerland)

**Key obligations:**
- **Article 6:** Processing of personal data requires a legal basis. AI-assisted analysis of client data is processing.
- **Article 14:** Disclosure obligation — clients must be informed about data processing, including AI processing.
- **Article 16:** Data processing agreements required when processing is delegated to third parties (e.g., AI vendors).
- **Article 28:** Cross-border transfer restrictions — data may only be transferred to countries with adequate protection or under appropriate safeguards.

**Personal exposure:** Managing directors who use AI tools without assessing nFADP implications are personally exposed. Unlike corporate fines, personal liability can attach to the signing partner.

**Practical gap:** Most small Geneva boutiques have not conducted this assessment. They do not know whether their AI tool use triggers nFADP obligations, whether they have adequate vendor agreements, or whether they have met disclosure requirements with clients.

---

## 2. FINMA Guidance 08/2024 — AI Governance in Financial Institutions

**Status:** Published 18 December 2024. Applies to supervised institutions (banks, insurers, securities dealers) under FINMA oversight.

**Relevance to consulting boutiques:**
Many Geneva consulting boutiques serve financial institution clients. When a bank or insurer sends an AI governance questionnaire or attestation request to a consulting vendor, they are applying FINMA expectations to their supply chain. The consulting boutique does not need to comply with FINMA guidance directly, but their financial institution clients will demand equivalent standards.

**Key FINMA expectations for AI governance:**
- Comprehensive governance framework with clear responsibilities for AI development, implementation, monitoring, and use
- AI system inventory with risk classification based on materiality and likelihood
- Data quality and model robustness requirements
- Explainability and human oversight in AI-driven decisions
- Third-party risk management for AI vendors
- Documentation and staff training
- Compliance with existing laws (including data protection)

**What this means for consulting boutiques:**
A financial institution client will expect a consulting vendor to have:
- A written AI governance framework
- An inventory of AI tools used in client work
- Human oversight documentation for AI-assisted deliverables
- Data protection compliance for AI tool use
- Third-party vendor assessment for AI tools

**Personal exposure:** Partners signing engagement letters with financial institution clients face personal reputational and potentially contractual exposure if they cannot demonstrate AI governance practices when those clients are audited by FINMA.

---

## 3. EU AI Act — Extraterritorial Application

**Status:** EU AI Act entered into force August 2024. Full application from August 2026. Certain provisions apply earlier for high-risk systems.

**Relevance to Geneva consulting boutiques:**
Swiss boutiques serving EU-based clients or providing AI outputs used in the EU fall within the Act's scope. More importantly, EU-based clients apply EU AI Act due diligence standards to their vendors — including Swiss consulting firms.

**Key obligations under EU AI Act:**
- Classification of AI systems by risk category
- Technical documentation requirements
- Conformity assessments for high-risk AI applications
- Logging and transparency obligations
- Human oversight requirements for high-risk AI

**What this means for consulting boutiques:**
EU clients sending AI governance questionnaires are applying EU AI Act standards. A Geneva boutique that cannot demonstrate EU AI Act alignment will lose EU-facing business.

**Practical gap:** Most small Geneva boutiques do not know whether their AI tool use qualifies as high-risk under EU AI Act classification, and have not prepared the required documentation.

---

## 4. ISO/IEC 42001:2023 — AI Management Systems

**Status:** Published December 2023. International standard for AI management systems.

**Relevance to consulting boutiques:**
ISO42001 provides a certification-ready framework for AI governance. For boutiques seeking carrier attestation or responding to enterprise client questionnaires, ISO 42001 alignment is increasingly expected.

**What ISO 42001 covers:**
- Governance and accountability for AI systems
- Risk management for AI
- Data quality and management
- Transparency and explainability
- Human oversight and intervention
- Third-party vendor management
- Documentation and records

**Personal exposure angle:** ISO 42001 certification creates a documented governance record that demonstrates personal best efforts. For a managing director, having ISO 42001-aligned practices provides a defensible record of good governance.

**Practical gap:** Full ISO 42001 certification is resource-intensive. However, implementing ISO 42001-aligned practices without pursuing full certification is a practical middle path — demonstrating alignment without the full certification burden.

---

## 5. Council of Europe AI Convention

**Status:** Open for signature since2025. Switzerland is a signatory.

**Relevance to consulting boutiques:**
The Convention establishes human rights-based AI governance standards. While not yet fully ratified or implemented, it signals the direction of Swiss and European AI governance expectations. Consulting firms aligned with Convention principles will be better positioned for evolving regulatory expectations.

---

## 6. Personal Liability Framework — The Individual Dimension

**The gap no regulatory framework addresses directly:**

Swiss law holds managing directors and partners personally accountable for their firm's actions. When AI-assisted advice causes client harm, the personal liability question is not abstract.

**Relevant liability vectors:**
- **Contractual liability** (engagement letter terms, scope of work)
- **Professional liability** (advice that causes financial harm)
- **Data protection liability** (nFADP violations)
- **Regulatory liability** (when serving regulated industry clients)

**The problem:** No regulatory framework provides a personal liability shield. However, documented AI governance practices — a governance framework, methodology documentation, sub-consultant controls, data handling rules — create a record of best efforts that is the closest practical equivalent.

**This is the unique angle for Geneva boutiques:** A managing director cannot eliminate personal liability through insurance alone. They need a governance paper trail that demonstrates they took reasonable steps to govern AI use in their practice. This is what exists nowhere in the market.

## Compliance Context Summary: What a Geneva Boutique Actually Needs

| Regulation | Applies To | Boutique Exposure | Practical Need |
|---|---|---|---|
| nFADP | All firms processing personal data | Data processing without consent/notification | AI data handling assessment and client disclosure |
| FINMA 08/2024 | Firms serving FI clients | Client questionnaire failure, contract loss | FI-client-ready AI governance documentation |
| EU AI Act | Firms serving EU clients or outputs used in EU | Client due diligence failure | EU AI Act-aligned documentation |
| ISO 42001 | Voluntary (certification) | Competitive disadvantage with enterprise clients | Aligned practices (not necessarily full certification) |
| CoE AI Convention | Signaling | Future-proofing | Principles-based governance alignment |
| Personal liability | Managing directors personally | Career and license risk | Governance paper trail for personal protection |

---

## The Core Insight

Geneva consulting boutiques need practical, documented AI governance that:
1. Aligns with nFADP data processing requirements
2. Meets FINMA-informed client expectations (without being a supervised institution)
3. Prepares for EU AI Act due diligence standards
4. Creates a personal liability protection record for the managing director

No existing solution addresses all four dimensions for a small Geneva boutique. This is the positioning opportunity.
