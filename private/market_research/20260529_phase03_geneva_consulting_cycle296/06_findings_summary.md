# 06 — Findings Summary: Key Insights and Ready-to-Use Language

## Phase 03 | C296 | Geneva Consulting ICP

---

## 6 NET-NEW Findings (Distinct from C214–C295)

### Finding 1: Client AI Governance Questionnaires Are a Contract Risk Event, Not an IT Issue

Geneva managing directors are receiving AI governance questionnaires embedded in RFPs and vendor onboarding packages. The trigger is not internal — it is external. A client sends the questionnaire, and the partner scrambles to answer with no documentation.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "The client questionnaire is the moment of truth. Most boutiques fail it before they even open the document."
- "You do not get to decide when AI governance becomes urgent. Your client decides."
- "A questionnaire you cannot answer is a contract you will not win."

**Implication:** The entry point to this market is the questionnaire response moment, not the "build an AI strategy" moment.

---

### Finding 2: Carrier Attestation Is Extending FINMA Standards to the Supply Chain

Insurance carriers and financial institutions under FINMA oversight are extending AI governance requirements to their consulting vendors. This is a new form of vendor gatekeeping — not about service quality, about regulatory risk transfer.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "Your financial institution clients are not asking about your AI strategy. They are asking because their regulator is asking them."
- "When a carrier asks for attestation, they are not looking for a policy document. They are looking for evidence."
- "Attestation without documentation is attestation you cannot support."

**Implication:** Boutiques serving financial institution clients need FI-ready AI governance documentation, not just internal practices.

---

### Finding 3: Sub-Consultant AI Use Is the Invisible Liability

Small Geneva consulting firms regularly delegate to sub-consultants. The sub-consultant's AI use is invisible to the signing partner. The partner's name is on the deliverable. The client is beginning to ask.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "Your name is on the document. The sub-consultant's AI use is not."
- "You do not need to control every tool your sub-consultants use. You need to know what they are using."
- "The blind spot is not the AI. The blind spot is what you cannot see."

**Implication:** Sub-consultant AI governance is a distinct product that no existing vendor addresses for consulting firms.

---

### Finding 4: Proposal Methodology Indefensibility Is a Systemic Problem, Not a Tool Problem

Boutiques using AI in proposals and deliverables cannot explain the methodology. AI produces outputs that cannot be traced to source data, that the partner does not understand, and that cannot be audited.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "The AI gave you a number. You put it in the deck. When the client asks how you got there, 'the machine told us' is not a methodology."
- "A proposal you cannot defend is a proposal that puts you at risk."
- "The difference between a defensible methodology and a liability is documentation."

**Implication:** Methodology documentation is not a quality issue — it is a liability management issue.

---

### Finding 5: nFADP Compliance for AI Tool Use Is an Active Gap, Not a Future Risk

The nFADP transition period is over. Small Geneva boutiques using AI tools with client data have likely not assessed nFADP obligations — lawful basis, client notification, vendor data processing agreements, cross-border transfer restrictions.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "The data your AI tool processes is your responsibility. The fact that you did not know that does not protect you."
- "Client data in an AI tool is a data processing event. It requires a legal basis, disclosure, and vendor agreements."
- "Most small Geneva boutiques are already in violation and do not know it."

**Implication:** nFADP AI compliance assessment is a concrete, concrete service with clear deliverables.

---

### Finding 6: Personal Liability Is the Unifying Fear That No Product Addresses

All of the above problems converge on a single underlying fear: the managing director is personally accountable. Their name is on the engagement letter. D&O insurance covers financial exposure. Nothing creates a governance paper trail.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "At the end of the day, it is your signature. Your license. Your liability."
- "D&O insurance tells you you are covered financially. It does not tell you how to govern AI so that you never need to use it."
- "The best liability protection is a documented governance practice. Not an insurance policy — a paper trail."
- "You cannot eliminate personal liability. You can document that you did everything reasonable to govern AI use in your practice."

**Implication:** Personal liability protection through governance documentation is the emotional core of this ICP's AI governance need. All positioning should connect back to this.

## Positioning Summary

**The ICP:** Small Geneva consulting boutiques, 2–50 employees. Managing director personally accountable. Reacting to client AI governance questionnaires, carrier attestation requests, and personal liability exposure.

**The white space:** No existing vendor serves this ICP with practical, personal, documented AI governance support. Big Four serve enterprises. Legal firms are reactive. Certification bodies require heavy processes. No one addresses the boutique-sized, personally-liable, document-ready need.

**The entry point:** The client questionnaire. The carrier attestation request. The concrete trigger event that forces action.

**The emotional core:** Personal liability. Not "our firm might have a problem." "I might have a problem."

**The product needed:** Practical AI governance documentation — a framework, a methodology, sub-consultant controls, nFADP assessment, personal protection record — designed for a small Geneva boutique, achievable in weeks, not months.

**The positioning language:** Defensible. Accountable. Documented. Client-ready. Personally protected.
