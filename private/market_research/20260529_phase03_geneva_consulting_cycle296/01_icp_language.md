# 01 — ICP Language: How Geneva Consulting Boutiques Talk About AI Problems

## Phase 03 | C296 | Geneva Consulting ICP

---

## Language Patterns Observed

### The Accountability Frame
Geneva managing directors and partners do not say "AI is changing our industry." They say things like:

- "I signed off on that deliverable — if the client comes back with an AI governance questionnaire, I need to be able to show exactly what we did and why."
- "We used AI tools in the analysis, but I can't prove it. That's a problem."
- "I don't mind the team using AI — I mind that I can't control what it outputs."

The operative word is **accountability**. Not efficiency. Not innovation. Personal exposure.

### The Client Governance Questionnaire Fear
This is the trigger event. A managing director at a boutique firm receives — or anticipates receiving — a client AI governance questionnaire. The questionnaire asks:
- What AI tools did you use in this engagement?
- How do you govern AI use in your deliverables?
- Can you demonstrate methodology defensibility?
- How do you handle client data when AI is involved?

The response pattern: **defensive urgency**. Not "this is interesting" — "I need to get ahead of this before it becomes a contract issue."

Language they use:
- "carrier attestation" — they mean insurer or financial institution client demanding AI governance proof
- "client questionnaires" — the RFP or onboarding document that now includes AI-specific questions
- "defending the proposal" — not winning, defending. The mental frame is legal exposure, not sales
- "putting something in writing" — they need a documented methodology they can show, not just a pitch

### The Sub-Consultant Delegation Problem
Small boutiques work with sub-consultants. When those sub-consultants use AI tools in shared client work, the partner cannot answer:
- Which AI tools did they use?
- Was client data involved?
- Can we audit the output?

Language:
- "I can't see what they're doing with AI"
- "The client will ask and I won't have an answer"
- "It's my name on the deliverable"

### The Methodology Defensibility Gap
Partners describe a specific anxiety: their proposals and deliverables increasingly contain AI-assisted content, but they cannot explain the methodology in a way that holds up to scrutiny.

Phrases:
- "I can't defend how we got there"
- "The client wants audit trails and I don't have them"
- "What if the AI hallucinated and we delivered it?"

### The nFADP / Data Protection Angle
Swiss firms are aware of the revised Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP). The concern is concrete: client data used in AI tools may constitute a data processing violation if not properly governed.

Language:
- "client data going into AI tools" — who is responsible?
- "consent and notification" — what did we tell the client?
- "processor agreements" — do we have them with our AI vendors?

### The Carrier Attestation Request
More sophisticated boutiques have heard of or encountered carrier attestation requirements — insurers or banks asking for formal AI governance documentation before awarding contracts.

Language:
- "attestation requirements" — formal written proof of AI governance
- "ISO42001" — they know the standard exists but don't know what to do about it
- "FINMA guidance" — for those in financial services, awareness of FINMA 08/2024

---

## What They Are NOT Saying

They are not saying:
- "We need an AI strategy"
- "How do we adopt AI?"
- "What AI tools should we use?"

They are saying:
- "How do I stay out of trouble?"
- "What do I tell the client?"
- "How do I prove we did it right?"

---

## Tone Markers

- **Cautious, not curious** — they approach AI with liability awareness, not excitement
- **Retrospective** — they are reacting to client pressure, not proactively building
- **Personally accountable** — the partner's name is on the work; this is not abstract corporate risk
- **Documentation-focused** — they want paper trails, not just better tools
- **Skeptical of black boxes** — they distrust AI they cannot explain or audit

## Implications for Positioning

The ICP does not want an AI strategy. They want **a paper trail and a defensible process** they can show to clients. Positioning language should emphasize:
- Documentation and auditability
- Personal protection for the managing director
- A methodology they can put in writing
- Sub-consultant governance controls
- Swiss regulatory alignment (nFADP, FINMA where applicable)

Avoid: "cutting-edge AI," "transform your firm," "AI-powered insights."
Use: "defensible," "accountable," "documented," "client-ready," "attestation-supporting."
