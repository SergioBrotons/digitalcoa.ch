# 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

## Phase 03 | C296 | Geneva Consulting ICP

---

## NET-NEW Finding 1: Client AI Governance Questionnaires Are a Contract Risk Event

Geneva consulting boutiques are receiving AI governance questionnaires embedded in RFPs and vendor onboarding packages. The questionnaires come from:
- Large corporate clients requiring AI governance documentation before awarding contracts
- Financial institution clients (banks, insurers) subject to FINMA oversight who demand AI governance proof from all vendors
- International clients applying EU AI Actdue diligence standards to Swiss suppliers

**The problem**: Boutique firms have no standardized process to respond. Partners are answering these questionnaires ad hoc, often retroactively, and with no internal documentation to support their answers. This creates:
- Inconsistent responses across the firm
- No methodology documentation to reference
- Personal exposure for the signing partner

**Complaint verbatim**: "I got a 40-question AI governance form from a client's legal department. I had to answer it myself, overnight, with nothing to go on."

---

## NET-NEW Finding 2: Carrier Attestation Requests Are Increasing and Boutique Firms Are Unprepared

Insurance carriers and financial institutions in Switzerland are beginning to require formal AI governance attestation from consulting vendors. This is driven by:
- FINMA Guidance 08/2024 requiring supervised institutions to govern third-party AI risk
- EU AI Act extraterritorial application to Swiss firms serving EU clients
- NAIC Model Bulletin influence on global carrier AI governance standards

**The problem**: Small boutiques lack the infrastructure to provide attestation. They cannot:
- Produce a written AI governance framework
- Show an AI tool inventory with risk classifications
- Demonstrate human oversight mechanisms for AI-assisted deliverables
- Provide documentation of AI system validation and monitoring

**Complaint verbatim**: "A carrier asked us to attest to our AI governance practices. We don't have practices. We have ChatGPT."

---

## NET-NEW Finding 3: Sub-Consultant AI Use Is Ungoverned and Creates Blind Liability

Small Geneva consulting firms regularly delegate work to sub-consultants. When those sub-consultants use AI tools in shared client engagements, the signing partner cannot:
- Audit what AI tools were used
- Verify that client data was not exposed to third-party AI systems
- Confirm the quality and accuracy of AI-assisted outputs
- Answer client questions about AI methodology in that workstream

**The problem**: The partner's name is on the deliverable. The sub-consultant's AI use is invisible. Clients are beginning to ask.

**Complaint verbatim**: "I send work to a sub-consultant. They run it through AI tools. I have no idea what tools, what data, what output. It's my name on the final document."

---

## NET-NEW Finding 4: Proposal Methodology Cannot Be Defended Against Client Scrutiny

Managing directors report that their proposals and deliverables increasingly contain AI-assisted analysis, but the methodology behind that analysis cannot be explained or defended.

Key failure modes:
- AI tools produce analysis that cannot be traced back to source data
- Methodology sections in proposals are vague because the partner does not understand the AI tool's process
- No audit trail exists for AI-assisted decisions in the deliverable
- Hallucination risk: AI produces plausible but incorrect content that gets delivered to clients

**The problem**: When a client asks "how did you arrive at this analysis?", the partner has no defensible answer.

**Complaint verbatim**: "The AI gave us a market sizing number. We put it in the deck. If the client asks how we got there, I have to say 'the machine told us.' That's not a methodology."

---

## NET-NEW Finding 5: nFADP Compliance Gaps When AI Tools Process Client Data

The revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) requires lawful basis for processing personal data. When consulting firms use AI tools in client engagements, client data may be processed by:
- Cloud-based AI platforms (potentially outside Switzerland)
- Third-party AI vendors without appropriate data processing agreements
- AI tools that retain inputs for model training

**The problem**: Boutique firms have not assessed whether their AI tool use constitutes data processing under nFADP, whether they have the required consent or notification, and whether their AI vendor agreements include adequate data protection terms.

**Complaint verbatim**: "We used a client's data in an AI tool to run an analysis. Did we need their consent? Did we tell them? I don't know."

---

## NET-NEW Finding 6: Personal Liability of Managing Directors Is the Underlying Fear

All of the above problems converge on a single underlying fear: the managing director or partner is personally accountable. Their name is on the engagement letter, the proposal, the deliverable. If something goes wrong — a client suffers harm from AI-assisted advice, a data breach occurs, a regulator investigates — the personal exposure is real.

This is distinct from corporate risk. It is not "our firm might have a problem." It is "I might have a problem."

**The problem**: There is no structured way for a small boutique managing director to protect themselves personally through documented AI governance practices.

**Complaint verbatim**: "At the end of the day, it's my signature. My license. My liability."

## What This Means for Market Entry

The ICP is not shopping for AI tools. They are shopping for **protection**. The product they need is a combination of:
- A documented AI governance framework they can show to clients
- A methodology they can defend in writing
- A sub-consultant AI governance process
- Personal liability protection through documented best practices
- Alignment with Swiss regulatory expectations (nFADP, FINMA)

The entry point is the **client questionnaire** or **carrier attestation request** — the concrete trigger event that forces action.
