## What This Briefing Actually Covers

### Session 1: The Carrier Attestation — What You Signed and What It Actually Commits You To

My first question is always the same: *Did you know you signed something personal?*

The carrier AI attestation form has your name on it. Not your firm's — yours. The form was written by the carrier's legal team to manage the carrier's risk. It uses an undefined standard: "adequate AI governance controls." That standard was designed by underwriting. Nobody told you what "adequate" means at the scale of your boutique, with your team, using your specific tools.

Here is what I find, almost every time: the MD signed the form. Nobody reviewed it before signature. The form became part of the carrier's file. The MD's personal liability is now documented against an undefined standard.

**What I do in this session:** I pull the carrier form apart. I show you precisely what you certified — line by line — and what documentation would be required to support that certification if the carrier ever asks. I identify the gap between what you certified and what you can actually evidence. And I build the pre-signing review architecture you need so that the next carrier form — and there will be a next one — comes with your own analysis attached before your signature goes on it.

This is not a compliance exercise. This is a personal liability analysis before you sign your name to something.

### Session 2: The Client AI Governance Questionnaire — What You're Actually Committing To

Your client sent you a 40-page AI governance questionnaire. It feels like a administrative exercise. It is not.

Every question you answer in that questionnaire is a formal representation about your firm's AI governance practice. When you send it back, the client holds that representation. If something goes wrong — if they find out you were using AI tools in their work without the methodology you implied you had — that representation is in their file, in their lawyer's file, and possibly in a legal proceeding.

Here is the part nobody is telling you: the third client questionnaire you respond to may contradict the first one. You have no tracking system to detect this. You submitted the first response without a cross-client consistency architecture. You will submit the next one the same way unless someone builds the system.

**What I do in this session:** I build your questionnaire response architecture so that every future response is triaged, strategized, and cross-client consistent. I show you which questions to answer immediately, which require pre-documentation, and which questions you can push back on — because disclosure of certain data creates more risk than the value of the information the client is asking for.

The client questionnaire is not a request for your IT documentation. It is a risk management instrument being deployed by your client. You should respond with the same level of strategy.

### Session 3: The Sub-Consultant Delegation Problem — The Accountability Chain You Are Already Carrying

You use sub-consultants on roughly a third of your mandates. Your sub-consultant uses AI tools to produce their part of the work. Your name is on the deliverable. Your client's name is on the engagement letter. You have no idea what AI systems your sub-consultant ran, what data they processed, or where that data went.

Under Swiss law and nFADP, you are the data controller. Your sub-consultant is a processor. Your AI vendor is a sub-processor. **You are responsible for all of it — including the parts you did not evaluate and cannot audit.**

No sub-consultant contract in the Geneva boutique market contains an AI governance clause. None of them. Your engagement letters predate your firm's AI adoption by years. The processor chain from your client to your sub-consultant to their AI vendor has never been documented.

**What I do in this session:** I build your sub-consultant delegation architecture. I draft the AI governance clause for your engagement letters. I map the processor chain and document it in a way that satisfies nFADP Art.9 without requiring you to audit your sub-consultants' proprietary systems. You stay accountable without having to become an AI auditor for your entire supply chain.

### Session 4: Proposal Methodology — When the Client's Technical Team Starts Asking Questions

After one of your AI-assisted proposals or analyses is delivered, the client's technical team or AI governance officer starts asking detailed questions. Where did the data come from? What model version was used? Who reviewed this before it went out? How did you arrive at this specific conclusion?

You realize you have nothing. Not because you did anything wrong — because you never thought to document the reasoning chain from client question to analytical framework to AI-assisted analysis to recommendation.

**What I do in this session:** I build your methodology documentation architecture as a discrete deliverable for every client engagement. This is not a policy document. This is the documented analytical authorization chain that shows what you asked, what framework you applied, what the AI produced, what you reviewed, and what you recommended — with the version and timestamp intact. It survives the client's technical challenge. It does not require you to reconstruct the thinking six months after the fact.

Every AI-assisted deliverable should have a defensibility file. Most Geneva boutiques have zero.

### Session 5: nFADP — The Active Obligations You Are Violating Without Knowing It

The transition period is closed. The statutory obligations are active. Most Geneva consulting boutiques are in active violation of nFADP and do not know it.

Here is what is active right now, in your firm, whether or not you have addressed it:

**Art.9:** You are using AI tools with client data. You have not completed Data Processing Agreements with those vendors. The obligations are active. The violations are active.

**Art.6:** You have not documented your lawful basis for processing client data with AI tools. This is a present-tense obligation.

**Cross-border transfer:** Most AI tools used by Geneva consultancies process data outside Switzerland. "Swiss-hosted" vendor marketing does not resolve the compliance question. Processing location and data residency are different questions.

**What I do in this session:** I map your actual AI tool environment against nFADP obligations. Not a template. Not a policy document. Your specific tool stack, your specific client data flows, your specific processor chain. I identify what is compliant, what is not, and what your immediate, proportionate action items are — ordered by liability exposure, not by theoretical compliance priority.

I do not make this sound easier than it is. Getting compliant at boutique scale requires architectural decisions, not just form completion. We go through your specific situation.

The managing director at a Geneva consulting boutique carries personal liability for everything the firm does. Your name is on the engagement letter. Your signature is on the carrier form.
