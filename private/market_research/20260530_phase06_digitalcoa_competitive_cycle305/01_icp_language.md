## Purpose

Document how the market — competitors, ICPs, vendors, and institutions — talks about AI accountability services for Geneva SMBs. This is the language layer the competitive landscape operates in. Understanding how others frame the space reveals what's missing, what's claimed, and where the white space is.

---

## How the Market Talks About AI Accountability Services

### The Category Naming Void

**The market talks about:** AI governance, AI compliance, digital transformation, data protection compliance, AI risk management, AI strategy

**What they mean:** A category of work that involves AI tools, some documentation obligation, and a need for external help

**What's missing:** No market participant uses the language of "evidence production" or "accountability architecture" in their public positioning. The category as DigitalCoa defines it does not exist as a named market. ICPs search for "AI compliance Geneva" or "AI governance consultant Switzerland" — not for evidence production services.

**Implication:** The category must be named in ICP language before it can be owned. The naming must connect to the felt problem (what they signed, what they're afraid of) not the analytical framework.

**EN ready-to-use:** "You're not looking for AI governance. You're looking for what to do about what you already signed."

**FR ready-to-use:** « Vous ne cherchez pas une gouvernance de l'IA. Vous cherchez quoi faire avec ce que vous avez déjà signé. »

---

### The Vendor Language Layer

**How vendors talk:** "We're fully compliant," "Your data is protected," "We meet all Swiss regulations," "Our AI is GDPR compliant," "We take data protection seriously"

**What vendors actually mean:** Their own internal compliance posture — not the client's obligations

**The gap:** Vendor compliance language is entirely about the vendor's obligations, not the client's. The client signs the vendor's DPA and believes they are covered for AI governance purposes. The belief is universal and wrong.

**EN ICP translation:** "The vendor said I'm covered" → actually means → "I signed a DPA and assumed that was the documentation"

**FR ICP translation:** « Le fournisseur a dit que j'étais couvert » → veut dire → « J'ai signé un DP et j'ai supposé que c'était la documentation »

**Market signal:** Carrier forms now explicitly ask clients to confirm they have documentation independent of vendor attestations. The carrier is correcting the market's false belief through the form itself.

---

### The Institutional Guidance Language

**How Geneva institutions talk:** "Members should ensure adequate AI governance," "Best practice recommends documented oversight," "Members are encouraged to review their AI arrangements," "Appropriate measures should be in place"

**What institutions actually commit to:** Principles without operational translation. Guidance without Tuesday implementation pathway.

**The gap:** The institutional language creates an obligation (adequate AI governance) without providing the methodology to determine what adequate means at boutique scale. The principal reads the guidance and believes they are following it by having a policy.

**EN ICP translation:** "I follow the SAV/FMH/SRO guidance" → actually means → "I have a policy document that uses the right words"

**FR ICP translation:** « Je suis les directives du SAV/FMH/OSR » → veut dire → « J'ai un document de politique qui utilise les bons mots »

**Market signal:** Institutions publish guidance and then refer members to external advisors when asked for specifics. The guidance functions as a demand generator for the services market, not as a substitute for it.

### The Competitor Language Layer

**How Geneva AI governance competitors position themselves:**

- "AI strategy and governance frameworks"
- "Digital transformation advisory"
- "AI risk and compliance assessment"
- "Data protection and privacy compliance"
- "AI ethics and governance advisory"
- "Regulatory compliance consulting"
- "Technology risk management"

**What competitors actually deliver:** Assessments, frameworks, gap analyses, policy templates, training programs, awareness sessions.

**The gap:** Every competitor positioning uses framework language — what adequate AI governance looks like in general. No Geneva competitor positions around evidence production — what specific evidence the ICP needs to produce for their specific attestation, carrier, and institutional context. The framework-language competitor educates. The evidence-production competitor operates.

**EN distinction:** "We help you understand AI governance requirements" (framework) vs. "We produce the evidence your carrier is asking for" (production)

**FR distinction:** « Nous aidons à comprendre les exigences de gouvernance IA » (cadre) vs. « Nous produisons les preuves que votre assureur demande » (production)

### The ICP Language Layer — How SMB Professionals Describe Their Situation

**Pre-crisis language (undocumented):**
- "I think we're fine, we have a policy"
- "The vendor said everything was covered"
- "We reviewed it internally"
- "We don't really use AI that much"

**Attestation-confusion language:**
- "The form asked about AI governance and I didn't know what to put"
- "I filled it in but I'm not sure if it's right"
- "They want to know about our AI oversight and I'm not sure what that means"
- "The attestation came with the renewal and I just signed it"

**Vendor-contract language:**
- "We signed with [vendor name], they're supposed to be compliant"
- "Our AI tool is Swiss-based so it should be fine"
- "The terms were standard, I didn't read the whole thing"
- "The vendor's DPA covers our data protection obligations"

**Personal-consequence language:**
- "What does this actually mean for my license?"
- "If something goes wrong, can they come after me personally?"
- "My carrier paid the claim — does that protect my name?"
- "Is this just a fine or is it career-ending?"

**Future-trajectory language:**
- "I feel like this is just starting"
- "Every year there's another form"
- "The requirements keep getting stricter"
- "I need someone who actually knows what's coming"

## Cross-Competitive Language Analysis

| Speaker | What They Say | What They Mean | What's Actually True |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vendor | "We're fully compliant" | Vendor's internal posture | Client's obligations are separate and additional |
| Institution | "Ensure adequate AI governance" | Principle without method | No proportionate Tuesday translation provided |
| Competitor | "AI governance framework" | Generic framework language | Not evidence production, not attestation-calibrated |
| ICP | "I have a policy" | Policy existence = compliance | Policy ≠ evidence of human authorization |
| ICP | "The vendor covered this" | Vendor DPA = firm coverage | Carrier forms now explicitly reject this assumption |
| ICP | "I signed the attestation" | Compliance complete | Created representations, not documentation |
| Carrier | "Provide evidence of oversight" | Specific documentation | Not asking for policy documents |
| Institutional client | "Describe your AI governance" | Architecture of decision authorization | Not asking for framework description |

## The Language Gap That Creates the White Space

The market uses six different vocabulary systems for what is structurally the same problem:

1. **Vendor vocabulary:** "Compliant," "DPA," "data protection"
2. **Institutional vocabulary:** "Adequate oversight," "appropriate measures," "best practice"
3. **Competitor vocabulary:** "AI governance," "risk management," "framework"
4. **ICP vocabulary:** "The form," "what I signed," "what this means for my license"
5. **Carrier vocabulary:** "Evidence of human authorization," "attestation," "oversight documentation"
6. **Regulatory vocabulary:** "Art.321 CC," "Art.63 nFADP," "adequate documentation"

No single vocabulary connects all six. The evidence production category bridges them: it speaks the ICP's language while satisfying the carrier's documentation requirement and addressing the vendor's separate obligation. The category naming must happen in ICP vocabulary while the service delivers in carrier documentation language.

**EN naming test:** The ICP who hears "evidence production" and thinks "that's what I need" — that is the naming moment.

**FR naming test:** Le mandataire qui entend « production de preuves » et qui pense « c'est ce dont j'ai besoin » — c'est le moment de nommer.
