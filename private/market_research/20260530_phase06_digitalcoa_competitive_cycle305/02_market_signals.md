## Competitor Claim vs. Delivery Analysis

### Category 01: International Consultancies (Big Four + Global Advisory)

**What they claim:**
- "AI governance frameworks designed for your organization"
- "End-to-end AI compliance advisory"
- "Boutique-appropriate methodology"
- "Swiss market expertise"
- "Practical implementation support"

**What they actually deliver:**
- Enterprise-scale frameworks that require internal compliance teams to implement
- Documentation outputs that need a 10-person team to execute
- Pricing calibrated for multinationals, not Geneva boutiques
- General Swiss market context without Geneva circuit participation
- Assessment reports that identify gaps without closing them
- No personal liability framing, no carrier attestation calibration

**The gap:**
The enterprise consultancy creates a beautiful framework document. The Geneva boutique principal receives a PDF. The principal doesn't know what to do with it. The implementation guidance requires a compliance team the boutique doesn't have. The principal paid CHF 40,000 for a document that describes what adequate AI governance looks like in theory — and produces no evidence of what they actually need for their carrier attestation.

**Market signal:** Enterprise consultancy AI governance engagements in Geneva boutique market consistently fail at implementation stage. The methodology outpaces the boutique's execution capacity. The gap is structural — designed in, not accidental.

**Serviceable by DigitalCoa.ch:** Vendor contract review (bounded, specific) + carrier attestation preparation (high-intent, discrete) = the engagement the boutique can actually complete.

### Category 02: Geneva Law Firms (Legal Vertical)

**What they claim:**
- "AI governance advice integrated with your legal obligations"

**What they actually deliver:**

They cannot produce the contemporaneous evidence of human authorization that the carrier attestation actually asks for. The gap between legal analysis and evidence production is unaddressed.

**Market signal:** Geneva law firms are receiving AI governance questions from their own clients and have no methodology to answer them. The trusted advisor doesn't have the trusted answer.

**Serviceable by DigitalCoa.ch:** Evidence architecture briefing (operational, not just legal) + ongoing AI governance advisory (quarterly, EN + FR) = what the law firm can't produce for themselves.

### Category 03: Swiss IT and Cybersecurity Firms

**What they claim:**
- "AI tool security assessment"
- "Vendor due diligence for AI systems"
- "AI data protection compliance"
- "Technical AI governance"

**What they actually deliver:**
- Security assessment of AI tools (are they secure? do they have vulnerabilities?)
- Technical review of vendor infrastructure
- Data protection compliance for AI processing (GDPR/FADP)
- No calibration for professional liability context (Art.321 CC, carrier attestation, personal exposure)
- No human decision authorization architecture
- No carrier attestation preparation methodology

**The gap:**
The IT security firm addresses whether the AI tool is safe to use. The professional accountability framework addresses whether the principal's use of the tool is documented. These are different questions. The IT firm answers one. The professional needs the other.

**Market signal:** IT security firms are not addressing the professional liability documentation layer. The AI tool being secure doesn't produce the evidence that a human authorized the specific decision.

**Serviceable by DigitalCoa.ch:** Carrier attestation preparation (documentation layer, not technical layer) + evidence architecture (human authorization record, not tool security).

### Category 07: Geneva Insurance Brokers

**What they claim:**
- "Complete insurance solutions for AI risk"
- "Professional liability coverage for AI-assisted services"
- "Guidance on AI coverage requirements"

**What they actually deliver:**
- Insurance product placement (coverage identification, policy placement)
- Annual renewal administration
- Claims support after events
- No AI governance methodology or advisory
- No carrier attestation preparation
- No pre-signing review of attestation language

**The gap — The Quadruple Bind:**
The broker faces a structural paradox: they must identify inadequate AI governance to place coverage appropriately, they cannot advise on AI governance without creating new liability exposure, they know their clients need documentation but cannot specify what, and they cannot refuse to place coverage based on undocumented AI governance. The broker is the first point of contact for the AI accountability problem but has no legal mechanism to address it.

**Market signal:** Brokers are actively referring clients to external AI governance advisors because they cannot provide the advice themselves. The referral relationship is forming without a named service category to refer to.

**Serviceable by DigitalCoa.ch:** Pre-engagement calibration with brokers (who the broker calls when a client needs AI governance documentation) + vendor contract review (the bounded entry service the broker can refer to).

## Market-Wide Claim vs. Delivery Failures

### Failure 01: The Vendor Coverage Myth

**Market claim:** "AI tool providers cover your compliance obligations"

**Actual delivery:** Vendor DPAs cover the vendor's processing obligations under data protection law. They do not cover the client's professional liability obligations, carrier attestation requirements, or human decision authorization documentation requirements. The vendor's compliance posture is entirely separate from the firm's accountability architecture.

**Evidence of market recognition:** Carrier forms now explicitly ask firms to confirm they maintain documentation independent of vendor attestations. The carrier is correcting the market's false belief through the form itself.

---

### Failure 02: The Policy = Evidence Myth

**Market claim:** "Having an AI governance policy satisfies your obligations"

**Actual delivery:** The carrier attestation asks for evidence of human authorization for specific AI-assisted decisions — not policy existence. The policy document describes what the firm intends to do. The evidence document shows what actually happened. These are different artifacts with different evidentiary value.

**Evidence of market recognition:** Fourth-generation carrier forms now ask behavioral questions ("describe your process for authorizing AI-assisted decisions") that policy documents cannot answer.

---

### Failure 03: The Framework = Implementation Myth

**Market claim:** "Our AI governance framework will guide your practice"

**Actual delivery:** Enterprise AI governance frameworks require internal compliance teams to implement. Boutique practices don't have compliance teams. The framework describes the destination. It doesn't produce the documentation for the journey.

**Evidence of market recognition:** Geneva boutique engagement with enterprise frameworks consistently fails at implementation. The principal receives the framework and doesn't know what to do with it on Tuesday morning.

---

### Failure 04: The Training = Compliance Myth

**Market claim:** "Your team completed AI governance training"

**Actual delivery:** Training completion demonstrates awareness. It does not produce documentation of human authorization for specific AI-assisted decisions. The training record is not the evidence record.

**Evidence of market recognition:** Carrier forms ask for documentation of actual AI governance processes — not training completion records.

---

## The Net Market Signal

The Geneva AI accountability services market has:
- Many participants claiming coverage
- No participant delivering the specific documentation the carrier asks for
- Universal false beliefs about what vendor coverage means
- A structural gap between framework language and evidence production
- No turnkey service for solo practitioners
- An unnamed category (evidence production) that no competitor has claimed

The white space is specific, measurable, and still open.
