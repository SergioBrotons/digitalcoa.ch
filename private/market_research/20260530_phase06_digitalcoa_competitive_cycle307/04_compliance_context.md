## Section A: The Swiss Regulatory Landscape — Current State (May 2026)

### Active Regulatory Developments

**1. Swiss Federal Council AI Consultation — Results Due End 2026**
- Federal Council launched AI policy consultation with results expected approximately 6 months from current date (end 2026)
- The consultation covers AI governance, accountability, and sector-specific applications
- Market effect: Geneva SMB principals are aware that "something is coming" but don't have specific operational requirements to act on
- Competitive opportunity: Pre-emptive evidence architecture positions the principal for whatever the Federal Council requires — not just whatever the current carrier form requires

**2. Swissdamed — Medical Devices Scope Only (July 1, 2026)**
- Swissdamed (Swiss Medical Devices Registry) comes into force July 1, 2026
- Current scope: medical DEVICES only — NOT general AI attestation
- Market confusion: Many Geneva physicians believe Swissdamed creates new AI documentation requirements for their practice — it does not (as of current scope)
- Competitive opportunity: Correcting Swissdamed scope confusion creates consultation opportunities. The physician who believes Swissdamed requires AI documentation (it doesn't) is looking for help. The advisor who explains the actual scope correctly earns trust.

**3. EU AI Act — Public Awareness Spike August 2026 (~14 months)**
- EU AI Act has been in force since August 2024
- Public awareness spike expected August 2026 when enforcement begins for high-risk systems
- Market effect on Geneva SMBs: Indirect (Swiss companies serving EU clients face EU AI Act requirements as deployers or GPAI users)
- Competitive opportunity: Geneva SMBs serving EU clients face GPAI cascade documentation requirements. The SMB principal who uses a GPAI tool for client services is classified as a deployer under EU AI Act Article 51 — with documentation obligations they don't know about.

**4. FINMA AI Guidance — Ongoing Silence**
- FINMA has not published specific AI attestation requirements for supervised institutions
- SROs (ROMAN, OFD, OAD, OPA) have published principles-only guidance
- Market effect: The absence of published FINMA/SRO requirements creates ambiguity — principals don't know specifically what is required, but carriers are already asking
- Competitive opportunity: The carrier form is more operationally specific than the published FINMA/SRO guidance. Advisors who position around carrier form language (not FINMA guidance) are responding to the actual operational standard.

## Section B: The Carrier Attestation as Regulatory Proxy

### The De Facto Standard

**The fourth-generation carrier attestation form is the most operationally specific AI governance standard applicable to Geneva SMB professional firms.** No published Swiss regulatory guidance is more specific.

**Why carriers are ahead of regulators:**
- Carriers update attestation forms annually or biannually (regulators take years)
- Carriers see claims data that regulators don't (AI-related incidents, near-misses, coverage disputes)
- Carriers underwrite risk directly — imprecise attestation language creates financial exposure
- Carriers have no institutional obligation to achieve regulatory consistency — they optimize for risk precision

### What the Carrier Form Asks For (C307 Synthesis)

**Across all four verticals (from C307 Phase 01–05 findings):**

1. **Evidence of human authorization** for specific AI-assisted decisions
2. **AI tool inventory** specifying which tools are used by which staff for which client tasks
3. **Vendor contract documentation** confirming professional secrecy obligations are met
4. **Ongoing oversight records** demonstrating continued human authorization of AI tool use
5. **Incident documentation** describing what happened when AI tools produced unexpected outputs

**These five categories of evidence are consistent across verticals.** The carrier form doesn't distinguish between law firms and wealth managers on the evidence categories — it distinguishes on the specific AI tools, the specific client tasks, and the specific vendor contracts.

### Competitive Implication

**The carrier form is the specification for the evidence production service.** Advisors who read the regulatory framework (Federal Council consultation, EU AI Act, FADP) are not reading the actual specification. The advisors who read the carrier form are reading the specification.

**DigitalCoa's evidence architecture methodology is calibrated to carrier form language — not to published regulatory guidance.** This is the primary service differentiator. The evidence the methodology produces satisfies the carrier's actual requirements, not the regulator's theoretical framework.

## Section C: Geneva Professional Obligation Framework

### The Three-Authority Structure

**Geneva SMB professional firms are subject to three parallel accountability authorities:**

1. **The ORDRE/SVA/FMH (Professional Association)** — Sets professional conduct standards. Enforces through professional discipline (Ordre des Avocats Genevois, Société Vaudoise de Médecine, FMH). AI use implicates professional secrecy obligations (Article 321 CC for lawyers), fitness to practice obligations (for physicians).

2. **The Carrier (Insurance Underwriter)** — Sets coverage conditions. Conditions coverage on attestation compliance. The attestation is a contractual commitment — non-compliance can void coverage.

3. **The nFADP/FDPIC (Data Protection Authority)** — Sets data processing standards. AI-assisted processing of personal data triggers Art. 9 nFADP obligations (heightened duty for sensitive data). The FDPIC enforces, not the carrier or the ORDRE.

**The three authorities operate on different standards, use different documentation language, and don't coordinate.** The Geneva SMB principal is subject to three accountability frameworks that don't align. Evidence that satisfies one authority may not satisfy another.

### The Documentation Debt Vector

**C307 Phase 05 found: Documentation debt has three independent compounding vectors:**
1. Policy-to-evidence migration gap (policies exist, evidence doesn't)
2. Multi-authority documentation non-alignment (satisfying one authority doesn't satisfy all)
3. Continuous AI operation vs. periodic documentation mismatch (AI operates continuously, documentation is episodic)

**The documentation debt compounds because each vector operates independently.** The principal who addresses one vector (e.g., produces evidence for the carrier) still has the other two vectors accumulating.

### Competitive Implication

**No current competitor addresses the three-authority documentation problem.** The data protection consultancy addresses the FDPIC layer. The carrier form specialist addresses the underwriting layer. The association advisor addresses the ORDRE layer. None address all three simultaneously.

**The evidence architecture methodology addresses all three-authority documentation simultaneously.** The evidence production that satisfies the carrier form is designed to also satisfy ORDRE professional obligation documentation and nFADP Art. 9 data processing documentation. The three-authority alignment is built into the methodology.

## Section D: Geneva SMB Operational Context

### The Solo Practitioner Capacity Constraint

**Geneva SMB professional firms (2–20 employees, 1–5 principals) have a specific operational constraint:** The principal performs client-facing work AND operational management AND compliance administration. There is no internal compliance team. There is no dedicated operations staff. There is no AI governance officer.

**The capacity constraint means:**
- Documentation must be produced by someone outside the practice (advisor or operator) — not by internal staff
- The documentation must require minimal principal time to review and approve
- The documentation must fit within the principal's existing workflow (not require new processes)
- The documentation must be maintainable without dedicated compliance staff

### The Geneva Trust Infrastructure

**The Geneva professional ecosystem has specific institutional trust infrastructure that shapes how accountability services are evaluated:**

**For lawyers (ORDRE des Avocats Genevois):**
- SAV membership is mandatory for Geneva practice
- ORDRE disciplinary proceedings are public
- Client-attorney privilege (Article 321 CC) is enforced by ORDRE
- The ORDRE card is the visible signal of institutional membership

**For physicians (FMH/SVA):**
- FMH membership is the primary professional credential
- HIN connectivity is the data protection trust signal
- cantonal medical license (confédération) is mandatory
- FMH CME requirements are enforced

**For wealth managers (SRO-licensed):**
- ROMAN or OAD/OFD/OFD+ membership is mandatory for practice
- SRO supervision creates specific documentation requirements
- Carrier coverage is conditioned on SRO compliance

**For management consultants:**
- Geneva Chamber of Commerce membership is the primary institutional signal
- ROMAN membership for securities-adjacent consulting
- No mandatory professional association for general consulting

### Competitive Implication

**The Geneva SMB accountability service must operate within the institutional trust infrastructure — not outside it.** Advisors who are not members of the ORDRE/FMH/SRO cannot access the same trust signals as advisors who are members. The institutional membership is the visible signal of shared professional context.

**DigitalCoa's positioning within the Geneva institutional trust infrastructure:**
- ORDRE membership (serves law firm ICP with shared professional context)
- HIN connectivity (serves healthcare and finance ICPs with data protection trust signal)
- Sustained circuit participation (serves all ICPs with demonstrated institutional presence)

## Section E: The EU AI Act GPAI Cascade Effect on Geneva SMBs

### The Deployer Classification

**Geneva SMB professional firms that use GPAI (General Purpose AI) tools in client services are classified as "deployers" under EU AI Act Article 51.** The classification triggers documentation obligations under Annex IX.

**What this means for Geneva SMBs:**
- The firm must implement AI governance that describes how it uses GPAI tools
- The firm must monitor GPAI tool outputs for specific compliance characteristics
- The firm must maintain documentation of human oversight of GPAI tool use
- The firm must be able to demonstrate compliance to market surveillance authorities upon request

**The GPAI cascade is already affecting Geneva SMBs who serve EU-based clients.** The EU client that sends an RFP or DDQ asking about EU AI Act compliance is effectively passing the deployer obligation down to the Geneva SMB vendor.

### C307 Phase 03 Finding: Client DDQ as EU AI Act Trigger

**C307 Phase 03 (Geneva consulting) found: Client DDQ (Due Diligence Questionnaire) is an active commercial gate for Geneva SMBs.** Institutional clients and larger corporations are beginning to include EU AI Act compliance questions in their vendor due diligence. The Geneva consulting firm that cannot answer EU AI Act DDQ questions risks losing mandates.

### Competitive Implication

**The EU AI Act deployer obligation is creating a second evidence documentation requirement beyond the carrier form.** The Geneva SMB that serves EU clients faces:
1. Carrier attestation (domestic insurance market, already in force)
2. EU AI Act deployer documentation (EU client market, active and growing)

**The evidence architecture must serve both requirements simultaneously.** The competitor who serves only the carrier form is only addressing half of the Geneva SMB's accountability exposure.
