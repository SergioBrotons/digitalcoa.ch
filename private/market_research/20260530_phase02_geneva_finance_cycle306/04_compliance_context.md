## Section A — Swiss AI Governance Regulatory Landscape

### FINMA Guidance 08/2024 (December 18, 2024)

**The foundational document:**
FINMA's Guidance 08/2024 on Governance and Risk Management when using Artificial Intelligence is the primary reference for Swiss financial institutions' AI governance obligations. Published December 18, 2024, it establishes a technology-neutral, principles-based, risk-based regulatory approach.

**Key obligations for boutique wealth management firms:**

1. **Governance Framework:** Institutions must establish robust governance frameworks with clearly defined responsibilities and accountabilities for AI development, implementation, monitoring, and use.

2. **AI Inventory:** Institutions must maintain a centralized inventory of all AI applications, classifying their risks based on materiality and likelihood of occurrence, and outlining measures to address and mitigate those risks.

3. **Data and Model Quality:** Strong emphasis on ensuring quality, accuracy, and reliability of data and models — institutions must critically assess data, models, and outcomes.

4. **Testing and Monitoring:** Regular testing, evaluation, and continuous monitoring of AI systems. Routine checks on output quality and reliability. Documentation of processes. Independent review where necessary.

5. **Transparency and Explainability:** AI systems affecting customer outcomes must be transparent; decisions must be understandable and explainable by human operators.

6. **Human Oversight and Responsibility:** Responsibility for decisions cannot be delegated to AI or third parties. Staff involved must possess sufficient AI expertise.

7. **Risk Management Integration:** AI risk management must be integrated into existing governance structures and operational risk frameworks, covering model risks, data-related risks, IT and cyber risks, third-party dependencies, and legal and reputational risks.

**Attestation implication:** FINMA's guidance strongly implies attestation — institutions must be able to demonstrate auditable compliance with these expectations. The guidance does not specify a formal attestation mechanism, but the documentation requirements effectively require evidence production.

### FINMA Technical Blueprint (February 2026)

**Market development:** In February 2026, Swiss companies LatticeFlow AI and Unique AI delivered a FINMA-aligned technical blueprint translating FINMA Guidance 08/2024 into concrete technical assessments. The blueprint provides banks with "audit-ready evidence" for evaluating, deploying, and overseeing AI systems in banking-specific use cases.

**Relevance for boutique wealth management:** The technical blueprint was developed for banks but provides a reference for what "audit-ready evidence" looks like under FINMA's expectations. Boutique wealth management firms can use the blueprint as a reference point for what technical evidence artifacts a supervisory review would expect to see.

**Key insight:** The blueprint confirms that FINMA's standard is evidence-first, not policy-first. The documentation expectation is specific technical artifacts — model validation records, oversight logs, integration chain evidence — not general policy documents.

---

### FINMA Guidance 02/2026 (April 2026)

**Digital fraud risks:** FINMA Guidance 02/2026 addresses digital fraud risks, noting the use of generative AI in fraud attempts. This reinforces the evolving risk landscape financial institutions must manage.

**Implication:** AI governance is not only about compliance — it is about operational risk management. The use of AI creates new fraud vectors that must be managed. Documentation of AI oversight is evidence of risk management capacity.

---

### SIF AI Analysis (Expected Q2 2026)

**State Secretariat for International Finance:** The SIF is expected to publish its comprehensive analysis of financial market regulations concerning AI in Q2 2026. This publication will likely further clarify the technical evidence expectations for Swiss financial institutions.

**Current window:** The period before SIF publication is a documentation preparation window. Firms that address documentation gaps before the SIF publication will be better positioned for whatever clarifications emerge.

---

### Swiss Federal Council AI Approach

**Sector-specific, not horizontal:** Switzerland formally rejected the idea of a horizontal "Swiss AI Act" in February 2025, opting instead for a sector-specific, technology-neutral approach. Amendments to existing sectoral laws implementing the Council of Europe AI Convention are anticipated by end of 2026.

**Implication:** There will be no single Swiss AI Act creating a single compliance framework. Instead, AI governance obligations will emerge from sector-specific regulatory guidance — primarily FINMA for financial institutions. The documentation requirement is therefore FINMA-centric, not EU AI Act-centric (for purely domestic firms), with EU AI Act applying when EU nexus exists.

## Section B — nFADP Article 63 Personal Liability

### The Article 63 Framework

**Art. 63 nFADP — Personal liability for automated decisions:**
Article 63 of the Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection establishes personal liability for decisions based solely on automated processing that significantly affect the data subject. The provision creates a right for affected individuals to request human review of the automated decision.

**Application to wealth management:** When an automated AI system (automated rebalancing, algorithmic allocation) makes a decision that significantly affects a client's financial position, Art. 63 is activated. The affected client can request human review of the decision.

**Personal liability dimension:** The managing director or partner who signed the attestation that the firm's AI systems comply with nFADP requirements is personally exposed if an Art. 63 violation occurs. The carrier's coverage (firm-level) does not discharge the managing director's personal liability.

### Documentation Implication

**Art. 63 creates a specific evidence requirement:** The managing director must be able to demonstrate personal oversight of the AI systems that make automated decisions. This is not the same as a firm-level AI governance policy — it is evidence that the managing director personally reviewed and authorized the AI system's decisions.

**The evidence package must show:**
- That the managing director personally reviewed the AI system's operation
- That the AI system's decision logic was understood and authorized
- That the oversight was documented contemporaneously, not retroactively
- That the managing director can demonstrate human review capacity for Art. 63 requests

**Different from firm-level compliance:** The Art. 63 evidence package serves the managing director's personal protection interest. The carrier attestation serves the firm's insurance coverage interest. These are different documentation architectures with different purposes.

## Section C — Institutional Client DDQ Requirements

### The DDQ as Competitive Gate

**The institutional client DDQ:** Institutional clients (family offices, pension funds, institutional investors) are sending AI governance due diligence questionnaires (DDQs) to wealth management firms as part of the RFP process. The DDQ evaluates the firm's AI governance capability — not the firm's investment performance.

**DDQ content typically includes:**
- AI governance framework description
- Oversight records and review documentation
- Model validation records
- AI vendor integration chain documentation
- Data processing and privacy compliance documentation
- Human oversight mechanisms
- Incident response and review procedures

**Competitive implication:** The DDQ is functioning as a competitive gate. Firms that cannot produce adequate DDQ responses are losing mandates to competitors who can — not because of investment performance, but because the RFP scoring weights AI governance capability.

### Documentation Requirement

**The evidence problem:** The institutional client wants evidence of oversight capability — records showing oversight actually happened — not a policy document describing what should exist. The documentation gap is: policy (what should exist) ≠ evidence (what actually happened).

**Service implication:** The institutional DDQ response file is a discrete service — producing the response file and supporting evidence attachments that demonstrate the firm's AI governance capability. The service is bounded (specific to the DDQ) and high-value (directly tied to mandate outcomes).

## Section D — Carrier Form v4 Attestation

### The Carrier Attestation Mechanism

**Form v4 evidence requirements:** Carriers require wealth management firms to complete attestation forms (Form v4) confirming their AI governance practices. The attestation requires the firm to confirm specific practices — oversight mechanisms, model validation, vendor data processing, integration chain documentation.

**The attestation gap:** The carrier requires evidence of the practices being attested to. The firm's attestation signature confirms the practice exists, but the carrier requires documentation showing the practice was actually performed.

**Documentation requirements typically include:**
- AI vendor contract terms and data processing documentation
- Model validation records
- Integration chain evidence (which data flows between which tools)
- Oversight logs (records showing review occurred)
- Incident documentation and response records

### Personal Attestation Exposure

**The managing director signs:** The carrier attestation is signed by the managing director or partner. The signature represents personal confirmation that the firm's AI governance practices match what was attested.

**If the attestation is inaccurate:** The managing director faces personal exposure — the carrier can challenge the attestation if evidence does not support it, and the managing director's personal assets may be at risk.

**Carrier subrogation:** If the carrier pays a settlement based on an AI incident, the carrier may seek subrogation against the managing director personally if the attestation was inaccurate. The carrier's interest and the managing director's personal interest are not aligned.

## Section E — EU AI Act Deployer Obligations

### The August 2026 Enforcement Date

**EU AI Act timeline:** The EU AI Act enters full enforcement on August 2, 2026 (~14 months from current date). The regulation assigns specific obligations to deployers of AI systems — organizations that use AI systems in their operations.

### Deployer Obligations

**For non-EU AI vendors serving EU clients:** A Geneva boutique wealth management firm using a US-based AI portfolio tool for EU-based clients is an EU AI Act deployer. The firm has obligations under the EU AI Act's deployer provisions.

**Key deployer obligations:**
- Use AI systems in conformity with their designated purpose
- Take into account instructions provided by the provider
- Support the provider in enabling bias and correctness testing
- Maintain human oversight during AI system operation
- Monitor AI system operation and report serious incidents to the provider
- Maintain technical documentation for deployer activities

**Documentation requirement:** The deployer must maintain documentation showing conformity with the AI system's designated purpose and human oversight requirements. This is a specific documentation obligation with a defined deadline.

### Non-EU Vendor Problem

**Structurally difficult for boutique firms:** The EU AI Act's deployer obligations require cooperation from the AI vendor — the vendor must provide technical documentation, support bias testing, and receive incident reports. Non-EU vendors (US-based AI portfolio tools) may not provide this cooperation.

**The boutique firm problem:** A boutique wealth management firm using a non-EU AI vendor cannot satisfy EU AI Act deployer obligations without vendor cooperation. The vendor's unwillingness to provide required documentation creates a structural compliance gap.

**Service implication:** EU AI Act deployer due diligence file production for non-EU vendors is a discrete service — identifying which vendor relationships create EU AI Act exposure and producing the documentation the firm can produce independently while identifying gaps that require vendor cooperation.

## Section F — Cross-Framework Documentation Problem

### FINMA + nFADP + EU AI Act = Multi-Framework Documentation

**The documentation complexity:** Boutique wealth management firms face documentation requirements from multiple frameworks simultaneously:
- FINMA Guidance 08/2024 (evidence of AI governance capability)
- nFADP Art. 5(c), Art. 9, Art. 26, Art. 63 (data protection and automated decision documentation)
- EU AI Act deployer obligations (if EU nexus exists)
- Carrier Form v4 (evidence of specific practices)
- Institutional client DDQ (AI governance capability evidence)

**The problem:** Each framework requires specific documentation types. The firm's documentation architecture must serve multiple frameworks simultaneously. Existing approaches typically produce separate documentation for each framework — creating redundancy and inconsistency.

**The service opportunity:** A documentation architecture that serves multiple frameworks simultaneously — producing evidence that satisfies FINMA, nFADP, EU AI Act, carrier Form v4, and institutional client DDQ requirements from a single evidence production system.

**Cross-framework documentation serving:** This is distinct from existing approaches (which produce separate documentation for each framework) because it is designed from the evidence requirements backward, identifying which evidence artifacts serve multiple frameworks.

## Section G — Documentation Sequencing

### Multi-Authority Documentation Architecture

**The sequencing problem:** The firm must produce documentation in a specific sequence to satisfy multiple authorities. The sequence matters — producing documentation in the wrong order creates gaps that cannot be closed retroactively.

**Correct sequence:**
1. AI vendor integration chain mapping (Art. 26 foundation)
2. AI inventory and risk classification (FINMA + nFADP)
3. AI governance policy (FINMA + institutional client baseline)
4. Continuous oversight methodology (FINMA + carrier Form v4)
5. Model validation records (FINMA + carrier + institutional client)
6. Art. 63 personal due diligence evidence package (personal protection)
7. EU AI Act deployer due diligence file (if EU nexus)

**Why sequence matters:** Each step produces evidence that subsequent steps depend on. The integration chain map is the prerequisite for Art. 26 documentation. The AI inventory is the prerequisite for the governance policy. The continuous oversight methodology is the prerequisite for model validation records.

**The problem:** Firms typically produce documentation in response to specific requests — carrier asks for Form v4, firm produces Form v4 — without following the correct sequence. The resulting documentation is reactive, inconsistent, and inadequate for the full scope of requirements.
