# Market Signals: Geneva Boutique Wealth Management — C306 Phase 02
## Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures (Distinct from C305 Phase 02)

## Market Signal 01 — Institutional DDQ Responses Are Failing at the Evidence Level

**Signal:** Boutique wealth management firms that received institutional client DDQs in 2025 are failing at the evidence level — not the policy level. The institutional clients have moved from evaluating AI governance policies to evaluating AI governance evidence records. Firms that prepared policy documents (2024 response strategy) are discovering that policies are no longer sufficient to pass the DDQ scoring.

**Complaint pattern:** *"We had an AI governance policy. We submitted it. The institutional client came back and asked for records — oversight logs, model validation documents, integration chain records. We didn't have those. The RFP score reflected it."*

**What this reveals:** The institutional client DDQ evaluation has shifted from document review to evidence review. The scoring rubric now awards points for demonstrable oversight capability, not described oversight intent. A policy describes intent. Evidence demonstrates capability.

**Market behavior:** Firms that lost mandates to documentation gaps are now attempting to retrofit evidence production into their operations — a structurally difficult task because retroactive evidence is inherently weaker than contemporaneous evidence.

**Carrier intersection:** The carrier Form v4 is requiring the same evidence types the institutional clients are requesting — oversight records, model validation, integration chain documentation. The two pressure sources (institutional clients and carriers) are now requiring identical evidence types, creating a single documentation architecture that serves both.

**Implication:** The documentation that firms produced for the 2024 institutional client DDQ cycle is now inadequate for the 2026 institutional client DDQ cycle. The standard has moved. The market has not uniformly recognized the shift.

## Market Signal 02 — Carrier Form v4 Attestations Are Being Completed Without Underlying Evidence

**Signal:** Boutique wealth management firms are completing carrier Form v4 attestations without the underlying evidence required to make the attestation accurate. The attestation is signed, but the evidence the attestation represents is not present. The carrier is receiving a signed attestation from a firm that cannot produce the evidence behind it.

**Complaint pattern:** *"The carrier asked me to confirm nine specific things about our AI vendor. I signed the form. But when I actually looked at what I was confirming — I couldn't confirm most of it. I signed anyway because the carrier required it."*

**What this reveals:** The attestation function has become decoupled from the evidence function. The attestation is a compliance mechanism (signed = compliant), but the substantive accuracy of the attestation (does evidence support what was attested) is not being verified by either the carrier or the firm. The carrier receives a signed form. The firm bears the attestation risk without the evidence to support it.

**Market behavior:** Managing directors are describing a pattern where they sign attestations in situations where they cannot substantiate the claims being attested to. The signature completes the compliance requirement but creates personal exposure under Art. 63 if the attestation is later found to be inaccurate.

**FINMA intersection:** FINMA's Guidance 08/2024 requires demonstrable AI governance capability — not completed attestation forms. The carrier Form v4 is a carrier requirement. FINMA's standard is a supervisory requirement. The two are not equivalent. A firm with a completed Form v4 may still fail a FINMA supervisory review if the evidence behind the attestation is absent.

**Implication:** The market has developed a pattern of attestation completion without evidence substantiation. This pattern is creating a gap between compliance documentation (what was signed) and regulatory compliance (what can actually be demonstrated). The gap is the active risk.

## Market Signal 03 — AI Vendor Contract Terms Are Not Calibrated to Client Attestation Requirements

**Signal:** AI vendors serving boutique wealth management firms are providing contract terms that do not align with the evidence requirements the firm's carriers and institutional clients are imposing. The vendor's terms-of-service document does not constitute evidence of the specific documentation types the carrier and institutional client require. The vendor is not providing the evidence artifacts the ICP needs to complete their attestation obligations.

**Complaint pattern:** *"The carrier asked for the vendor's model validation documentation. The vendor said they don't provide that — it's proprietary. But my carrier requires it. I have a vendor contract that doesn't give me what I need for my attestation."*

**What this reveals:** The AI vendor market is not designed to produce the evidence artifacts boutique wealth management firms need to satisfy carrier and institutional client requirements. The vendor's incentive is to protect proprietary model information, not to produce audit-ready evidence for the vendor's clients. The ICP has a vendor relationship but not a documentation relationship with their AI vendors.

**Market behavior:** Firms are discovering that vendor relationships do not automatically produce the documentation the carrier and institutional clients require. The vendor will not produce the evidence the firm's carrier requires unless the contract specifically requires it — and most vendor contracts do not specify evidence production obligations.

**nFADP Art. 26 intersection:** The vendor integration chain means client PII is processed across multiple vendor platforms. The vendor's terms govern which data the vendor processes and how. The firm's attestation obligations require documenting data processing across the integration chain. The vendor terms and the attestation requirements are not aligned by design.

**Implication:** The AI vendor contract review is a discrete service — reviewing the vendor contract to identify gaps between what the vendor provides and what the firm's attestation obligations require. The output is a vendor contract gap analysis and a recommendation for contract amendments or supplementary evidence production.

## Market Signal 04 — FINMA Guidance 08/2024 Is Being Interpreted Without a Technical Blueprint

**Signal:** Boutique wealth management firms are attempting to interpret FINMA Guidance 08/2024 into operational practice without a technical implementation blueprint. The guidance is principles-based — it establishes the standard (governance framework, AI inventory, risk classification, data quality, testing, monitoring, transparency, human oversight) but does not specify the technical artifacts required to demonstrate compliance. Firms are producing general AI governance policies and believing they have addressed the guidance, when the guidance actually requires specific evidence types.

**Complaint pattern:** *"We read FINMA's guidance. We created an AI governance framework. We thought we were compliant. Then we saw what the technical blueprint from LatticeFlow AI and Unique AI actually requires — and we're not there. Our framework is high-level. The technical blueprint is specific."*

**What this reveals:** The gap between principles-based guidance and technical implementation is not being bridge by the average boutique wealth management firm. The firm's internal resources (compliance, legal, IT) can produce a principles-based framework but cannot translate it into the specific technical evidence artifacts a supervisory review would require.

**Market behavior:** The February 2026 technical blueprint (LatticeFlow AI + Unique AI, FINMA-aligned) represents a market development that has clarified what FINMA-compliant evidence actually looks like. Boutique wealth management firms did not participate in the blueprint development and are not uniformly aware of its existence or implications.

**SIF intersection:** The State Secretariat for International Finance (SIF) is expected to publish its comprehensive analysis of financial market regulations concerning AI in Q2 2026. This publication will likely further clarify the technical evidence expectations for Swiss financial institutions. The current period (pre-SIF publication) represents a documentation preparation window.

**Implication:** A technical gap analysis — comparing the firm's current AI governance documentation against the FINMA technical blueprint requirements — is a discrete service with direct regulatory relevance. The output identifies specific evidence gaps the firm must address before a supervisory review.

## Market Signal 05 — Boutique Firms Cannot Scale AI Governance Documentation to Match Operational Reality

**Signal:** Boutique wealth management firms (50–200 employees) have AI governance documentation needs that are functionally equivalent to large institutions, but they lack the internal resources to produce and maintain the documentation. The documentation requirements for AI oversight (continuous monitoring, model validation records, integration chain evidence, vendor attestation management) require dedicated staff that the boutique firm does not have.

**Complaint pattern:** *"I have an 80-person firm. I have the same AI oversight requirements as a bank. I don't have a compliance department. I have a part-time operations manager who also does IT."*

**What this reveals:** The documentation requirements for AI governance are not calibrated to firm size. FINMA's expectations for AI oversight do not distinguish between a 50-person boutique and a 5,000-person bank. The evidence requirements are similar; the resource base is not.

**Market behavior:** Boutique firms are either under-producing documentation (operating below the required standard) or over-allocating internal resources to documentation production (creating operational inefficiency). Neither approach is sustainable.

**Carrier intersection:** The carrier Form v4 requires the same evidence types regardless of firm size. The boutique firm with limited internal resources must find a way to produce evidence that a large institution produces with a dedicated compliance team. This is the structural service gap.

**Implication:** Turnkey evidence production for boutique firms — where the service provider manages the documentation production on behalf of the firm — is the appropriate service model. The ICP does not need to become a documentation expert. The ICP needs a service that produces the evidence they cannot produce internally.

## Market Signal 06 — nFADP Art. 63 Personal Liability Is Being Treated as a Hypothetical Risk

**Signal:** Boutique wealth management managing directors are treating nFADP Art. 63 personal liability as a hypothetical future risk rather than an active personal exposure. The language pattern is consistent: *"I know Article 63 is a concern, but I'm not sure it applies to my situation."* The managing director has not separated the firm's carrier coverage from their personal exposure. They believe the carrier's payment of a settlement discharges their personal liability.

**Complaint pattern:** *"My carrier covers me for AI incidents. If something goes wrong, the carrier handles it."* — followed by discovery that carrier subrogation rights and Art. 63 personal liability are separate tracks with different resolution mechanisms.

**What this reveals:** The structural misunderstanding about carrier coverage and personal liability is not being corrected by the market until an incident occurs. Managing directors believe they are protected by carrier coverage when they are not protected for the specific risk Art. 63 addresses.

**Market behavior:** The market is not self-correcting on this point. The managing director's understanding of their personal exposure under Art. 63 does not change until they receive specific legal counsel on the separation of carrier coverage and personal criminal liability. The documentation need (Art. 63 personal due diligence evidence package) is not being identified by the market without external prompting.

**Legal intersection:** The C306 Phase 01 (legal) findings on broker accountability gap are directly relevant here — the broker's E&O coverage does not transfer to the managing director's personal Art. 63 exposure. The documentation architecture required for personal protection under Art. 63 is distinct from the documentation architecture required for carrier attestation.

**Implication:** The Art. 63 personal due diligence evidence package is a discrete service specifically serving the managing director's personal protection interest. It is not a firm-level compliance document — it is a personal evidence package demonstrating the managing director's personal oversight of AI systems. The target buyer is the managing director or partner, not the compliance department.

## Market Signal 07 — AI Tool Proliferation Is Creating Integration Chain Documentation Debt

**Signal:** Boutique wealth management firms are adding AI tools to their operations faster than they are documenting the integration chains between those tools. The typical boutique firm has 4–8 AI tools in active use (portfolio analytics, risk modeling, CRM with AI features, automated rebalancing, client reporting, compliance monitoring). Each tool processes client data. The integration between tools (data flows, API connections, shared client records) is not documented. The documentation debt compounds with each new tool added.

**Complaint pattern:** *"We added a new AI analytics tool last quarter. It's connected to our CRM. I don't know exactly what data flows between them. I know client data is involved. I didn't document the connection."*

**What this reveals:** The integration chain documentation debt is a direct consequence of operational tool adoption without documentation process. The firm adopts tools to improve operations; the documentation process does not keep pace with the operational changes. The result is an integration chain that exists operationally but is not documented.

**Market behavior:** The integration chain documentation debt is invisible until a triggering event (carrier attestation, institutional client DDQ, supervisory review) requires the firm to document the data flows. At that point, the firm must reconstruct documentation for an integration chain that was never intentionally documented.

**nFADP Art. 26 intersection:** Art. 26 requires documentation of data processing activities. The integration chain between AI tools represents data processing activities that must be documented. The undocumented integration chain is an active Art. 26 compliance gap.

**Implication:** An AI vendor integration chain mapping exercise — documenting which tools are connected, which client data passes between them, and which vendor infrastructure processes the data — is a discrete service that creates the Art. 26 documentation foundation. The output is a data flow map and a gap analysis against Art. 26 requirements.

## Market Signal 08 — EU AI Act Deployer Obligations Are Approaching Without Preparation Infrastructure

**Signal:** Boutique wealth management firms with EU-based clients or EU-nexus AI tool vendors are approaching the EU AI Act August 2026 enforcement date without understanding their deployer obligations. The EU AI Act assigns specific due diligence requirements to deployers of AI systems — including conformity assessment, technical documentation, and human oversight obligations. The boutique firm using non-EU AI vendors for portfolio analytics and client management is a deployer with obligations it has not begun to address.

**Complaint pattern:** *"We use an AI portfolio tool from a US vendor. Our clients are partly EU-based. I've heard of the EU AI Act but I don't know what it requires of us as a user of AI."*

**What this reveals:** The EU AI Act deployer obligations are structurally under-resourced at the boutique wealth management level. The regulation requires technical documentation, conformity assessment, and oversight mechanisms that the typical boutique firm has not produced. The enforcement date is approximately 14 months away.

**Market behavior:** The boutique firm is not uniformly aware that the EU AI Act applies to them. The understanding gap is not willful — it is a consequence of the regulation's complexity and the boutique firm's lack of regulatory resources.

**Cross-border intersection:** The EU AI Act's extraterritorial reach (applies to non-EU entities placing AI systems on the EU market or serving EU-based users) means that a Geneva boutique using a US-based AI portfolio tool for EU-based clients is likely an EU AI Act deployer with active obligations.

**Implication:** An EU AI Act deployer readiness assessment — identifying which obligations apply to the firm, which AI systems are in scope, and what documentation must be produced — is a discrete service with a defined deadline. The assessment has a known end date (August 2026) and a defined output (deployer due diligence documentation).
