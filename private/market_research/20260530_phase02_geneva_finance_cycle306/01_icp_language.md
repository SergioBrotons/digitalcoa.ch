# ICP Language: Geneva Boutique Wealth Management — C306 Phase 02
## How the ICP Talks About AI Problems (Distinct from C305 Phase 02)

---

## The ICP Profile

Boutique wealth management firm, 50–200 employees, Geneva-based. Managing director or partner personally accountable. Concerned about institutional client AI governance questionnaires, carrier attestation requirements, FINMA expectations, and nFADP Art.63 personal liability. Multiple AI tools in portfolio construction, analytics, and client management. Serving HNWI, UHNWI, family offices, institutional clients, and EU-based clients.

---

## Language Pattern 01 — "I didn't know I was supposed to document that"

The ICP describes AI governance as something that happened to them — tools were adopted, integrations were connected, vendor relationships were established — without an intentional documentation process happening alongside. The language reveals a gap between tool adoption and evidence production: *"We started using the portfolio analytics tool in 2023. We signed the vendor agreement. It was integrated into our workflow. I didn't know there was something I was supposed to document about it."*

The triggering moment is typically one of two events: an institutional client sends a DDQ, or a carrier requests evidence for Form v4. The ICP discovers the documentation gap at the moment of highest consequence — when the evidence is needed, not when the gap was created.

**How they describe the problem:**
- *"We didn't know we needed to document the integration."*
- *"The vendor said everything was in order."*
- *"I thought the vendor's terms covered us."*
- *"I assumed the carrier's questions were formalities."*
- *"Nobody told me I needed a separate documentation file for this."*

The ICP is not avoiding documentation — they are discovering for the first time that it was required. The language reveals that the documentation requirement was never made explicit at the time of tool adoption.

---

## Language Pattern 02 — "The institutional client asked questions I didn't know how to answer"

The ICP describes the institutional client DDQ as a documentation assessment — not a technology assessment. The questions asked about AI governance, oversight records, and evidence of decision documentation don't match the mental model the ICP has of their AI tools. The ICP describes the DDQ as revealing a gap between what they understand their AI tools to be and what the institutional client expects the documentation to show.

**How they describe the problem:**
- *"The institutional client asked about our AI oversight framework. I didn't know what they were actually asking for."*
- *"They wanted to know who reviews the AI decisions and when. I don't have a process for that."*
- *"They asked for evidence of model validation. We don't have a model validation record."*
- *"The questions were specific. My answers were vague. I knew that wasn't going to be enough."*
- *"The institutional client was evaluating governance, not investment performance."*

The ICP describes the DDQ as exposing a gap between operational reality and documentation reality. The operational reality (AI tools are in use, decisions are being made) is not reflected in documentation reality (no records of decisions, oversight, or validation).

---

## Language Pattern 03 — "My carrier asked me to attest to something I don't control"

The ICP describes carrier attestation as creating a documentation problem where the attestation language requires vendor cooperation the ICP cannot compel. The carrier Form v4 requires attestation to AI vendor terms, data processing practices, and model oversight — but the vendor's willingness to provide the specific documentation the carrier requires is outside the ICP's control.

**How they describe the problem:**
- *"The carrier asked me to confirm the vendor's data processing terms. I don't control what the vendor discloses."*
- *"The attestation requires the AI vendor to sign something. The vendor won't sign it."*
- *"I have a carrier requirement I cannot satisfy because the vendor won't cooperate."*
- *"The carrier wants evidence from the vendor. The vendor says they're not required to provide it."*
- *"I signed the attestation but I couldn't actually confirm what I was attesting to."*

The ICP describes this as a structural problem — they have a carrier obligation they are fulfilling by attestation, but the evidence behind the attestation is incomplete because the vendor has not provided it. The attestation is technically completed but substantively inadequate.

---

## Language Pattern 04 — "I'm the one who signs. I'm the one who's exposed."

The ICP describes personal liability under nFADP Art.63 as creating a different risk calculus than firm-level compliance. The managing director or partner who signs the carrier attestation is not just representing the firm — they are personally exposed if the attestation does not reflect reality. The language reveals that the personal exposure is not theoretical — it is the managing director's actual concern when they describe the documentation gap.

**How they describe the problem:**
- *"I sign the attestation. If something goes wrong, I'm the one they're looking at."*
- *"The carrier coverage is for the firm. Article 63 is personal."*
- *"I need documentation that protects me specifically, not just the firm."*
- *"My broker's E&O covers the firm. It doesn't cover my personal Article 63 exposure."*
- *"If there's an incident and they investigate me personally, what do I show them?"*

The ICP separates firm liability from personal liability and recognizes that standard firm-level documentation does not address the personal exposure. The managing director wants evidence that demonstrates their personal oversight — not just the firm's AI governance policy.

---

## Language Pattern 05 — "The systems operate continuously. My documentation is annual."

The ICP describes a structural mismatch between how AI systems operate and how documentation is produced. The AI tools in use — automated rebalancing, algorithmic portfolio allocation, real-time risk analytics — operate continuously without human intervention at the decision point. The documentation cadence (annual review, quarterly report) does not match the operational cadence of the AI systems.

**How they describe the problem:**
- *"The rebalancing algorithm runs every day. My documentation is annual."*
- *"I review the AI output monthly. The AI is making decisions every hour."*
- *"I don't have a way to document what the AI does between review cycles."*
- *"The system runs autonomously. I don't know what decisions it made last week."*
- *"There is no record of what the AI actually did. Only what the AI was supposed to do."*

The ICP describes a gap between system operation and documentation coverage. The AI systems are producing outcomes continuously, but the documentation only captures the periodic review — not the continuous operation.

---

## Language Pattern 06 — "The vendor is a black box. I can't document what I don't see."

The ICP describes AI vendor opacity as creating a documentation problem where the ICP cannot produce the evidence the carrier or institutional client requires because the vendor does not provide the necessary visibility. The ICP has a vendor relationship but not a documentation relationship — the vendor's internal processes, model updates, and decision logic are not visible to the ICP.

**How they describe the problem:**
- *"The vendor says they handle everything. I don't know what that means."*
- *"The AI model updates automatically. I don't know when it changes."*
- *"The vendor's documentation is a terms-of-service document. It doesn't describe how the model actually works."*
- *"I can't show the institutional client how the AI makes decisions. The vendor doesn't disclose that."*
- *"The vendor is a black box. I can only show them the inputs and outputs. Not the logic."*

The ICP describes the AI vendor as creating a documentation boundary — they can document the contract and the outputs, but the internal logic and model changes are opaque. The carrier requires evidence of oversight that cannot be produced without vendor cooperation the ICP cannot compel.

---

## Language Pattern 07 — "We have one person who knows how this works"

The ICP describes AI governance knowledge as concentrated in one or two individuals within the firm — typically the CIO or the IT lead who managed the vendor relationship. This concentration creates a single-point-of-failure risk: if that individual leaves, the firm's institutional knowledge of its own AI governance documentation leaves with them.

**How they describe the problem:**
- *"Ask me about our AI setup. I'm the only one who knows."*
- *"If I leave, nobody here knows which vendors we're using for what."*
- *"We have documentation but nobody knows how to interpret it."*
- *"The CIO negotiated the vendor contracts. He's the only one who understands the terms."*
- *"We have an AI governance policy but nobody can explain how it connects to our actual workflow."*

The ICP describes a knowledge concentration problem that creates two risks: operational (if the key person leaves, oversight continuity is broken) and documentation (if the key person leaves, the evidence architecture cannot be maintained or audited).

---

## Language Pattern 08 — "Our clients are starting to ask about AI"

The ICP describes a shift in client behavior — the institutional clients and sophisticated family offices are now asking AI governance questions in their due diligence process. This is a new behavior pattern: twelve months ago, AI governance was not a client due diligence topic. Now it is. The ICP is encountering AI governance questions in client relationships where they were not previously present.

**How they describe the problem:**
- *"Our largest institutional client sent us a questionnaire about AI last quarter. We didn't have anything to send back."*
- *"The family office is asking who oversees the algorithmic allocation. We don't have a formal answer."*
- *"Clients are now asking about our AI governance in the onboarding process. We have to tell them something."*
- *"We lost a mandate to a competitor who had better AI governance documentation. We didn't even know that was a scoring factor."*

The ICP describes this as a client-driven trigger — the documentation need is being created by clients asking questions the ICP cannot answer. The ICP recognizes that this client behavior will intensify, not diminish.
