# Competitive Analysis: Geneva Boutique Wealth Management — C306 Phase 02
## What Exists in Market vs. What's Missing (Distinct from C305 Phase 02)

---

## Section A — What the Market Currently Offers

### Category 1: General AI Governance Consultancies

**What exists:**
Large international consultancies (Big Four, major strategy firms) offer AI governance frameworks for financial institutions. The deliverable is typically a comprehensive AI governance policy document — a framework that describes what the firm should have in place.

**Strengths:**
- Internationally recognized brand
- Cross-jurisdictional regulatory knowledge
- Large teams capable of handling complex engagements

**Weaknesses for the Geneva Boutique WM ICP:**
- Not Geneva-calibrated — the frameworks are generic and require significant localization
- Not firm-size-calibrated — the documentation output assumes compliance department resources the boutique firm does not have
- Not bilingual — the output is typically English-only
- Not positioned for personal liability exposure — the framework serves firm-level compliance, not the managing director's individual protection
- High cost — engagements starting at CHF 50,000+
- Slow timeline — engagements typically 3–6 months
- No evidence production — the deliverable is a policy document, not evidence records

**Gap:** The boutique firm receives a framework document, not a evidence production system. The framework describes intent; the firm's carrier and institutional client require evidence of capability.

---

### Category 2: Swiss Financial Regulatory Consultancies

**What exists:**
Swiss-based regulatory consultancies offer FINMA compliance services including AI governance guidance. These firms understand the Swiss regulatory context and produce documentation calibrated to Swiss supervisory expectations.

**Strengths:**
- Swiss regulatory context expertise
- Local language capability (German, French, Italian)
- Established relationships with Swiss financial regulators
- FINMA interpretation knowledge

**Weaknesses for the Geneva Boutique WM ICP:**
- Often focused on large institutions (banks, insurers) — boutique firms are not the primary ICP
- Documentation output is policy-oriented, not evidence-oriented
- No specific Art. 63 personal liability documentation capability
- No integration chain mapping service
- No institutional DDQ response file production
- Not positioned for the carrier Form v4 evidence requirements

**Gap:** The Swiss regulatory consultancy offers a compliance document, not evidence production. The compliance document satisfies a general supervisory expectation but does not produce the specific evidence types required by carriers and institutional clients.

---

### Category 3: Legal Firms

**What exists:**
Geneva and Zurich-based legal firms offer AI governance legal opinions, contract review, and regulatory compliance advice. Services include AI vendor contract review, FINMA guidance interpretation, and nFADP compliance assessment.

**Strengths:**
- Legal privilege protection
- Contractual expertise (vendor agreements, carrier attestation)
- Art. 63 personal liability understanding
- Regulatory interpretation authority

**Weaknesses for the Geneva Boutique WM ICP:**
- Document review, not evidence production — legal opinions describe what should exist, not the ongoing production of evidence records
- Hourly billing model creates cost uncertainty
- Not positioned for continuous oversight documentation (methodology development)
- No institutional DDQ response file production
- No AI vendor integration chain mapping
- No turnkey evidence production model

**Gap:** The legal firm provides legal interpretation, not evidence production infrastructure. The managing director receives legal advice about what they need, not a service that continuously produces the evidence they need.

---

### Category 4: IT and Cybersecurity Consultancies

**What exists:**
Cybersecurity firms offer AI vendor security assessments, data processing documentation, and technology risk management. Services include vendor security questionnaires, penetration testing, and data protection impact assessments.

**Strengths:**
- Technical security assessment capability
- Data protection expertise (GDPR, nFADP)
- Vendor security evaluation methodology
- Technical documentation production

**Weaknesses for the Geneva Boutique WM ICP:**
- Security assessment is not the same as governance evidence — the carrier and institutional client require oversight evidence, not security assessment results
- No positioning for institutional DDQ response files
- No positioning for carrier Form v4 evidence requirements
- No continuous AI oversight methodology
- Not calibrated for the specific evidence types FINMA requires
- Security posture and governance capability are different documentation types

**Gap:** The cybersecurity firm produces a security assessment. The carrier and institutional client require governance evidence. These are different document types serving different purposes.

---

### Category 5: AI Tool Vendors

**What exists:**
AI tool vendors (portfolio analytics platforms, risk modeling tools, CRM systems with AI features) provide vendor documentation, terms of service, and model specifications. Some vendors offer compliance packages for enterprise clients.

**Strengths:**
- Direct access to model documentation
- Integration technical specifications
- Enterprise compliance documentation packages

**Weaknesses for the Geneva Boutique WM ICP:**
- Vendor documentation serves the vendor's interest (liability protection, proprietary model protection), not the ICP's interest (evidence production for carriers and institutional clients)
- Vendor is not positioned to produce the oversight evidence the ICP needs — the vendor's documentation describes the tool, not the ICP's use of the tool
- Vendor terms of service do not satisfy carrier Form v4 evidence requirements
- Vendor cannot produce the ICP's personal Art. 63 documentation
- Conflict of interest — the vendor is not an independent evidence producer

**Gap:** The vendor produces vendor documentation. The ICP needs ICP evidence documentation. These are fundamentally different document types with different purposes and different audiences.

---

### Category 6: In-House Compliance Teams

**What exists:**
Boutique wealth management firms with compliance teams attempt to manage AI governance documentation internally. The compliance team produces AI governance policies, manages vendor relationships, and responds to carrier and institutional client requests.

**Strengths:**
- Internal institutional knowledge
- Ongoing operational awareness
- No external cost

**Weaknesses for the Geneva Boutique WM ICP:**
- Compliance teams at boutique firms are typically 1–3 people with multiple regulatory responsibilities — AI governance documentation is one of many competing demands
- No dedicated AI governance documentation expertise — the team is learning simultaneously as documentation is produced
- Documentation is reactive (produced in response to requests) rather than proactive (produced as part of an ongoing evidence architecture)
- No calibration to FINMA technical blueprint
- No methodology for continuous AI oversight documentation
- Single point of failure (one person leaves, documentation expertise leaves)

**Gap:** The in-house compliance team produces documentation within resource constraints, but the documentation produced does not meet the evidence standard required by carriers and institutional clients. The team is working hard at the wrong deliverable.

## Section B — What Is Missing from the Market

### Missing Capability 01: Institutional DDQ Response File Production

**What exists:** No market service specifically produces institutional DDQ response files for boutique wealth management firms. The market offers general AI governance frameworks that the firm must translate into DDQ responses.

**What's missing:** A turnkey service that receives the institutional client DDQ, analyzes the firm's existing documentation against each question, produces the response file with supporting evidence attachments, and calibrates the response to the specific institutional client's scoring rubric.

**Why it matters:** The institutional DDQ response file is the discrete service with direct revenue consequence. A mandate won or lost is directly attributable to the quality of the DDQ response file. The service is not "help me write a policy" — it is "produce the response file that wins the mandate."

**Market positioning:** The service is distinct from general AI governance consulting because the deliverable is specifically the DDQ response file — a bounded, high-value document with measurable competitive impact.

---

### Missing Capability 02: AI Vendor Integration Chain Mapping

**What exists:** No market service specifically maps AI vendor integration chains for boutique wealth management firms. Cybersecurity firms assess vendor security; legal firms review vendor contracts; neither produces a data flow map showing how client data moves between AI tools.

**What's missing:** A service that documents the integration chain between AI tools — identifying which tools are connected, which client data passes between them, which vendor infrastructure processes the data, and what documentation gap exists between the integration chain and Art. 26 nFADP requirements.

**Why it matters:** The integration chain documentation debt is active and compounding. The firm cannot produce Art. 26 documentation for data processing activities it has not documented. The mapping exercise is the prerequisite for all downstream Art. 26 compliance documentation.

**Market positioning:** The service is distinct from security assessment because the output is a data flow map (governance evidence) not a security posture assessment. The audience is the carrier, the institutional client, and FINMA — not the IT department.

---

### Missing Capability 03: Continuous AI Oversight Methodology Development

**What exists:** No Geneva-calibrated methodology exists for what "continuous AI oversight documentation" means in practice for boutique wealth management firms. The market offers general guidance (have a policy, conduct reviews) but no methodology that defines the documentation cadence, format, and evidence type for continuous AI systems.

**What's missing:** A methodology that defines: what oversight means operationally, how frequently it must be documented, in what format documentation must be produced, and how the documentation demonstrates oversight capability to carriers and institutional clients.

**Why it matters:** The structural mismatch between continuous AI system operation and periodic documentation is a named market failure. The firm that has automated rebalancing running 24/7 but produces annual oversight documentation has a documentation architecture problem no existing market service addresses.

**Market positioning:** The methodology is not a template — it is a developed framework calibrated to the specific AI systems the firm operates. The service is methodology development, not document production.

---

### Missing Capability 04: Art. 63 Personal Due Diligence Evidence Package

**What exists:** No market service specifically produces Art. 63 personal due diligence evidence packages for managing directors and partners. Legal firms offer opinions on Art. 63 exposure; compliance firms offer firm-level nFADP documentation. Neither produces a personal evidence package specifically serving the managing director's individual protection interest.

**What's missing:** A service that produces a personal evidence package demonstrating the managing director's personal oversight of AI systems — distinct from the firm's carrier attestation documentation architecture, specifically designed to address Art. 63 personal liability exposure.

**Why it matters:** The managing director's personal exposure under Art. 63 is not addressed by firm-level documentation. The carrier coverage and personal criminal liability are separate legal tracks. The managing director needs evidence of personal oversight, not evidence of firm-level governance.

**Market positioning:** The target buyer is the managing director or partner, not the compliance department. The evidence package is personal protection infrastructure, not firm compliance documentation.

---

### Missing Capability 05: Carrier Form v4 Evidence Production

**What exists:** No market service specifically produces the evidence artifacts required for carrier Form v4 completion. Firms receive the Form v4 and must independently produce the evidence. No service exists that systematically produces the specific evidence types the Form v4 requires.

**What's missing:** A service that receives the carrier's Form v4 requirements, identifies the specific evidence types required, maps the firm's existing documentation against those requirements, and produces the missing evidence artifacts — including vendor attestation documentation, model validation records, integration chain evidence, and oversight logs.

**Why it matters:** The Form v4 evidence requirements are specific and documented. The firm's failure to produce the required evidence creates a carrier coverage gap and a personal attestation exposure. The service that produces the evidence directly addresses the identified gap.

**Market positioning:** The service is bounded by the carrier's specific requirements — it produces exactly what the carrier's Form v4 requires, no more and no less. The output is audit-ready evidence in the format carriers expect.

---

### Missing Capability 06: EU AI Act Deployer Due Diligence File

**What exists:** No market service specifically produces EU AI Act deployer due diligence files for non-EU AI vendors serving Swiss financial institutions. The EU AI Act's August 2026 enforcement creates a defined documentation obligation with a known deadline. No service exists that specifically addresses this need for Swiss boutique wealth management firms.

**What's missing:** A service that identifies which EU AI Act deployer obligations apply to the firm, maps the firm's existing AI tools against those obligations, and produces the technical documentation (conformity assessment, human oversight documentation, deployer due diligence file) required for compliance.

**Why it matters:** The enforcement date is known (August 2026). The obligations are defined. The service has a defined scope and a defined deadline. The firm that does not begin preparation now will face enforcement without the required documentation.

**Market positioning:** The service is specifically for firms with EU-nexus AI vendors — non-EU vendors serving EU-based clients or Swiss firms with EU client relationships. The deliverable is a deployer due diligence file calibrated to the specific AI systems in use.

## Section C — Competitive Positioning Analysis

### The Gap Between What Exists and What's Needed

| What Exists | What's Missing | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| AI governance frameworks (consultancies) | Evidence production systems | Policy ≠ oversight capability evidence |
| Swiss regulatory consulting (legal, compliance) | Personal Art. 63 evidence package | Firm documentation ≠ personal protection |
| Vendor security assessments | Integration chain data flow mapping | Security posture ≠ governance evidence |
| General policy documents | Institutional DDQ response file production | Frameworks don't win mandates |
| Annual review documentation | Continuous AI oversight methodology | Periodic review doesn't match continuous operation |
| Vendor terms of service | Carrier Form v4 evidence production | Vendor docs ≠ carrier-required evidence |
| GDPR/nFADP data protection | EU AI Act deployer due diligence file | Data protection ≠ AI Act compliance |

### The Unoccupied Position

**The market offers frameworks. What the ICP needs is evidence production.**

The unoccupied position is turnkey evidence production for the Geneva boutique wealth management ICP — a service that produces the specific evidence types required by carriers, institutional clients, and FINMA, calibrated to the firm's size and operational context.

The evidence production system operates as an ongoing service relationship (retainer) rather than a one-time project (framework document). The output is evidence records, not policy documents. The buyer is the managing director (personal exposure) and the compliance function (operational need).

The competitive differentiation is not expertise (others have expertise) — it is operational capability (others offer expertise; we produce evidence). The service model is: receive the requirement (DDQ, Form v4, FINMA request) → produce the evidence → deliver the documentation.
