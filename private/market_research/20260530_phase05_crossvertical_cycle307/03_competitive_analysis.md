## Cross-Vertical Competitive Landscape

The cross-vertical competitive analysis identifies white space that is not addressed by any competitor in the Geneva market across legal, finance, consulting, and healthcare verticals. Each finding represents a position that is genuinely unoccupied.

---

## Finding 01: No Competitor Addresses the Pre-Signing Intervention Point

**The white space:** Every competitor in the Geneva market responds to AI governance demand after the ICP has already signed something. The market is dominated by post-signature response (attestation review, gap analysis, remediation planning) and pre-awareness education (seminars, articles, guides). The single highest-value intervention point — the moment before a carrier attestation is signed — is unoccupied. No Geneva-based competitor offers a pre-signing carrier form review as a discrete service.

**The mechanism:** The managing partner receives a carrier form. They have two options: sign it (after some internal review) or delay (and risk carrier relationship consequences). No one tells them what the specific language commits them to before they sign. The pre-signing intervention gap is not a missed educational opportunity — it is a missed accountability protection.

**Why it's vacant:** Pre-signing review requires specific expertise in both the regulatory framework and the specific carrier form language. It is a targeted, bounded service that does not scale to volume the way training programs or framework documentation do. The service requires an expert who can review form language against the ICP's specific practice and produce a specific written assessment. This is a high-value, low-volume service that most advisory firms are not structured to deliver profitably.

**The positioning opportunity:** "Before you sign your carrier's AI attestation form — send it to me. I'll tell you what your signature actually commits you to, where your actual practice falls short of what you're certifying to, and what to do about it. Same working day."

## Finding 02: The Evidence Architecture Category Is Unnamed in the Market

**The white space:** The service that produces contemporaneous documentation of what AI systems actually did — as distinct from policy documentation of what AI systems should do — has no established category name in the Geneva market. "AI governance" is too broad. "Compliance" implies regulatory box-checking. "AI risk management" sounds like enterprise software. The evidence production service is nameless, which means no competitor has explicitly claimed it.

**The mechanism:** The market is full of AI governance frameworks, AI policies, AI risk assessments, and AI compliance documents. None of these produce evidence of what AI actually did. The ICP who receives a carrier attestation request or a client questionnaire is being asked for evidence — not frameworks. The gap between framework documents and evidence records is the white space.

**Why it's vacant:** Evidence architecture requires a different documentation paradigm — continuous event logging, not periodic summary production. It requires operational understanding of how AI systems actually operate in professional contexts. It requires the ability to map evidence production to multiple authority demands simultaneously. This is a specialized expertise that combines legal, regulatory, and operational knowledge that most single-vertical advisors don't have.

**The positioning opportunity:** "The market offers frameworks. We produce evidence." The category naming itself is the primary competitive act. Whoever names the evidence production category in the Geneva market will own the category — because no one else has claimed it.

## Finding 03: The Delegation Accountability Documentation System Is Structurally Absent

**The white space:** No competitor in the Geneva market offers a documentation system designed specifically for the accountability gap created by AI delegation in small professional firms. Existing solutions — enterprise AI governance platforms, supervision training, policy templates — are designed for organizations with the resources to implement them. The specific problem of "I delegate AI use and am personally accountable for it but cannot personally review every output" has no proportionate solution.

**The mechanism:** Enterprise AI governance platforms require implementation resources that a 2–5 person firm doesn't have. Supervision training assumes the supervisor can substantively evaluate AI outputs, which they cannot. Policy templates produce documents that satisfy a carrier's request for a policy without producing evidence that the policy is followed. The delegation accountability documentation system — a lightweight, proportionate system that documents the delegation structure rather than every individual AI use event — is structurally absent.

**Why it's vacant:** Building a proportionate delegation documentation system requires understanding both the accountability structure (what must be documented to protect the ICP) and the operational reality (what a small firm can actually maintain). Most advisors understand one side. Few understand both.

**The positioning opportunity:** "You can't stop delegating to AI. You can't review every output yourself. But you can document the delegation structure — and that's what protects you. That's what we build."

## Finding 04: The Personal Exposure / Carrier Coverage Separation Is Not Articulated by Any Competitor

**The white space:** The distinction between personal liability exposure (Article 321 CC, Article 63 nFADP, Article 52 nFADP, HRA) and carrier/professional liability coverage (firm-level E&O, civil liability) is not articulated as a distinct service by any Geneva competitor. The market speaks in terms of firm-level compliance. The personal exposure is acknowledged in the abstract but not addressed as a discrete documentation and protection service.

**The mechanism:** Personal exposure protection requires a different deliverable than firm-level compliance documentation. The personal evidence package documents what the individual professional did, not what the firm did. It serves the ICP's personal interest (individual liability protection) rather than the firm's interest (compliance posture). The deliverable is distinct and the client motivation is distinct — but no competitor has separated these as distinct services.

**Why it's vacant:** Most compliance advisors are oriented toward firm-level engagements. The personal exposure framing requires an individual-level engagement that is harder to sell (individual buyers, not firm procurement) and harder to deliver (requires understanding both the regulatory framework and the individual's specific role). The separation requires expertise that spans legal, regulatory, and personal risk management.

**The positioning opportunity:** "Your professional liability insurance covers the firm's exposure. It doesn't cover you personally. Article 321 CC, Article 63, Article 52 — these create personal liability for you individually. Your firm's policies protect the firm. They don't protect you. I can build the personal documentation that protects your personal exposure."

## Finding 05: Cross-Vertical Evidence Architecture Has No Single-Service Competitor

**The white space:** No Geneva competitor offers a single evidence architecture service that spans legal, finance, consulting, and healthcare verticals with vertical-specific calibration. The market is organized by vertical — legal compliance firms, financial services advisors, healthcare compliance consultants — with no cross-vertical evidence production service. The finding that the evidence production problem is structurally identical across all four verticals has not been translated into a cross-vertical service offering.

**The mechanism:** The evidence production methodology is the same in every vertical: tool inventory documentation, supervision record production, vendor contract gap analysis, and cross-regulator documentation mapping. The regulatory calibration differs by vertical. A single cross-vertical evidence architecture service with vertical-specific calibration would be more efficient to deliver and would create a stronger competitive position than any vertical-specific competitor.

**Why it's vacant:** Vertical specialization is the dominant model in Geneva professional services. Advisors are organized by vertical (legal, finance, healthcare) and rarely span verticals. The cross-vertical evidence architecture service requires expertise that few advisors have and a positioning decision to compete across verticals rather than within one.

**The positioning opportunity:** "The evidence problem is the same in every vertical. The regulatory calibration is vertical-specific. I work across all four — legal, finance, consulting, healthcare — because the documentation architecture is the same. The calibration is where the vertical expertise applies."

## Finding 06: Vendor Contract Privilege Review Is Not Offered as a Discrete Service in Any Vertical

**The white space:** The specific intersection of vendor contract terms and professional privilege obligations (Article 321 CC for attorneys, professional secrecy for other verticals) is not addressed by any Geneva competitor as a discrete service. General vendor due diligence exists. General contract review exists. The specific question — "does this vendor's access to our client data constitute a privilege waiver under Article 321 CC?" — is not answered by any market service.

**The mechanism:** AI vendor contracts typically include provisions allowing the vendor to access submitted data for model training, system improvement, or maintenance purposes. For attorneys processing privileged client communications through AI tools, this language may constitute disclosure under Article 321 CC. The same structural problem exists in finance (client financial data processed through AI vendors) and healthcare (patient data processed through AI vendors) — but the specific privilege angle is unique to legal and the professional secrecy equivalent in healthcare.

**Why it's vacant:** The privilege review requires expertise in both professional obligation law (Article 321 CC, FMH professional secrecy) and AI vendor contract analysis. Few advisors have both. The intersection is narrow and specific — it applies most directly to legal and healthcare verticals — and the general advisory market has not focused on it as a discrete service.

**The positioning opportunity:** "The vendor contract you signed gives the vendor access to your client data. For attorneys: that may be a privilege waiver under Article 321 CC. I can review every AI vendor contract in your stack against your professional secrecy obligations and tell you which ones create exposure — and what to do about it."

## Competitive Positioning Implications

### The Category Is the Play
The cross-vertical competitive analysis confirms: the primary competitive act is naming the category. The evidence production service is unnamed. The delegation accountability documentation system is unnamed. The personal exposure / carrier coverage separation is not articulated as a discrete service. The naming is the positioning.

### The Francophone Circuit Operates on Different Trust Logic
Most competitive analysis is conducted in English and reflects English-language market dynamics. The Geneva Francophone circuit operates on referral trust — a colleague's recommendation, a professional association reference, a peer introduction. The competitive response to this is not digital marketing but relationship-led positioning: working through the referral networks that actually drive Geneva professional service buying decisions.

### The Broker Referral Architecture Is Universal Across Verticals
The broker (financial advisor, insurance broker, legal correspondent) is the primary referral gateway across all four verticals in Geneva. The broker's incentive to refer is highest when the referral protects the broker (reduces their exposure to a claim from the client they referred). AI governance services that position the broker as the referrer — and protect the broker's position — will capture the broker referral channel more effectively than direct-to-ICP marketing.
