## Phase 05 C307 | Cross-Vertical Synthesis | All Four Verticals

## Cross-Vertical Problem Mapping

The following problems appear across all four verticals (legal, finance, consulting, healthcare) in structurally identical forms. Each problem has the same mechanism regardless of the vertical — the only variable is the regulatory framework applied.

---

## Problem 01: The Evidence Architecture Gap Is Universal

**Appears in:**
- Legal: Attorney signs carrier attestation, has no contemporaneous record of what AI actually did in specific matters
- Finance: CIO completes FINMA documentation, has no record of what AI systems actually did between annual reviews
- Consulting: Managing partner certifies AI governance, has no record of AI contribution to specific client deliverables
- Healthcare: Physician signs off on AI-influenced clinical decisions, has no record of the AI interpretation layer

**The common mechanism:** All four verticals operate under a documentation paradigm built for human decision-making. AI systems operate continuously and make decisions that go undocumented. The documentation gap is structural — it cannot be closed by producing more documents within the same paradigm.

**The market signal:** ICPs across all verticals report: "We have policies. We don't have evidence." The gap between policy documents and evidence records is universal, unaddressed, and increasingly visible as carrier forms and client questionnaires probe actual practice.

---

## Problem 02: The Vendor Contract Accountability Shift Is Universal

**Appears in:**
- Legal: Vendor DPA accepted without privilege review → potential Art.321 CC waiver through vendor access to privileged communications
- Finance: Vendor Terms of Service accepted → sub-processor chain creates undocumented Art.26 data processing relationships
- Consulting: Sub-consultant vendor terms not reviewed → managing partner accountable for processing chains they don't know exist
- Healthcare: AI vendor model training provisions accepted → de-identified patient data used for model training without patient disclosure

**The common mechanism:** The vendor's contract defines part of the ICP's professional accountability. The ICP accepted vendor terms without reviewing them against their professional obligations. The accountability exposure is embedded in contract language — not in the ICP's intentions.

**The market signal:** ICPs across verticals are encountering the vendor contract problem for the first time when they receive a specific question from a client, carrier, or regulator. The problem is structural and has been accumulating silently since the first AI tool was adopted.

---

## Problem 03: Delegation Accountability Is Structurally Incompatible with Personal Liability

**Appears in:**
- Legal: Managing partner cannot enumerate associate AI use, is personally accountable for all of it
- Finance: CIO is single point of failure for entire firm's AI governance knowledge
- Consulting: Managing partner accountable for sub-consultant AI use they cannot track
- Healthcare: Physician accountable for staff AI use and patient portal AI report delivery they didn't govern

**The common mechanism:** The delegation of AI-assisted work is operationally necessary and irreversible. The accountability for delegated AI use is personal and non-delegable. The mismatch between operational necessity and accountability structure is universal across all four verticals.

**The market signal:** "I can't enumerate what my team is using. I'm accountable for all of it." This phrase appears in every vertical. The delegation accountability gap is not a supervision problem — it is a structural design problem. Existing solutions (supervise more thoroughly, review all outputs) are operationally impossible and are recognized as such by the ICP.

---

## Problem 04: The Carrier/Authority Documentation Conflict Creates Paralysis

**Appears in:**
- Legal: Documentation built for carrier attestation creates record usable in Ordre disciplinary proceedings
- Finance: Documentation satisfying FINMA may be architecturally inadequate for Art.63 personal defense
- Consulting: Client questionnaire responses create commercial exposure; carrier attestation creates insurance exposure; nFADP documentation creates regulatory exposure — no unified system
- Healthcare: FMH human oversight documentation creates records usable in liability proceedings; HRA documentation conflicts with FMH requirements

**The common mechanism:** Different authorities demand different documentation types. The documents built for one authority create exposure in another authority's proceedings. The ICP is caught between documentation and no-documentation — both options feel risky.

**The market signal:** "The carrier wants records. The Ordre might use those same records." This documentation conflict is creating decision paralysis — not compliance action. The ICP is choosing not to build documentation specifically to avoid creating a record that could be used against them. This is the opposite of what authorities want and creates the worst possible outcome: no documentation AND no evidence of good practice.

---

## Problem 05: Multi-Standard Confusion Without a Geneva-Defined Baseline Is Universal

**Appears in:**
- Legal: nFADP + EU AI Act + OFL silence = no defined standard → French clients assume Geneva standard, English clients expect international standard
- Finance: FINMA Guidance + nFADP + SRO silence + carrier standards = no unified reference → MD knows the pieces don't fit together
- Consulting: Client RFP criteria + carrier attestation + nFADP + EU AI Act = no Geneva-calibrated methodology
- Healthcare: FMH + HIN + nFADP + carrier attestation = five non-aligned standards with no cross-reference methodology

**The common mechanism:** The applicable standard is undefined in every vertical. Each authority references "adequate" or "appropriate" governance without defining the term. The ICP is expected to demonstrate adequacy without knowing what adequacy means. The ambiguity is not a transitional problem — it is the permanent structure of the regulatory environment.

**The market signal:** "The regulation says adequate. Nobody has told us what that means." Multi-standard confusion is not causing inaction — it is causing paralysis-specific inaction. The ICP wants to act but cannot because the standard is undefined. This creates a specific service opportunity: define the standard for the ICP in their specific context, using available references, and document the rationale.

---

## Problem 06: Personal Exposure Recognition as the Decisive Trigger Is Universal

**Appears in:**
- Legal: Managing partner learns carrier attestation signature is personal → action triggered
- Finance: MD learns attestation language creates personal criminal exposure → action triggered
- Consulting: Managing partner learns Art.52 nFADP creates personal administrative liability separate from firm-level coverage → action triggered
- Healthcare: Physician learns Art.321 CC creates personal criminal exposure and HRA creates personal administrative exposure → action triggered

**The common mechanism:** The shift from firm-level to personal-level accountability is the decisive trigger. The ICP has personal liability exposure that their professional liability insurance may not cover. The personal exposure is structured differently than firm-level coverage in every vertical — and the separation is not widely understood. Once the ICP understands the personal exposure separately from the firm-level coverage, they act.

**The market signal:** "My signature is personal." This recognition appears across all four verticals as the decisive moment that converts consideration into action. The service that can demonstrate personal exposure clearly and specifically — separate from firm-level compliance framing — gets the engagement.

## What's Creating Urgency Across All Verticals

### Universal Acute Triggers
1. **Carrier attestation signatures** — present in legal, finance, consulting; creates personal exposure awareness across all three; healthcare has FMH attestation equivalents
2. **Client AI governance questionnaires** — finance (institutional DDQ) and consulting (RFP) as competitive gates; legal (client questions) and healthcare (patient questions) as emerging
3. **Vendor product updates** — functional boundary crossing in healthcare; vendor terms acceptance in legal and finance; sub-consultant tool adoption in consulting
4. **Peer claims and incidents** — the "my colleague got a claim" signal propagates across verticals; the signal is more powerful than any regulatory communication

### Universal Decision Blockers
1. **Proportionate solution absence** — every vertical: "everything on the market is for larger firms"
2. **Credibility before content** — every vertical: "who do we trust to tell us what this actually means for us?"
3. **Sequencing confusion** — every vertical: "I know I need to act, I don't know where to start"
4. **Bilingual complexity** — Geneva-specific: French vs. English client expectations; regulatory texts in both languages; no Geneva-calibrated bilingual methodology

---

## Market State Summary

The cross-vertical market signal is convergence on a single problem architecture:

1. **Problem recognition is universal** — the ICP in every vertical knows they have an AI governance gap
2. **The trigger is personal, not firm-level** — "my signature is personal" is the decisive conversion moment across verticals
3. **The action barrier is sequencing** — the ICP who wants to act doesn't know what to do first
4. **The solution must be proportionate** — enterprise frameworks are not implementable at boutique scale
5. **The vendor contract is the hidden accountability mechanism** — accepted without review, defines part of professional accountability
6. **The evidence gap is structural** — not a resource problem; requires a different documentation paradigm
