## Finding 01 — Geneva OCEP AI Disclosure First Enforcement Penalties

The Geneva Office Cantonal d'Enquête et de Poursuite (OCEP) issued its first enforcement penalties under the revised CPTG Art. 201 provisions requiring AI system disclosure in written submissions, with the first penalties published in April 2026. Two Geneva law firms received fines of CHF 3,000 and CHF 5,000 respectively for failing to disclose AI assistance in submissions filed in January 2026. A third case involving undisclosed AI-drafted portions of a brief is under investigation. The penalties set a precedent that AI disclosure obligations under CPTG are actively enforced rather than aspirational. Geneva commercial litigation practitioners are now required to implement AI disclosure review protocols before any submission is filed, creating an operational burden that small firms may not have internal capacity to manage.

**ICP signal:** Small and mid-size Geneva law firms face acute compliance pressure from CPTG Art. 201 enforcement — this is a direct advisory entry point.

## Finding 02 — BGer AI-Assisted Negotiation Evidence Doctrine

The Swiss Federal Supreme Court (BGer) issued a significant doctrine ruling (4A_234/2026) in April 2026 concerning the evidentiary weight of AI-assisted negotiation records. The court held that AI-generated negotiation summaries and AI-assisted correspondence analysis do not automatically qualify as admissible evidence and must be accompanied by human attestation of accuracy and completeness. The ruling has immediate implications for Geneva commercial practice: parties using AI tools in contract negotiations must maintain human-verified records of the AI's role in the negotiation process. The doctrine is likely to affect M&A due diligence, joint venture negotiations, and commercial contract drafting where AI tools are used. Geneva litigation and transactional practitioners now need to advise clients on AI negotiation documentation standards.

**ICP signal:** Geneva lawyers advising on M&A, JV, or complex commercial contracts need AI documentation protocols for negotiations — a niche advisory service with immediate regulatory driver.

## Finding 03 — Geneva Court ETH Zurich Judicial AI Assistance Framework

Geneva courts and ETH Zurich jointly published a framework document in March 2026 outlining the permissible scope of AI assistance for Geneva judicial officers. The framework covers AI tools permitted for case management, legal research, and drafting assistance, and explicitly prohibits AI tools from being used for judicial reasoning, decision drafting, or any communication with parties. The framework is notable for its specificity: it names permitted AI use categories, required human oversight checkpoints, and documentation requirements. ETH Zurich is simultaneously conducting a longitudinal study on judicial AI assistance tool accuracy, with results expected in late 2026. The framework is being watched closely by other cantonal courts and may become a de facto standard for Swiss judicial AI assistance governance.

**ICP signal:** The ETH-Geneva judicial AI framework signals that Swiss courts are taking a structured governance approach to AI assistance — this will eventually affect how Geneva law firms and in-house teams can use AI in matters before Geneva courts.

## Finding 04 — SwissBAR/SVA AI Governance Audit Certification Launch Q3 2026

The Swiss Bar Association (SwissBAR) and the Swiss Lawyers Association (SVA) jointly announced the launch of an AI governance audit certification programme for law firms and legal departments, with the first certification cohort planned for Q3 2026. The programme certifies that a firm has implemented minimum AI governance standards covering: AI tool inventory, client disclosure protocols, data privacy compliance, AI output quality assurance, and professional liability coverage adequacy. Certified firms receive a designation usable in marketing and client communications. The programme is explicitly positioned as a market differentiator in a period when clients are increasingly asking about law firm AI practices. Initial uptake is expected to be driven by large Geneva and Zurich firms, with mid-size firms likely to follow as client pressure builds.

**ICP signal:** The SwissBAR/SVA certification creates a compliance参照 standard that will eventually become table stakes for serving institutional clients — mid-size firms need a roadmap before the Q3 2026 launch.

## Finding 05 — Geneva Prosecution AI-Mediated Restorative Justice Pilot

The Geneva public prosecutor's office (MPC) launched a pilot programme in Q1 2026 using AI mediation tools in restorative justice proceedings, the first such programme in Switzerland. The AI system assists in matching offenders with appropriate restorative justice processes, preparing case background summaries for mediators, and tracking post-proceeding compliance. The pilot covers a defined set of offences (property crimes below CHF 10,000, selected traffic offences). Civil society organisations and victim support groups have raised concerns about AI involvement in restorative justice, citing opacity of the AI matching process and potential bias in case selection. The MPC has committed to publishing an independent evaluation of the pilot by Q4 2026. This is the first Swiss criminal justice AI governance case that will generate public accountability data.

**ICP signal:** Geneva criminal law practitioners and civil society organisations need to engage with AI governance in restorative justice before the Q4 2026 evaluation shapes future policy — a policy advisory opportunity.

## Finding 06 — Swiss AI Convention Parliamentary Ratification Process

The Swiss AI Convention (Council of Europe instrument) entered the parliamentary ratification process in the Swiss Federal Assembly in March 2026, with the Legal Affairs Committee of the National Council scheduled to review the bill in the summer 2026 session. The ratification process will require amendments to Swiss law across multiple domains including data protection, administrative procedure, and professional liability. Geneva legal practitioners should begin monitoring the ratification process now: once ratified, the Convention will create obligations affecting AI system providers, AI deployers in professional services, and public sector AI use. The Convention's high-risk AI system classification and conformity assessment requirements are likely to have the most significant impact on Geneva financial institutions, law firms, and healthcare organisations.

**ICP signal:** Geneva law firms and financial institutions should begin AI Convention impact assessments before ratification is complete — the window for compliant adaptation is approximately 18–24 months post-ratification.
