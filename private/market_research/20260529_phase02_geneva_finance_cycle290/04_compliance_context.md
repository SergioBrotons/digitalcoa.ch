# Compliance Context: Geneva Finance — Boutique Wealth Management

**Phase:** C290 Phase 02
**Date:** 2026-05-29
**ICP:** Boutique wealth management firms, 50–200 employees, Geneva-based. Managing director or partner personally accountable.
**Focus:** AI tool deployment for client portfolios and data.

---

## Switzerland-Specific Regulatory Landscape for AI in Wealth Management

### 1. FINMA Guidance 08/2024 (December 2024)
**What it is:**
FINMA Guidance 08/2024 operationalizes existing duties for financial institutions using AI. It covers governance, risk management, outsourcing, data quality, testing, documentation, explainability, and independent review.

**Key requirements from FINMA's perspective:**
- **Governance:** Clear internal responsibility for AI systems
- **Risk management:** Identification, assessment, and control of AI-related risks
- **Outsourcing:** AI vendor management and accountability
- **Data quality:** Ensuring data used by AI systems is accurate, representative, and unbiased
- **Testing:** Ongoing validation of AI model performance
- **Documentation:** Audit trails and evidence of AI governance decisions
- **Explainability:** Ability to explain AI-driven decisions to clients and regulators
- **Independent review:** Regular internal or external review of AI systems

**Current state:** FINMA has noted that many institutions are still in early stages of establishing these structures, particularly for explainability and documentation.

**What this means for boutique firms:**
- They need an accountable person (typically the managing director) responsible for AI governance
- They need to document AI tool usage, testing, and oversight decisions
- They need to be able to explain AI-driven portfolio decisions
- They need to demonstrate independent review of AI systems

**The problem:** Boutique firms lack the resources to implement this comprehensively and don't have clear guidance on what "enough" looks like.

---

### 2. No Comprehensive Swiss AI Act — Sector-Specific Approach
**What it is:**
Switzerland has opted against a comprehensive "Swiss AI Act." Instead, AI governance principles are integrated into existing laws and sector-specific regulations.

**What this means for boutique firms:**
- No single AI law to comply with — instead, AI obligations are scattered across existing regulations
- Financial regulations (FINMA), data protection (nFADP), and sector-specific rules all apply simultaneously
- Boutique firms must understand how existing obligations apply to AI, rather than following a single AI-specific framework

**The implication:** AI governance for Swiss wealth managers must be built by integrating multiple regulatory frameworks, not by following a single AI-specific law.

---

### 3. The Council of Europe AI Convention (Signed March 2025)
**What it is:**
Switzerland signed the Council of Europe AI Convention in March 2025, committing to principles of transparency, accountability, human oversight, and risk management for AI systems.

**What this means for boutique firms:**
- Implementing legislation is expected for consultation by end of 2026
- The convention establishes baseline AI governance principles that Swiss law will need to reflect
- Boutique firms should anticipate AI governance requirements aligning with these international standards

**The implication:** AI governance frameworks adopted now should anticipate alignment with the Council of Europe Convention principles.

---

### 4. EU AI Act Extraterritorial Reach
**What it is:**
Swiss firms with clients in the EU or AI systems deployed in the EU may need to comply with the EU AI Act. Most provisions, including those for high-risk AI systems, become fully operational by August 2026.

**What this means for boutique firms:**
- If they manage portfolios with EU-based clients or use AI systems operated in the EU, the EU AI Act may apply
- High-risk AI systems (which could include AI-assisted financial advice) have specific compliance requirements
- The EU AI Act's risk-based approach (prohibited, high-risk, limited risk, minimal risk) may influence Swiss regulatory expectations

**The implication:** Boutique firms with any EU nexus should map their AI systems against EU AI Act classifications.

---

### 5. Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) — Article 63
**What it is:**
The revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP) includes Article 63, which creates direct personal liability for breaches of data protection obligations.

**Key provisions affecting AI:**
- Data controllers must ensure proper data processing safeguards
- AI systems processing personal data (client financial data) must have appropriate data protection measures
- Personal liability can attach to managing directors and responsible persons for data breaches
- AI amplifies data protection risk because AI systems process more data, in more complex ways, often with less transparency

**What this means for boutique firms:**
- Managing directors are personally exposed if AI tools mishandle client data
- nFADP compliance obligations must be updated to account for AI tool usage
- Existing data processing inventories and consent frameworks likely don't cover AI
- The combination of FINMA + nFADP creates a layered personal liability exposure

**The implication:** AI governance must be integrated with nFADP compliance — particularly for managing directors managing personal liability risk.

---

### 6. SRO Silence — The Operational Vacuum
**What it is:**
Swiss SROs (Selbstregulierungsorganisationen) for asset management and wealth management have not published actionable AI governance guidance.

**The practical consequence:**
- Firms that typically wait for SRO operationalization of FINMA guidance are stuck
- No SRO framework exists to help boutique firms implement AI governance in practice
- SROs have indicated they are "monitoring" but not providing concrete guidance

**The implication:** Boutique firms cannot rely on SRO guidance in the near term. They must act on FINMA principles directly, or through advisory support that interprets FINMA guidance for boutique-scale implementation.

---

### 7. Asset Manager Authorization Requirements (FINMA)
**What it is:**
Boutique wealth management firms operating under FINMA authorization (as asset managers, trustees, or similar) have existing compliance obligations that apply to AI tool deployment.

**Key obligations:**
- Duty to act in clients' best interests (suitability, appropriateness)
- Duty to ensure operational compliance and risk management
- Duty to maintain adequate documentation
- Duty to ensure outsourcing (AI vendor) meets regulatory standards

**What this means for boutique firms:**
- AI tool deployment is not exempt from existing compliance obligations
- The firm remains accountable for AI-assisted decisions and actions
- AI vendor failures can create firm liability — vendor management is critical

**The implication:** AI governance is not separate from existing compliance — it's an extension of it. Boutique firms need to integrate AI into existing compliance frameworks rather than treating it as a new standalone domain.

## Strategic Compliance Implication

The regulatory landscape creates a **layered accountability structure** for boutique wealth managers:

1. **FINMA layer:** You are accountable for AI governance, explainability, documentation, and testing
2. **nFADP layer:** You are personally accountable for data protection failures involving AI
3. **Client layer:** Institutional clients are demanding AI governance evidence
4. **Carrier layer:** Insurers are asking AI questions on renewal forms
5. **SRO layer:** No guidance is coming in the near term — you must act without SRO cover

The managing director or partner sits at the center of all these layers. They carry personal liability for all of them simultaneously.

**The opportunity:** A compliance framework that helps boutique firms address all these layers simultaneously — rather than treating them as separate problems — would directly address the ICP's core pain point.
