# The Managing Director's Accountability Problem: AI Governance in Geneva's Boutique Wealth Management Firms

## What I'm Seeing in Geneva Right Now

Three things are happening simultaneously, and if you're a managing director or partner at a boutique wealth management firm in Geneva, they're converging on you personally.

**First:** Your institutional clients are sending you AI governance questionnaires. Family offices, pension funds, foundations — the clients you've spent years building relationships with — are now asking you to document what AI tools you use, how you ensure explainability, and how you manage model risk. These aren't informal questions. They're formal due diligence requirements.

**Second:** Your insurance carrier is asking about AI. The renewal form has new questions. Your broker isn't sure how to advise you. You're being asked to confirm whether you use AI in portfolio management, client communication, or data processing — and any "yes" without documented controls could create a coverage gap. Any "no" that's inaccurate is misrepresentation.

**Third:** FINMA published Guidance 08/2024 in December 2024. You read it. It's principles-based, which means it's correct but not actionable. You still don't know what you need to do differently on Monday morning.

Here's what nobody is telling you: these three pressures are not separate problems. They are the same problem, seen from three different angles.

The problem is that you're personally accountable for AI governance — and you don't have a framework to answer any of it.

## The Personal Liability Layer Nobody Is Talking About

Let me be specific about what I'm seeing.

There's a provision in Switzerland's revised Federal Act on Data Protection — Article 63 — that creates direct personal liability for managing directors when client data is mishandled.

Now layer AI on top of that.

AI tools processing client financial data create an exponentially larger surface area for data protection failures. If an AI vendor has a breach, if data is processed improperly, if consent isn't properly documented — the liability attaches to you personally.

I spoke with a managing director at a Geneva boutique last month. He runs roughly 80 employees. He told me: "I know my advisors are using AI for client work. I haven't authorized it. I also can't prove it's not happening. That's worse than knowing."

He's right. Shadow AI is the most immediate and least visible risk in boutique wealth management today. And it's his personal liability.

## Why Existing Solutions Don't Work for Boutique Firms

You may have looked for help. Here's what you found:

**The big consulting firms** will sell you an enterprise AI governance framework designed for institutions with dedicated AI risk teams. It's the right framework for the wrong size firm. You can't implement it with one compliance officer who also does everything else.

**The AI tool vendors** tell you their systems are "compliant." When you ask what that means for your FINMA obligations, they can't answer. They're vendors, not advisors. They have no liability if their AI creates problems for your firm.

**Your SRO** says they are "monitoring the situation." That's not guidance. That's a non-answer.

**Your legal counsel** knows about nFADP and FINMA. They're not sure what to tell you about AI specifically.

**Your carrier** is asking questions but not giving answers about what satisfies them.

The result: you're surrounded by people with partial information, none of whom are accountable for the outcome. And you're the one holding the liability.

## What a Framework Actually Has to Do

For a boutique wealth management firm in Geneva, an AI governance framework has to do six things simultaneously — and it has to be something a single compliance officer, supported by external advisory, can actually operate.

**1. It has to satisfy institutional client AI governance questionnaires.**
This means documented evidence of what AI tools you use, how decisions are made, how explainability is ensured, and how model risk is managed. Not marketing material — regulatory-grade documentation.

**2. It has to give you a defensible position on carrier attestation.**
This means an accurate AI inventory translated into answers that close coverage gaps without misrepresentation. You need to know what AI you're running, not just what you approved.

**3. It has to operationalize FINMA Guidance 08/2024 at boutique scale.**
This means translating principles into specific controls, documentation requirements, and review processes that a 50-200 person firm can actually implement.

**4. It has to integrate nFADP obligations — especially Article 63.**
This means your AI governance and your data protection compliance have to be the same system, not parallel systems. The managing director needs protection that covers both.

**5. It has to handle the SRO vacuum.**
Your SRO hasn't published anything actionable. The framework has to position you ahead of SRO requirements, not behind them.

**6. It has to address shadow AI.**
You need to know what's running in your firm. You need to bring it into the governance framework or eliminate it. "I didn't know" is not a defense.
