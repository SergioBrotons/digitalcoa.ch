# Findings Summary: Geneva Finance — Boutique Wealth Management

**Phase:** C290 Phase 02
**Date:** 2026-05-29
**ICP:** Boutique wealth management firms, 50–200 employees, Geneva-based. Managing director or partner personally accountable.
**Focus:** AI tool deployment for client portfolios. Institutional AI governance questionnaires, carrier attestation, FINMA expectations, SRO silence, nFADP Art. 63 personal liability.

---

## Six NET-NEW Findings (Distinct from C214–C289)

### Finding 1: The Tripartite Accountability Convergence
**The finding:** Three separate pressures — institutional client AI governance questionnaires, carrier attestation requirements, and FINMA Guidance 08/2024 — are converging on the same managing director at boutique Geneva wealth management firms. These are being treated as separate problems by the market, but they are the same structural problem: personal accountability without a governance framework.

**Language to use:** "The managing director sits at the intersection of client expectations, carrier requirements, and regulatory obligations — and none of these three has given them an actionable framework."

**Why it matters:** The ICP experiences this as one overwhelming problem, not three distinct ones. Messaging that addresses all three simultaneously will resonate more than messaging that tackles them sequentially.

---

### Finding 2: Shadow AI as the Invisible Liability Multiplier
**The finding:** Individual advisors and portfolio managers at boutique firms are using AI tools for client-facing work without formal authorization, creating ungoverned data flows and undocumented AI-assisted decisions. The managing director has no inventory, no oversight, and no defense. "I didn't know" is the most dangerous position a managing director can be in under FINMA Guidance 08/2024 and nFADP Art. 63.

**Language to use:** "Shadow AI is your most immediate and least visible risk — and it's your personal liability."

**Why it matters:** Every other AI governance problem assumes the firm knows what AI it's running. Shadow AI means they don't. It's the foundation problem that makes every other AI governance challenge unsolvable.

---

### Finding 3: The SRO Vacuum Is Creating a Compliance Debt Accumulation Problem
**The finding:** Swiss SROs have provided no actionable AI governance guidance. Boutique firms that typically wait for SRO operationalization of FINMA guidance are accumulating compliance debt with each passing month. Clients, carriers, and regulators are asking questions now. "We're waiting for SRO guidance" is becoming an indefensible position.

**Language to use:** "The SRO told us they're monitoring the situation. That's not guidance. That's a non-answer. And the compliance debt is compounding."

**Why it matters:** Firms that wait for SRO guidance will find themselves playing catch-up. Firms that act proactively will have a defensible position — and a competitive differentiator with institutional clients demanding AI governance evidence.

---

### Finding 4: nFADP Art. 63 Creates a Personal Liability Layer That Existing Frameworks Don't Address
**The finding:** Every AI governance framework in the market addresses firm-level compliance. None specifically address the managing director's personal liability exposure under nFADP Article 63 when AI tools mishandle client financial data. This is the layer that keeps managing directors awake at night — and it's systematically underserved.

**Language to use:** "Your existing nFADP compliance doesn't cover AI. And Article 63 makes you personally liable for the gap."

**Why it matters:** The ICP's deepest fear is personal liability, not firm liability. Messaging that explicitly addresses personal liability protection — not just firm compliance — will differentiate from generic AI governance offerings.

---

### Finding 5: Carrier Attestation Is the Immediate Trigger Point — Not FINMA
**The finding:** While FINMA Guidance 08/2024 sets the strategic framework, carrier attestation renewal forms are the immediate trigger that forces boutique firms to confront their AI governance gaps. The carrier questionnaire is the forcing function that makes the problem real and urgent for managing directors who have been deferring action on AI governance.

**Language to use:** "Your carrier is asking about AI on the renewal form. That's the trigger. Everything else can wait. This can't."

**Why it matters:** Understanding the decision trigger is critical for positioning. Messages framed around carrier attestation will get attention faster than messages framed around FINMA compliance, even though they're the same underlying problem.

---

### Finding 6: The Market Has No Boutique-Specific AI Governance Solution
**The finding:** The market offers enterprise frameworks (too complex), generic frameworks (not Swiss-specific), vendor documentation (not regulatory-grade), legal advice (no specific AI governance offering), and SRO silence (no guidance). No provider has developed a Swiss-specific AI governance solution designed for 50-200 person boutique wealth management firms with a managing director personally accountable.

**Language to use:** "The frameworks being sold to you assume you have resources you don't have. That's not your failure. That's a market gap."

**Why it matters:** This is an open positioning space. DigitalCoa.ch can own "AI governance for Geneva boutique wealth management" if the message is specific enough to the ICP's exact circumstances — not generic AI governance, not enterprise AI governance, but boutique-scale Swiss AI governance with personal liability protection.

## Key Insight: The Personal Liability Core

Everything in this ICP's AI governance problem traces back to one root cause: **the managing director is personally accountable for something they don't understand, can't document, and can't control.**

This is not a technical problem. It's an accountability problem.

All six findings are expressions of this core insight:
- The tripartite convergence → three directions of personal accountability pressure
- Shadow AI → personal liability for things they don't know about
- SRO vacuum → no institutional support to reduce personal exposure
- nFADP Art. 63 → explicit legal layer of personal liability
- Carrier attestation → immediate trigger forcing personal accountability into the open
- Market gap → no solution exists that protects the person, not just the firm

**Messaging implication:** The framing should always return to personal accountability protection, not just compliance or governance abstraction. The ICP thinks in terms of "what happens to me" — not "what happens to the firm."
