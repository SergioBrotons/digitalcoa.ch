# Market Signals: Geneva Finance — Boutique Wealth Management

**Phase:** C290 Phase 02
**Date:** 2026-05-29
**ICP:** Boutique wealth management firms, 50–200 employees, Geneva-based. Managing director or partner personally accountable.
**Focus:** AI tool deployment for client portfolios. Institutional AI governance questionnaires, carrier attestation, FINMA expectations, SRO silence, nFADP Art. 63 personal liability.

---

## Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

### Signal 1: The Institutional AI Governance Questionnaire Problem
Boutique wealth managers in Geneva are receiving AI governance questionnaires from large institutional clients (family offices, pension funds, foundations) that they cannot meaningfully answer.

**What's happening:**
- Large institutional clients have started including AI governance assessments as part of their manager due diligence
- Questions cover: what AI tools are used for client portfolios, how explainability is ensured, how model risk is managed, how client data is protected
- Boutique firms — often with no formal AI governance framework — are caught unprepared
- They lack documentation, audit trails, and internal policies to respond

**The failure mode:**
- Either they ignore the questionnaire (losing the client)
- Or they respond inadequately (damaging credibility)
- Or they fabricate answers (creating liability exposure)

**Language they use:** "We got an AI governance questionnaire from one of our largest clients. We had no idea what to put in it. We basically guessed."

---

### Signal 2: Carrier Attestation as Liability Trap
Insurance carriers serving Swiss wealth management firms have started including AI-related questions in their renewal questionnaires.

**What's happening:**
- Carriers ask whether the firm uses AI in portfolio management, client communication, or data processing
- They want to know if there are formal AI governance policies
- A "yes" without documented controls creates a coverage gap
- A "no" that's inaccurate creates misrepresentation risk
- Boutique firms are caught in the middle, unsure how to answer

**The failure mode:**
- Firms are answering conservatively ("no AI") even when they do use AI tools
- This creates a false inventory that doesn't reflect actual operations
- When a claim arises, the misrepresentation becomes a problem

**Language they use:** "Our insurer wants to know if we use AI. We do use AI — for portfolio analytics, risk reporting, client communication. But I'm terrified of checking the wrong box and voiding coverage."

---

### Signal 3: FINMA Guidance 08/2024 Compliance Gap
FINMA published Guidance 08/2024 in December 2024 operationalizing existing duties for financial institutions using AI — governance, risk management, outsourcing, data quality, testing, documentation, explainability, and independent review.

**What's happening:**
- FINMA noted many institutions are still in early stages of establishing these structures
- Particularly weak: explainability and documentation
- The guidance is principles-based, not prescriptive
- Boutique firms don't have the internal resources to interpret and implement it

**The failure mode:**
- Firms don't know what "explainability" means in their context
- They don't know what documentation FINMA expects
- They assume they'll figure it out when inspected — which is the wrong time to discover gaps

**Language they use:** "FINMA published guidance on AI. I read it three times. I still don't know what I need to do differently on Monday morning."

---

### Signal 4: SRO Silence Creating Operational Vacuum
The Swiss SROs (self-regulatory organizations) for asset management and wealth management have not published actionable guidance on AI governance.

**What's happening:**
- FINMA sets principles; SROs typically operationalize them
- SROs have been silent on AI governance expectations
- Firms that depend on SRO guidance are waiting indefinitely
- Meanwhile, clients, carriers, and regulators are asking questions now

**The failure mode:**
- Firms delay action because "we're waiting for SRO guidance"
- SRO guidance may not come in a actionable form for months or years
- The delay accumulates compliance debt

**Language they use:** "We asked our SRO point-blank what they expect from us on AI governance. The answer was basically 'contact FINMA.' That's not guidance."

---

### Signal 5: Shadow AI Creating Uncontrollable Risk
Individual advisors and portfolio managers at boutique firms are using AI tools — ChatGPT, Claude, and others — for client-facing work without formal authorization.

**What's happening:**
- Client notes, portfolio summaries, investment commentary are being drafted with AI assistance
- No documentation of which tools are used, how outputs are reviewed, or what client consent exists
- If a client complains or a regulator asks, there's no evidence of oversight
- The managing partner has no inventory of what's happening

**The failure mode:**
- Client data enters unvetted AI tools (privacy violation)
- AI-generated content goes to clients without human review (quality and liability issue)
- No audit trail for what was AI-assisted vs. human-authored

**Language they use:** "I know my advisors are using AI for client work. I haven't authorized it. I also can't prove it's not happening. That's worse than knowing."

---

### Signal 6: nFADP Article 63 Personal Liability Amplification
The revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP), particularly Article 63, creates direct personal liability for managing partners when client data is mishandled.

**What's happening:**
- AI tools processing client financial data create amplified data protection exposure
- If an AI vendor has a breach, if data is processed improperly, if consent is unclear — the managing partner can be personally liable
- AI increases the surface area for data protection failures dramatically

**The failure mode:**
- Managing partners don't fully understand how AI amplifies their nFADP exposure
- They haven't updated their data processing inventories or consent frameworks to account for AI
- They assume their existing nFADP compliance covers AI — it likely doesn't

**Language they use:** "Article 63 makes me personally liable for data breaches. Add AI to the equation and I'm potentially liable for things I don't even know are happening."
