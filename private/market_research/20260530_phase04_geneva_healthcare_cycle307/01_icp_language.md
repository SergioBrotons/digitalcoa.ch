# 01 — ICP Language: How Geneva Independent Practices Talk About AI Problems

## Phase 04 C307 | Geneva Healthcare ICP | 2026-05-30

---

## ICP Definition
Independent practitioners and small group practices in Geneva (2–10 physicians). Senior physician or practice director personally accountable. Concerned about FMH human oversight obligations, carrier AI attestation, patient confidentiality with AI tools, and personal liability under nFADP. Highest personal stakes of any vertical — physician license is irreplaceable.

**Positioning anchor:** "For physicians, evidence is personal."

---

## How the ICP Actually Talks About AI Problems

### The Passive Adoption Frame
The most common opening language pattern among Geneva independent physicians:

> *"L'IA est arrivée dans notre cabinet sans qu'on l'ait vraiment choisie."*

Translation: "AI arrived in our practice without us really choosing it."

This is not a complaint about AI. It is a precise structural observation: the adoption happened to them, not through them. The physician is now responsible for AI usage they did not initiate, evaluate, or authorize in a governance-aware manner. The accountability arrived through practice management system updates, EHR AI feature activations, and staff-initiated tool adoptions — none of which went through a physician authorization decision.

The ICP does not say: "I need an AI governance framework."
The ICP says: "AI is already in my records. I'm signing those records. I don't know what that means."

This distinction is critical. The physician is not in discovery phase about AI generally. They are in a specific accountability moment: their signature is on records that contain AI-generated content, and they cannot explain what the AI contributed versus what they clinically decided.

---

### The Carrier Attestation Language
When carrier AI attestation forms arrive, the language shifts to urgency and uncertainty:

> *"J'ai reçu le formulaire de l'assureur. Ils demandent de décrire notre gouvernance IA. J'ai quatre semaines."*

Translation: "I received the form from the carrier. They're asking us to describe our AI governance. I have four weeks."

The physician is not asking "what should AI governance look like." They are asking "what do I write on this form so I can sign it and not create a problem for myself." The carrier form has created an acute documentation occasion — but the documentation occasion does not define what adequate governance actually requires.

The carrier form creates its own internal contradiction: it asks for governance documentation the physician does not have, on a timeline that prevents building it properly, for a signature that commits personal liability regardless of whether the documentation is adequate.

---

### The FMH Oversight Question
On FMH human oversight requirements, the ICP language reveals operational emptiness:

> *"FMH dit qu'il faut une supervision humaine appropriée. Mais appropriate à quoi ? Je suis le seul médecin. Je fais tout."*

Translation: "FMH says appropriate human oversight is required. But appropriate to what? I'm the only physician. I do everything."

The ICP recognizes that the principle is correct and operationally useless. FMH oversight obligations land in a structural void because the same individual simultaneously holds the roles of AI user, compliance officer, billing administrator, and clinical decision-maker. The oversight requirement is architecturally impossible to satisfy with the existing guidance structure.

---

### The Staff AI Accountability Gap
For practices with employed physicians, medical assistants, or administrative staff:

> *"Mon assistant médical utilise un outil IA pour la rédaction de comptes rendus. Je signe le dossier. C'est ma responsabilité. Je ne sais pas ce que l'outil fait."*

Translation: "My medical assistant uses an AI tool for report writing. I sign the record. It's my responsibility. I don't know what the tool does."

The physician is personally accountable for AI decisions made by staff members whose tool usage the physician cannot audit. The accountability chain runs through the physician's signature upward, while the AI decision chain runs through staff tool usage downward — with no systematic connection between the two.

---

### The Swissdamed Registration Question
On Swissdamed medical device registration (July 1, 2026):

> *"Mon vendor m'a dit que mon outil IA pourrait nécessiter un enregistrement Swissdamed. Je ne sais pas ce que ça veut signifie pour mon cabinet."*

Translation: "My vendor told me my AI tool might require Swissdamed registration. I don't know what that means for my practice."

The physician is being directed to registration obligations by vendors who have commercial interest in broad registration, through a classification framework designed for manufacturers, not outpatient practices. The physician faces a regulatory deadline with a structurally proportionate assessment methodology that does not exist at their scale.

---

### The Patient AI Content Problem
When patients arrive with consumer AI-generated health analyses:

> *"Les patients arrivent avec des analyses IA de leurs montres connectées. Je dois décider quoi en faire cliniquement. Le dossier montre les données. Pas ma délibération."*

Translation: "Patients arrive with AI-generated analyses from their smartwatches. I need to decide what to do with them clinically. The record shows the data. Not my deliberation."

The physician exercises clinical judgment that diverges from AI recommendations. The record documents the data input and the physician's decision — but not the specific deliberation moment where the AI recommendation was evaluated and rejected. This is the highest-accountability clinical moment, and it is undocumented by design.

---

### The Post-Incident Realization
When an adverse outcome occurs and AI was involved:

> *"Après l'incident, on m'a demandé de montrer ma gouvernance IA. J'avais des certificats de formation. On m'a dit que ça ne démontre pas la compétence."*

Translation: "After the incident, I was asked to demonstrate my AI governance. I had training certificates. I was told they don't demonstrate competency."

The physician built documentation to an implied standard that was never operationally defined. The documentation created false confidence. When substantive evaluation occurred, the documentation collapsed under challenge.

## Key Language Markers for ICP Engagement

**What they say vs. what they mean:**

| What They Say | What They Mean |
|---|---|
| "AI arrived without us choosing it" | I'm accountable for AI I didn't authorize |
| "I have four weeks to sign the form" | The carrier's timeline is creating my documentation problem |
| "FMH says appropriate oversight" | The principle is right but operationally empty |
| "My assistant uses AI — I sign the record" | I'm liable for staff AI decisions I can't audit |
| "Swissdamed — my vendor told me to register" | The vendor's commercial interest is driving my compliance decision |
| "Patients bring AI analyses" | The AI recommendation was evaluated and rejected — but that moment is invisible in the record |
| "I had training certificates" | I built documentation to a standard nobody defined |
