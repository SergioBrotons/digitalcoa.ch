# 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

## Phase 04 C307 | Geneva Healthcare ICP | 2026-05-30

---

## Current Problems in the Geneva Independent Practice AI Market

### Signal 01: The Passive Adoption Accumulation Problem
AI tools are entering independent practices through practice management system updates, EHR AI feature activations, and staff-initiated tool adoptions — without any physician governance decision. The accumulation has preceded governance choice by 18–36 months in most practices. The physician is now personally accountable for AI usage they did not consciously authorize.

**Market signal source:** The passive adoption frame appears in every ICP conversation as the default opening. Physicians describe AI as something that "arrived" rather than something they selected. This is a structural market condition, not a physician knowledge gap.

**Complaint language:** "L'IA est arrivée sans qu'on l'ait choisie" — AI arrived without us choosing it.

**Failure mode:** No mechanism exists for retrospective governance authorization of AI that arrived through system updates and staff adoption. The accountability gap is already present in active medical records.

---

### Signal 02: The Carrier Attestation Stampede Effect
Carrier AI attestation forms are arriving in Geneva independent practices with 2–4 week response deadlines. Practices respond by attempting to document AI governance retroactively — using vendor-supplied templates, internet searches, and staff-compiled training records — under time pressure that precludes accurate representation.

**Market signal source:** Multiple Geneva physician networks and medical associations reporting sharp increase in attestation-related inquiry volume since Q1 2026. The deadline-driven documentation creates evidence that does not accurately represent actual AI practice.

**Complaint language:** "J'ai reçu le formulaire, j'ai quatre semaines, je ne sais pas ce qu'il faut écrire" — I received the form, I have four weeks, I don't know what to write.

**Failure mode:** The attempt to comply creates self-incriminating documentation. The carrier form, designed for underwriting evaluation, becomes the physician's own documented representation of governance adequacy — signed under time pressure by a physician who did not build the governance it describes.

---

### Signal 03: The Swissdamed Classification Paralysis
The July 1, 2026 Swissdamed registration deadline is creating classification paralysis among independent practices. The Tier 1/Tier 2 criteria were designed for medical device manufacturers, not outpatient practices. Physicians receive vendor communications suggesting registration is required, with no proportionate methodology for determining whether their specific AI tool configuration meets the threshold.

**Market signal source:** Physician association helplines reporting Swissdamed inquiry volume increase. Vendor marketing actively positioning broad registration obligation. No Geneva-calibrated independent assessment service identified in market.

**Complaint language:** "Mon vendor me dit de m'enregistrer. Est-ce que je dois vraiment ?" — My vendor tells me to register. Do I really have to?

**Failure mode:** Practices either over-comply (register everything as a precaution) or under-comply (assume nothing requires registration). Neither is proportionate to actual classification status. The structural uncertainty prevents any proportionate compliance response.

---

### Signal 04: The FMH Principle Without Operational Endpoint
FMH physician ethics guidelines correctly identify the human oversight principle but provide no operational definition proportionate to independent practice scale. The guidance does not specify what "appropriate oversight" means when one physician simultaneously occupies the roles of AI user, compliance officer, and billing administrator.

**Market signal source:** FMH continuing medical education programs reporting highest enrollment in AI competency modules, despite no operational definition of what competency standard those modules satisfy.

**Complaint language:** "FMH dit supervision humaine appropriée. Appropriate à quoi exactement ?" — FMH says appropriate human oversight. Appropriate to what exactly?

**Failure mode:** Practices build documentation to an implied standard that was never defined. Training certificates, attendance records, and vendor training completion badges are presented as evidence of FMH compliance. When substantive challenge occurs, the documentation collapses because no Geneva-defined standard exists to evaluate it against.

---

### Signal 05: The Multi-Physician Accountability Diffusion
In small group practices (3–6 physicians), each physician independently adopts different AI tools, activates different EHR AI features, and implements different documentation practices without practice-level governance coordination. The signing physician certifies to carrier attestation for an AI surface they cannot enumerate.

**Market signal source:** Group practice principals reporting they do not know what AI tools their partners use, despite being personally liable for the practice's overall governance adequacy.

**Complaint language:** "Je signe pour le cabinet mais je ne sais pas ce que mes associés utilisent comme outils" — I sign for the practice but I don't know what tools my partners use.

**Failure mode:** Carrier attestation signed by the practice principal creates personal liability for governance decisions made by partners whose AI usage the principal cannot audit. The documentation gap is structural, created by the independent decision-making authority of each physician in the group.

---

### Signal 06: The Patient AI Override Documentation Void
Patients arriving with consumer AI-generated health analyses (wearable diagnostics, symptom checkers, genetic risk tools) create a documentation category that no Geneva protocol addresses: the physician's clinical deliberation when choosing to diverge from AI recommendation.

**Market signal source:** Physicians reporting increasing frequency of patient-arrived AI content in consultations, with no documentation framework for the deliberation moment.

**Complaint language:** "Le patient arrive avec l'analyse IA de sa montre. Je décide autre chose. Le dossier ne montre pas ma réflexion." — The patient arrives with the AI analysis from their watch. I decide something different. The record doesn't show my reasoning.

**Failure mode:** The highest-accountability clinical decision moment — where physician judgment diverged from AI recommendation — is invisible in the medical record. Post-incident review processes evaluate physician decisions without any mechanism to see what the AI recommended at the time.

---

### Signal 07: The AI-Associated Adverse Outcome Documentation Gap
When an adverse clinical outcome occurs in a practice using AI tools, existing incident documentation infrastructure has no category for AI system state, AI tool version, AI recommendation made, or physician's AI-influenced versus AI-independent decision components.

**Market signal source:** Medical liability insurers and physician defense associations reporting increasing difficulty in evaluating AI's contribution to adverse outcomes in post-incident reviews.

**Complaint language:** "L'incident a été révisé. Ils ont regardé ma décision. Ils n'ont pas pu évaluer ce que l'outil IA avait recommandé." — The incident was reviewed. They looked at my decision. They couldn't evaluate what the AI tool had recommended.

**Failure mode:** Post-incident review evaluates physician decisions as if the AI assistance did not exist. The physician's name is on the record. The AI's contribution is invisible to the review process. This creates a structural evidentiary gap that operates against the physician precisely when the physician most needs their documentation to tell the complete story.

---

### Signal 08: The Vendor Commercial Interest Confusion
Vendors with commercial interest in broad AI tool deployment are the primary source of "guidance" for independent practices on compliance questions. Vendor communications position registration obligations broadly and AI governance standards in terms of vendor product capabilities.

**Market signal source:** Independent practices reporting vendor communications as their primary source of Swissdamed and AI governance guidance. No independent Geneva-calibrated assessment service identified in physician practice.

**Complaint language:** "C'est le vendor qui m'a expliqué ce que je dois faire. J'ai confiance mais je ne sais pas si je devrais." — The vendor explained to me what I need to do. I trust them but I don't know if I should.

**Failure mode:** Compliance decisions are being made based on vendor commercial interest, not independent professional assessment. The physician's professional license is being managed through vendor guidance channels.

---

## What the Market Is Not Saying (The Gap)

The market is not yet articulating these as named structural problems:

1. **Retrospective accountability for pre-governance AI adoption** — they describe the situation but do not name it as a specific accountability mechanism
2. **Carrier attestation as de facto operative standard** — they experience the form as a carrier requirement but do not recognize it as defining their professional accountability standard
3. **Swissdamed classification methodology absence** — they ask "do I need to register" without recognizing that the methodology for determining registration obligation at their scale does not exist
4. **AI-associated post-incident documentation architecture** — they understand incidents are reviewed but do not name the specific gap in how AI contribution is invisible to review
5. **Staff AI delegation accountability chain** — they understand staff use AI but do not name the specific delegation documentation requirement
6. **Physician override documentation as discrete category** — they document AI input and physician decision but do not recognize the missing deliberation moment as a specific documentation gap

## Acute vs. Structural Signals

**Acute signals (trigger immediate engagement):**
- Carrier attestation form receipt with deadline
- Swissdamed registration notice from vendor
- Post-incident review involving AI tools
- Staff AI tool adoption by practice principal discovery
- Patient arriving with AI-generated diagnostic content

**Structural signals (create ongoing vulnerability):**
- Passive AI adoption without governance decision
- Multi-physician AI surface enumeration impossibility
- FMH principle without operational endpoint
- Medical record AI attribution absence
- Vendor-driven compliance decision-making
