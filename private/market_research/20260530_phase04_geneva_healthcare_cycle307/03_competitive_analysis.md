# 03 — Competitive Analysis: What Exists vs. What's Missing

## Phase 04 C307 | Geneva Healthcare ICP | 2026-05-30

---

## What Exists in the Geneva Healthcare AI Accountability Market

### Category 01: Medical AI Tool Vendors
**Who they are:** AI diagnostic support tool vendors, EHR AI module providers, AI-powered clinical documentation companies operating in the Swiss market.

**What they offer:** Product training, vendor DPA terms, product-specific compliance documentation, Swissdamed registration guidance (for their specific tool classification interest).

**The positioning trap:** Vendors have commercial interest in broad AI deployment and broad registration obligation. Their guidance is calibrated to their product category, not to the physician's professional liability position. Vendor compliance documentation satisfies the vendor's risk position, not the physician's.

**What they cannot offer:** Independent professional liability assessment. Carrier pre-signing review. Named accountability architecture design. Independent classification methodology.

**Why they fail the ICP:** The physician's accountability runs through their personal license. The vendor's accountability runs through their product liability. These are not the same accountability circuit.

---

### Category 02: Generalist Management Consultants
**Who they are:** Strategy and management consulting firms operating in Geneva with healthcare practice management advisory services.

**What they offer:** Practice efficiency optimization, workflow improvement, digital transformation advisory.

**The positioning trap:** AI governance is treated as a workflow efficiency question. The professional liability dimension is not the primary frame. The advice is calibrated to practice operations, not to physician personal accountability protection.

**What they cannot offer:** FMH-specific human oversight protocol design. Carrier attestation pre-signing review. Medical record AI attribution architecture. nFADP personal liability assessment.

**Why they fail the ICP:** A general efficiency optimization engagement leaves the physician's personal license exposure unaddressed. The physician needs accountability architecture, not workflow optimization.

---

### Category 03: General Legal Counsel
**Who they are:** Swiss law firms and attorneys offering contract law, commercial law, and regulatory compliance advisory.

**What they offer:** Vendor contract review, general regulatory compliance assessment, data protection (nFADP) advisory.

**The positioning trap:** Generalist legal counsel addresses AI as a contract and data protection question. The specific intersection of medical record accountability, FMH human oversight obligations, carrier attestation personal liability, and medical device classification at independent practice scale is not the core competency of generalist legal practice.

**What they cannot offer:** Carrier attestation pre-signing review calibrated to medical practice. FMH operational oversight protocol design proportionate to independent practice. Swissdamed classification methodology at physician practice scale.

**Why they fail the ICP:** Legal counsel addresses the legal question as written. The physician's accountability problem is not primarily a contract question — it is a documentation architecture and professional liability question that requires both legal and operational expertise at the specific intersection of medical practice and AI governance.

---

### Category 04: Medical Associations and Professional Bodies
**Who they are:** FMH, cantonal medical associations, specialty societies.

**What they offer:** Ethics guidelines, continuing medical education (including AI competency modules), regulatory update communications.

**The positioning trap:** Professional bodies provide principle without operational endpoint. The guidance is correct in principle and structurally unusable at independent practice scale. Training modules address knowledge acquisition, not evidence of oversight competency.

**What they cannot offer:** Operational oversight protocol design for dual-role physicians. Carrier attestation preparation. Medical record AI attribution methodology. Swissdamed classification assessment proportionate to practice type.

**Why they fail the ICP:** FMH CME credits are evidence of training attendance, not evidence of oversight competency. The physician needs documentation that demonstrates oversight adequacy — not evidence of knowledge acquisition in an AI competency module.

---

### Category 05: IT and Cybersecurity Firms
**Who they are:** IT managed service providers, cybersecurity firms, practice IT support companies in Geneva.

**What they offer:** Data security assessment, vendor due diligence, IT infrastructure review, general AI tool evaluation.

**The positioning trap:** IT security assessment addresses the data protection layer, not the professional accountability layer. Securing AI tool access is distinct from establishing physician oversight competency over AI analytical contribution to medical records.

**What they cannot offer:** FMH human oversight protocol design. Carrier attestation pre-signing review. Medical record AI attribution documentation. Clinical accountability architecture.

**Why they fail the ICP:** A secure AI tool is not the same as a physician who can demonstrate adequate oversight of AI analytical contribution to signed medical records. IT security and professional accountability are different accountability circuits.

---

### Category 06: Carrier-Provided AI Governance Resources
**Who they are:** Medical liability insurers and health insurers providing AI governance guidance as part of their risk management offerings.

**What they offer:** AI governance questionnaires, attestation forms, risk management guidelines.

**The positioning trap:** Carrier resources are designed for carrier risk evaluation, not physician professional liability protection. The attestation satisfies carrier underwriting requirements while potentially confirming the physician's exposure. The carrier's documentation standard is their risk standard, not the physician's protection standard.

**What they cannot offer:** Independent pre-signing review. Carrier form analysis from physician's liability perspective before signature. Documentation that protects physician position, not just satisfies carrier requirement.

**Why they fail the ICP:** The carrier's interest and the physician's interest in AI governance documentation are not aligned. The carrier wants to evaluate risk. The physician needs to demonstrate oversight adequacy. These are different documentation purposes that happen to use the same form.

## What Is Missing (The White Space)

### Gap 01: Pre-Signing Carrier Attestation Review
No service exists in the Geneva market that reviews carrier AI attestation forms from the physician's personal liability perspective before signature. The physician signs without understanding what they are committing to personally. The carrier's form is reviewed by carrier legal teams. The physician reviews it alone.

**Why this is the highest-value entry point:** The carrier attestation creates an acute accountability occasion. The physician is already inside the documentation occasion. The documentation is being built. Pre-signing review at this moment prevents self-incriminating documentation from being created, rather than attempting to correct it after signature.

**What would need to exist:** A service that reviews the carrier form, identifies what the physician is certifying to personally, compares that certification against the physician's actual AI practice, and produces documentation that accurately represents practice AI governance — before the form is signed.

---

### Gap 02: Independent Swissdamed Classification Assessment
No independent service exists in the Geneva market that provides proportionate Swissdamed classification determination for independent physician practices. The only guidance available is from vendors with commercial interest in broad registration.

**Why this matters:** The July 1, 2026 deadline creates a compliance occasion where the physician must act under structural uncertainty. Either over-compliance (register everything) or under-compliance (ignore it) is not proportionate response. A classification methodology calibrated to independent practice scale does not exist.

**What would need to exist:** A classification assessment methodology that evaluates specific AI tool configurations against Tier 1/Tier 2 criteria at independent practice scale, produces a defensible classification determination, and documents the methodology so the determination can be defended if challenged.

---

### Gap 03: Medical Record AI Attribution Protocol
No documented methodology exists for creating contemporaneous evidence of what the physician documented versus what the AI contributed analytically at the point of medical record creation. Retroactive reconstruction is structurally impossible.

**Why this matters:** Medical records are legally authoritative. The physician's signature makes them so. The physician's evidentiary position on every AI-assisted record signed in the past 24 months is: "My name is on this record. I cannot explain the AI's contribution." This exposure is present-tense and accelerating.

**What would need to exist:** A documentation protocol that establishes at time of record creation: what the physician documented, what the AI contributed analytically, what the physician reviewed and decided — before the record is signed.

---

### Gap 04: FMH Oversight Operational Protocol for Dual-Role Physicians
No operational definition exists for what "appropriate human oversight" means when one physician holds the roles of AI user, compliance officer, and billing administrator simultaneously. The FMH principle lands in a structural void.

**Why this matters:** The absence of operational definition means every independent practice is building oversight documentation to an implied standard that has never been defined. Documentation built to an undefined standard cannot be evaluated for adequacy.

**What would need to exist:** An operational oversight protocol specifically designed for dual-role independent physician practices — one that works within actual structural constraints, that can be documented contemporaneously, and that establishes evidence of oversight adequacy even when one person holds all roles.

---

### Gap 05: Multi-Physician Practice AI Governance Coordination
No methodology exists for small group practices (2–10 physicians) to establish practice-level AI governance accountability when each physician independently adopts different AI tools without practice-level coordination.

**Why this matters:** The practice principal signs carrier attestation for the practice. Partners use different AI tools. The signing physician cannot enumerate the AI surface they are certifying to. The accountability gap is structural and created by independent physician decision-making authority.

**What would need to exist:** A practice-level AI governance coordination mechanism that establishes what AI tools are in use, who is using them, what governance documentation exists for each tool, and how the practice principal can certify to governance adequacy without being able to audit each partner's specific AI usage.

---

### Gap 06: AI-Associated Post-Incident Documentation Architecture
No documentation architecture exists for capturing AI system state, AI tool version, AI recommendation made, and physician's AI-influenced vs. AI-independent decision components at the moment of an adverse clinical outcome.

**Why this matters:** Post-incident review processes evaluate physician decisions without any mechanism to see what the AI recommended at the time. The physician's name is on the record. The AI's contribution is invisible to the review. This creates a structural evidentiary gap that operates against the physician precisely when the physician most needs their documentation to tell the complete story.

**What would need to exist:** A post-incident documentation architecture that captures AI system state and recommendation at the moment of decision, distinct from the human clinical decision documentation — so that post-incident review can evaluate both the AI's contribution and the physician's evaluation of that contribution.

---

## Structural Competitive Moats

1. **License-first framing:** Every competitor positions AI governance as a compliance question. The physician's license is the primary asset at risk. Compliance framing misses the emotional core of the physician's actual concern.

2. **Evidence language, not framework language:** The market offers governance frameworks. The physician needs evidence architecture. "Appropriate oversight" is framework language. "Here is the contemporaneous documentation of what you reviewed and decided before signing this record" is evidence language.

3. **Pre-signing intervention:** All existing services respond to problems that already exist. The pre-signing carrier attestation review is a preventive intervention — the highest value, zero competitors occupy it.

4. **Bilingual execution:** Francophone Geneva operates on different trust logic. French-language service delivery is not a nice-to-have — it is the structural requirement for trust-building in this market.

5. **Documentation architecture over training:** Training certificates and CME credits are the market's current answer to AI accountability. They do not produce contemporaneous evidence of oversight adequacy. Documentation architecture produces the evidence that survives substantive challenge.

## The Naming Is the Competitive Act

The market has not named these gaps. Independent practices experience the structural problems as personal confusion rather than market-wide service gaps. Naming the gap creates the service category. The physician who hears "the medical record AI attribution protocol is a specific documentation architecture problem with a specific solution" recognizes their situation precisely — and recognizes that no one has offered them this before.

**Key naming for competitive positioning:**
- "Pre-signing carrier attestation review" (not "AI compliance support")
- "Medical record AI attribution documentation" (not "AI governance")
- "Swissdamed classification determination" (not "regulatory registration support")
- "FMH oversight operational protocol" (not "AI competency training")
- "Practice-level AI governance coordination" (not "group practice AI policy")
- "AI-associated incident documentation architecture" (not "incident reporting")
