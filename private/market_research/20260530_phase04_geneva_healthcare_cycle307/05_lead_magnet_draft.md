## English

**The Situation**

I signed the carrier form. Like everyone does.

It asked a series of questions about how I use AI in my practice. I answered them honestly — yes, I use AI tools. Yes, I review output before using it. Yes, I supervise how it's used in my clinic.

The renewal went through. I moved on.

Six months later, I realized something that kept me up at night: I had no idea what the form had actually asked me to attest to.

It turns out the form was architecture, not just a form. When I said I "adequately supervise AI tools," I was creating a standard — a documented standard — that I couldn't demonstrate I met. The carrier didn't ask me to prove it at the time. But the form's architecture assumed proof existed. And I had none.

## The Documentation Gap No One Talks About

The conversation about AI in Geneva medical practice is full of frameworks, guidance, and principles. "Follow best practices." "Use AI responsibly." "Maintain professional standards."

What no one talks about is **the evidence**.

When the carrier asks whether you supervise AI tools adequately — they don't want to see your policy. They want to see what you actually documented about how you supervised those tools, for which patients, and what you found when you reviewed the output.

That's the gap. Not the policy gap. The evidence gap.

---

## What I Learned About Patient Confidentiality

I didn't think about patient confidentiality when I started using AI tools. The tool was "internal." It was just me, using it to assist with diagnosis and documentation.

Then I realized: the tool processes everything on the vendor's servers. Not mine. The vendor can access it. Their sub-processors can access it. Depending on where the data is processed, law enforcement in the vendor's jurisdiction may be able to access it.

And the vendor's terms of service? I clicked through them. Like everyone does.

I went back and tried to read them. The data retention clause. The audit rights. The jurisdiction clause. I had accepted all of it — without knowing whether any of it conflicts with what Swiss law requires of me as a physician.

**I didn't know I was supposed to have reviewed the vendor contract against my patient confidentiality obligations. No one told me. The vendor certainly didn't.**

## The Efficiency-Liability Paradox

AI tools make me faster. That's the point.

But every efficiency gain I make with AI tools also expands my documentation surface area. The more I use, the more I need to document. And I don't have a system for documenting any of it.

The paradox: the physicians most aggressively adopting AI tools are also the ones most exposed — because they're using AI most extensively with the least documentation to show for it.

## What "Adequate" Actually Means

I went looking for a Geneva standard. A definition of "adequate" AI governance for a small medical practice. Something I could point to and say: if I do this, I'm doing enough.

There isn't one.

The FMH has stated human oversight obligations. The carrier attestation acknowledges it. No one has defined what adequate means for a two-to-ten physician Geneva practice. Nobody.

**That's not a gap in the market. That's a first-mover opportunity.**
