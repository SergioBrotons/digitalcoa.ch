## Regulatory Architecture (C315)

The C315 Geneva legal AI regulatory architecture is the C314 architecture activated. What was preparation in C314 is enforcement in C315.

The architecture consists of three regimes, each now activated:

1. **Swiss national and cantonal regime** — Article 321 Swiss Criminal Code (professional secrecy / attorney-client privilege); nFADP (new Federal Act on Data Protection, revised 2023); OaF (Ordre des avocats de Genève) post-deadline AI guidance (June 2026); FSA cantonal directives updated for Article 26 (June 2026); FDPIC guidance on AI vendors and post-deadline auditing

2. **Carrier regime** — Professional liability insurance attestation with new EU AI Act post-deadline clauses (added mid-year 2026 refresh); carrier cooperation duty with MSA audit requests

3. **EU AI Act regime** — Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, with high-risk applicability provisions now operative; designated Member State authorities; first formal notices issued

Each regime has moved from C314 readiness posture to C315 enforcement posture. The Geneva boutique is now in the enforcement perimeter across all three regimes.

## Regime 1: Swiss National and Cantonal Regime (Post-Deadline)

### Article 321 Swiss Criminal Code (Professional Secrecy)

**Post-deadline status (C315):**

Article 321 is unchanged in text but materially changed in enforcement context. The C314 question was theoretical: "is AI ingestion Article 321 violation?" The C315 question is procedural: "how does Article 321 violation interact with EU AI Act MSA logging audit?"

Three new C315 dynamics:

1. **Article 321 + MSA logging audit interaction.** The MSA sends an Article 12 logging request to the AI vendor. The vendor responds with logs containing attorney-client communication metadata. The vendor's logs are now Article 321 violation evidence — created by the audit itself. The Geneva firm is caught between carrier cooperation duty (Article 12 audit response) and Article 321 violation (metadata disclosure to a third party via the vendor's log pipeline).

2. **Article 321 + carrier attestation cooperation.** The carrier sends the firm a letter requesting EU AI Act audit-response documentation. The firm must cooperate with the carrier. The cooperation may require the firm to disclose attorney-client metadata to the carrier. The disclosure may constitute Article 321 violation. The firm cannot simultaneously satisfy both duties without Article 321 breach documentation.

3. **Article 321 + bar discipline parallel exposure.** When a Genevoise peer was named in a formal MSA notice, the bar opened a parallel ethics file. Article 321 is now enforced through two parallel instruments: the MSA's Article 99 procedural letter and the bar's parallel disciplinary action. The named individual is now exposed under two regimes for the same fact pattern.

### nFADP (New Federal Act on Data Protection, revised 2023)

**Post-deadline status (C315):**

nFADP obligations remain. The C315 activation is FDPIC's role:

1. **FDPIC position on MSA audit requests.** FDPIC has published preliminary guidance on how a Swiss firm's data may be lawfully shared with an EU MSA in response to an Article 12 audit. The guidance: Swiss data may be transferred if (a) the EU MSA is recognized as adequate, (b) Standard Contractual Clauses are executed, or (c) Article 321 is overridden by the carrier cooperation duty. None of the three is currently satisfied for legal AI vendors.

2. **FDPIC position on vendor cross-border processing.** FDPIC has begun formal inquiry into U.S.-parented legal AI vendor cross-border processing under CLOUD Act. The inquiry may produce vendor-side operational changes (e.g., Swiss data segregation, vendor subprocesssor audit) before the end of 2026.

3. **FDPIC + FDPIC inquiry overlap with MSA.** When an MSA audits a legal AI vendor, FDPIC may begin a parallel inquiry. The dual inquiry creates dual disclosure obligations. The Geneva firm is exposed to FDPIC and MSA simultaneously through the vendor's infrastructure.

### OaF (Ordre des avocats de Genève) — Post-Deadline Guidance (June 2026)

**Key publication (C315 specific):**
- OaF post-deadline AI guidance bulletin (June 2026) — referencing EU AI Act Article 26 named-sub-principal attribution to attorneys
- OaF interim guidance on Article 12 MSA audit response posture (June 2026)
- OaF guidance on client disclosure of bar disciplinary events (June 2026)

**C315 specific implications:**

1. **Article 26 attribution is the new bar enforcement instrument.** The named-sub-principal (associate, junior partner, attorney who used the tool) is now read by the OaF as subject to bar discipline for Article 26 violations. The bar is following the MSA + carrier regimes.

2. **MSA audit response posture.** The OaF has issued interim guidance recommending firms cooperate with MSA logging audits while preserving Article 321 protections. The guidance is interim, not definitive. The named managing partner must make a posture decision in real time.

3. **Client disclosure obligation under nFADP Article 7 (transparency).** When the firm is subject to a formal MSA notice or bar disciplinary action, the firm may be required to inform clients of the regulatory event under nFADP transparency obligations. The OaF has referenced this obligation but has not yet specified the form.

### FSA (Fédération Suisse des Avocats) Cantonal Directives (Updated for Article 26)

**Key update (C315 specific):**
- FSA cantonal directive on EU AI Act Article 26 attribution to attorneys (June 2026)
- Cross-cantonal harmonization on Article 26 named-sub-principal enforcement
- Disciplinary framework integration with MSA and carrier regimes

**C315 specific implications:**

1. **Named-sub-principal attribution is now bar-discipline enforceable.** Article 26 attribution extends bar discipline from the managing partner (named principal) to the associate (named sub-principal). The associate who used the tool is now potentially subject to bar discipline.

2. **Cross-cantonal coverage.** The Geneva firm with cross-cantonal practice (Geneva + Vaud + Zurich) faces FSA harmonized Article 26 enforcement across cantons. The exposure is no longer Geneva-limited.

3. **Disciplinary case integration with MSA.** The first Genevoise peer firm named in a formal MSA notice has triggered a parallel bar disciplinary file. The two instruments cross-reference. The named individual is exposed under two regimes simultaneously.

### FDPIC (Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner) Guidance

**Key publications (2026):**
- FDPIC preliminary guidance on MSA audit data transfer (post-deadline, June 2026)
- FDPIC preliminary guidance on CLOUD Act and Swiss data exposure (post-deadline, June 2026)
- FDPIC inquiry overlap with MSA audit pipeline (June 2026)

**C315 specific implications:**

1. **CLOUD Act exposure formalized.** FDPIC has acknowledged CLOUD Act as a documented exposure for U.S.-parented legal AI vendors. The exposure is no longer theoretical — it is now basis for vendor-side inquiry.

2. **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCC) as inadequate.** FDPIC has signaled that SCC with U.S.-parented vendors may not be sufficient to address CLOUD Act exposure. The Geneva firm with U.S.-parented legal AI vendor may need to switch vendors or accept the exposure.

3. **FDPIC + MSA dual inquiry.** When an MSA audits a legal AI vendor, FDPIC may begin a parallel inquiry. The dual inquiry creates dual disclosure obligations. The Geneva firm is exposed to FDPIC and MSA simultaneously.

## Regime 2: Carrier Regime (Post-Deadline Attestation Updates)

### Carrier Mid-Year 2026 Refresh — New EU AI Act Clauses

**Updated clauses (C315 specific):**

The 2026 mid-year carrier attestation refresh has added three new clauses to professional liability attestation language:

1. **Article 9 risk management attestation.** The firm attests that it has implemented Article 9 risk management for any high-risk AI use. The attestation requires named-individual evidence of the risk management system.

2. **Article 12 logging audit response attestation.** The firm attests that it will cooperate with carrier-side MSA logging audit requests. The attestation requires named-individual point of contact for audit response.

3. **Article 26 named-sub-principal attestation.** The firm attests that it has identified all named-sub-principals (associates, junior partners) using AI on behalf of the firm. The attestation requires named-individual roster with role-specific evidence of AI literacy, supervision, and authorization.

**C315 specific implications:**

1. **Named-individual attestation is now standard.** The attestation is no longer firm-level. It is named-individual. The named managing partner signs. The named associate is referenced. The named vendor contact is referenced. The attestation is the named-individual evidence architecture carrier side.

2. **Cooperation duty with MSA.** The firm attests that it will cooperate with MSA audit requests through the carrier. The carrier becomes the audit-response intermediary. The firm cannot refuse cooperation without breach of attestation.

3. **Article 26 named-sub-principal roster.** The firm must maintain a roster of named individuals who use AI on behalf of the firm. The roster must be current. The roster must be auditable. The roster is the named-individual evidentiary architecture carrier side.

### Carrier Cooperation with MSA Audit Pipeline

**C315 status:**

The carriers have established internal MSA audit cooperation protocols. When an MSA sends an Article 12 audit request to a legal AI vendor, the carrier is informed. The carrier then sends the firm a letter requesting EU AI Act audit-response documentation. The firm has 14–30 days to respond.

**C315 specific implications:**

1. **Carrier-side audit-response coordination.** The carrier is now the firm's audit-response coordinator. The firm receives a letter from the carrier, not directly from the MSA. The carrier letter says "we have received an audit request from MSA. Please respond by [date]."

2. **Carrier-side named-individual response architecture.** The carrier letter will request named-individual evidence: name of the AI tool user, name of the matter, date and timestamp of use, logging configuration, authorization documentation. The firm must produce this named-individual response.

3. **Carrier-side exposure for non-response.** If the firm does not respond to the carrier's audit-response letter, the carrier reserves the right to (a) deny future coverage, (b) rescind current attestation, (c) report the firm to the bar. Non-response is now a documented attestation breach.

**C315 specific implications:**

1. **Which authority has jurisdiction over a Swiss firm?** The jurisdictional test is (a) the firm's classification (provider, deployer, importer, distributor), (b) the firm's location (CH vs EU), and (c) the firm's clients (EU persons or matters in EU). For a Genevoise boutique, the likely jurisdiction is (a) the EU Member State of the firm's primary EU client, (b) the EU Member State where the matter is filed or adjudicated, or (c) the EU Member State where the AI tool output is consumed.

2. **Federal coordination role.** The WBF/FMEC (Federal Ministry of Economic Affairs, Education and Research) has been designated as the federal AI coordinator for AI Act coordination with the EU AI Office. The coordination role does not include direct enforcement over Genevoise firms (which falls to the bar, the carrier, and FDPIC), but it does include information exchange with the designated EU MSAs.

3. **FOEN coordination role.** The Federal Office for the Environment (FOEN) has been designated for environmental and social impact obligations. For legal services, FOEN is not the primary contact. For data center or compute vendor compliance, FOEN is a relevant secondary contact.

4. **EU AI Office coordination.** The EU AI Office (Brussels) coordinates MSA activities across Member States. The Geneva firm facing multiple potential MSA exposures can request EU AI Office coordination through Swiss federal channels.

### Article 99 Procedural Letter Pipeline (First Notices)

**C315 status:**

The first Article 99 procedural letters have been issued to deployers in legal services, financial services, and healthcare deployer categories. The first Genevoise peer firm named in a formal notice was identified in mid-June 2026.

**Procedural letter structure:**

The Article 99 procedural letter contains:
1. Identification of the deployer (named legal person — the firm)
2. Identification of the named principal (managing partner)
3. Identification of named sub-principals (associates)
4. Identification of the AI system (provider + name)
5. Description of the alleged non-compliance (Article referenced + fact pattern)
6. Required response (named-individual evidence, certified documentation)
7. Deadline for response (typically 30 days)
8. Notice of available procedures (objection, settlement, formal action)

**C315 specific implications:**

1. **The named-individual is named.** Article 99 procedural letters are addressed to the firm but reference named individuals. The exposure is personal. The named individual is named in a regulatory instrument. The named individual is named in carrier correspondence. The named individual is named in bar disciplinary files.

2. **30-day response window.** The procedural letter has a 30-day response window. The Geneva firm must produce named-individual response in 30 days. The response architecture (named-individual evidentiary architecture) must be pre-built.

3. **Procedural posture options.** The firm may (a) respond in full, (b) respond partially with objection, (c) negotiate a settlement, (d) request formal hearing. Each option requires named-individual evidence. The C315 service provides the posture architecture.

### Article 12 Logging Audit Pipeline

**C315 status:**

The first Article 12 logging audit requests have been issued to legal AI vendors in May–June 2026. The vendor's logs are the audit source. The vendor responds. The carrier is informed. The firm is asked to respond.

**Article 12 logging audit request structure:**

The MSA sends a letter to the AI vendor requesting:
1. Logs of all EU-jurisdiction deployers using the AI system
2. Logs of named attorney users (when identifiable through account)
3. Logs of matter references (when metadata indicates)
4. Logs of timestamps of use
5. Logs of input and output data (where Article 321 conflict is not resolved)

**C315 specific implications:**

1. **The vendor's logs are the audit trigger.** The Geneva firm has not seen the MSA letter — the vendor has. The carrier letter comes next. The named-individual evidence must be pre-built at the firm level. The C315 service provides the named-individual evidentiary architecture for audit response.

2. **Article 321 violation may be triggered by the audit itself.** The audit requests logs. The logs may contain attorney-client communication metadata. The disclosure of metadata to the MSA may constitute Article 321 violation. The firm is exposed to Article 321 violation through the audit pipeline. The C315 service provides the Article 321 preservation architecture within the audit response.

3. **Carrier-side coordination is mandated.** The carrier has attested the firm will cooperate with the MSA through the carrier. The firm cannot refuse cooperation without attestation breach. The C315 service provides the cooperation-with-protection architecture.

### Article 26 Named-Sub-Principal Attribution

**C315 status:**

Article 26 of the EU AI Act extends deployer obligations to natural persons who authorize or use AI on behalf of the deployer. The OaF and FSA cantonal directives have activated Article 26 attribution in bar enforcement (June 2026).

**Article 26 named-sub-principal attribution mechanism:**

1. The named principal (managing partner) attests to the firm's deployer status
2. The named sub-principal (associate) authorizes AI use on behalf of the firm
3. The named sub-principal uses AI on the firm's behalf
4. The named sub-principal is named in any incident, audit, or regulatory event
5. The named sub-principal is personally subject to MSA, bar, and carrier enforcement

**C315 specific implications:**

1. **The associate is now personally exposed.** Article 26 attribution means the associate who used the AI tool is now personally subject to bar discipline, carrier malpractice exposure, and MSA procedural liability. The exposure is no longer firm-level — it is named-individual.

2. **Authorization documentation is critical.** Article 26 attribution requires that the named sub-principal's authorization to use AI be documented. The authorization document must specify the AI tool, the matter scope, the supervision requirement, and the named principal's oversight. The C315 service provides the authorization documentation architecture.

3. **AI literacy attestation at the named-sub-principal level.** Article 4 requires named-individual AI literacy. Article 26 attribution means the AI literacy must be documented at the named-sub-principal level — not firm-level. The named associate must have a documented AI literacy credential. The C315 service provides the named-individual AI literacy architecture.

## Three-Regime Post-Deadline Reconciliation (C315 Service Module)

The C315 reconciliation memo addresses the following post-deadline conflicts:

### Conflict 01: Article 321 vs MSA Article 12 Logging Audit

**Article 321:** Prohibits disclosure of attorney-client communication metadata to third parties.

**MSA Article 12 audit:** Requests vendor logs that may contain attorney-client communication metadata.

**Resolution methodology (C315):**
1. Pre-built named-individual Article 321 preservation architecture (zero-content logging configuration, vendor-side deletion, sub-processor segregation)
2. Carrier-side coordination with MSA to limit audit scope to non-Article 321 data
3. Documented Article 321 precedence invocation in audit response
4. FDPIC + OaF reference for Swiss-side protection

### Conflict 02: EU AI Act Article 14 Human Oversight vs OaF Article 26 Supervision

**EU AI Act Article 14:** Effective human oversight during AI system use.

**OaF / FSA Article 26 supervision:** Attorney's duty to supervise AI-generated work product, attributed to named sub-principal.

**Resolution methodology (C315):**
1. Named-individual human oversight protocol (which named supervisor reviews, how, when, with what documentation)
2. Named-individual AI literacy credential at the named-sub-principal level (Article 4 + Article 26)
3. Cross-reference between EU AI Act Article 14 documentation and OaF / FSA supervision file
4. The two regimes align in principle but require named-individual evidence architecture

### Conflict 03: Carrier Attestation vs MSA Audit Response

**Carrier attestation:** Attests cooperation with MSA through carrier coordination pipeline.

**MSA audit response:** Requires named-individual evidence under Article 99 procedural letter timeline.

**Resolution methodology (C315):**
1. Pre-built named-individual evidentiary architecture (named principal, named sub-principals, named vendor contacts, named matter references, named timestamps)
2. Carrier-side coordination pipeline (carrier letter response in 14–30 days)
3. MSA-side procedural letter response (30-day window from Article 99 letter)
4. Cross-reference architecture between carrier response and MSA response
5. Non-response attestation breach prevention
