# Market Signals: Geneva Healthcare — C290 Phase 04

## Current Problems, Complaints, Failures

---

## Finding 1: AI Tool Portfolio Accumulation Without Inventory (NEW)

**Signal:** Physicians discovering 5–8 active AI features across their practice management system, billing software, imaging platforms, and documentation tools — none of which they consciously selected or authorized.

**What practitioners report:**
- Practice management software "suggested improvements" that activated AI features without explicit consent step
- Three to four separate AI tools across their stack, each activated at different times by staff or vendor updates
- No one has visibility into the full AI surface they are now professionally accountable for
- The accumulation happened during periods of normal patient care — the physician was busy practicing medicine

**Why it is distinct:** Prior findings addressed the "first AI decision" problem. This finding addresses the portfolio inventory problem — independent practices now have a multi-tool AI surface that has never been documented in aggregate, where each tool was added incrementally without a governance decision, and no physician has a methodology for taking stock.

**Market complaint:** "I have been using AI for a year and I couldn't tell you everything it is doing in my practice right now if my carrier asked."

## Finding 2: Patient-Driven AI Input Into Records (NEW)

**Signal:** Patients arriving at consultations with AI-generated symptom analyses, self-diagnosis documents, or AI-summarized medical histories produced at home using consumer AI tools. The physician must decide how to document AI-generated patient input — a category no Swiss guidance addresses.

**What practitioners report:**
- Patients presenting printed summaries or screengrabs of AI-generated health analyses they ran at home
- The content enters the medical record through physician documentation of patient-reported AI content
- Uncertainty about whether acknowledging the AI content in the record implies endorsement or clinical validity
- No Swiss medical record standard addresses AI-generated patient input as a documentation category
- The physician is now potentially documenting the output of an AI system they did not deploy and cannot evaluate

**Why it is distinct:** All prior physician-facing AI governance guidance addresses physician-deployed AI. No prior finding has addressed patient-deployed consumer AI entering the clinical record through patient initiative — creating physician accountability for AI output they neither requested nor supervised.

**Market complaint:** "A patient showed me a 10-page AI analysis they printed out at home. Now it is part of their record. I never asked for that. What am I supposed to do with it?"

## Finding 3: Canton Geneva Public Health AI Mandate Gap (NEW)

**Signal:** The Service de la santé publique (Geneva cantonal health authority) has issued no canton-specific guidance on AI deployment for independent practices. Physicians are navigating federal frameworks (FMH, Swissdamed, MedDO) with no Geneva-calibrated interpretation or cantonal-level practical guidance.

**What practitioners report:**
- Geneva physicians looking for local guidance and finding only federal-level frameworks
- A specific expectation in Geneva that cantonal-level practical guidance would exist for independent practitioners — it does not
- A belief that "Geneva-specific" interpretation is available for institutional actors but not accessible to independent practices
- FMH guidance increasingly authoritative without cantonal proportionality filter applied for small independent practice scale

**Why it is distinct:** Prior findings identified FMH-level guidance lacking operational methodology. This finding isolates the cantonal dimension — Geneva physicians expect a Geneva cantonal practical standard and cannot find it, while institutional actors operate within a governance infrastructure independent practices are structurally excluded from.

**Market complaint:** "I called the FMH. They directed me to guidelines that assume I have a hospital behind me. I called the canton. They said to check the federal guidance. I am in the middle with no local standard to refer to."

## Finding 4: AI Tool Vendor Contract Physician Accountability Trap (NEW)

**Signal:** Vendor terms of service for commonly used practice management and clinical software contain AI usage terms that, when accepted by email, create physician professional accountability structures the physician has not evaluated — specifically around AI model updates, data usage for model training, and diagnostic responsibility delegation language.

**What practitioners report:**
- No independent Geneva physician has reviewed the AI-specific terms in their practice management software contracts
- Standard vendor contract Click-through acceptance activates AI terms that establish data processing obligations, model update provisions, and in some cases limitation-of-liability language that does not align with physician professional accountability architecture
- Some vendors include AI model training opt-out buried in settings not surfaced during normal software use
- A physician materially cannot technically opt out of vendor AI without losing platform functionality
- Vendor terms and FMH physician accountability obligations contain structural gaps that create a gap between what the vendor disclaims and what FMH requires the physician to bear

**Why it is distinct:** Prior findings established vendor contract signature as professional liability acceptance. This finding specifically isolates the technical inability to opt out as creating de facto rather than de jure AI authorization — and identifies the data usage for model training as a discrete nFADP Art.6 lawful basis problem distinct from the AI output accountability problem.

**Market complaint:** "My software has AI features I never specifically enabled. I checked the settings and if I turn them off, the system loses functionality I actually need. So I am stuck with AI I did not choose, using data I cannot fully trace."

## Finding 5: Physician AI Training Documentation As Professional Exposure (NEW)

**Signal:** When AI tools are deployed in a practice, vendors provide product documentation, video tutorials, and in-app guidance. No existing standard or practice defines what AI training documentation a physician is required to maintain as professional evidence — and vendor-provided training does not map to FMH oversight documentation requirements.

**What practitioners report:**
- Training records from software vendors are generic and not physician-specific
- No FMH guidance exists on what AI competency documentation a small practice must maintain
- Evidence that a physician "did not know how the AI worked" is not a recognized defense in the same way FMH oversight applies — but proving competent deployment requires documentation that does not exist in standardized form
- The most common current practice: zero documentation of how AI was deployed, trained on, or supervised
- A physician who cannot demonstrate AI oversight methodology faces the same exposure as a physician who deployed AI negligently

**Why it is distinct:** Prior findings addressed FMH oversight principle without operational endpoint. This finding isolates the training documentation gap as a discrete exposure category — the competency question is distinct from the oversight question, and no Geneva-calibrated guidance addresses what AI training documentation a small practice physician must maintain or for how long.

**Market complaint:** "I watched some videos from the vendor when they rolled out the AI features. That is not really training. That is not documentation. But I don't know what training documentation I am supposed to keep."

## Finding 6: Second-Physician AI Consultation Accountability Problem (NEW)

**Signal:** When a physician requests a second opinion from a colleague through any channel — informal corridor consultation, formal second opinion request, or networked practice consultation — and the second physician uses AI to support their review, the original physician faces AI-assisted medical opinion arriving through a second physician's AI system with no documentation standard for how that AI-assisted second opinion should be recorded, attributed, or incorporated.

**What practitioners report:**
- Informal second opinion consultations now routinely include AI-generated diagnostic suggestions from the consulting colleague
- The original physician receives AI-generated input from a second physician they cannot independently evaluate
- No Swiss medical record standard addresses AI-assisted second opinion documentation
- The accountability chain becomes circular: the second physician has AI-generated content in their own records, the original physician documents "second opinion provided" with no AI disclosure
- There is no Geneva protocol for what "disclose AI use in second opinion" means operationally

**Why it is distinct:** All prior findings address the primary physician's AI use. This finding addresses a genuinely new accountability structure: AI use by a second physician creating a derivative accountability concern for the primary physician. The problem is structurally different because the primary physician did not deploy the AI and cannot document what they did not control.

**Market complaint:** "A colleague I asked for a second opinion ran it through an AI tool. The recommendation came back with AI analysis. Now it is part of my patient's record. I never asked for AI and I cannot explain what it did. But it is in my documentation."
