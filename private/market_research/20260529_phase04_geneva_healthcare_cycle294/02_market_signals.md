### Finding 1: nFADP Art.22 reversal right vs. medical record integrity creating simultaneous legal paradox (NET-NEW)

**What the problem is:**
Art.22 grants data subjects right to request reversal of automated decisions, applies where AI substantially informs a decision reflected in the patient record, creates physician legal obligation to reverse/override AI-derived record content on patient request.

**Why it's a market signal:**
Geneva independent practices have no protocol for Art.22 reversal requests in clinical context — records authoritative under physician name can't be casually reversed without clinical process, yet non-response is non-compliance, response without protocol creates different liability.

**Specific mechanism:**
Automated decision reversal in medical record ≠ reversing a transaction — reversal implies annotation/correction with maintained record integrity, no Geneva protocol for proportionate clinical notation satisfying Art.22 without altering medical record architecture, physician faces two incompatible obligations simultaneously: maintain record integrity + execute reversal right.

**Why NET-NEW for C294:**
All prior Phase 04 nFADP signals addressed Art.5 lawful basis or Art.20 access rights — reversal right (Art.22) has been absent from Phase 04 analysis, specifically the interaction between reversal right and medical record integrity creating structural paradox not addressed by any framing in C214–C293.

### Finding 2: AI vendor data extraction clause as active professional secrecy exposure (NET-NEW)

**What the problem is:**
Vendor click-through terms increasingly include explicit clauses authorizing patient data extraction for model training, benchmarking, or aggregate analysis — practice accepts terms for core functionality, data extraction provision activates without separate consent, physician's patient data processed under vendor's claimed lawful basis incompatible with medical confidentiality.

**Why it's a market signal:**
Physician professional secrecy obligations under Art.321 CP extend to data physician controls — vendor processing creates external data flow physician did not authorize and cannot stop without losing core AI functionality, physician potentially in breach of professional secrecy through acceptance of terms they didn't evaluate against professional obligations.

**Specific mechanism:**
This isn't the same as the "I signed the terms" finding from prior cycles — prior findings addressed vendor signature/processing authorization problem in general terms, this finding identifies the specific mechanism: data extraction clause embedded in click-through terms with no separate physician-controlled opt-out, creating active processing under vendor's claimed lawful basis against physician's own Art.321 CP obligations.

**Why NET-NEW for C294:**
All prior Phase 04 vendor-term findings framed the problem as "terms accepted without review" or "lawful basis unclear" — this naming isolates the specific data extraction clause as active professional secrecy exposure, naming the specific clause type (extraction/benchmarking/processing authorization) not just the acceptance mechanism, distinct from C287–C293 vendor signature findings.

### Finding 4: AI deployment consent documentation as discrete medical-legal instrument with no market template (NET-NEW)

**What the problem is:**
Three-consent architecture (treatment + nFADP + AI) identified in prior cycles as structural incompatibility, but the specific problem is: no Geneva market template exists for AI deployment consent as discrete medical-legal instrument — practice attempting compliance has no reference document, creates exposure through attempt (incomplete written consent implies consent wasn't given if challenged).

**Why it's a market signal:**
Practices attempting to create AI-specific consent language have no calibrated template, in-house drafted consent language may not satisfy either nFADP or FMH standards, incomplete documentation creates evidence of negligence in consent process, practices face liability paradox: attempting compliance creates documentation trail that may be used against them.

**Specific mechanism:**
The consent instrument problem is specifically that AI deployment consent doesn't map to any existing consent category — treatment consent framework doesn't apply (AI isn't treatment), nFADP consent framework doesn't apply cleanly (clinical context), no medical-specific AI consent framework exists at Geneva level — physician attempting to document creates instrument with unknown legal weight.

**Why NET-NEW for C294:**
C293 three-consent-architecture finding named the structural incompatibility without isolating the specific absence of a template instrument — this finding names the market template gap as the acute problem, distinct from structural incompatibility framing — specifically the liability created by attempting to document without template, distinct from all prior Phase 04 consent findings which addressed the incompatibility or absence of consent language.

### Finding 5: Independent practice AI governance audit readiness as unquantified personal liability exposure (NET-NEW)

**What the problem is:**
No Geneva independent practice has a current AI governance audit trail ready for carrier/FMH challenge, same physician accountable for patient care + billing + compliance + now AI governance with no designated AI governance documentation system, audit readiness gap is present-tense and unquantified.

**Why it's a market signal:**
Carrier attestation wave (arriving in healthcare 6-12 months after legal/finance as identified in C291) will require documented AI governance evidence — independent practice has no audit architecture, no documentation system, no review schedule, no evidence base, personal liability exposure is unquantified and unpositioned, worst positioned practice type for carrier AI attestation.

**Specific mechanism:**
The audit readiness problem is specifically about the independent practice's structural incapacity to produce evidence on demand — not just that documentation is missing, but that no architecture exists to create documentation systematically, every audit-triggering event (incident, complaint, carrier query, FMH inquiry) requires retroactive reconstruction of governance decisions never documented, physician's name on record with no explainable AI governance process.

**Why NET-NEW for C294:**
All priorPhase 04 findings addressed specific named AI governance gaps — no finding addressed the independent practice's structural audit-readiness incapacity as discrete exposure category — liability exists because governance cannot be demonstrated on demand, not because specific rule was violated, distinct from all C214–C293 Phase 04 findings which named specific governance gaps without isolating audit-readiness incapacity as exposure mechanism.

### Finding 6: AI triage referral deferral accountability mismatch as clinical gateway exposure (NET-NEW)

**What the problem is:**
AI triage systems de facto operate as referral gatekeepers in outpatient workflow — when AI suggests referral and physician defers, AI-advised deferral becomes physician's clinical decision under physician's authority with no documentation standard for AI-influenced-but-not-AI-determined clinical choices, accountability attribution structurally impossible when clinical judgment documented as "deferred AI suggestion."

**Why it's a market signal:**
AI triage deployment accelerating in Geneva independent practices without referral deferral documentation protocol — physician accountable for clinical outcome of deferral but deferral logic came from AI system not evaluated for individual patient context, no Geneva protocol for documenting AI-influenced clinical decisions at point of deferral, emerging liability category with no existing market vocabulary or documentation standard.

**Specific mechanism:**
This is distinct from "AI-assisted second opinion" (C293 Finding 4) which addressed AI-generated advice entering medical record through colleague consultation — this finding addresses the specific structural mismatch when AI-advised clinical action is clinician-deferred under physician authority without AI system involvement in deferral decision, physician accountable for judgment AI influenced without AI determining, no documentation standard for AI influence attribution at time of clinical decision.

**Why NET-NEW for C294:**
C293 second opinion derivative accountability finding addressed three-party AI chain through colleague consultation — this finding addresses the specific referral-deferral scenario as distinct clinical pathway with different accountability attribution problem, same three-party structure but different trigger (deferral vs. consultation), distinct from C293 second opinion finding and distinct from all prior Phase 04 AI clinical use findings which addressed AI-generated content entering records, specifically naming AI-influenced clinical deferral as discrete unnamed documentation gap.
