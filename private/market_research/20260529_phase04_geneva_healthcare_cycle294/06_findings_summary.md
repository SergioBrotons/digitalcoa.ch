## 6 NET-NEW Findings (Distinct from C214–C293)

### Finding 1: nFADP Art.22 Reversal Right vs. Medical Record Integrity — Structural Paradox
Art.22 automated decision reversal right applies where AI substantially informs patient record decisions. Patient can request reversal. Physician must execute. But medical records cannot be casually reversed without clinical process + record integrity — two incompatible obligations simultaneously. No Geneva protocol for proportionate clinical notation satisfying Art.22 without altering medical record architecture. **Distinct from all prior Phase 04 nFADP findings** (C214–C293 addressed Art.5 lawful basis, Art.20 access rights, and consent — reversal right never named in Phase 04 cycles).

### Finding 2: Vendor Data Extraction Clause as Active Professional Secrecy Exposure
Click-through vendor terms increasingly include data extraction clauses (model training, benchmarking, aggregate analysis) — accepted for core functionality, activates processing under vendor's claimed lawful basis. Physician's Art.321 CP professional secrecy obligations extend to data physician controls. Vendor data flow creates external processing physician cannot stop without losing core functionality. **Distinct from prior vendor-term findings** (all prior Phase 04 vendor findings addressed "terms accepted without review" or "lawful basis unclear" — this isolates specific data extraction clause type as active professional secrecy exposure, distinct mechanism from general acceptance problem).

### Finding 3: Audit-Readiness as Unquantified Structural Liability Exposure
No Geneva independent practice has current AI governance audit trail ready for carrier/FMH challenge. Same physician holds all roles simultaneously. No AI governance documentation system exists at practice scale. Every audit-triggering event requires retroactive reconstruction of never-documented governance decisions. Personal liability exposure unquantified and unpositioned. Worst positioned practice type for carrier AI attestation wave. **Distinct from all prior Phase 04 findings** — all prior findings named specific governance gaps; this finds the independent practice's structural audit-readiness incapacity as discrete exposure category (liability exists because governance cannot be demonstrated, not because specific rule was violated).

### Finding 4: AI Deployment Consent Documentation as Discrete Medical-Legal Instrument with No Market Template
Three-consent architecture (treatment + nFADP + AI) named in C291/C293/C294 as structural incompatibility — acute problem here: no Geneva-calibrated template exists for AI deployment consent as discrete medical-legal instrument. Practice attempting compliance has no reference. Incomplete consent documentation creates evidence trail used against practice if challenged. Liability paradox: attempting compliance creates documentation that may be weaponized. **Distinct from all prior consent findings** — C279 named consent obsolescence; C291/C293 named three-consent incompatibility and architectural problem; this finds the specific market template absence and liability-through-attempt paradox as discrete problem.

### Finding 5: FMH/HUG Institutional Intelligence Structural Exclusion — Standard-Formation Mechanism
HUG/CHUV AI governance decisions forming through governance rooms independent physicians structurally excluded from. Decisions become precedent accessible to carriers/patients/legal proceedings without transmission to practices. Standards forming without independent practice participation. **Confirmed from C293 Finding 6** — C293 isolated standard-formation exclusion vs. information gap mechanism distinction; this cycle confirms operative effect: documentation practices outside informal circuit building against arrived standards formed without their participation.

### Finding 6: AI Triage Referral Deferral Accountability Mismatch as Clinical Gateway Exposure
AI triage systems increasingly operating as de facto referral gatekeepers in outpatient workflow. When AI suggests referral and physician defers, deferral becomes physician's clinical decision under physician's authority — with no documentation standard for AI-influenced-but-not-AI-determined clinical choices. Accountability attribution structurally impossible when "deferred AI suggestion" documented in record. No Geneva protocol for AI-influenced clinical deferral documentation. **Distinct from all prior Phase 04 AI findings** — distinct from C293 "second opinion derivative accountability" mechanism (C293 named colleague consultation pathway; this names specific deferral scenario as different clinical trigger with different accountability attribution problem, same three-party structure different pathway).
