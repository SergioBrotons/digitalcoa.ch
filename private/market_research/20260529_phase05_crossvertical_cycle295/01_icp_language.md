## How the Four ICPs Describe Their AI Problem

### The Universal Opening Frame

Across all four verticals, the ICP begins with the same sentence structure:

> *"I signed something / I responded to something / I agreed to something — and now I'm not sure what I actually committed to."*

This is not metaphorical. Every professional in Geneva's boutique sector has signed, attested to, or otherwise bound themselves to an AI governance standard — and the content of that binding is only now becoming legible. The market is built on retrospective exposure recognition, not anticipatory concern.

## Cross-Vertical Language Map

### 1. The Retrospective Confirmation Vocabulary

| Vertical | The Retrospective Confirmation |
|---|---|
| **Legal** | "I signed the carrier attestation and wasn't sure what I was certifying to" |
| **Finance** | "I told the institutional client we had adequate AI governance processes — we didn't" |
| **Consulting** | "I signed an AI tool's terms of service. I didn't read it. I'm not sure what I agreed to." |
| **Healthcare** | "I signed the carrier renewal with the AI attestation clause. I filled it in based on two nights of research." |

The retrospective confirmation vocabulary appears across all four verticals as the primary entry-point conversation. The ICP is not discovering a risk — they are naming an existing commitment they made without full understanding.

---

### 2. The Adequacy Anxiety Pattern

Every ICP uses "adequate" — and no one can define it:

| Vertical | "Adequate" Usage |
|---|---|
| **Legal** | "How do I know what adequate AI governance looks like for a 3-person firm?" |
| **Finance** | "We have adequate controls in place — I just can't demonstrate it under audit conditions" |
| **Consulting** | "The client asked if we had adequate human oversight. I didn't know what to say." |
| **Healthcare** | "FMH says 'adequate human oversight.' I don't know what that means in my practice." |

The adequacy anxiety vocabulary is universal. It is the anxiety that the word "adequate" creates when paired with a standard no one has defined. All four verticals feel this identically.

---

### 3. The Accountability Question Vocabulary

Each vertical frames the personal accountability question through its professional context:

| Vertical | The Accountability Question |
|---|---|
| **Legal** | "If I use AI in a client matter and something goes wrong — am I still the attorney of record?" |
| **Finance** | "If the algorithm recommended it and the client loses money — am I personally accountable?" |
| **Consulting** | "If my recommendation was AI-assisted and the client challenges it — can I defend the methodology?" |
| **Healthcare** | "If a patient brings me an AI-generated analysis, and I follow it, and something goes wrong — what do I document?" |

The accountability question vocabulary is structurally identical across verticals — only the professional context changes. The underlying fear (personal exposure through professional role) is the same.

---

### 4. The Evidence Problem Vocabulary

Cross-vertical inability to produce evidence of human authorization:

| Vertical | The Evidence Problem |
|---|---|
| **Legal** | "I can't show which AI was used, on which matter, with which client data — because I never tracked it" |
| **Finance** | "We don't have any documentation of human authorization preceding algorithm-assisted investment decisions" |
| **Consulting** | "The sub-consultant's AI output is in my deliverable. I can't reconstruct how we got to the recommendation." |
| **Healthcare** | "The AI recommended X. I documented the decision X. I didn't document that I considered and rejected an alternative." |

The evidence problem vocabulary describes the same structural failure: contemporaneous documentation of human authorization does not exist in any vertical's current process.

---

### 5. The Swiss Hosting False Adequacy Vocabulary

A cross-vertical myth that functions as false compliance confidence:

| Vertical | Swiss Hosting Assumption |
|---|---|
| **Legal** | "We're using a Swiss-hosted AI tool — that's our DPA" |
| **Finance** | "We selected a Swiss data center vendor. I assumed that covered the processing agreement question." |
| **Consulting** | "The tool has a Swiss option. We ticked that box and moved on." |
| **Healthcare** | "Patient data stays in Switzerland — that's what our vendor told us. We didn't verify further." |

The Swiss hosting vocabulary reveals a cross-vertical pattern: "Swiss hosted" is used as a proxy for "legally compliant" even when the processing agreement, data flow architecture, and API processing location have not been verified.

---

### 6. The Scale Problem Vocabulary

Each vertical names scale-specific structural impossibilities:

| Vertical | Scale-Specific Structural Barrier |
|---|---|
| **Legal** | "Every framework I found was written for a firm with a compliance department. We have three attorneys." |
| **Finance** | "FINMA tells us what adequate looks like in seven dimensions. We have one compliance officer who's also the CIO." |
| **Consulting** | "The SRO guidance assumes a firm with legal counsel and an IT team. I'm both." |
| **Healthcare** | "I activated AI features because my assistant showed me how. I'm the solo practice." |

---

### 7. The Delegation Blind Spot Vocabulary

Across all four verticals, the accountable professional has delegation exposure they cannot see:

| Vertical | Delegation Blind Spot |
|---|---|
| **Legal** | "My associate has been using consumer ChatGPT with client data. I found out six months later." |
| **Finance** | "We started using an agentic AI portfolio tool. I didn't realize it was running continuously until I saw the trading logs." |
| **Consulting** | "My sub-consultant produced a workstream using AI. My name is on the deliverable. I don't know what they used." |
| **Healthcare** | "My staff uses scheduling AI, billing AI, patient communication AI. None of it is in a policy. I'm theattending physician." |

## Universal Operational Phrases

### English — Ready-to-Use

- "I signed something and I'm not sure what it means"
- "I need to know what I'm responsible for before I recommend anything to my partners"
- "How do I prove I actually reviewed what AI produced?"
- "Adequate — but adequate to what standard?"
- "The documentation I have would not survive an audit"
- "I didn't realize that was a requirement"
- "Swiss hosting doesn't mean what I thought it meant"
- "Before my carrier asks again"
- "3-person firm. No compliance team. Personal liability. That's the setup."

### French — Ready-to-Use

- "J'ai signé quelque chose et je ne sais pas ce que ça signifie"
- "J'ai besoin de savoir ce dont je suis responsable avant de recommander quoi que ce soit à mes associés"
- "Comment est-ce que je prouve que j'ai réellement examiné ce que l'IA a produit ?"
- "Adéquat — mais adéquat à quelle norme ?"
- "La documentation que j'ai ne survivrait pas à un audit"
- "Je ne savais pas que c'était une exigence"
- "Hébergement suisse ne veut pas dire ce que je pensais"
- "Avant que mon assureur ne repose la question"
- "Étude de 3 personnes. Pas de service conformité. Responsabilité personnelle. C'est le contexte."
