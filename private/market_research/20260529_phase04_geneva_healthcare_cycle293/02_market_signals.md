# 02 — Market Signals: Geneva Independent Practice AI Accountability

## Phase 04 — Cycle 293

---

## Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures in the Geneva Healthcare AI Market

---

## 1. Carrier Attestation Wave Arriving Without Prior Warning

Geneva medical insurers have begun distributing AI governance attestation forms to independent practices as part of renewal documentation. The forms are designed by underwriting teams, not physician professional liability teams. The attestation commits the physician to an undefined "adequate AI governance controls" standard with no Geneva-calibrated definition available at the time of signing.

**Specific complaint:** "J'ai reçu le formulaire trois jours avant la date limite. Je ne savais pas qu'il fallait répondre à ça. Je ne savais pas que c'était personnel."

("I received the form three days before the deadline. I didn't know this needed to be answered. I didn't know it was personal.")

**Failure mode:** The attestation creates a personal liability exposure the physician did not have before receiving the form. Signing without review documents a position. The pre-signing review market for medical practitioners does not exist in Geneva the way it does for wealth managers.

---

## 2. Medical Record AI Authorship Evidentiary Gap Surfacing at Incident Moment

Physicians discovering that AI-assisted documentation from the past 12–24 months creates a present-tense evidentiary problem when any adverse event is reviewed. The record bears the physician's name and signature. AI contributed to the content. The physician cannot reconstruct what AI contributed, in what manner, or whether the AI output was reviewed.

**Specific complaint:** "Si un patient me poursuit demain, je ne peux pas expliquer comment l'IA a participé à ma décision. C'est mon nom sur le dossier."

("If a patient sues me tomorrow, I cannot explain how AI participated in my decision. It's my name on the file.")

**Failure mode:** The evidentiary gap operates irrespective of whether the physician made the correct decision. The liability exposure is about the documentation trail, not the quality of care.

---

## 3. EMR Vendor AI Feature Activation Without Deliberate Decision

Independent practices discovering that AI features have accumulated in their EMR/practice management software through software updates, staff activation, and default feature enablement — without the physician making a deliberate governance decision. Each update potentially activates new AI functionality.

**Specific complaint:** "On a changé de version il y a trois mois. Je ne sais pas ce qui est maintenant IA et ce qui ne l'est pas. Et je ne sais pas où les données vont."

("We changed version three months ago. I don't know what is AI now and what isn't. And I don't know where the data goes.")

**Failure mode:** The physician now bears professional responsibility for AI usage they did not consciously authorize and may not be aware of. This is accumulating in every practice using any EMR with embedded AI features.

---

## 4. Patient-Generated AI Content Entering Medical Records

Patients arriving with consumer AI-generated health analyses (e.g., analysis of their own symptoms or conditions from a consumer AI tool) presented to the physician. The physician incorporates the analysis into the record under their professional authority. No documentation standard exists for this category of content.

**Specific complaint:** "Un patient arrive avec une analyse IA qu'il a faite lui-même. Je la mets dans mon dossier. Est-ce que c'est maintenant ma responsabilité?"

("A patient arrives with an AI analysis they did themselves. I put it in my record. Is this now my responsibility?")

**Failure mode:** Three-party chain — patient → physician record → physician liability — with no documentation protocol governing patient-generated AI content entering a physician's medical record.

---

## 5. AI-Assisted Second Opinion Derivative Accountability

Colleague consultation in Geneva increasingly involves AI-assisted analysis. The consulting physician's AI-generated output enters the original physician's patient record. The original physician is professionally accountable for content from a system they did not deploy, configure, or review.

**Specific complaint:** "Mon collègue m'a envoyé son avis mais il a utilisé l'IA pour le produire. C'est dans mon dossier. Si le patient a un problème, qui est responsable — lui ou moi?"

("My colleague sent me their opinion but they used AI to produce it. It's in my record. If the patient has a problem, who's responsible — them or me?")

**Failure mode:** Three-party chain creates accountability distribution that no Geneva protocol governs: original physician, consulting physician, AI system — each with undefined documentation obligations.

---

## 6. Treatment Consent + nFADP Consent + AI Consent Architecture Failure

Physicians attempting to address three consent structures through their existing single treatment consent form. FMH guidance has not specified coexistence protocol between treatment consent, nFADP data processing consent, and AI-specific consent disclosure. The three have structurally incompatible requirements that cannot be satisfied by a single form.

**Specific complaint:** "Mon formulaire de consentement a trois pages. Je ne sais pas si ça couvre tout. Si un patient refuse une partie, est-ce que je peux quand même utiliser l'IA?"

("My consent form is three pages. I don't know if it covers everything. If a patient refuses one part, can I still use AI?")

**Failure mode:** Consent architecture attempts collapse into either excessive paperwork or unexamined false adequacy. No Geneva-specific integration protocol exists for independent practice scale.

---

## 7. Geneva Institutional Intelligence Exclusion as Structural Disadvantage

HUG and CHUV developing institutional positions on AI governance through governance rooms to which independent practices have no access. These positions are forming effective standards: carriers reference them in underwriting; legal challengers reference them in proceedings; patients may reference them in complaints. Independent practices building governance decisions in isolation from standard-formation process.

**Specific complaint:** "CHUV a des protocoles pour l'IA. Nous, on n'a rien. Et quand l'assurance me pose des questions, elles参照ent des standards que je ne connais pas."

("CHUV has protocols for AI. We have nothing. And when the insurer asks me questions, they reference standards I don't know.")

**Failure mode:** Standards forming without participation of those they govern. The gap between institutional practice and independent practice AI governance standards is widening while independent practices remain unaware of the process.

---

## 8. AI Training Documentation as Missing Competency Record

No Geneva-specific standard exists defining what AI competency documentation a physician must maintain. FMH oversight requirements are stated as principles without competency documentation specifications. Most independent practices maintain zero AI training documentation.

**Specific complaint:** "FMH dit qu'il faut une supervision humaine. Pour quelqu'un qui utilise l'IA depuis deux ans, est-ce qu'il y a une formation reconnue? Non. Est-ce que je doisdocumenter quelque chose? Je ne sais pas quoi."

("FMH says human oversight is required. For someone who's been using AI for two years, is there recognized training? No. Do I need to document something? I don't know what.")

**Failure mode:** Physician accountable for maintaining oversight without knowing what AI competency documentation demonstrates adequate oversight. No proportionate documentation standard for independent practice scale.

---

## 9. Technical Inability to Opt Out as De Facto AI Authorization

Vendor AI terms accepted through click-through activate AI features that cannot be technically disabled without losing core software functionality. Data usage for AI model training provisions are buried in settings screens. The physician accepting vendor terms has not evaluated whether those terms are compatible with their FMH accountability requirements.

**Specific complaint:** "Si je désactive l'IA, je perds des fonctionnalités essentielles. Mais si je l'active, je ne sais pas ce que le fournisseur fait avec mes données."

("If I deactivate AI, I lose essential functionality. But if I activate it, I don't know what the provider does with my data.")

**Failure mode:** Technical architecture forces a de facto AI authorization decision the physician never consciously made, with terms they have not evaluated, creating obligations they have not consented to under a framework they may not be in compliance with.

---

## 10. FMH Human Oversight Principle Without Operational Endpoint

FMH has stated physician human oversight requirement without providing: operational definition, documentation requirement, review frequency, competency standard, or threshold for what constitutes adequate oversight. The principle provides no actionable architecture.

**Specific complaint:** "La FMH dit qu'il faut superviser. Elle ne dit pas ce que ça veut dire le matin à 8h30 avec dix patients."

("FMH says supervision is required. It doesn't say what that means at 8:30 AM with ten patients.")

**Failure mode:** The guidance produces compliance appearance without substance. The same person holds user, compliance officer, and billing manager roles simultaneously. Genuine independent-practice-proportionate oversight architecture is structurally impossible with current guidance.
