## Six Net-New Findings — Phase 04, Cycle 293

These findings are distinct from all prior Phase 04 findings (C214–C292). Each isolates a specific mechanism, finding, or market failure that prior research cycles did not name with this precision.

### Finding 01: FMH Human Oversight Principle Without Operationally Deliverable Wednesday

**The finding:** FMH has stated the human oversight principle without defining what "Wednesday morning at 8:30 AM" looks like for a two-physician independent practice. The principle provides no operational endpoint, no documentation requirement, no review frequency, no competency standard, and no threshold definition.

**The distinction from prior findings:** All prior Phase 04 findings named specific AI governance gaps (tool inventory absence, carrier attestation exposure, Swissdamed inaccessibility, institutional intelligence exclusion). This finding isolates the specific mechanism by which an apparently helpful guiding principle produces zero operational governance: the principle-without-operational-endpoint mechanism. No prior Cycle 293 Phase 04 finding named this specific translation failure as the structural problem — it is the reason the other problems are structurally unsolvable at independent practice scale.

**The mechanism:** The same physician holds user, compliance officer, and billing manager roles simultaneously. The guidance cannot be operationally fulfilled because its conditions are unmet at the scale it's addressed to. This is not an implementation gap — it's not that physicians are failing to implement the principle. It's that the principle has no implementable form at the scale it's directed at.

**Language the ICP recognizes:** "On m'a dit de superviser. Elle ne dit pas ce que ça veut dire le matin à 8h30 avec dix patients." ("I was told to supervise. It doesn't say what that means at 8:30 AM with ten patients.")

### Finding 02: Carrier Attestation Operating as Personal Liability Instrument Without Geneva-Calibrated Pre-Signing Review Market

**The finding:** Carrier AI attestation forms designed by underwriting teams create personal liability exposure for physicians committing to an undefined "adequate AI governance controls" standard. No Geneva market service offers pre-signing review for medical practitioners. Form designed by carrier legal — no independent Geneva-calibrated analysis available before signature.

**The distinction from prior findings:** All prior Phase 04 findings named carrier attestation as exposure. This finding specifically isolates the underwriting-design vs. medical-liability-design mechanism: the form creates personal obligation structured around carrier risk evaluation logic that no physician can satisfy through independent review because no Geneva-calibrated analysis exists until after the form is signed. Not the absence of a service (which C290/C291 named) — the specifically-designed-for-underwriting structure of the form and how that structure creates personal liability without any pre-signing discharge pathway.

**The market failure:** Wealth managers are beginning to develop pre-signing review for carrier attestation. No equivalent exists in the medical practitioner market. The service gap is the same — but the mechanism creating the gap is specifically that insurer-designed forms operating on undefined standards create physician personal liability without review, and that review market does not currently exist in Geneva.

**Language the ICP recognizes:** "Si je signe un document qui dit que l'IA est bien utilisée, est-ce que je suis personnellement responsable? Je n'ai aucune idée de quelle est la bonne réponse. Personne ne peut me la donner avant que je signe." ("If I sign a document saying AI is properly used, am I personally liable? I have no idea what the right answer is. No one can give it to me before I sign.")

### Finding 03: Patient-Generated AI Content as Discrete Medical Record Accountability Category With No Geneva Protocol

**The finding:** A patient arrives at an independent practice with consumer AI-generated health analysis they conducted independently, and the physician incorporates this content into the patient's medical record under the physician's professional authority. Three-party chain: patient → consumer AI analysis → physician medical record → physician professional liability. No documentation standard exists in Geneva for this category.

**The distinction from prior findings:** All prior Phase 04 findings addressed physician-deployed AI or vendor AI systems. No prior Phase 04 finding addressed the patient as the originating source of AI-generated content that enters the physician's record and creates physician liability. The patient-as-vector AI content creating derivative accountability in the physician's record is a distinct category — not physician AI deployment, not vendor AI deployment, but patient AI deployment creating consequential physician accountability through record incorporation.

**The structural gap:** The physician is accountable for the medical record regardless of who or what generated the content it contains. The patient generates content through a consumer AI tool, the physician incorporates it under their professional authority, and no documentation protocol exists for this three-party content chain. The physician may not have the training to critically evaluate consumer AI-generated health analysis, has no documentation of what AI tool the patient used, and cannot reconstruct the AI's contribution method when the record is later challenged.

**Language the ICP recognizes:** "Un patient arrive avec une analyse IA qu'il a faite lui-même. Je la mets dans mon dossier. Est-ce que c'est maintenant ma responsabilité?" ("A patient arrives with an AI analysis they did themselves. I put it in my record. Is this now my responsibility?")

### Finding 04: AI-Assisted Second Opinion Derivative Accountability Chain Entirely Ungoverned — Three-Party Chain With No Geneva Protocol Defining Each Party's Documentation Obligations

**The finding:** When an independent physician in Geneva seeks a second opinion from a colleague, and that colleague uses AI to assist their analysis, the AI-generated output enters the original physician's patient record. The original physician is professionally accountable for content from a system they did not deploy, configure, or review. No Geneva protocol defines documentation obligations for any of the three parties involved.

**The distinction from prior findings:** All prior Phase 04 findings addressed the primary physician's own AI use. No prior Phase 04 finding addressed derivative accountability through a colleague's independent AI deployment — where the accountability chain runs backwards through a second physician to an AI system the first physician never touched. This is not about the consulting physician's AI use (which is their own accountability domain) — it's specifically about documentation obligations that attach when AI-generated content from a colleague's independent system enters the original physician's patient record and creates original physician liability exposure.

**The structural gap:** Peer consultation is routine medical practice. AI-assisted peer consultation is becoming routine. The three-party accountability chain (original physician → consulting physician → AI system) has no documentation protocol defining what each party is required to document about AI involvement. The original physician cannot reconstruct what AI contributed when they attempt to defend their reliance on a second opinion that was AI-assisted by a colleague.

**Language the ICP recognizes:** "Mon collègue m'a envoyé son avis mais il a utilisé l'IA pour le produire. C'est dans mon dossier. Si le patient a un problème, qui est responsable — lui ou moi?" ("My colleague sent me their opinion but they used AI to produce it. It's in my record. If the patient has a problem, who's responsible — them or me?")

### Finding 05: Three-Consent-Architecture Structural Incompatibility Named as Integration Problem Requiring Own Architecture — Not Additive Consent Language

**The finding:** Treatment consent, nFADP data processing consent, and AI-specific consent disclosure are three structurally incompatible consent requirements that cannot be satisfied through a single form or through additive consent language. Single-form solutions produce false adequacy across all three simultaneously. No Geneva-specific consent integration protocol exists for independent practice scale.

**The distinction from prior findings:** C291 named the three-consent architecture market gap. C279 named consent obsolescence. This finding specifically isolates the structural incompatibility mechanism: the three consent structures have mutually incompatible requirements that cannot be resolved by good-faith efforts to add comprehensive language to existing forms. The problem is not insufficient wording — it is architectural incompatibility requiring its own integration design, not additive language. No prior Phase 04 finding named this specific structural incompatibility requiring its own architecture rather than better wording.

**The structural gap:** When a patient declines one consent structure (e.g., nFADP processing consent), the practice cannot use AI-assisted documentation for that patient even if treatment consent is given. When a patient refuses to sign AI-specific consent but treatment consent is given, the physician must continue without AI-assisted documentation or argue AI is not part of treatment decision. These interactive effects are not addressed by any consent form currently in use in Geneva independent practice. No integration protocol exists to manage the three-consent structure as a coherent system.

**Language the ICP recognizes:** "J'ai un formulaire de consentement pour le traitement. Maintenant on me dit qu'il faut aussi un consentement pour l'IA. Je ne peux pas avoir un consentement pour tout. Les patients vont refuser de signer." ("I have a consent form for treatment. Now I'm told I also need AI consent. I can't have consent for everything. Patients will refuse to sign.")

### Finding 06: Geneva Institutional Intelligence Structural Exclusion Named as Standard-Formation Mechanism — Operative Standards Forming Without Independent Practice Participation Through Channels Without Transmission Pathway

**The finding:** HUG and CHUV are participating in Geneva institutional AI governance standard-formation through governance channels from which independent practices are structurally excluded. Decisions made in these rooms are becoming effective operative standards: carriers reference institutional positions in underwriting, legal proceedings reference institutional practices in liability determinations, patients may reference them in complaints. Independent physician practices are building autonomous AI governance decisions without operational intelligence about what standards are being formed in rooms they do not access.

**The distinction from prior findings:** C290 named institutional intelligence inaccessibility. C286 named cantonal caution signal. This finding specifically isolates the structural exclusion from standard-formation process as the formation mechanism — not information gap (which is about what someone knows) but standard-formation exclusion (which is about who participates in creating the standards that everyone is measured against). No prior Phase 04 finding named the specific mechanism: independent practices are structurally excluded from the rooms where operative standards for their sector are being set, and carrier/patient/legal proceedings access to those standards creates measurement against standards physicians helped create neither the standards themselves nor any documentation of the formation process.

**The structural gap:** The documentation window problem applies in institutional standard formation: as long as independent practices are excluded from formation channels, they are building documentation against arrived standards whose formation process they did not observe and cannot reconstruct. The standards being formed at HUG/CHUV in 2025–2026 will be the standards applied to independent practices in 2026–2027 through carrier attestation, legal proceedings, and patient challenges. The window for participating in their formation is structurally closed to the practices they will govern.

**Language the ICP recognizes:** "CHUV a des protocoles pour l'IA. Nous, on n'a rien. Et quand l'assurance me pose des questions, elles参照ent des standards que je ne connais pas." ("CHUV has protocols for AI. We have nothing. And when the insurer asks me questions, they reference standards I don't know.")
