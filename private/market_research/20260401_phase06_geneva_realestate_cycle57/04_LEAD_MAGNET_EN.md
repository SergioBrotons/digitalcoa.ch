# The Small Real Estate Agency AI Governance Question
**What Geneva's real estate agencies need to know about AI compliance — before it's a problem**

---

Geneva's real estate agencies are using AI. Listing description generators. Automated Valuation Models. Tenant screening tools. Pricing analytics. Virtual tour narration.

Most agencies haven't stopped to ask: what are our legal obligations for these tools?

The answer matters — not just for compliance, but for the trust of your clients, the protection of your business, and the quality of the decisions you're making with AI assistance.

And now there's a new pressure: foreign property purchase restrictions are coming. The G7 summit is ten weeks away. The Federal AI Ordinance has been operative for fifteen months. Most Geneva agencies haven't classified a single AI tool under it.

## The Five-Layer Problem

### Layer 1: AVMs + FADP Art. 21 — Now Operating Without Federal AI Ordinance Compliance

Every Automated Valuation Model (AVM) output that influences a transaction or lending decision requires human review under FADP Art. 21. This is not optional. It doesn't matter what the AVM vendor claims about accuracy. The legal obligation is human review before the valuation is used for a consequential decision.

But the Federal AI Ordinance (operative since January 1, 2025 — fifteen months) adds a second layer: AVM tools must be classified by risk level. High-risk AI requires documented human oversight, technical documentation, and transparency disclosures. Most Geneva agencies using AVMs haven't classified them under either framework.

**What this means in practice:**
- When you use an AVM to set a listing price: human review required under FADP; risk classification required under Federal AI Ordinance
- When a lender uses your AVM data: both parties have obligations
- When you present an AVM valuation to a buyer or seller: they should know it was AI-generated and reviewed

The AVM vendor's accuracy rate is irrelevant to both legal obligations. The Federal AI Ordinance has been in force for 15 months. The compliance gap is not a minor issue.

### Layer 2: Tenant Screening AI — The Most Legally Complex Use Case in Real Estate

AI tenant screening tools are powerful. They are also the most legally complex AI application in Geneva real estate, because they trigger simultaneous obligations under multiple frameworks:

**FADP obligations:**
- Data minimization: only process what's necessary for the screening purpose
- Purpose limitation: don't use screening data for anything other than tenant evaluation
- Transparency: disclose to every applicant what criteria were used and what data was processed
- Legal basis: documented basis for processing each screening data category

**Geneva cantonal rental law:**
- Non-discrimination obligations apply to screening criteria — AI or not
- An AI that systematically disadvantages a protected class creates liability even if criteria were "objective"
- The burden is on you to demonstrate your screening process is non-discriminatory

**Federal AI Ordinance:**
- Tenant screening AI is high-risk AI. Human oversight documentation required.

Most agencies using AI tenant screening have addressed none of these. The AI vendor said it was compliant. That is not a legal defense.

### Layer 3: Property Management AI — The Widest Set of FADP Triggers

Property managers using AI for operational decisions face FADP Art. 21 in multiple directions. With the Swiss labor shortage (400,000 workers across the economy), property management agencies face pressure to adopt more AI tools to maintain service levels with limited staff. This makes the governance gap more operationally consequential.

**Rent-setting recommendations:** An AI system recommending a rent increase based on market analysis is making an automated decision affecting the tenant. Human review required.

**Maintenance prioritization:** AI prioritizing one maintenance request over another is an automated decision. Tenants affected deserve to know how decisions were made.

**Lease renewal assessments:** AI flagging a lease for non-renewal based on payment history or property damage records is an automated decision. FADP Art. 21 requires human review.

### Layer 4: Foreign Property Purchase Restrictions — Geneva Luxury Market Under Pressure

Swissinfo (April 2026) confirms: foreign property purchase restrictions are coming. Geneva's luxury property market — which relies heavily on international HNWI buyers — faces a shrinking addressable market.

For AI governance, this creates a specific dynamic: as the international buyer market shrinks, the agencies that survive will be those serving fewer but more complex transactions. Those complex transactions will require higher governance standards — more due diligence, more client documentation, more scrutiny of every decision. AI tools used in those transactions will face more regulatory attention, not less.

The agencies that build compliant AI governance frameworks now will be positioned for the more complex, more scrutinized transaction environment that foreign restrictions are creating.

### Layer 5: G7 Évian — Near-Term Transaction Uncertainty

The G7 summit in Évian (June 15–17, 2026) creates near-term uncertainty for Geneva's international luxury property market:

- HNWI buyers may delay Geneva property decisions ahead of or during the G7
- International buyer due diligence requirements may increase during the security period
- AI systems managing international client data during the G7 period may attract additional regulatory scrutiny — particularly if data flows through US-based AI infrastructure

Geneva agencies should use the 10-week window before the G7 to audit their AI governance: international client data flows, AVM documentation completeness, and tenant screening legal compliance. The G7 period is not the time to be explaining compliance gaps to clients or regulators.

## The DPIA Requirement — Why It's Mandatory

Under FADP Art. 21, a Data Protection Impact Assessment is mandatory for high-risk data processing. AI-based AVMs, tenant screening tools, and property management decision systems are high-risk.

**A DPIA documents:**
- What AI systems you're using and what data they process
- The purpose of each processing activity
- The legal basis for each processing activity
- The risks to data subjects (tenants, buyers, sellers)
- The measures you've implemented to address those risks

**Most Geneva real estate agencies have not conducted a DPIA for their AI tools.** The DPIA is not optional for high-risk AI uses. It's a legal requirement that has been in force since the Federal AI Ordinance took effect in January 2025. The absence of a DPIA is itself a compliance gap — and it has been accumulating for fifteen months.

## Your AI Governance Checklist

**1. AVM Documentation**
For every AVM tool in use: vendor, data inputs, decision output, Federal AI Ordinance risk classification, human review process documented.

**2. Tenant Screening Legal Basis**
For every AI screening tool: documented legal basis for each data category, criteria disclosed to applicants, non-discrimination assessment completed, Federal AI Ordinance risk classification documented.

**3. Property Management AI Human Review**
For rent-setting, maintenance prioritization, and lease renewal AI: human review checkpoint documented in each process.

**4. DPIA Completeness**
Has a DPIA been conducted for each high-risk AI system in use? Is it current? (15+ months of non-DPIA use is a compliance gap.)

**5. International Client Data Flows**
Are AI tools processing data of international buyers/clients? Is the processing location documented? Is client consent documented for cross-border data processing?

**6. Staff Training**
Do your agents and property managers understand when AI is making a recommendation requiring human review? Do they understand the Federal AI Ordinance risk classification?

**7. Vendor DPA Coverage**
Do you have executed Data Processing Agreements with every AI vendor processing client or tenant data?

**8. Client/Tenant Transparency**
Are clients and tenants informed when AI is used in decisions affecting them?

**Score:**
- 0–2: Immediate attention required — significant exposure across multiple AI uses; Federal AI Ordinance non-compliance accumulating
- 3–4: Framework developing — AVM and tenant screening gaps likely; foreign restrictions/G7 not yet addressed
- 5–6: Ahead of most Geneva agencies — maintain and document; address G7/foreign restrictions implications
- 7–8: Governance in place — monitor regulatory developments and market conditions
