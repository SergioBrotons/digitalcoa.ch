## Problem Statement (5 layers)

### Layer 1: AVMs + FADP Art. 21 (Highest-Risk AI in Real Estate)
Automated Valuation Models produce figures that influence lending and transaction decisions. Under FADP Art. 21, every automated valuation requires human review before being used in a consequential decision. The AVM vendor's "AI accuracy" claim doesn't eliminate your human review obligation. The Federal AI Ordinance (operative 15+ months) adds risk classification requirements on top — most agencies using AVMs haven't classified them under either framework.

### Layer 2: Tenant Screening AI — Most Legally Complex AI Use Case
AI tenant screening tools: (a) process sensitive personal data (credit, income, behavioral signals), (b) must comply with FADP data minimization and purpose limitation, (c) must disclose screening criteria to applicants, (d) must comply with Geneva cantonal non-discrimination obligations. Most agencies using these tools haven't addressed all four.

### Layer 3: Property Management AI — Diverse FADP Triggers
Rent-setting recommendations, maintenance prioritization, lease renewal assessments — each is an automated decision triggering FADP Art. 21 human review. Property managers have the most diverse set of FADP triggers in the real estate sector. With Swiss labor shortage making AI operational efficiency critical, property managers face pressure to adopt more AI tools — widening the governance gap further.

### Layer 4: Foreign Purchase Restrictions — Geneva Luxury Market Under Pressure
Swissinfo (April 2026): foreign property purchase restrictions coming. Geneva's luxury property market — which relies heavily on international HNWI buyers — faces a shrinking addressable market. The agencies serving this market face a dual pressure: reduced commercial opportunity AND increased regulatory scrutiny as transaction volume shifts to fewer, more scrutinized deals. AI governance frameworks become a competitive differentiator for agencies managing fewer but more complex international transactions.

### Layer 5: G7 Évian — Near-Term Transaction Uncertainty
G7 summit in Évian (June 15–17, 2026) creates near-term uncertainty for Geneva's international luxury property market. HNWI buyers may delay Geneva property decisions ahead of or during the G7. Geneva agencies should anticipate: (a) transaction timing delays in Q2 2026, (b) increased due diligence requirements for international buyers during the G7 security period, (c) potential AI data flow scrutiny for international client information during the security period.
