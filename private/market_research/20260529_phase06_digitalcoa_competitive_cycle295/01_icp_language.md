## Purpose

Map how each of the four C295 vertical ICPs — legal, finance, consulting, healthcare — describes the problem that DigitalCoa.ch is positioned to solve, using the language patterns surfaced in C295 Phases 01–04 and cross-vertical synthesis from Phase 05. The naming patterns reflect the specific accountability geometry each ICP faces in the current regulatory and market environment (May 2026).

---

## The Core Language Shift in C295

In C294, the dominant framing was "I have a gap I need to close." In C295, the dominant framing — confirmed across all four verticals — is shifting toward: "I have already committed to something I cannot defend, and I need to understand what that commitment means before someone examines it."

This is a structural shift from urgency about future requirements to anxiety about present-tense exposure. The language below reflects this shift.

---

## Legal ICP — Naming Pattern (C295)

**Before they can name the problem:** "The carrier asked me to attest to our AI authorization architecture. I realized I was attesting to something I had not actually built. I signed it anyway because the renewal deadline was concrete and the alternative was a gap in the form."

**What surfaces the urgency:** "I have associates using Copilot. I did not authorize Copilot. I cannot demonstrate that I supervised what I did not authorize. The shadow AI situation is not a future risk — it is a present-tense exposure that already exists."

**What they are most afraid of naming aloud:** "The SAV will ask what our decision tree looks like. We don't have a decision tree. We have a framework document that was written to satisfy the SAV requirement, not to actually govern anything."

**What triggers the search for help:** "A colleague mentioned he had gone through a carrier renewal where the AI questions had gotten more specific. He said the third-generation questions were a different kind of question than the second-generation ones. I realized I hadn't tracked the difference — I just answered what was in front of me."

**What they need before they trust:** "I need to know this practice has sat across from a Geneva attorney who had the same specific problem — a 2-to-5 person firm, a carrier renewal in the next 90 days, shadow AI they didn't know about. I need the person to name my situation before they propose a solution."

**How they describe the ideal outcome:** "I want to be able to show the SAV a decision tree, not a framework document. I want to know what I actually signed when I signed the vendor DPAs. I want to be able to tell a client what our AI methodology is and have it mean something operationally, not just on paper."

## Finance / WM ICP — Naming Pattern (C295)

**Before they can name the problem:** "The carrier third-generation attestation asked me to confirm human authorization for each AI system in our portfolio. I realized I could not produce that record. I could not identify all the systems. I signed the form with gaps I knew about."

**What surfaces the urgency:** "An institutional client sent an AI methodology DDQ. The questions were not the same as the carrier questions — they were asking about our AI decision attribution architecture. I had never heard that phrase used as a specific documentation category. I realized I was being asked about something I didn't have a name for, let alone a document for."

**What they are most afraid of naming aloud:** "The MD is personally named in the FINMA guidance. The guidance says the MD is responsible for AI governance adequacy. I have been assuming this is an organizational obligation. I am beginning to understand it is a personal one."

**What triggers the search for help:** "The cross-firm comparison happened at a dinner. Someone said their documentation had been built specifically to survive a supervisory examination. The phrase 'survive a supervisory examination' hit differently than 'be compliant.' It suggested there was a specific evidentiary standard being aimed at, not just a documentation requirement."

**What they need before they trust:** "I need to know this practice has tracked not just what the carrier questionnaire asks, but how the logic of the questions has evolved across renewal cycles — and what the evolution tells us about what the carrier is actually trying to assess."

**How they describe the ideal outcome:** "I want to walk into the next carrier renewal with a complete AI authorization record. I want to be able to show institutional clients a methodology document that was designed to survive examination, not just satisfy a form. I want the documentation to have a logic — a reason for each element — not just a list of tools."

## Consulting ICP — Naming Pattern (C295)

**Before they can name the problem:** "We lost a client engagement because we couldn't respond to their AI DDQ. We were asked to confirm our AI governance methodology. We didn't have one to confirm. The prospect asked us to describe it. We couldn't."

**What surfaces the urgency:** "My sub-consultant used AI on a client deliverable. I reviewed it, approved it, presented it. The AI contribution to the work was invisible — I couldn't extract it, and I couldn't demonstrate what human judgment had actually done. I am now accountable for work that includes AI decisions I cannot identify, let alone justify."

**What they are most afraid of naming aloud:** "The CHF 250,000 personal liability under nFADP Art.63. I have heard it mentioned. I have not had anyone explain what it means for my specific practice type — what actions or omissions actually trigger it, and what the personal exposure actually looks like in practice."

**What triggers the search for help:** "My insurance broker asked three questions about AI governance at renewal. Two I couldn't answer. One I answered wrong — I realized later I had misunderstood the question. The broker noted my answers. I don't know what they did with them."

**What they need before they trust:** "I need to know this practice has explained the Art.63 personal liability ceiling to Geneva consulting principals before — and that it was explained in terms that relate to what I actually do on a Wednesday afternoon, not as a general regulatory summary."

**How they describe the ideal outcome:** "I want a methodology document I can include in proposals. I want to know what I can and cannot delegate to sub-consultants who use AI. I want to be able to respond to client AI DDQs with something that demonstrates genuine governance, not just a policy document written to look compliant."

## Healthcare ICP — Naming Pattern (C295)

**Before they can name the problem:** "Swissdamed is coming. I don't know what it will ask. I don't know what my AI tool's vendor will commit to before Swissdamed asks. I have been assuming these two things would align automatically. I am no longer sure they do."

**What surfaces the urgency:** "A patient asked me to explain how their data is processed by the AI triage tool I use. I couldn't give them a coherent answer. The explanation the vendor provides is written for a regulatory audience, not a patient. I realized I am the one who has to bridge that gap — and I don't have the materials to do it."

**What they are most afraid of naming aloud:** "The FMH human oversight principle. I have read it. I don't know what it requires me to do on a Tuesday morning when I am reviewing AI-suggested triage decisions for twelve patients. The principle exists. The Tuesday translation does not."

**What triggers the search for help:** "At an FMH event, a colleague mentioned she had had her vendor terms reviewed for the first time in two years. She said the review had revealed things she wished she had found earlier. It hadn't occurred to me to get a review. It also hadn't occurred to me I might need one."

**What they need before they trust:** "I need to know whether the Art.22 reversal right and the FMH human oversight principle can actually be reconciled operationally — or whether they are in genuine conflict that requires an explicit resolution decision on my part, not just a documentation effort."

**How they describe the ideal outcome:** "I want to be able to show a patient how their data was processed by my AI tool. I want to be able to pass a Swissdamed compliance review without having rebuilt my entire documentation system. I want to know whether my AI triage tool decisions are covered by my malpractice insurance if something goes wrong — and what 'something goes wrong' actually means in the context of AI-assisted triage."

## The Cross-Vertical Language Identity

Across all four verticals in C295, a single structural experience produces the same emotional entry point: the ICP has signed something, delegated something, or committed to something — and only later understood what they committed to. The experience is:

1. **Something was signed or done** (carrier form, vendor DPA, sub-consultant engagement, patient data processing)
2. **Only later did the full implication become clear** (not at signing, not at delegation — after)
3. **The gap is now present-tense** (not future, not anticipated — now)
4. **The ICP is looking for someone who names the specific situation before proposing a solution**

DigitalCoa.ch's positioning language must lead with this structure: name the specific thing the ICP signed, delegated, or committed to, then name the specific gap that creates, then offer the evidence architecture as the resolution.
