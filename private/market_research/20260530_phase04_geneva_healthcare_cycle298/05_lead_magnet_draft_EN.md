## What This Guide Covers

### Part 1: The Accountability Question Nobody Has Answered

When I talk to physicians in Geneva about AI, the question that comes up first — before which tool, before how much it costs — is: "Who is liable if something goes wrong?"

The honest answer is that Swiss law doesn't give a clean answer. What I can tell you is how the liability actually flows today, based on how medical liability law works and how AI vendor contracts are structured. This is what I've found after reviewing the actual frameworks.

### Part 2: The Data Protection Problem With Most AI Tools

I have reviewed a number of AI diagnostic support tools marketed to independent practices. Most of them route patient data through servers outside Switzerland. Under the revised Federal Data Protection Act (nFADP), this creates a compliance gap that the physician — not the vendor — is ultimately responsible for.

I don't think most physicians know this. And I don't think most vendors are transparent about it.

### Part 3: What FMH Actually Says (And Doesn't Say)

The FMH has published on digitalization and AI. I've read what they published. What they have not published is operational guidance for independent practice AI use. The difference matters — principles are not practice standards.

### Part 4: The Consent Question

When you use AI in a patient consultation, do you need to disclose it? Do you need a separate consent for data processing? Is the patient's existing care consent sufficient? I've looked at the legal framework and the honest answer is that there is no standardized approach. Each practice is improvising.

### Part 5: What "AI-Ready" Actually Means — A Practical Assessment Framework

I've developed a framework I use with practitioners to evaluate whether an AI tool is actually ready for use in an independent Geneva practice. It covers: data residency, DPA quality, vendor liability limitation clauses, consent requirements, and FMH alignment. It's not a approval — it's a structured way to ask the right questions.
