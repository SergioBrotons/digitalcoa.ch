# ICP Language: Geneva Healthcare — AI Deployment for Patient Data

## Phase 04 | Cycle 298 | 2026-05-30

---

## How the ICP Talks About AI Problems

### The Accountability Frame

The Geneva independent practitioner does not say "I'm worried about AI regulation." They say:

- **"If something goes wrong with an AI-assisted diagnosis, am I the one who's responsible?"**
- "Who is liable when the algorithm is wrong and I've already relied on it?"
- "I sign off on every decision — but I'm not the one who built the system. How does that work?"

The frame is personal, professional, and legal. They are not asking about technology. They are asking about accountability.

---

### The Patient Confidentiality Frame

AI tools in healthcare means patient data leaving the practice. The practitioner's concern is not abstract:

- **"Can I use a cloud-based AI diagnostic tool without violating patient confidentiality?"**
- "My patients trust me with their data. If I feed it into an AI system I don't control, what am I actually doing?"
- "The hospital has IT departments. I have myself and a part-time secretary."
- "I don't even know where the data goes when I use that radiology AI tool."

The issue is not awareness — they know AI tools exist. The issue is informed use and control.

---

### The FMH Obligation Frame

The FMH (Foederatio Medicorum Helveticorum) is the reference point for professional conduct:

- **"Does FMH have any actual guidance on AI, or are we in a grey zone?"**
- "I'm required to practice according to the state of the art. Does AI count as the state of the art, or a liability?"
- "My FMH membership means something to my patients. If I use AI and something goes wrong, does FMH stand behind me?"
- "There are professional standards for everything — prescriptions, referrals, record-keeping. Where are the standards for AI?"

They do not want to be first movers on AI. They want clear professional standards before they adopt.

---

### The Administrative Burden Frame

Geneva practices are small. Administrative overload is a persistent complaint:

- **"I already spend more time on paperwork than on patients. Will AI add to that?"**
- "Every new system means more time learning, more time documenting, more things that can go wrong."
- "I've been told AI will streamline things. In practice, it's added another layer of complexity."
- "My staff needs training. Who has time for that?"

AI is perceived as potentially increasing rather than reducing administrative load — especially during the transition period.

---

### The Trust Gap in AI Vendors

Practitioners are skeptical of AI vendor marketing:

- **"The salesperson tells me it's compliant. But can they prove it?"**
- "I've asked vendors directly: where does our patient data go? Some couldn't answer clearly."
- "Big tech companies have terms of service longer than my entire consent form. That tells me something."
- "There's a difference between 'GDPR compliant' and actually respecting patient confidentiality."

The issue is credibility of claims, not existence of compliance frameworks.

---

### The Diagnosis Support Liability Question

This is the most acute concern for physicians using AI for clinical decision support:

- **"If the AI suggests a diagnosis and I miss it because I overrode the AI, am I liable?"**
- "And if I follow the AI suggestion and it's wrong — am I also liable?"
- "I was taught to exercise independent clinical judgment. What happens when the AI and my judgment disagree?"
- "Patients are starting to ask me: 'Did you use AI?' I don't know what to say."

The physician sees a catch-22: they are liable whether they follow AI or ignore it, without clear guidance.

---

## Language Markers (How to Recognize the ICP)

| Marker | Example phrasing |
|--------|-----------------|
| Personal accountability | "Am I responsible?" "Who is liable?" |
| Professional obligation | "FMH standards" "state of the art" "professional duty" |
| Data control concern | "Where does the data go?" "I don't control it" |
| Vendor skepticism | "Can they prove it?" "Terms of service" "marketing" |
| Liability ambiguity | "What if the AI is wrong?" "Catch-22" |
| Administrative fear | "More paperwork" "Learning curve" "training time" |

---

## Emotional State

The dominant emotion is **frustrated paralysis**. They want to use appropriate tools. They know AI can help. But they cannot find clear guidance on:
- What is permitted
- What creates liability
- What constitutes proper use
- Who is responsible when something goes wrong

This creates a wait-and-see posture that vendors interpret as resistance, but which is actually rational risk management by professionals who are personally and professionally accountable for every decision.
