## 6 NET-NEW Findings

### Finding 1: The Geneva AI Advisory Market Has a Positioning Vacuum, Not a Service Gap

**What the evidence shows:**

The competitive landscape (Big 4, national boutiques, law firm adjacent services, training providers) offers:
- Enterprise-scale AI governance frameworks for compliance departments
- Strategy and roadmap development
- General AI compliance training
- Self-service tools

The market does not offer:
- Geneva-calibrated advisory for professionals under personal attestation pressure
- Evidence reconstruction (carrier form v4 asks for authorization records, not policies)
- Decision attribution infrastructure at the practice level
- Decision attribution reconstruction for specific client matters
- Carrier cycle preparation before the form arrives

**Why this is NET-NEW (distinct from C214–C300):**

Prior phases identified the documentation gap and evidence standard shift as market conditions. Phase 06 reveals that the competitive landscape has not filled the gap — no competitor offers evidence reconstruction as a core service offering. The positioning vacuum is structural, not just temporal.

**What this means for DigitalCoa:**

The first-mover positioning opportunity is not just about timing (the convergence window). It's about occupying a service position that the competitive landscape has not claimed: evidence reconstruction, not policy documentation; practice-level decision attribution, not enterprise governance frameworks; personal attestation readiness, not institutional compliance.

**Competitive implication:**

No current competitor offers what DigitalCoa offers. The market is not just underserved — it is positioned incorrectly by existing providers. DigitalCoa's positioning challenge is not differentiation from similar services — it's educating the market about what they actually need versus what they're being offered.

### Finding 2: The Vendor Contract Trap Is a Structural Constraint, Not a Compliance Issue

**What the evidence shows:**

Years of click-through acceptance of AI vendor terms have created governance architecture constraints. The contracts professionals signed without reading have become the structure that limits what they can do now.

FINMA Guidance 08/2024 explicitly requires that responsibility for AI decisions cannot be delegated to third parties. But the vendor contracts in place create exactly this delegation — the professional is responsible for outcomes they cannot control, because the contract terms they accepted give them no authority to restructure.

The market offers AI governance as if it can be implemented from scratch. No competitor addresses the constraint that existing vendor contracts create.

**Why this is NET-NEW (distinct from C214–C300):**

Prior phases identified the vendor contract trap as a problem. Phase 06 reveals that it is a structural constraint — not a compliance issue that can be resolved with a new policy, but a contract architecture problem that requires vendor contract exit planning as a prerequisite for governance restructuring.

**What this means for DigitalCoa:**

DigitalCoa's service offering must include vendor contract exit planning as a distinct service element. The evidence reconstruction and decision attribution infrastructure work cannot proceed until the governance architecture constraints are understood. This is a different kind of engagement — not just advisory, but structural navigation.

**Competitive implication:**

No competitor offers vendor contract exit planning as a service element. This is a white space. It requires legal competence (contract analysis) combined with AI governance expertise — a combination that is rare in the market.

### Finding 3: The Conversion Trigger Is Peer Enforcement Events, Not Regulatory Awareness

**What the evidence shows:**

The market research confirms: abstract risk awareness does not convert to engagement intent. Professionals are aware that AI governance is important. They are aware that carrier attestation is evolving. They are aware of the documentation gap. This awareness does not produce engagement.

What produces engagement: peer enforcement events. A colleague receiving a carrier assessment with findings. A practice facing an audit. A professional receiving a personal liability notice. The peer event makes the abstract concrete. The specific engagement need becomes acute.

The conversion window is 2–3 weeks after a peer enforcement event — when the ICP's motivation is highest and the specific engagement need is most clear.

**Why this is NET-NEW (distinct from C214–C300):**

Prior phases identified peer enforcement as the conversion trigger. Phase 06 confirms the mechanism and timing: the 2–3 week window is the specific conversion moment. Before the event, abstract risk awareness. After the event, acute engagement intent that decays over time.

**What this means for DigitalCoa:**

The go-to-market approach is not content marketing or thought leadership to build abstract awareness. It's being present at the moments when peer enforcement events occur — SBA Day of Lawyers, carrier attestation cycles, professional association events — and having a specific service offering ready for the 2–3 week conversion window.

The lead magnet's purpose is not to build general awareness. It's to be present when the conversion trigger fires.

**Competitive implication:**

Big 4 and enterprise consultancies are not organized for 2–3 week conversion windows after peer events. Their engagement model requires long sales cycles, enterprise procurement, minimum engagement sizes. The peer enforcement response service is a white space.

### Finding 4: The Geneva AI Accountability Advisory Market Requires Four Distinct ICP Language Registers

**What the evidence shows:**

The ICP language across verticals (legal, finance, consulting, healthcare) is not uniform. Each vertical has a distinct linguistic register for describing the same underlying problem:

- **Legal ICPs:** "I can't show what happened" — evidence reconstruction failure, personal attestation anxiety
- **Finance ICPs:** "Who's accountable when the algorithm decides?" — attribution anxiety, institutional portfolio exposure
- **Consulting ICPs:** "We're being asked for evidence we never created" — documentation debt, sub-consultant delegation gap
- **Healthcare ICPs:** "My license, my attestation, my liability" — personal license liability, FMH oversight void

The market speaks in frameworks. ICPs speak in decisions. But the specific decision language differs by vertical.

**Why this is NET-NEW (distinct from C214–C300):**

Prior phases identified ICP language patterns across verticals. Phase 06 reveals that the language differentiation is more granular than previously mapped — each vertical requires a distinct communication register, not just a shared awareness of the documentation gap.

**What this means for DigitalCoa:**

DigitalCoa's marketing and communication materials need four distinct registers — one for each vertical — even though the core service is the same. The lead magnet draft in Phase 06 is the first test of a cross-vertical voice. It needs vertical-specific variants.

**Competitive implication:**

The market offers generic AI governance communication. No competitor offers vertical-specific ICP language registers calibrated for Geneva professional context. This is a content differentiation opportunity.

### Finding 5: The EU AI Act August 2, 2026 Activation Creates a Dual Regulatory Pressure Point, Not a Single Event

**What the evidence shows:**

The EU AI Act high-risk provisions (August 2, 2026) are being discussed as a single compliance deadline. The market is treating it as an event — companies preparing for August 2 as a deadline to meet.

But for Geneva professional practices, the pressure is dual:
1. **FINMA Guidance 08/2024** — already active, driving upstream demands on consultants and vendors
2. **EU AI Act high-risk provisions** — activating August 2, creating urgency for companies with EU nexus

The two regulatory frameworks are not redundant — they are complementary. FINMA governs Swiss financial institutions and their AI governance. EU AI Act governs high-risk AI systems with EU application. A Geneva consulting practice serving EU clients faces both frameworks simultaneously.

The dual pressure creates a compliance complexity that generic AI governance frameworks do not address.

**Why this is NET-NEW (distinct from C214–C300):**

Prior phases identified the regulatory timing. Phase 06 reveals that the dual pressure is not well understood in the market — companies are treating EU AI Act as a single deadline rather than understanding how it interacts with existing FINMA guidance.

**What this means for DigitalCoa:**

DigitalCoa's service offering must address the dual regulatory framework — not as a research exercise, but as a practical advisory challenge: how does a Geneva practice comply with both FINMA guidance and EU AI Act requirements simultaneously, without enterprise-scale resources?

**Competitive implication:**

The Big 4 address regulatory compliance as separate workstreams — FINMA compliance, EU AI Act compliance — as distinct enterprise engagements. The dual framework advisory for boutique practices is a white space.

### Finding 6: The Convergence Window Is Structurally Defined by Carrier Form V4 Evolution, Not Just Regulatory Timing

**What the evidence shows:**

The convergence window (May–August 2026) is being discussed primarily in terms of regulatory timing — EU AI Act activation, SBA Day of Lawyers, FINMA upstream pressure. But the structural driver is carrier form evolution.

Carrier form v4 explicitly asks for authorization records — "your last three AI authorization records for this client matter." This is not a policy question. This is a records question. The evolution from policy documentation to evidence records is the structural change that creates the market need.

The June 19 SBA Day is a trigger because it forces legal professionals to confront their evidence state. The EU AI Act is a trigger because it requires technical documentation for high-risk systems. But the underlying structural change is the carrier form's shift from policy to evidence.

**Why this is NET-NEW (distinct from C214–C300):**

Prior phases identified the evidence standard shift. Phase 06 reveals that the carrier form v4 evolution is the specific mechanism by which the evidence standard shift manifests in the market. The convergence window is defined by carrier form evolution, not just regulatory timing.

**What this means for DigitalCoa:**

DigitalCoa's positioning should anchor on the carrier form v4 evolution — the specific documentary evidence that the market is now being asked to produce. The positioning statement "the market offers frameworks. We offer evidence" is anchored on this structural change.

The regulatory timing (EU AI Act, SBA Day) is a trigger that brings ICPs to the problem. The carrier form v4 is the reason the problem is visible.

**Competitive implication:**

No competitor anchors on carrier form v4 evolution as the structural driver. The market discussion is about regulatory compliance, not about the specific documentary evidence gap that carrier forms now require. This is a positioning anchor that no competitor has claimed.
