# 04 — Compliance Context: Geneva/Switzerland Specific Regulatory
## Phase 03 C299 | Geneva Consulting ICP | 2–50 Employees

---

## Regulatory Environment Overview

### Layer 1: nFADP — Post-Transition Active Obligations

The revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP / revDSG) is fully operative. For a Geneva consulting boutique using AI tools in client engagements, the following obligations are active and statutory:

**Art.9 Third-Party Processor Obligations:** When the boutique uses an AI tool to process client data (which is every AI-assisted consulting engagement), the AI vendor is a third-party data processor under nFADP. A compliant Data Processing Agreement (DPA) must be in place before processing begins. Most boutiques accepted vendor terms without evaluating DPA adequacy. The active obligation has been present since nFADP entry into force — the transition period has closed.

**Art.6 Lawful Basis:** The boutique must have a documented lawful basis for each AI-assisted processing of client data. For consulting engagements, this is typically contract performance (Art.6(1)(c) nFADP) — the engagement letter establishes the service relationship, AI-assisted analysis falls within its scope. But this must be evaluated, not assumed. Most boutiques have not made this evaluation.

**Art.26 Controller Liability:** The boutique is the data controller for its client engagements. The AI vendor is a processor acting under the boutique's instructions. The boutique cannot transfer controller liability through vendor terms. The boutique is responsible for the lawfulness of all processing, including processing by AI vendors.

**Art.82 Personal Liability:** Under nFADP Art.82, the managing director can face personal liability for violations — not just the firm. This is distinct from the EU GDPR framework where liability typically attaches to the entity. The personal dimension is specifically Swiss.

**Cross-Border Transfer Restrictions:** Most US-based AI tools (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Microsoft AI tools) process data outside Switzerland. This triggers nFADP cross-border transfer restrictions. "Swiss-hosted" marketing does not resolve actual data processing location. Most boutiques cannot confirm where their AI vendor actually processes data.

### Layer 2: Carrier Attestation — Documentation Architecture Required

Carrier AI attestation forms are written by carrier underwriting teams to manage carrier risk exposure. They create documentation obligations — but they also create liability if the MD signs without understanding what is being committed.

**The undefined adequacy standard problem:** Carrier forms ask MDs to confirm their AI governance "meets adequacy standards" without defining what those standards are. The undefined standard creates a documentation gap — the MD cannot know what they are committing to, and the carrier cannot evaluate what was actually certified.

**Personal signature = personal exposure:** The MD's signature is personal. The carrier form is written by the carrier's legal team. No Geneva-calibrated pre-signing review service exists. The MD who signs on autopilot creates documented liability without governance infrastructure to back it up.

**Carrier renewal as new event:** When the carrier presents a renewal attestation, it creates a new comparison point — what has changed in the firm's AI governance practice since the prior attestation? The MD who signed last year and built nothing now faces the same questions with additional new ones. The carrier renewal attestation becomes evidence of non-progress if no governance build occurred.

---

### Layer 3: Client AI Governance Questionnaire — Documentation Evidence Problem

EU-domiciled clients and institutional clients are sending AI governance questionnaires to Geneva consulting boutiques. The questionnaire response functions as a documented representation about the firm's AI governance practice. When the client audits that representation, the boutique must produce evidence.

**The evidence gap:** Most boutiques responded to questionnaires without documentation architecture. They answered from memory or from a policy document written for the purpose. When clients request evidence of what was claimed, the boutique cannot produce it.

**Cross-client contradiction risk:** A boutique that responded to three client questionnaires over 12 months may have given inconsistent answers — not because they did anything wrong, but because they had no consistency tracking system. Clients who compare notes create a problem the boutique may not know exists.

**Client AI governance audit as new event type:** The client audit request (signal 2 in C299) is a new event pattern. It is distinct from the initial questionnaire. It is a follow-up request for documentation evidence. The boutique that treated the questionnaire response as a one-time administrative task now faces an evidence request they cannot satisfy.

### Layer 4: EU AI Act — Proximity Effect for Geneva Boutique Consulting

**August 2, 2026 is approximately 14 months away.** For Geneva consulting boutiques with EU-domiciled clients or EU market nexus, the EU AI Act creates specific concerns:

**Deployer obligations (Art.4 EU AI Act):** Deployers of high-risk AI systems must have sufficient technical documentation to demonstrate compliance. A Geneva consulting boutique using AI tools in client work may be classified as a deployer. If any AI tool used in client engagements is classified as high-risk, the boutique must maintain the required documentation.

**Transparency obligations:** If the boutique uses AI systems that interact with clients (e.g., AI-assisted communication, automated recommendations), disclosure obligations apply. The boutique must inform clients when they are interacting with AI.

**Existing engagement letter language:** Most boutique engagement letters predate the EU AI Act. They contain no AI governance provisions. The question of whether existing engagement letter language creates any AI Act obligations is specific to each firm's client relationships and mandate structure.

**Boutique-appropriate compliance pathway:** No Geneva-calibrated EU AI Act compliance pathway exists for a 2–50 person consulting boutique. General EU AI Act guidance is designed for enterprises with compliance functions. The boutique MD needs a proportionate methodology.

### Layer 5: CoE AI Convention — Present-Tense Deployer Obligations

The Council of Europe AI Convention (Framework Convention on AI, CETS No.225) creates deployer obligations that operate present-tense through existing mandate contracts and carrier underwriting terms — before any implementing legislation arrives in Switzerland.

Geneva consulting boutiques with EU nexus are potentially in scope for Convention deployer obligations already operating. The obligations include:
- Deployer must take into account the AI system's intended purpose and overall context
- Deployer must implement appropriate oversight measures
- Deployer must maintain human oversight to enable meaningful human intervention

These are not compliance checkbox obligations — they are operational governance requirements that apply through existing client engagement contracts.

---

### Layer 6: Sub-Consultant Processor Chain — nFADP Art.9 Chain

The prime boutique's use of sub-consultants who use AI tools creates a three-party processor chain:
1. **Prime boutique** — data controller (to the client)
2. **Sub-consultant** — data processor (to the prime boutique)
3. **AI vendor** — sub-processor (to the sub-consultant)

Each link in the chain creates nFADP Art.9 documentation obligations. The prime boutique bears controller liability for the entire chain. The chain is typically invisible — no one in the boutique can map which sub-consultants use which AI tools, what data flows through those tools, and what the vendor terms say about processor obligations.

**No Geneva boutique engagement letter contains an AI governance clause.** The sub-consultant delegation structure has no contractual foundation for the processor chain it has already created.

## Documentation Window — Present-Tense Burden of Proof

The documentation window is not a compliance deadline. It is a present-tense burden-of-proof event that will be evaluated retrospectively.

Documents created now will be presented as evidence of governance adequacy or inadequacy on future dates — when clients audit, carriers renew, or regulators review. New documentation cannot retroactively reconstruct the reasoning that existed at the time of prior decisions.

**The structural paradox:** Documentation created to satisfy carrier attestation requirements may not be the same documentation that actually protects the MD's personal liability position. The MD may be building documentation for carrier underwriting standards while under-documenting the governance decisions that would actually defend them in a personal liability scenario.

**The 90-day governance build is not primarily about compliance.** It is about creating the evidence trail that proves the MD evaluated what they were signing, made intentional governance decisions, and documented those decisions over time. This evidence trail is what D&O coverage does not create, what carrier attestation forms do not create, and what client questionnaire responses without architecture do not create.

## Immediate Compliance Action Points for This ICP

1. **Map the AI tool inventory for all active client engagements** — not a policy document, the actual tools currently processing client data
2. **Audit carrier attestation responses already given** — identify what was certified, what evidence exists to support it, what gaps need filling
3. **Review client AI governance questionnaire responses already sent** — identify cross-client inconsistencies, build consistency architecture
4. **Initiate nFADP Art.9 DPA evaluation** — identify which AI vendors need DPAs, evaluate what "compliant" means at boutique scale
5. **Assess EU AI Act nexus** — which clients, which mandates, which AI tools, what obligations already triggered
6. **Build sub-consultant AI governance clause** — engage first sub-consultant on processor chain documentation as a pilot

The compliance environment for this ICP is not overwhelming in scope — it is specific in nature. The problem is: no Geneva-calibrated, boutique-proportionate methodology exists for the MD to address these obligations systematically. That is the specific white space. That is the service gap.
