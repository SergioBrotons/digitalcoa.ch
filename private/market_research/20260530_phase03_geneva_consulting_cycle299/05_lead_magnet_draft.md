# The Geneva Consulting MD's AI Governance Self-Assessment

**If you signed a carrier AI attestation in the last 18 months, this document is for you.**

---

## Before You Read Further

If you are a managing director or partner at a Geneva consulting boutique — 2 to 50 people — and you have signed a carrier AI attestation form in the last 18 months, read this now.

The attestation you signed is being renewed. New questions have been added. The carrier wants to know what has changed in your AI governance practice since you last signed.

If you are like most of the MDs I speak with in Geneva, the honest answer is: nothing has changed. You signed it. You moved on. You had work to do.

Now the renewal is here. And the questions are harder.

This document is a self-assessment, not a sales document. It will tell you where you actually stand. What you do with that information is your decision.

---

## Section 1: The Attestation Renewal Test

**Answer these questions honestly:**

**Question 1:** Can you name every AI tool that has processed client data in your firm in the last 18 months?

**Question 2:** Do you have a signed Data Processing Agreement with each of those AI vendors that satisfies nFADP Article 9?

**Question 3:** If your carrier asked you today to provide evidence of your AI governance adequacy — not the attestation form you signed, but the actual documentation — could you produce it?

**Question 4:** Have you tracked consistency across client AI governance questionnaire responses? Can you confirm that what you told Client A is not contradicted by what you told Client B?

**Question 5:** If a sub-consultant you work with produced an AI-generated error in a deliverable, could you document the processor chain from your firm to the sub-consultant to the AI vendor?

**Question 6:** Do you know what your EU AI Act obligations are through your existing client engagements?

**Question 7:** When your AI vendor changes its terms of service — and they will — do you have a process for evaluating what changed and what risk it creates?

---

If you answered yes to all seven questions, your governance architecture is solid. Close this document.

If you did not answer yes to all seven — and most MDs I speak with do not — you have a documentation gap. This document explains what that gap means, what it creates, and what proportionate action looks like at your scale.

## Section 2: What You Actually Signed

When you signed the carrier AI attestation form, you signed a document written by the carrier's legal team. The form was designed to manage the carrier's risk, not to protect yours.

Here is what the form probably said, in substance:

*"The undersigned confirms that adequate AI governance controls are in place."*

Here is what is not defined in that statement:
- What "adequate" means
- What "governance" includes
- What "controls" must cover
- What evidence would demonstrate compliance
- What the carrier can require if they believe the attestation was inaccurate

You signed something that creates personal liability — on a standard that was written by the other side, for the other side's benefit, and was never explained to you before you signed.

I am not your lawyer. I am not your insurance broker. But I have worked with enough Geneva consulting MDs to know what happens next.

The carrier renews the form. New questions appear. The MD who signed last year and built nothing faces the same questions with six additional ones. The carrier is asking: what has changed?

Nothing has changed. That is the honest answer. And the attestation renewal makes that visible.

## Section 3: What the Carrier Renewal Actually Is

The carrier renewal is not an administrative exercise. It is a documentation event.

The carrier is creating a new timestamp. They are asking you to confirm again that adequate AI governance controls exist. If your situation has not changed — if you signed last year's form without a governance build, and you have not built anything since — this year's attestation will be weaker, not stronger.

Here is why: the carrier now has evidence of two dates. On Date A, you said you had adequate controls. On Date B, you are saying the same thing, with additional questions, and nothing has demonstrably changed.

The carrier underwriter who reviews your renewal is asking a simple question: has this firm's AI governance posture improved, stayed the same, or deteriorated since last year's attestation?

If your firm has not built a governance architecture, the honest answer to that question is: same or worse. Not because you did anything wrong. Because you treated an attestation as a form to complete rather than a governance act to perform.

## Section 4: The Client Questionnaire Audit Problem

In the last 12 months, you probably responded to at least one client AI governance questionnaire. If you have EU-domiciled clients or institutional clients, you almost certainly have.

Here is what happened: you received the questionnaire, you read it, you answered the questions as best you could, you sent it back. You moved on to the work.

Now the same client is coming back. They are not asking new questions. They are asking for evidence of what you said in the original response.

Your AI governance team — which is a polite way of saying "your client's internal compliance function" — is auditing your attestation. They want to know if what you claimed is actually in place.

Can you produce the evidence they are asking for?

Most MDs cannot. Not because they were negligent — because they answered the questionnaire without a documentation architecture behind them. They answered from knowledge, from memory, from the best intentions. None of that is evidence.

Evidence is: a document dated before the questionnaire response, describing your actual AI governance practice, that was in effect when you made the claims. You do not have that document. I know because I have asked this question to enough Geneva MDs to know what the answer usually is.

## Section 5: The Sub-Consultant Processor Chain You Cannot Draw

Here is a question I ask every MD I speak with: can you draw the processor chain for your last client engagement?

By processor chain, I mean: which sub-consultants worked on the mandate, which AI tools did they use, what data did those tools process, where did that data go, and what do your engagement letters say about AI governance obligations?

Most MDs cannot draw this chain. Not because they are disorganized — because the chain is invisible. They never thought to ask. They did not know they needed to.

Here is what is actually happening in your firm: you hire a sub-consultant for specialized work. The sub-consultant uses an AI tool — probably not one you have heard of. Client data goes into that tool. The tool processes it. The sub-consultant produces an analysis. You put your name on it and deliver it to your client.

You are the data controller. The sub-consultant is a processor. The AI vendor is a sub-processor. Under nFADP Article 9, you have controller liability for the entire chain — even though you did not evaluate the vendor, did not sign a DPA with the vendor, and did not know what tool was being used.

The engagement letter you have does not mention this. The processor chain exists in practice. It does not exist in documentation.

If a problem arises — an AI-generated error in the deliverable, a client audit, a data breach — you will be asked to produce documentation for a chain you did not know you had created.

## Section 6: The EU AI Act Is Not Abstract Anymore

August 2, 2026 is approximately 14 months away.

If you have EU-domiciled clients, or if your consulting work touches EU markets, the EU AI Act applies to you. Not as a future concern. As a present-tense obligation through existing client contracts.

Here is what I know about Geneva consulting MDs in this situation: most of them know the deadline exists. None of them have a clear picture of what it means for their specific firm.

The EU AI Act creates deployer obligations. As a deployer, you must have sufficient technical documentation to demonstrate that the AI systems you use meet the requirements applicable to their risk classification. You must implement appropriate oversight measures. You must maintain human oversight.

This is not a compliance checklist. It is an operational requirement that applies now — through your existing client relationships.

The question is not whether the EU AI Act applies to you. The question is: do you have any idea what it requires of your firm in the context of the specific AI tools and client engagements you already have?

If the answer is no, you are not behind because you are懒. You are behind because no one has published a Geneva-calibrated, boutique-proportionate EU AI Act compliance pathway for a 2–50 person consulting firm.

That is the gap I work to fill.

## Section 7: What Proportionate Action Looks Like at Your Scale

I am going to tell you what I tell every MD I work with. This is not a sales pitch. This is what the situation actually requires.

**First: make the actual AI tool inventory.** Not a policy document. Not a template. The actual list of AI tools that have processed client data in your firm in the last 18 months. Name them. Know what data went into each one.

**Second: assess your carrier attestation exposure.** The form you signed is personal. The adequacy standard is undefined. The carrier is the other side of the document — their interests are not yours. Before you sign the renewal, know what you are committing to.

**Third: build the documentation that proves what you claimed.** If you responded to client AI governance questionnaires, you made representations about your firm's AI governance practice. Those representations have evidence requirements. Build the evidence file.

**Fourth: map the sub-consultant processor chain.** Identify which sub-consultants use AI tools in your engagements. Update your engagement letter with an AI governance clause. Build the documentation architecture that makes the chain visible.

**Fifth: assess your EU AI Act nexus.** Which clients, which mandates, which AI tools. What obligations are already triggered. What you need to build before August 2026.

**Sixth: establish a vendor terms monitoring process.** AI vendors will change their terms. When they do, you need to know what changed, what risk was created, and what action you took in response. This is not a one-time review. It is a continuous governance function.

None of this is optional if you have been signing carrier attestations and accepting AI vendor terms without a governance architecture. The question is not whether to act. The question is whether to act before the next attestation renewal, the next client audit, and the next vendor terms change create a problem you cannot explain.
