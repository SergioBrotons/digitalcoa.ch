### Finding 01: Broker Accountability Gap Creates Structural Liability Transfer Problem

**The finding:** Carrier attestation forms are delivered to Geneva managing partners through their insurance broker relationship. The broker presents the form as administrative routine. The form requires the managing partner's personal signature — not a firm signature, not a compliance attestation by a designated officer. The broker's facilitation of the signature — presenting it as routine, not explaining the personal liability implications — creates a structural liability transfer: the broker's relationship with the managing partner creates trust that the form does not deserve, and the broker's E&O coverage does not clearly extend to explaining carrier form implications. The managing partner signs because they trust the broker and because the alternative is non-renewal under time pressure.

**Mechanism:** Broker presents carrier form as routine → managing partner trusts broker → personal signature applied without explanation → carrier has documented personal commitment to undefined standard → broker relationship did not provide the explanation a professional advisor would have provided → managing partner exposed without understanding the exposure.

**Implication:** Broker role review is a discrete pre-audit service. Understanding whether the broker's facilitation constitutes a separate liability — and whether the broker's E&O covers explanation obligations — changes the defense architecture and creates a distinct service from both broker services and legal advisory services.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your broker handed you a form that created a personal commitment to an undefined standard. The broker's E&O may not cover explanation of forms they didn't design. That's a separate exposure — and you need to know whether it exists before you build your defense."

### Finding 02: Tool Sprawl as Organic Uncontrolled Proliferation Creating Systematic Article 321 Exposure

**The finding:** Small Geneva law firms did not decide to adopt multiple AI tools. The proliferation occurred organically: associates brought tools, practice areas adopted different solutions, free trials became subscriptions, new associates joined with their own tool preferences. The managing partner never approved the proliferation and cannot produce a complete tool inventory. Every tool added: a vendor, a server location, a data processing relationship, a contract accepted without review, and an Article 321 CC professional secrecy exposure the firm has never evaluated. The tool sprawl problem is not "I chose too many tools" — it is "I don't know how many I have, and every one I don't know about is an undocumented Article 321 exposure."

**Mechanism:** Organic tool adoption by associates → no central tracking → managing partner unable to produce tool inventory → Article 321 vendor contracts never reviewed → every unknown tool is an unevaluated professional secrecy exposure → carrier asks for evidence of AI governance → firm cannot produce tool inventory → exposure confirmed.

**Implication:** Tool inventory documentation is a discrete bounded service — and the inventory process surfaces the deeper problem: none of the vendor contracts were reviewed against Article 321 obligations. The service output is a documented inventory plus an Article 321 compliance assessment for each tool.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You don't know how many AI tools are in your practice right now. Every one you can't account for is an Article 321 exposure you haven't evaluated. I can give you the inventory — and the review."

### Finding 03: Evidence Architecture vs. Policy Documentation — Structurally Different Documentation Types

**The finding:** AI governance policies are documents that state what should happen. Evidence is documentation of what did happen. Carrier attestation forms are architected to collect evidence — representations about what the firm actually documented — not policy statements. The market confusion about which documentation type satisfies which demand creates a specific service gap: managing partners who built AI governance policies believe they have documentation, and only discover the gap when the carrier asks for evidence they cannot produce. The documentation they built is the wrong documentation type.

**Mechanism:** Policy built → carrier asks for evidence → policy presented → carrier asks for records of what actually happened → policy does not satisfy evidence request → documentation gap confirmed → firm exposed without knowing the gap existed.

**Implication:** Evidence architecture design is a discrete service architecturally different from policy template delivery. The output is records — contemporaneous documentation of what actually happened — not documents stating what should happen. The category naming opportunity: "AI evidence architecture" is distinct from "AI governance policy."

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your policy is not evidence. Evidence is what you documented that you actually did. The form wants records, not statements. I can build the evidence architecture that produces records — not policies."

### Finding 04: Multi-Authority Documentation Sequencing Problem — Different Authorities, Different Requirements, No Cross-Mapping

**The finding:** Geneva small law firms face potential demands from multiple authorities: carrier at renewal, Ordre des Avocats de Genève in disciplinary proceedings, FINMA for financial services matters, FDPIC for data processing challenges, cantonal court in litigation discovery. Each authority asks for different documentation in different forms for different purposes. Carrier attestation evidence is aggregate and self-reported. Ordre disciplinary documentation requires demonstrating actual supervision process. Litigation discovery requires disclosing AI use in specific matters. The documentation architecture that satisfies one authority may not satisfy another — and no Geneva service maps which documentation simultaneously satisfies multiple authority demands.

**Mechanism:** Multiple authorities → different documentation requirements → firm builds separate responses for each → operationally impractical for 2–5 attorney firm → without cross-regulator mapping, firm cannot know whether documentation protects across all relevant authorities.

**Implication:** Cross-regulator documentation architecture is a discrete service. The managing partner needs one evidence system that protects across carrier, Ordre, and litigation discovery simultaneously. The service maps which records satisfy which authority's demand and designs a single documentation system that works across all of them.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The Ordre wants one thing. The carrier wants another. A cantonal court in discovery wants a third. They're not asking for the same documentation. I can build one system that works across all of them."

### Finding 05: Bilingual Client Communication Bifurcation Creating Separate Exposure Moments per Language

**The finding:** Geneva small firms serve both French-speaking and English-speaking clients. The two populations ask different questions in different languages with different implicit expectations. French clients assume the attorney knows the local Geneva standard and do not probe AI questions directly. English-speaking clients — often more informed about AI risks from international media — ask more specific questions about data use, AI involvement in their case, and the firm's AI governance policy. The firm cannot give a consistent answer in either language because they do not have a defined AI governance standard to communicate, and the bilingual communication burden is not addressed by any available service.

**Mechanism:** Client intake → French client expects Geneva-standard disclosure → no Geneva-defined standard exists → managing partner gives vague answer → client accepts vague answer → English-speaking client asks specific AI questions → managing partner has no calibrated response → exposure in both linguistic contexts without documentation in either.

**Implication:** Bilingual client-facing AI governance communication is a discrete service. Engagement letter clauses, intake disclosures, client communication templates — all calibrated for Geneva's specific bilingual context with different implicit client expectations in each language. Not translation — culturally adapted communication.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your French clients expect you to know the Geneva standard. Your English-speaking clients expect you to have a policy. Neither has been defined, and you don't have language for either. I can give you both — in both languages."

### Finding 06: Post-Attestation Signature Evidence Reconstruction Problem — Backward-Looking Architecture Gap

**The finding:** Managing partners who have already signed carrier attestation forms are now in a specific position: the signature is already on file with the carrier. The documented standard is already created. The documentation gap already exists. The managing partner cannot undo the signature. The managing partner also cannot easily produce backward-looking evidence of AI use that occurred before any documentation system existed. The service need is not "help me avoid signing" — it is "help me document what actually happened before documentation existed, and build what comes next without claiming the past was different from what it was."

**Mechanism:** Signature already applied → carrier has documented commitment → documentation gap exists → carrier could ask for evidence at next renewal → firm needs to produce evidence of past AI use → no documentation system existed → reconstruction required without fabrication → the service is not "undo the past" but "document what actually happened and build what comes next."

**Implication:** Post-attestation evidence reconstruction is a discrete service with specific framing requirements. The service must: reconstruct what AI use occurred before documentation existed, produce defensible evidence of that use, build forward-looking evidence architecture, and create a documented response that demonstrates current state without misrepresenting the past. The service is distinct from pre-attestation planning because the firm cannot claim the gap doesn't exist — they can only document around it.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The form is already signed. The gap is already there. I can't help you undo that — but I can help you document what actually happened, build what comes next, and know what to show when the letter arrives."
