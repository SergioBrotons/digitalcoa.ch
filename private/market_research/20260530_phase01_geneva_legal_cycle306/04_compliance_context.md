# Compliance Context: Geneva Legal — C306 Phase 01

## Geneva/Switzerland Specific Regulatory — C306

---

## Framework 01: Carrier Attestation Architecture as Active Exposure Document

**The structure:** Carrier PI renewal forms for Geneva law firms now include AI governance attestation questions. The managing partner's personal signature is required — not a firm signature, not a compliance officer signature. The signature commits the individual to a standard of "adequate" supervision of AI tools in the practice. The form does not define "adequate." The form does not ask for evidence at signing. The form's architecture assumes evidence exists.

**The exposure:** The signature creates a documented standard. The standard is undefined. The evidence is absent. The carrier has the signed form. The next renewal cycle will ask whether the standard was met — and the firm will need to produce documentation demonstrating compliance. The documentation does not exist.

**The Geneva-specific context:** Geneva law firms face carrier attestation requirements at each PI renewal. The attestation questions are standardized across carriers but no carrier has published a Geneva-specific operational definition of "adequate." The managing partner signs without having seen what adequate looks like for their firm size, in their canton, under their bar's guidance.

**The personal liability dimension:** The signature is personal. If a carrier challenges the attestation — if a client complaint triggers a carrier investigation — the documented signature on a form the managing partner signed personally is the primary evidence of their commitment. The signature is the exposure, not just the form.

## Framework 02: Article 321 CC Professional Secrecy — Vendor Contract Compliance Gap

**The obligation:** Article 321 of the Swiss Criminal Code establishes professional secrecy for lawyers. All information received in professional context is subject to secrecy obligations. The obligation covers client information, case details, correspondence, and any information related to professional activities.

**The AI vendor problem:** AI tools process client-related information on vendor servers. The vendor's terms of service — accepted by the managing partner when adopting the tool — define what the vendor can do with that information: data retention, sub-processor access, jurisdiction of data storage, audit rights, and compliance with legal requests. The vendor contract was not reviewed against Article 321 obligations at the time of acceptance. The managing partner does not know whether the vendor's terms are compatible with their professional secrecy obligations.

**The specific exposure:** Article 321 is a criminal provision. Violation is a criminal offense. The question of whether a specific AI vendor's data processing constitutes a violation of Article 321 is not answered by "the vendor is Swiss" or "the data stays in Switzerland." The legal qualification depends on: whether the vendor is subject to professional secrecy obligations, whether the data processing relationship creates third-party access rights, whether the vendor's sub-processors are subject to equivalent obligations, and whether the firm's use of the tool constitutes a "disclosure" under Article 321.

**The documentation gap:** No Geneva small firm has systematically reviewed their AI vendor contracts against Article 321 obligations. The documentation of that review — what tools are in use, what data each processes, what the vendor's obligations are, whether the relationship satisfies Article 321 — is absent.

## Framework 03: Multi-Authority Exposure Mapping

**The authorities:** Geneva small law firms face potential demands from multiple authorities:

- **Carrier** — at PI renewal, asking whether AI governance meets "adequate" standard
- **Ordre des Avocats de Genève** — disciplinary proceedings if a client complaint involves AI use
- **FINMA** — if the firm provides financial services, additional oversight applies
- **FDPIC** — if data processing challenges arise under Swiss data protection law
- **Cantonal court** — in litigation, opposing counsel may challenge AI-assisted work product
- **Swiss Federal Council** — ongoing AI consultation may produce new obligations

**The sequencing problem:** Each authority asks for different documentation. Carrier attestation evidence is aggregate and self-reported. Ordre disciplinary documentation requires demonstrating actual supervision process. Litigation discovery requires disclosing AI use in specific matters. The documentation architecture that satisfies one authority may not satisfy another — and building separate documentation for each authority is operationally impractical for a 2–5 attorney firm.

**The cross-regulator gap:** No Geneva service maps which documentation simultaneously satisfies multiple authority demands. The managing partner needs one evidence architecture that protects across all relevant authorities — not separate compliance tracks for each.

---

## Framework 04: FINMA Oversight Scope for Geneva Law Firms

**The scope question:** FINMA has published guidance on AI use in financial services. Geneva law firms that handle financial services matters — asset management, securities, banking — may fall within FINMA's supervisory scope. The question of whether FINMA oversight extends to AI governance documentation requirements for law firms providing ancillary financial services is not clearly resolved.

**The specific risk:** If a Geneva law firm advises on matters subject to FINMA oversight, the firm's AI governance documentation may be reviewed as part of a broader FINMA examination. The carrier's "adequate" standard and FINMA's AI governance expectations may not be aligned. The firm could satisfy the carrier while being unprepared for a FINMA request.

**The gap:** No Geneva service maps FINMA-specific AI governance requirements for law firms operating at the edge of financial services regulation. The documentation architecture that satisfies carrier attestation and FINMA expectations simultaneously is missing.

## Framework 05: Client Intake AI Disclosure — Engagement Letter Framework

**The emerging demand:** Clients are beginning to ask during intake whether AI will be used on their case and how their information will be protected. The existing engagement letter template — with vague "technology-assisted tools" language — does not specifically address AI. The language was written before AI became a material issue in legal practice.

**The documentation requirement:** Engagement letters for Geneva law firms should specifically address: whether AI will be used in the matter, what data will be processed through AI tools, what vendor relationships are involved, how client confidentiality is protected, and what the client's rights are regarding AI-assisted work product. This is not just client communication — it is evidence of informed client consent and documented professional judgment.

**The bilingual complexity:** Geneva firms serving both French-speaking and English-speaking clients need engagement letter language that works in both linguistic contexts. The French version must satisfy Geneva bar expectations for client disclosure. The English version must satisfy international client expectations for AI governance transparency. Neither standard has been formally defined, but both client populations are beginning to expect it.

**The service gap:** No Geneva service offers engagement letter AI clause framework specifically calibrated for 2–5 attorney Geneva firms serving bilingual client populations.

## Framework 06: EU AI Act — August 2026 Application Window

**The timeline:** The EU AI Act becomes applicable in August 2026 for high-risk AI systems. For deployers of AI systems, Article 4 AI literacy obligations have been live since February 2025. The August 2026 application triggers new documentation requirements for firms operating in EU regulatory scope.

**The Geneva exposure:** Geneva law firms with EU client relationships operate as AI deployers under the EU AI Act. The Act's risk classification and compliance requirements may apply to how the firm uses AI in client matters. Swiss domestic law does not have an equivalent AI Act — but firms with EU exposure must consider EU AI Act obligations.

**The documentation requirement:** AI literacy documentation — evidence that personnel have adequate AI competency for the systems they operate — is a live obligation. The documentation of who has what AI literacy level at the firm is an active exposure. No Geneva small firm has this documentation in place.

**The service gap:** Geneva-specific EU AI Act compliance documentation for small law firms with EU client exposure is missing from the market.

Each represents a discrete compliance obligation with no Geneva-specific small-firm documentation methodology available.*
