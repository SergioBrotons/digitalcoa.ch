# Competitive Analysis: Geneva Legal — C306 Phase 01

## What Exists vs. What's Missing — C306

---

## Category 01: Broker Services

**What exists:**
- Insurance brokers selling PI coverage to Geneva law firms
- Carrier attestation forms presented as administrative renewal documents
- Broker facilitation of form submission without explanation of personal liability implications

**What's missing:**
- Broker-side explanation of carrier form personal liability architecture to attorney clients before signature
- Broker-specific AI governance E&O risk review for their own exposure in facilitating attestation signatures
- No Geneva broker has published AI liability explanation methodology for their attorney clients
- Broker's own E&O coverage does not clearly extend to explanation obligations on forms designed by carriers

**The gap:** The broker is an intermediary creating personal liability exposure for the attorney without having explained that exposure. The attorney-broker relationship is structurally different from the attorney-advisor relationship — but no one has mapped where the difference becomes legally material. A service that reviews the broker's role and the attorney's position simultaneously fills a gap that neither party has named.

---

## Category 02: Policy Services

**What exists:**
- AI governance policy templates available from various providers
- SAV/SBA national AI guidance documents (general principles, not Geneva-specific)
- General AI governance frameworks applicable across jurisdictions
- Policy documents that state what should happen without documenting what did happen

**What's missing:**
- Evidence architecture design for small Geneva firms — not policy documents but record systems
- Geneva-specific evidence documentation for 2–5 attorney firms
- Documentation that satisfies carrier underwriting evidence requirements AND personal criminal defense evidentiary requirements simultaneously
- No service that produces evidence architecture as the primary output (instead of policy as primary output)

**The gap:** The market offers policies. The carrier form architecture asks for evidence. The gap is not "no policy exists" — it is "the policy type doesn't satisfy the evidence type being requested." A service that produces records, not documents, is architecturally different from everything currently available.

---

## Category 03: Vendor Services

**What exists:**
- AI vendors selling tools to law firms with standard terms of service
- Data Processing Agreements appended to vendor contracts (FADP compliance surface)
- Vendor-provided AI governance documentation for enterprise clients
- Vendor risk assessments for large firms with procurement departments

**What's missing:**
- Geneva-specific Article 321 CC vendor contract review methodology for small firms
- No systematic review of what vendor contracts actually obligate the firm to regarding professional secrecy
- No service that maps vendor data flows, sub-processor relationships, and Article 321 compliance implications for small Geneva firms
- The vendor's DPA architecture satisfies FADP obligations but does not address Art.321 CP professional secrecy requirements — these are different legal frameworks with different documentation needs

**The gap:** Vendor contracts were accepted without professional secrecy review. The service that systematically reviews vendor contracts against Art.321 obligations — producing documentation of where data goes, who can access it, and what the vendor's obligations are — is missing from the Geneva market.

---

## Category 04: Bar Services

**What exists:**
- SAV/SBA AI guidance (June 2024) — national, general principle level
- Ordre des Avocats de Genève — no Geneva-specific AI guidance published
- General continuing legal education on AI topics
- No cantonal bar working groups on AI governance for small firms

**What's missing:**
- Ordre des Avocats de Genève-specific AI governance guidance for 2–5 attorney firms
- No Geneva-specific operational definition of "adequate" AI governance
- No participation pathway for small firms to engage in standard formation at cantonal level
- No Ordre-calibrated AI governance training or documentation methodology
- The Ordre's absence from AI governance guidance is specifically named by firms as a pain point — they trust the Ordre as an institution but perceive it as absent on this issue

**The gap:** The trusted local institution has not provided Geneva-specific guidance. First to provide Geneva-specific operational guidance aligned with Ordre expectations becomes the reference for the canton. The first-mover category naming opportunity remains unoccupied.

---

## Category 05: Documentation Services

**What exists:**
- Document management systems for legal files
- General record retention policies for legal matters
- Matter management systems with activity logs
- No specific AI use documentation for legal matters

**What's missing:**
- Geneva-specific AI retention protocol for legal matters — what records to keep, how long, in what format
- No defined retention period for AI use records in legal matters under Geneva law
- No documentation system that captures AI use at the matter level (which tools, which prompts, what data processed, what decisions made with AI assistance)
- The retention debt compounds because no one has defined when the clock stops

**The gap:** Every matter adds to retention debt with no defined stop. A Geneva-specific AI retention protocol for legal matters — bounded, specific, actionable — is a discrete service. The protocol defines what records to keep, how long, and when the debt stops accumulating.

---

## Category 06: Cross-Regulator Documentation

**What exists:**
- Carrier-specific attestation documentation
- General GDPR/FADP compliance documentation
- General professional responsibility documentation
- No cross-regulator documentation architecture

**What's missing:**
- Documentation that simultaneously satisfies carrier, Ordre, and litigation discovery requirements
- No mapping of which documentation type satisfies which authority's demand
- The documentation that protects against carrier exposure may not protect against Ordre disciplinary proceedings or civil discovery
- Cross-regulator evidence architecture is unnamed in the market

**The gap:** The managing partner needs to know that a single evidence architecture protects across carrier, Ordre, and litigation discovery simultaneously. The service that designs this cross-regulator documentation architecture is missing.

---

## Category Naming Opportunity

**The category:** "AI Evidence Architecture for Geneva Small Law Firms"

Not "AI governance policy" — the market has policies. Not "AI compliance" — compliance frameworks are abstract. "Evidence architecture" names exactly what the carrier form architecture is asking for: documented evidence of what the firm actually did, not what it intends to do.

First namer owns the category. C306 competitive landscape: no one has claimed this naming.

---

## Bilingual Competitive Position

**The gap:** No Geneva service offers AI governance documentation in both French and English with cultural calibration for both client populations. The bilingual service requirement — not translation, but culturally adapted communication — is unmet.

**The opportunity:** English and French client communication requires different framing, different disclosure emphasis, different engagement letter language. A service that produces bilingual client-facing AI governance documents — calibrated for Geneva's specific bilingual context — fills a gap that generic policy templates cannot address.

---

*Competitive landscape: broker accountability unnamed, policy/evidence gap structural, vendor review missing, Ordre guidance absent, retention undefined, cross-regulator documentation unnamed, evidence architecture category unclaimed, bilingual service absent.*
