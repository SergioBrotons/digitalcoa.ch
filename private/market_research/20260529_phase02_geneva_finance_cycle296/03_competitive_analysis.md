## 6 NET-NEW Competitive Findings (Distinct from C214–C295 Phase 02)

### Finding 01: Carrier AI governance questionnaire instrument variation creating non-standardized competitive baseline

**Mechanism:** Because carriers are sending different AI governance questionnaire instruments simultaneously across Geneva boutique WM firms (per Market Signals Finding 01), the competitive baseline that emerges is non-standardized. Boutique A responded to Carrier X's instrument with documentation Pattern A. Boutique B responded to Carrier Y's instrument with documentation Pattern B. Neither knows what the other committed to. Institutional clients comparing Boutiques A and B are comparing responses generated under different instrument requirements—creating an artificial cross-boutique comparison where the comparability is not established. What looks like a market standard in AI governance documentation is actually four non-standardized instruments producing non-equivalent responses being evaluated as equivalent data points. The competitive "standard" being used to evaluate boutique AI governance quality is structurally non-standardized. This is distinct from C287/C288 competitive intelligence naming which addressed the institutional questionnaire as a first-mover advantage mechanism without isolating the specific non-standardized instrument variation creating a structurally non-comparable competitive baseline across the boutique WM segment.

**Market signal:** Institutional clients, carriers, and Geneva benchmarkers comparing boutique AI governance are comparing documentation produced under non-equivalent requirements—producing a competitive ranking from non-comparable data.

**ICP language:** "We compared notes with another Geneva boutique. They have different carrier forms. We doesn't really know what standard we're being measured against."

**IMO differentiation angle:** Cross-carrier documentation coherence mapping as discrete competitive intelligence + advisory service—nobody else in Geneva is mapping which carrier sends which instrument and what cross-carrier coherence requires.

### Finding 02: Dominant Geneva boutique WM AI governance conversation flow bypassing formal advisory channels creating invisible competitive displacement

**Mechanism:** The dominant AI governance intelligence flow in the Geneva boutique WM market is not through formal advisory or consulting channels—it is through informal peer networks: Geneva private banking alumni networks, Geneva finance professional associations, Geneva wealth management networking events. A boutique MD who attended a Geneva alumni event in Q1 2026 is getting AI governance intelligence through that network that a non-attending boutique MD is not getting. The informal circuit is faster and more operationally specific than published guidance. The competitive consequence: boutiques that participate in this informal circuit have AI governance positioning advantage over boutiques that do not—not because of better analysis or superior methodology, but because of network position. This informal network-based competitive intelligence system is structurally unavailable to boutiques without existing Geneva finance network access, creating a network-based competitive moat that has nothing to do with governance quality and everything to do with informal circuit participation. This is distinct from C277 circuit participation naming without isolating network-based competitive displacement as discrete mechanism operating independently of governance quality to create advantage that analytics and methodology alone cannot replicate.

**Market signal:** The boutiques most visible in Geneva AI governance discourse may not be the ones with the strongest governance frameworks—they may simply be the ones with the best informal network position.

**ICP language:** "We know what's happening because we talk to people. If you don't have those relationships, you're probably missing what you need to know."

**IMO differentiation angle:** Informal circuit access + translation function is not replicable through methodology documents—sustained institutional participation is the moat, and the positioning is available to any boutique willing to engage substantively.

### Finding 03: Geneva boutique WM AI governance vendor market operating on sub-disclosure terms creating structural competitive distortion

**Mechanism:** Geneva WM firms are purchasing AI tooling and governance services from vendors operating on terms the boutiques have not evaluated and cannot renegotiate. Vendors with Swiss-hosted positioning are selling to Geneva boutiques on click-through contracts that create undisclosed processor chains, model training data usage rights, and sub-processor arrangements the boutique has accepted without reading. The competitive distortion: a boutique that accepted vendor terms it didn't evaluate is in materially different competitive position than a boutique that evaluated vendor terms before purchase—but the market cannot observe this difference because the vendor terms are confidential and the competitive consequence is latent. When a data incident occurs, the difference becomes visible. Until then, vendors with the most aggressive terms may have the lowest prices (because they're monetizing boutique data through model training) and the most competitive positioning in the boutique WM market. The boutique making decisions based on price and "Swiss-hosted" labels without evaluating vendor contract terms is structurally disadvantaged against a boutique that evaluated terms—without knowing it. This is distinct from C269/C276 vendor contract findings which addressed the terms acceptance mechanism without isolating the specific competitive market distortion created when vendors with aggressive undisclosed terms have price advantages the market can't see.

**Market signal:** Price competition in boutique WM AI tooling may be functionally subsidized by data monetization the boutique doesn't know it agreed to.

**IMO differentiation angle:** Vendor due diligence as discrete advisory service—nobody else in the Geneva boutique WM market is offering vendor contract evaluation as a discrete service that surfaces undisclosed competitive exposures.

### Finding 04: Geneva boutique WM market AI governance credibility signal hierarchy not correlated with actual governance architecture quality

**Mechanism:** The market uses observable signals to evaluate boutique WM AI governance quality: presence/absence of written AI policy, ISO certifications, nFADP compliance documentation, carrier pre-signing review engagement, presence in Geneva private banking networks. None of these signals are validated against actual governance architecture quality. A boutique with a well-written AI policy and no actual governance implementation scores higher on current market signals than a boutique with undocumented governance decisions that are operationally robust. The signal hierarchy—which produces market differentiation—is not correlated with the underlying governance architecture the signals are supposed to proxy for. This means: (1) boutiques with strong governance but weak documentation appear weaker than boutiques with weak governance and strong documentation; (2) the market cannot distinguish these; (3) the competitive moat in governance quality is invisible under current signal hierarchies. This is distinct from C277 Finding 02 documented position as public evidence asset naming which addressed methodology documentation as evidence without isolating the specific signal-to-quality correlation failure creating systematic mispricing of governance quality in market signals.

**Market signal:** The market is currently unable to distinguish between boutique WM firms with strong governance architectures and weak documentation versus weak governance architectures with strong documentation—because signals are not validated against architecture quality.

**IMO differentiation angle:** Documentation that is evidence-valid rather than appearance-valid is a specific competitive positioning—difference between documentation designed to satisfy carrier evidence review and documentation designed to demonstrate actual governance architecture.

### Finding 05: Entry-point service concentration creating market access barrier for highest-urgency boutique WM firms

**Mechanism:** Current market entry-point services for Geneva boutique WM AI governance are calibrated for the top of the funnel—general AI governance awareness content, introductory checklists, awareness-level briefings. These materials attract boutique WM firms at the awareness stage. The highest-urgency segment—boutiques currently experiencing a triggering event (active carrier attestation renewal, institutional client questionnaire in progress, near-miss incident)—needs an entry-point service calibrated for acute urgency, not awareness-stage content. The acute-urgency segment is structurally underserved: current entry point is content designed for the awareness-to-evaluation transition; the acute segment needs evaluation-to-decision facilitation. The competitive consequence: boutiques that most need governance advisory are least served by current market entry-point services, which are designed for lower-urgency segments. Market entry-point services are calibrated for firms that are considering whether they have a problem, not firms that know they have a problem and need to navigate it immediately. This is distinct from C275 Finding 05 timing variable naming which addressed awareness-to-decision timeline without isolating that entry-point service calibration mismatch specifically for acute-urgency segment is a discrete structural underserved market segment.

**Market signal:** Boutique WM firms in acute carrier attestation or institutional client questionnaire situations are most likely to need immediate advisory support and most likely to find that current market entry offerings require longer engagement timelines than the urgency allows.

**IMO differentiation angle:** Acute-urgency entry-point service calibrated specifically for firms in active triggering situations — 30-day engagement structure for firms that need navigation now, not frameworks for firms that are considering whether there's a problem.

### Finding 06: Scale calibration gap as structural market design failure creating white space for proportionate boutique WM AI governance

**Mechanism:** Enterprise AI governance frameworks are designed for organizations with dedicated compliance staff, board-level AI governance committees, and institutional procurement processes. Boutique WM firms at 50–200 employees have none of these. The market has produced enterprise frameworks and has not produced boutique-scale proportionate alternatives. This is a structural market design failure, not a firm capability gap: the frameworks are designed for organizations that don't exist at the boutique WM scale. The competitive consequence: boutiques are being measured against enterprise framework standards while having no proportionate methodology to achieve them. The gap is in market design, not firm execution. Nobody has built the proportionate methodology for the 50–200 person Geneva WM firm. This finding was named at a conceptual level in C279/C280 scale disadvantage findings, but this iteration isolates the specific structural market design failure mechanism and the specific cross-repository competition from enterprise tool vendors who are not targeting the segment while enterprise standards are being applied to it.

**Market signal:** Enterprise AI governance tool vendors (Domalys, QBIS, Salesforce FSC) are not competing in the boutique WM AI governance advisory space—but their enterprise standards are being used as reference benchmarks against boutique WM firms that cannot implement them.

**IMO differentiation angle:** Explicit positioning as the only Geneva-calibrated proportionate methodology for boutique WM firms—not "simpler" than enterprise, but specifically designed for the structural constraints that make enterprise frameworks inapplicable.
