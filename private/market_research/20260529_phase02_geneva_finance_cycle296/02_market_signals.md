## Executive Summary

Geneva boutique wealth management firms at 50–200 employees face a compound accountability acceleration: institutional clients upgrading AI governance questionnaires to second-generation use, carriers introducing third-generation evidence requirements explicitly targeting portfolio construction authorization chains, and SRO silence on AI creating structural accountability vacuum during an active documentation window. The ICP is simultaneously managing client questionnaire responses, carrier attestation renewals, FINMA proportionate framework expectations, and nFADP obligations—with no Geneva-calibrated methodology mapping how these four tracks interact or compound. Six NET-NEW findings surface distinct structural problems unnamed in current market discourse.

## 6 NET-NEW Findings (Distinct from C214–C295 Phase 02)

### Finding 01: Carrier AI governance questionnaire mapping as discrete accountability layer unnamed in Geneva market

**Mechanism:** Multiple carriers are sending materially different AI governance questionnaires simultaneously across Geneva boutique WM firms—not standardized instruments. Each questionnaire maps different exposure questions, generates different response documentation, and commits the signing MD to different adequacy representations. Response to Carrier A creates documentation inventory that does not discharge obligations to Carrier B. No Geneva market service has mapped which carriers are sending which questionnaire versions, which documentation each version requires, and where the MD's signatures create exposure across carrier relationships simultaneously. The MD is generating non-standardized accountability documentation across multiple carriers simultaneously without awareness that the documents are non-standardized. This is distinct from C287/C288 carrier pre-signing review absence which named the individual carrier attestation problem without isolating that multiple carriers are operating different instruments creating compound non-standardized exposure simultaneously.

**Market signal:** Geneva boutiques with D&O policies from multiple carriers are signing different attestation instruments with different documentation requirements, operating under the assumption that "signing the carrier form" is a single categorical event. It is not.

**ICP language:** "We have two carriers. They sent different forms. I signed both." / "I think we answered the same question differently on each one."

**FR:** "Nous avons signé des attestations différentes auprès de nos assureurs. Je ne sais pas si c'est cohérent."

### Finding 02: Institutional client AI governance questionnaire second-generation data use creating ongoing cross-mandate exposure

**Mechanism:** Institutional clients that received first-generation AI governance questionnaire responses from Geneva boutique WM firms are now using those responses as operational intelligence for second purposes: assessing manager selection for new mandates, conducting portfolio construction AI methodology comparisons across their manager roster, and flagging boutiques for future瑞 recommended / disrecommended status. The responses provided in 2024-2025 are now live intelligence feeding decisions Boutiques don't know are being made about them. Any inconsistency between what Boutique A committed to in its response and what Boutique B committed to in an equivalent response creates a comparative record the institutional client uses for selection decisions. First-generation response documentation was built as a point-in-time obligation disclosure. It is now functioning as a persistent comparative intelligence instrument. This is distinct from C293 institutional cross-firm simultaneous comparison naming which addressed the market-level distortion effect without isolating the specific second-generation data use mechanism where 2024-2025 responses are feeding 2026-2027 selection decisions the boutique never anticipated when responding.

**Market signal:** Institutional clients that sent AI governance questionnaires in 2024 are not archiving responses—they are actively using them for ongoing mandate decisions.

**ICP language:** "Our big client asked us to update our AI governance questionnaire response from last year." / "I didn't realize that questionnaire was going to be updated and re-reviewed."

**FR:** "Le client institutionnel nous a demandé de mettre à jour notre réponse de l'année dernière. Je ne savais pas que c'était toujours actif."

### Finding 03: Portfolio construction AI authorization chain documentation structural gap different from advisory recommendation documentation

**Mechanism:** Geneva boutiques using AI in portfolio construction face a multi-step authorization chain: AI generates an allocation recommendation, the investment committee approves it, the PM implements it, results are reported to the client. Current AI governance documentation frameworks address the endpoint (AI-assisted recommendation) or the tool (vendor inventory, data processing agreements). None address the authorization chain as a discrete documentation requirement with its own evidence standard. When the institutional client or carrier asks "can you document the human authorization moments preceding each AI-assisted portfolio decision?", the boutique must reconstruct a sequential authorization record it never built. The documentation gap is specifically in the chaining between AI output and human authorization moments—not the content of the AI recommendation, not the vendor terms, but the specific sequential documentation of what human said yes to what AI output at what point in the mandate cycle. This is distinct from C292 third-gen carrier evidence targeting portfolio construction which named evidence of specific human review without isolating that the structural gap is specifically the chaining documentation between AI output and authorization moment with its own sequential integrity requirement.

**Market signal:** FINMA Guidance 08/2024 expects documented human oversight of AI-assisted decisions. No Geneva methodology exists for building a sequential authorization chain documentation architecture proportionate to boutique investment committee process.

**ICP language:** "We have an investment committee. AI generates ideas. We discuss them. We approve or modify. But I can't document what the AI actually recommended at each step." / "If someone asks me to show the trail from AI output to final decision, I don't have it."

**FR:** "Nous utilisons l'IA dans la construction de portefeuille. L'IA propose, le comité approuve. Mais je ne peux pas prouver la séquence."

### Finding 04: Third-generation carrier evidence requirements targeting AI vendor processor chain as undisclosed third-party compliance obligation

**Mechanism:** Third-generation carrier attestation questionnaires are beginning to ask not just about the boutique's own AI governance but about the AI vendor processor chain: what AI vendors does the boutique use, what data do those vendors process, what are the sub-processor arrangements, what happens to data if the vendor is acquired or Updated or enters insolvency proceedings. These questions probe nFADP Art.9 third-party processor obligations that the boutique's vendor contracts either don't address or address through click-through terms the boutique never read. The carrier is functioning as a de facto nFADP enforcement instrument requiring the boutique to disclose processor chain details the boutique may not have, because the vendor terms create undisclosed processing the boutique accepted without evaluation. Carrier third-generation evidence targeting vendor processor chain = undisclosed third-party nFADP compliance obligation the MD did not know existed when vendor terms were accepted. This is distinct from C293/C292 vendor due diligence naming which addressed vendor terms generally without isolating the specific mechanism where third-generation carrier questions probe processor chain creating nFADP Art.9 disclosure obligations triggered by carrier interrogation that the boutique has been accumulating through years of click-through acceptance.

**Market signal:** Carriers are now asking about vendor sub-processors and data termination protocols—not just internal AI governance policy.

**ICP language:** "The carrier asked about our AI vendors' sub-processors. I didn't know we were supposed to know that." / "What does our vendor do if they're acquired? I have no idea."

### Finding 05: SRO licensing silence on AI creating structural accountability vacuum with no Geneva methodology for resolution

**Mechanism:** Geneva boutique WM firms affiliated with SROs (VQF, FINIA) are in a structurally aggravated accountability position: the SRO is their primary regulatory interface, the SRO has been entirely silent on AI, and the boutique is simultaneously subject to FINMA Guidance expectations, carrier attestation requirements, institutional client questionnaires, and nFADP obligations—all with no guidance from the one regulatory body the boutique interacts with routinely. SRO silence is not passive absence; it is an active structural condition. The absence of SRO AI guidance creates a vacuum where the boutique cannot use its SRO relationship as an accountability reference point for AI governance. When the boutique asks "what does my SRO expect of me on AI?" the answer is silence. When the SRO sends renewal communications, there is no AI content. The SRO is requiring ongoing AML/KYC compliance using AI tools without telling the boutique what AI governance obligations that creates. This is distinct from C287/C288 SRO vacuum naming which identified the structural vacuum without isolating the specific aggravated condition where SRO silence operates simultaneously with FINMA Guidance, carrier attestation, institutional questionnaire, and nFADP all creating obligations the SRO has never acknowledged or contextualized for the boutique's regime.

**Market signal:** VQF and FINIA affiliate communications are entirely devoid of AI content, even as their member firms are adopting AI tools subject to multiple other accountability tracks.

**ICP language:** "Our SRO has said absolutely nothing about AI. We pay them to supervise us and they've never mentioned it." / "If our SRO has no position, what do we tell FINMA if they ask?"

**FR:** "Notre OAR n'a absolutamente rien dit sur l'IA. C'est le silence complet."

### Finding 06: Documentation coherence paradox across multiple simultaneous AI governance accountability mandates

**Mechanism:** Geneva boutique WM firms are simultaneously managing AI governance accountability documentation requirements from four non-integrated sources: (1) FINMA Guidance 08/2024 governance documentation, (2) institutional client AI governance questionnaire response documentation, (3) nFADP ROPA, DPIA, and Art.26 disclosure documentation, and (4) carrier attestation evidence documentation. Each documentation system requires different formatting, different evidence standards, different update cycles, different named responsible persons, and different disclosure scopes. The boutique is producing four independent documentation programs for what is substantively the same AI governance architecture, maintained by different people at different times with no Geneva methodology for cross-document coherence. When a carrier asks for evidence, the boutique produces carrier-standard documentation that may not satisfy the institutional client questionnaire response standard. When an institutional client probes AI methodology, the response references governance documentation the boutique has not connected to carrier evidence. Documentation coherence across multiple simultaneous mandates is unnamed as a discrete service category. This is distinct from C274 three-track parallel documentation naming which addressed the general parallel accountability without isolating the specific documentation coherence paradox where four non-integrated systems are describing the same AI governance reality without cross-reference methodology and producing documentation that may be internally inconsistent despite individually satisfying each accountability track.

**Market signal:** No Geneva service offers cross-document AI governance coherence assessment—where the boutique's FINMA documentation, institutional client response, nFADP records, and carrier evidence are evaluated as an integrated cross-referenced documentation portfolio.

**ICP language:** "We have our FINMA documentation. We have what we sent the client. We have our nFADP ROPA. And we have the carrier form. They don't talk to each other. I'm not sure they're even consistent."

**IMO language for market:** Documentation coherence assessment across multiple accountability mandates as discrete service category.

**ICP language:** "Different documents for different purposes. Probably consistent. Probably. I haven't checked." / "When one updates, the others don't. I try to keep track but it's not systematic."

**FR:** "Nous avons quatre文档 types pour la même architecture IA. Je ne sais pas si ils sont cohérents entre eux."
