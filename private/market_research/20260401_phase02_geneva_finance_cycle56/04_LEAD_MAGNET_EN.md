# The Boutique Wealth Manager's AI Governance Readiness Checklist
**What Geneva's wealth management boutiques need to have in place before the next FINMA review**

---

Your wealth management firm is using AI — for client reporting, portfolio analytics, suitability assessments, or AML screening. Your clients may not know the extent. Your regulators will be asking.

The question is whether your governance framework is ready to answer those questions before you're asked.

---

## The Four-Layer Problem

### Layer 1: FINMA's 2025 AI Guidance

FINMA's updated AI guidance (2025) is operative. For Geneva wealth managers, the key requirements are:

- **Suitability assessments:** AI-assisted suitability determinations must have a documented human review trail. The human reviewer must be identified, must have reviewed the AI output, and must have independently confirmed or overridden the recommendation.
- **Robo-advisory disclosures:** When AI plays a substantive role in investment recommendations, disclosure requirements apply. The client must understand the extent of AI involvement.
- **Portfolio rebalancing accountability:** AI-driven rebalancing recommendations require documented human approval before execution. The AI is a tool; the human is accountable.

If you can't show this documentation for every AI-assisted decision in your practice, a FINMA review will find gaps.

### Layer 2: The Federal AI Ordinance + FADP Dual-Track

Building on the framework from Geneva law firms: the Federal AI Ordinance (in force January 1, 2025) classifies AI systems by risk level. For wealth management, AI tools used in client advisory, portfolio analytics, and reporting are typically "high risk" or "limited risk" depending on the decision weight of their output.

Combined with FADP Art. 21 (human review for automated decisions) and Art. 26 (transparency), this creates a dual-track compliance requirement. The WM firm must:

1. Classify each AI tool by risk level
2. Document human review checkpoints for high-risk AI uses
3. Disclose AI involvement in client-facing communications

### Layer 3: The AML AI + FADP Tension — The Most Acute Operational Conflict

This is the sharpest new finding in Geneva wealth management AI governance.

AML screening tools require maximum data — broad scanning of client communications, transaction patterns, and behavioral signals to detect suspicious activity. FADP requires data minimization — processing only the minimum data necessary for the stated purpose.

These requirements are in direct conflict. A Geneva WM using an AML AI tool is simultaneously:

- Fulfilling its AML obligation to process broadly
- Potentially violating FADP's data minimization principle

The industry has not resolved this cleanly. Your firm needs a documented position: how do you balance AML obligations with FADP constraints? What data does your AML AI actually process? What is your legal basis for that processing? Have you documented this in your FADP compliance records?

Without this documentation, you're exposed in two directions simultaneously.

### Layer 4: CARF/CRS 2026 Implementation

The OECD Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework (CARF) takes effect in 2026, amending CRS reporting requirements for Geneva wealth managers with crypto asset exposure. Using AI tools to generate CARF/CRS reports means the AI tool itself must produce an auditable decision trail under FINMA standards.

The AI is now a regulatory artifact. If you can't demonstrate how the AI reached its reporting conclusions, the reporting itself may be non-compliant.

## Your AI Governance Readiness Checklist

**1. AI Tool Inventory**
Document every AI tool used in your firm: vendor, data processed, decision types, human review status.

**2. Federal AI Ordinance Risk Classification**
Classify each tool: high-risk, limited risk, or minimal risk under the ordinance framework.

**3. FINMA Human Override Documentation**
For suitability assessments, rebalancing recommendations, and robo-advisory outputs: do you have a named human reviewer, a review timestamp, and a documented decision record?

**4. FADP Transparency in Client Communications**
Do your client reports and communications disclose when AI was used in generating the content?

**5. AML AI / FADP Tension Resolution**
Have you documented your legal basis for the data processing your AML AI performs? Have you assessed whether the processing complies with FADP data minimization requirements? Do you have a documented position that a regulator could review?

**6. Robo-Advisory Disclosure Compliance**
For clients receiving AI-assisted investment recommendations: do you have documented disclosure that satisfies FINMA 2025 guidance?

**7. CARF/CRS AI Audit Trail Readiness**
Can your AI tools used in CARF/CRS reporting demonstrate an auditable trail of how conclusions were reached?

**8. Staff Training and Accountability**
Do your advisors and compliance staff understand which AI tools are in use, what they do, and what human oversight is required?

**9. Vendor DPA Coverage**
Do you have executed Data Processing Agreements with every AI vendor that handles client data?

**10. Annual Governance Review**
Is your AI governance framework reviewed annually and updated when new tools are adopted?

**Score:**
- 0–3: Significant gaps — regulatory exposure in multiple directions
- 4–6: Framework developing — AML/FADP tension likely unresolved
- 7–9: Ahead of most boutique WMs — focus on CARF/CRS readiness and documentation completeness
- 10: Robust framework — maintain and monitor regulatory developments

The firms that build this framework first will have:
