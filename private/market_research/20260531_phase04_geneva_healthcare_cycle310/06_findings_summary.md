## Headline Findings

### 1. Retroactive Documentation Gap Is the Primary Market Problem

**C310 NET-NEW finding:** The documentation gap is not forward-looking — it is retrospective. Physicians who deployed AI diagnostic tools in 2023-2024 have no documentation from that period. Carrier attestation forms now ask specific questions about AI use beginning in 2023. The physician has 12–24 months of undocumented AI deployment and no retroactive reconstruction methodology.

**Evidence:** Carrier forms naming individual physicians, asking specific questions about AI use from 2023. Physician reports: "I started using AI diagnostic tools 18 months ago. I didn't document anything at the time. Now carriers are asking specific questions — and I don't have a single record from when I actually started."

**Implication:** Retroactive reconstruction is the primary product need — not forward documentation policy. The methodology for reconstructing undocumented AI deployment is distinct from creating new documentation going forward.

### 2. Clinical Reasoning Accountability Gap Is Distinct From Tool Use Documentation

**C310 NET-NEW finding:** Documenting "I used AI" is not the same as documenting clinical reasoning when AI was involved in a diagnostic decision. Swissmedic inquiries about diagnostic decisions will ask: what was the role of the AI tool? Can you show your clinical reasoning? The medical record documents conclusions, not reasoning chains.

**Evidence:** Physicians articulate: "The AI suggested a diagnosis. I agreed with it. I documented my agreement. But what do I actually document about how much weight I gave the AI's input?"

**Implication:** Clinical reasoning documentation is a distinct product component from administrative AI documentation. It requires a specific format for capturing physician judgment in AI-assisted diagnostic decisions — fast enough for real-time use, specific enough to show reasoning chain.

### 3. nFADP Data Minimization Conflict With AI Tool Design Is Active

**C310 NET-NEW finding:** AI diagnostic tools require comprehensive patient data to function. nFADP Article 6 requires data minimization. These requirements are in direct, operational tension — and no published resolution exists for clinical AI use in Switzerland. This is not theoretical: physicians are deploying AI tools that require full patient history while simultaneously being asked about data minimization compliance by carriers.

**Evidence:** Physicians report: "My AI diagnostic tool explicitly requires full patient history to generate accurate recommendations. But nFADP says I should only collect what's necessary. I don't know how to resolve this."

**Implication:** Data minimization conflict resolution is a specific component of healthcare evidence architecture. The resolution is not "don't use AI" — it is documenting the clinical necessity for comprehensive data processing through AI tools.

### 4. FMH Human Oversight Silence Is Now a Documented Condition

**C310 NET-NEW finding:** Physicians who sought clarification from FMH about what human oversight means in practice received no operational guidance. The principle is published; the protocol is not. This absence is now a documented condition — physicians can demonstrate they sought clarity and received none, and they implemented their own protocol. This is distinct from "no standard exists" — it is "I asked, I was referred back to the principle, I implemented my own."

**Evidence:** Three colleagues at AMGe independently reported: "I wrote to FMH asking specifically what human oversight means in a 3-physician practice. They sent me the principle. Nothing more."

**Implication:** A physician-developed, documented, and implemented human oversight protocol is evidence of active engagement with the FMH requirement. It is not FMH-approved — FMH has not approved any protocol. It is the physician's protocol, demonstrating responsible self-regulation in the absence of official guidance.

### 5. Real-Time Patient Encounter Documentation Gap Is the Operational Core

**C310 NET-NEW finding:** The documentation gap occurs during the patient encounter, not after. Physicians are focused on clinical care during the consultation. Capturing clinical reasoning when AI was involved requires a documentation format that is fast enough to use during a 20-minute encounter, non-disruptive to clinical flow, and specific enough to show physician judgment. Standard EMR systems do not provide this format.

**Evidence:** Physicians report: "During the consultation, I'm thinking clinically, not documenting AI involvement. But if I don't capture it in the moment, it doesn't exist."

**Implication:** Real-time documentation format is the operational core of healthcare evidence architecture. Without this component, the evidence architecture fails at the most critical point: the moment when AI influenced a clinical decision.

### 6. AI Tool Tiering Without a Physician Framework

**C310 NET-NEW finding:** Physicians use 2–4 different AI tool categories simultaneously: administrative (scheduling, billing), communication (patient messaging), documentation (note generation), and diagnostic support (clinical decision support, imaging). Each category creates different accountability exposure — diagnostic AI is highest risk (Swissmedic operator obligations), administrative is lowest. No framework exists for physicians to tier their tools and apply proportionate documentation.

**Evidence:** Physicians report: "I use AI for scheduling, for notes, for diagnostic support, for patient communication. They're all AI. Are they all creating the same exposure?"

**Implication:** AI tool tiering framework is a product component unique to healthcare. The framework allows physicians to apply proportionate documentation effort based on clinical risk of each tool category — preventing both over-documentation of low-risk tools and under-documentation of high-risk diagnostic AI.

**The core frame (C310 confirmed):** "I deployed AI tools in my practice — and now I'm realizing I don't know what I was actually responsible for."

**The six pressures physicians manage:**
1. Retroactive documentation gap — 12–24 months of undocumented AI use now visible to carriers
2. Clinical reasoning accountability — documenting AI's role in diagnostic reasoning, not just tool use
3. nFADP data minimization conflict — AI tool data requirements vs. minimization principle
4. FMH human oversight silence — sought clarity, received principle, implemented own protocol
5. Real-time patient encounter documentation — must capture AI involvement during consultation
6. AI tool tiering without framework — 2–4 tools, different risk categories, same approach

**The evidence language physicians respond to:**
- "Documentation from when you actually started using the AI"
- "Evidence of your clinical reasoning when AI was involved in the diagnosis"
- "Your protocol for maintaining human oversight — designed by you, documented by you"
- "Swissmedic operator classification — separate from vendor certification"
- "A format that captures AI involvement during the patient encounter"
- "Proportionate documentation by tool risk category"

- "I need an AI governance framework" — consultant language
- "I want to understand the regulatory landscape" — abstract, not personal
- "Can you help me with my AI strategy?" — not the immediate concern

**What physicians ARE saying:**
- "I started using AI 18 months ago. I have no documentation. What do I tell my carrier?"
- "How do I document my clinical reasoning when AI suggested the diagnosis?"
- "My AI tool needs full patient data. nFADP says minimize. I don't know how to handle this."

- "During the patient encounter, I'm thinking clinically, not documenting AI — but if I don't capture it in the moment, it doesn't exist."
