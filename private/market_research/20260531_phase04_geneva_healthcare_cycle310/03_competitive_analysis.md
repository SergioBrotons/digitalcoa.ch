## Competitive Analysis Overview

The Geneva healthcare AI accountability market has no dedicated competitor for small practice (2–10 physicians) evidence architecture. Existing competitors serve adjacent problems with wrong-scale, wrong-language, or wrong-delivery-model solutions. The evidence architecture position is unoccupied specifically for healthcare.

---

## Competitive Landscape Map

### Tier 1: Generalist AI Governance Firms (Wrong Language)

**Competitors:** Large consulting firms, international AI governance providers
**What they offer:** AI governance frameworks, compliance programs, policy documents
**Target:** Enterprise, hospitals, large group practices
**Gap for small practice:** Framework language, enterprise pricing, hospital-centric design
**Physician response:** "They gave me a framework. I don't have time to implement a framework. And it's not designed for a 3-physician practice."

### Tier 2: Medical Device Compliance Consultants (Wrong Problem)

**Competitors:** Swissmedic registration consultants, medical device compliance firms
**What they offer:** Medical device registration, CE marking support, regulatory submissions
**Target:** Medical device manufacturers, hospital procurement
**Gap for small practice:** Focus on device registration, not physician operator accountability; wrong problem framing
**Physician response:** "They helped me register my AI tool. They didn't help me document what I was actually doing with it clinically."

### Tier 3: Healthcare IT Vendors (Conflict of Interest)

**Competitors:** AI tool vendors offering "compliance support" with their tools
**What they offer:** Vendor certification, tool-specific compliance documentation
**Target:** Practices using or considering their specific tool
**Gap for small practice:** Vendor is not independent; certification supports vendor's product, not physician's accountability position
**Physician response:** "The vendor says they're compliant. But they're not the ones who'll be answering to Swissmedic if something goes wrong."

### Tier 4: Medical Society Programs (Principles Without Procedure)

**Competitors:** FMH, AMGe, specialty societies offering AI guidance
**What they offer:** Principles, ethical guidelines, position papers
**Target:** All physicians
**Gap for small practice:** Principles without operational protocol; no documentation methodology; no carrier form preparation
**Physician response:** "FMH says human oversight is required. They don't tell me what to document or how. I read everything they've published — it's principles, not protocol."

### Tier 5: Healthcare Lawyers (Wrong Rate, Wrong Problem)

**Competitors:** Geneva healthcare law firms
**What they offer:** Legal opinions on AI liability, regulatory defense, contractual review
**Target:** Hospitals, large practices, medical device companies
**Gap for small practice:** Hourly rates incompatible with small practice budget; problem defined as legal, not operational
**Physician response:** "A lawyer told me I have exposure. He didn't tell me what to document. And I can't afford to pay CHF 400/hour to figure out my documentation protocol."

## The Evidence Architecture Position (Healthcare Specific)

### What "Evidence Architecture" Means for Healthcare

The evidence architecture for healthcare has four components specific to the vertical:

1. **Retroactive reconstruction** — methodology for documenting undocumented AI use from 2023-2024
2. **Clinical reasoning documentation** — format for capturing physician judgment in AI-assisted diagnostic decisions
3. **Real-time encounter documentation** — fast, non-disruptive format for use during patient consultations
4. **AI tool tiering framework** — risk-stratified documentation by tool category

### Why No Competitor Has Built This

The healthcare evidence architecture requires:
- Deep understanding of Swissmedic operator obligations AND nFADP AND FMH requirements
- Clinical workflow knowledge to design non-disruptive real-time documentation
- Carrier form preparation experience to match documentation to specific attestation questions
- Small practice operational experience — enterprise solutions don't translate
- Bilingual EN/FR delivery — Geneva-specific requirement

No single competitor has this combination. Medical device consultants lack clinical workflow knowledge. Healthcare IT vendors have conflicts of interest. FMH provides principles without procedure. Medical societies lack operational focus.

## Competitive Vulnerability Assessment

### Vulnerability 1: Vendor Certification as False Security

**The vulnerability:** AI tool vendors provide "certification" (CE mark, "Swissmedic compliant") that physicians interpret as personal protection. Vendor certification does not protect the physician's license — it only confirms the tool meets certain standards.

**Why it's exploitable:** Physicians trust vendor certification until a Swissmedic inquiry or carrier attestation exposes the gap. The vendor certification pitch is "our tool is compliant." The physician's actual question is "am I compliant as the operator?" These are different questions.

**Market signal:** "My vendor says they're Swissmedic compliant. But Swissmedic doesn't ask the vendor — they ask me. What do I show them?"

### Vulnerability 2: Medical Society Guidance as Inaction Excuse

**The vulnerability:** FMH published principles without operational protocol. Physicians interpret this as "wait for more guidance." The absence of guidance is not protection — it is an accountability gap.

**Why it's exploitable:** Physicians who are waiting for FMH to publish a protocol are accumulating retroactive documentation debt. The moment of action is now — before Swissmedic inquiries or carrier form submissions.

**Market signal:** "I keep waiting for FMH to publish something specific. But they haven't. I'm realizing waiting isn't protecting me."

### Vulnerability 3: Enterprise Solution Non-Transferability

**The vulnerability:** Large consulting firms and enterprise healthcare AI governance solutions are not designed for 2–10 physician practices. The scale, language, and pricing are incompatible.

**Why it's exploitable:** Small practice physicians who have looked at enterprise solutions recognize the gap. They need something designed for their practice size, their budget, and their operational reality.

**Market signal:** "I talked to a firm that helps hospitals with AI governance. They couldn't tell me what to do for a 3-physician practice. The scale is completely different."
