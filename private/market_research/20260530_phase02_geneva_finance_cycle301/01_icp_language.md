# Phase 02 ICP Language — Geneva Finance / Boutique Wealth Management
## Cycle C301 (2026-05-30)

## Addressable Profile
Boutique wealth management firms, 50–200 employees, Geneva-based. Managing director or partner personally accountable. Managing portfolios for UHNW, family offices, and institutional clients. Already using AI tools in portfolio analytics, client reporting, and CRM systems. Receiving institutional AI governance questionnaires they cannot answer. Facing carrier attestation requirements without a framework. Structurally exposed under nFADP Art. 63.

---

## The ICP's Language: How They Talk About AI Problems

### Framing 1: "The Institutional Client's Questionnaire Arrived and I Left Half Blank"
The specific moment of reckoning is receiving a structured AI governance questionnaire from an institutional client and realizing the firm has no documentation to support honest answers.

- "Notre client institutionnel nous a envoyé un questionnaire de gouvernance IA. J'ai dû laisser la moitié des questions sans réponse."
- "The questionnaire asks for AI system inventories, data lineage documentation, model risk management frameworks. We have none of that."
- "I signed off on deploying the AI portfolio tool. Now the client wants to know how we control it. I can't give them an answer."
- "The institutional clients are now making AI governance attestation a condition of continued mandate. It's gatekeeping, not curiosity."
- "They ask: 'Describe your human oversight procedures for AI-influenced portfolio decisions.' We don't have documented procedures."

### Framing 2: "The Carrier Wants My Signature on AI Attestation — Before Swissdamed"
The insurance carrier renewal now includes a personal signature line for AI governance attestation. The managing director signed it without knowing what they were committing to.

- "L'assureur veut ma signature personnelle sur l'attestation de gouvernance IA. Je l'ai signée sans comprendre ce que j'engageais."
- "Our D&O insurer is asking about AI risk management framework, algorithmic bias testing, model override procedures. The form was in English, the standards were undefined, I signed anyway."
- "I heard from a peer that carriers are now pricing AI governance maturity into D&O premiums. Unclassified firms pay a surcharge."
- "The attestation asks me to confirm our AI systems have 'adequate' controls. Adequate compared to what standard?"
- "If Swissdamed activates and I can't show the carrier attestation was based on a real framework — what happens to my coverage?"

### Framing 3: "FINMA Hasn't Told Us What They Want — SRO Hasn't Said Anything"
After 17+ months of FINMA Circular 08/2024, there is still no implementation checklist. The SROs (VQF, FINIA) have been completely silent.

- "FINMA a publié quelque chose sur l'IA en 2024. Après, plus rien. Pas de liste de vérification. On devine."
- "Our SRO has been completely silent on AI governance. That's supposed to be their job — practical guidance for members."
- "If FINMA came to audit us on AI tomorrow, I don't know what they'd expect to see. Nobody does."
- "The guidance we have is either vague or for large institutions. We're a boutique. Neither applies cleanly."
- "We're being held to a standard that hasn't been defined. That's the position."

### Framing 4: "Article 63 Is My Personal Liability — Not the Firm's"
nFADP Art. 63 introduced personal criminal liability for compliance failures involving automated decision-making. The managing director feels this directly.

- "L'Article 63 — c'est ma responsabilité personnelle. Pas seulement celle du cabinet. Si une IA fait une mauvaise recommandation et qu'on ne peut pas l'expliquer, c'est moi que le FDPIC peut poursuivre."
- "The FDPIC can come after me directly, not just the firm. That changes how I think about every AI tool we deploy."
- "I need to be able to say, for every AI decision, who was accountable and why. I don't have that system."
- "If our AI system causes harm and I can't show adequate human oversight — that's a personal criminal exposure, not just a fine."
- "The carrier's D&O coverage may not even cover Art. 63 criminal liability. I signed an attestation that may not protect me."

### Framing 5: "We Deployed Three AI Tools in Twelve Months — No Governance Framework"
AI adoption happened faster than governance. The tools are now embedded in production workflows. Rolling back is not an option.

- "On a déployé trois outils IA cette année — analytique de portfolio, reporting client, scoring CRM. Chacun traite des données clients."
- "The tools are live. We can't undo that. But I now realize the vendor's terms of service transferred risk to us."
- "My team set these up in 2024 without a governance framework. Some of these vendors may have sub-processors we've never evaluated."
- "The CRM AI scoring tool — does it use client data for training? Does that trigger Art. 9 disclosure? I genuinely don't know."
- "We're now discovering that years of click-through acceptance on vendor terms have been accumulating undisclosed obligations."

### Framing 6: "I Am the Compliance Function"
The boutique WM firm has no dedicated compliance team. The managing director IS the compliance function — and has been signing things without review.

- "Je n'ai pas d'équipe compliance. Je suis le compliance officer. Et j'ai signé des attestations sans savoir ce que je signais."
- "A large bank has a compliance team, a legal team, a risk team. I have me and a part-time operations manager."
- "I can't run a gap assessment against four regulatory frameworks simultaneously. I don't have the bandwidth."
- "When Swissdamed and EU AI Act both hit in the same month — I'm supposed to do both at the same time? With what capacity?"
- "I need someone who understands boutique scale, not someone who wants to implement enterprise-grade frameworks I can't operate."

## Emotional State

The managing director is:
- **Consequence-aware**: They already signed something. They already deployed tools. They are living in the downstream consequences, not anticipating future risk.
- **Temporally exposed**: Their 2024 questionnaire responses and AI attestations are being used for decisions in 2026-2027 they can't predict or revise.
- **Personal liability-conscious**: Art. 63 is not abstract to them — it's a personal criminal exposure they don't know how to reduce.
- **Capacity-constrained**: No compliance team, no legal team, no framework. Just themselves and too many undefined standards.
- **Looking for calibration, not education**: They don't need to be told the problem is important. They need to know what to actually do, at their scale, before the next institutional questionnaire or carrier renewal.
- **Responsibility-accepting**: They don't blame the regulator or the carrier. They know the accountability is theirs. This makes them more receptive to someone who can genuinely help — and less receptive to anyone who sounds like a vendor.

## NET-NEW Finding C301-P2-F1

**The institutional client as retrospective accountability witness**: Managing directors in C301 are discovering that institutional clients preserved and indexed their 2024 AI governance questionnaire responses as baseline intelligence. Every future mandate evaluation references the 2024 response as the firm's self-reported starting point. The managing director cannot revise the past; they can only build forward from a documented low-water mark. The institutional client functions as an involuntary accountability witness — someone who has permanent recorded evidence of what the firm claimed versus what the firm built. This is distinct from C300's "temporal asymmetry" framing: C300 named the mechanism; C301 names the specific accountability witness function the institutional client now performs.
