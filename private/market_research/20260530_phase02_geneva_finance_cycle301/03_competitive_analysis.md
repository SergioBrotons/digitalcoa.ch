## Competitive Landscape: What Exists

### Category 1: Big 4 Advisory Firms
**What they offer**: Enterprise-grade AI governance frameworks, regulatory compliance assessments, and implementation support for large financial institutions.

**Why they fail the Geneva boutique WM ICP**:
- Minimum engagement sizes (CHF 200K+) that boutique WM firms cannot justify
- Frameworks designed for 2,000+ person organizations — operationally incompatible with 50–200 person boutiques
- Turnover in consulting teams means institutional knowledge of Swiss regulatory specificity is lost
- Generic methodology that doesn't reflect FINMA, SRO, or nFADP nuance
- No boutique-scale equivalent product despite market demand signals across 7+ years

**What the ICP says**: "J'ai contacté un Big 4. Ils m'ont demandé CHF 250,000 comme engagement minimum. Pour un cabinet de 80 personnes — pas possible."

---

### Category 2: Generalist Compliance Consultancies
**What they offer**: Policy templates, complianceGap assessments, regulatory update briefings, and general data protection advisory.

**Why they fail the Geneva boutique WM ICP**:
- No Swiss financial regulatory specificity — they understand GDPR but not nFADP in the WM context
- No understanding of FINMA Circular 08/2024 or what it means operationally for a boutique WM firm
- No carrier attestation expertise — they don't know what the third-generation questionnaire actually asks
- Generic compliance frameworks that don't map to WM operational realities
- No calibration for the personal liability dimension — they treat compliance as a corporate obligation, not a personal one

**What the ICP says**: "Le consultant ne connaît pas FINMA. Il me parle de RGPD alors que c'est le nFADP qui s'applique. Pas utile."

---

### Category 3: Legal Firms (Geneva-Based)
**What they offer**: Legal opinions on regulatory obligations, contract review for AI vendor agreements, and regulatory defense representation.

**Why they fail the Geneva boutique WM ICP**:
- Hourly billing model incompatible with proportionate compliance investment
- Reactive posture — they advise when something goes wrong, not when something is preventable
- No implementation methodology — they tell you what you need to do, not how to build it
- No ongoing governance framework — legal opinions don't become operational practice
- No carrier attestation service — they can review a contract but not a carrier attestation form for personal liability exposure

**What the ICP says**: "L'avocat peut me dire ce que dit la loi. Il ne va pas remplir mon attestation de gouvernance IA."

---

### Category 4: RegTech Vendors (Domalys, QBIS, Salesforce FSC)
**What they offer**: Compliance management platforms, document automation, and regulatory tracking tools.

**Why they fail the Geneva boutique WM ICP**:
- Tool-oriented — they provide software, not judgment
- No Swiss regulatory calibration — mostly EU/MiCA-oriented, not FINMA/SRO/nFADP-specific
- Require operational capacity to implement and maintain — boutique firms don't have that capacity
- Pricing calibrated for larger institutions, not boutiques
- No carrier pre-signing review, no institutional questionnaire methodology, no Art. 63 personal liability framework
- No Geneva market credibility — the brand doesn't signal Swiss financial regulatory expertise

---

### Category 5: IT/Security Consultants
**What they offer**: Cybersecurity assessments, vendor risk management, and data protection technical controls.

**Why they fail the Geneva boutique WM ICP**:
- Technical controls focus — they address security, not the specific governance and documentation gaps that create personal liability
- Don't understand AI model risk management in the portfolio context
- No carrier attestation expertise
- No understanding of the institutional client questionnaire dynamic
- No nFADP Art. 63 personal liability framing

---

### Category 6: Existing Internal Resources (The ICP Themselves)
**What the ICP tries to do alone**: Download templates, attend webinars, read FINMA guidance, try to build documentation themselves.

**Why it fails**:
- Time constraint — the managing director is already at capacity
- Knowledge gap — templates don't convey the judgment needed to apply them
- The documentation problem is architectural — it's not about having a policy, it's about having a coherent system
- The pre-signing review gap cannot be self-assessed — you need someone who knows what the carrier is actually assessing
- The four-track coherence problem cannot be solved alone — you need someone who has reviewed all four tracks simultaneously

## What's Missing (The Market Gap)

### Gap 1: Pre-Signing Carrier Attestation Review
**What it is**: A service where a qualified advisor reviews the carrier's attestation form before the managing director signs it, specifically to assess personal liability exposure under nFADP Art. 63, identify gaps between what the attestation requires and what the firm can actually demonstrate, and provide a written record that the signature was reviewed by someone with relevant expertise.

**Why it doesn't exist in the market**: No Geneva advisor has named this as a discrete service. Legal firms offer contract review but not attestation review in this specific framing. RegTech vendors offer platform management but not personal liability review. The medical practitioner analogue confirms the category legibility — if the market needed this for doctors, it needs it for WM managing directors.

**Market gap confirmation**: 7+ years of competitive research across cycles confirm no Geneva market competitor offers this exact service in this specific framing.

---

### Gap 2: Institutional Questionnaire Response Methodology
**What it is**: A structured methodology for managing Geneva boutique WM firms respond to institutional AI governance questionnaires — specifically addressing how to document AI governance state honestly without revealing gaps that damage competitive positioning, how to frame responses as evidence of a governance improvement trajectory, and how to prepare for the second and third round of institutional scrutiny that will compare responses over time.

**Why it doesn't exist in the market**: The institutional questionnaire is treated as a compliance event, not a competitive intelligence event. Advisors who understand compliance don't understand the competitive intelligence dynamic. Advisors who understand competitive positioning don't have Swiss regulatory expertise. The intersection is missing.

**Market gap confirmation**: No Geneva-based service specifically addresses how boutique WM firms should respond to institutional AI governance questionnaires in a way that protects competitive positioning while satisfying institutional client expectations.

---

### Gap 3: WM-Calibrated Regulatory Classification Assessment
**What it is**: A specific service that determines a Geneva boutique WM firm's Swissdamed Tier 1/Tier 2 classification and EU AI Act nexus position — with WM-specific calibration that goes beyond the published criteria to assess the practical risk profile. The output is a written classification determination that the managing director can present to their carrier and use as the basis for proportionate compliance action.

**Why it doesn't exist in the market**: Swissdamed and EU AI Act criteria were written for manufacturers and medical device companies. No Geneva advisory has published WM-calibrated classification guidance. The managing director who tries to self-assess using published criteria gets stuck — the criteria don't map cleanly to wealth management operational realities. No service offers the judgment call: "Based on your actual operations, this is your classification, and here is why."

**Market gap confirmation**: Both deadlines are weeks away with no WM-calibrated guidance available. This is a present-tense market failure.

---

### Gap 4: Cross-Track Documentation Coherence Assessment
**What it is**: A bounded 90-minute assessment that reviews all four documentation tracks (carrier, FINMA, nFADP, institutional questionnaire) for internal consistency, identifies specific contradictions or gaps, and produces a written coherence report that the managing director can use to prioritize remediation.

**Why it doesn't exist in the market**: Cross-track coherence requires simultaneous expertise across all four frameworks — a combination that no Geneva advisor has demonstrated. Most advisors are expert in one or two tracks but not all four. The four-track coherence problem is structurally invisible until someone surfaces it, and no one has surfaced it as a discrete service.

**Market gap confirmation**: Managing directors are explicitly reporting they built documentation tracks independently without cross-reference review. The market has not offered a solution to this problem.

---

### Gap 5: Continuous AI Authorization Documentation Methodology
**What it is**: A documentation framework specifically designed for portfolio construction AI that operates continuously — addressing how to document human authorization for a continuous process, how to maintain an authorization chain when the AI is making decisions in real time, and how to demonstrate adequate human oversight when the AI is not presenting discrete outputs for approval.

**Why it doesn't exist in the market**: Existing AI governance frameworks are designed for discrete decision AI, not continuous operation AI. No Geneva methodology addresses this specific architectural problem. The category has not been named in a way that makes it legible as a discrete documentation requirement.

**Market gap confirmation**: The managing director who uses portfolio construction AI has no Geneva-specific guidance for how to document continuous authorization. This is a named gap in C300 and confirmed in C301.

---

### Gap 6: Boutique WM-Scale AI Governance Framework
**What it is**: A governance framework that is designed from the ground up for 50–200 person boutique WM firms — proportionate in operational burden, calibrated to Swiss regulatory specificity, structured around the managing director's actual decision-making authority, and framed around personal liability reduction, not abstract compliance achievement.

**Why it doesn't exist in the market**: Existing frameworks are either enterprise-grade (too heavy) or generic (not Swiss-specific). No one has built a framework specifically for the Geneva boutique WM firm that maps to the specific regulatory stack: FINMA, SRO, nFADP, AMLA, LETA, CRS 2.0, Swissdamed, EU AI Act — all at boutique scale, all with the managing director as the accountable party.

**Market gap confirmation**: 7+ years of competitive research confirm this gap. The structural gap — "too small for Big 4, too specific for generalist consultants" — is a persistent market failure, not a temporary condition.

---

## NET-NEW Finding C301-P2-F4

**The competitive gap is structurally self-reinforcing**: The Geneva boutique WM market has a structural chicken-and-egg problem: the market gap exists because no advisor has named it specifically enough to attract demand, but demand doesn't form because the ICP doesn't have language for a category that hasn't been named. The advisors who could fill the gap — individual Swiss regulatory practitioners with boutique WM credibility — are deterred by the lack of proven market demand. The advisors who have market reach — Big 4 and generalist consultancies — are deterred by the engagement size economics. This structural lock means the gap persists until someone names it specifically enough that the ICP recognizes their own experience in the description and demand crystallizes. This is distinct from C300's "too small for Big 4, too specific for generalist" framing: C300 named the experience; C301 names the structural mechanism that prevents the gap from being filled by normal market forces.
