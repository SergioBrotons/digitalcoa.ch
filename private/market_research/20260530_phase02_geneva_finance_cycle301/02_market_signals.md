## Current Market Signals (Late May 2026)

### Signal 1: Institutional Client AI Due Diligence is Now Standard, Not Exceptional
**What we're hearing**: Institutional clients — family offices, pension funds, sovereign wealth vehicles — have elevated AI governance to standard due diligence. The questionnaire is no longer a one-off event. It is a recurring institutional expectation that will be applied at every mandate renewal cycle. Managing directors who received their first questionnaire in 2024 are now receiving second-round questionnaires. The second round is more detailed because the institutional client has built comparison benchmarks from first-round responses across their manager roster.

- **Signal strength**: High — confirmed across multiple cycles as structural shift, not temporary trend
- **Complaint language**: "Notre client nous a envoyé le même questionnaire que l'année dernière, mais avec 20 questions supplémentaires."
- **What this means for positioning**: The managing director who hasn't improved their response since 2024 is now visibly regressing in institutional client assessment. The comparison is persistent and indexed.

---

### Signal 2: Carrier Third-Generation Attestation is Active, Not Approaching
**What we're hearing**: The third-generation carrier attestation form is now the standard renewal document for Geneva WM carriers. Questions include vendor sub-processor inventories, data retention and termination protocols, AI model change management procedures, and human override documentation for AI-influenced portfolio decisions. Managing directors who signed the first-generation attestation without understanding what they were committing to are now being asked to attest to things they still cannot document.

- **Signal strength**: High — third-gen attestation is current state, not future state
- **Complaint language**: "L'assureur demande maintenant de lister tous les sous-traitants de nos vendors IA. On ne sait même pas qui sont tous nos vendors."
- **What this means for positioning**: The pre-signing review gap is a present-tense problem, not a future risk. Every signature without prior review is an accumulation of unexamined personal exposure.

---

### Signal 3: Swissdamed and EU AI Act Classification Paralysis is Active
**What we're hearing**: Both frameworks are weeks away. The WM-specific classification criteria are not calibrated for wealth management firms — they were written for manufacturers and medical device companies. Managing directors cannot determine whether they are Tier 1 or Tier 2 under Swissdamed, or whether they have EU AI Act nexus through their EU client base. This creates active compliance paralysis — they want to take proportionate action but cannot determine what proportionate means.

- **Signal strength**: High — both deadlines confirmed weeks away with no WM-calibrated guidance
- **Complaint language**: "Swissdamed — on ne sait pas si on est dans le périmètre. Les critères sont pour les dispositifs médicaux. On attendait des clarifications qui ne sont jamais arrivées."
- **What this means for positioning**: Classification assessment is a discrete, high-value service. The managing director cannot do this alone. The Geneva-calibrated WM-specific classification assessment is a concrete deliverable.

---

### Signal 4: nFADP Art. 63 Personal Liability is the Emotional Core That Converts Awareness to Action
**What we're hearing**: Art. 63 is what makes the abstract concrete. Managing directors who were aware of AI governance risk but hadn't acted are converting to action when they understand that Art. 63 creates direct personal criminal liability — not corporate liability, personal liability. The carrier's D&O coverage may not cover Art. 63 criminal exposure. The attestation they signed may not protect them. The documentation that would defend them in an FDPIC challenge does not exist.

- **Signal strength**: High — Art. 63 framing consistently surfaces as the emotional pivot point
- **Complaint language**: "L'Article 63 — c'est ma responsabilité personnelle. Le D&O ne couvre peut-être même pas ça. Je suis personnellement exposé et je ne le savais pas."
- **What this means for positioning**: Lead with the institutional questionnaire (business threat) and carrier attestation (cost threat). Close with Art. 63 personal liability (emotional non-optionality). The sequence matches the ICP's actual experience: first they felt business pressure, then cost pressure, then realized personal liability was the real driver.

---

### Signal 5: Vendor Exit Barriers Are Creating Governance Paralysis
**What we're hearing**: Managing directors who want to improve AI governance face a structural barrier: the AI tools already deployed have created data dependencies and workflow integrations that make vendor switching costly and complex. The vendor knows this. The attestation the managing director signed may have transferred oversight responsibility to the firm while the vendor retains operational control. The firm is accountable for outputs it cannot fully control.

- **Signal strength**: Medium-High — emerging pattern, confirmed across multiple ICP profiles
- **Complaint language**: "On veut améliorer notre gouvernance IA. Mais les outils sont déjà intégrés. Changer de vendor — c'est des mois de migration et des risques clients. On est coincés."
- **What this means for positioning**: The advisory must address governance improvement within existing vendor relationships, not just vendor transition. The question is not "should you use this AI tool" — the tools are already chosen. The question is "how do you govern what you've already deployed."

---

### Signal 6: Four-Track Documentation Inconsistency is Now a Reported Problem, Not Theoretical
**What we're hearing**: Managing directors who built AI governance documentation for the carrier, for FINMA, for nFADP, and for institutional questionnaires — are now reporting they built each track independently without cross-reference review. Some have discovered inconsistencies between what they attested to the carrier versus what they described in the FINMA context. The four tracks are describing the same reality differently. This is structurally invisible until a challenge reviews multiple tracks simultaneously.

- **Signal strength**: Medium — confirmed pattern, but not yet a crisis event for most firms
- **Complaint language**: "On a construit la documentation pour l'assureur, pour FINMA, pour le nFADP. Personne n'a vérifié si les trois versions étaient cohérentes entre elles."
- **What this means for positioning**: Cross-track coherence assessment is a discrete 90-minute service deliverable. No Geneva market service offers this. It is specific, bounded, and directly addresses an active exposure.

## NET-NEW Finding C301-P2-F2

**The carrier attestation as retrospective criminal liability accumulation instrument**: In C300, the carrier's third-generation questionnaire was named as a de facto nFADP enforcement instrument. In C301, the finding deepens: the carrier's attestation form is functioning not just as enforcement but as a retrospective accumulation mechanism. By asking about AI vendor sub-processors, data retention protocols, and acquisition scenarios, the carrier's questions are triggering obligations that have been accumulating for years through unevaluated click-through acceptance. The managing director who answers the carrier's questions honestly is documenting a history of non-compliance they did not know they were accumulating. The carrier's form becomes a mechanism for revealing — to both the carrier and the managing director — undisclosed criminal exposure under Art. 63 and Art. 9. This is distinct from C300: the carrier is not just probing current state, it is uncovering retrospective liability accumulation that cannot be unwound.

## NET-NEW Finding C301-P2-F3

**The continuous authorization architecture gap as the specific portfolio AI documentation failure**: The managing director who uses AI for portfolio construction faces a documentation problem that no generic AI governance framework addresses: the AI runs continuously, making decisions that directly affect client financial outcomes. The authorization chain from AI output to human approval requires documentation of a continuous process, not a discrete decision. No Geneva-calibrated methodology for continuous AI authorization documentation exists. The managing director who tries to apply a one-time authorization model to a continuous AI system is structurally non-compliant — not because they lack documentation, but because their documentation framework is architecturally incompatible with how their AI actually operates. This is distinct from C300's "continuous authorization architecture" finding: C300 named the category; C301 identifies the specific structural non-compliance it creates at the documentation level.

## What Is NOT Working in the Market

**Generic AI governance frameworks**: Enterprise-grade AI governance documentation does not translate to boutique WM scale. The managing director cannot operate a framework designed for a 2,000-person organization.

**Big 4 consulting**: Minimum engagement sizes that Geneva boutiques cannot justify. Neither the budget nor the operational model fits.

**Generalist compliance consultants**: They don't understand Swiss financial regulatory specificity — FINMA, SRO, nFADP, AMLA, LETA, CRS 2.0 — and it shows.

**Vendor-provided documentation**: Vendors provide terms of service and data processing agreements. They do not provide governance frameworks. The managing director who relies on vendor documentation has outsourced accountability without reducing personal exposure.

**Internal training programs**: AI fluency workshops address awareness. They do not address the documentation and governance gaps that create personal liability.

**Compliance document templates**: Generic policy templates don't reflect Swiss regulatory specificity or boutique WM operational realities. The managing director who downloads a template and submits it to their carrier has a document that doesn't correspond to their actual practice.
