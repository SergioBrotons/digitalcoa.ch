# Findings Summary: Geneva Finance — C301 Phase 02

## 6 NET-NEW Findings: Distinct from C214–C300

---

### Finding 01 — Institutional Questionnaire Second-Generation Follow-Up as Active Accountability Event

**The finding:** Institutional clients are not simply sending questionnaires — they are conducting follow-up audits on prior responses. The 2024 responses are now being treated as documented representations requiring evidence support. Managing directors who responded 18 months ago are discovering that the response created an accountability track they didn't anticipate. The institutional client is functioning as a de facto compliance auditor, and the boutique WM firm is not prepared for the follow-up.

**Mechanism:** Initial questionnaire → response (without documentation) → institutional compliance review → follow-up evidence request. The boutique cannot produce pre-response documentation because none was created. The response was a best-effort answer, not a documented decision.

**Implication:** The service is not helping firms complete questionnaires. It is helping firms survive follow-up audits on responses already given. The accountability event has already occurred — the evidence gap is already created.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your institutional client is asking for evidence of what you told them 18 months ago. Do you have the documentation to support it?"

### Finding 02 — Carrier Third-Generation Attestation as nFADP Art.9 Enforcement Instrument

**The finding:** The carrier's third-generation attestation is asking sub-processor chain questions that directly activate nFADP Art.9 obligations. The carrier is functioning as a de facto nFADP enforcement instrument — without being transparent about this role. The managing director signs the attestation believing it is a risk management exercise, while actually making representations that activate undisclosed Art.9 accountability.

**Mechanism:** Carrier attestation → sub-processor disclosure required → managing director discovers vendors with sub-processors in non-Swiss jurisdictions → attestation signed without evaluation of sub-processor compliance → personal liability exposure created through carrier form.

**Implication:** Pre-signing review of carrier attestation is the critical intervention. The managing director needs to understand what the attestation actually requires before signing — and what nFADP Art.9 obligations it may be activating.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your carrier's form is asking about sub-processors you probably haven't evaluated. When you sign it, you're representing compliance you may not have."

---

### Finding 03 — The Four-Track Documentation Inconsistency as Structural Contradiction

**The finding:** FINMA expectations, institutional AI governance questionnaires, nFADP data protection requirements, and carrier AI attestations are four independent documentation systems, each with different evidence standards, different audiences, and different consequences for inconsistency. The boutique WM firm has typically built each track independently — without cross-reference review. The tracks contradict each other. A statement made in an institutional questionnaire may be inconsistent with what the carrier attestation represents.

**Mechanism:** Four independent tracks built at different times for different audiences. No cross-reference review process. The inconsistencies are discovered only when an audit or dispute surfaces them.

**Implication:** A four-track coherence review is a discrete service. The output is not a single document — it is a cross-reference map that identifies contradictions and a resolution protocol.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You have four documentation tracks. They're probably inconsistent with each other. Let me show you where."

---

### Finding 04 — Portfolio Construction AI Authorization Chain Has No Geneva Methodology

**The finding:** Boutique WM firms are using AI in portfolio construction — for data aggregation, scenario modeling, risk analysis, and client reporting. This use is continuous, not discrete. The authorization framework required for continuous AI operation is fundamentally different from the framework for discrete AI tools. No Geneva-specific methodology exists for documenting the authorization chain for continuous AI portfolio tools.

**Mechanism:** AI tools activated for portfolio construction → used continuously across client portfolios → no documented go/no-go decision → no authorization chain → no evidence that human oversight occurs at meaningful intervals. The AI operation is de facto authorized by default.

**Implication:** A portfolio AI authorization framework — specific to boutique WM scale, calibrated to continuous vs. discrete AI distinction — is the missing tool. This is not a policy template. It is a decision architecture.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your portfolio AI is running continuously. When did someone last review what it was doing and decide it should continue?"

---

### Finding 05 — Swissdamed Classification for Boutique WM — The Uncertainty Tax

**The finding:** Swissdamed July 1, 2026 applies specifically to medical devices. For boutique WM firms, the direct Swissdamed obligation is generally not applicable to general AI portfolio tools. However, the classification uncertainty itself creates a cost: firms cannot take proportionate compliance action because they cannot determine whether the obligation applies. The uncertainty tax — the cost of not knowing whether you're covered — is higher than the cost of compliance for firms that know they need to comply.

**Mechanism:** Swissdamed regulation → applies to medical devices → boutique WM uncertain whether AI tools qualify → paralysis → neither compliant nor clearly non-compliant → exposed anyway.

**Implication:** A Swissdamed classification determination is a discrete service for boutique WM. The output is not a compliance framework — it is a clear determination of whether Swissdamed applies, and what to do if it does.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Swissdamed probably doesn't apply to your portfolio AI tools — but you're not sure. Let me determine it definitively."

---

### Finding 06 — The Boutique WM Attestation Debt Has a Specific Personal Liability Structure

**The finding:** The attestation debt for boutique WM managing directors has a specific personal liability structure distinct from other verticals. The carrier attestation requires a personal signature from the managing director. The representation concerns AI governance infrastructure the firm may not have. If a client claim or regulatory inquiry occurs, the signed attestation becomes evidence of a personal representation that cannot be supported. The liability is not entity-level — it is personal and professional.

**Mechanism:** Carrier attestation → personal signature required → managing director signs without documentation of actual governance infrastructure → claim or inquiry occurs → attestation becomes evidence of personal misrepresentation → license and personal assets exposed.

**Implication:** The pre-signing review for WM carrier attestations is the critical intervention. The managing director needs to understand the personal liability exposure before signing — and what documentation is required to support the representations made.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your carrier attestation requires your personal signature. That means your personal assets and license are on the line. Do you know what you're representing?"
