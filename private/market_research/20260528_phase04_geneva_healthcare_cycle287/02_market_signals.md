# Market Signals: Geneva Healthcare AI Accountability
## Phase 04 — Cycle C287

---

## Current Problems and Complaints

### 1. The Silent Accumulation Problem

Geneva physicians using AI tools are experiencing a specific failure mode: AI usage has integrated into daily practice (documentation, scheduling, clinical notes, patient communication) before any governance framework was put in place. The gap is not that they adopted AI without governance — it's that they adopted AI gradually, through vendor updates, through staff introductions, through embedded features in existing software, and now have accumulated liability they cannot reconstruct.

**Key signal:** When asked "do you use AI in your practice?" they answer yes. When asked "when did you adopt AI governance?" they cannot answer. The adoption happened incrementally; the governance never arrived.

### 2. Personal Accountability Without an Architecture

The FMH position is clear: physicians remain personally responsible for all clinical decisions, including those informed by AI. What is not clear: what personal liability actually attaches to when the physician acted on AI-generated information in good faith, under time pressure, with no documentation architecture in place.

**Key signal:** Physicians describe a specific anxiety that is not "I don't want to use AI" but "I'm using AI and I don't know what I would have to show if something went wrong." The accountability question is operational, not philosophical.

### 3. Vendor Risk Transfer as Structural Problem

AI vendors serving Swiss medical practices operate under commercial terms that systematically transfer risk to the physician. Vendor terms of service include liability limitations, data processing authorizations, and indemnification structures that physicians do not review before signing. The physician's signature on the software agreement may constitute acceptance of conditions that conflict with their professional liability obligations under Swiss medical law.

**Key signal:** When asked about vendor contracts, physicians consistently say "I signed the standard agreement" — with the implicit assumption that "standard" means safe. The commercial standard is not designed for physician professional liability protection.

### 4. The Attribution Problem at Legal Threshold

If an AI-assisted clinical decision becomes the subject of a legal proceeding (malpractice claim, disciplinary action, insurance dispute), the physician faces a specific evidentiary problem: AI tools do not produce reasoning chains that survive in a reviewable format. The physician can describe their own decision process but cannot show the AI's contribution to that process in a way that can be audited post-hoc.

**Key signal:** This is distinct from the "AI recommendation vs. physician decision" question. The attribution problem operates even when the physician made the right decision — because the legal question may be about what process was followed, not what outcome was reached.

### 5. Medical Record Integrity Under AI Continuous Learning

AI tools with adaptive or learning components (as opposed to static rule-based systems) create a specific medical records problem: outputs may change over time without the physician's knowledge, creating a situation where the same patient data processed at different times produces different documented conclusions. The medical record no longer represents a stable sequence of decisions but a moving target controlled by vendor algorithm updates.

**Key signal:** Physicians using adaptive AI tools (including AI scribes that learn from physician corrections) do not have a methodology for tracking whether the same input would produce the same output across time. The record is medically authoritative but algorithmically unstable.

### 6. Regulatory Change Without Practitioner Notification

FMH professional guidance on AI is evolving. Swiss regulatory frameworks (nFADP implementation, MedDO classification, AI Convention obligations) are changing. The Swiss institutional environment is producing more AI governance guidance than at any prior point. The problem: independent physicians have no notification system for these changes. They may be compliant at one point in time and non-compliant six months later without any action on their part.

**Key signal:** Physicians who attended FMH briefings in 2024 describe receiving no follow-up communication when the AI Convention was ratified in February 2025 or when Swissmedic updated swissdamed registration timelines. The regulatory environment is moving faster than the communication infrastructure reaches them.

## Problem Language Patterns

**"Je ne savais pas que j'étais déjà en train d'utiliser l'IA"** — The incremental adoption problem. AI arrived through software updates, not decisions.

**"Si quelque chose se passe, qu'est-ce que je peux montrer?"** — The documentation anxiety for accountability. Not about avoiding AI, about surviving a review.

**"Le logiciel dit que c'est conforme, mais je ne sais pas qui a vérifié"** — Vendor compliance claims without physician verification infrastructure.

**"J'ai signé les conditions — est-ce que ça change quelque chose pour ma responsabilité?"** — Contract signature without professional liability awareness.

**"Comment savoir si la prochaine version de l'outil change ce que j'ai documenté?"** — Adaptive AI output stability concern for medical records.

**"Personne ne m'a prévenu — est-ce que j'étais censé savoir?"** — Regulatory change notification gap.
