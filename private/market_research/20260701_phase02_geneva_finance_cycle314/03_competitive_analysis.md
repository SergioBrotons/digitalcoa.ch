# Competitive Analysis: Geneva Finance — C314 Phase 02

## What Exists in Market vs. What Is Missing — EU AI Act 30-Day Window

---

## Overview

The Geneva boutique wealth management (WM) AI governance service market in July 2026 contains exactly four categories of provider. None of them produces the C314 P02 service. The C314 P02 service is the 30-day EU AI Act readiness pack that addresses six specific deliverables — Article 3 deployer applicability memo, Article 4 AI literacy program, Article 14 human oversight documentation, Article 50 transparency protocol, nFADP Art.63 personal liability map, and concurrent jurisdiction reconciliation memo (nFADP + FINMA + EU AI Act + carrier attestation) — for the Geneva boutique WM with EU-domiciled institutional clients.

This document maps what exists, where each provider category fails to deliver the C314 P02 service, and what the gap looks like from the MD's seat.

---

## Provider Category 1 — Big Four Custody Banks and Global Asset Managers (Out-of-Category)

### What exists

The big four custody banks (BNY Mellon, State Street, Citi, JPMorgan) and global asset managers (BlackRock, UBS Asset Management, Pictet) have published EU AI Act readiness frameworks, deployer applicability analyses, and AI literacy programs. These are enterprise-grade frameworks designed for institutions with 500+ compliance staff, dedicated AI governance teams, and multi-year implementation budgets.

### Where they fail for the Geneva boutique WM

- **Scale mismatch.** The frameworks assume enterprise organizational structure — AI steering committee, chief AI officer, dedicated legal team. The Geneva boutique WM has an MD, a compliance officer (often part-time), and a partner who handles AI vendor questions.
- **Documentation language is enterprise-formatted.** The frameworks produce 200-page reports with multi-tier governance diagrams. The institutional EU pension fund's questionnaire expects a 14-page response with named MD attestation. The enterprise document does not produce what the institutional client asks for.
- **Pricing model is enterprise.** The frameworks are bundled into custody, asset management, or audit engagements at six- and seven-figure price points. The Geneva boutique WM cannot afford them and does not need them.
- **They are not for sale as a service to the boutique.** The big four custody banks offer these frameworks to their own clients, not to independent Geneva boutique WMs. The boutique cannot buy what the big four produces internally.
- **The frameworks are not Geneva-specific.** They are designed for EU-domiciled institutions under direct EU AI Act jurisdiction. The Geneva boutique WM operates under nFADP + FINMA + carrier attestation + EU AI Act. The enterprise frameworks do not address FINMA Circular 2026/03 or carrier attestation integration.

### Gap they leave

The big four custody banks and global asset managers do not serve the independent Geneva boutique WM market. They serve their own institutional clients. The boutique WM is structurally outside their addressable market. They produce nothing the boutique can buy.

## Provider Category 2 — Geneva-Based Compliance and Regulatory Consultancies

### What exists

Geneva-based compliance consultancies (Big Four advisory arms, mid-tier Swiss regulatory consultancies, FINMA-exit consultants) offer FINMA compliance reviews, nFADP gap analyses, and operational resilience assessments. Some have started offering AI governance add-ons since 2024.

### Where they fail for the Geneva boutique WM

- **Generic FINMA compliance framing.** The consultancies frame AI governance as an extension of operational risk, modeled on the big four custody bank frameworks. The boutique WM does not have an operational risk department. The framing does not fit.
- **No EU AI Act deployer-specific deliverable.** The consultancies address EU AI Act from the perspective of an EU-domiciled institution. They do not produce an Article 3 deployer applicability memo for a Swiss-domiciled boutique serving EU clients. They do not address Article 3's extraterritorial trigger.
- **No nFADP Art.63 personal liability documentation.** The consultancies address nFADP at the firm level (data controller obligations). They do not produce Art.63 personal-liability documentation naming the MD as the responsible person. The Art.63 personal layer is not their deliverable.
- **No carrier attestation integration.** The consultancies do not coordinate their AI governance deliverables with the carrier attestation. The carrier asks for AI oversight documentation; the consultancy produces it independently; the boutique must reconcile them manually.
- **Long implementation timelines.** The consultancies quote 6–12 month engagements for AI governance implementation, modeled on enterprise ISO/IEC 42001 timelines. The boutique WM has 30 days.
- **Pricing model is engagement-based.** CHF 30,000–100,000+ for a multi-month engagement. The boutique WM's procurement decision is for a single named deliverable in 30 days.

### Gap they leave

The Geneva-based compliance consultancies produce generic AI governance documentation at enterprise pricing and timeline, with no Article 3 deployer-specific deliverable, no Art.63 personal liability layer, and no carrier attestation integration. The boutique WM cannot buy the C314 P02 service from them because the C314 P02 service does not exist in their offering.

## Provider Category 3 — AI Governance Software Vendors

### What exists

AI governance software vendors (OneTrust, TrustArc, Collibra, Securiti, Credo AI) offer AI governance platforms with policy templates, risk assessment modules, and audit log functionality. Some offer EU AI Act-specific modules since 2024. The vendors typically price on annual subscription with per-seat licensing.

### Where they fail for the Geneva boutique WM

- **Platform dependency.** The vendors deliver a platform, not a deliverable. The boutique WM needs a 30-day readiness pack they can send to the EU pension fund, not a platform login that requires implementation.
- **No Article 3 deployer applicability memo template.** The vendors address EU AI Act from the provider perspective (their customers are AI providers, not deployers in the C314 sense). They do not produce a deployer-side applicability memo for a Swiss boutique WM serving EU institutional clients.
- **No nFADP Art.63 personal liability framework.** The vendors address GDPR (EU) and CCPA (California), not nFADP Art.63 personal liability. The Swiss personal-liability layer is not in their template library.
- **No FINMA Circular 2026/03 module.** The vendors do not address FINMA-specific operational resilience requirements. The boutique WM's FINMA supervision is not covered.
- **No carrier attestation integration.** The vendors do not produce documentation that satisfies the carrier's AI oversight questions. The boutique must reconcile the vendor output with the carrier attestation independently.
- **Per-seat pricing.** The vendors price per seat or per active AI use case, typically USD 50,000–200,000+ annually for a boutique-sized deployment. The boutique WM cannot afford the platform and does not need it for a 30-day deliverable.
- **Implementation time.** The vendors require 3–6 months for platform implementation, training, and integration. The boutique WM has 30 days.

### Gap they leave

The AI governance software vendors deliver platforms, not deliverables. The boutique WM needs a deliverable. The vendors produce nothing the boutique can attach to the EU pension fund's readiness questionnaire response.

## Provider Category 4 — Boutique Geneva Law Firms with AI Practices

### What exists

Two or three Geneva boutique law firms have established AI practices offering AI governance advice, contract review for AI vendor agreements, and EU AI Act horizon monitoring. These firms typically have 2–5 attorneys specializing in technology and data protection law. They serve Geneva boutique WM clients on AI vendor contract questions and nFADP compliance reviews.

### Where they fail for the Geneva boutique WM

- **Legal advice framing.** The law firms deliver legal opinions and contract reviews. The boutique WM needs operational documentation (authorization chain records, vendor sub-processor audit memos, Article 4 literacy program templates). Legal advice is not operational documentation.
- **No EU AI Act deployer deliverable for WM specifically.** The law firms address EU AI Act from the legal research and analysis perspective (Article 3 interpretation, Article 6(3) high-risk classification analysis). They do not produce a deployer applicability memo for the specific portfolio construction AI use cases of a Geneva boutique WM.
- **No authorization chain documentation system.** The law firms advise on the need for authorization chain documentation. They do not produce the documentation system itself — the templates, the approval workflows, the dated authorization records.
- **No vendor sub-processor audit methodology.** The law firms review vendor contracts. They do not produce a sub-processor audit methodology the boutique can use to map sub-processors, classify extraterritorial routing, and document the audit for institutional client DDQ responses.
- **No FINMA Circular 2026/03 integration.** The law firms are not FINMA-supervised and do not produce documentation integrating FINMA operational resilience requirements with EU AI Act deployer obligations.
- **Hourly pricing model.** The law firms bill hourly, typically at CHF 400–800/hour. A 30-day readiness pack delivered through hourly billing is economically irrational for the boutique WM.
- **Conflict of interest for peer boutiques.** Geneva WM boutiques often share the same law firms. The boutique MD may be reluctant to use a law firm that also advises peer boutiques, particularly when disclosing peer DDQ gap analysis.

### Gap they leave

The boutique Geneva law firms produce legal advice, not operational documentation. They address EU AI Act interpretation, not deployer applicability memo production. They advise on authorization chain documentation, they do not produce the documentation. The boutique WM cannot buy the C314 P02 service from them.

## Provider Category 5 — Independent AI Governance Consultants (Sole Practitioners)

### What exists

A small number of independent consultants in Geneva and Zurich offer AI governance advisory on hourly or project basis. They typically have backgrounds in data protection, FINMA regulatory affairs, or AI ethics. They offer custom AI governance documentation for SMEs and boutiques.

### Where they fail for the Geneva boutique WM

- **Custom-only delivery.** The consultants produce custom deliverables for each engagement. The boutique WM needs a 30-day readiness pack that can be benchmarked against peer boutiques. Custom delivery is not benchmarkable.
- **No Geneva boutique WM specialization.** The consultants serve a range of clients (legal, healthcare, NGO, financial services). They do not produce deliverables specific to Geneva boutique WM institutional client relationships. The deliverable language does not reference FINMA Circular 2026/03 Section 7.4 or portfolio construction AI specifically.
- **No EU AI Act deployer-specific template library.** The consultants build deliverables from scratch. They do not have a deployer applicability memo template, an Article 4 literacy program template, an Article 14 oversight documentation template, or an Article 50 transparency protocol template ready for delivery.
- **No carrier attestation integration methodology.** The consultants do not produce documentation that satisfies the carrier's AI oversight questions in the attestation language.
- **Pricing model is hourly or project.** Hourly rates of CHF 250–500/hour or project fees of CHF 15,000–50,000 for custom deliverables. The boutique WM's procurement decision is for a named deliverable at a fixed fee.
- **Capacity constraints.** The independent consultants serve a handful of clients simultaneously. They cannot deliver a 30-day readiness pack to multiple Geneva boutique WMs in the August 2026 window. The boutique WM risks timeline failure if the consultant is overcommitted.

### Gap they leave

The independent consultants produce custom deliverables with no deployer-specific templates, no carrier attestation integration, and no capacity for the August 2026 deadline window. The boutique WM cannot reliably buy the C314 P02 service from them.

## What Does Not Exist — The C314 P02 Service Gap

The C314 P02 service is a specific deliverable: a 30-day readiness pack for Geneva boutique WM firms with EU-domiciled institutional clients, addressing six named modules:

1. **Article 3 deployer applicability memo** — which mandates are in scope of EU AI Act
2. **Article 4 AI literacy program** — curriculum, assessment, certification record
3. **Article 14 human oversight documentation** — portfolio construction AI review protocol
4. **Article 50 transparency protocol** — client-facing AI use disclosure language
5. **nFADP Art.63 personal liability map** — MD-named, AI-tool-named coverage
6. **Concurrent jurisdiction reconciliation memo** — nFADP + FINMA + EU AI Act + carrier attestation

The service must be delivered in 30 days, in fixed-fee format, in EN and FR, with named deliverables the boutique MD can attach to the EU institutional client questionnaire response, the carrier attestation, and the FDPIC filing.

**No current provider category produces this service.** The big four custody banks do not serve the boutique market. The compliance consultancies produce enterprise frameworks at multi-month timelines. The AI governance software vendors deliver platforms, not deliverables. The boutique law firms deliver legal advice, not operational documentation. The independent consultants deliver custom work without deployer-specific templates or carrier integration.

The first Geneva boutique WM service to publish the C314 P02 30-day readiness pack — six named modules, fixed-fee, EN+FR, 30-day delivery — owns the category. The first mover publishes the templates. The second mover adapts the templates. The third mover benchmarks against the templates. The first mover sets the language the boutique WM MD uses to describe what they bought.

## What the C314 P02 Service Must Replace in the MD's Current Buying Pattern

Today, the Geneva boutique WM MD purchases AI governance from four different sources simultaneously:

1. **Big four custody bank research** (free, but not actionable for a boutique) — the MD reads the big four's public EU AI Act analysis, recognizes the framework is designed for institutions with 500+ compliance staff.
2. **Geneva compliance consultancy** (CHF 30,000–100,000+ annual engagement) — the MD buys a generic FINMA compliance review with an AI add-on.
3. **Boutique law firm** (hourly billing, CHF 400–800/hour) — the MD buys legal opinions on AI vendor contracts and nFADP compliance questions.
4. **Independent AI consultant** (hourly or project, CHF 15,000–50,000) — the MD buys custom AI governance documentation.

**The MD's total AI governance spend in 2025 was likely CHF 50,000–150,000 across these four sources.** None of the four sources produced the C314 P02 30-day readiness pack. None of the four sources produced the six named modules. None of the four sources addressed the EU AI Act deployer applicability for a Swiss boutique WM serving EU institutional clients.

**The C314 P02 service consolidates the four buying patterns into one named deliverable, at fixed fee, in 30 days.** The MD no longer needs to buy the big four's public analysis and recognize it is not actionable. The MD no longer needs to buy the consultancy's generic FINMA review. The MD no longer needs to buy the law firm's hourly legal opinion. The MD no longer needs to buy the consultant's custom deliverable.

**The C314 P02 service is what the MD's procurement function has been asking for and could not find.** The first mover to publish it owns the procurement pattern.

## First-Mover Advantage Analysis

The C314 P02 service is publishable in 30 days from cycle kickoff because:

- The EU AI Act is published law (Regulation 2024/1689). Article 3, 4, 14, 26, 50 are stable.
- nFADP Art.63 is published law (in force since September 2023). FDPIC guidance is published.
- FINMA Circular 2026/03 Section 7.4 is published.
- The carrier attestation template is in the MD's file drawer.
- The institutional EU AI Act readiness questionnaire language is documented in the public domain.

The first mover to publish the C314 P02 service does not need to invent the regulatory framework. The first mover needs to package the existing framework into a 30-day, fixed-fee, six-module deliverable for the Geneva boutique WM ICP. The first mover needs to name the deliverable in the language the MD uses. The first mover needs to demonstrate the deliverable can be produced in 30 days.

**The first mover's templates become the benchmark.** The second mover's templates are benchmarked against the first mover's. The third mover's templates are dismissed as derivative.

**The first mover's language becomes the procurement language.** When the MD describes what they bought, they describe the C314 P02 service. When the institutional client references what they want, they reference the C314 P02 service. When the carrier broker references what they expect, they reference the C314 P02 service.

**The first mover's pricing becomes the procurement benchmark.** CHF 2,200 for the 90-minute briefing + CHF X for the six-module readiness pack becomes the price the MD expects to pay. Second movers price below or above the benchmark.

**The first mover's documentation becomes the institutional client's reference.** The EU pension fund's procurement team references the first mover's templates when comparing boutique WM responses. Boutiques using the first mover's templates score higher in the institutional client's assessment.

The first-mover advantage is structurally locked in by the 30-day deadline. After August 2026, the EU AI Act is applicable. The boutique WMs that bought the C314 P02 service before August 2026 are documented as compliant. The boutique WMs that did not buy it are documented as non-compliant (or as having purchased a non-C314 P02 service and being insufficiently prepared). The institutional client's procurement team uses this documentation as the basis for mandate retention or transfer decisions.

## What the Second Mover Cannot Replicate

The second mover's C314 P02 service, even if competently delivered, cannot replicate:

- **The template-first position.** The first mover's templates are published and cited. The second mover's templates are adaptations.
- **The procurement-language position.** The first mover's naming is the language the MD uses.
- **The conference-circuit position.** The first mover disclosed at the June 2026 Geneva WM conference circuit. The second mover discloses at subsequent conferences.
- **The peer-benchmark position.** The first mover's named boutique clients are the benchmark. The second mover's named boutique clients are benchmarked against the first mover's.
- **The institutional-client validation position.** The first mover's templates are referenced in EU pension fund procurement documentation. The second mover's templates are not.

The second mover must offer something the first mover does not — for example, deeper FINMA Circular 2026/03 implementation support, or carrier attestation direct integration, or post-deadline remediation services for boutiques that missed the August 2026 window. The second mover's differentiation must be structural, not template-based.
