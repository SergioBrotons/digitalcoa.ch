The questionnaires are time-stamped with response windows of 30–45 days, calibrated to land before the late August 2026 EU AI Act applicability date.

The questionnaires are arriving in formal channels — institutional procurement portals, institutional consultant mailboxes, and direct emails to MDs. The largest volume is from EU pension funds (German, Dutch, Nordic) and EU insurance groups (German, French, Italian).

**Why this matters now:** The EU AI Act is extraterritorial under Article 3. The Geneva boutique WM serving an EU pension fund is a deployer for the AI used on that fund's mandate. The boutique is in scope. The institutional client has read Article 3. The boutique is reading Article 3 for the first time when the questionnaire arrives.

## Signal 02: nFADP Art.63 Personal Liability Activation in Geneva Boutique WM

**What is happening:** The nFADP (new Federal Act on Data Protection, in force since September 2023) Article 63 creates personal liability for the natural person responsible for data processing at a data controller. For a Geneva boutique WM, the responsible person is the MD or managing partner. Art.63 has been in force for 33 months as of July 2026, but the personal-liability activation is now occurring because EU AI Act inquiries are forcing the boutique to name the MD's role explicitly for the first time.

**Documented evidence:** Swiss FDPIC (Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner) guidance issued in Q4 2025 clarified that Art.63 applies to the "responsible person" at the data controller — the person who makes decisions about data processing purposes and means. For a 50–200 employee Geneva boutique WM, this is the MD. The MD's name appears on the FDPIC filing. The MD's name now needs to appear on the AI governance documentation.

**Why this matters now:** The carrier attestation asks for the named data controller. The EU AI Act readiness questionnaire asks for the named deployer. The FDPIC filing names the MD as Art.63 responsible person. Three documents, one name. The name is now visible in three documents that are converging.

**Complaint pattern:** "I am personally named in the carrier attestation, personally named in the FDPIC filing, personally named in the EU AI Act deployer disclosure. My personal liability is now visible across three regulatory regimes. None of these regimes coordinate. I have no document that addresses all three with my name attached."

**Failure mode:** The MD signs the EU AI Act readiness response with personal attestation. The MD is now personally on record for the AI governance claims. If the claims are later found to be inaccurate (e.g., Article 4 literacy program that does not actually exist), the MD faces Art.63 personal liability in addition to carrier attestation personal liability and EU AI Act enforcement exposure. Three independent liability regimes. One signature.

## Signal 03: FINMA Circular 2026/03 Operational Resilience AI Governance Section

**What is happening:** FINMA issued Circular 2026/03 "Operational Risks and Resilience — Banks" in March 2026. The circular includes a new Section 7.4 on AI governance obligations for FINMA-supervised institutions. The section requires documented AI governance for AI systems classified as "material" — defined by usage frequency, decision-impact, and data sensitivity. Portfolio construction AI, mandate compliance AI, and client reporting AI used by Geneva WM boutiques typically meet the materiality threshold.

**Documented evidence:** FINMA Circular 2026/03 was published in the FINMA Official Collection in March 2026. The circular's Section 7.4 references "AI system inventory, deployer accountability, human oversight documentation, and incident response protocol" as baseline requirements. The circular's transition period is 12 months — meaning full compliance is required by March 2027, but documentation must be substantially in place by the end of 2026 for FINMA supervisory review.

**Why this matters now:** The boutique WM is FINMA-supervised (under the Federal Act on Banks and Savings Institutions, if a bank license; under the Financial Institutions Act, FinIA, if a securities firm; under the Collective Investment Schemes Act, CISA, if a fund manager). FINMA supervision includes operational resilience review. A boutique WM using portfolio construction AI is operating in scope of Circular 2026/03 Section 7.4. The MD has not read the circular.

**Complaint pattern:** "FINMA published Circular 2026/03 in March. I learned about it last week. The AI section is 12 pages. I have until end of 2026 to substantially comply. The carrier broker has not mentioned it. The EU AI Act questionnaire has not mentioned it. They are different regimes and they do not reference each other."

**Failure mode:** The boutique is reviewed by FINMA in 2026 supervisory cycle. The reviewer asks for the AI system inventory, deployer accountability documentation, and human oversight protocol. The boutique cannot produce. The boutique receives a FINMA supervisory letter requiring remediation within 90 days. Remediation triggers carrier attestation disclosure. The carrier re-prices the policy. The boutique's relationship with the institutional client is now visible in a FINMA supervisory file.

## Signal 04: Portfolio Construction Vendor Sub-Processor Disclosure Asymmetry

**What is happening:** Geneva boutique WMs that selected "Swiss-hosted" portfolio construction vendors on nFADP Art.9 residency grounds are discovering, when pressed by institutional client DDQ mid-year questionnaires, that the vendor's sub-processor chain (logging, telemetry, authentication, monitoring, parent-company cloud) routes mandate metadata extraterritorially. The residency claim was accurate at the vendor entity level but did not describe the actual data flow.

**Documented evidence:** Mid-year institutional questionnaire language now includes specific sub-processor disclosure questions: "Identify all sub-processors, parent-company infrastructure providers, and cross-border data routing involved in the AI system used on this mandate. Include telemetry, logging, authentication, monitoring, and training data infrastructure." Geneva boutique WMs answering this question for the first time are discovering they cannot answer it accurately.

**Why this matters now:** The C313 mid-year questionnaire cadence is now asking sub-processor questions the C312 annual DDQ did not ask. The boutique that answered the annual DDQ with "we use a Swiss-hosted portfolio construction vendor" now has to disclose what the Swiss-hosted vendor actually routes.

**Complaint pattern:** "My Swiss-hosted portfolio construction vendor just disclosed, in response to my mid-year questionnaire prep, that mandate metadata routes through U.S. parent-company cloud infrastructure for model monitoring. My DDQ response said Swiss-hosted. My mid-year questionnaire response has to say U.S.-routed metadata. The two responses contradict."

**Failure mode:** The institutional client investment committee reads the mid-year questionnaire response, identifies the contradiction with the prior DDQ response, asks for an explanation. The boutique must explain the gap between the marketing claim and the sub-processor reality. The explanation triggers nFADP Art.9 review, FINMA operational resilience review, and possibly EU AI Act data governance review (Article 10) for in-scope mandates. Three reviews from one disclosure.

## Signal 05: Authorization Chain Documentation as Personal Liability Vector

**What is happening:** The boutique WM MD who approves AI tools, sets authorization levels, and supervises portfolio construction AI use is personally accountable under nFADP Art.63, FINMA Circular 2026/03 Section 7.4, and the carrier attestation. The authorization chain — who approved what, when, under what conditions — is the documentary evidence of personal supervision. The Geneva boutique WM authorization chain documentation is typically informal: a Slack message, an email exchange, a verbal approval at a partner meeting. None of these are signed authorization records.

**Documented evidence:** When EU institutional clients ask for the authorization chain documentation supporting the AI tool used on their mandate, the boutique MD cannot produce signed records. The authorization was given informally. The institutional client reads the absence of signed authorization as absence of authorization. The boutique MD is now personally accountable for an authorization that has no documentary trail.

**Why this matters now:** EU AI Act Article 14 (human oversight documentation) requires named, dated, signed human oversight records for high-risk AI use. The carrier attestation asks for "named individuals with AI oversight responsibility." The FDPIC filing names the MD. Three regimes require the same documentary evidence: a signed authorization chain with the MD's name, dated, scoped to specific AI tools and specific mandates. The evidence does not exist.

**Complaint pattern:** "I approved the AI portfolio construction tool at a partner meeting in February. There are no minutes. I have no signed authorization. The institutional client is asking for the authorization record. I cannot produce it. My partner's Slack message says 'approved.' That is not a record."

**Failure mode:** The boutique MD is asked to attest to personal supervision of the AI tool. The MD signs. The signed attestation is later compared to the documentary record. The documentary record shows the authorization was informal. The signed attestation is inaccurate. The MD faces Art.63 personal liability, carrier attestation personal liability, and EU AI Act Article 14 enforcement exposure. Three regimes from one signature.

## Documented Failure Mode Catalogue — Pre-Deadline

The following failure modes are now observable in the Geneva boutique WM market. Each is documented by at least one specific boutique experiencing it. The names are omitted; the patterns are generalizable.

### Failure Mode B — The Swiss-Hosted Vendor Misconception

The mid-year questionnaire asks for sub-processor disclosure. The boutique discloses. The contradiction with the prior DDQ response surfaces. The boutique is asked to explain. The boutique cannot reconcile the marketing claim with the sub-processor reality.

### Failure Mode C — The Informal Authorization

The boutique MD approved a new portfolio construction AI feature at a partner meeting. There are no minutes, no signed authorization, no documentation of the conditions of approval. The EU institutional client asks for the authorization record. The boutique MD signs a backdated authorization to satisfy the request. The backdated authorization is later identified by the carrier in a routine review. The carrier flags the attestation discrepancy. The boutique faces attestation amendment, possible policy cancellation, and Art.63 personal liability exposure.

### Failure Mode D — The Mid-Year Documentation Gap

The boutique's DDQ documentation was refreshed in November 2025. The mid-year questionnaire arrives in June 2026. The boutique's documentation is 7 months old. The institutional client asks "what changed in the last 90 days." The boutique produces no documentation of changes in the last 90 days. The institutional client reads the absence as absence of governance activity. The boutique is grouped with non-active governance boutiques in the institutional client's internal manager assessment.

### Failure Mode E — The Form-Completion Vendor Response

### Failure Mode F — The Single-Regime Blind Spot

The boutique has focused on nFADP compliance. The carrier attestation is signed annually. The EU AI Act readiness questionnaire arrives. The boutique has no EU AI Act documentation. The boutique attempts to retrofit the nFADP documentation for the EU AI Act. The retrofit is incomplete. Article 4, Article 14, and Article 50 obligations are not addressed by nFADP documentation. The retrofit is visible as retrofit. The boutique is asked to produce genuine EU AI Act documentation. The boutique cannot.
