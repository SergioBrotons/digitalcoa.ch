# 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

## Phase 04 C304 | Geneva Healthcare ICP | Independent Physician / Small Group Practice (2–10 physicians)

---

## Purpose
This document captures current market signals — real-world problems, complaints, documented failures, and observable behavior patterns. These are the evidence layer that grounds the ICP language in real market conditions.

---

## Signal 01: Carrier Attestation Scramble Pattern Intensifying

**Signal:** Medical liability carriers are now sending AI governance questionnaires at policy renewal for independent physician practices in Geneva. The forms ask for evidence of AI governance structures, human oversight documentation, and AI tool inventory. Physicians are receiving these forms with 2–4 week response windows.

**Behavioral pattern:** The scramble response is consistent across the ICP:
1. Receive carrier form
2. Search internet for "AI governance policy template"
3. Download vendor-provided template
4. Fill in practice name
5. Sign
6. Submit before deadline

**The failure:** This creates documentation that does not accurately represent actual AI practice. The physician has certified to a carrier that AI governance exists in a form that does not reflect reality. The documentation created under time pressure becomes the evidence base if a challenge occurs.

**Market signal:** No pre-signing review service exists in the Geneva market. Physicians sign personal liability instruments written by carrier legal teams without independent analysis of what they are committing to.

---

## Signal 02: AI Arrived Through System Updates — Physician Accountability Without Governance Decision

**Signal:** Independent practices are discovering AI features active in their EHR systems that were activated through system updates — not through any governance decision by the physician. The physician is now personally accountable for AI usage they did not consciously authorize.

**Behavioral pattern:**
- EHR system update activates AI clinical decision support
- AI features appear in the workflow without physician activation
- Physician begins using AI-assisted records without documentation protocol
- Records bearing physician's name contain AI-generated content physician cannot reconstruct

**The failure:** The documentation debt compounds silently. Each AI-assisted record signed by the physician adds to the retroactive reconstruction burden. The physician's name is on records where the AI's analytical contribution is zero-documented.

**Market signal:** No Geneva-based service exists for retrospective AI contribution reconstruction for physician-signed medical records. The problem is acknowledged in FMH guidance but no operational solution exists.

---

## Signal 03: FMH Guidance Creates Compliance Theater Without Operational Content

**Signal:** FMH physician ethics principles include AI oversight requirements. The guidance states human oversight is required but provides no operational definition of what documented oversight looks like at independent practice scale.

**Behavioral pattern:**
- Physician reads FMH guidance on AI
- Guidance is principle-correct and operationally empty
- Physician documents AI input as notes in the record
- This is called "human oversight" but would not survive carrier or FMH substantive challenge
- Physician believes compliance has been achieved

**The failure:** The documentation created to satisfy FMH oversight is built to a wrong standard. It creates false confidence. The evidence would not survive carrier attestation review or FMH complaint investigation. False adequacy of wrong-standard compliance is more dangerous than no documentation at all.

**Market signal:** A documented human oversight protocol specific to small practice workflows — that satisfies both FMH principle and carrier attestation evidence requirements — is a discrete missing service category.

---

## Signal 04: Swissdamed Classification Determination Paralysis

**Signal:** Swissdamed has active timelines for medical device registration. AI-enabled diagnostic tools used by independent practices may qualify as medical devices. Most independent practices have not conducted a classification determination for the AI tools in use.

**Behavioral pattern:**
- Physician hears "Swissdamed" and "July2026" from colleagues
- Classification criteria (Tier 1/Tier 2) were designed for device manufacturers
- Criteria do not describe independent physician practice operations
- Physician concludes "this doesn't apply to me" without basis for the conclusion
- Alternatively: physician concludes "I need to comply" without pathway to determine what compliance means

**The failure:** Compliance paralysis or compliance assumption without determination. Both are dangerous. The physician either operates potentially illegally without knowing it, or builds compliance documentation to the wrong standard.

**Market signal:** Swissdamed classification determination service calibrated to independent physician practice — that produces a definitive answer, not a framework — is a discrete missing service category.

---

## Signal 05: Patient-Provided AI Content Documentation Void

**Signal:** Patients are arriving at independent practices with consumer AI-generated health analyses — wearable data, symptom checkers, diagnostic AI outputs. The physician must evaluate, possibly disagree with, and document a clinical decision that may diverge from the AI recommendation.

**Behavioral pattern:**
- Patient presents AI-generated analysis
- Physician evaluates with clinical judgment
- Physician may disagree with AI recommendation
- Medical record documents patient AI data and physician decision
- Deliberation moment — where the AI input was evaluated and rejected — is not documented

**The failure:** The documentation gap is most acute precisely when clinical judgment diverged from AI recommendation — the highest-accountability moment. No documentation category exists for "AI-influenced-but-not-AI-determined clinical decision."

**Market signal:** A medical record documentation protocol for patient-provided AI evaluation and physician override is a discrete missing service category that does not exist in the Geneva market.

---

## Signal 06: Multi-Physician Practice Internal Accountability Diffusion

**Signal:** In small group practices (2–10 physicians), each physician independently adopts different AI tools, activates different EHR AI features, and implements different AI-assisted documentation practices without practice-level AI governance coordination.

**Behavioral pattern:**
- Practice principal physician receives carrier attestation
- Attestation requires signature from the principal
- Principal certifies to AI governance for the practice
- Partners are each using different AI tools independently
- Principal has no mechanism to enumerate what each partner uses
- Signature commits principal to governance adequacy for AI usage they cannot audit

**The failure:** Internal accountability diffusion across independent physician decision-makers is a structural problem. The signing physician cannot audit the AI surface of the practice. The documentation gap is created by the independent decision-making authority of each physician.

**Market signal:** A methodology for multi-physician practice internal AI accountability that preserves individual physician autonomy while creating practice-level accountability documentation is a discrete missing service category.

---

## Signal 07: AI-Associated Adverse Outcome Documentation Architecture Absence

**Signal:** When an adverse clinical outcome occurs, existing incident documentation infrastructure has no category for AI system state, AI tool version at time of event, AI recommendation made, or the physician's AI-influenced vs. AI-independent decisions.

**Behavioral pattern:**
- Adverse outcome occurs
- Incident review questions whether AI diagnostic support tool influenced decision
- Existing documentation captures physician decision and patient outcome
- Does not capture: AI tool version active, AI recommendation at time of decision, physician's AI-influenced vs. AI-independent decision components
- Review occurs as if decision was made without AI assistance

**The failure:** The physician's name is on the record. The AI's contribution is invisible to the review process. The physician cannot demonstrate what the AI contributed versus what they independently decided.

**Market signal:** Post-incident AI-associated documentation architecture for independent practice is a discrete missing service category. No Geneva medical body or carrier has published proportionate requirements.

---

## Signal 08: Staff AI Supervision Gap Creating Personal Liability

**Signal:** Junior staff at independent practices are using AI tools — ChatGPT for patient communications, AI-assisted scheduling, AI-generated patient instructions — without documented physician supervision. The physician is personally liable for staff AI usage under FMH supervision obligations.

**Behavioral pattern:**
- Staff member uses AI tool for patient communication
- Patient receives AI-generated instruction with physician's name associated
- Physician did not review or approve the AI output
- Staff member did not document AI use
- No supervision protocol exists

**The failure:** The physician's name is on the patient interaction. The AI output was not reviewed. The supervision gap is unaddressed. The physician's personal liability for staff AI usage is real and unnamed.

**Market signal:** A staff AI supervision protocol that creates documented physician oversight of staff AI use — proportionate to independent practice scale — is a discrete missing service category.

---

## Signal 09: Vendor Contract Article 321 CC Exposure Unnamed

**Signal:** AI vendor contracts for clinical decision support tools, EHR AI modules, and practice management AI are being signed without Article 321 CC professional secrecy review. Patient data flows through AI vendor systems without documented professional secrecy protections.

**Behavioral pattern:**
- Practice adopts AI tool
- Vendor provides standard terms of service
- Physician or practice manager signs without legal review
- Patient data processed through vendor's AI infrastructure
- Professional secrecy obligations under Article 321 CC are not addressed in vendor contract

**The failure:** The physician is accountable for professional secrecy violations even when the violation occurs through a third-party AI vendor. The vendor contract does not satisfy Article 321 CC obligations. The exposure is unnamed and unaddressed.

**Market signal:** Article 321 CC vendor contract review for AI tools — specifically calibrated to independent physician practice scale and fee structure — is a discrete missing service category.

---

## Signal10: The Efficiency-Liability Paradox Creating Structural Immobility

**Signal:** Independent practices are caught between operational necessity (staffing shortage requires AI for efficiency) and liability risk (AI use creates personal license exposure). Neither option is safe.

**Behavioral pattern:**
- Staffing shortage intensifies
- AI adoption becomes operationally necessary
- AI adoption creates liability exposure
- FMH oversight requirement + carrier attestation + nFADP obligations require documentation
- Documentation doesn't exist
- Liability exposed
- Practice cannot move forward on either axis

**The failure:** The paradox is structural. The efficiency need and the liability risk are not separable at independent practice scale. The market offers "AI governance" services that restrict AI use. No service enables AI adoption for efficiency while managing the liability exposure.

**Market signal:** An efficiency-enabling compliance framework — not a restrictive governance service — is the missing offering. The service that resolves the paradox lets the practice use AI without risking the license.
