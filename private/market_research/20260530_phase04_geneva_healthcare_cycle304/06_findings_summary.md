# 06 — Findings Summary: Key Insights and Ready-to-Use Language

## Phase 04 C304 | Geneva Healthcare ICP | Independent Physician / Small Group Practice (2–10 physicians)

---

## Six NET-NEW Findings: Distinct from C214–C303

These findings are specific to this phase rotation and distinct from all prior research in C214–C303. Each finding includes the core insight, the ready-to-use language, and the distinctiveness claim.

---

### Finding 01: The Carrier Attestation Evidence Creation Paradox

**The finding:** Carrier AI attestation forms create a documented standard — not a protection. The physician signs personal attestation to AI governance adequacy. The attestation creates the evidence that will be requested if a challenge occurs. The form is designed for carrier risk evaluation, not physician liability protection. Signing the attestation without pre-signing review means the physician has committed to evidence they do not have, created through documentation that does not reflect their actual practice.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The carrier form asks for evidence of AI governance. You signed it. Now the carrier has a documented standard — and you have nothing to show for it."

**Why this is distinct from C214–C303:** This finding isolates the specific paradox that carrier attestation simultaneously creates the documentation requirement AND makes the documentation gap worse through time-pressure-driven documentation creation. Prior research named carrier attestation pressure and pre-signing review absence. This finding specifically names the evidence creation paradox: the attestation does not produce evidence of governance — it produces evidence of governance commitment that the physician cannot support.

---

### Finding 02: The Solo Practice Dual-Role Architectural Impossibility

**The finding:** In a solo independent practice, the same individual holds the roles of AI user, compliance officer, billing administrator, and supervising physician. FMH human oversight obligations land in a structural void because the oversight requirement requires a separation of roles that does not exist. The physician cannot document human oversight of their own AI use when they are the only human involved in the decision. The FMH standard is architecturally impossible to satisfy without a specific documentation protocol designed for solo practice reality.

**Ready-to-use language:** "FMH says human oversight. It doesn't say what that looks like when you're the only person in the room. I can show you."

**Why this is distinct from C214–C303:** This finding specifically isolates the solo practice dual-role impossibility as a structural problem distinct from the general FMH guidance emptiness. Prior research named FMH guidance as operationally empty. This finding identifies the specific mechanism: the solo practice has no architectural pathway to satisfy the oversight requirement because the requirement was designed for multi-person organizations where the overseer is different from the user. The solo practice needs a documentation protocol that works within the architectural constraint — not an idealized governance structure.

---

### Finding 03: The AI Vendor Contract Article 321 CC Invisible Exposure

**The finding:** Every AI tool adopted by the practice creates a patient data flow through a third-party vendor infrastructure. This is a disclosure under Article 321 CC — physician professional secrecy obligations — regardless of whether the vendor calls the data processing "secure" or "compliant." The vendor contract does not satisfy Article 321 CC obligations because most vendor contracts were not written by physicians and do not address physician professional secrecy specifically. The physician signs vendor contracts without professional secrecy review, unaware that the signature commits them to Article 321 CC obligations the contract does not satisfy.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The AI vendor says your data is secure. What they mean is their infrastructure is secure. Your obligation under Article 321 CC — physician professional secrecy — is a different standard. The vendor contract doesn't address it."

**Why this is distinct from C214–C303:** This finding specifically isolates the Article 321 CC invisible exposure as distinct from general nFADP data protection gaps. Prior research addressed data protection compliance generally. This finding names the specific mechanism: the vendor contract creates a disclosure to a third party (the AI vendor) that triggers Article 321 CC professional secrecy obligations the contract does not satisfy. The physician's personal criminal exposure under Article 321 CC is activated by vendor contracts they sign without review — and the exposure is invisible because no one has named it.

---

### Finding 04: The Retroactive Documentation Reconstruction Structural Impossibility

**The finding:** Medical records bearing the physician's name contain AI-generated content. The physician cannot reconstruct what the AI contributed versus what they independently decided. Retroactive reconstruction of AI contribution to physician-signed medical records is structurally impossible — not practically difficult, structurally impossible. The documentation that would enable reconstruction must be created at time of record creation. The physician's evidentiary position on every AI-assisted record signed in the past 24 months is: "My name is on this record. I cannot explain the AI's contribution to this record." This is not a documentation improvement opportunity. This is a present-tense personal liability exposure.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You signed records with AI-assisted content. You can't reconstruct what the AI contributed versus what you decided. Your name is on the record. That's all that exists."

**Why this is distinct from C214–C303:** This finding specifically isolates the structural impossibility of retroactive reconstruction as distinct from the general medical record AI authorship ambiguity named in prior research. Prior research named the authorship ambiguity as a documentation gap. This finding identifies the specific mechanism: the documentation that would enable reconstruction must be created before the record is signed — after-the-fact reconstruction is structurally impossible regardless of effort or resources. The implication is different: the service is not "improve your documentation going forward" — it is "establish the documentation protocol at time of record creation."

---

### Finding 05: The Swissdamed Classification Determination Active Deadline Problem

**The finding:** Swissdamed medical device registration timelines are active. AI-enabled diagnostic tools used by independent practices may qualify as medical devices under Swissdamed. The classification determination is active for these tools. Most independent practices have not conducted it. The regulatory exposure is real and specific: operating a medical device without Swissdamed registration is non-compliant. The classification criteria were designed for medical device manufacturers, not independent physician practices — creating a framework mismatch that produces compliance paralysis rather than compliance resolution. The physician either concludes "this doesn't apply to me" without basis, or concludes "I need to comply" without pathway to determine what compliance means.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Swissdamed probably doesn't apply to most of your AI tools — but you're not sure. Let me determine it definitively."

**Why this is distinct from C214–C303:** This finding specifically isolates the classification determination service as a discrete bounded offering distinct from general Swissdamed compliance framing. Prior research referenced Swissdamed as a regulatory requirement without isolating the specific service category: a definitive classification determination for the specific AI tools in use, producing a clear answer (applies / does not apply / partially applies with specific obligations), not a compliance framework. The physician needs the answer. The compliance framework is not the deliverable.

---

### Finding 06: The Efficiency-Liability Paradox Structural Resolution Service Gap

**The finding:** Independent practices are caught between operational necessity (staffing shortage requires AI for efficiency) and liability risk (AI use creates personal license exposure). The paradox is structural. The efficiency need and the liability risk are not separable at independent practice scale. Current market offerings are binary: IT consultants who implement AI without addressing liability documentation, or restrictive compliance services that prevent AI use. Neither resolves the paradox. The missing service is an efficiency-enabling compliance framework — one that lets the practice use AI for efficiency while managing the liability exposure through documented evidence architecture. This is not "AI governance." This is "operational enablement with documented protection."

**Ready-to-use language:** "You need AI to survive. You also can't afford the liability. The service you need is one that lets you use AI without risking your license — not one that prevents you from using it."

**Why this is distinct from C214–C303:** This finding specifically isolates the missing third option — efficiency-enabling compliance — as a discrete service category distinct from both "AI implementation" and "restrictive compliance." Prior research named the efficiency-liability paradox as a structural problem. This finding identifies the specific missing service: a framework that enables AI adoption for efficiency while managing liability exposure through documented evidence. The positioning is distinct: not "AI governance" (restrictive framing), not "AI implementation" (operational framing), but "operational enablement with documented license protection" (resolution framing).
