## Part I: The Five Regulatory Deadlines Affecting Your Practice

### 1. Federal Act on Data Protection (FADP) — Art. 21
**Status:** In force since September 1, 2023
**What it means:** If you use AI to make decisions that affect clients — even draft decisions, even preliminary assessments — you must inform clients that automated processing was used. Clients have the right to request human review.

**Practical implication:** Every AI-assisted workflow in your practice that touches a client decision needs a disclosure clause in your engagement letter and a human review protocol.

---

### 2. Federal AI Ordinance
**Status:** In force since January 1, 2025 (15+ months)
**What it means:** The ordinance implements FADP obligations for AI systems and introduces specific requirements for high-risk AI applications. If your AI use falls into a high-risk category under the ordinance, conformity assessment obligations apply.

**Practical implication:** You need to classify your AI use cases by risk level. Not all AI use in a law firm carries the same risk — using AI for internal knowledge management is different from using it to assess client creditworthiness or generate case strategy.

---

### 3. EU AI Act — GPAI Obligations (Already In Force)
**Status:** In force since August 2, 2025
**What it means:** If any part of your practice serves clients or users in the EU — or if the output of your AI-assisted work is used in EU business decisions — the EU AI Act applies to you. GPAI provider and user obligations are already in effect, including transparency requirements and technical documentation obligations.

**Key point:** The EU AI Act does not require a physical presence in the EU. Offering services that are commercially available to EU users — signaled by EUR pricing, EU-directed platforms, or EU client relationships — is sufficient to trigger obligations.

**Practical implication:** Geneva firms with any international practice should conduct an EU AI Act exposure assessment now. This is not a future planning exercise. The obligations are already active.

---

### 4. EU AI Act — High-Risk AI Conformity Assessments
**Status:** Due August 2, 2026 (~4 months)
**What it means:** High-risk AI systems — including AI used in employment decisions, credit assessments, and certain professional services applications — must undergo conformity assessment by August 2, 2026. Legacy GPAI model grandfathering rules expire on the same date, expanding the scope of affected systems.

**Practical implication:** If your firm uses AI in client-facing decision-making processes, you should begin documenting your AI systems, classifying their risk level, and preparing technical documentation now. Four months passes quickly.

---

### 5. US Section 301 — Written Comments Deadline: April 15, 2026
**Status:** Active investigation — 14 days from today
**What it means:** Two Section 301 investigations formally target Switzerland:
- Investigation A: Structural excess capacity in manufacturing (15 countries including Switzerland)
- Investigation B: Failure to prevent goods produced with forced labour (~60 economies affected)

Geneva law firms advising clients on cross-border trade, supply chain compliance, or US-Switzerland commercial relationships should consider submitting written comments to USTR before April 15.

**Practical implication:** This deadline is immediate. If your practice includes US trade advisory work, AI tools used for export controls, customs classification, or supply chain due diligence now carry a documentation requirement for FADP + professional liability exposure purposes.

## Part II: The Five-Question AI Governance Audit

Before you can govern AI, you need to know what AI you are using. Answer these five questions honestly:

**Q1: AI Inventory**
Have we documented which AI tools are used, by whom, and for what specific client tasks? (Not a general policy — a specific, dated inventory.)

**Q2: Client Consent**
Do we have documented client consent protocols for AI-assisted work? This means engagement letters that disclose AI use, or a specific AI consent addendum.

**Q3: Vendor Contracts**
Do our contracts with AI vendors (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Microsoft, or others) include data processing agreements that specify where data is hosted, whether content is used for training, and what data retention policies apply?

**Q4: FADP DPIA**
Have we conducted a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) for our AI-assisted workflows? A DPIA is mandatory when AI processing poses a high risk to the personality or fundamental rights of data subjects.

**Q5: EU AI Act Exposure**
Have we assessed whether the EU AI Act applies to our practice — specifically whether we are "placing AI on the EU market" or whether "AI outputs are being used within the EU" by our clients?

*If you answered "no" or "not yet" to any of these five questions, your firm has an active AI governance gap.*

## Part III: The "Envelope vs. Letter" Framework

Not all AI use in a law firm carries the same risk. The envelope vs. letter framework distinguishes between AI uses that are low-risk and self-contained (envelope) and those that constitute a client-facing output requiring disclosure (letter).

### Safe AI Workflows (Envelope — Low Disclosure Burden)
1. **Internal legal research and knowledge management** — AI assists your lawyers' own analysis. The output is a lawyer's work product, reviewed and authenticated by a human before any client interaction.
2. **Draft review and redlining** — AI reviews a draft and suggests edits. The lawyer reviews, accepts, or rejects. Human in the loop throughout.
3. **Document organization and categorization** — AI organizes discovery documents or contract clauses. Human reviews the organizational output.

### Require Disclosure (Letter — Human Review + Documentation)
4. **Contract template population** — AI generates a first draft from a template with firm-provided parameters. Lawyer reviews entire output before client delivery. Full disclosure in engagement letter.
5. **Due diligence checklists** — AI applies a checklist to a set of documents. Lawyer reviews findings and makes final judgment. Disclosure + documentation required.
6. **Client communication drafts** — AI generates a first draft of a client email or letter. Lawyer edits and approves before sending. Disclosure + human approval trail.

### Do Not Use Without Full Governance Framework
- Fully automated client-facing AI advisory (no human in the loop)
- AI that makes final decisions without human review
- AI processing client data without a data processing agreement
