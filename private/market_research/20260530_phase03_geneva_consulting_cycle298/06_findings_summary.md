# Findings Summary: Geneva Consulting — AI Accountability
## Small Consulting Boutique, 2–50 Employees | Phase 03 C298

---

## 6 NET-NEW Findings (Distinct from C214–C297 Phase 03)

### Finding 1: Proposal Methodology Forensic Gap

The AI-assisted proposal created an unrecoverable methodology state in client files. When Geneva consulting boutiques won work through AI-accelerated proposal delivery in 2023–2024, the speed advantage embedded unrecoverable AI configuration states in deliverables now sitting in client files. The methodology question — which recommendations came from human judgment versus AI inference — cannot be answered for past proposals because the documentation was never created during the window when it could have been. This is not a future risk. It is a present-tense exposure in active client relationships.

**Why distinct from C214–C297:** All prior consulting Phase 03 findings addressed current AI usage governance or future tool adoption. This finding addresses the specific historical forensic problem created by the speed of past AI-assisted proposal delivery — the inability to reconstruct what was done, why, and by whom in past deliverables.

---

### Finding 2: Sub-Consultant AI Chain Three-Party Accountability Accumulation

The Geneva consulting sub-consultant model creates a three-party AI accountability chain where the primary boutique bears accountability for AI-assisted deliverables from firms it does not directly supervise. Sub-consultant AI tool usage is invisible to the primary firm. The accountability flows in one direction — from sub-consultant AI decisions upward to the primary firm — with no contractual, technical, or operational mechanism to manage it. This is the most unmanaged single risk in the Geneva consulting accountability landscape. No Geneva service addresses this chain specifically.

**Why distinct from C214–C297:** Prior Phase 03 sub-consultant findings named the delegation problem as a general governance gap. This finding isolates the specific three-party accumulation mechanism — the invisible AI contribution at the sub-consultant tier creating unreconstructed accountability at the primary firm level — as a discrete structural problem with no existing solution.

---

### Finding 3: Carrier Attestation Iterative Standard Creep Without Guidance Arrival

Carrier AI attestation questions at boutique consulting renewal are iterating faster than any Geneva guidance can arrive. Each renewal cycle introduces new terms — "human oversight protocol," "documented AI governance framework," "sub-consultant AI verification" — without definitions. The attestation signed at cycle N creates representations that become inadequate at cycle N+1 as the attestation language evolves. The boutique is signing to a moving target with no reference point for what "adequate" means and no Geneva professional body publishing definitions. This is not a documentation problem. It is a standard-formation problem happening without the boutique's participation.

**Why distinct from C214–C297:** Prior Phase 03 findings addressed carrier attestation as a compliance event. This finding isolates the specific iterative creep mechanism — the attestation standard moving faster than guidance can arrive, with each cycle's signature creating representations that the next cycle's language supersedes — as a distinct structural exposure.

---

### Finding 4: nFADP Article 9 Processor DPA Architecture Gap for Consulting Deliverables

The nFADP Article 9 processor obligation applies to consulting deliverables for the first time in a structurally distinct way. Client data processed through AI tools during a consulting engagement triggers DPA requirements that most AI vendors cannot satisfy with compliant agreements. The consulting-specific complexity is that client data in a consulting engagement may include strategic, financial, and operational information with higher sensitivity than generic business data — and the DPA architecture required to protect it does not exist in any vendor offering. Geneva consulting boutiques are processing high-sensitivity client data through AI tools with no DPA in place. The personal criminal exposure under Article 63 (up to CHF 250,000) applies to intentional violations, and intentionality can be argued from deliberate tool use without DPA verification.

**Why distinct from C214–C297:** Prior Phase 03 nFADP findings addressed general data protection compliance. This finding isolates the specific consulting deliverable context — the intersection of high-sensitivity client data, AI tool processing, and unavailable compliant DPA architecture — as a discrete acute exposure with personal criminal liability implications.

---

### Finding 5: Geneva Consulting Peer Accountability Circuit Absence as Structural Market Failure

The Geneva consulting peer accountability circuit operates differently from Geneva legal (SVA/ORDRE) and Geneva finance (SRO) peer circuits because no institutional substrate exists for consulting principals. The informal accountability discussions that happen in Geneva consulting peer networks produce awareness without action, shared anxiety without resolution, and mutual recognition of exposure without any mechanism to address it collectively. This is a structural market failure, not a knowledge gap. The peer circuit is present but has no formal architecture to convert shared recognition into accountable action. The legal and finance communities have institutional substrates (bar association guidance, SRO frameworks) that provide both accountability standards and peer discussion forums. Consulting has neither.

**Why distinct from C214–C297:** Prior Phase 03 peer circuit findings addressed the Geneva informal accountability circuit as a general phenomenon. This finding isolates the specific structural absence in consulting versus law and finance — the absence of institutional substrate, not just informal circuit — and names it as a discrete market failure creating compounding accountability exposure.

---

### Finding 6: AI Tool Configuration Lock-In as Unnamed Deliverable Ownership Problem

Geneva consulting boutiques have embedded AI tool configurations into their deliverable methodology in ways that create structural exit costs and ownership ambiguity. The AI tool configuration is not just a productivity choice — it has become part of the institutional deliverable methodology in a way that cannot be cleanly separated. If the AI vendor changes its model, raises prices, or discontinues service, the boutique's methodology does not simply adjust — it requires reconstruction. Deliverables already in client files reflect configurations that may no longer exist. The boutique owns deliverables it cannot independently reproduce without the original AI tool configuration. This is an unnamed problem at the intersection of methodology ownership, deliverable defensibility, and business continuity.

**Why distinct from C214–C297:** Prior Phase 03 findings addressed AI tool selection and vendor management. This finding isolates the specific deliverable ownership problem — the embedded AI configuration in past deliverables creating structural exit costs and reproduction ambiguity — as a discrete unnamed problem with deliverable defensibility and business continuity implications.

## Summary

Geneva consulting boutiques face a compound accountability problem with no institutional substrate, no proportionate framework, and no Geneva-specific guidance. The six gaps — proposal methodology forensic gap, three-party sub-consultant chain, carrier attestation iterative creep, nFADP Article 9 DPA architecture, peer accountability circuit absence, and AI tool configuration lock-in — are structurally distinct and collectively create an accountability exposure that is specific to this ICP, this city, and this moment.

The market gap is clear: no player serves the small Geneva consulting boutique AI accountability problem with a proportionate, Geneva-specific, implementation-ready solution. This is the specific opening.
