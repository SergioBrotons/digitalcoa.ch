# Compliance Context: Geneva Consulting — AI Accountability
## Small Consulting Boutique, 2–50 Employees | Phase 03 C298

---

## Applicable Regulatory Framework

### 1. nFADP (Revised Federal Act on Data Protection) — In Force September 2023

**Article 9 — Processor Obligations**

When a Geneva consulting boutique uses an AI tool to process client data, the boutique acts as the data controller and the AI vendor as the processor. This triggers Article 9 DPA requirements:

- Written DPA with AI vendor required before processing
- DPA must specify processing scope, purpose, security measures, and sub-processing authorizations
- Vendor breach = boutique's direct liability to clients
- **Key gap:** Most AI vendors do not offer nFADP-compliant DPAs. Most boutiques have not requested them.

**Article 63 — Personal Criminal Liability**

Intentional data protection violations under nFADP carry personal fines up to CHF 250,000. For a consulting boutique principal, this is personal liability — not firm liability. The intentional element can be satisfied by deliberate use of an AI tool without verifying DPA compliance.

**Article 13 — Data Subject Rights in Consulting Context**

When client data is processed through AI tools, clients (as data subjects) have rights to explanation, access, and correction. The AI tool's output — a recommendation, analysis, or deliverable — may itself constitute processing requiring explanation architecture.

### 2. Swiss Professional Codes — OR Articles 321a/321b

The Swiss Code of Obligations Articles 321a–321b establish employee and employer obligations in professional relationships. In consulting practice:

- Employee use of AI tools without disclosure to employer creates unauthorized delegation
- Principal is accountable for employee AI use regardless of authorization
- No published Geneva guidance maps these provisions to AI-assisted consulting practice

### 3. Carrier Professional Liability Insurance — AI Attestation

Swiss PI insurers for consulting professionals have introduced AI-specific questions at renewal. The attestation language references "adequate AI governance" without defining adequacy. The attestation creates a contractual representation with direct policy coverage implications:

- Attestation submitted without governance = potential misrepresentation
- Coverage defense may turn on whether governance was "adequate" at time of attestation
- No published definition of "adequate" for boutique consulting exists

### 4. Client AI Governance Questionnaires — Institutional Pressure

Geneva institutional clients (corporates, NGOs, public institutions) are circulating AI governance questionnaires to their consulting vendors. These questionnaires:

- Ask for documented AI methodology
- Probe sub-consultant AI governance
- Request AI tool inventory
- Require representation of human oversight

Responding without documented governance creates misrepresentation exposure. Not responding may result in disqualification from client engagements. No proportionate response template exists for boutique consulting.

### 5. EU AI Act — Indirect Application

Geneva consulting boutiques serving EU-based clients or processing EU data subjects may encounter EU AI Act obligations indirectly:

- High-risk AI system use in client engagements may trigger EU AI Act compliance requirements
- EU institutional clients may require EU AI Act compliance representation from vendors
- No Geneva advisory offers EU AI Act gap analysis for consulting boutiques

### 6. Geneva Cantonal Professional Context

Geneva consulting practice operates without the formal cantonal regulatory substrate that exists for law (SVA/ORDRE) and finance (FINMA/SRO). This creates:

- No Geneva-specific AI governance guidance for consulting
- No peer accountability forum for consulting principals
- No channel for collective voice on AI accountability questions
- No institutional substrate for standards formation

## The Unique Geneva Cantonal Challenge for Consulting

Geneva consulting boutiques face AI accountability obligations from multiple frameworks simultaneously — Swiss data protection, carrier insurance, institutional client requirements, and potentially EU AI Act — with no Geneva professional body providing consulting-specific guidance or a peer accountability forum. This is structurally different from Geneva law firms (SVA/ORDRE provide guidance) and Geneva wealth managers (SRO framework exists). The consulting boutique is the only major Geneva professional services segment without an institutional AI governance substrate.
