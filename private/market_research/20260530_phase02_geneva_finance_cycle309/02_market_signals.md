# Market Signals: Geneva Finance — C309 Phase 02

## Current Problems, Complaints, Failures — C309 Phase 02

## Signal 01: Institutional DDQ as Competitive Gate — DDQ Failure Is a Revenue Event

**Signal:** Geneva boutique wealth management firms are discovering that institutional clients — family offices, institutional funds, international HNW clients — are sending AI governance DDQs as part of their manager selection and retention process. The DDQ is not a compliance checklist. It is a competitive gate. Firms that cannot answer the DDQ lose mandates. The institutional client is now setting AI governance standards that exceed what FINMA requires. The DDQ is more detailed than the FINMA December 2024 AI guidance in specific respects. The institutional client is more sophisticated about AI governance than the typical Swiss regulator. The C309 Phase 02 ICP is discovering that DDQ failure is a mandate retention crisis, not a compliance gap.

**Market signal:** Managing directors reporting that institutional clients have sent AI governance DDQs they cannot answer, and that the inability to answer is being treated as a reason to review the manager relationship. The DDQ is the forcing function for AI governance action in a way that FINMA guidance has not been.

**Commercial angle:** "The institutional DDQ is the document that determines whether you keep the mandate. I read institutional AI governance DDQs — I'll show you what they actually want and how to answer it."

## Signal 02: nFADP Article 63 Personal Liability for Investment Decisions — The Managing Director Exposure

**Signal:** The Geneva WM managing director has discovered that nFADP Article 63 creates personal liability for AI-influenced investment decisions. Article 63 of the Swiss Data Protection Act establishes that those responsible for data processing bear personal liability for compliance failures. For a WM firm using AI in portfolio decisions, the managing director is personally responsible for ensuring that AI systems processing client data comply with nFADP. This is distinct from FINMA AI guidance — it is a personal liability exposure under Swiss data protection law that the managing director has not previously identified. The exposure is active, not theoretical. The managing director who signs off on AI-assisted investment decisions without documented human oversight is personally exposed under Article 63.

**Market signal:** Managing directors beginning to surface the Article 63 question: "I didn't know this applied to me personally." The question is surfacing through internal counsel conversations, not through regulatory guidance. The Geneva WM managing director is discovering that Swiss data protection law creates personal liability for AI-influenced investment decisions that they have not addressed.

**Commercial angle:** "Article 63 creates personal liability for you — the managing director — for AI decisions affecting client data. I map the exposure and build the documentation that makes your oversight defensible."

## Signal 03: Human Oversight Operationalization Gap — "We Don't Know What 'Human Oversight' Means in Practice"

**Signal:** FINMA's December 2024 AI guidance requires "human oversight" of AI systems in supervised entities. The Geneva WM firm acknowledges this requirement but does not know what it means operationally. Is human oversight a formal sign-off on every AI-influenced investment recommendation? Is it a periodic review? Is it a documented conversation? The guidance does not specify. The C309 Phase 02 ICP is asking: "What does human oversight actually mean for our portfolio construction process?" The gap is not policy — the firm knows human oversight is required. The gap is operational: how to document human oversight in a way that satisfies FINMA if asked, and that the managing director can actually implement given their AI competence limitations.

**Market signal:** WM firms reporting that they have a policy requiring human review of AI recommendations but no documented procedure for what that review looks like, who performs it, and how it is recorded. The human oversight operationalization gap is a documentation architecture problem, not a policy problem.

**Commercial angle:** "Human oversight is required but undefined. I'll show you what it looks like operationally — who reviews what, how it is documented, and how it survives scrutiny from FINMA or an institutional client DDQ."

## Signal 04: Vendor AI Governance Contract Gap — "We Signed the Contract, We Don't Know What It Says About AI"

**Signal:** Geneva WM firms are using AI systems from portfolio management vendors, risk analysis providers, and performance attribution platforms — with vendor contracts that do not adequately address AI governance obligations. The typical WM vendor contract was negotiated for technology services, not for AI accountability. The contracts do not specify: (1) what client data the AI system processes, (2) where the data is stored, (3) what happens to data when the contract ends, (4) whether the vendor will notify the WM firm of AI model changes, (5) whether the vendor's AI output can be compelled in litigation, and (6) who bears liability if the AI produces a bad investment recommendation. The vendor AI governance contract gap is a specific exposure that the WM firm has not addressed because no Geneva service has framed it as a discrete problem.

**Market signal:** Managing directors reporting that they signed vendor contracts without reviewing the AI governance terms, and that they do not have a basis to assess vendor AI compliance with nFADP or FINMA requirements. The question of vendor liability for AI-influenced investment decisions is surfacing without resolution.

**Commercial angle:** "Your vendor contracts were not negotiated for AI governance. I'll review them against your nFADP and FINMA obligations and tell you what is missing and what it costs you."

## Signal 05: Portfolio AI Shadow Systems — "The Associate Set Up an AI Tool We Didn't Evaluate"

**Signal:** Geneva WM firms are running AI tools in investment operations that were implemented by associates without full managing director evaluation. The portfolio construction AI, the risk analysis AI, the performance attribution AI — these systems are processing client data, influencing investment decisions, and generating outputs that go to clients — without a documented AI inventory, without a nFADP DPIA, and without managing director authorization. The associate who implemented the system is more AI-fluent than the managing director. The managing director does not know what AI systems are processing client data, what those systems do with the data, or what their outputs look like. This is the same shadow AI problem as the legal ICP, but with direct fiduciary consequences for investment decisions.

**Market signal:** Managing directors beginning to ask: "What AI are we actually running?" The question is surfacing because institutional DDQs are asking for an AI inventory that the managing director cannot produce. The AI inventory gap is a direct consequence of associate-implemented systems without managing director oversight.

**Commercial angle:** "You don't know what AI systems are processing your client data. I'll conduct the AI inventory and show you what you are running, what it does with the data, and what your obligations are for each system."

## Signal 06: The FINMA AI Guidance Gap — "Adequate Is Not Defined"

**Signal:** FINMA's December 2024 AI guidance is principles-based. It establishes that supervised entities should have AI governance, risk management, transparency, and non-discrimination frameworks. It does not specify what "adequate" means in practice for a boutique WM firm. The C309 Phase 02 ICP is asking: "What does adequate AI governance look like for a 50-person Geneva wealth manager?" The guidance does not answer. The ICP is left without an operational standard. The Geneva WM firm wants to comply with FINMA expectations but cannot determine what compliance looks like at their scale and complexity. The "adequate" gap is a service opportunity — an operational definition of FINMA AI governance adequacy for the boutique WM scale.

**Market signal:** WM firms reporting that they have read the FINMA guidance, they understand the principles, and they do not know what to do next. The guidance gap is not awareness — it is operationalization. The firm knows what FINMA expects in principle. The firm does not know what to build in practice.

**Commercial angle:** "FINMA's guidance is principles-based. I'll translate it into what 'adequate' means for your firm — at your scale, with your AI systems, for your institutional clients. The output is a FINMA-adequate AI governance framework that fits your boutique scale."
