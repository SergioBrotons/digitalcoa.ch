# ICP Language: Geneva Finance — C309 Phase 02

## How the ICP Talks About AI Problems — C309 Phase 02

---

## Primary Language Register

The Geneva finance ICP at C309 Phase 02 speaks from a distinct position from the legal ICP: they manage institutional client relationships where the institutional DDQ (Due Diligence Questionnaire) has become the operative accountability document. The wealth manager ICP is not primarily worried about what their carrier asks — they are worried about what their institutional clients ask. The DDQ is the document that determines whether mandates are retained or lost. The C309 Phase 02 ICP has discovered that the institutional client is asking AI governance questions the firm cannot answer — and that the inability to answer is a competitive gate, not just a compliance gap.

This is the decisive language shift for Phase 02: the DDQ as competitive gate, not just regulatory obligation.

---

## The "Institutional Client Is Asking" Language (What Triggered Action)

The C309 Phase 02 ICP describes their AI governance awakening in terms of institutional client pressure:

- **"A major client sent us their AI governance DDQ"** — the institutional DDQ as the document that surfaced the problem
- **"We couldn't answer half the questions"** — DDQ failure as mandate retention crisis
- **"The client has more sophisticated expectations than our compliance team"** — institutional client as the forcing function
- **"We thought we were fine — the DDQ said otherwise"** — assumption of adequacy disrupted by institutional review
- **"The DDQ asked about things we hadn't even thought about"** — discovery of governance gap through client process
- **"Our biggest client is basically auditing our AI governance"** — institutional DDQ as de facto regulatory inspection

The language reveals that institutional clients are driving AI governance urgency more effectively than Swiss regulators. The DDQ is the operative document.

---

## The DDQ Language (What They Received)

The C309 Phase 02 ICP describes the institutional AI governance DDQ in specific terms:

- **"They want evidence of human oversight of AI portfolio decisions"** — the human-in-the-loop question for AI-assisted investment decisions
- **"They asked about our AI vendor contracts and data processing agreements"** — vendor governance as institutional concern
- **"They wanted to know which AI systems process client data and how"** — AI inventory as institutional requirement
- **"They asked about nFADP compliance for our AI systems"** — data protection as institutional DDQ topic
- **"They wanted documentation of who is accountable for AI-influenced investment decisions"** — personal accountability mapping as institutional question
- **"The DDQ was more detailed than FINMA's guidance"** — institutional standards exceeding regulatory baseline

The language reveals that institutional AI governance DDQs are now more sophisticated than FINMA's December 2024 guidance in specific respects. The institutional client is setting a higher standard than the Swiss regulator.

---

## The nFADP Article 63 Language (What They Now Fear)

The C309 Phase 02 ICP has discovered a specific personal liability exposure:

- **"Article 63 — I didn't know that applied to me personally"** — nFADP personal liability for managing director
- **"If the AI recommends a portfolio that underperforms, am I personally liable?"** — AI-influenced investment decision as personal liability trigger
- **"The managing director is responsible for AI decisions under Swiss data protection law"** — Article 63 as personal accountability exposure
- **"I signed off on AI-assisted recommendations — I didn't know I was personally exposed"** — managing director personal liability now active
- **"Our general counsel said Article 63 creates personal liability — we haven't addressed it"** — internal legal recognition of the exposure
- **"This isn't just a compliance question — it's a personal liability question for me"** — nFADP Art.63 as distinct from FINMA AI guidance

The nFADP Article 63 language is NET-NEW for Phase 02. The legal ICP (Phase 01) worried about carrier attestation and litigation disclosure. The finance ICP worries about personal liability under Swiss data protection law for AI-influenced investment decisions.

---

## The Portfolio AI Language (What They're Using)

The C309 Phase 02 ICP describes their AI use in investment-specific terms:

- **"We use AI for portfolio construction and rebalancing"** — algorithmic portfolio management as primary AI use
- **"Risk analysis AI — we rely on it heavily"** — AI for risk assessment and modeling
- **"Client reporting is AI-assisted now"** — AI in client communication and reporting
- **"Performance attribution — the AI does it faster than we could"** — AI for investment performance analysis
- **"We have AI tools we didn't fully evaluate before implementing"** — shadow AI in investment operations
- **"The associate set up the AI — I don't know exactly what it does"** — managing director AI competence gap in WM context

The portfolio AI language reveals that Geneva WM firms are using AI for core investment functions — portfolio construction, risk analysis, performance attribution — with the same competence gap problem as the legal ICP but with direct investment performance and fiduciary consequences.

---

## The FINMA Language (What They Cite)

The C309 Phase 02 ICP references FINMA in specific ways:

- **"FINMA's December2024 AI guidance — we read it, we didn't know what to do with it"** — FINMA AI guidance as baseline acknowledged but not operationalized
- **"FINMA expects us to have governance for AI systems"** — board-level AI governance expectation from FINMA
- **"We are a FINMA-supervised entity — this applies to us"** — FINMA supervision as the regulatory frame
- **"FINMA hasn't told us specifically what they want"** — FINMA guidance gap as operational obstacle
- **"The FINMA guidance is principles-based — I don't know what adequate means in practice"** — "adequate" undefined against FINMA principles

The FINMA language reveals that the Phase 02 ICP acknowledges the FINMA AI guidance baseline but lacks operational guidance on what "adequate" means in practice. The principles-based nature of the guidance creates the same "adequate" problem the legal ICP faces with OFL silence.

## The Institutional Gate Language (What Is at Stake)

The C309 Phase 02 ICP frames AI governance in competitive terms:

- **"If we can't answer the DDQ, we lose the mandate"** — DDQ as competitive gate, not compliance checkbox
- **"Our competitors who can answer the DDQ will take the business"** — competitive advantage for AI governance-prepared firms
- **"The institutional client is smarter about AI governance than we are"** — humbling realization about client sophistication
- **"We are at risk of losing institutional mandates because of AI governance gaps"** — mandate retention as the stakes
- **"This isn't just regulatory — it's a client relationship and revenue problem"** — commercial consequences of DDQ failure
- **"The DDQ failure is a competitive gate — pass it or lose the business"** — institutional DDQ as binary outcome

The institutional gate language is the decisive commercial frame for Phase 02. The legal ICP worried about carrier renewal and personal liability. The finance ICP worries about institutional mandate retention — a direct revenue consequence.

## The Vendor Technology Language (What They Haven't Resolved)

The C309 Phase 02 ICP describes AI vendor relationships in investment-specific terms:

- **"Our portfolio management system vendor uses AI — we don't control what the AI does"** — vendor AI as unmanaged dependency
- **"The AI risk tool is a third-party system — where does the data go?"** — data residency for investment AI systems
- **"If the AI makes a bad recommendation, who is liable — us or the vendor?"** — vendor liability question for AI-influenced investment decisions
- **"We signed the vendor contract — I don't think we negotiated the AI governance terms"** — vendor contract AI governance gap
- **"The vendor says their AI is compliant — we have no way to verify"** — reliance on vendor compliance claims without verification

The vendor technology language reveals that WM firms are deeply dependent on AI systems they did not fully evaluate, with vendor contracts that do not adequately address AI governance obligations, and with unclear liability allocation for AI-influenced investment decisions.

## The Human Oversight Language (What They Need)

The C309 Phase 02 ICP describes what they need in fiduciary terms:

- **"We need to document that a human actually reviewed the AI recommendation"** — human oversight documentation for investment decisions
- **"The FINMA guidance says human oversight — we don't know what that means in practice"** — human oversight operationalization gap
- **"I need to be able to show that a qualified person reviewed the AI output before it went to the client"** — investment decision review documentation
- **"The human oversight requirement — is that a formal sign-off or just a conversation?"** — human oversight standard undefined
- **"We use AI for things where the client expects a human decision — we need to document that human review happened"** — client expectation vs. AI-assisted process gap

The human oversight language reveals a specific documentation need: evidence that a qualified human reviewed AI output before an investment decision was made or communicated to the client. This is distinct from the legal ICP's supervision architecture — it is investment decision documentation with fiduciary consequences.
