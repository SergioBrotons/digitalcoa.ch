## State of Phase 02 Geneva Finance — C309

### Core ICP (Phase 02)
Boutique Geneva wealth managers (50–200 employees, CHF 200M–2B AUM) face a dual accountability: institutional client DDQ obligations (competitive gate) and nFADP Article 63 personal liability (managing director exposure). The Phase 02 ICP has discovered that the institutional client is asking AI governance questions the firm cannot answer — and that the inability to answer is a competitive gate, not just a compliance gap. The primary fear is mandate loss due to DDQ failure — a direct revenue consequence the legal ICP (Phase 01) does not face.

### Core Distinction from Phase 01
Phase 01 (legal) worried about carrier attestation renewal and personal liability in litigation. Phase 02 (finance) worries about institutional mandate retention and personal liability under Swiss data protection law. The stakes are commercial (revenue) rather than legal (litigation), though both ultimately create personal liability exposure.

## Six NET-NEW Findings (Phase 02)

### Finding 01: Institutional DDQ as Competitive Gate — Not Regulatory Obligation, Commercial Consequence

**The finding:** The institutional AI governance DDQ is the operative accountability document for the Geneva boutique WM firm. DDQ failure is a mandate retention event — a direct revenue consequence. The institutional client is setting AI governance standards that exceed what FINMA requires in specific respects. The DDQ is more detailed than the FINMA December 2024 AI guidance. The institutional client is more sophisticated about AI governance than the typical Swiss regulator. The competitive gate framing changes the ICP's psychology: this is not compliance cost, it is mandate survival.

**Market intelligence:** Managing directors reporting that institutional clients have sent AI governance DDQs they cannot answer, and that the inability to answer is being treated as a reason to review the manager relationship. The DDQ is the forcing function for AI governance action.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The institutional DDQ is the document that determines whether you keep the mandate. I read institutional AI governance DDQs — I'll show you what they actually want and how to answer it."

---

### Finding 02: nFADP Article 63 Personal Liability for Investment Decisions — NET-NEW from Phase 01

**The finding:** nFADP Article 63 creates personal liability for the managing director of a WM firm using AI in portfolio decisions. The managing director is personally responsible for ensuring AI systems processing client data comply with nFADP. This is distinct from FINMA regulatory exposure — it is a personal liability exposure under Swiss data protection law that the managing director has not previously identified. The exposure is active, not theoretical. The managing director who signs off on AI-assisted investment recommendations without documented human oversight is personally exposed.

**Market intelligence:** Managing directors beginning to surface the Article 63 question: "I didn't know this applied to me personally." The question is surfacing through internal counsel conversations and institutional DDQ scrutiny, not through regulatory guidance.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Article 63 creates personal liability for you — the managing director — for AI decisions affecting client data. I map the exposure and build the documentation that makes your oversight defensible."

---

### Finding 03: Human Oversight Operationalization Gap — "Adequate" Is Not Defined

**The finding:** FINMA's December 2024 AI guidance requires "human oversight" of AI systems. The guidance does not define what human oversight means operationally for portfolio construction, risk analysis, or performance attribution AI. The gap is not policy — the firm knows human oversight is required. The gap is operational: how to document human oversight in a way that satisfies FINMA if asked, and that the managing director can actually implement given their AI competence limitations. The human oversight operationalization gap is a documentation architecture problem, not a policy problem.

**Market intelligence:** WM firms reporting that they have a policy requiring human review of AI recommendations but no documented procedure for what that review looks like, who performs it, and how it is recorded. The institutional DDQ asks for evidence of human review before client communication — a specific documentation requirement.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Human oversight is required but undefined. I'll show you what it looks like operationally — who reviews what, how it is documented, and how it survives scrutiny from FINMA or an institutional client DDQ."

---

### Finding 04: Vendor AI Governance Contract Gap — Liability Allocation Unresolved

**The finding:** Geneva WM firms are using AI systems from portfolio management vendors, risk analysis providers, and performance attribution platforms — with vendor contracts that do not adequately address AI governance obligations. The typical WM vendor contract was negotiated for technology services, not for AI accountability. The contracts do not specify liability allocation for AI-influenced investment decisions, compelled disclosure exposure through vendor records, or notification obligations when AI models change. The vendor AI governance contract gap is a specific exposure that the WM firm has not addressed.

**Market intelligence:** Managing directors reporting that they signed vendor contracts without reviewing the AI governance terms, and that they do not have a basis to assess vendor AI compliance with nFADP or FINMA requirements. The question of vendor liability for AI-influenced investment decisions is surfacing without resolution.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your vendor contracts were not negotiated for AI governance. I'll review them against your nFADP and FINMA obligations and tell you what is missing and what it costs you."

---

### Finding 05: Portfolio AI Shadow Systems — Associate-Implemented AI Without Managing Director Oversight

**The finding:** Geneva WM firms are running AI tools in investment operations that were implemented by associates without full managing director evaluation. The portfolio construction AI, the risk analysis AI, the performance attribution AI — these systems are processing client data, influencing investment decisions, and generating outputs that go to clients — without a documented AI inventory, without a nFADP DPIA, and without managing director authorization. The associate who implemented the system is more AI-fluent than the managing director. This is the same shadow AI problem as Phase 01, but with direct fiduciary consequences for investment decisions.

**Market intelligence:** Managing directors beginning to ask: "What AI are we actually running?" The question is surfacing because institutional DDQs are asking for an AI inventory that the managing director cannot produce. The AI inventory gap is a direct consequence of associate-implemented systems without managing director oversight.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You don't know what AI systems are processing your client data. I'll conduct the AI inventory and show you what you are running, what it does with the data, and what your obligations are for each system."

---

### Finding 06: FINMA "Adequate" Gap — Principles-Based Guidance Without Operational Definition

**The finding:** FINMA's December 2024 AI guidance is principles-based. It establishes that supervised entities should have AI governance, risk management, transparency, and non-discrimination frameworks. It does not specify what "adequate" means in practice for a boutique WM firm. The Phase 02 ICP is asking: "What does adequate AI governance look like for a50–200 person Geneva wealth manager?" The guidance does not answer. The ICP is left without an operational standard. The "adequate" gap is a service opportunity — an operational definition of FINMA AI governance adequacy for the boutique WM scale.

**Market intelligence:** WM firms reporting that they have read the FINMA guidance, they understand the principles, and they do not know what to do next. The guidance gap is not awareness — it is operationalization. The firm knows what FINMA expects in principle. The firm does not know what to build in practice.

**Ready-to-use language:** "FINMA's guidance is principles-based. I'll translate it into what 'adequate' means for your firm — at your scale, with your AI systems, for your institutional clients."

## Core Insight (Phase 02)

**The market has moved from "do I have a problem?" to "can I keep the mandate?"**

The Phase 01 legal ICP asked: "Do I have an AI governance problem?" The Phase 02 finance ICP asks: "Can I answer the institutional DDQ and keep the mandate?" The DDQ as competitive gate changes the psychology from compliance cost to commercial survival. The institutional client is the forcing function the Swiss regulator has not yet become.

The Phase 02 ICP also faces the Article 63 personal liability question that the legal ICP does not face directly. The managing director of a WM firm is personally liable under nFADP for AI-influenced investment decisions. This is a distinct exposure from carrier attestation or litigation disclosure — it is a personal liability exposure with fiduciary consequences.

The managing partner (legal) and the managing director (finance) share the same competence gap problem: accountable for AI decisions they cannot personally evaluate. The solution is the same in principle: supervision architecture, not training. The implementation differs: investment decision documentation versus legal matter documentation.
