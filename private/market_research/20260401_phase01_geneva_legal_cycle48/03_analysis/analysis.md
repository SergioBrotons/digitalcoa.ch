## Net-New Finding 1: AXA 2/3 vs 1/3 Governance Gap — SMB Universal Pattern
**Source:** CASUS citing AXA KMU-Arbeitsmarktstudie 2025 (published March 25, 2026)
**Finding:** Two in three Swiss SMEs already using or experimenting with AI — but only one in three has clear internal rules governing AI use.
**Gap:** 67% AI use vs 33% with governance rules = ~34-point governance gap at SMB level.
**Portfolio relevance:** This AXA SMB finding directly corroborates the 3-tier adoption pyramid at the aggregate Swiss economy level:
- 67% SMEs using AI = the "individual/employee level" AI use
- 33% with governance rules = the "formal firm policy" level
- This is the same 34-point gap we see in the legal sector specifically (69% individual vs 34% formal)
**Interpretation:** The governance gap is not unique to legal — it is a universal SMB phenomenon in Switzerland. This strengthens Phase 01 C47 finding that law firms are not unusual — they are simply operating within a broader SMB AI governance crisis.
**Confidence:** MEDIUM-HIGH — AXA is major Swiss insurer; KMU-Arbeitsmarktstudie is annual survey.

## Net-New Finding 3: "Automating the Envelope is Safe" — Practical Governance Distinction
**Source:** Meikai.ch
**Finding:** Clear practical distinction for law firms on where AI governance risk begins:
- Safe: routing, scheduling, reminders, document formatting, billing preparation
- Not safe: interpreting document content, drafting advice, substantive analysis
- Rule: if AI reads the content, it's high-risk; if it just moves/manages the content, it's low-risk
**Interpretation:** This "envelope vs. letter" distinction provides an immediately actionable framework for law firms who want a simple rule-of-thumb before conducting a formal Shadow AI audit. This is Decision 0 material — the question "does the AI read the content?" is the first filter before any formal DPIA or policy review.
**Confidence:** HIGH — consistent with FADP Art. 21 (automated decisions) and general data protection principles.

## Net-New Finding 4: Swiss Liability Framework — AI Systems Cannot Be Liable
**Source:** Chambers AI 2025 Switzerland (May 22, 2025)
**Finding:** Swiss civil law: AI systems themselves cannot be held liable — the liability attaches to the owner, operator, or manufacturer. Criminal liability: primarily natural persons; companies only for select offences.
**Portfolio relevance:** This is critical for law firm ICPs: the lawyer (as operator/user of AI) bears personal liability for AI-assisted work product. This is distinct from the FADP compliance obligation and adds a professional liability layer on top.
**Confidence:** HIGH — Chambers cites Swiss Code of Obligations and Swiss criminal law directly.

## Net-New Finding 5: Product Liability Act May Apply to AI Products

**Confidence:** MEDIUM — Chambers says "could potentially apply" indicating legal uncertainty.
