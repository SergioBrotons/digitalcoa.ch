## Net-New Primary Sources — Cycle 48

### Source 1: Chambers Practice Guide Artificial Intelligence 2025 Switzerland
**URL:** https://practiceguides.chambers.com/practice-guides/artificial-intelligence-2025/switzerland
**Updated:** May 22, 2025
**Reliability:** HIGH — Chambers is primary legal market intelligence source

**Key Data Points:**
- Switzerland has no legislation dealing specifically with AI currently
- Switzerland signed Council of Europe's AI Convention; ratification expected after 2026
- FADP technology-neutral: applies to AI-based processing of personal data
- FDPIC confirmed FADP applies to AI in November 2023 statement
- FDPIC highlighted: transparency requirement for purpose, functionality, and data sources of AI-based processing
- PwC Strategy& March 2024: Swiss economy has highest growth potential worldwide for generative AI
- Swiss government bottom-up approach + technology-neutral regulation confirmed
- Civil liability: AI systems cannot be held liable; owner/operator/manufacturer can
- Criminal liability: natural persons primarily; legal entities only for select offences
- Product Liability Act could potentially apply to AI-powered products
- Employment law: employer duty of care extends to AI in employment context

### Source 2: Meikai.ch — AI automation for law firms in Switzerland
**URL:** https://www.meikai.ch/en/insights/ai-automation-law-firms-switzerland
**Reliability:** HIGH — Swiss legal AI vendor primary source

**Key Data Points:**
- Swiss law firms lose 8–14 hours/week across intake, billing, and document tracking (4–8 lawyer firm)
- Time-to-bill gap in manual billing practices: typically 10–15%
- Client intake automation case study: 9.2 partner-hours/month in 4-lawyer firm = CHF 34,440/year recovered
- Practical AI governance distinction: "automating the envelope is safe. Automating the letter is not."
  - Envelope = routing, scheduling, reminders, document formatting, billing preparation
  - Letter = interpreting document content, drafting advice, substantive legal analysis
- Risk only increases when: automation involves interpreting document content; AI drafts advice; data flows through platforms without appropriate DPAs
- MEIKAI builds on platforms meeting Swiss nFADP/FADP requirements and EU GDPR data processing standards
- Clarity Scan for law firms includes data residency review as standard diagnostic component

### Source 3: CASUS — AI and Switzerland's Data Protection Act (March 25, 2026)
**URL:** https://www.getcasus.com/blog/ai-dsg-switzerland-legal
**Published:** March 25, 2026 (VERY RECENT — 6 days ago)
**Author:** Céleste Urech | Co-Founder & CTO, CASUS
**Reliability:** HIGH — Swiss legal AI vendor co-founder primary source

**Key Data Points:**
- FDPIC updated guidance document issued May 8, 2025 (confirming FADP applies to AI without new law)
- FADP Art. 22 DSG: DPIA required for AI processing with high risk to personality rights or fundamental rights
- DSG prohibits: mass real-time facial recognition in public spaces; social scoring
- AXA KMU-Arbeitsmarktstudie 2025: two in three Swiss SMEs are already using or experimenting with AI
- AXA KMU-Arbeitsmarktstudie 2025: only one in three Swiss SMEs has clear internal rules governing AI use
- In most organisations: employees independently decide which tools to use and what data to enter
- FDPIC international focus: March 2025 preliminary investigation into X (formerly Twitter) for using public posts to train AI model Grok without sufficient transparency
- Switzerland's regulatory approach (three goals): strengthening innovation; protecting fundamental rights including economic freedom; building public trust in AI systems
- Switzerland does not yet have dedicated AI legislation (unlike EU AI Act)
- Federal Council signed CoE AI Convention March 2025; ratification planned; consultation proposals expected end-2026
- BAKOM leading regulatory review process
- DSG closely modelled on GDPR; DSG compliance aligns with GDPR but not fully equivalent
