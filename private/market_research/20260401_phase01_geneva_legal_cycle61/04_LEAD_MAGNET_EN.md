## The Five Regulatory Layers Currently in Force

Most small firm partners assume AI governance is something to plan for. It is not. It is something to manage now.

**Here is the complete picture:**

### Layer 1: Federal Act on Data Protection (FADP)
*In force since September 1, 2023. Confirmed AI-applicable by FDPIC, April 2026.*

If your firm processes any client personal data through AI tools — and it does — FADP applies. Specifically:
- **Article 21 transparency obligations**: You must inform clients when AI produces automated decisions affecting them
- **DPIA requirement**: If your AI processing creates high risk to data subjects' rights, you must conduct a formal Data Protection Impact Assessment
- **Data security**: Your AI tools must meet FADP security standards for personal data protection
- **Prohibited applications**: Certain AI uses (mass real-time facial recognition in public spaces) are flatly prohibited

**The FDPIC has confirmed: this law already applies to your AI. There is no grace period.**

### Layer 2: Federal AI Ordinance
*In force since January 1, 2025 — now over 15 months operative.*

The Federal Council's ordinance on "Procurement of AI Systems by the Federal Administration" sets the domestic standard for how Swiss government agencies procure AI. While it directly governs federal procurement, it signals the standard of care the Swiss government considers minimum acceptable for AI use. Your clients who work with federal agencies will encounter this standard. So should you.

### Layer 3: EU AI Act — GPAI Obligations
*In force since August 2, 2025. Already operative. Most Geneva firms do not know this.*

The EU AI Act's General-Purpose AI obligations have been in force since August 2025. This means:

- Any Geneva firm using GPAI models (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, etc.) **for EU-facing client work** is already subject to EU AI Act transparency and documentation requirements
- If your firm has EU-based clients, or if your AI-generated outputs are used in EU business decisions, **the EU AI Act applies to you**
- Non-compliance penalties: up to **€35 million or 7% of worldwide annual turnover**

You do not need to be an EU company. The EU AI Act has extraterritorial reach.

### Layer 4: Section 301 — US Trade Investigation
*USTR written comments deadline: April 15, 2026. ~14 days from today.*

The United States Trade Representative has opened two Section 301 investigations targeting Switzerland:

**Investigation A (March 11, 2026):** Structural excess manufacturing capacity — Switzerland among 16 economies named. The US alleges currency manipulation alongside overproduction.

**Investigation B (March 12, 2026):** Failure to restrict importation of goods produced with forced labour — 60 economies named.

The Federal Council has formally rejected both allegations (Federal Council meeting, April 1, 2026). Switzerland will present its views in US proceedings.

**For Geneva law firms:** If any of your clients have US-Swiss trade exposure, they may need to submit individual comments by April 15. This is a live advisory opportunity. Firms that help clients file are positioning for ongoing US-Swiss trade defence work.

### Layer 5: Council of Europe AI Convention
*Signed March 2025. FDJP-led implementation in Switzerland. Public consultation draft expected end-2026.*

The CoE Framework Convention on AI, Human Rights, Democracy and the Rule of Law is the closest thing Switzerland has to a future AI governance framework. The FDJP is leading implementation, with a public consultation draft expected around the end of 2026. Industry-specific solutions and self-declaration pathways are expected to feature.

**Your window to shape that framework is now — during the consultation period, not after it closes.**

## The August 2, 2026 Deadline

In approximately four months, the EU AI Act enters its second major phase:

- **High-risk AI conformity assessments** become mandatory
- **Legacy GPAI model grandfathering** expires
- **Technical documentation, data governance, human oversight, and cybersecurity requirements** for high-risk AI systems become enforceable

For most small Geneva firms, the immediate GPAI exposure (already in force since August 2025) is more urgent than the high-risk AI obligations — but the high-risk obligations will arrive before most firms are ready.

## The Swiss Advantage: Use It Without Abusing It

The Federal Council's deliberate choice not to create a "Swiss AI Act" is an advantage — not a gap. Switzerland's technology-neutral, sector-specific approach means your firm faces lighter domestic regulatory burden than counterparts in the EU or UK.

But this advantage comes with conditions: you must manage your EU AI Act exposure directly, because Switzerland will not build a wall between you and Brussels.

**The framing that works for clients:** Swiss freedom, EU compliance.

## 5-Minute Self-Assessment

Answer these five questions:

1. Do your AI tools process any client personal data? (Almost certainly yes)
2. Have you conducted a DPIA for your AI use? (If no, you have a FADP exposure)
3. Do any of your clients operate in the EU or use outputs in EU business decisions? (If yes, EU AI Act applies now)
4. Does your firm have a written AI governance policy? (If no, you have a professional liability exposure)
5. Do any of your clients need to submit Section 301 comments by April 15? (If yes, the window is open and closing)

If you answered "no" or "I don't know" to any of these, your firm has an active governance gap.
