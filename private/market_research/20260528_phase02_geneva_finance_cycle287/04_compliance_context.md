## Geneva/Switzerland Specific Regulatory Framework

### 1. FINMA Supervisory Expectations

**Current State:** FINMA has not published AI-specific regulatory text for wealth management firms. Supervisory expectations are forming through examination practice, not published guidance.

**What this means for boutique wealth managers:**
- No published FINMA standard defining what "adequate AI governance" means for a Geneva wealth management boutique
- FINMA supervisory priorities observable only through carrier iteration patterns (carrier questionnaires adjusting in response to FINMA feedback)
- Boutique building AI governance documentation against standards they cannot observe forming
- Carrier third-generation questionnaire evolution as the most operationally specific signal of what FINMA expects

**Key FINMA-relevant provisions:**

**FINMA Circular 2017/1 (Risk Distribution):** AI-assisted portfolio construction may create correlated risk concentrations not visible through traditional risk distribution analysis. Not AI-specific, but AI application raises questions the circular doesn't address.

**FINMA Circular 2023/1 (Operational Risk):** AI tool dependencies, vendor risk, model risk, and documentation requirements fall under operational risk. Boutique building operational risk documentation for AI tools for the first time may not have baseline architecture.

**FINMA Asset Management Guidance:** Asset managers using AI for portfolio construction must demonstrate adequate governance — definition of "adequate" forming through examination, not published.

**What boutique MDs need to know:** FINMA is not waiting for legislation to form expectations. Examination practice is the operative standard-formation mechanism. The documentation window before examination is when proportionate architecture must be built — not after FINMA publishes guidance (may be too late for decisions already made).

### 2. SRO Obligations — The Silence as Market Condition

**Current State:** Geneva wealth management SROs (self-regulatory organizations recognized by FINMA) have not issued AI-specific guidance for wealth managers. This silence is the operative market condition.

**Relevant SROs for Geneva wealth management:**
- **SO-FIG** (Swiss Association of Securities Dealers) — covers securities dealers, some wealth management operations
- **Asset Management Switzerland (AMS)** — asset management industry association, not SRO per se but relevant
- **SFA** (Swiss Funds & Asset Management Association) — for fund management companies
- **Geneva private banking associations** — French-language circuit operating under different professional standards

**What SROs cover vs. don't cover:**
- SROs supervise compliance with Anti-Money Laundering, organizational requirements, audit standards
- AI governance is not within traditional SRO scope — no regulatory basis for issuing AI-specific guidance
- SROs waiting for FINMA to define AI obligations before incorporating into supervision framework
- This creates a structural vacuum: SRO can't tell boutique what AI governance requires because SRO doesn't know

**The operative problem:**
Boutique wealth manager asking SRO about AI obligations receives no answer. The absence of answer is not the same as "no obligations." Boutique making governance decisions based on SRO silence — decisions that will be evaluated against standards the SRO hasn't articulated.

**What boutique MDs need to know:** SRO silence is not comfort. The Geneva accountability ecosystem (institutional clients, carriers, informal circuit intelligence) is forming standards that SRO hasn't incorporated. MDs operating on "SRO hasn't said anything, so we're fine" are making decisions against forming standards they cannot observe.

### 3. nFADP (Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection) — Art.15, Art.21, Art.63

**Art.15 — Right to Correct Inaccurate Data**
AI-inferred content about client financial behavior may be inaccurate. Inaccurate AI inferences embedded in client reports activate Art.15 correction obligations when client relies on them.

Key distinction: Art.15 applies to inferences about specific clients embedded in client-facing documents, not just raw data accuracy or model performance accuracy.

**Art.21 — Automated Decision-Making**
AI-assisted portfolio construction may constitute profiling under nFADP Art.8. Client-facing AI-influenced decisions activate Art.21 obligations:
- Right to know if automated decision-making was used
- Right to human review of AI-influenced decisions
- Right to explanation of logic used

Boutique wealth managers have not built response infrastructure for Art.21 requests on AI-influenced portfolio decisions. The service doesn't exist in Geneva market.

**Art.63 — Personal Criminal Liability**
nFADP Art.63 creates personal criminal liability for violations — not corporate liability, personal liability of responsible individuals.

For wealth management: AI-assisted portfolio decisions that constitute nFADP violations may create personal criminal exposure for MDs who signed carrier attestations committing to adequacy.

**Profiling Notification (Art.8)**
AI-assisted portfolio construction constitutes profiling under nFADP Art.8. Data subjects (clients) have right to notification and human review.

Geneva wealth management boutiques have not issued profiling notifications as a category. The obligation exists regardless of firm awareness.

**What boutique MDs need to know:** nFADP obligations are present-tense, not future. AI-assisted decisions made in 2024-2025 are subject to nFADP obligations that haven't been discharged. Art.63 personal liability exposure exists for decisions made during the undocumented window.

### 4. AI Convention (Council of Europe) — Ratification and Obligations

**Status:** Switzerland signed the Council of Europe AI Convention. Ratification creates binding obligations in Swiss law through the treaty mechanism, before Swiss implementing legislation.

**For Geneva wealth management:**
- AI Convention obligations flow through existing client contracts and carrier underwriting terms
- Professional liability carriers may reference AI Convention compliance as condition of coverage
- Boutique discovering obligations through carrier questionnaires referencing convention compliance

**Key Convention obligations relevant to wealth management:**
- Transparency obligations for AI systems affecting individuals
- Accountability obligations for AI-assisted decisions
- Human oversight requirements for high-risk AI applications
- Documentation requirements for AI system governance

**The implementation gap:** AI Convention obligations exist, Swiss implementing legislation doesn't. Boutique must satisfy treaty obligations through existing legal relationships (client contracts, carrier terms) without guidance on what compliance requires.

**What boutique MDs need to know:** AI Convention ratification is not a future event. Obligations are present-tense, flowing through existing mandates. The carrier referencing convention compliance in renewal questionnaire means coverage depends on obligations the boutique may not have mapped.

### 5. Carrier PI Insurance AI Attestation Evolution

**First Generation:** Basic AI tool disclosure. Carrier asked if firm uses AI. Yes/no response. Administrative compliance, minimal personal exposure.

**Second Generation:** AI governance policy review. Carrier asked for policy document. MD signed acknowledgment of having a policy. Policy existence sufficient.

**Third Generation (current):** Carrier asks for evidence of specific AI decision processes. MD personally signs attestation warranting:
- Three specific AI-assisted decisions reviewed by MD
- Documentation of review process for each decision
- Evidence that MD reviewed the specific output, not just existence of policy
- Personal commitment to adequacy standard carrier has not defined in accessible language

**Fourth Generation (emerging):** Carrier probing reasoning-chain architecture — how decisions were constructed, not just what was decided. Performance evidence (did governance produce intended outcomes) replacing policy documentation (what governance framework exists).

**Key carrier pattern intelligence:**
- Carrier iterations track FINMA supervisory feedback — carrier questions are leading indicator of what FINMA expects
- Carrier ISAE 3000 report proves vendor controls, not professional governance — boutiques discovering this during third-gen questionnaire completion
- Carrier attestation commits MD personally — no Geneva market service offers MD-focused pre-signing review of what signatory commits to

**What boutique MDs need to know:** Carrier attestation is not administrative compliance. Personal signature creates personal liability against an undefined standard. The 90-day pre-renewal window is when proportionate evidence architecture can be built for decisions already made. After renewal, the window closes.

### 6. Institutional Client AI Governance Requirements

**The Institutional DDQ Pattern:**
Large institutional clients (pension funds, family offices, foundations, international institutions) sending 20-40 page AI governance questionnaires to Geneva boutiques as part of due diligence process.

**Questionnaire content patterns:**
- AI governance policy (existence and content)
- Human oversight mechanism (specific, not generic "we have review")
- Portfolio construction AI methodology (evidence, not policy)
- AI tool inventory and version tracking
- Staff AI training and competency documentation
- nFADP compliance documentation
- AI incident history and response
- Third-party AI tool vendor assessments

**The institutional standard-formation mechanism:**
First boutique to receive questionnaire from an institutional client responds in whatever format they can. That response becomes a benchmark the institutional client uses for subsequent questionnaires. The first responder sets an implicit standard the market didn't know was being set.

**What boutique MDs need to know:** Responding to institutional questionnaires is not just compliance — it's standard-setting. The way boutique responds to first institutional questionnaire shapes what all subsequent boutiques are evaluated against.

### 7. FIDLEG / FINIA Relevance

**FIDLEG (Federal Act on Financial Services):** 
Art.26 algorithmic routine disclosure — financial services providers must disclose when using algorithmic decision-making for client portfolio decisions. AI-assisted portfolio construction may be algorithmic decision-making.

Boutique wealth managers haven't issued algorithmic routine disclosure as category because:
- FIDLEG interpretation unclear for boutique scale
- Disclosure content and format not defined for Geneva market
- No service offering FIDLEG Art.26 mapping for AI-assisted wealth management

**FINIA (Financial Services Act):**
Consumer-facing disclosure obligations, less relevant for institutional client relationships but relevant for high-net-worth individual clients.

**What boutique MDs need to know:** FIDLEG Art.26 algorithmic disclosure is activated by AI-assisted portfolio decisions. The obligation exists, the disclosure format doesn't. Boutique operating without published format is exposed even if the obligation is acknowledged.

### 9. The Documentation Window Property

**Critical timing insight for boutique wealth managers:**

The pre-carrier-renewal window (typically 90 days) is the only period when proportionate documentation architecture can be built for decisions already made during the undocumented window.

**Before window closes:**
- Building documentation evaluated against forming standards
- Evidence architecture can be built for decisions made 2024-2025
- Documentation reconstruction methodology can be applied to undocumented period

**After window closes:**
- Explaining absence of documentation evaluated against arrived standards
- New documentation cannot retroactively reconstruct window-period reasoning
- Carrier examination asks about decisions made during window — reconstruction may be impossible

**What boutique MDs need to know:** The 90-day pre-renewal window is not a compliance deadline. It's a burden-of-proof positioning exercise. The evidence you build during this window determines how you appear when carrier examines you.
