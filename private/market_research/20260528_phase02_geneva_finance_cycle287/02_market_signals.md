## Current Market Problems, Complaints, and Failures

### 1. Carrier Attestation Evolution Creating Unexpected Exposure

**Signal:** Third-generation carrier questionnaires now require MD personal signature committing to adequacy standard that carriers have not defined in accessible language.

**What's failing:**
- Boutique MDs signed first- and second-generation questionnaires as administrative compliance exercises
- Third-generation asks for specific evidence of AI decision processes with personal attestation language
- MDs discovering post-signature that the attestation commits them to standards they cannot articulate
- No pre-signing review service available in Geneva market

**Specific failure pattern:** Carrier's own ISAE 3000 report (183 AWS services) referenced in questionnaire as if it provides coverage the boutique can rely on — it doesn't. It proves vendor controls, not professional governance.

**ICP complaint formulation:** "I signed it because the carrier required a signature — I didn't understand what I was committing to personally."

---

### 2. Institutional Client AI Governance Questionnaires as de facto Standard-Setting

**Signal:** Large institutional clients (pension funds, family offices, international foundations) sending 20-40 page AI governance questionnaires to Geneva boutiques.

**What's failing:**
- First-questioned boutique sets implicit standard that subsequent boutiques must address
- Institutional clients comparing Geneva boutiques against standards derived from conversations with large banks
- Boutique responses revealing documentation that wasn't designed to answer methodology questions
- No Geneva-specific institutional response framework exists

**Specific failure pattern:** Institutional client asking about portfolio construction methodology specifically, not just AI policy existence. Boutique has AI policy document, not methodology evidence for specific decisions.

**ICP complaint formulation:** "Page 12 asks how AI was used in constructing our Q3 recommendations — I have a policy document, not evidence of how specific decisions were made."

---

### 3. SRO Guidance Vacuum as Active Market Condition

**Signal:** Geneva wealth management SROs have not issued AI-specific guidance. This silence is being interpreted by boutique MDs in two conflicting ways: (a) no obligations exist, or (b) obligations exist but SRO hasn't figured out how to communicate them.

**What's failing:**
- MDs who interpret silence as absence are operating without guidance and without documentation
- MDs who interpret silence as pending are waiting for guidance that may not arrive before personal liability crystallizes
- Neither interpretation produces appropriate action because appropriate action requires SRO guidance that doesn't exist

**Specific failure pattern:** Boutique making decisions about AI governance based on SRO silence — decisions that will be evaluated against standards that SRO hasn't articulated.

**ICP complaint formulation:** "I asked my SRO about AI obligations — they said they'd follow up. That was six months ago."

### 4. Silent Model Update Propagation Across Active Mandates

**Signal:** AI vendors updating models without adequate notice. Boutique discovering that active client recommendations were produced under model versions that no longer exist.

**What's failing:**
- No version tracking architecture in boutique operations
- No reassessment trigger when model updates occur
- Engagement letters don't address version dependency of active recommendations
- Carrier questionnaires don't ask about model update management — yet

**Specific failure pattern:** Client comparing two quarterly reports produced under different model versions without awareness that recommendations may reflect different analytical frameworks.

**ICP complaint formulation:** "The AI tool updated and I don't know what changed — our recommendations may have changed without us knowing."

### 5. FINMA Supervisory Expectations Opacity

**Signal:** FINMA has not issued AI-specific guidance for wealth managers. Boutique MDs perceive this as either safety or exposure — no way to know which.

**What's failing:**
- Absence of published FINMA expectations creates uncertainty that boutique MDs resolve differently based on risk tolerance
- FINMA supervisory priorities forming through examination practice, not published guidance
- Boutique exposed to standards they cannot observe forming
- Carrier iteration pattern analysis as only available intelligence on what FINMA expects

**Specific failure pattern:** Boutique building AI governance documentation against wrong standard because they cannot observe what standard FINMA is building toward through examination.

**ICP complaint formulation:** "FINMA hasn't said anything specific about AI for wealth managers — does that mean we're okay, or does it mean we should be preparing for something we don't know is coming?"

### 6. nFADP Art.63 Personal Liability Crystallization

**Signal:** French Geneva wealth managers beginning to discuss Art.63 nFADP as personal criminal liability provision applicable to AI-assisted portfolio management decisions.

**What's failing:**
- Art.63 creates personal criminal liability for nFADP violations — not corporate liability, personal liability
- AI-influenced portfolio decisions may constitute automated decision-making under Art.21
- Boutique MDs who didn't know Art.63 applied to them discovering it applies to them personally
- No Geneva market service offering Art.63 personal liability mapping for wealth managers

**Specific failure pattern:** MD who signed carrier attestation committing to adequacy, without understanding that nFADP Art.63 creates personal criminal exposure for the same decisions.

**ICP complaint formulation:** "Article 63 — does that actually apply to me as someone who uses AI for portfolio decisions? What exactly does it cover?"

### 7. Staff Shadow AI Creating Carrier Attestation Exposure

**Signal:** Boutique wealth management firms discovering that staff have been using AI tools outside MD awareness — tools the MD has attested to having governance over.

**What's failing:**
- Carrier attestation commits MD to governance of all AI tool use in the firm
- Staff using AI tools the MD doesn't know about creates attestation gap
- Shadow AI use discovered during carrier renewal preparation or institutional audit
- No small-firm AI governance infrastructure to monitor what staff are doing with AI

**Specific failure pattern:** MD signs attestation covering AI governance — carrier examines specific AI decisions — MD cannot account for AI decisions staff made without MD knowledge.

**ICP complaint formulation:** "I attested to having governance over all AI tools in the firm — I didn't know staff were using tools I hadn't approved."

### 8. Documentation Window Burden-of-Proof Reversal

**Signal:** Boutique MDs beginning to understand that the documentation they build now will be evaluated against standards that don't yet exist.

**What's failing:**
- Before window closes: building documentation means evaluated against forming standards
- After window closes: explaining absence of documentation means evaluated against arrived standards
- New documentation cannot retroactively reconstruct what decisions were made and why during the undocumented period
- The 90-day pre-carrier-renewal window is the only opportunity to build evidence architecture for decisions already made

**Specific failure pattern:** Boutique trying to build retrospective documentation after carrier renewal questions arrive — cannot reconstruct original reasoning for AI-assisted decisions made 12-18 months ago.

**ICP complaint formulation:** "I need to document decisions made over the past two years — can I do that now, or is it too late?"

- Carrier underwriting terms referencing AI Convention compliance — coverage depends on obligations the firm didn't know it had
- No Geneva market service mapping existing client contracts against AI Convention obligations

**Specific failure pattern:** Boutique discovering AI Convention obligations through carrier questionnaire referencing convention compliance — obligations existed before boutique knew about them.

**ICP complaint formulation:** "The carrier is asking about AI Convention compliance — I didn't know that applied to us yet."

### 10. Institutional Counterparty AI Requirements Convergence

**What's failing:**

**Specific failure pattern:** Boutique responding to institutional questionnaire in a way that creates commercial representations affecting relationship with a different institutional client.

**ICP complaint formulation:** "Three of our institutional clients have sent AI governance questionnaires in the past three months — each one asks for something different."
