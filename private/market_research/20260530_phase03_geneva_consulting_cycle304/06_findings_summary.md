# Findings Summary: Geneva Consulting — C304 Phase 03

## 6 NET-NEW Findings: Distinct from C214–C303

---

### Finding 01 — Engagement Letter AI Silence Is Contractual Ambiguity Against the Drafting Party

**The finding:** Geneva consulting firms' engagement letters have no AI clauses, no data processing framework, no liability allocation for AI-assisted work. Under Swiss Code of Obligations Art. 2, contract ambiguity is interpreted against the drafting party. The consulting firm bears the ambiguity risk for every silence in the engagement letter. The silence is not neutral — it creates exposure.

**Mechanism:** Client engagement → engagement letter silent on AI → AI used in project → client challenges AI use or outcome → no contract provision governs AI liability → ambiguity interpreted against consulting firm → firm bears liability for circumstances it didn't anticipate or allocate.

**Implication:** A consulting-specific engagement letter AI clause template is a discrete service. The clause is not a liability waiver — it is a liability allocation framework that names the AI use, defines the data processing obligations, and allocates risk appropriately.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your engagement letter is silent on AI. That silence is interpreted against you. I can show you what your contracts don't cover and what AI clauses would protect you."

### Finding 02 — Client AI Governance Questionnaires Create Documented Evidence of Inconsistent Response

**The finding:** Client AI governance questionnaires arrive as part of RFP processes and ongoing compliance. The consulting firm that cannot respond creates documented evidence of inadequate AI governance. The firm that responds partially creates documented evidence of partial compliance. Both responses become part of the client's compliance record. The documented response is worse than no response if the firm cannot support it.

**Mechanism:** Client questionnaire → partial or inconsistent response → response documented in client file → client references prior response in future challenge → consulting firm cannot distinguish between "didn't know" and "doesn't have" → documented inconsistency worse than silence.

**Implication:** A structured client questionnaire response methodology is a discrete service. The methodology produces defensible documentation without revealing proprietary methodology.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your client questionnaire response is documented evidence. If you can't support what you said, the document works against you. I can show you how to respond defensively."

### Finding 03 — Sub-Consultant Delegation Chain Creates Accountable Exposure Without Accountability Visibility

**The finding:** Managing directors of consulting firms are accountable for sub-consultant work, including sub-consultant AI use. The delegation chain — managing director → sub-consultant → AI tool — has no documented authorization, no scope definition, no monitoring. The managing director is exposed for AI use they didn't authorize and cannot see.

**Mechanism:** Engagement → sub-consultant retained → sub-consultant uses AI tools → no managing director authorization documented → no scope defined → client harmed → client challenges consulting firm → managing director personally accountable for sub-consultant AI use → no documentation of delegation or its limits.

**Implication:** A sub-consultant delegation framework is a discrete service. The framework enables managing directors to authorize sub-consultant AI use while maintaining accountability visibility.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You're accountable for your sub-consultants' AI use — and you have no visibility into what they're using. That's your exposure. I can design a delegation framework that creates accountability without blocking effective use."

### Finding 04 — "AI-Assisted Analysis" Is Not a Documentation Category

**The finding:** Consulting proposals use "AI-assisted analysis" as a descriptive phrase without defining what it means. No documentation exists for: what AI tools were used, what data was input, what methodology was applied, what the AI output was, how the output was evaluated. When a client challenges the work, the consulting firm cannot document the methodology's AI component. The phrase provides no protection and no defensibility.

**Mechanism:** Proposal language → "AI-assisted analysis" → client challenge → no documentation of AI component → firm cannot defend methodology integrity → client relationship damaged, exposure documented.

**Implication:** A consulting methodology documentation standard for AI use is a discrete service. The standard defines what "AI-assisted" means in the consulting context and what documentation is required.

**Ready-to-use language:** "'AI-assisted analysis' is not a documentation category. It provides no protection and no defensibility. I can show you what the documentation category actually looks like for your methodology."

### Finding 05 — EU AI Act Deployer Obligations Create Structural Exposure for Non-EU Vendor Use

**The finding:** EU AI Act deployer obligations apply to consulting firms using AI tools in client work. Non-EU AI vendors cannot provide EU Declaration of Conformity. The consulting firm using non-EU AI tools cannot satisfy EU AI Act deployer obligations regardless of internal governance. The exposure is structural — it cannot be closed by documentation effort within the firm.

**Mechanism:** EU AI Act deployer obligations → require EU Declaration of Conformity from vendor → non-EU vendor cannot provide declaration → consulting firm cannot demonstrate compliance → structural exposure regardless of internal governance.

**Implication:** A vendor evaluation for EU AI Act compliance is a discrete service for consulting firms serving EU-regulated clients. The evaluation determines whether current vendor stack creates EU AI Act deployer exposure.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The EU AI Act creates deployer obligations that your non-EU vendors can't satisfy. That's structural exposure, not a documentation problem. I can evaluate your vendor stack for this."

### Finding 06 — nFADP Art.63 Personal Liability Requires Specific Structuring Beyond Corporate Structure

**The finding:** nFADP Art.63 creates personal criminal liability for unauthorized data processing. The corporate structure of a consulting firm does not protect the managing director from personal liability under Art.63. The managing director's personal assets and professional license are at risk, not just the firm's corporate liability. Specific structuring is required beyond what the corporate structure provides.

**Mechanism:** Data processing during consulting engagement → unauthorized under nFADP → Art.63 criminal liability triggered → managing director personally exposed → corporate structure does not protect → personal assets and license at risk.

**Implication:** A personal liability structuring review for managing directors of consulting firms is a discrete service. The review assesses what the corporate structure does and doesn't protect, and what additional measures are required.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your corporate structure doesn't protect you from Art.63 personal liability. That's a specific exposure that requires specific structuring. I can assess what you need."
