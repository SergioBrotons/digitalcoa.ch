## Three Timelines Are Running Simultaneously

**Timeline 1 — FADP: Already Operative**
The revised Federal Act on Data Protection (FADP), in force since September 1, 2023, is technology-neutral. It applies directly to AI systems that process personal data. The Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner (FDPIC) confirmed this explicitly in guidance issued May 8, 2025.

Your obligations under the FADP are not future obligations. They are already operative:
- **Art. 5(c) FADP**: Data may only be processed for the declared purpose. If your AI tools are using client financial data for purposes beyond what was originally disclosed, this principle may be breached.
- **Art. 16 FADP**: Adequate data security measures are required. Criminal penalties up to CHF 250,000 for individuals.
- **Art. 21 FADP**: If AI is used to make automated decisions that significantly affect clients, they have the right to request human review.
- **Privacy by Design (Art. 7 FADP)**: Data protection must be considered from the earliest planning stages — not retrofitted after deployment.
- **DPIA (Art. 22 FADP)**: If AI processing carries high risk to client rights and interests, a Data Protection Impact Assessment is required.

**Timeline 2 — CARF/CRS 2.0: Live Since January 1, 2026**
Common Reporting Standard Version 2.0 (CRS 2.0) went live on January 1, 2026. The OECD's Frontal Assault / Cartographie / Research (CARF) framework — the new real-time transaction reporting standard — is now operative.

As of April 1, 2026, CARF/CRS 2.0 has been live for three months. The FATF 2026–2027/2028 review cycle means that FATF examiners will assess Swiss financial institutions' CARF/CRS compliance — including the governance of any AI systems used in the reporting process.

The connection most wealth managers miss: if AI tools are involved in client data processing, portfolio reporting, or transaction monitoring for CARF/CRS purposes, those AI tools need to be documented in your CARF/CRS compliance framework. FATF examiners will look for this documentation.

**Timeline 3 — EU AI Act + Federal Council Legislation: 2026 Deadline**
Wealth management firms with EU-based clients must comply with the EU AI Act by August 2026. This is an extraterritorial obligation — it applies to Swiss firms serving EU clients, regardless of where the firm is based.

The Federal Council is expected to introduce Swiss legislation implementing the Council of Europe's AI Convention by the end of 2026. This will create the first Swiss-specific AI law, likely with expanded obligations beyond the current FADP framework.

FINMA has already articulated its expectations for Swiss financial institutions using AI in Circular 08/2024. FINMA's AI Desk and on-site inspections are already active. Wealth management firms are in the examination window now.

The gap between belief and action is not a capability gap. It is a governance gap. The firms that want to deploy AI cannot because they do not have the governance framework to do so safely. The firms that deploy AI without governance are accumulating regulatory exposure.

## The Agentic AI Risk: The Next Wave Is Already in the Pipeline

78% of wealth management firms are exploring agentic AI — AI systems that autonomously execute transactions, rebalance portfolios, send client communications, or generate compliance reports without human approval at each step.

This is a qualitatively different risk profile from current AI use. Agentic AI in wealth management creates specific regulatory exposure:

**FINMA Suitability Requirements**: AI agents making investment recommendations or executing trades must comply with FINMA suitability rules. An AI agent that rebalances a portfolio without human approval may be making suitability determinations autonomously — which triggers obligations the firm may not be equipped to meet.

**FATF AML Flags**: Agentic AI processing client transactions for AML purposes must produce outputs that meet FATF reporting standards. If the AI agent is not documenting its decision logic in a way that FATF examiners can review, the firm has a FATF exposure problem.

**CARF/CRS**: If AI agents are involved in generating CARF/CRS reports, those reports must reflect the AI's logic and be attributable to a human responsible party. Agentic AI that operates without a clear accountability chain creates CARF/CRS documentation gaps.

**The Envelope Test for Wealth Management**: Before deploying agentic AI, ask: does this AI read client financial data and take action based on what it reads? If yes, it is a high-risk deployment requiring DPA, DPIA, formal policy, and documented human oversight.

Safe (automating the envelope):
- Scheduling and calendar management for client meetings
- Document formatting and template population (portfolio reports, meeting agendas)
- Billing preparation and fee calculation
- KYC document routing and checklist tracking
- Performance data aggregation for reporting

Not safe without DPA and DPIA (automating the letter):
- Autonomous portfolio rebalancing based on client financial data
- AI-driven client communication that interprets portfolio performance
- Automated CARF/CRS reporting from raw transaction data
- AI-generated investment recommendations or suitability assessments
- Autonomous transaction monitoring with AI-flagged suspicious activity reports

## The Three-Layer Liability Stack for Wealth Managers

Wealth managers using AI carry personal regulatory exposure at three distinct layers:

**Layer 1 — FADP Compliance Obligation**
Your firm is already subject to the FADP. The obligations are already operative. The FDPIC has signaled enforcement is coming. The personal criminal exposure (up to CHF 250,000) is already in force.

**Layer 2 — FINMA Professional Responsibility**
As a FINMA-licensed advisor or wealth manager, you bear personal responsibility for investment recommendations made to clients — whether those recommendations are made by you directly or by an AI tool you deployed. If an AI-assisted recommendation causes client harm, you cannot deflect responsibility to the AI vendor. FINMA will hold the licensed individual accountable.

**Layer 3 — Product Liability**
If an AI investment tool you deployed causes financial harm to clients, product liability exposure may attach — particularly as Swiss courts and regulators develop jurisprudence on AI-specific liability questions.

The common thread across all three layers: the licensed individual bears the exposure. The AI vendor's terms of service do not protect you from FINMA accountability or FADP criminal liability.

## The FATF Examination Readiness Question

The FATF 2026–2027/2028 review cycle is not a distant threat. FATF examiners will assess Swiss financial institutions' AI governance as part of their review. The specific questions they will ask:

1. What AI tools does your firm use?
2. Who is responsible for those AI tools' outputs?
3. How do you ensure AI-generated recommendations meet FATF AML standards?
4. How is AI involved in CARF/CRS reporting?
5. What documentation exists for your AI governance framework?

A firm that cannot answer these five questions clearly is not FATF-ready — regardless of how sophisticated its AI deployment is.

The FATF review creates a specific forcing function: the question is not whether to build AI governance, but whether to build it proactively (on your timeline, with your documentation standards) or reactively (under FATF examination pressure).

## The Swiss Data Residency Reality for Wealth Management

Swiss wealth management firms handle highly sensitive client financial data — account details, investment positions, family wealth information, estate planning documents. This data is precisely the type that the US CLOUD Act makes accessible to US government authorities if processed through US-based cloud providers.

This is not a theoretical risk. For wealth managers with US clients, US beneficial ownership interests, or US-connected assets, the CLOUD Act exposure is immediate.

Swiss-hosted alternatives for AI tools processing client financial data (FABIAN for enterprise; CASUS and Meikai for SMB-accessible options) keep data within Swiss jurisdiction. For enterprise-level wealth management AI tools, FABIAN's ISO 27001:2022 certification and Swiss-only data processing provides the highest available assurance.

The question for wealth managers: does your AI tool vendor have a DPA that keeps your clients' financial data in Switzerland? If the answer is no or unknown, that is a FADP compliance gap.

## A Minimal Viable AI Governance Framework for Wealth Managers

You do not need a 50-page policy document. You need five things:

1. **AI Tool Inventory**: A documented list of every AI tool used in your firm, what data it accesses, and who is responsible for its outputs. This is your starting point for both FADP and FATF compliance.

2. **Data Processing Agreements**: Signed DPAs with every AI vendor processing client financial data. Confirm Swiss or EU data residency. Confirm no training data use of client information.

3. **Human Accountability Chain**: For every AI tool, document who is the human responsible party — the person accountable to FINMA for the AI's outputs. This person must be FINMA-licensed.

4. **Agentic AI Assessment**: Determine whether any AI tool in your firm takes autonomous action (without human approval at each step). If yes, conduct a DPIA and establish a documented human oversight procedure before continuing deployment.

5. **FATF Documentation Readiness**: Ensure you can answer the five FATF examination questions above. If you cannot, build the documentation now — before the examiners ask.
