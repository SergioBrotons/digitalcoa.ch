### Finding 01: The Incremental Accumulation Without Governance Decision Problem

**Core Insight:**
The primary structural problem for Geneva independent practices is not tool selection — it's AI accumulation that preceded governance choice. AI arrived through practice management system updates, template automations, and staff-initiated tool adoption before the physician made any conscious governance decision. The physician is now personally accountable for AI usage they did not authorize because the AI arrived before governance existed to govern it.

**Ready-to-Use Language:**
> "You didn't choose AI. It came through updates you approved without reading. Now you're responsible for AI in records you signed your name to."

**Distinct From C214–C288 Finding:**
This naming of "incremental accumulation as primary structural problem distinct from tool selection" is new. Prior research named AI adoption, AI governance gaps, and vendor contract liability — but this finding specifically isolates the mechanism of pre-governance AI arrival creating retrospective accountability for unauthorized usage.

**Implication:**
The first actionable question is not "which AI tool should I choose" — it is "what is the AI accountability state of every record in active use bearing my name today." A documentation architecture audit of AI-assisted records in current use is the immediate priority.

### Finding 02: The Swissdamed Classification Inaccessibility Problem

**Core Insight:**
Swissdamed registration requirements have active timelines. The classification criteria (Tier 1/Tier 2) were designed for medical device manufacturers, not independent physician practices. The framework is structurally inapplicable to independent practice operations, producing compliance paralysis rather than compliance resolution. Independent practices are potentially non-compliant without having a pathway to determine compliance status.

**Ready-to-Use Language:**
> "Swissdamed has real deadlines and categories that don't describe what you do. Tier 1 and Tier 2 were designed for device manufacturers. You provide outpatient care."

**Distinct From C214–C288 Finding:**
The specific naming of Swissdamed accessibility failure at independent practice scale — as a classification framework structural mismatch producing compliance paralysis — is new. Prior research referenced Swissdamed in general terms as a regulatory requirement without isolating the specific mechanism of manufacturer-oriented criteria creating independent practice inaccessibility.

**Implication:**
Swissdamed classification navigation calibrated to independent physician practice is a discrete service category that does not exist in the Geneva market. This is an immediate actionable offering: physician-scale Swissdamed classification determination service.

### Finding 03: The FMH Operational Endpoint Absence Problem

**Core Insight:**
FMH physician ethics principles are correct in principle and operationally empty at independent practice scale. The guidance does not define what "appropriate oversight" means for a 2-to-10 physician practice where the same individual simultaneously holds the roles of AI user, compliance officer, and billing administrator. FMH oversight obligations land in a structural void because the principle provides no operational endpoint.

**Ready-to-Use Language:**
> "FMH says you need appropriate oversight. It doesn't define what oversight looks like when you're the only person making decisions and you're also responsible for everything."

**Distinct From C214–C288 Finding:**
The specific isolation of FMH principle-without-operational-endpoint as structural compliance theater at independent practice scale is new. Prior research referenced FMH guidance in general terms. This finding isolates the specific mechanism: the same person holding user/compliance/billing roles simultaneously makes the oversight obligation architecturally impossible to satisfy with the existing guidance structure.

**Implication:**
A documentation-based FMH oversight protocol specifically designed for dual-role independent physician practices is the required deliverable. The protocol must work within the actual structural constraints of small practice operations, not present an idealized governance architecture.

### Finding 04: The Medical Record AI Attribution Evidentiary Gap

**Core Insight:**
AI-generated content appears in medical records under the physician's authoritative name. The records are legally authoritative in every sense. The AI's analytical contribution to those records is zero-documented. Retroactive reconstruction of AI contribution to physician-signed medical records is structurally impossible. The physician's evidentiary position on every AI-assisted record signed in the past 24 months is: "My name is on this record. I cannot explain the AI's contribution to this record."

**Ready-to-Use Language:**
> "You signed records with AI-assisted content. You can't reconstruct what the AI contributed versus what you decided. Your name is on the record. That's all that exists."

**Distinct From C214–C288 Finding:**
The specific naming of medical record AI authorship ambiguity as present-tense evidentiary exposure at the point of physician signature — distinct from general attribution problems — is new. Prior research addressed AI attribution in consulting, finance, and legal contexts. This finding specifically isolates the medical record as different: medical records are legally authoritative in a categorically different way than client reports or financial recommendations, and the evidentiary gap persists regardless of whether the physician made a correct clinical decision.

**Implication:**
A medical record AI attribution protocol is required infrastructure, not documentation improvement. The protocol must establish at time of record creation: what did the physician document, what did the AI contribute analytically, what did the physician review and decide — before the record is signed.

### Finding 05: The Carrier Attestation Theater Problem

**Core Insight:**
Carrier AI attestation forms are designed for carrier risk evaluation, not physician professional liability protection. Signing the attestation satisfies the carrier's documentation requirement while potentially confirming the physician's exposure. The documentation gap is structural: what satisfies carrier underwriting does not equal what protects professional liability position. No pre-signing review service exists in the Geneva market. Physicians sign personal liability instruments written by carrier legal teams without independent analysis.

**Ready-to-Use Language:**
> "You signed the carrier form because that's what renewal requires. You didn't analyze what you committed to personally. The form was designed to evaluate carrier risk, not yours."

**Distinct From C214–C288 Finding:**
The specific identification of carrier attestation as de facto operative standard through structural absence of pre-signing review — distinct from "carrier pre-signing review" as general service gap — is new. This finding specifically isolates why the carrier form fails the physician: it is designed for underwriting risk evaluation, not professional liability protection, and no market mechanism exists to identify the gap before signature.

**Implication:**
Pre-signing carrier AI attestation review is the highest-conversion entry point for Geneva independent practice AI accountability services. The physician is already in the carrier form — the documentation is already being built — the moment of carrier renewal is the acute accountability occasion.

### Finding 06: The Institutional Intelligence Inaccessibility Problem

**Core Insight:**
Geneva institutional actors (CHUV, HUG, cantonal hospitals, institutional clinical networks) are forming effective AI governance standards through adequately resourced internal processes. These standards are not visible through FMH channels or Bern doctrine. Independent practices observe institutional caution from the outside without access to institutional methodology. The standards forming in institutional contexts will flow down to independent practice accountability evaluation. Independent practices are building governance positions in isolation from the standards forming around them.

**Ready-to-Use Language:**
> "CHUV is being careful. You see that. You don't know how. The standards forming in those rooms will be the standards applied to you. You're building without the intelligence."

**Distinct From C214–C288 Finding:**
The specific naming of institutional risk signal inaccessibility at independent practice scale as structural positioning disadvantage — distinct from general institutional observation — is new. Prior research referenced institutional caution. This finding specifically isolates the competitive disadvantage created by the intelligence gap: the best-resourced institutions are building the most stringent standards and not transmitting the methodology.

**Implication:**
An intelligence function tracking Geneva institutional AI governance decisions — and translating them into proportionate guidance for independent practices — is a discrete service category. The information economy is structured so that most exposed principals have least access to the intelligence that would help them.
