# 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Complaints, Failures

## Phase 04 C289 | Geneva Healthcare ICP | 2026-05-28

---

## NET-NEW Finding 01: The Incremental Accumulation Problem

### The Mechanism
Independent physicians did not adopt AI through a governance decision. AI accumulated incrementally through practice management system updates, template automations, staff-initiated tool adoption, and embedded features in existing software. By the time the physician recognizes AI is present, they are already responsible for usage they did not consciously authorize.

### Current Problem in ICP Language
> "I didn't choose AI. It came through updates I approved without reading. My staff started using tools I never evaluated. Now I sign records that contain AI-assisted content, and I'm told I'm responsible."

### Why This Is Acute Now
- Patient records with AI-assisted content are now active legal documents
- The physician's name is on records with undocumented AI analytical contribution
- Incidents involving those records create retrospective accountability exposure
- No methodology exists for independent practices to audit their accumulated AI surface

### Market Complaint
"That update last year — I don't even know what it changed. But the records it touched are my records."

## NET-NEW Finding 02: The Swissdamed Classification Paralysis

### The Mechanism
Swissdamed classification criteria are written for medical device manufacturers, not independent physician practices. The Tier 1/Tier 2 distinction has operational consequences that independent practices cannot evaluate because the criteria do not describe their situation. Compliance paralysis results from a structurally inapplicable framework.

### Current Problem in ICP Language
> "Swissdamed has categories but I can't find my category. The descriptions seem to assume I manufacture something. I provide outpatient care. I don't know which tier applies to me, or if I need to register at all."

### Why This Is Acute Now
- Swissdamed registration timelines are active
- Practices are operating without knowing whether they are in compliance
- The classification question has operational consequences for AI tool deployment
- No Geneva-calibrated methodology translates manufacturer-oriented criteria for physician practices

### Market Complaint
"I've read the Swissdamed guidance three times. The categories don't describe what I do. Either I'm not in scope, or I'm supposed to do something that isn't explained."

## NET-NEW Finding 03: The FMH Principle Without Operational Endpoint

### The Mechanism
FMH physician ethics principles require appropriate AI use and human oversight, but do not define what constitutes adequate oversight at the level of an independent practice.

### Current Problem in ICP Language
> "FMH says AI must be used appropriately and that I need to maintain oversight. But I've read the guidance and I still don't know what oversight looks like on a Tuesday morning with 30 patients. Who determines if my oversight is sufficient? How would I know if I'm doing it wrong?"

### Why This Is Acute Now
- FMH guidance produces appearance of compliance without operational substance
- One person simultaneously holds user, compliance, and billing roles at small-practice scale
- Carrier forms ask about AI governance frameworks — FMH's own guidance does not translate to documentation requirements
- No Geneva-calibrated methodology operationalizes FMH principles for small independent practices

### Market Complaint
"The FMH puts out principles. Principles are correct and useless. I need to know what documentation to produce, what process to follow, and what happens if I'm wrong."

## NET-NEW Finding 04: The Medical Record Authorship Ambiguity

### The Mechanism
AI-generated content appears in medical records under the physician's authoritative name. The records are legally authoritative. The AI's analytical contribution to those records is not documented. When an incident occurs, the physician cannot reconstruct how AI contributed to the recorded clinical assessment.

### Current Problem in ICP Language
> "I'm signing records every day. Some of what I signed was generated by AI. Not ghostwritten by an assistant — generated by a model I haven't evaluated. When something goes wrong, how do I explain what I knew, what the AI suggested, and what I decided?"

### Why This Is Acute Now
- AI-generated content in medical records creates present-tense evidentiary exposure
- Retroactive reconstruction of AI analytical contribution is structurally impossible
- The legal standard asks what the physician documented, not what the physician intended
- nFADP DPIA obligations for AI-assisted documentation remain unmapped for small practices

### Market Complaint
"I've been using AI-assisted records for two years. If someone questions a record from last year, I can't explain what the AI contributed versus what I decided. I signed it. That's all I can say."

## NET-NEW Finding 05: The Carrier Attestation Theater Problem

### The Mechanism
Carrier AI attestation forms are designed to solve carrier risk evaluation, not physician professional liability protection. Signing the attestation satisfies the carrier's documentation requirement while leaving the physician's actual AI accountability exposure unaddressed.

### Current Problem in ICP Language
> "The carrier renewal has an AI governance section. I answered the questions by checking boxes. I have no idea if what I checked accurately represents my practice. I signed it. That was the requirement."

### Why This Is Acute Now
- Carrier attestations now require personal physician signature with personal liability implications
- No pre-signing review service exists in the Geneva market for carrier AI attestation forms
- The documentation that satisfies the carrier may confirm exposure rather than mitigate it
- Independent practices are signing personal liability instruments written by carrier legal teams without independent review

### Market Complaint
"I signed the carrier form because that's what you do at renewal. I didn't review it with anyone. I didn't analyze what I was committing to personally. I don't know if I'm covered."

## NET-NEW Finding 06: The Institutional Risk Signal Inaccessibility

### The Mechanism
Geneva institutional actors (CHUV, HUG, cantonal医院) are making careful,slow AI deployment decisions through internal governance processes not visible to independent practices. Independent practices are making autonomous AI governance decisions without access to the operational intelligence available to better-resourced institutional actors.

### Current Problem in ICP Language
> "CHUV is being careful. I see that from the outside. But I don't know how they're being careful. FMH doesn't tell me. I make my own decisions. I think I'm being appropriate, but I have no way to know what 'appropriate' means in Geneva terms."

### Why This Is Acute Now
- Institutional AI governance decisions are forming effective standards without independent practice access
- The accountability expectations forming in Geneva institutional context will flow down to independent practices
- No Geneva channel transmits institutional AI governance intelligence to independent practitioners
- Independent practices are building AI governance positions in isolation from the standards forming around them

### Market Complaint
"I practice alone or with a small group. I see institutional decisions from the outside. I don't know what they know. I make my own choices. I don't know if my choices will be evaluated against institutional standards I've never seen."
