## NET-NEW Findings (6)

### Finding 01: Swiss-US Data Transfer Framework — DPF Adequacy + FADP Divergence Advisory
- Swiss FDPIC granted adequacy to EU-US Data Privacy Framework (July 2023) — substantially reduced compliance burden for Swiss companies transferring data to DPF-certified US companies; SCCs and BCRs no longer required as primary transfer mechanism
- FADP (Swiss data protection, in force 2023) and GDPR diverge on cross-border transfer rules — companies with dual CH/EU obligations face parallel compliance frameworks rather than unified approach
- US government surveillance risk (Section 702 FISA, Executive Order 12333) persists for sensitive data categories — Geneva financial institutions, pharma, and UN/IOs cannot assume adequacy eliminates transfer risk
- FADP Art. 16 adequacy standard vs. GDPR Chapter V adequacy standard are not identical — compliance officers need jurisdiction-specific analysis
- DPIA requirements for US cloud provider onboarding (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) remain mandatory for high-risk transfers under both regimes
- Geneva IO/NGO exposure: UN organizations, international NGOs headquartered in Geneva transfer significant sensitive personal data globally — DPF certification status of their US vendor ecosystem requires ongoing monitoring
- Advisory entry points: DPF gap analysis, FADP vs. GDPR transfer compliance audits, US surveillance risk assessment, DPIA drafting, Geneva IO data transfer governance

### Finding 02: Swiss Competition Damages — Private Enforcement + Follow-on Actions Maturing
- Swiss Cartel Act Art. 12a (2022 revision): follow-on damages actions now clearly admissible after WEKO infringement decisions — private enforcement landscape maturing significantly
- 5-year limitation period (from knowledge of damage/perpetrator/anti-competitive nature); absolute 10-year ceiling from infringement end — creates defined litigation window for cartel victims
- Geneva Commercial Court preferred forum: English-language proceedings available, internationally recognized judges, developed expertise in complex competition damages (automotive parts, financial benchmarks, freight forwarding)
- WEKO leniency program: immunity applicants gain priority but face ongoing civil litigation exposure — coordination between EU Commission leniency and Swiss WEKO leniency is critical for multinationals
- Passing-on defense available but practically limited in Swiss context — cartel overcharges tend to be absorbed rather than fully passed on
- Cartel insurance: growing niche — D&O and business interruption coverage for investigation costs and damages
- Advisory entry points: follow-on damages risk assessment, WEKO leniency coordination, damages quantification expert support, post-WEKO decision litigation strategy, dawn raid readiness training, cartel insurance advisory

### Finding 03: Swiss Corporate Governance — ESG Disclosure Convergence + Shareholder Activism Surge
- Climate Reporting Ordinance (Jan 2024): TCFD-based mandatory climate disclosures for large public interest companies — transition plan aligned with Swiss 2050 net-zero target required
- Climate and Innovation Act (CIA, Jan 2025): national net-zero framework creating mandatory intermediate targets — legally binding basis for climate disclosure accuracy
- CSRD convergence proposed (consultation closed March 2025): amendment to Art. 964a CO would introduce mandatory external audit of sustainability disclosures, ESRS reference, broader company scope — Swiss multinationals face EU CSRD + Swiss CO parallel obligations
- SIX DCG (2023 revision) and SIX Management Transactions Directive (Feb 2024): updated disclosure obligations for listed companies
- Shareholder activism surge: nearly 20 publicly known activist campaigns at Swiss companies in 2024 — including large-cap targets; 2023 company law reform (lowered agenda item thresholds) emboldening activists
- Ethos Foundation (Geneva-based responsible investment): actively challenging executive pay, board elections, sustainability disclosure quality via voting power
- Advisory entry points: CSRD/ESRS gap analysis, CIA transition plan development, non-financial reporting audit preparation, activist defense strategy, AGM governance advisory, ESG disclosure quality review, greenwashing risk mitigation

### Finding 04: Switzerland-Russia Sanctions — CHF 7.4bn Frozen Assets + Divestment Deadline
- CHF 7.4 billion in frozen Russian assets in Switzerland as of April 2025 (up from CHF 5.8bn in April 2024) — including 14 real estate assets; significant increase driven by EU 15th-18th sanctions package alignment
- Switzerland adopted EU 15th-18th sanctions packages through 2024-2025: shadow fleet vessel bans, extended divestment deadlines, strengthened court ruling regulations, price cap lowering, software/service restrictions
- UKRO Art. 30a/28e licensing grounds: divestment deadline December 31, 2025 — creating urgency for Swiss companies with Russian operations or assets
- SECO enforcement active: annual reporting obligation (Feb 15 deadline), fines for negligent breaches, penalty proceedings initiated throughout 2024-2025
- Magnitsky case ruling (Feb 2025): Swiss Supreme Court permitted unfreezing of CHF 14 million — created legal uncertainty on scope of sanctions enforcement; further challenge cases expected
- CH-EU alignment risk: Swiss alignment with EU is political, not automatic — Federal Council decides independently; future divergence possible if CH-EU institutional framework negotiations fail
- Advisory entry points: SECO annual reporting compliance, UKRO divestment licensing applications (Dec 2025 deadline), sanctions exposure audits, Russia-related transaction screening, frozen asset management compliance, shadow fleet/services ban compliance, CH-EU alignment monitoring

### Finding 05: Swiss M&A Transaction Disputes — Post-Closing Mechanisms + Earn-out Litigation
- Switzerland significant mid-market and large-cap M&A hub — pharmaceuticals, commodities, financial services, luxury goods sectors dominant; Geneva/Zurich seat most cross-border transactions
- Locked-box pricing increasingly preferred over closing accounts — reduces post-closing disputes but creates different risk allocation; MAC clauses under Swiss law apply high threshold (COVID-19 MAC claims generally failed in Swiss-seated arbitrations)
- Earn-out disputes: most common post-closing dispute category in Swiss biotech/pharma acquisitions — valuation methodology disagreements, regulatory milestone achievement, accounting interpretation
- W&I insurance market growing: Geneva brokers and London-market insurers active; claims frequency increasing as policy wordings tested in disputes
- SECO merger control (Cartel Act Art. 10): clearance conditions increasingly subject to dispute where parties disagree on remedy scope
- Swiss Arbitration Centre (new trust/estate/foundation supplemental rules in force July 2025): Geneva-seated arbitration preferred for M&A disputes; English language, interim measures via Swiss courts (Art. 183 lex arbitri), 170+ New York Convention state enforcement
- Advisory entry points: SPA drafting (locked-box, MAC, earn-out mechanisms), post-closing dispute prevention, earn-out mechanism design, W&I insurance policy review and claims support, SECO merger control strategy, post-closing arbitration and litigation management

### Finding 06: Swiss Employment Law — Pay Equity + mHRDD + Platform Work Compliance
- Gender pay equity (Art. 416 OR): employers with ≥100 employees must conduct internal pay equity analysis every 4 years — first major deadline June 2024; results confidential but aggregated government analysis published; strengthened GEA (2024 reform) shifted burden of proof to employer
- EU Pay Transparency Directive (2023): not directly applicable to Switzerland but Swiss multinationals with EU subsidiaries face parallel compliance — dual Swiss/EU pay equity framework emerging
- Responsible Business Initiative (RBI) / counter-proposal (in force 2025): Swiss companies with ≥1,000 global employees must conduct human rights and environmental due diligence, report, establish complaints mechanism — Geneva multinationals (commodity traders, financial institutions) significantly affected
- mHRDD cross-border overlap: Swiss RBI overlaps with EU CSDDD — Swiss companies with EU operations face dual due diligence obligations on identical supply chains
- EU Platform Work Directive (Dec 2024, transposition ongoing): algorithmic transparency, worker reclassification presumption — applies to Swiss platforms serving EU market; Geneva-based platforms particularly exposed
- Geneva-specific: cantonal CCT compliance (construction, hospitality, security), frontalier remote work L-permit implications, OR Art. 328 home workstation obligations
- Advisory entry points: gender pay equity analysis and 4-year reporting cycle, RBI/mHRDD readiness framework, EU Pay Transparency gap analysis, platform worker classification risk assessment, cross-border employment structuring, HR due diligence in M&A
