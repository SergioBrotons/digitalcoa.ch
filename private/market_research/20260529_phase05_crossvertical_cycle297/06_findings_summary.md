# Findings Summary: Cross-Vertical Synthesis — C297 Phase 05

## 6 NET-NEW Findings: Distinct from C214–C296

---

### Finding 01 — Vendor Consolidation Accountability Debt: The Silent Accumulation

**The finding:** AI vendor acquisition, merger, and end-of-life events in 2026–2027 are creating a specific liability window that has already opened. Outputs from discontinued tools remain in client files — under the professional's name and accountability — while the vendor no longer exists to provide documentation, configuration records, or incident support. No Geneva authority has published guidance on this. No vendor contract requires it. The liability has already been created. The protocol for managing it does not exist.

**Mechanism:** Law firm: Claude outputs in briefs, vendor acquired, configuration records gone, no reconstruction capability. Wealth manager: portfolio construction AI discontinued, algorithm parameters unrecoverable, client files contain results from a system that no longer exists. Consulting firm: AI-assisted deliverable from tool that shut down, mandate-period output undocumentable under Art.398 CO. Healthcare practice: AI-generated documentation from tool no longer supported, patient records contain outputs from a system with no available audit trail.

**Cross-vertical universality:** Every vertical uses AI tools from vendors who will not exist in their current form in 24 months. Every vertical has outputs in client files from tools that no longer exist. Every vertical lacks a protocol for managing the discontinuity.

**Implication:** Vendor consolidation is not a future risk — it is a present-tense accountability debt. The outputs are already in the files. The liability has already attached. The protocol for managing the gap does not exist.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The AI tool that produced some of your client file outputs no longer exists in the form you contracted. The liability for those outputs is yours. There's no protocol for managing this."

### Finding 02 — AI Vendor Classification Paradox: The Compliance Entry Point Nobody Can Pass

**The finding:** Before a firm can execute a compliant nFADP Art.9 data processing agreement with an AI vendor, the firm must determine the vendor's legal role — processor, sub-processor, or independent controller. Modern AI vendors occupy a role no existing data protection framework was designed to assign: they process client data to deliver services, they train models on that data, they provide outputs that function as independent professional judgments. No Geneva authority has published AI vendor classification guidance. The standard DPA template has no AI vendor classification clause. Firms are stuck at the entry point: they cannot execute compliant DPAs because they cannot determine what they are signing.

**Mechanism:** The firm identifies an AI vendor it uses. The firm wants a compliant Art.9 DPA. The DPA requires the vendor to acknowledge its role. The vendor's standard terms describe the service, not the legal role under Swiss law. The firm's data protection advisor cannot confirm the vendor's classification because no Geneva authority has published guidance. The DPA is never executed. The obligation under nFADP Art.9 has been running since the first client data touched the vendor's system. The firm cannot document the compliance step because the classification framework does not exist.

**Cross-vertical universality:** Legal: AI brief drafting tools, contract analysis tools. Finance: portfolio construction AI, risk modeling tools. Consulting: AI deliverable tools, research synthesis platforms. Healthcare: clinical documentation AI, scheduling AI, diagnostic support tools. None have Geneva-published classification guidance.

**Implication:** The classification paradox is the entry point to nFADP compliance that cannot be passed. Resolving it requires either a Geneva authority classification list or a proportionate self-assessment methodology. Neither exists. This is a discrete product opportunity: AI vendor classification service with documentation.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You can't execute a compliant DPA with your AI vendor because no one can tell you what role to assign the vendor under nFADP Article 9. That's where the compliance work stops."

### Finding 03 — Cross-Client Commitment Proliferation as Structural Inconsistency

**The finding:** Small Geneva boutiques have made AI governance commitments to multiple clients, carriers, and vendors — across engagement letters, questionnaire responses, carrier attestation submissions, and vendor agreements — with no internal record of what they committed to, when, and to whom. The commitments were made by different people at different times for different purposes. No cross-reference exists. Consistency is structurally impossible without a live tracker. The inconsistency is not a governance quality problem — it is a data infrastructure absence. The fix is not better governance; it is a named data infrastructure: the commitment proliferation tracker.

**Mechanism:** In finance: boutique responds to institutional client's first-generation questionnaire in 2024. Same boutique signs carrier attestation in 2025 with different language on AI usage. Same boutique uses an AI vendor whose agreement contains yet another description of the boutique's AI governance. Three descriptions of the same reality, made to three different parties, with no cross-reference. When the institutional client circulates its second-generation questionnaire in 2026 comparing responses against peers, the boutique discovers its 2024 response has been functioning as live comparative intelligence for decisions it never anticipated.

**In consulting:** Client AI DDQ response describes AI methodology. Engagement letter contains different AI governance clause. Carrier form yet another version. Sub-consultant agreement silent on AI. Commitment made in one document may contradict commitment made in another. No mechanism for detecting inconsistency.

**Cross-vertical universality:** Every vertical that serves multiple clients or maintains multiple vendor relationships has this problem. It is structural, not operational.

**Implication:** The commitment proliferation tracker is a specific named data infrastructure. Most firms do not have it. No Geneva vendor offers it as a discrete service. This is the infrastructure gap behind the cross-mandate coherence paradox (C295 Finding 04) — specific enough to be a discrete product category.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You've made AI governance commitments to your clients, your carriers, and your vendors — probably inconsistently — with no single record of what you committed to. You can't audit your own consistency. That's the infrastructure gap."

### Finding 04 — Emergency Protocol Infrastructure Absence: Secondary Exposure Under Pressure

**The finding:** When an AI-assisted error is discovered, Geneva boutiques of every type face a 48–72 hour window before evidence disappears — browser history, session logs, AI tool activity records, model version states. No Geneva boutique of any type has a protocol for this window. The improvisation that follows creates secondary exposure: decisions made under pressure without documentation architecture, contamination of the record being reconstructed. In finance: portfolio loss discovered, AI recommendation was a factor, 60 hours later the evidence of what the AI actually recommended is gone. In healthcare: adverse clinical outcome, AI-influenced decision involved, medical record reconstruction at 72 hours with no AI documentation category. In legal: client matter error, AI-assisted drafting involved, no AI version record available. In consulting: client discovers AI-assisted error in deliverable, mandate-period AI model version unrecoverable.

**Mechanism:** Error occurs → 48–72 hours → evidence gone → improvisation → secondary documentation created under pressure → this documentation now functions as the record of what happened, not the original record. The secondary record is worse than no record because it reconstructs events with human memory and post-hoc logic, not AI system state at time of event.

**Cross-vertical universality:** Every vertical has incident response protocols for human errors. None have an AI-specific incident response component. None cover the 48–72 hour window before evidence disappears.

**Implication:** The emergency protocol is a discrete product concept — a 90-minute protocol design session for the specific 48–72 hour window. The product is not incident management; it is the specific infrastructure for preserving and reconstructing the AI decision record in the critical window.

**Ready-to-use language:** "When an AI-assisted error is discovered, you have 48 to 72 hours before the evidence of what the AI actually did is gone. Most Geneva boutiques have no protocol for that window. What they do instead creates the secondary exposure."

### Finding 05 — Attestation Adequacy Paradox: Documentation That Looks Compliant But Isn't

**The finding:** The carrier attestation form has a field for AI governance documentation. The firm has vendor-provided templates, training records, and policy documents. The field gets filled in. The documentation looks complete. The form gets signed and submitted. The firm believes it has demonstrated adequate AI governance. The carrier believes the firm has adequate AI governance. Both are wrong — and neither can tell. The documentation proves the firm has AI governance policies. It does not prove the firm has documented human authorization for each AI-assisted decision. The attestation form measures documentation completeness, not governance effectiveness. The gap between the form's adequacy criteria and the accountability standard's adequacy criteria is invisible to both parties.

**Mechanism:** Carrier form: "Describe your AI governance framework." Firm responds: "We have a policy requiring human review of all AI-assisted work. Staff completed training on AI tools in Q3 2025." Attestation signed and submitted. What the form does not require: evidence that a human authorized each specific AI recommendation before implementation. What the firm did not provide: the Human Decision Record for each AI-assisted decision. The documentation satisfies the form's criteria. The documentation does not satisfy the underlying accountability standard. The carrier cannot distinguish between the two because the form does not ask for the evidence that would differentiate them.

**Cross-vertical universality:** Legal: SAV guidelines require adequate human review. Attestation form asks for policy documentation. Physician: FMH implied standard requires human authorization for AI-influenced clinical decisions. Carrier form asks for training certificates. Finance: FINMA guidance requires evidence of human authorization for AI-influenced portfolio decisions. Institutional questionnaire asks for governance framework description. Consulting: Client AI DDQ asks for methodology description. None of these forms ask for the Human Decision Record.

**The paradox:** The attestation form creates an adequacy proxy — documentation completeness — that is structurally different from the accountability standard — authorization documentation. The proxy is visible. The actual standard is invisible. Both parties use the proxy as if it were the standard.

**Implication:** The attestation adequacy paradox is the mechanism behind the stampede effect: firms produce complete-looking documentation under time pressure, submit it with confidence, and are exposed without knowing it. The paradox is inherent to current attestation instrument design, not to firm behavior.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your attestation form measures whether you have documentation. The accountability standard requires evidence that a human authorized each AI decision. Those are different things. Your form doesn't ask for the second. You didn't know to produce it."

### Finding 06 — The Invisible Standard: Human Decision Record Required But Never Named

**The finding:** Every accountability framework across all four verticals implies the same unnamed documentation requirement: a contemporaneous record showing that a named human authorized a specific AI recommendation before it was implemented. This requirement is unnamed in every framework — SAV, FINMA, FMH, nFADP, carrier attestation instruments, institutional questionnaires. The frameworks do not ask for it explicitly. They require adequate governance and adequate human review, and the evidence of adequate governance is this record. But no framework names it. No form asks for it. No Geneva resource defines it. Firms do not know it exists as a specific documentation category. They build policies, they conduct training, they produce frameworks — and none of it produces the unnamed standard because it was never named.

**Mechanism:** The unnamed standard is the Human Decision Record: timestamp, named human, specific AI recommendation, explicit authorization decision, signature or equivalent authentication. The record must be made at the moment of authorization — not reconstructed afterward. No boutique process in any vertical currently produces this record. The record would be specific to each AI-assisted decision. It would be contemporaneous. It would be the accountable person's authorization, not a supervisor's countersignature after the fact.

**Why it is invisible:** Because no regulatory framework or attestation instrument names it explicitly. The requirement is implicit in "adequate human review" and "documented human authorization" — but the specific record type is never defined. This is why all the documentation produced in response to attestation forms looks adequate: it satisfies the visible criteria. The invisible standard is what would actually demonstrate adequate governance.

**Cross-vertical universality:** Legal: SAV adequate human review requires documented authorization of each AI-assisted brief, contract analysis, or legal research output. Finance: FINMA documentation of human authorization for each AI-influenced portfolio decision. Healthcare: FMH human authorization for each AI-influenced clinical decision. Consulting: Client methodology validation requires documented authorization of each AI-assisted deliverable component.

**Why this is the structural origin of the accountability gap:** Because every prior finding — delegation chain, stampede effect, documentation impossibility, wrong-standard compliance — traces back to this: the standard was never named. Firms could not produce what they did not know existed as a specific requirement. The naming is the product. The Human Decision Record is the specific documentation category. Defining it operationally for each vertical is the implementation.

**Implication:** The first actor to name this standard explicitly — in ICP language, in form design, in protocol architecture — converts an invisible requirement into a specific product. The naming is defensible. The methodology is buildable. The product is clear.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Every framework you've been asked to comply with requires the same thing. None of them name it. It's a record that shows a specific person authorized a specific AI recommendation before acting on it. You don't have one. No one told you that's what you needed. Now you know."
