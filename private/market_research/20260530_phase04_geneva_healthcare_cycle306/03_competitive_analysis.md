# 03 — Competitive Analysis: What Exists vs. What's Missing
## Phase 04 C306 | Geneva Healthcare | Independent Physician / Small Group Practice (2–10 physicians)

---

## Competitive Landscape: What Exists

### Category 1: EHR Vendor AI Governance Programs

**What they offer:** AI governance guidance embedded in EHR contract language, vendor-provided attestation templates, and periodic AI feature notifications through software update notes.

**What they don't offer:** Independent assessment. Vendor interests are structurally aligned with demonstrating AI governance adequacy — not with identifying gaps. Vendor programs create documentation artifacts that serve vendor risk management, not physician personal protection.

**Market gap they create:** Physicians believe vendor-provided attestation frameworks satisfy regulatory and carrier requirements. Vendor programs prevent independent accountability assessment by framing their tool as the governance solution.

**Why this is distinct from C305:** C305 named the EHR vendor post-sale activation problem (Finding 1). C306 extends this to name the vendor-provided attestation template ecosystem as a competitive category that prevents independent market assessment.

---

### Category 2: Medical Association Guidance (FMH)

**What they offer:** Human oversight principles, ethical guidelines for AI use, general positioning statements on AI accountability in medical practice.

**What they don't offer:** Operational definitions. No Geneva-specific protocol defining what FMH human oversight means in practice for a 2-10 physician office. No attestation preparation support. No evidence architecture model.

**Market gap they create:** The FMH guidance is sufficient to establish that a problem exists. It is not sufficient to establish what to do about it. Physicians are left with "you are responsible for human oversight" without any operational path to demonstrating it.

**Why this is distinct from C305:** C305 noted FMH principle without endpoint as a paralysis trigger. C306 extends this by naming the specific market opportunity: operationalizing FMH human oversight for independent practice is unoccupied territory.

---

### Category 3: Insurance Broker AI Risk Consulting

**What they offer:** Carrier attestation form preparation, coverage continuity guidance, general AI risk language for insurance renewal.

**What they don't offer:** Independent evidence architecture. Broker services are structurally constrained by carrier relationships — their advice must not contradict carrier positions. No multi-standard evidence mapping (carrier + FMH + Art.63). No operational documentation protocol for independent practice.

**Market gap they create:** Broker services reduce physician anxiety about coverage continuity while potentially increasing personal liability under FMH and Art.63 standards. The physician gets coverage continuity and potentially worse personal protection.

**Why this is distinct from C305:** C305 identified carrier attestation as a liability mechanism. C306 extends this to name the specific structural constraint that prevents brokers from providing genuine accountability architecture — their commercial relationships require carrier alignment.

---

### Category 4: General Data Protection Consultants (non-medical)

**What they offer:** nFADP compliance frameworks, data protection policies, processor agreement templates, general AI governance documentation.

**What they don't offer:** Medical-specific accountability architecture. General GDPR/nFADP consultants apply enterprise frameworks to independent practice without understanding: FMH professional accountability, Art.321 CP criminal scope, medical record integrity requirements, or the specific FADP Art.21 + Art.22 compound obligations in clinical documentation.

**Market gap they create:** Independent practices receive generic data protection frameworks that are structurally inapplicable to medical practice. The documentation produced satisfies general data protection requirements while creating medical accountability gaps.

**Why this is distinct from C305:** C305 named the FADP Art.21 + Art.22 compound obligation as a specific problem. C306 extends this by identifying the specific competitive failure: no existing competitor addresses medical-practice-specific FADP obligations at independent practice scale.

---

### Category 5: Swiss AI Governance Frameworks ( general, non-physician )

**What they offer:** SEFRI AI guidelines, algorithmic transparency frameworks, sector-specific AI guidance for institutional use.

**What they don't offer:** Independent practice adaptation. Available frameworks are designed for institutions with legal departments, compliance officers, and documented governance processes. No Geneva independent practice-specific guidance exists in any publicly available framework.

**Market gap they create:** The absence of proportionate, independently applicable guidance leaves Geneva independent physicians without a credible self-help path. The enterprise frameworks are not actionable; the actionable path requires external expertise.

**Why this is distinct from C305:** C305 identified solo practitioner operational inaccessibility as a universal market failure. C306 extends this by naming the specific structural reason: no existing framework is designed for independent practice scale and accountability structure.

## What's Missing: The Six Unoccupied Market Positions

### Gap 1: Independent AI Accountability Assessment (Non-Vendor)

**What it would do:** Provide independent, physician-advocate assessment of AI governance adequacy without vendor conflict of interest. Not aligned with carrier underwriting. Not aligned with EHR vendor risk management. Aligned with physician personal professional protection.

**Why no one does it:** Requires both regulatory expertise and medical practice operational understanding. The combination is rare. Most advisors choose one domain and provide services framed through that domain lens.

**Position to claim:** The only AI accountability service that is structurally aligned with the physician's personal professional interest — not with the carrier's underwriting interest, not with the vendor's product continuity interest.

---

### Gap 2: Multi-Standard Evidence Mapping

**What it would do:** Map physician AI governance documentation simultaneously to three accountability standards: carrier attestation requirements, FMH human oversight standard, and Art.63 FADP criminal standard. Produce documentation that satisfies all three without creating contradictions.

**Why no one does it:** Requires deep expertise in all three standard systems and understanding of where they are compatible and where they contradict. No existing advisor has published this mapping publicly or offered it as a discrete service.

**Position to claim:** The only service that maps AI governance documentation to all three accountability standards simultaneously — closing the evidence standard gap identified in C305 Finding 4.

---

### Gap 3: Medical Record FADP Art.21 + Art.22 Operational Protocol

**What it would do:** Provide independent practices with an operational protocol for: identifying AI-influenced decisions in clinical records, disclosing AI involvement to patients, handling Art.22 reversal requests for AI-influenced clinical decisions, and managing the conflict between Art.22 reversal rights and medical record integrity requirements.

**Why no one does it:** Requires understanding of both FADP legal obligations and medical record management conventions. No data protection lawyer understands the medical record context. No medical professional understands the Art.21 + Art.22 compound obligation in clinical documentation context.

**Position to claim:** First operational protocol for FADP Art.21 + Art.22 in clinical documentation context — specifically designed for independent practice operational constraints.

---

### Gap 4: Medical Practice AI Tool Discontinuation Protocol

**What it would do:** Provide a documented protocol for AI tool discontinuation: governance decision documentation, patient data handling after tool discontinuation, alternative approach documentation, and audit trail for the decision. Transform the highest-governance-awareness moment into the best-documented governance event.

**Why no one does it:** All existing governance frameworks address AI tool adoption. None address AI tool abandonment. The discontinuation decision — the moment of maximum physician governance engagement — is the least documented governance event.

**Position to claim:** First named AI governance protocol for tool discontinuation in independent medical practice — closing the gap identified in C305 Finding 5.

---

### Gap 5: Staff AI Delegation Supervision Framework

**What it would do:** Provide Geneva-calibrated operational definitions for physician supervision of staff AI use. Define what "adequate AI supervision" means, what documentation is required, and what the physician can and cannot be expected to review. Give the physician a defensible supervision structure.

**Why no one does it:** Requires understanding of both Swiss medical professional delegation standards and AI system decision logic. The combination does not exist in any existing advisory service.

**Position to claim:** First operational supervision framework for staff AI delegation in Swiss independent practice — naming and closing the accountability gap identified in C305 Finding 2.

---

### Gap 6: Post-Adoption Evidence Capture Architecture (for existing AI users)

**What it would do:** Address the specific problem of physicians who adopted AI tools before any governance framework existed (the majority of current independent practice AI users). Build forward evidence capture without requiring retroactive reconstruction of past decisions. Document current state and document forward.

**Why no one does it:** Existing AI governance services start at adoption and work forward. No existing service addresses the retrospective evidence gap for practices that have been using AI tools without governance documentation.

**Position to claim:** The only service designed for the majority problem (existing AI users without evidence architecture) rather than the minority problem (new AI adopters needing adoption documentation).
