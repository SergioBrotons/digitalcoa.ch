# 02 — Market Signals: Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures
## Phase 04 C306 | Geneva Healthcare | Independent Physician / Small Group Practice (2–10 physicians)

---

## Current Market Signals

### Signal 1: AI Tool Boundary Drift Without Governance Response

**The signal:** Independent practices report that AI tools initially adopted for one function (documentation, scheduling) have expanded into adjacent functions (diagnostic support, treatment recommendation, outcome prediction) without any practice governance decision. The boundary crossing happens through vendor product development, not through physician decision. The practice is now using an AI tool for purposes beyond what they evaluated when they adopted it.

**What this reveals:** The governance gap is not just about AI tools in use — it's about AI tool scope drift. The physician cannot enumerate what their AI tools do today because the tools have changed since adoption without notification.

**Market complaint:** *"L'outil est passé dehelp documentation àhelp diagnostic — je ne sais pas à quel moment ça a changé"*

**Why this matters for positioning:** This is the operational equivalent of C305 Finding 1 (EHR vendor post-sale activation) but for third-party AI tools — the mechanism is identical, the accountability consequence is identical.

---

### Signal 2: HIN Corridor Paradox

**The signal:** HIN (Health Info Net) certification is the Swiss healthcare data protection gold standard. Physicians believe HIN certification means their patient data is protected. What HIN certification actually protects is the transmission channel between covered entities. If an AI tool is interposed in that channel — or if the AI tool processes data before or after the HIN-protected transmission — HIN does not cover that processing. No Geneva independent practice has a documented understanding of where HIN protection ends and AI processing begins.

**What this reveals:** HIN is being used as an accountability alibi when it is actually only a transport security certification. The physician's mental model (HIN = data protection) does not match the actual scope of what HIN covers.

**Market complaint:** *"Le corridor HIN est sécurisé — nos données sont protégées"*

**Why this matters for positioning:** This is a market-wide false confidence signal. The physician believes they have data protection architecture when they have only transport security for one segment of the data flow.

---

### Signal 3: Emergency AI Use Creating Undocumented Exception

**The signal:** In urgent care settings within independent practices, AI tools are used without documentation of the AI's role in the decision. The justification is time pressure. The result is that the highest-stakes decisions — those made under time pressure — are exactly the decisions most likely to be made with AI input and least likely to be documented as AI-assisted. The documentation gap is structurally largest exactly where the liability is highest.

**What this reveals:** The documentation framework being proposed by vendors and carriers cannot be followed in the conditions where following it matters most. Emergency settings create a systematic exception to documentation requirements — the exception is invisible in the record.

**Market complaint:** *"On a utilisé l'IA pendant l'urgence — il n'y avait pas le temps de documenter l'utilisation de l'outil"*

**Why this matters for positioning:** This reveals that the documentation requirement is designed for planned, non-urgent settings. The real liability exposure — time-critical decisions — is exactly where the documentation norm cannot be met.

---

### Signal 4: Patient as AI Output Co-Author

**The signal:** Patient portals increasingly deliver AI-generated reports directly to patients. The patient then references or uploads that report back into the clinical record. The clinical record now contains patient-authored content that was generated by an AI tool, without any documentation of what the AI tool contributed to the patient-generated text. The physician is accountable for the record content regardless of how it entered.

**What this reveals:** The AI governance problem is not limited to physician-AI interaction. Patient-AI interaction creates AI-influenced content that flows into the record under the physician's professional authority — without any AI governance architecture applied to the patient-side interaction.

**Market complaint:** *"Le patient a reçu le rapport IA directement par le portail — maintenant le dossier contient quelque chose que le patient a généré via l'outil IA"*

**Why this matters for positioning:** This extends the accountability surface beyond the physician's own AI tool choices. The patient has become an uncontrolled AI governance variable in the clinical record.

---

### Signal 5: AI Interpretation Layer Accountability Gap

**The signal:** Laboratory results, imaging outputs, and diagnostic data are increasingly processed through AI interpretation tools before the physician sees them. The physician receives an AI-interpreted result, makes a clinical decision based on that interpretation, and documents the decision. The record shows the physician's decision based on the AI-interpreted result — not the AI interpretation itself. The accountability for the AI interpretation layer is unowned.

**What this reveals:** A second layer of AI decision support has been interposed between raw clinical data and physician decision, without any documentation of the AI's interpretive contribution. The physician's decision record contains an invisible AI contribution that the physician cannot review or correct.

**Market complaint:** *"L'outil IA a interprété les résultats du laboratoire — mais qui a interprété l'interprétation de l'IA ?"*

**Why this matters for positioning:** This names the layered AI interpretation problem as a specific accountability gap — distinct from simple AI decision support, this is AI-to-AI-to-physician decision chain where only the final human step is documented.

---

### Signal 6: FMH CME Credit AI Evaluation Chain

**The signal:** FMH continuing medical education requirements are increasingly administered through AI evaluation platforms. Physicians earn CME credits through platforms that use AI to assess competency, generate recommendations, and track progress. The physician cannot enumerate what AI systems process their CME data, what those systems conclude about their competency, or how those conclusions might flow back to FMH recertification processes. The AI evaluation chain is invisible to the physician.

**What this reveals:** Even when physicians are not using AI in clinical care, their professional credentialing is increasingly mediated through AI evaluation systems over which they have no governance visibility. The AI governance problem extends to professional identity management, not just clinical care.

**Market complaint:** *"Mon crédit FMC a été évalué par un système IA — est-ce que ça compte vraiment pour la FMC ?"*

**Why this matters for positioning:** This extends the AI accountability problem to the physician's professional standing and credentialing — not just clinical liability but career integrity.
