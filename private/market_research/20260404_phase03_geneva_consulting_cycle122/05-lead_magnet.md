## Primary Lead Magnet: Family Business AI Governance

### Title

> **"The Family Business AI Governance Gap: Why Swiss Family Companies Are Falling Behind — and What to Do About It"**

### Subtitle

> **A practical framework for family business boards and owners navigating AI adoption, AI risk, and AI governance — without hiring a full-time Chief AI Officer**

### Format Recommendation

**Primary format:** Long-form PDF report (8,000–10,000 words / 16–20 pages)
**Supplementary format:** LinkedIn article series (5 posts) derived from report sections
**Language:** English primary; French adapted version for Geneva distribution

**Rationale for PDF report format:**
- Family business owners and board members are high-information-decision-makers who value comprehensive, credible documents they can review offline and circulate to family council members
- PDF format enables sophisticated visual design (governance framework diagrams, risk matrices) that communicates seriousness and expertise
- PDF gating on a landing page creates an email capture mechanism that enables nurture follow-up
- A well-designed PDF report serves as a physical/digital business card — it is circulated within family networks and referenced at family governance meetings

---

### Target ICP

**Primary audience:** Owners and board members of Swiss family businesses (CHF 10M–200M revenue range) who are beginning to grapple with AI tool adoption by family members, employees, or advisors

**Secondary audience:** Family office principals and family wealth managers who want to understand AI governance for their investment portfolios and operating company holdings

**Decision-maker profile:** Family patriarch/matriarch, senior family member in management, or external family council chairman — typically aged 50–70, highly educated, digital awareness moderate to high, primarily French-speaking

**Pain as expressed by ICP:**
*"We use AI tools but I don't know if we have a policy. I worry about what happens if someone uses AI wrong and it creates a problem for the business or the family."*
*"My son/daughter joined the business and wants to bring in AI tools. The rest of the family doesn't know what to think."*
*"We've started using AI for investment decisions but I don't think we have a framework for understanding the risks."*

---

### Three Key Takeaways

**Takeaway 1: The AI governance gap in Swiss family businesses is structural — not a technology problem**
Family businesses lack AI governance not because they are resistant to technology, but because their governance structures were designed for a pre-AI era. Board agendas, family council terms of reference, and succession planning frameworks rarely include AI governance as a topic. This structural gap is what creates both the risk and the advisory opportunity.

**Takeaway 2: AI governance for family businesses has three distinct dimensions — and most advisors only address one**
The three dimensions are: (1) Business AI governance (AI tools used in operations, customer service, or internal processes), (2) Family AI governance (AI tools used by family members in managing family assets or family council functions), and (3) Portfolio AI governance (AI tools used in family investment decisions). Most advisors address only the first dimension. The family business AI governance advisory opportunity requires addressing all three.

**Takeaway 3: A proportionate AI governance framework for family businesses is achievable without a large internal team**
The answer is not "hire a CISO and an AI Ethics Officer." The answer is a proportionate, practical governance framework that fits the family's governance style, uses existing governance structures (family council, board), and sets clear boundaries on AI tool use without creating bureaucratic overhead. A well-designed framework takes 6–12 weeks to implement and can be maintained with quarterly review.

## Secondary Lead Magnet Concept: PMI Advisory

### Title

> **"UBS, Credit Suisse, and the Integration Lessons Every Geneva Financial Institution Should Learn Now"**

### Subtitle

> **A practical PMI framework for Geneva financial sector leaders facing — or considering — consolidation**

### Format Recommendation

**Format:** Executive briefing paper (3,000–4,000 words / 6–8 pages)
**Supplementary format:** Webpage article with CTA to full briefing
**Language:** English (primary) — financial sector audience in Geneva is comfortable with English

**Three Key Takeaways:**
1. **The UBS/CS integration proves that cultural integration failure is as costly as technical integration failure** — and Geneva financial institutions are now specifically seeking cultural integration advisory as part of PMI
2. **FINMA integration milestones create hard regulatory deadlines that cannot slip** — integration planning must build in regulatory approval time from day one
3. **Client retention during integration requires proactive communication and dedicated relationship management** — firms that waited to communicate with clients about integration saw higher attrition than firms with proactive client communication programmes

**Conversion path:** Download → email nurture → discovery call for "PMI readiness assessment"
