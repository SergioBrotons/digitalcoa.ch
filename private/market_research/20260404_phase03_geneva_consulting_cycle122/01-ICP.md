## Segment 1: Luxury / Hospitality ESG Supply Chain Advisory (C122-FRESH-01)

### Firmographics

| Attribute | Profile |
|---|---|
| **Sector** | Luxury goods (watchmaking, jewellery, leather goods, fashion accessories), luxury hotels and hospitality groups |
| **Headcount** | 50–300 employees |
| **Annual Revenue** | CHF 10M–200M |
| **Location** | Geneva (canton), Vaud (particularly watchmaking corridor: Le Brassus, Le Sentier, La Chaux-de-Fonds), Geneva city |
| **Ownership** | Family-controlled, privately held, or PE-backed with founder involvement |
| **Supply Chain Complexity** | High — multi-tiered, international supplier networks; Swiss manufacturing + international components |
| **ESG Maturity** | Low-to-moderate — sustainability narrative exists (brand values, provenance storytelling) but systematic data collection absent |

### Role Demographics

| Role | Profile |
|---|---|
| **Primary Contact** | CEO / Managing Director (often family owner or family-appointed professional manager) |
| **Secondary Stakeholder** | Head of Operations / Procurement Director (owns supply chain data collection) |
| **ESG Owner** | Often no dedicated ESG role — responsibility distributed across ops, communications, or finance |
| **Board Influence** | Family board members increasingly asking about ESG; external board members may raise ESG investor requirements |

### Pain Points

- **Cascading ESG requirements from EU clients:** EU luxury brands (the primary buyers of Swiss watch components and materials) are requesting supplier ESG data packages that the Swiss firm has no system to produce
- **Scope 3 data collection:** Category 1 (purchased goods) and Category 2 (capital goods) emissions data requires supplier engagement the firm has no process for
- **Provenance documentation:** Ability to demonstrate raw material provenance (e.g., gold, diamonds, exotic materials) is becoming a commercial requirement, not just a brand narrative
- **Investor ESG pressure:** PE-backed firms face portfolio ESG reporting requirements; family-owned firms face reputational pressure from family council or next-generation shareholders
- **No internal ESG team:** The firm lacks dedicated sustainability headcount; the work falls to operational leaders who have no ESG background
- **CSRD confusion:** Uncertainty about whether the firm is directly in scope vs. indirectly affected through supply chain pressure

### Buying Signals

1. Receiving ESG questionnaires or supplier declaration requests from EU-based clients
2. PE investor or family council requesting ESG data for the first time
3. Attending industry event where competitors are announcing ESG commitments
4. Preparing for a sale or succession where ESG due diligence is expected
5. Winning or losing a contract where ESG criteria were cited in the decision

## Segment 2: Swiss Family Business AI Governance (C122-FRESH-02)

### Firmographics

| Attribute | Profile |
|---|---|
| **Sector** | Diversified — manufacturing, trade, professional services, family office, real estate holding |
| **Headcount** | 10–100 employees (operating entities) |
| **Family Wealth Scope** | CHF 10M–500M in family-controlled assets |
| **Location** | Geneva canton, bordering French cantons (family cross-border living patterns) |
| **Ownership Structure** | Multi-generational family; often with family council, family assembly, or informal family governance |
| **AI Exposure** | Low-to-moderate — AI tools in use (often personally adopted by family members or employees) but no governance framework |

### Role Demographics

| Role | Profile |
|---|---|
| **Primary Contact** | Family patriarch/matriarch (founding generation), or senior family member in professional management |
| **Decision Influencer** | Family council or board with family member majority; external board members may raise AI risk |
| **AI Decision Maker** | Often the founder or a family-member CEO — no formal AI governance process |
| **Succession Context** | Often active succession planning or recently completed; next generation joining or preparing to join |
| **Risk Owner** | Family risk — AI governance is discussed as part of family risk management, not enterprise IT risk |

### Pain Points

- **No AI governance framework:** No documented policies for employee AI use, AI-generated content review, AI vendor due diligence, or AI risk assessment
- **AI risk to family assets:** Family office investment portfolios may use AI-driven investment tools without understanding AI risk; personal data processed by AI tools without family awareness
- **Succession and AI readiness:** Next-generation family members expect AI-native business operations; current governance structures have no AI competency or oversight plan
- **Board-level AI liability:** Family board members increasingly aware of AI liability exposure (output errors, IP issues, data breaches) but have no framework to govern it
- **Confidentiality paradox:** Family businesses often handle sensitive commercial and personal information; ad-hoc AI tool use by employees may be creating data exposure without the family's knowledge
- **AI governance as family governance extension:** The absence of AI governance is increasingly seen as a governance failure by next-generation family members pushing for formalisation

### Buying Signals

1. A family member or senior employee used AI tool and made an error visible to clients or counterparties
2. Next-generation family member joins the business and raises AI governance as a concern
3. Family office is evaluating AI-driven investment tools and needs an independent risk framework
4. Peer family businesses are publishing AI governance policies; board feels behind
5. Regulatory development (EU AI Act, Swiss AI guidance) is discussed at family council level

## Segment 3: Post-Merger Integration Advisory (C122-FRESH-03)

### Firmographics

| Attribute | Profile |
|---|---|
| **Sector** | Financial services: private banks, asset managers, insurance brokers, wealth management boutiques, fintechs |
| **Headcount** | 50–500 employees |
| **Annual Revenue** | CHF 20M–200M |
| **Location** | Geneva city, La Praille, Grand-Saconnex |
| **Post-Merger Context** | Either actively in integration (post-merger announcement) or anticipating consolidation decisions in the current market environment |
| **Regulatory Interface** | Subject to FINMA oversight; integration plans require FINMA notification and approval for material changes |

### Role Demographics

| Role | Profile |
|---|---|
| **Primary Contact** | CEO, COO, or newly created Integration Management Office (IMO) lead |
| **Board Sponsor** | Board or shareholder committee overseeing the merger or consolidation decision |
| **Integration Leader** | Often an interim executive hired specifically for the integration; may lack Swiss financial sector integration experience |
| **HR Lead** | CHRO or HR director managing workforce integration, social plan, and collective agreement implications |
| **Technology Lead** | CIO or IT director managing systems integration, data migration, and platform consolidation |

### Pain Points

- **Integration leadership gap:** The IMO lead or integration manager lacks experience with Swiss financial sector integration complexities (FINMA interface, Swiss employment law, industry collective agreements)
- **Cultural integration failure risk:** Prior UBS/CS integration challenges have raised awareness that cultural alignment is as important as technical integration — Geneva financial sector firms are actively seeking to avoid UBS/CS-style cultural mistakes
- **Regulatory milestone management:** FINMA requires specific integration milestones and approvals; missed milestones create regulatory risk
- **Workforce and social plan complexity:** Swiss employment law, CCT/CC application, and any existing social plan creates a constrained timeline for workforce integration decisions
- **Client communication during integration:** Managing client confidence, retention, and communication during an extended integration period — particularly sensitive for wealth management firms with UHNW client bases
- **Systems integration sequencing:** Core banking system, portfolio management, CRM, and reporting system consolidation requires careful sequencing; errors create operational and regulatory risk

### Buying Signals

1. Merger announcement or board decision to pursue consolidation
2. FINMA integration plan submission deadline approaching
3. Integration is behind schedule or over budget
4. Key client departures following merger announcement
5. Workforce integration difficulties surfacing (union pressure, departures of key staff)
6. Post-UBS/CS awareness: firm leadership explicitly referencing UBS/CS case as context for seeking experienced PMI advisory

## Segment 4: UN/IO Institutional Consulting (C122-FRESH-04)

### Firmographics

| Attribute | Profile |
|---|---|
| **Sector** | International organisations (UN family), NGOs (humanitarian, development, humanitarian), diplomatic missions, Geneva-based IOs |
| **Headcount** | 20–500 (Geneva delegation or country office) |
| **Location** | Geneva (Palais des Nations campus, buildings across Geneva: CICG, WTO, ICRC, WHO, UNHCR) |
| **Budget Profile** | Mix of assessed contributions (UN) and voluntary contributions (NGOs); CHF-denominated with USD/EUR revenue base |
| **Procurement Context** | UN procurement rules apply; Geneva IO procurement follows UN Financial Regulations and Rules (FRR); LTS/piggyback contracts common |

### Role Demographics

| Role | Profile |
|---|---|
| **Primary Contact** | Director of Administration / CFO / COO (institution's operational leadership) |
| **Programme Director** | For NGOs: programme director managing programmatic delivery and results-based management |
| **UN Reform Lead** | A director or senior adviser specifically tasked with reform implementation, often external consultant-embedded |
| **Procurement Officer** | Head of Procurement or Procurement Reform Officer — the operational buyer for consulting services |
| **Donor Relations** | For NGOs and agencies dependent on voluntary contributions: donor relations director managing donor reporting requirements |

### Pain Points

- **Efficiency mandates without resources:** UN reform directives require operational cost reduction but do not provide implementation resources; internal capacity is fully consumed by current operations
- **Procurement reform complexity:** Reforming procurement to reduce costs requires navigating UN Financial Regulations, LTS contracts, and political sensitivities around vendor relationships
- **Digital transformation funding gaps:** Cloud migration and ERP consolidation projects are approved in principle but funding and implementation resources are constrained
- **Results-based management (RBM) operationalisation:** RBM frameworks exist at policy level but are not operationalised in day-to-day programme management; staff need practical tools and training
- **CHF strength operating cost pressure:** IOs with USD or EUR budgets face real-terms budget cuts as CHF strengthens; consulting support needed to identify cost reduction without programme impact
- **Headcount rationalisation and change management:** Voluntary separation programmes (VSPs) require careful change management, communications, and organisational redesign support

### Buying Signals

1. New reform directive or efficiency mandate received from headquarters or governing board
2. Donor funding round where RBM outcomes reporting is a scored criterion
3. Procurement audit finding recommendations that need external implementation support
4. Cloud/ERP project stalled due to internal capacity constraints
5. Upcoming voluntary separation programme requiring change management support
6. Peer IO published a reform roadmap that the institution is being compared against

## Segment 5: Swiss SME ESG Reporting Automation (C122-FRESH-05)

### Firmographics

| Attribute | Profile |
|---|---|
| **Sector** | Manufacturing (precision instruments, specialty chemicals, food & beverage), trade and distribution, professional services |
| **Headcount** | 20–150 employees |
| **Annual Revenue** | CHF 5M–80M |
| **Location** | Geneva canton, French-speaking Switzerland broadly |
| **Customer Base** | Mix of B2B (institutions, corporates, other SMEs) and B2C; EU-based customers increasingly requesting ESG data |
| **ESG Maturity** | Very low — no dedicated ESG function; basic environmental data (energy, waste) may exist; no systematic ESG reporting |

### Role Demographics

| Role | Profile |
|---|---|
| **Primary Contact** | CEO / Managing Director (often owner or professional manager) |
| **ESG Responsibility** | No dedicated ESG role; distributed: CFO (financial data), Operations (environmental data), HR (social/diversity data) |
| **External Advisor** | May have existing relationship with an accountant or business advisor who is not ESG-specialist |
| **IT/Data** | Limited IT function; Excel-based data management; no dedicated ERP ESG module |

### Pain Points

- **Supply chain ESG pressure with no internal capacity:** Receiving ESG questionnaires from EU-based customers with no system to collect or report the requested data
- **First-time ESG reporting:** The firm is being asked to produce its first formal ESG data package and has no template, tool, or process
- **Affordable tooling gap:** Available ESG reporting tools are designed for large enterprises (SAP Sustainability, Workiva, Persefoni) — the SME segment has very few proportionate solutions
- **Data collection burden:** ESG data collection requires input from multiple departments (operations, HR, procurement) with no owner and no system for aggregating it
- **Scope 1/2 baseline challenge:** Many SMEs have not yet calculated their Scope 1 and 2 emissions, let alone the Scope 3 data being requested by customers
- **Proportionality uncertainty:** No clarity on what level of ESG reporting is sufficient to satisfy customer requirements without over-investing

### Buying Signals

1. Receiving first ESG questionnaire from a major customer or prospective customer
2. EU-based customer contract renewal includes ESG compliance clause for the first time
3. Business advisor (banker, accountant) raises ESG reporting as a customer relationship risk
4. Peer companies in the same supply chain are publishing ESG data; the firm feels behind
5. Preparing for a business sale or succession where buyer has ESG due diligence requirements
6. Financial institution (bank, insurer) requests ESG data as part of credit or insurance review

## Segment 6: Geneva Fractional GC / Interim Legal Advisory (C122-FRESH-06)

### Firmographics

| Attribute | Profile |
|---|---|
| **Sector** | Fintech, scaling professional services, family businesses in management transition, startups with Series A+ funding |
| **Headcount** | 30–150 employees |
| **Annual Revenue** | CHF 5M–50M (or funding stage equivalent for startups) |
| **Location** | Geneva city, La Praille, Lancy |
| **Legal Complexity** | Moderate-to-high — international contracts, regulatory exposure (FINMA, FINMA-adjacent for fintech), IP management, employment law, corporate governance |
| **Current Legal Setup** | Traditional outside counsel relationship (law firm); no in-house legal; volume and complexity beginning to exceed outside counsel efficiency |

### Role Demographics

| Role | Profile |
|---|---|
| **Primary Contact** | CEO or Managing Director |
| **HR/Education Coordinator** | CFO or Head of HR who would manage the relationship and coordinate legal matters |
| **Board Sponsor** | For family businesses or board-managed firms: the board is the decision maker |
| **GC Vacancy Context** | For firms in GC transition: CHRO, board chair, or CEO directly managing the vacancy |

### Pain Points

- **Outside counsel inefficiency:** Routine legal matters are expensive when handled by law firm partners; a large volume of smaller matters is not efficiently managed by traditional hourly billing
- **GC vacancy gap:** The firm is between GCs or has decided not to hire a full-time GC, creating a leadership vacuum for legal strategy and urgent matter management
- **Legal cost unpredictability:** Outside counsel billing is unpredictable; the firm wants more predictable legal support cost
- **Strategic legal input gap:** Outside counsel handles matters referred to them but does not proactively identify legal risk or strategy; the firm needs a legal thinking partner, not just a matter handler
- **Regulatory volume growth:** Particularly for fintech and regulated firms: the volume of regulatory questions (FINMA, AML, data protection) exceeds what the CEO/CFO can handle and exceeds what traditional outside counsel can handle efficiently at hourly rates
- **International operations complexity:** Geneva firms with international operations need legal leadership who understands cross-border contract management, international employment law, and jurisdiction-specific risk

### Buying Signals

1. GC departure or parental leave announcement — creating an immediate interim leadership need
2. Legal spend review showing outside counsel costs are high relative to matter complexity
3. CEO expressing frustration that "our lawyer only handles what we bring to them"
4. Regulatory enforcement action or near-miss in the firm's sector — triggering a legal risk reassessment
5. Rapid business growth creating legal volume that the current setup cannot absorb
6. Board or investor requiring formal legal risk governance that requires in-house legal leadership
