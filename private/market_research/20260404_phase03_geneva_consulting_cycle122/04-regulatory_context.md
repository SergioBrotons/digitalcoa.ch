## Regulatory Context 1: Luxury ESG Supply Chain Disclosure

### Primary Regulatory Drivers

**EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)**
- **Status:** In force; phased implementation from FY2024
- **Scope:** Large EU companies (listed and non-listed) — progressive phase-in through FY2026
- **Supply chain implication:** Obligated companies must report on Scope 3 emissions, including Category 1 (purchased goods and services)
- **Swiss relevance:** Swiss companies that are suppliers to EU CSRD-obligated companies face indirect disclosure requirements from their EU customers — not direct legal obligation, but commercial necessity
- **Double materiality:** CSRD requires companies to report on both financial materiality (how sustainability issues affect the company) and impact materiality (how the company affects people and environment) — creating a broader scope of disclosure than previous sustainability reporting

**EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD/CS3D)**
- **Status:** Adopted 2024; transposition into member state law pending
- **Scope:** Large EU companies — supply chain due diligence obligations
- **Swiss relevance:** Swiss companies in EU supply chains face indirect due diligence requirements: EU companies must conduct due diligence on their supply chains, which means requesting due diligence information from Swiss suppliers
- **This is the key regulatory driver for Swiss SME ESG:** Not CSRD direct applicability (which is paused in Switzerland), but CSDDD indirect supply chain pressure

**EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR)**
- **Status:** In force from December 2024 (with phased compliance deadlines)
- **Scope:** Operators placing commodities (soy, palm oil, coffee, cocoa, wood, rubber, beef) and products derived from them on the EU market
- **Swiss relevance:** Swiss companies importing these commodities or products containing them face obligations if they are supplying EU market — particularly relevant for Geneva trading houses active in commodities
- **Note:** EUDR is primarily a commodities regulation, but its due diligence framework is influencing ESG disclosure expectations across luxury supply chains

**Swiss ESG Reporting Context**
- Switzerland does not have a domestic equivalent of CSRD
- The Swiss Federal Council's position (June 2025): alignment with EU CSRD is paused, pending EU simplification of reporting requirements (the EU CSRD "simplification" is under discussion in 2025–2026)
- Swiss ESG reporting expectations are currently driven by: (1) EU supply chain pressure, (2) Swiss financial market ESG expectations (SIX ESG disclosure, FINMA climate risk guidelines), (3) voluntary frameworks (GRI, TCFD, SASB/IFRS Sustainability Standards)

### Regulatory Advisory Implications

The regulatory complexity creates an advisory need that is genuinely complex:
1. **Which regulations apply to our firm directly vs. indirectly?** — Many Swiss SMEs are uncertain about their regulatory status
2. **What level of disclosure satisfies EU customer requirements?** — Not full CSRD compliance, but credible, proportionate ESG data collection
3. **How do we collect supply chain data from our suppliers?** — This is the hardest part; suppliers may be unwilling or unable to provide the data
4. **Which ESG frameworks should we align with?** — GRI, CSRD, TCFD, SFDR — the landscape is confusing for non-specialists

## Regulatory Context 2: Family Business AI Governance

### Primary Regulatory Drivers

**EU AI Act (EU Artificial Intelligence Act)**
- **Status:** Published 2024; phased implementation from 2025; full application from 2027
- **Classification:** Risk-based framework — prohibited AI practices, high-risk AI systems, limited-risk AI systems, minimal-risk AI systems
- **Swiss relevance:** The EU AI Act applies to EU companies and to companies providing AI systems or placing AI-powered products in the EU market. Swiss companies with EU operations, EU subsidiaries, or EU-facing AI products may face EU AI Act obligations. Swiss companies that use AI systems provided by EU vendors may have downstream obligations. Switzerland is not required to implement the EU AI Act domestically, but Swiss companies serving EU markets will be affected.
- **Family business exposure:** Family businesses using AI-powered tools (CRM systems, accounting software, marketing automation, investment management tools) may be indirectly affected if those tools are classified as high-risk under the EU AI Act and the vendor does not provide adequate documentation.

**ISO/IEC 42001:2023 — AI Management System Standard**
- **Status:** Published December 2023
- **Relevance:** Provides a certifiable framework for organisations that build or use AI systems. For family businesses, ISO 42001 certification or alignment can serve as a governance framework and a market differentiator.
- **Swiss market status:** Adoption in Switzerland is emerging, primarily among larger enterprises and AI-intensive organisations. ISO 42001 is beginning to appear in B2B procurement qualification criteria.

**Swiss Data Protection (nFADP / revDSG)**
- **Status:** Revised nFADP in force since September 2023
- **Relevance for family businesses:** Family businesses that process personal data (customer data, employee data, family member data) must comply with nFADP. The revised nFADP introduced enhanced rights for data subjects, mandatory data breach notification, and requirements for automated decision-making (which includes some AI systems).
- **AI-specific concern:** If a family business uses AI systems that make automated decisions about individuals (e.g., AI-based hiring tools, AI customer scoring systems), the nFADP requires transparency about the automated decision-making logic and gives data subjects the right to request human review.

**NIST AI Risk Management Framework**
- **Origin:** US National Institute of Standards and Technology
- **Swiss relevance:** Used by Swiss companies with US operations, partnerships, or that serve US markets. Some Swiss family offices and financial sector family businesses are familiar with NIST frameworks through their US counterparties.

### Family Business AI Governance Regulatory Landscape Assessment

**Current state:** Switzerland has no comprehensive AI law analogous to the EU AI Act. Swiss AI regulation is sector-specific and guidance-based. This creates a specific dynamic for family businesses: they are not facing immediate regulatory compliance obligations (unlike EU companies facing EU AI Act requirements), but they are beginning to face AI governance expectations from:
- **Next-generation family members** who want to see formal governance
- **Investors or PE backers** who require AI risk management as part of governance standards
- **Commercial counterparties** who are themselves subject to AI regulations and may require AI governance from their partners
- **Insurance and banking counterparties** who are increasingly assessing AI risk as part of credit and insurance reviews

**Advisory implication:** Family business AI governance is primarily a risk management and governance best-practice advisory, not a regulatory compliance advisory (unlike, for example, CSRD compliance). This is important for positioning: the value proposition is risk reduction and governance improvement, not regulatory penalty avoidance.

## Regulatory Context 3: Post-Merger Integration Advisory (Financial Sector)

### Primary Regulatory Drivers

**FINMA Integration Oversight Framework**
- **Authority:** FINMA (Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority)
- **Framework:** Under Swiss banking law, FINMA requires notification and approval for material changes in banking organisations. In the context of a merger or acquisition, FINMA reviews the integration plan, approves integration milestones, and supervises the combined entity's risk management during integration.
- **PMI relevance:** Any merger or acquisition involving a FINMA-regulated entity (bank, insurance, securities dealer, asset manager) requires FINMA engagement throughout the integration process. Missed milestones, regulatory capital issues, or risk management failures during integration can result in FINMA enforcement action.
- **UBS/CS context:** FINMA has been actively supervising the UBS/CS integration, approving integration milestones, and publicly communicating about integration progress. This has made FINMA's integration oversight framework more visible and more of a reference point for other Swiss FI M&A.

**Swiss Merger Control**
- **Authority:** Swiss Competition Commission (COMCO) — for mergers above certain thresholds
- **Relevance:** COMCO merger control review may be required for larger Swiss FI transactions. The COMCO review process adds timeline and complexity to M&A transactions.
- **EU interaction:** For transactions with EU dimensions, EU Merger Regulation may also apply — creating dual regulatory process management.

**Swiss Employment Law Integration Constraints**
- **Collective Labour Agreements (CCT/CC):** Many Geneva financial institutions are subject to collective bargaining agreements that impose constraints on workforce restructuring, redundancy processes, and terms of employment during integration
- **Voluntary Separation Programmes (VSPs):** Swiss employment law makes involuntary redundancy complex and costly; VSPs (offering departing staff a financial package to leave voluntarily) are the standard integration workforce management tool
- **Consultation requirements:** Significant workforce reductions require mandatory consultation with employee representatives (workers' council or equivalent) under Swiss law
- **Timeline constraints:** The combination of consultation requirements, CCT constraints, and VSP negotiation processes means Swiss FI workforce integration timelines are extended compared to other jurisdictions

**FINMOSTA (Swiss Financial Market Supervision Act)**
- **Relevance:** Governs the licensing and ongoing supervision of financial institutions. Integration activities that affect the licensed entity's risk profile, governance, or internal control framework require FINMA notification and may require FINMA approval.

### PMI Regulatory Advisory Implications

The regulatory dimension of Swiss FI post-merger integration is a genuine differentiator for advisory positioning:
- Advisors who understand FINMA integration approval milestones, COMCO process, and Swiss employment law constraints can provide regulatory navigation support that generic PMI advisors cannot
- This is particularly valuable for acquirers from outside Switzerland who may be unfamiliar with Swiss regulatory requirements
- The advisory work is not primarily about understanding the regulations — it is about managing integration timelines, stakeholder communication, and risk management in a context where regulatory milestones create hard deadlines

## Regulatory Context 4: UN/IO Institutional Consulting

### Primary Regulatory Drivers

**UN Financial Regulations and Rules (FRR)**
- **Scope:** Govern financial management, procurement, and accountability for all UN entities
- **Key provisions:** Competitive procurement requirements, vendor registration (UNGM), financial authority delegation, audit and oversight
- **Reform implication:** The UN FRR creates a specific procurement framework that is distinct from corporate or government procurement. IO efficiency mandates often involve redesigning processes within the UN FRR framework, not replacing it.
- **Geneva IO applicability:** All UN agencies in Geneva (WHO, WTO, ITU, UNHCR, ILO, etc.) operate under the UN FRR or equivalent institutional financial rules. Procurement reform advisory must work within the UN FRR framework.

**UN Secretary-General Reform Agenda (UN2025 and Subsequent)**
- **Scope:** Efficiency mandates, decentralisation reform, management, development, and peace and security pillar restructuring
- **Geneva IO implication:** Geneva-based UN agencies are implementing reform directives from UN headquarters. This creates consulting demand in: organisational restructuring, process efficiency, digital transformation, and change management.
- **Progress:** UN reform agenda has been in implementation since 2023–2024; the consulting demand is ongoing through 2025–2026 as reform implementation moves into operational delivery.

**Donor Reporting and Results-Based Management (RBM) Requirements**
- **Mechanism:** Voluntary contribution donors (governments, foundations) increasingly require outcome-level reporting, not just output reporting
- **RBM framework:** UN entities have RBM frameworks at policy level, but operationalising these at programme delivery level is a known challenge
- **Consulting implication:** Designing and implementing practical RBM systems — indicators, data collection, reporting templates — is a specific consulting skill that donor-funded IOs and NGOs need

**Swiss Hosting Environment for IOs**
- **Host State Agreement:** Switzerland, as the host state for Geneva-based IOs, has specific obligations regarding IO privileges and immunities, working conditions for international civil servants, and conference facilities
- **Swiss regulatory interface:** IOs operating in Geneva are subject to certain Swiss regulations (safety, environment, employment law for local staff) while UN staff with diplomatic status are exempt from many Swiss regulations
- **Consulting implication:** Advisory work for Geneva IOs often involves navigating the intersection of UN internal regulations and Swiss host state requirements

## Regulatory Context 5: Swiss SME ESG Reporting Automation

### Primary Regulatory Drivers

**EU CSRD — Indirect Applicability to Swiss SMEs**
- Swiss companies are not directly subject to CSRD (the Federal Council paused alignment pending EU simplification)
- However, Swiss SMEs with EU customers face indirect CSRD pressure: EU companies must collect ESG data from their supply chain
- The Swiss cantonal chambers of commerce and trade associations are beginning to report member complaints about ESG questionnaire burden from EU customers
- The gap between "indirect pressure from EU customers" and "no Swiss domestic regulation" creates advisory opportunity

**Swiss Climate Disclosure Expectations**
- SIX Swiss Exchange has introduced ESG disclosure requirements for listed companies
- FINMA has issued climate risk supervisory guidance for financial institutions (following NGFS recommendations)
- Swiss pension funds (BVG occupational pension institutions) face ESG disclosure requirements under Swiss pension law (OPA/OPP2)
- These create a domestic Swiss ESG disclosure context that affects Swiss SMEs indirectly (through their institutional investor relationships and banking relationships)

**SwissCO (Swiss Commercial Code) — Sustainability Reporting Evolution**
- Swiss commercial law does not currently mandate ESG reporting for SMEs
- The Federal Council has signalled that Swiss sustainability reporting standards will evolve in alignment with international developments
- The uncertainty about future Swiss domestic ESG reporting requirements makes it difficult for SMEs to know how much to invest now

**Affordable ESG Reporting Frameworks for SMEs**
- **GRI Standards:** The Global Reporting Initiative standards are the most widely used sustainability reporting framework globally; GRI has an SME manual
- **VSME (VSME EU Accounting Directive small undertakings):** The EU has developed a proportionate sustainability reporting standard for small undertakings (VSME) under the CSRD; this is a practical framework for SMEs even outside the EU
- **B Corp Certification:** Some Swiss SMEs pursue B Corp certification as a way to demonstrate ESG credibility to customers and investors
- **Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi):** For SMEs that want to set emissions reduction targets; requires methodology guidance

### Proportionate Reporting Principle
The key regulatory concept for SME ESG reporting advisory is **proportionality**: the ESG reporting effort should be proportionate to the firm's size, complexity, and stakeholder requirements. For a 30-person Swiss SME, the ESG reporting burden should not replicate the full CSRD double materiality assessment required of a large EU listed company. The advisory role is to help the firm identify which ESG disclosures are actually required (by customers, banks, insurers, or future regulations) and design a proportionate data collection and reporting process.

## Regulatory Context 6: Fractional GC / Interim Legal Advisory

### Primary Regulatory Drivers

**FINMA Regulatory Landscape for Fractional GC ICP**
The primary regulatory exposure for the ICP (fintechs, scaling SMEs, regulated firms) is FINMA oversight:
- **Fintech licensing:** Swiss fintech firms requiring FINMA licensing (payment institution, asset manager, securities dealer) face complex regulatory requirements; fractional GC can provide ongoing regulatory leadership during the licensing process and post-licensing compliance
- **FINMA circulars and guidance:** Ongoing FINMA regulatory developments require in-house legal monitoring; fractional GC can provide this function
- **AML obligations:** FINMA- supervised institutions face ongoing anti-money laundering compliance obligations; fractional GC can provide AML governance and oversight

**Swiss Data Protection (nFADP) — Legal Compliance Angle**
- Revised nFADP in force since September 2023; enforcement is maturing
- Companies processing personal data at scale face genuine nFADP compliance obligations
- Fractional GC can provide ongoing nFADP compliance governance: data mapping, DPA management, breach notification

**Swiss Employment Law for Scaling Companies**
- Scaling companies face growing employment law complexity: CCT applicability, employee misclassification risk (especially for contractors), remote work policies, cross-border employment (Geneva-French border workers — frontaliers)
- Fractional GC can provide ongoing employment law advisory and template governance

**Corporate Law and Governance**
- Swiss company law (OR — Obligationenrecht) governs Swiss corporate governance; family businesses and scaling companies face governance obligations (board composition, AGM requirements, capital increases)
- Fractional GC can provide ongoing corporate governance advisory, particularly valuable for companies preparing for financing rounds or succession events

### Interim vs. Fractional: Legal Distinction
The regulatory dimension of interim legal advisory is important to distinguish:
- **Interim GC (covering a GC vacancy):** The interim GC is exercising full GC authority; the firm must ensure appropriate FINMA notification (if regulated entity) and that the interim GC has appropriate qualifications
- **Fractional GC (ongoing part-time):** The fractional GC provides ongoing legal leadership without the full authority or liability of a permanent GC; the commercial arrangement must be clearly documented (scope of authority, escalation protocols, liability allocation)
