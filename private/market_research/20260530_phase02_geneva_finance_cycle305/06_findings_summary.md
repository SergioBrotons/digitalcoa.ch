### Finding 01 — Institutional DDQ Is a Competitive Gate with Measurable Revenue Consequence

**The finding:** The institutional client AI governance due diligence questionnaire (DDQ) is functioning as a competitive gate in Geneva boutique wealth management. Firms that cannot produce adequate DDQ responses are losing mandates to competitors who can — not because of investment performance, but because the RFP scoring weights AI governance capability. The competitive consequence is active, measurable, and happening now. The losing firm often doesn't know exactly why they lost.

**Mechanism:** Institutional client sends DDQ → firm cannot answer most questions → RFP score reflects documentation gap → mandate awarded to competitor with documentation → firm doesn't know why they lost → loss attributed to price or relationship.

**Implication:** The institutional DDQ response file is a discrete service with a direct revenue consequence. The service is not "help me write a policy." The service is "produce the response file that wins the mandate." The competitive differentiator is evidence quality, not price.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The institutional client asked a40-question AI governance DDQ. You couldn't answer most of them. The competitor who won the mandate could. That's not a compliance gap — it's a revenue gap."

### Finding 02 — AI Vendor Integration Chain Creates Unmapped PII Processing Exposure

**The finding:** Boutique wealth management firms have connected multiple AI tools (portfolio analytics, CRM, risk profiling, rebalancing engines) without mapping the data flow between them. Client PII processed by one tool passes through integration connections to other tools and their vendor servers — often in non-Swiss jurisdictions. The firm cannot document which client data touches which vendor infrastructure because the integration chain was never mapped. nFADP Art. 26 and Art. 9 exposure is active, unmapped, and growing as more AI tools are connected.

**Mechanism:** Tool A (CRM) → Tool B (analytics) → Tool C (risk profiler) → vendor servers in different jurisdictions → client PII traverses the integration chain → no documentation of data flow → no documentation of which vendor processes what → Art. 26 and Art. 9 nFADP exposure active.

**Implication:** An AI vendor integration chain mapping exercise is a discrete service. The output is a documented data flow showing which client data touches which vendor infrastructure in which jurisdiction — the foundation for Art. 26 and Art. 9 nFADP compliance documentation. This is not a vendor security assessment — it is a data flow mapping exercise serving a different documentation purpose.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You connected five AI tools. Each one processes client data on vendor servers. Nobody mapped which data goes where. That's your integration chain exposure — and it's active."

### Finding 03 — FINMA Supervisory Expectations Have Shifted Without Notice to Evidence Standard

**The finding:** FINMA's interactions with boutique wealth management firms have shifted from "do you have an AI governance policy" to "demonstrate your AI oversight." Firms that prepared for the first-generation FINMA questionnaire are discovering that the standard has moved. The documentation they prepared for the previous expectation is not adequate for the current expectation. The shift from policy to records is active and firm-specific — not all firms have noticed.

**Mechanism:** 2024 FINMA questionnaire → firm produces AI governance policy → 2025 FINMA letter requests records → policy doesn't constitute records → firm scrambles → documentation produced under pressure is inadequate.

**Implication:** The firm that prepared for the first-generation FINMA questionnaire is underprepared for the current FINMA standard. Documentation architecture must be designed for the current standard (evidence of oversight capability), not the previous standard (policy documents). The current standard is records-first, not policy-first.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Last year FINMA asked if you had a policy. This year they ask you to demonstrate oversight. The standard moved. You didn't know it moved. That's the gap."

### Finding 04 — Carrier Coverage and Personal Criminal Liability Are Separate Legal Tracks

**The finding:** Managing directors are discovering that carrier insurance coverage and personal criminal liability protection under nFADP Art. 63 are separate legal tracks. Carrier payment of a settlement does not discharge personal criminal liability. The carrier's interest (subrogation protection) and the managing director's personal interest (Art. 63 protection) are not aligned. The same attestation cannot satisfy both. The structural misunderstanding is active and widespread.

**Mechanism:** Incident occurs → carrier pays settlement → carrier seeks subrogation against managing director → Art. 63 personal criminal liability exposure remains → carrier coverage did not protect personal assets → managing director faces criminal exposure personally.

**Implication:** An Art. 63 personal due diligence evidence package is a distinct document type serving the managing director's personal protection interest — not the firm's carrier subrogation interest. These require separate documentation architectures. The managing director who believes carrier coverage equals personal protection is operating under a structural misunderstanding with active personal exposure.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your carrier covers the settlement. Your carrier doesn't cover your personal criminal exposure under Article 63. Those are different legal tracks. The same attestation doesn't satisfy both."

### Finding 05 — No Methodology Exists for continuous AI Oversight Documentation

**The finding:** Boutique wealth management firms need to document continuous oversight of AI systems that operate 24/7. No Geneva-calibrated methodology exists for what "continuous oversight documentation" means in practice. Is it a weekly sign-off? A monthly review? A real-time supervision log? The methodology question is unanswered in the Geneva market. Firms are attempting to apply discrete documentation approaches (annual reviews, matter-based records) to continuous AI systems — a structural mismatch.

**Mechanism:** AI system operates 24/7 → oversight documentation is annual → structural mismatch between system operation and documentation cadence → carrier asks for oversight records → firm cannot produce records because the methodology doesn't exist → documentation gap is systemic, not incremental.

**Implication:** A continuous AI oversight methodology — defining what oversight means, how frequently it must be documented, and in what format — is a discrete service with direct demand. The methodology must be Geneva-calibrated (specific to Swiss regulatory context) and firm-size-calibrated (specific to 50–200 person boutique firms). This is not a template — it is a methodology development exercise.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The systems operate 24/7. Your oversight documentation is annual. There's a structural mismatch. I can give you a methodology that matches the documentation cadence to the system operation."

### Finding 06 — AI Governance Policy ≠ Demonstrable Oversight Capability

**The finding:** Boutique wealth management firms have AI governance policies. Institutional clients and FINMA are asking for evidence of oversight capability — not policies. The gap between "we have a policy describing what should exist" and "we can show records proving oversight actually happened" is now the named failure mode. The policy is not the problem. The inability to demonstrate capability through evidence is the problem. The service request is not "help me write a policy" — it is "help me produce evidence."

**Mechanism:** Institutional client asks for DDQ response → firm has AI governance policy → policy submitted → client asks for records showing oversight occurred → policy cannot substitute for records → firm scrambles to produce records retroactively → retroactive records are inadequate.

**Implication:** The service is not "help me write a policy." The service is "help me produce evidence of oversight capability." These are different deliverables with different production methodologies. The policy is table stakes — the evidence is the competitive differentiator. The market has moved from policy-first to evidence-first, and firms with only policies are losing.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You have a policy. The institutional client asked for evidence of oversight capability. A policy describes what should exist. Evidence shows what actually happened. That's the gap."
