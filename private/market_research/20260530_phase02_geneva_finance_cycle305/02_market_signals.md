## Phase 02 | Cycle C305 | Geneva Finance / Boutique Wealth Management

## Current Problems and Complaints (NET-NEW from C304)

### Problem 1: Institutional DDQ as Competitive Gate with No Response Capability

**The signal:** Boutique wealth management firms are receiving institutional client AI governance due diligence questionnaires (DDQs) as part of mandate evaluation. The RFP scoring criteria weight AI governance capability. Firms that cannot produce adequate responses are losing mandates they would otherwise win. The competitive consequence of inadequate AI governance documentation is now active and measurable — not theoretical.

**Mechanism:** Institutional client sends DDQ → firm cannot answer most questions → RFP score reflects documentation gap → mandate awarded to competitor with documentation → firm doesn't know why they lost → loss attributed to price or relationship.

**What they're saying:**
- "We lost the mandate. We were the low-cost option. The client chose the more expensive firm."
- "The DDQ was40 questions. We could answer five confidently."
- "The client wasn't asking about our philosophy. They wanted records."
- "We didn't know AI governance was weighted in the RFP scoring."

**Implication:** The institutional DDQ response file is a discrete service with a direct revenue consequence. Firms that produce the response file retain mandates. Firms that cannot produce it lose mandates to competitors who can.

---

### Problem 2: AI Vendor Integration Chain Creates Unmapped PII Processing Exposure

**The signal:** Boutique wealth management firms have connected multiple AI tools (portfolio analytics, CRM, risk profiling, rebalancing engines) without mapping the data flow between them. Client PII processed by one tool passes through integration connections to other tools and their vendor servers. The firm cannot document which client data touches which vendor infrastructure because the integration chain was never mapped. The exposure is active, unmapped, and growing as more AI tools are connected.

**Mechanism:** Tool A (CRM) → Tool B (analytics) → Tool C (risk profiler) → vendor servers in different jurisdictions → client PII traverses the integration chain → no documentation of data flow → no documentation of which vendor processes what → Art. 26 and Art. 9 nFADP exposure active.

**What they're saying:**
- "We connected everything and it worked. We didn't think about what that meant for data flow."
- "The CRM has client addresses, income, family details. The analytics tool pulls from that. Where does that data actually go?"
- "We need to map the integration chain. Nobody's done it."
- "Each vendor has their own terms. The integration between them creates something nobody's looked at."

**Implication:** An AI vendor integration chain mapping exercise is a discrete service. The output is a documented data flow showing which client data touches which vendor infrastructure in which jurisdiction — the foundation for Art. 26 and Art. 9 nFADP compliance documentation.

### Problem 3: FINMA Supervisory Expectations Have Shifted Without Notice

**The signal:** FINMA's interactions with boutique wealth management firms have shifted from "do you have an AI governance policy" to "demonstrate your AI oversight." Firms that prepared for the first-generation FINMA questionnaire are discovering that the standard has moved. The carrier Form v4 evidence requirements and the FINMA supervisory evidence expectations are now aligned in demanding actual records — not policy documents. Firms that prepared documentation for the first interaction are underprepared for the second.

**Mechanism:** 2024 FINMA questionnaire → firm produces AI governance policy →2025 FINMA letter requests records → policy doesn't constitute records → firm scrambles → documentation produced under pressure is inadequate.

**What they're saying:**
- "Last year FINMA asked if we had a policy. This year they asked for evidence of oversight."
- "We built what they asked for last year. This year they want something different."
- "The standard moved while we were preparing for the old standard."
- "We thought we were in compliance. Then FINMA sent a letter."

**Implication:** The shift from policy documentation to oversight evidence documentation is a named market signal. Firms need to understand what the current FINMA standard actually requires — and it is not the policy-first approach most prepared for.

---

### Problem 4: Carrier Coverage and Personal Criminal Liability Are Separate Legal Tracks

**The signal:** Managing directors are discovering that carrier insurance coverage and personal criminal liability protection under nFADP Art. 63 are separate legal tracks. Carrier payment of a settlement does not discharge personal criminal liability. The carrier's interest (subrogation protection) and the managing director's personal interest (Art. 63 due diligence evidence) are not aligned. The same attestation cannot satisfy both. Firms that believe carrier coverage equals personal protection are operating under a structural misunderstanding with active personal exposure.

**Mechanism:** Incident occurs → carrier pays settlement → carrier seeks subrogation against managing director → Art. 63 personal criminal liability exposure remains → carrier coverage did not protect personal assets → managing director faces criminal exposure personally.

**What they're saying:**
- "I thought the carrier covered everything. My lawyer explained — coverage and personal liability are separate."
- "The carrier protects the firm's balance sheet. Art. 63 is personal criminal exposure."
- "I can be personally liable even if the carrier pays the settlement."
- "Carrier coverage and personal protection — I thought they were the same thing."

**Implication:** An Art. 63 personal due diligence evidence package is a distinct document type serving the managing director's personal protection interest — not the firm's carrier subrogation interest. These require separate documentation architectures.

### Problem 5: No Methodology Exists for Continuous AI Oversight Documentation

**The signal:** Boutique wealth management firms need to document continuous oversight of AI systems that operate 24/7. No Geneva-calibrated methodology exists for what "continuous oversight documentation" means in practice. Is it a weekly sign-off? A monthly review? A real-time supervision log? The methodology question is unanswered in the Geneva market. Firms are attempting to apply discrete documentation approaches (annual reviews, matter-based records) to continuous AI systems — a structural mismatch.

**Mechanism:** AI system operates 24/7 → oversight documentation is annual → structural mismatch between system operation and documentation cadence → carrier asks for oversight records → firm cannot produce records because the methodology doesn't exist → documentation gap is systemic, not incremental.

**What they're saying:**
- "I need to oversee AI systems continuously. Nobody told me what continuous oversight documentation looks like."
- "Is it a weekly sign-off? A monthly review? A real-time log? There's no Geneva standard."
- "The systems operate24/7. Our oversight documentation is annual. There's a mismatch."
- "I can document a decision. I don't know how to document continuous oversight."

**Implication:** A continuous AI oversight methodology — defining what oversight means, how frequently it must be documented, and in what format — is a discrete service with direct demand. The methodology must be Geneva-calibrated (specific to Swiss regulatory context) and firm-size-calibrated (specific to 50–200 person boutique firms).

### Problem 6: AI Governance Policy ≠ Demonstrable Oversight Capability

**The signal:** Boutique wealth management firms have AI governance policies. Institutional clients and FINMA are asking for evidence of oversight capability — not policies. The gap between "we have a policy describing what should exist" and "we can show records proving oversight actually happened" is now the named failure mode. The policy is not the problem. The inability to demonstrate capability through evidence is the problem.

**Mechanism:** Institutional client asks for DDQ response → firm has AI governance policy → policy submitted → client asks for records showing oversight occurred → policy cannot substitute for records → firm scrambles to produce records retroactively → retroactive records are inadequate.

**What they're saying:**
- "We have a policy. The institutional client asked for evidence of oversight capability."
- "A policy describes what should exist. Evidence shows what actually exists."
- "The gap isn't the policy. The gap is that we can't show the oversight actually happened."
- "I can tell you what we do. I can't show you the records that prove it."

**Implication:** The service is not "help me write a policy." The service is "help me produce evidence of oversight capability." These are different deliverables with different production methodologies. The policy is table stakes — the evidence is the competitive differentiator.

## Market Failure Modes

### Failure Mode 1: Retroactive Documentation Under Institutional Pressure
Firm receives institutional DDQ → cannot respond adequately → scrambles to produce documentation under mandate deadline pressure → documentation produced under pressure is inadequate → mandate lost. The failure is not the documentation gap — it is the inability to produce documentation under deadline pressure because no systematic documentation architecture exists.

### Failure Mode 2: Carrier Attestation Without Evidence Production
Managing director signs carrier attestation → believes attestation satisfies obligations → carrier requests evidence at renewal → firm cannot produce evidence → attestation creates documented exposure (signed standard, no evidence to support it). The failure is the attestation architecture creating a documented standard the firm cannot demonstrate it meets.

### Failure Mode 3: Policy-First Compliance vs. Evidence-First Compliance
Firm prepares AI governance policy → believes compliance is achieved → FINMA or institutional client requests records → policy cannot substitute for records → firm discovers policy-first compliance and evidence-first compliance are different things. The failure is not the policy — it is the assumption that a policy constitutes evidence.

### Failure Mode 4: Integration Chain Expansion Without Mapping
Firm connects new AI tool to existing stack → integration works operationally → data flow never documented → client PII now flowing through new vendor infrastructure → nFADP Art. 26 exposure created by the connection → exposure exists without the firm's awareness. The failure is operational AI deployment without documentation architecture.

### Failure Mode 5: Carrier Coverage Misunderstanding
Managing director believes carrier coverage = personal protection → does not produce Art. 63 personal due diligence evidence → incident occurs → carrier pays settlement → carrier seeks subrogation → managing director has no Art. 63 evidence package → personal assets exposed. The failure is not the incident — it is the structural misunderstanding of what carrier coverage actually protects.
