## Executive Summary

The Federal Council's AI Action Plan First Draft arrives in May 2026. FDPIC enforcement is absent from the legal sector — for now. Individual AI adoption among lawyers has reached 69%, but only 34% of law firms have formal AI policies. That 35-point gap is your window.

This framework gives you:

**Time to act: before your first FDPIC complaint, not after.**

## Section 1: The Gap You're Sitting In

**69% of lawyers use AI tools individually.**
**34% of law firms have formal AI policies.**

That gap is not a statistics problem — it's a liability problem.

When a lawyer uses ChatGPT to draft a contract analysis and shares it with a client, several questions arise:
- Was the client informed AI was used?
- Was the AI tool's data protection posture assessed?
- Was a DPIA conducted for that tool?
- Does using that AI tool comply with lawyer confidentiality obligations?
- Is the lawyer's use of that tool consistent with the firm's (nonexistent) AI policy?

The 35-point governance gap means most small firms are exposed without knowing it.

**What FDPIC expects (confirmed by FDPIC itself):**
- Purpose: Why is the AI tool being used?
- Functionality: What does the AI tool actually do?
- Data sources: What data feeds the AI tool?

FDPIC Data Protection Day Jan 28, 2026 confirmed: FADP applies to AI-supported data processing **without new legislation** — it's already your obligation.

## Section 3: The Shadow AI Problem

**Shadow AI = employees using AI tools without formal approval or governance.**

For a small law firm, this means:
- A paralegal using free ChatGPT to draft a contract summary → client data leaves the firm's systems
- A partner using Copilot to analyze a due diligence dataset → data uploaded to Microsoft's servers
- An associate using an AI legal research tool → queries and documents processed outside firm control

Each of these creates:
1. **FADP exposure** — data processed by AI tools without proper documentation
2. **Client confidentiality risk** — privileged information shared with AI providers
3. **Professional liability** — unauthorized practice if AI output is relied upon without review
4. **Managing director liability** — criminal exposure under FADP Art. 45 (CHF 250,000 for intentional failures)

**The question is not whether your firm uses AI.**
**The question is whether your firm has governed the AI it's already using.**

---

## Section 4: DPIA and Privacy by Design — What Actually Applies

**Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA):**
Required for high-risk AI processing under FADP Art. 22. For a small law firm, "likely high-risk" AI use includes:
- AI-assisted contract analysis (processing client legal documents)
- AI due diligence tools (processing counterparty data)
- AI document review for litigation (processing sensitive evidence)
- AI legal research tools (processing case law and client matter data)

A DPIA is not optional if you're using high-risk AI. It's an enforceable obligation.

**Privacy by Design / Privacy by Default:**
When procuring or adopting a new AI tool, you must assess:
- Purpose limitation: Is the AI tool used only for stated purposes?
- Data minimization: Does the tool collect only what it needs?
- Storage limitation: Is client data retained only as long as necessary?
- Accuracy: Can the AI tool produce inaccurate outputs that could harm clients?
- Security: Is the tool's data security posture adequate?

**FDPIC expects you to document these assessments** — purpose, functionality, data sources.

## Section 5: The Three Decisions

### Decision 0: Audit the AI You Already Have

Before you can govern AI, you need to know what AI is being used.

**Questions to answer:**
- Which AI tools are my lawyers and staff using today?
- Which of those tools process client data?
- Are those tools covered by a firm policy? (Almost certainly: no)
- What would happen if FDPIC asked to see our AI governance documentation?

If you can't answer these questions with confidence, you're in the Shadow AI zone.

**This is the entry point.** Most small firms haven't done this. That's your opportunity.

---

### Decision 1: Govern the AI You're Already Using

Once you've audited your AI use, the next decision is whether to:
- **Formalize approved AI tools** (with documented purpose, functionality, data sources, DPIA if needed)
- **Restrict certain AI tools** (based on data protection risk assessment)
- **Replace with compliant alternatives** (if existing tools don't meet FADP standards)

The outcome of Decision 1 is an **AI Governance Policy** — a written document that defines which AI tools are approved, how they're used, and what documentation is required.

**Key elements:**
- List of approved AI tools and their permitted uses
- Data protection obligations for each approved tool
- Client notification requirements (when AI is used in matter handling)
- Documentation requirements (DPIA, purpose/functionality/data sources)
- Staff training and acknowledgment
