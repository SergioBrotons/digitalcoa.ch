### Finding 1: Client Counter-Questionnaire Accountability Chain (NET-NEW C290)

A boutique receives a client AI governance questionnaire and responds with a formal written submission. That submission becomes a corporate record. The client then uses the response as a baseline—future AI-assisted deliverables are evaluated against the standards the boutique self-certified in that document. Every subsequent delivery inconsistency with the documented response creates a liability chain. The boutique has no mechanism for tracking whether it is meeting its own stated standards across active engagements. The response document functions as an implied warranty against itself.

**Mechanism:** Client AI governance questionnaires are bilateral accountability instruments. A boutique that responds "we maintain human oversight of all AI-assisted deliverables" in writing without documenting what that human oversight consists of has created an implied warranty it cannot discharge. The client holding that document can evaluate each subsequent deliverable against it. The boutique cannot reconstruct whether human oversight was actually performed on deliverables produced before the questionnaire response.

**ICP Language (EN):** "We answered their AI questionnaire. Now every time we deliver something, they might be measuring it against what we wrote."
**ICP Language (FR):** "Nous avons répondu à leur questionnaire IA. Maintenant, chaque fois que nous livrons quelque chose, ils pourraient l'évaluer par rapport à ce que nous avons écrit."

**Implication:** Pre-questionnaire response preparation is a discrete service category. The boutique's own documented response must be one it can actually perform—not one it writes to satisfy the questionnaire. The methodology for aligning response content with actual delivery practice before submission is unnamed in Geneva market.

### Finding 2: Carrier PI Insurance Coverage Completeness Problem (NET-NEW C290)

Boutique MD signs carrier AI attestation committing to AI governance controls. The firm's professional liability policy predates the carrier's AI attestation form. The policy does not contain explicit AI coverage language. Neither the carrier nor the broker can confirm coverage applies to AI-assisted professional error in a dispute. The MD has signed a document warranting AI governance controls; the firm's PI policy may or may not cover claims arising from AI-assisted decisions. This is not a policy gap the MD can close by asking—the insurance market does not have Geneva-specific AI professional liability coverage analysis mapping. The MD cannot obtain a definitive written answer to what the policy covers for AI-assisted errors without triggering underwriting review that could adjust the policy.

**Mechanism:** Two separate questions—attestation adequacy and insurance coverage—are being answered as one. The attestation warrants governance practice. The policy covers financial loss from professional error. Whether AI-assisted professional error is covered under a pre-AI policy is structurally undetermined. No Geneva carrier has published coverage position. No Geneva broker has issued Geneva-calibrated AI professional liability gap analysis.

**ICP Language (EN):** "I signed the carrier form. But I've never actually seen a policy that says AI is covered. Nobody can tell me in writing."
**ICP Language (FR):** "J'ai signé le formulaire de l'assureur. Mais je n'ai jamais vu une police qui dit que l'IA est couverte. Personne ne peut me le confirmer par écrit."

**Implication:** Pre-policy AI coverage mapping is a discrete service category. MD maps personal exposure → maps existing coverage → identifies gap → documents strategy for discharge. The exercise is naming-specific and proportionate to boutique scale. No Geneva service offers this as a named discrete offering.

### Finding 3: AI Deliverable Production Timestamp Integrity (NET-NEW C290)

A boutique submits an AI-assisted deliverable with a date. The date represents when it was presented, not the model version state under which the analysis was produced. The AI model generating the analytical framework has updated multiple times since the client's mandate began. The deliverable's timestamp is chronologically after model updates occurred. Under Art.398 CO proper performance, the MD certifies analysis reflecting conditions at the time of the mandate. The MD presenting the deliverable cannot confirm what model version state the analysis reflects. The timestamp certifies presentation date, not analytical validity.

**Mechanism:** AI model update propagation creates version state between mandate commencement and deliverable presentation. The deliverable timestamp documents the presentation moment, not the analytical framework state under which the content was produced. For sophisticated clients or in a dispute, the question is whether the analysis reflected conditions as of mandate date or current model state. The boutique cannot answer this without version tracking it does not have.

**ICP Language (EN):** "The recommendation has a date on it. But nobody can tell you what the model was thinking on that date versus what it's thinking now."
**ICP Language (FR):** "La recommandation a une date. Mais personne ne peut vous dire ce que le modèle pensait à cette date par rapport à ce qu'il pense maintenant."

**Implication:** Deliverable version state documentation is a structural Gary requirement for any boutique whose active clients include sophisticated institutions with AI governance expectations. Timestamp integrity is a named service category. No Geneva consulting-specific protocol exists for managing version state documentation across a boutique mandate book.

### Finding 4: Peer Circuit Silence as Accountability Signal (NET-NEW C290)

The Geneva consulting peer circuit has no AI governance dialogue. SVA does not cover management consulting; no ORDRE equivalent exists. Peers are not discussing AI governance in any formal or informal circuit that a Geneva MD can observe. The absence of peer dialogue on AI governance accountability is itself a signal: (a) no peer has a defensible position they are willing to stand behind; (b) AI governance accountability problems are being managed in isolation by each boutique; (c) whatever standards are forming are forming without observable public input. A boutique that initiates structured peer AI governance dialogue would be the first to be visibly wrong—which explains the silence. The silence functions as a market-wide embarrassment signal.

**Mechanism:** Peer silence operates differently for accountability-specific subjects than for general business challenges. A MD who asks a peer "how are you handling client AI governance questionnaires" in a professional setting receives either an unhelpful general answer or no answer. The absence of concrete responses across the peer circuit indicates universally unresolved status on this specific subject—not absence of concern.

**ICP Language (EN):** "I've asked three peers. Nobody really has an answer. Which means nobody is comfortable talking about it."
**ICP Language (FR):** "J'ai demandé à trois pairs. Personne n'a vraiment de réponse. Ce qui signifie que personne n'est à l'aise pour en parler."

**Implication:** Peer circuit initiation as discrete market positioning move. The first Geneva boutique to openly discuss its AI governance methodology in a peer context does something risky—shows potential clients exactly where its position is—but simultaneously demonstrates its methodology, which most peers cannot match because they have none. The asymmetry between early disclosure and no disclosure flips into competitive advantage before the category is saturated.

### Finding 5: Engagement Letter AI Disclosure Obligation Paradox (NET-NEW C290)

An engagement letter that describes the boutique's AI-assisted approach to the mandate creates nFADP Article 9 processing obligations toward the client. The client is now a data subject whose data will be processed through the boutique's AI tools—and the engagement letter documents this processing. If the boutique has not established a legal basis for that processing, the engagement letter itself is evidence of an unbased processing operation. Conversely, an engagement letter that does not describe the AI-assisted approach preserves privilege over AI methodology—but creates no documented client disclosure. The boutique that discloses AI methodology in engagement letters generates legal exposure under nFADP while protecting itself from client disclosure claims. The boutique that does not disclose maintains privilege but accepts client disclosure risk.

**Mechanism:** Engagement letter AI language creates a structural paradox: disclosure creates nFADP processor obligations the engagement letter does not satisfy; non-disclosure maintains privilege but leaves client without disclosure. The engagement letter AI disclosure decision is not binary—it determines which accountability regime governs the boutique's AI relationship with the client. No Geneva market service maps this trade-off at boutique scale.

**ICP Language (EN):** "If I write in the engagement letter that we use AI, I think that creates some kind of data processing obligation I'm not sure I've satisfied. But if I don't write it, the client might claim they didn't know."
**ICP Language (FR):** "Si j'écris dans le mandat que nous utilisons l'IA, je pense que cela crée une sorte d'obligation de traitement de données dont je ne suis pas sûr de l'avoir satisfaite. Mais si je ne l'écris pas, le client pourrait claims qu'il ne savait pas."

**Implication:** Engagement letter AI disclosure mapping is a discrete named service category. The mapping documents: (1) what disclosure language is required to satisfy client expectations; (2) what nFADP obligations that language creates; (3) what language creates the least exposure with the most protection. Proportionate to boutique scale. No Geneva market has named this as a discrete service offering.

### Finding 6: Geneva Consulting Accountability Formation Velocity Displacement (NET-NEW C290)

Informal Geneva accountability expectations are forming faster than Bern formal regulatory doctrine. Geneva institutional decisions, carrier iteration signals, and peer circuit exchanges create effective accountability standards before any published FINMA guidance or Federal Council formal AI doctrine exists. The Geneva boutique operating in this environment faces two categories of accountability simultaneously: published doctrine (which may be sparse) and forming expectations (which are operationally operative but unpublished). A boutique waiting for published doctrine to determine its accountability position discovers, upon inquiry, that operative expectations already formed. No published framework describes the relationship between formal doctrine and informal formation.

**Mechanism:** Accountability expectations crystallize in Geneva through sustained institutional participation—not through formal publication channels. The boutique not participating in these informal channels cannot observe what is being formed. By the time published doctrine catches up, the informal standard is already operative. The documentation window close makes this structurally consequential: what is built before the standard crystallizes determines the boutique's position; what is built after can only describe what arrived.

**ICP Language (EN):** "By the time anything gets published, the decision has already been made somewhere. The question is whether you were in the room."
**ICP Language (FR):** "Quand quelque chose est publié, la décision a déjà été prise quelque part. La question est de savoir si vous étiez dans la pièce."

**Implication:** Geneva accountability formation velocity mapping is a discrete intelligence function. The boutique that tracks which accountability signals are crystallizing—through institutional contacts, carrier iteration patterns, and peer circuit exchanges—positions its documentation before the standard arrives. This is different from monitoring published doctrine; it is monitoring the formation process itself. No Geneva consulting-specific intelligence function addresses this velocity differential at boutique scale.
