**Who this is for:**

You are a managing director or partner at a Geneva consulting boutique with between 2 and 50 employees. You personally sign carrier AI attestation forms. ### Problem 1: Your Client Questionnaire Response is an Implied Warranty Against Itself

When you responded to your client's AI governance questionnaire, you wrote something like this: "We maintain human oversight of all AI-assisted deliverables."

That document is now a corporate record. Your client is evaluating every subsequent deliverable against what you wrote. You have no mechanism for tracking whether your delivery practice matches that statement. You cannot reconstruct whether human oversight was performed on deliverables produced before you answered the questionnaire.

The questionnaire response you submitted is not documentation. It is an implied warranty.

**What you get in the briefing:** A methodology for mapping your actual delivery practice against every questionnaire statement before you submit a response. Not a template — a methodology for building the alignment before you commit to something you cannot discharge.

---

### Problem 2: You Signed the Carrier Form. Your Insurance May Not Cover What You Signed.

You signed a personal attestation committing to AI governance controls. Your professional liability policy predates the carrier's AI attestation form. It does not contain explicit AI coverage language.

Here is what is structurally wrong with that situation: Two separate questions are being answered as one. The attestation warrants governance practice. The policy covers financial loss from professional error. Whether AI-assisted professional error is covered under a pre-AI insurance policy is structurally undetermined. No Geneva carrier has published a coverage position. No Geneva broker has issued Geneva-calibrated AI professional liability gap analysis.

Your signature is personal. Your coverage confirmation is not available.

**What you get in the briefing:** A pre-incident mapping of your personal exposure against your existing PI policy coverage — identifying the gap before a claim event makes it visible. Not reassurance. A named, documented position you can act on.

---

### Problem 3: Your Deliverable Timestamp Documents When it Was Presented, Not What the Model Was Thinking

You have deliverables in active client use. The AI model that produced the analytical framework has updated silently since the mandate began. Your deliverable has a date. The date is when it was presented. It is not when the analysis was produced under the model version state that produced it.

For sophisticated clients or carriers asking "what model version produced this analysis, under what conditions?" you have no answer. Art.398 CO proper performance requires analysis reflecting mandate-period conditions. You cannot confirm your deliverable meets this standard without the documentation architecture you do not have.

**What you get in the briefing:** A version state documentation architecture proportionate to boutique mandate book scale — not an enterprise IT system, a proportionate methodology for tracking deliverable AI model version state across active client engagements.

---

### Problem 4: Your Peers Are Not Talking About This. That is an Embarrassment Signal.

You have asked peers how they handle client AI governance questionnaires. You have received no concrete responses. The absence of peer methodology dialogue is not an information gap. It is an embarrassment signal: no peer has a methodology they are willing to stand behind publicly.

The first Geneva consulting firm to publicly discuss its AI governance methodology is the first to be visibly wrong — which is why nobody is doing it. The silence is market-wide, not individual.

**What you get in the briefing:** Understanding of what the peer silence means structurally — and what it means for your competitive positioning if you develop a methodology before the category crystallizes.

---

### Problem 5: Your Engagement Letter Has an AI Disclosure Paradox Nobody Has Named

If your engagement letter describes your AI-assisted approach, it creates nFADP Article 9 processing obligations toward your client. You are now processing your client's data through AI tools, and your engagement letter documents this processing. If you have not established a legal basis, your letter is evidence of unbased processing.

If your engagement letter does not describe your AI-assisted approach, you preserve privilege over methodology — but your client can claim non-disclosure.

The engagement letter AI disclosure decision determines which accountability regime governs your AI relationship with your client. No Geneva protocol maps this trade-off at boutique scale.

**What you get in the briefing:** A named decision framework for the engagement letter AI disclosure paradox — what disclosure language creates what obligations, what non-disclosure creates what exposure, and what proportionate architecture exists at your firm scale.

---

### Problem 6: By the Time Anything Gets Published, the Decision Has Already Been Made

Geneva accountability expectations are forming through informal channels before Bern formal doctrine arrives. Your carrier's questionnaire evolution, institutional client requirements, and Geneva institutional exchanges are creating operative standards you cannot observe through published guidance.

By the time a standard reaches publication, it is already describing what is in effect. The documentation window that matters is before the standard crystallizes — not after it is published. What you build before determines your position. What you build after can only describe what arrived.

**What you get in the briefing:** A Geneva accountability formation velocity briefing — understanding which signals are crystallizing now, what the formation window means for your documentation architecture, and why sustained Geneva institutional participation is operationally relevant to your accountability position.
