# Competitive Analysis: Geneva Boutique Wealth Management — C310 Phase 02

## What Exists in the Market vs. What Is Missing

---

## The Competitive Landscape

### What Exists

**1. FINMA-Aligned Consulting**
Large international consulting firms (Big Four, large Swiss advisory) offer FINMA-aligned AI governance frameworks for financial institutions. Their product is a framework document — a policy template adapted for FINMA requirements. Positioned as regulatory compliance. Price point: CHF 50,000–200,000+. Target: large banks, asset managers, insurance companies. NOT positioned for boutique WM firms (50–200 employees). The framework they produce is not calibrated to institutional DDQ specificity and is not designed for the boutique WM authorization chain problem.

**2. General nFADP Advisory**
Law firms and data protection advisors offer nFADP compliance reviews — general Article 5–12 obligations, privacy policy updates, consent frameworks. Their product is Article 5–12 compliance, not Article 63 personal liability for investment decisions. They do not read institutional AI governance DDQs as market intelligence. They do not address the FINMA human oversight operationalization gap. They are not reading carrier forms as a competitive intelligence function. Price point: CHF 10,000–80,000 for nFADP gap assessment. Not positioned for WM-specific AI accountability.

**3. General AI Governance Frameworks**
International AI governance frameworks (NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, EU AI Act compliance) are offered by management consulting firms. These frameworks are designed for large enterprises with dedicated compliance infrastructure. They are principles-based and undefined at boutique WM scale — the same problem the FINMA guidance creates. The boutique WM firm that adopts an international framework discovers the framework does not produce the institutional DDQ-ready documentation they need. Frameworks exist; evidence architecture does not.

**4. Regulatory Technology (RegTech) Platforms**
RegTech platforms offer AI governance monitoring tools — AI inventory dashboards, model performance tracking, attestation management. These platforms are designed for larger institutions with existing compliance infrastructure and dedicated compliance staff. The boutique WM firm (50–200 employees, no dedicated AI compliance officer) cannot operate these platforms without significant implementation support. The platforms solve the monitoring problem; they do not solve the documentation architecture problem or the retroactive reconstruction problem.

**5. IT/Vendor Managed Services**
The portfolio management system vendors (Temenos, Finantix, Avaloq-based systems, independent WM platforms) offer AI module activation and support. Their focus is system operation, not AI governance documentation. They are not responsible for the MD's personal liability under Article 63. They are not reviewing the institutional DDQ as a design specification. Their AI governance documentation responsibility ends where the MD's personal accountability begins.

### What Is Missing

**1. No One Reads Institutional AI Governance DDQs as Market Intelligence**

No competitor reads institutional AI governance DDQs systematically. No competitor produces a "DDQ reading service" that maps the specific questions in the current institutional DDQ to the firm's existing documentation. No competitor says: "I read institutional AI governance DDQs for a living — I can tell you what your clients are actually asking for." This is the first-mover language. The institutional DDQ is the design specification — nobody is reading it.

**2. No One Addresses the Boutique WM Authorization Chain Problem Specifically**

The authorization chain for portfolio AI runs through vendor implementation and associate activation. No competitor has built a product specifically addressing this chain for boutique WM firms. The Big Four address AI governance frameworks. The RegTech platforms address AI monitoring. Neither addresses the specific documentation gap created by associate-activated AI modules running on vendor-implemented systems — with the MD's personal liability for the output.

**3. No One Does Retroactive Reconstruction for Institutional DDQ Readiness**

The boutique WM firm needs to reconstruct 12–24 months of undocumented AI use in a form that answers the institutional DDQ's specific questions. No competitor offers retroactive reconstruction as a discrete service offering — bounded, deliverable, priced for the boutique WM firm. Retroactive reconstruction is not a framework. It is a specific methodology applied to specific undocumented AI use over a specific time period, producing documentation the institutional client will accept.

**4. No One Addresses the Two-Document Problem (Carrier Attestation vs. Institutional DDQ)**

No competitor maps the carrier attestation against the institutional DDQ to identify the documentation gap between them. The two documents ask different question types and require different documentation architectures. The firm that thinks they are prepared because they completed the carrier attestation is not prepared for the institutional DDQ. No competitor has built a service that reads both documents and produces the gap analysis.

**5. No One Provides Bilingual EN/FR Documentation for Boutique WM Firms**

Geneva's institutional clients operate in both English and French. The WM firm's AI governance documentation needs to serve both language contexts — English for the institutional client, French for Swiss regulatory proceedings. No competitor offers bilingual EN/FR AI governance documentation as a standard service for boutique WM firms. The language requirement is treated as a localization task, not a design requirement.

**6. No One Addresses the Multi-Mandate Documentation Coherence Problem**

The boutique WM firm managing multiple institutional mandates needs a coherent underlying documentation architecture that produces consistent evidence across all mandates. No competitor addresses this specific problem: multiple clients, multiple DDQ versions, one coherent documentation system. The coherence requirement is distinct from the individual DDQ response — it requires an underlying architecture that generates consistent evidence regardless of which client asks.

## Competitive White Spaces — C310 Phase 02

### White Space 01: Institutional DDQ Reading as Market Intelligence

**The opportunity:** The institutional AI governance DDQ is the most specific, most current, most actionable accountability document in the boutique WM market. It reflects what the institutional client actually requires — not what FINMA guidance suggests, not what the carrier form asks, but what the ultimate decision-maker (the institutional client's investment committee) wants to know. No one is reading it systematically. Reading it produces: the specific questions the firm needs to answer, the specific documentation gaps they have, and the specific language to use in responses.

**Positioning language:** "I read institutional AI governance DDQs for a living. I'll show you what your clients are actually asking for — in their exact language."

### White Space 02: Boutique WM Authorization Chain Documentation

**The opportunity:** The boutique WM authorization chain has a specific architecture: MD selection → vendor implementation → associate activation → AI module → client-facing recommendation. This chain is not documented at any point in the typical boutique WM firm. The documentation required at each step is specific to investment decisions — not general AI governance policy. No competitor has mapped this chain and produced the documentation architecture for it.

**Positioning language:** "I'll trace your AI authorization chain — from how the system was selected to how recommendations reach your clients — and document every step that needs evidence."

### White Space 03: Model Drift Documentation for Boutique WM

**The opportunity:** The institutional DDQ is beginning to ask for evidence of AI model performance monitoring. The boutique WM firm running AI-assisted portfolio construction has no documentation of model drift review. The documentation required — periodic human review of AI model recommendation consistency, documented against the benchmark — is specific and bounded. The model drift documentation service is a discrete deliverable: what was reviewed, when, by whom, what the finding was.

**Positioning language:** "Your institutional client is asking for evidence of AI performance monitoring. You don't have it. I'll produce the documentation."

### White Space 04: Pre-Signing Institutional DDQ Review

**The opportunity:** The equivalent of the carrier form pre-signing review (C310 Phase 01 finding) for institutional DDQs: the managing director receives the institutional client's DDQ and has no mechanism to review it before responding. The DDQ response goes out without internal review. The response is incomplete or inconsistent with the firm's actual documentation. No one has built the institutional DDQ pre-response review service — the bounded, specific, 90-minute review that maps the DDQ questions to existing documentation and identifies gaps before the response is submitted.

**Positioning language:** "Before you respond to the institutional DDQ — let me show you what you're answering and what's missing."

### White Space 05: Multi-Mandate Coherence Architecture

**The opportunity:** The boutique WM firm with multiple institutional mandates needs a documentation architecture that produces consistent evidence regardless of which client asks. This is not a one-time DDQ response service — it is an ongoing documentation coherence system. The service that produces consistent evidence across all mandates (coherent AI inventory, consistent authorization chain documentation, unified model performance records) is the service that prevents the coherence problem from becoming a competitive liability.

**Positioning language:** "Four clients, four DDQ versions, one coherent architecture. That is what you need — not four separate responses."

### White Space 06: Bilingual EN/FR Boutique WM Evidence Architecture

**The opportunity:** The Geneva boutique WM firm operates in two languages: English with institutional clients, French with Swiss regulators. The documentation architecture must serve both contexts — English for institutional DDQ responses, French for FINMA and SRO proceedings. No competitor offers bilingual EN/FR AI governance documentation as a designed system, not a translated document. The bilingual requirement is a design constraint, not a translation task.

**Positioning language:** "Your institutional clients read English. Your regulators read French. Your documentation needs to serve both — as a system, not a translation."

## The Structural Insight

The competitive landscape is solving the wrong problem. The market offers:
- Frameworks (what should your AI governance policy say?)
- General nFADP compliance (does your privacy policy meet Article 5–12?)
- Monitoring tools (can you track your AI systems?)
- Policy templates (do you have an AI governance document?)

The boutique WM ICP needs:
- Evidence that answers specific institutional DDQ questions (not a framework)
- Documentation of the authorization chain for specific portfolio decisions (not a policy)
- Retroactive reconstruction of 12–24 months of undocumented AI use (not a tool)
- A coherent architecture across multiple mandates (not a one-time response)

The positioning that occupies the white space: "The institutional DDQ is the specification. I read it so you don't have to guess what your clients actually want."
