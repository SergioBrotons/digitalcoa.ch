## The Core Market Signals — C310 Phase 02

### Signal 01: The Institutional DDQ Is Now Asking Matter-Level Questions the Managing Director Cannot Answer

**What is being reported:**
The institutional client has sent an AI governance DDQ that asks for documentation of AI use in specific portfolio decisions — not firm-level policy, not vendor list, but evidence of human authorization for specific AI-assisted investment recommendations. The managing director cannot produce this documentation. The DDQ is being treated as a material question about the manager's governance quality. The inability to answer is being noted in the client's internal review. The MD does not know what the client's internal review will conclude.

**The specific failure mode:**
The institutional client asks: "Describe the human oversight protocol for AI-assisted portfolio construction decisions. Provide evidence of human authorization for the AI systems used in our mandate." The firm has a policy stating human oversight exists. The policy is not evidence of authorization for specific decisions. The MD signed the attestation covering all AI use in the firm — including AI use they did not know was happening.

**What this means for the market:**
The institutional DDQ has shifted from firm-level to matter-level — the same structural shift documented in C310 Phase 01 for legal carrier forms. The boutique WM firm did not receive notice of the shift. The shift is happening at each renewal cycle. The documentation required to answer the current form did not exist when the AI use began.

### Signal 02: The Authorization Chain for Portfolio AI Is Undocumented and Unattributed

**What is being reported:**
The managing director authorized the use of a portfolio management system. The portfolio management system has an AI module. The AI module was activated by an associate. The activation was not reviewed by the MD. The AI module has been generating recommendations that go to clients — with the MD's name, as managing director of the firm. The MD did not know the AI module was active. The MD has been personally attesting to AI governance adequacy without knowing what AI was active.

**The specific failure mode:**
The authorization chain is: MD → portfolio system selection → vendor implementation → associate activation → AI module → client-facing recommendation. Steps 3 and 4 are undocumented. The MD cannot produce evidence of authorization for the AI module because the authorization was never documented. The evidence gap is not an oversight — it is a structural feature of how the AI was adopted.

**What this means for the market:**
The authorization chain is the managing director's accountability mechanism. When the institutional client asks for evidence of human authorization, the MD needs to show: who authorized the AI system, when, on what basis, and what the review protocol is. If this chain does not exist in documented form, the MD is personally attesting to AI governance they did not establish. This is the same invisible authorization chain problem as C310 Phase 01, applied to investment decisions — with the additional complexity that the authorization chain runs through a vendor implementation and an associate activation.

### Signal 03: Portfolio AI Model Drift Is Beginning to Surface as a Live Concern

**What is being reported:**
The boutique WM firm has been running AI-assisted portfolio construction for a period of time — 12 to 24 months is the typical undocumented window. The AI model's recommendation patterns have changed over this period (model drift). The MD has not been monitoring for model drift. The institutional DDQ now asks for evidence of AI performance monitoring. The MD has no record of AI model performance review. The AI model's recommendations over the past 18 months — which affected client portfolios — were generated by a model that changed over time without documented human review.

**The specific failure mode:**
AI models drift. The boutique WM firm running AI-assisted portfolio construction has an obligation — under FINMA human oversight requirements and under nFADP Article 63 — to monitor AI model performance and document human review of model drift. The documentation does not exist. The model has drifted. The client has received AI-influenced recommendations from a model that changed without documented human oversight.

**What this means for the market:**
Model drift monitoring is the operationalization gap that follows the authorization chain problem. The MD who now knows they need to document authorization also needs to know they need to document model performance review. These are two sequential documentation obligations — both currently absent. The market has not yet formed a clear picture of the model drift documentation obligation. The institutional DDQ is beginning to make it visible.

### Signal 04: The Institutional Client Is the Forcing Function Before FINMA Becomes One

**What is being reported:**
The institutional client's AI governance questions are more specific and more demanding than anything FINMA has asked directly. The institutional DDQ asks for evidence of human authorization, model performance monitoring, data lineage documentation, and vendor AI governance review — none of which FINMA has directly audited at boutique WM scale. The institutional client is setting a standard that exceeds current regulatory requirements in specific respects.

**The specific failure mode:**
The boutique WM firm has been preparing for FINMA requirements — which are principles-based and undefined at boutique scale — while the institutional client has been preparing a more specific standard. The firm is further from meeting the institutional client's standard than they are from meeting FINMA's standard. The competitive gate (institutional DDQ) is set higher than the regulatory floor (FINMA guidance).

**What this means for the market:**
The market is experiencing a standard-setting function shift. FINMA set the floor. Institutional clients are setting the operational standard. The boutique WM firm that aligns their AI governance documentation to FINMA principles is not aligned to what their institutional clients actually require. The service must read institutional DDQs as the primary design specification — not FINMA guidance.

### Signal 05: The Carrier Attestation and the Institutional DDQ Are Different Documents with Different Standards

**What is being reported:**
The boutique WM firm has completed the carrier's AI attestation form (typically a firm-level attestation about AI governance policy and vendor management) and believes they are adequately prepared for AI governance scrutiny. The institutional client sends a separate AI governance DDQ with materially different questions. The firm discovers they have answered one document but not the other. The two documents do not cover the same ground. The firm has been treating them as equivalent.

**The specific failure mode:**
The carrier attestation asks: "Do you have an AI governance policy?" The institutional DDQ asks: "Provide evidence of human authorization for AI-assisted portfolio construction decisions in your management of our assets." These are different question types requiring different documentation architectures. The firm-level policy cannot answer the matter-level question. The firm that completed the carrier attestation without reviewing the institutional DDQ discovers a documentation gap only when the DDQ arrives.

**What this means for the market:**
The two-document problem is creating false security. The managing director who completed the carrier attestation believes they are prepared. The institutional DDQ reveals they are not. The carrier attestation and the institutional DDQ are not interchangeable — they ask different questions at different levels. Pre-signing review of both documents — before the MD signs either — is the intervention point that prevents this failure mode.

---

### Signal 06: The Boutique WM Documentation Coherence Problem Is More Acute Than the Legal ICP

**What is being reported:**
The boutique WM firm has multiple institutional clients. Each institutional client sends a version of the AI governance DDQ. The versions differ in their specific questions. The firm has been responding to each DDQ separately without a coherent underlying documentation architecture. The result: inconsistent responses across clients, evidence of different documentation levels for different mandates, and — in the worst cases — conflicting statements about AI use across different institutional client DDQ responses.

**The specific failure mode:**
The WM firm managing multiple institutional mandates has been producing client-specific AI governance documentation without a coherent underlying architecture. When Client A asks about the same AI system as Client B, the responses differ because they were produced at different times, by different associates, without reference to a common documentation standard. The inconsistency is now becoming visible as institutional clients share AI governance expectations with each other through industry associations and peer networks.

**What this means for the market:**
The documentation coherence problem across mandates is specific to WM firms managing multiple institutional relationships. It is not a problem the legal ICP faces — a law firm typically has one client relationship at a time. The WM ICP managing multiple institutional mandates needs a coherent underlying documentation architecture that produces consistent evidence across all mandates — while the legal ICP needs matter-level documentation within a single firm. The WM problem is more complex: one firm, multiple mandates, multiple client DDQs, one documentation architecture.

The C310 Phase 02 ICP is in a state of **discovered exposure** — not discovery ("do I have a problem?") but the realization that the problem is larger and more structural than anticipated. The C309 ICP was managing a known problem. The C310 ICP is discovering the problem has a different architecture than they built for.

The dominant emotions:
