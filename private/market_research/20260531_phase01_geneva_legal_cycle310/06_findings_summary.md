### Finding 01: Pre-Signing Carrier Form Review Gap — The Intervention Point Nobody Built

**The finding:** The Geneva small firm has no mechanism to review the carrier's form before signing. The carrier sends the attestation form. The managing partner signs it. Nobody reviews what the form is actually asking until the carrier comes back at renewal with follow-up questions. The pre-signing review is the highest-value intervention point in the accountability chain — it is the moment at which the firm could map the carrier's questions to existing documentation and identify gaps before they become coverage problems. No service exists at this intervention point. The market has no "carrier form pre-signing review" product.

**Mechanism:** Carrier sends form → managing partner signs without review → carrier renewal asks questions form didn't prepare for → gaps become coverage concerns → post-signing review is too late.

**Implication:** The pre-signing carrier form review is the ideal broker-referral product: bounded, specific, deliverable in a single 90-minute session, directly tied to the renewal trigger. The broker who serves law firm clients is looking for a service to refer. Nobody has built it. This is first-mover opportunity.

**Ready-to-use language:** "There's no debrief. No one reviews the carrier's questions before you sign. You find out what you missed when they come back with follow-up."

### Finding 02: "Adequate" Has Become a False Security Blanket — The Comparison Trap

**The finding:** The Geneva small firm that has produced an AI governance policy believes they are "adequate" relative to their peers. They compare themselves to other small Geneva firms. They look at what their peers produced — a policy, a vendor list, a staff memo. They conclude their documentation is sufficient because it is similar to what others have done. This is the comparison trap. "Adequate" is not defined by peers — it is defined by the carrier's evolving standard. The carrier's standard is escalating faster than the peer group norm. The ICP who believes they are adequate because they are similar to their peers is falling further behind the carrier's requirement at every renewal cycle.

**Mechanism:** Firm produces policy → compares to peer group → concludes "adequate" → carrier's standard escalates → peer group norm remains static → gap widens → renewal reveals inadequate documentation.

**Implication:** The comparison trap is structurally invisible to the ICP — they cannot see that the peer group norm is not the carrier standard. The service that resolves this requires showing the ICP what the carrier's actual current standard is, not what the peer group produced. "I read carrier forms for a living — I can show you what they are actually asking for."

**Ready-to-use language:** "Adequate is not defined by your peers. It is defined by the carrier — and the carrier is raising the standard faster than your peer group is improving."

### Finding 03: Carrier Form Architecture Has Shifted from Firm-Level to Matter-Level — Documentation Design Problem

**The finding:** The carrier's AI attestation form has structurally shifted from asking firm-level questions ("do you have an AI policy?") to asking matter-level questions ("what AI was used in this specific matter, by whom, with what authorization?"). This is not a sophistication escalation in the same dimension as C309 Finding 06. This is a different question type requiring a different documentation architecture. A firm-level question can be answered with a policy document. A matter-level question requires contemporaneous records of AI use in specific client matters. The documentation architecture required to answer matter-level questions is fundamentally different from what the market has built.

**Mechanism:** Initial attestation (firm-level) → firm produces policy → carrier evolves form at renewal to matter-level questions → policy cannot answer matter-level questions → firm has no matter-level documentation → carrier has grounds for coverage concern.

**Implication:** The matter-level form shift requires a matter-level record system — not a policy, a contemporaneous documentation practice for each matter. The service that produces this is not a consultant delivering a document; it is a system designer delivering a practice. The firm needs to know how to document each matter as it happens, not produce a retrospective policy document.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The carrier's questions have changed from 'do you have a system?' to 'what happened in this specific matter?' These are different questions. You need different documentation."

### Finding 04: The Associate's AI Use Is Now the Managing Partner's AI Use — Invisible Authorization Chain

**The finding:** The associate at the Geneva small firm is using AI tools on client matters without the managing partner's authorization. The managing partner did not authorize the specific AI tool. The managing partner did not review the AI output. The associate's AI use is visible to the carrier (through the carrier's evolved attestation form) and is attributed to the managing partner — who is personally accountable for all AI use in the firm. The invisible authorization chain: associate uses AI → managing partner unaware → carrier attributes to managing partner → managing partner personally liable for unauthenticated delegation. The authorization chain was never documented, and the documentation that would have made the delegation defensible (associate training, tool authorization, supervision protocol, output review documentation) does not exist.

**Mechanism:** Associate uses AI → managing partner unaware → carrier sees outcome (all AI use in firm, attributed to MP) → MP signs attestation covering unauthenticated AI → renewal asks about authorization protocol → no protocol exists → personal liability confirmed.

**Implication:** The service must address the authorization chain documentation — not just the supervision documentation (C309 Finding 03) but specifically the authorization gap: how did the associate get permission to use this tool? If the answer is "they didn't," the managing partner needs to know what to document now and what the retroactive documentation looks like.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You never authorized your associate to use AI on your cases. You are personally liable for AI use you did not know about. The authorization chain is the first thing to document."

### Finding 05: Client Questions About AI Use Are Activating the Fee Agreement Gap

**The finding:** Clients at the Geneva small firm are beginning to ask direct questions about AI use in their matters. English-speaking clients, in particular, are asking whether the attorney used AI, what AI tools, and how the work was done. The managing partner has no calibrated response. The fee agreement has no AI clause. The client conversation has no documented basis. The managing partner either gives a vague answer (client loses trust) or avoids the question (client later discovers AI use and feels deceived). The fee agreement AI clause would resolve this: documented basis for disclosure, authorization language, billing framework, and data processing consent — in one document the client has agreed to.

**Mechanism:** Associate uses AI on matter → time billed to client → client notices AI tool in billing record → client asks directly → managing partner has no response → vague answer or avoidance → client trust erodes or deceived feeling activates → relationship risk.

**Implication:** Fee agreement AI clause drafting is a discrete, bounded, high-value deliverable. It is client-facing — the client sees it — and it resolves both the commercial question (AI-assisted billing) and the client trust problem simultaneously. The clause is the document that makes the client conversation straightforward.

**Ready-to-use language:** "A client asked you directly if you used AI on their case. You didn't know what to say. Your fee agreement needs an AI clause — it makes the conversation straightforward."

### Finding 06: Documentation Debt Is Non-Linear and Accelerating — The Retroactive Reconstruction Problem

**The finding:** The documentation debt accumulated by the Geneva small firm is not linear — it accelerates as AI use spreads through the practice. The firm that started using AI two years ago with one associate and one tool now has multiple associates using multiple tools across multiple matter types. The documentation debt at year two is not twice the debt at year one — it is ten times, because the tool count and matter count have compounded. Linear forward-looking solutions (write a policy, create a forward-looking log) cannot address non-linear retrospective debt. The retroactive reconstruction problem is the majority market problem: most firms have 12–24 months of undocumented AI use that needs to be reconstructed in a defensible form before the carrier's next question.

**Mechanism:** AI use begins (1 associate, 1 tool) → use spreads (2 associates, 3 tools) → use compounds (3 associates, 6 tools, 10 matter types) → debt compounds non-linearly → linear solutions applied → linear solutions insufficient → debt remains → carrier renewal arrives → no retroactive record exists.

**Implication:** Retroactive reconstruction is a specific, discrete service offering with a defined methodology. It is not "write a policy." It is "reconstruct 12–24 months of undocumented AI use in a form the carrier will accept." This requires understanding of what makes retroactive documentation defensible, what the carrier's specific questions are, and what the firm's matter-type breakdown looks like.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You've been using AI for two years without a system. A forward-looking policy cannot address retrospective non-linear debt. You need retroactive reconstruction — not another policy."
