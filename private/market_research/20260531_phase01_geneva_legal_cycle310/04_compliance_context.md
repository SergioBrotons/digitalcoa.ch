# Compliance Context: Geneva Legal — C310 Phase 01

## Switzerland-Specific Regulatory Environment

---

## 1. OFL Silence on "Adequate" — Structural Accountability Vacuum

The Swiss Bar Association (OFL / Ordre des Avocats Suisses) has not defined "adequate" AI governance for Geneva attorneys. This is not an oversight — it is a structural vacuum. The OFL's position is that AI governance is a professional responsibility question, but it has not issued operational guidance that defines what "adequate" means in practice.

**The vacuum creates a specific problem:** The managing partner who asks "what does adequate look like?" receives no answer from the bar. The carrier's form is the closest thing to a standard — not because carriers define professional standards, but because they define the evidence they will accept. The carrier's standard is now the de facto standard whether the bar defines one or not.

**Geneva-specific consideration:** The Canton of Geneva's bar has not issued local guidance distinct from the OFL position. The FDPIC (Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner) has published general AI guidance but nothing specific to attorney practice. No Geneva-specific AI governance standard exists.

**C310 implication:** The "adequate" question is answered by the carrier form, not by bar guidance. The service that maps carrier form questions to required evidence is providing the operational definition of "adequate" that the bar has not provided.

## 2. nFADP Article 321 CC — Attorney-Client Privilege and AI Vendor Records

The Swiss Tort Law (Article 321 of the Civil Code) establishes attorney-client privilege. The privilege protects communications between attorney and client, attorney work product, and records of the attorney's professional activities in connection with client matters.

**The AI vendor problem:** AI vendors are not bound by Article 321 CC. When a vendor stores records of AI queries made in connection with client matters — what tool was used, what data was input, what outputs were produced — those records are not protected by attorney-client privilege. If a matter goes to litigation, opposing counsel can subpoena the vendor directly. The vendor's records are discoverable without the attorney's consent and without the privilege that would normally apply.

**The compelled disclosure pathway:** Article 321 CC protects the attorney's records. It does not protect the vendor's records of the attorney's AI use. This is not a nuance — it is a structural gap in the privilege architecture that no current service addresses.

**Geneva-specific consideration:** Geneva civil procedure has no AI disclosure protocol. Swiss procedural law has not addressed whether AI use in matter preparation must be disclosed in submissions or discovery. The absence of a protocol does not mean disclosure is optional — it means the obligation is undefined and the risk is unquantified.

**C310 implication:** Vendor privilege review is a discrete, bounded service — reviewing vendor contracts to identify which vendor records are discoverable, what compelled disclosure obligations the vendor has, and what contract modifications would close the privilege gap. This is not "review the Terms of Service." It is a privilege architecture review of the vendor relationship.

## 3. Carrier Attestation as De Facto Standard — The Form Is the Specification

The carrier's AI attestation form has become the de facto standard for AI governance evidence in the Geneva legal market. This is not an intentional regulatory choice — it is a market structural result. The OFL has not defined a standard. The FDPIC has not issued specific guidance. The carrier form is the only document that asks specific questions with specific consequences for inadequate answers.

**How the form functions as standard:** The carrier form is the only document that (a) asks specific questions about AI use in client matters, (b) requires a signature from a named individual, and (c) has direct consequences for the firm's insurance coverage. The carrier's renewal form at C310 asks matter-level questions that require specific records. The form is the specification the evidence must satisfy.

**The escalation pattern:** Carriers are raising form sophistication faster than the market is improving documentation. The C309 cycle identified carrier form escalation (Finding 06). C310 identifies that the escalation has reached a specific structural shift: matter-level questions requiring matter-level documentation. The form is now asking for records the firm does not have.

**C310 implication:** Reading carrier forms systematically, tracking their evolution across renewal cycles, and mapping evidence requirements to form questions is a market intelligence function with no current competitor. "The form your carrier sent you is the specification" is the positioning that makes this competitive advantage explicit.

## 4. Attorney Professional Liability — Personal Accountability Architecture

The managing partner at a Geneva boutique firm is personally accountable for professional conduct in client matters. This accountability is not corporate — it is individual. The managing partner's signature on the carrier attestation form creates a documented standard of personal accountability for AI governance in the firm's client matters.

**The accountability architecture:** The managing partner signs the carrier attestation. The signature is personal. The attestation commits the managing partner to representations about AI use in the firm's client matters. If the carrier later determines the attestation was inaccurate, the managing partner's personal liability is engaged — not the firm's corporate liability, the managing partner's personal liability.

**The competence gap consequence:** The managing partner who cannot personally evaluate AI output produced by associates is signing attestations about AI use they do not understand. The personal accountability architecture does not accommodate the competence gap — it is activated regardless of whether the managing partner understood the AI. The accountability is personal even when the evaluation is structurally impossible.

**C310 implication:** The documentation the managing partner needs is not documentation of their evaluation of AI output — it is documentation of their supervision of the associate who did evaluate it. The supervision record is the document that makes the attestation defensible. "Document the supervision, not the AI" is the design principle for the managing partner's accountability architecture.

## 5. Swissdamed Scope and Legal AI — What Is and Is Not Covered

Swissdamed (the Swiss National Medical Devices Information System) scope clarification is relevant for legal AI governance in one specific way: the Swissdamed scope covers medical devices. It does not cover general AI tools used in legal practice.

**What Swissdamed covers:** Medical devices used in Swiss healthcare settings — which includes AI-assisted diagnostic tools used by physicians, AI imaging analysis, and other clinical AI applications.

**What Swissdamed does not cover:** General legal AI tools — research platforms, drafting assistants, document review tools, billing systems. These are not medical devices and are not subject to Swissdamed obligations.

**The confusion:** Legal ICPs conflate Swissdamed with general AI governance obligations because both are "Swiss AI regulatory frameworks" in the broadest sense. The result is that attorneys spend energy on Swissdamed compliance questions that don't apply to their practice, while missing the carrier attestation obligations that do.

**C310 implication:** Swissdamed clarification is a trust-building conversation — "here is what Swissdamed does and does not cover, and here is why it is not your primary AI governance obligation as an attorney." This separates the noise from the signal and positions the service as knowledgeable about the full regulatory landscape.

## 6. Geneva Civil Procedure — The AI Disclosure Protocol Gap

Geneva civil procedure has no protocol for AI disclosure in court submissions or discovery. The absence of a protocol is not the same as a permission — it is a gap. The question of whether an attorney must disclose AI use in preparing a filing, and what the disclosure obligation is, has not been addressed by any Geneva court or procedural rule.

**The practical problem:** Attorneys using AI on contested matters have no guidance on whether and how to disclose AI use. If opposing counsel challenges the use of AI in a filing, the attorney has no procedural framework to cite, no disclosure standard to apply, and no protocol for how to respond.

**The carrier implication:** The carrier's form asks about AI use in client matters. The civil procedure gap means the attorney is making representations to the carrier about AI use in matters that may go to litigation — with no clarity on what disclosure is required or what the carrier's questions would reveal in a dispute.

**C310 implication:** The civil procedure AI disclosure gap is a specific risk for contested matters. The service that addresses this gap produces a "AI use in litigation" protocol — a document the firm can apply to contested matters that discloses AI use appropriately and defensibly. This is distinct from the general AI governance policy and addresses a specific use case the policy doesn't cover.

## 7. The Bilingual Regulatory Environment — EN/FR Documentation as Design Requirement

Geneva's regulatory environment has two official languages operating simultaneously: carrier forms are often in English (as international insurance documents), while regulatory proceedings and bar complaints are in French. The documentation environment requires bilingual capacity — not translation, but design for two simultaneous language contexts.

**The carrier form language:** Many international insurance carriers use English-language attestation forms in the Swiss market. The attorney reads the form in English and responds in English. The carrier's questions are in English.

**The regulatory proceeding language:** Geneva bar disciplinary proceedings, civil procedure, and FDPIC investigations are conducted in French. Any documentation that might be used in a regulatory proceeding must be available in French.

**The documentation design problem:** The firm's AI governance documentation must function in both language contexts simultaneously. A policy written only in English is inadequate for French regulatory proceedings. A policy written only in French is inadequate for English-language carrier forms. The documentation must be designed for both — which is a design problem, not a translation problem.

**C310 implication:** Bilingual EN/FR documentation is not a localization task — it is a regulatory design requirement. The service that produces documentation designed for both language contexts (not just translated) has a structural advantage no competitor offers. "The documentation works in both languages because it was designed for both, not just translated."
