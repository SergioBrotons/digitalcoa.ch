# Market Signals: Geneva Legal — C310 Phase 01

## Current Problems, Complaints, Failures

Six NET-NEW findings distinct from C309 Phase 01 (C214–C308 reference). C310 ICP is in post-implementation failure mode — they did something, it didn't work, they don't know why. Market has moved from "do I have a problem?" to "I tried — why did it fail?" The questions being asked at C310 are more specific, more urgent, and more structurally complex than C309.

---

## Finding 01: Pre-Signing Carrier Review Gap — The Intervention Point Nobody Built

**The signal:** The Geneva small firm has no mechanism to review the carrier's form before signing. The carrier sends the attestation form. The managing partner signs it. Nobody reviews what the form is actually asking until the carrier comes back at renewal with follow-up questions. The pre-signing review is the highest-value intervention point — it is the moment at which the firm could map the carrier's questions to existing documentation and identify gaps before they become coverage problems. No service exists at this intervention point. The market has no "carrier form pre-signing review" product. The firm that wants this service today cannot buy it.

**Why it's different from C309 Finding 06:** C309 Finding 06 identified that carrier forms are escalating in sophistication. C310 Finding 01 identifies that the escalation has created a specific service gap: the pre-signing review. The form sophistication finding was descriptive. The pre-signing review gap is prescriptive — it names a specific, buildable service.

**What the ICP is saying:** "There's no debrief. No one reviews the carrier's questions before you sign. You find out what you missed when they come back with follow-up." And: "I read carrier forms for a living — I'll show you what they're actually asking for." (This second quote is our language, but the ICP is responding to it because it names a real gap.)

**Market signal:** A Geneva insurance broker has begun asking about AI governance services for their law firm clients. The broker is looking for a service to refer. Nobody has built the referral product. The pre-signing carrier form review is the ideal broker-referral product: bounded, specific, deliverable in a single session, and directly tied to the renewal trigger.

## Finding 02: "Adequate" Has Become a False Security Blanket — The Comparison Trap

**The signal:** The Geneva small firm that has produced an AI governance policy believes they are "adequate" relative to their peers. They compare themselves to other small Geneva firms. They look at what their peers produced (a policy, a vendor list, a staff memo). They conclude their documentation is sufficient because it is similar to what others have done. This is the comparison trap. "Adequate" is not defined relative to peers — it is defined by the carrier's evolving standard. The carrier's standard is escalating faster than the peer group norm. The ICP who believes they are adequate because they are similar to their peers is actually falling further behind the carrier's requirement. The OFL has not defined "adequate." The carrier form is the closest thing to a standard, and the carrier is raising it faster than the peer group is improving.

**Why it's different from C309:** C309 Finding 01 identified that written policies answer the wrong question (policy vs. evidence). C310 Finding 02 identifies that even adequate-seeming policies are inadequate by carrier standard — not because of quality, but because of the comparison logic. The C309 ICP knew they had a policy problem. The C310 ICP thinks they are fine because they are similar to their peers.

**What the ICP is saying:** "I thought we were okay. We wrote a policy. We told the carriers we had one. Then I saw what the renewal form actually wanted." The comparison trap is structurally invisible to the ICP — they cannot see that the peer group norm is not the carrier standard.

**Market signal:** The broker referral channel surfaces this problem repeatedly: "my client thought they were covered, they look like their peers, but the carrier is asking something different." The comparison trap is the reason clients come to brokers confused — they did what seemed reasonable and were still not prepared.

## Finding 03: Carrier Form Architecture Has Shifted from Firm-Level to Matter-Level — The Documentation Design Problem

**The signal:** The carrier's AI attestation form has structurally shifted from asking firm-level questions ("do you have an AI policy?") to asking matter-level questions ("what AI was used in this specific matter?"). This is not a sophistication escalation in the same dimension as C309 Finding 06. This is a different question type. A firm-level question can be answered with a policy document. A matter-level question requires contemporaneous records of AI use in specific client matters. The documentation architecture required to answer matter-level questions is fundamentally different from the documentation architecture required to answer firm-level questions. The market has not caught up to this shift. Firms are still producing firm-level documentation and expecting it to satisfy matter-level questions.

**Why it's different from C309:** C309 Finding 01 identified the policy-evidence gap (policy answers wrong question). C310 Finding 03 identifies that the form has changed the question type — from "do you have a policy?" to "what happened in this matter?" The policy is not just wrong for the question; the question itself has changed.

**Mechanism:** Initial attestation (firm-level) → firm produces policy → carrier evolves form at renewal to matter-level questions → policy cannot answer matter-level questions → firm has no matter-level documentation → coverage concern activates.

**Market signal:** Managing partners are encountering matter-level questions at renewal for the first time and discovering they have no answer. "They asked specifically about which AI tool was used on a specific matter last year. I don't have that record. Nobody asked me for that before."

## Finding 04: The Associate's AI Use Is Now the Managing Partner's AI Use — Invisible Authorization Chain

**The signal:** The associate at the Geneva small firm is using AI tools on client matters. The managing partner did not authorize the specific AI tool. The managing partner did not review the AI output. The associate's AI use is now visible to the carrier (through the carrier's evolved attestation form) and is attributed to the managing partner — who is personally accountable for all AI use in the firm. The invisible authorization chain is: associate uses AI → managing partner unaware → carrier attribute to managing partner → managing partner personally liable for unauthenticated delegation. The structural problem is that the authorization chain was never documented, and the documentation that would have made the delegation defensible (associate training, tool authorization, supervision protocol, output review documentation) does not exist.

**Why it's different from C309 Finding 03:** C309 Finding 03 identified the managing partner competence gap (cannot evaluate AI output). C310 Finding 04 identifies the authorization chain gap (did not authorize in the first place). C309 was about evaluation. C310 is about authorization. The managing partner didn't just fail to evaluate — they never authorized, which is a different documentation failure with different consequences.

**What the ICP is saying:** "I never authorized my associate to use AI on our cases. They started using it. I found out six months later." And: "The associate uses AI I don't recognize. I don't know what tool. I don't know what data went into it. I'm signing the carrier attestation."

**Market signal:** This is surfacing in broker conversations as the "how did this happen?" question — the managing partner is discovering that they are responsible for AI use they didn't know about, authorized, or supervised.

## Finding 05: Client Questions About AI Use Are Activating the Fee Agreement Gap

**The signal:** Clients at the Geneva small firm are beginning to ask direct questions about AI use in their matters. English-speaking clients, in particular, are asking whether the attorney used AI, what AI tools, and how the work was done. The managing partner has no calibrated response. The fee agreement has no AI clause. The client conversation has no documented basis. The managing partner either gives a vague answer (client loses trust) or avoids the question (client later discovers AI use and feels deceived). Either response creates a client relationship problem. The fee agreement AI clause would resolve this: it provides the documented basis for disclosure, the authorization language, the billing framework, and the data processing consent — in one document that the client has agreed to.

**Why it's different from C309 Finding 05:** C309 Finding 05 identified the AI billing commercial question (billing for AI-assisted work). C310 Finding 05 identifies the client question problem — clients are asking, and the lack of fee agreement AI clause means the firm has no calibrated response. C309 was about billing risk. C310 is about client relationship trust.

**What the ICP is saying:** "A client asked me directly whether I used AI on their case. I didn't know what to say." And: "Clients are starting to look at the billing records and ask questions."

**Market signal:** The client question problem is escalating because AI tool vendors are becoming more consumer-facing — clients recognize tool names in billing records and ask about them directly. The fee agreement AI clause is not just a governance deliverable; it is a client trust preservation document.

## Finding 06: Documentation Debt Is Non-Linear and Accelerating — The Retroactive Reconstruction Problem

**The signal:** The documentation debt accumulated by the Geneva small firm is not linear — it accelerates as AI use spreads through the practice. The firm that started using AI two years ago with one associate and one tool now has multiple associates using multiple tools across multiple matter types. The documentation debt at year two is not twice the debt at year one — it is ten times, because the tool count and matter count have compounded. Linear forward-looking solutions (write a policy, create a forward-looking log) cannot address non-linear retrospective debt. The retroactive reconstruction problem is the majority market problem: most firms have 12–24 months of undocumented AI use that needs to be reconstructed in a defensible form before the carrier's next question.

**Why it's different from C309:** C309 Finding 02 identified the documentation paradox as the primary action blocker (fear stops action). C310 Finding 06 identifies the non-linear acceleration structure of the debt — and specifically that linear solutions cannot address non-linear debt. The documentation paradox and the non-linear debt structure are related but distinct: the paradox stops action even when the ICP understands that linear solutions won't work.

**What the ICP is saying:** "We've been using AI for two years without a system. Every matter, every associate, every tool — no documentation." And: "The debt is not just that we didn't document. It's that the documentation should have been contemporaneous. Retroactive reconstruction is different."

**Market signal:** The retroactive reconstruction problem is the service that converts on the "I've tried everything and nothing works" ICP — the ICP who understands the problem, has tried linear solutions, and is frustrated that forward-looking approaches don't address what has already happened.
