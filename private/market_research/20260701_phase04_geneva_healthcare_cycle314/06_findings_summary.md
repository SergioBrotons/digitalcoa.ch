### Finding 02: Article 321 CP × EU AI Act Article 12 Logging Collision — The Swiss Criminal Law × EU Regulatory Law Named-Encounter Reconciliation Gap

**The finding:** The Geneva médecin operates under a collision that the legal, finance, and consulting verticals do not face: the Swiss Criminal Code Article 321 medical secrecy layer creates a direct, named-physician-criminal conflict with EU AI Act Article 12 logging requirements, at the named-encounter granularity. Article 321 CP (penalty: imprisonment up to 3 years or fine) prohibits disclosure of patient medical secrets to unauthorized parties, including any disclosure that flows from logging encounter-level AI processing to unauthorized recipients. Article 12 EU AI Act requires automatic logging of high-risk AI system operation. The log must contain: timestamp, encounter identifier, patient identifier (because the encounter is tied to the specific patient), the AI tool version, the model version, the configuration, the input size, the output size, the tokens used. The log is patient medical data under Article 321 CP. Disclosure of the log to an EU AI Act supervisory authority is a question of Swiss criminal law, EU regulatory law, nFADP Art.63 personal liability, carrier attestation accuracy, and named-physician named-encounter professional governance. The médecin cannot reconcile them with internal compliance language. The reconciliation requires named-physician, named-encounter, named-vendor, named-sub-processor architecture. The named physician sits between Article 321 CP criminal prosecution (Swiss side) and EU AI Act Article 12 enforcement (EU side) and cannot defer either. The named physician must produce the reconciliation architecture in 30 days. The named-physician named-encounter reconciliation memo is a discrete, named, 30-day deliverable the named physician produces.

**Mechanism:** Genève médecin uses AI on named encounter → AI scribe generates encounter note + automatic logging per Article 12 → log contains named-encounter content (because the log captures what was processed at the encounter level) → log is patient medical data under Article 321 CP → Article 321 CP prohibits disclosure to unauthorized parties → Article 12 may require disclosure to EU AI Act supervisory authority if authority requests log for named-encounter → Article 12 logging requirement collides with Article 321 CP criminal prohibition → médecin is named personally under both Article 321 CP (criminal exposure) and Article 26 EU AI Act (regulatory exposure) → médecin cannot reconcile with internal policy → named-physician, named-encounter reconciliation architecture required → carrier attestation wants encounter-level evidence → carrier attestation cannot request Article 321 CP-protected content → named-physician named-encounter evidence package that abstracts content (preserves Article 321 CP compliance) while providing named-physician human oversight record (satisfies Article 14 EU AI Act) and named-physician-named-encounter attestation accuracy (satisfies carrier attestation framework).

**Implication:** Article 321 CP × EU AI Act Article 12 named-encounter logging reconciliation architecture is a discrete, named C314 P04 service. It produces: named-physician internal protocol signed by the named physician specifying how automatic AI logs are generated (per Article 12), how logs are stored (named-physician access, named-encounter index), how logs are accessed (named-physician + named médecin remplaçant if authorized + named sub-specialty AI delegate if authorized), under what conditions logs are disclosed (patient consent for the named-encounter required; EU AI Act supervisory authority requests routed through the named physician with named-encounter Article 321 CP analysis), how nFADP Art.45(2) cross-border compliance is met (pseudonymization of patient identifiers where possible; sub-processor restriction; explicit patient consent for the log destination), and how the named physician integrates Article 321 CP × EU AI Act reconciliation through their internal compliance framework.
