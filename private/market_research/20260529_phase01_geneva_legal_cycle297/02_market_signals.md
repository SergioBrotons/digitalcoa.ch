# Market Signals — Geneva Legal (C297 Phase 01)

## Current Problems, Complaints, and Failures

### Finding 01: AI Vendor Wind-Down Liability — Unnamed Accountability Window

**Signal:** Small Geneva firms using AI tools that have been discontinued, significantly changed pricing, or were acquired by larger companies. The outputs produced using the discontinued version are still in client files. The attorney is accountable for work product produced under a tool configuration that no longer exists.

**Pattern:** Attorneys discovering that AI-generated work from tools that have since changed terms, updated models, or discontinued services remains in their matter files. No Geneva protocol exists for reconstructing what the tool's output was when it was used, or what representations were made about its capabilities at the time of use.

**Why distinct:** Prior findings addressed tool selection, vendor compliance, and configuration state. This is specifically the temporal dimension — accountability for AI outputs produced under tool configurations that no longer exist, with no reconstruction capability and no Geneva-specific response protocol.

**Urgency driver:** Vendor consolidation accelerating. More AI tools will be discontinued or acquired in 2026–2027. Matter files with AI-assisted work from discontinued tools are accumulating.

---

### Finding 02: Matter-Level AI Authorization Architecture — The No-AI Instruction Problem

**Signal:** Corporate clients and some private clients beginning to instruct Geneva firms not to use AI on their matters. Boutique firms have no system for flagging matter-level AI restrictions, tracking which matters have AI-use prohibitions, and ensuring no AI-assisted work enters those files.

**Pattern:** Instructions arrive in engagement letters, email, or verbal conversations. No system to make the instruction operative across the firm's AI tools. Attorneys continuing to use AI on restricted matters because the instruction was never operationalized into a firm-level system.

**Why distinct:** Prior findings named client AI concern generally. This is specifically the operational architecture gap — the instruction exists but cannot be honored because no system translates it into practice. Unique to small firms where one person manages both client relationships and AI tool usage.

**Urgency driver:** Corporate client AI governance questionnaires increasingly including matter-level AI restrictions. Instructions arriving faster than firms can operationalize them.

---

### Finding 03: AI-Assisted Billing Derivation Problem — Fee Attribution Opacity

**Signal:** When AI assists in drafting a brief, letter, research memo, or contract analysis, and the firm bills time for the work, the billing entry contains time attributable to AI assistance and time attributable to attorney review. Clients have no visibility into this split. In the event of a fee dispute or client complaint about AI-assisted work, the firm cannot demonstrate what portion of fees were for attorney judgment versus AI generation.

**Pattern:** Fee reconstruction requests from clients increasingly asking about AI involvement in billed work. Small firms with no protocol for documenting AI contribution to billed time, and no billing system designed to capture this split.

**Why distinct:** Prior billing findings addressed efficiency gains not converting to firm economics. This is specifically the client-facing fee transparency question — what does the client receive when they pay for AI-assisted work, and can the firm demonstrate the attorney review contribution that justifies the fee?

**Urgency driver:** Client sophistication increasing. Sophisticated clients beginning to ask what they are paying for when AI-assisted work appears on invoices.

---

### Finding 04: Privilege Architecture and Vendor Data Retention — Specific Collision Point

**Signal:** AI vendor data retention policies creating privilege interaction that hasn't been specifically mapped for Geneva practice. When a vendor retains query content, response content, and metadata, and the attorney-client communication about a matter flows through that vendor's systems, the question of privilege waiver through vendor access is unresolved and unnamed in Geneva practice guidance.

**Pattern:** Attorneys discovering that vendor terms include data retention provisions they accepted without review. The specific point where privilege obligations under Art.321 CP and vendor data retention under standard AI tool terms collide has not been mapped by any Geneva authority.

**Why distinct:** Prior findings named Swiss hosting as a consideration and nFADP Art.9 compliance generally. This is specifically the privilege-and-retention collision — the specific mechanism by which vendor data retention creates privilege waiver risk, and the absence of any Geneva-specific guidance on this intersection.

**Urgency driver:** Vendors updating terms regularly. Each update potentially modifies the retention posture of matter communications that flowed through their systems.

---

### Finding 05: Geneva AI Incident Response Protocol Gap — The 72-Hour Problem

**Signal:** When something goes wrong with AI use in a client matter — wrong advice, hallucinated case law, incorrect calculation, data processing error — small Geneva firms have no defined incident response protocol. No Geneva-specific guidance exists for what a boutique firm should do in the first 24, 48, or 72 hours after discovering an AI-assisted error in a matter.

**Pattern:** Attorneys describing scrambling responses to AI incidents with no Geneva-specific reference. Managing partner making decisions about client disclosure, file documentation, and carrier notification without a Geneva-calibrated protocol for professional services AI incidents.

**Why distinct:** Prior findings addressed AI research hallucination as liability and client disclosure sequencing. This is specifically the response protocol gap — what to do in the acute window after an incident is discovered, when reconstruction decisions must be made before the trail disappears. Named as discrete gap with specific time sensitivity.

**Urgency driver:** AI errors in legal practice becoming more frequent as AI use intensifies. No Geneva-specific incident response protocol for boutique firms means errors compound before any structured response occurs.

---

### Finding 06: SAV Guidelines Operational Translation Gap — The Missing Decision Tool

**Signal:** The SAV published AI guidelines for Swiss attorneys. Managing partners at small Geneva firms describe the guidelines as conceptually correct but operationally unusable — they provide principles without decision trees, responsibility assignments without implementation paths, and accountability standards without measurement methodology.

**Pattern:** Attorneys who have read the SAV guidelines and describe them as "correct but useless" — they confirm the attorney's responsibility is real, but provide no mechanism for discharge. The gap between "you are accountable for AI governance" and "here is how a 2–5 person firm actually implements this" is unnamed in any Geneva resource.

**Why distinct:** Prior findings named the SAV guidelines as accountability trigger and the gridlock pattern from SAV framework without decision tree (C294). This finding is specifically the operational translation gap as a discrete service product opportunity — the missing tool is not just a gap, it is a named product concept: a decision-tree implementation guide for SAV AI guidelines at boutique scale, specific to Geneva practice constraints, with attestation-ready output documentation.

**Urgency driver:** Carrier attestation questions explicitly referencing SAV guidelines. Attorneys need operational translation, not more principles.
