# Compliance Context — Geneva/Switzerland Legal AI (C297 Phase 01)

## Geneva/Switzerland Regulatory Framework

### Primary Regulatory Actors

**Swiss Bar Association (SAV/FSA):**
- Published AI guidelines for Swiss attorneys — accountability acknowledged, implementation absent
- Professional conduct authority over Geneva attorneys under Bundesgesetz über die Freizügigkeit der Anwälte (BGFA, SR 935.61)
- SAV guidelines constitute professional standard; breach = disciplinary exposure
- No small-firm operational translation exists for what SAV guidelines require at boutique scale
- Carrier attestation increasingly referencing SAV guidelines as accountability standard

**Geneva Cantonal Bar (ORDRE des Avocats Genevois):**
- Primary disciplinary authority for Geneva attorneys (Art. 12 LJAr)
- No Geneva-specific AI governance guidance published
- AI governance questions not addressed in cantonal practice rules
- ORDRE not providing small-firm AI governance support — structural gap
- Professional ethics jurisdiction over AI-related privilege and confidentiality questions

**Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner (FDPIC):**
- nFADP enforcement authority (in force September 1, 2023)
- Art. 9 processor obligations — data processing agreements required
- Art. 63 personal criminal liability — CHF 250,000 for intentional breaches
- FDPIC investigation trajectory observable through published patterns
- FDPIC enforcement in professional services sector (law firms) — specific enforcement attention
- No Geneva-specific guidance for how nFADP applies to attorney-client privilege context

**Geneva Court (Tribunal de première instance / Cour de justice):**
- CPTG (Code de procédure civile) — AI disclosure in civil proceedings increasingly variable by judge assignment
- No published Geneva civil procedure standard for AI-assisted filings, AI-generated evidence, or AI reasoning reconstruction
- Three-tier judicial AI exposure: filing → evidence → reasoning reconstruction (C279 Finding 03)
- Judge-specific AI disclosure expectations not systematically mapped for practitioner reference
- Geneva court AI credibility as tactical variable — bottom-of-court not just procedural (C233 Finding 04)

**Federal Council / Federal Office of Justice:**
- CoE AI Convention ratification process active — Parliamentary approval pending
- AI Convention deployer obligations flowing through existing client contracts and carrier underwriting before domestic implementation
- Federal Council AI coordination deliberation — coordination mechanism vs standard-setting authority question
- No implementing legislation yet; obligations arriving through indirect channels

### Key Regulatory Frameworks

**nFADP (revDSG / SR 235.1):**
- In force September 1, 2023
- Art. 9: Processor obligations — data processing agreements required, specific requirements for legal profession
- Art. 12: Right to explanation for automated decisions — AI-assisted legal advice potentially in scope
- Art. 13: Transparency for data subjects — beneficiary rights for AI-processed client data
- Art. 21: Accountability obligation — documentation of AI system processing
- Art. 63: Criminal liability — CHF 250,000 for intentional breaches, personal liability for managing partner
- Art. 61 lit. a: Administrative fines for breaches
- FDPIC enforcement pattern: law firms in scope, specific criteria unpublished

**BGFA (Bundesgesetz über die Freizügigkeit der Anwälte, SR 935.61):**
- Professional conduct framework for Swiss attorneys
- SAV guidelines as professional standard under BGFA
- Disciplinary exposure for AI governance inadequacy — standard defined by SAV guidelines
- Attorney liability for AI tool use under professional obligations — no operational discharge mechanism at small-firm scale

**Art. 321 CP (Swiss Criminal Code — Professional Secrecy):**
- Attorney-client privilege obligation under Swiss law
- Active contradiction with nFADP AI documentation obligations — no Geneva authority has mapped specific resolution
- Privilege-reconstruction paradox: nFADP documentation destroys privilege when implemented (C279 Finding 01)
- AI tool data processing authority absent from standard mandate letter architecture
- Vendor data retention creating privilege waiver risk unnamed in Geneva guidance

**CoE AI Convention (Council of Europe AI Convention):**
- Signed March 2025 — Parliamentary ratification pending
- Deployer obligations creating future urgency used to justify present action without published Geneva implementation
- Obligations flowing through existing client contracts and carrier underwriting before domestic implementation
- Present-tense urgency created by carrier/client/institutional references without implementation pathway existing

### Geneva-Specific Structural Factors

**CPTG (Code de procédure civile genevois):**
- AI-assisted filing disclosure — no standardized Geneva rule
- AI-generated evidence authentication — judicial discretion, no published standard
- AI reasoning reconstruction in civil proceedings — no established doctrine
- Geneva court AI procedural inconsistency — judge-specific expectations not mapped

**Geneva Judicial AI Exposure — Three Tiers:**
1. Filing: AI-assisted document submission — disclosure requirements vary by chamber
2. Evidence: AI-generated evidence admissibility — authentication standard undefined
3. Reasoning reconstruction: AI reasoning underlying legal analysis — no Geneva doctrine exists

**Cantonal Enforcement Variability:**
- Geneva CPTG enforcement more active than other cantons
- FDPIC enforcement trajectory: law firm audits in scope specifically (observable in C291–C294 rotation state)
- Carrier underwriting Geneva-specific iterations tracking (fourth-gen → fifth-gen questions)

**Swiss Hosting and Data Residency:**
- Swiss-hosted AI tools meeting FADP data residency requirements in principle
- In practice: DPA architecture requirement, vendor data retention policies, privilege implications
- Swiss hosting alone does not equal FADP compliance — architectural requirements not resolved

### The Specific Compliance Architecture Problem

**What the frameworks require vs. what they enable:**

| Framework | Obligation | Implementation Gap |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| SAV Guidelines | AI governance accountability | No decision tree, no small-firm translation |
| nFADP Art.9 | Data processing agreements | No AI-specific DPA format for legal profession |
| Art.321 CP | Client confidentiality | No resolution for AI vendor data retention collision |
| FDPIC enforcement | Law firm compliance | No Geneva-specific guidance for boutique firms |
| Carrier attestation | Defined adequacy standard | Standard undefined, attestation submitted anyway |
| AI Convention | Deployer obligations | No implementation methodology, obligations arriving indirectly |

**The gridlock pattern (C294 Finding 01):**
- SAV guidelines: framework without decision tree
- nFADP Art.9: requires DPA without specifying what compliant means for AI vendors
- FDPIC confirmation: creates more implementation questions than answers
- Result: accountability gridlock — professional knows they're responsible, has no path to discharge obligation

### Key Compliance Language

**"Adequate governance" — undefined at boutique scale:**
No Geneva authority has published a definition of adequate AI governance for a 2–5 attorney firm. The obligation is real. The discharge mechanism is absent. The attestation is signed anyway.

**"Attestation" — personal signature event:**
Carrier attestation commits the signing attorney to undefined adequacy standard with no published definition of what that standard requires. Personal liability exposure is immediate and concrete.

**"SAV compliant" — no measurement methodology:**
SAV guidelines acknowledged as professional standard but no methodology exists for measuring compliance at boutique scale. Attorneys can describe the principle, cannot demonstrate the implementation.

**"Swiss hosting" — necessary but not sufficient:**
Swiss data residency meets FDPIC Art.9 data residency requirement in principle. DPA architecture, vendor data retention, privilege implications require additional evaluation. Swiss hosting alone produces compliance appearance, not genuine protection.

### Compliance Context Summary

Geneva legal AI compliance operates in a gridlock state: obligations are real and accumulating, implementation pathways are absent, and attorneys are signing attestations to undefined standards under frameworks they cannot operationalize.

The specific Geneva structural factors:
- SAV as standards-setter without operational translation
- FDPIC as enforcement authority without small-firm guidance
- Art.321 CP as privilege obligation in active collision with nFADP documentation requirements
- Carrier attestation as acute accountability trigger with undefined standard
- AI Convention obligations arriving through indirect channels before implementation

No Geneva resource addresses the specific compliance architecture problem — the gap between obligations that are real and implementation paths that don't exist.
