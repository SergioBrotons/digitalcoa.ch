# Findings Summary: Geneva Healthcare — C303 Phase 04

## 6 NET-NEW Findings: Distinct from C214–C302

---

### Finding 01 — Carrier Attestation Activation Without Understanding

**The finding:** Medical liability carriers are asking AI governance questions at renewal. The attestation asks for evidence of "adequate AI governance" — undefined for solo and small group practices. The physician signs without understanding what the attestation commits them to personally, or what evidence they need to support the representations made. The attestation creates a record, not a defense.

**Mechanism:** Carrier attestation → personal signature required → physician signs without understanding exposure → carrier form evolves to ask for evidence → physician cannot produce evidence → personal liability exposed at renewal or claim.

**Implication:** Pre-signing review is the critical intervention. The physician needs to know what they're signing before they sign — and what documentation they need to have in place.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You signed something for your carrier. Do you know what you actually committed to — and whether you have the evidence to support it?"

---

### Finding 02 — FMH Human Oversight Has No Operational Definition for Small Practice

**The finding:** FMH states human oversight requirement without providing operational definition. The requirement exists; the Tuesday translation does not. Small practices cannot determine what documented oversight looks like, what evidence satisfies the requirement, or how to handle AI tools where the underlying decision logic is not interpretable.

**Mechanism:** "Human oversight" is interpreted differently by different practices. Some document AI input as notes. Others document the final decision. None have a consistent protocol. The FMH standard is intent-based; the evidence standard implied by carrier attestation is output-based.

**Implication:** A documented human oversight protocol specific to small practice workflows is the missing tool. This is not about whether AI can be used — it is about how to document that human oversight occurred.

**Ready-to-use language:** "FMH says human oversight. It doesn't say what that looks like in your practice at 8:30 AM on a Wednesday. I can show you."

---

### Finding 03 — The Efficiency-Liability Paradox Creates Structural Immobility

**The finding:** Independent practices are caught between operational necessity (staffing shortage requires AI for efficiency) and liability risk (AI use creates personal license exposure). Neither option is safe: not using AI means the practice cannot function; using AI means the license is at risk. The paradox creates structural immobility — the practice cannot move forward on either axis.

**Mechanism:** Staffing shortage → efficiency AI needed → AI adoption → liability exposure → FMH oversight requirement + carrier attestation → documentation required → documentation doesn't exist → liability exposed. The efficiency need and the liability risk are not separable. The practice is trapped.

**Implication:** The service that resolves this paradox is not "AI governance" or "compliance" — it is a framework that enables AI adoption for efficiency while managing the liability exposure. It is an efficiency-enabling compliance framework, not a restrictive one.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You need AI to survive. You also can't afford the liability. The service you need is one that lets you use AI without risking your license — not one that prevents you from using it."

---

### Finding 04 — Swissdamed Classification Determination Is Active and Unaddressed

**The finding:** Swissdamed July 1, 2026 applies to medical devices — not general AI systems. However, AI-enabled diagnostic tools used by independent practices may qualify as medical devices under Swissdamed. The classification determination is active for these tools, and most independent practices have not conducted it. The regulatory exposure is real and specific.

**Mechanism:** Practice uses AI tool for clinical decision support → tool qualifies as medical device under Swissdamed → classification required before July 1 → practice didn't know classification was required → operating illegally without knowing it.

**Implication:** A Swissdamed classification determination for the specific AI tools used in the practice is a discrete service. The output is not a compliance framework — it is a clear determination of whether Swissdamed applies, and what to do if it does.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Swissdamed probably doesn't apply to most of your AI tools — but you're not sure. Let me determine it definitively."

---

### Finding 05 — Patient AI Content Is Unaddressed Documentation Category

**The finding:** Patients are beginning to arrive with consumer AI-generated health analyses. The physician must evaluate and document AI input they did not request, did not use, and may have disagreed with. No documentation category exists for this scenario. The highest-accountability moment — where the physician deliberately overrides or rejects patient-provided AI — is the least-documented.

**Mechanism:** Patient brings AI-generated analysis → physician evaluates, may disagree → makes independent clinical decision → documentation normally records only final decision → AI's contribution to patient's thinking, and physician's evaluation, are invisible in record.

**Implication:** A documentation category for patient-generated AI content is a discrete named service component. The protocol handles: documenting that AI input was evaluated, documenting the physician's independent judgment, recording the final clinical decision.

**Ready-to-use language:** "When a patient brings in AI analysis, you have documentation obligations you probably haven't thought about. This is the highest-risk moment and the least-documented."

---

### Finding 06 — Vendor Contract Trap Prevents Governance Resolution Through Switching

**The finding:** Data portability limitations, model training data retention, and termination provisions in vendor contracts create structural exit barriers. The physician who wants to improve governance by switching to a more compliant vendor cannot do so — because the vendor contract prevents it. The governance improvement path is blocked by commercial terms, not by technical impossibility.

**Mechanism:** Vendor contracts written to protect vendor interests. Model training data retention means switching vendors means losing the benefit of prior interactions. Termination provisions create friction. The exit barrier is a feature, not a bug.

**Implication:** A vendor contract review and exit planning service is a discrete offering. The physician needs to know what they're locked into before they can plan their governance improvement.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Before you can improve your AI governance, you need to know what your current vendor contract actually says about data portability, model training, and termination. Most physicians don't know what they signed."
