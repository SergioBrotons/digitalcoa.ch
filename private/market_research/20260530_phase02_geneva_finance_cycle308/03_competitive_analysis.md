# Competitive Analysis: Geneva Boutique Wealth Management — AI Accountability
# C308 Phase 02 — What Exists in Market vs. What's Missing
# NET-NEW Findings

---

## Competitive Landscape Overview

The Geneva boutique wealth management AI accountability market has three observable segments:

1. **Framework providers** — publish AI governance frameworks, principles, guidelines
2. **Technology vendors** — provide AI tools with "compliance" or "governance" language
3. **Legal advisors** — address AI from a regulatory and liability perspective

The market is missing: **evidence production for specific AI-assisted decisions at the boutique wealth management scale**.

---

## What Exists in Market

### 1. Framework Providers

**What they offer:** AI governance frameworks, principles, guidelines, best practice documents.

**Limitations for Geneva boutique wealth management ICP:**
- Frameworks are abstract — they don't produce specific evidence for specific decisions
- Frameworks are often designed for large institutions, not 50–200 person boutiques
- Frameworks don't address the personal liability dimension (Article 63)
- Frameworks don't connect to carrier attestation language
- Frameworks don't address institutional DDQ documentation requirements

**Gap:** No framework provider offers a boutique-specific evidence architecture that connects institutional DDQ, carrier attestation, FINMA examination, and Article 63 personal liability in one coherent system.

---

### 2. Technology Vendors (Portfolio AI, Risk Modeling, Allocation Engines)

**What they offer:** AI-powered portfolio construction, risk modeling, allocation optimization tools.

**Limitations for Geneva boutique wealth management ICP:**
- Vendors say "you're covered" or "we're compliant" — but carrier forms ask for evidence of human authorization
- Vendor contracts define vendor liability, not managing director personal liability
- Vendors don't provide documentation for institutional DDQ questions
- Vendors don't produce evidence of human authorization for specific decisions
- Vendor language ("our AI is compliant") doesn't match carrier form language ("evidence of human authorization")

**Gap:** No portfolio AI vendor offers an evidence architecture that documents the human authorization chain for investment decisions made with AI assistance.

---

### 3. Legal Advisors (Geneva and Swiss)

**What they offer:** Regulatory analysis, liability assessment, contract review.

**Limitations for Geneva boutique wealth management ICP:**
- Legal analysis is retrospective — it explains the risk after the fact
- Legal analysis doesn't produce operational documentation
- Legal analysis doesn't connect to carrier attestation format
- Legal analysis doesn't address institutional DDQ as commercial gate
- Legal analysis doesn't offer ongoing evidence maintenance

**Gap:** No legal advisor offers a prospective evidence architecture that produces documentation in carrier-attestation-ready format while addressing institutional DDQ requirements and Article 63 personal liability.

## What's Missing (White Space)

### White Space 1: Institutional DDQ Response Documentation

**What the ICP needs:** Documentation specifically designed to answer institutional client AI governance DDQs.

**What exists:** Generic AI governance policies, framework documents.

**What's missing:** A documentation system that produces specific evidence of human authorization for AI-assisted investment decisions, formatted for institutional client review.

**Why no one serves this:** Framework providers produce principles, not evidence. Legal advisors produce analysis, not documentation. Technology vendors produce tools, not evidence architectures.

---

### White Space 2: Carrier Attestation Preparation (Renewal-Specific)

**What the ICP needs:** Pre-renewal documentation review that identifies misrepresentation risk and produces carrier-ready evidence.

**What exists:** General AI governance policy review.

**What's missing:** A renewal-specific attestation preparation service that:
- Maps actual AI use to attestation language
- Identifies gaps between what was attested and what actually happened
- Produces evidence in carrier-required format
- Addresses the misrepresentation risk for the informed ICP

**Why no one serves this:** The renewal-specific misrepresentation risk is a new problem. The ICP who signed an initial attestation without full knowledge is now in a different position at renewal. No one is addressing this distinct psychological and documentation profile.

---

### White Space 3: FINMA Examination Preparation (No Written Standard)

**What the ICP needs:** Examination preparation that addresses AI questions without a published standard to reference.

**What exists:** General regulatory compliance preparation.

**What's missing:** A preparation service that:
- Draws on documented FINMA examination AI questions
- Produces evidence of human authorization in supervisory-ready format
- Addresses the structural accountability/visibility mismatch
- Provides a defensible position when no written standard exists

**Why no one serves this:** The combination of no published standard and active examinations creates a specific preparation need that general regulatory advisors don't address.

---

### White Space 4: Portfolio Construction AI Authorization Chain Documentation

**What the ICP needs:** Specific documentation of the human authorization chain for AI-assisted investment decisions.

**What exists:** General AI governance policies that mention "human oversight" or "human-in-the-loop."

**What's missing:** A decision-level documentation system that:
- Maps specific AI recommendations to specific human decisions
- Documents when the portfolio manager follows AI recommendations vs. overrides them
- Produces evidence for institutional DDQ questions about investment methodology
- Creates a documentation trail for carrier questions about investment process

**Why no one serves this:** Portfolio construction AI is a specific, high-stakes use case. General AI governance frameworks don't address the specific documentation requirements of AI-assisted investment decisions.

---

### White Space 5: Boutique-Scale Evidence Architecture (One Partner, Full Accountability)

**What the ICP needs:** A documentation system designed for the boutique wealth management structural reality: one managing director or partner personally accountable, distributed AI use across multiple employees and mandates.

**What exists:** Documentation systems designed for large institutions with dedicated compliance teams.

**What's missing:** A scaled evidence architecture that:
- Operates within the boutique firm's operational constraints
- Provides the managing director with coherent documentation across the firm's AI footprint
- Doesn't require a dedicated compliance team to maintain
- Addresses Article 63 personal liability for the managing director specifically

**Why no one serves this:** The boutique wealth management structural reality is specific. Large-firm documentation systems don't scale down. Small-firm general compliance tools don't address the specific accountability model.

## Competitive Moats

### Moat 1: Evidence Architecture (Not Framework)

The primary competitive moat is **evidence architecture**, not framework language. The market has frameworks. It needs evidence. Evidence architecture is the structural moat: it requires understanding carrier form language, institutional DDQ format, FINMA examination patterns, and Article 63 personal liability — all in one coherent system.

### Moat 2: Boutique-Scale Operation Model

The boutique wealth management ICP (50–200 employees, one managing director accountable) requires an operation model that works at their scale. Large-firm approaches don't scale down. The operation model that serves the boutique wealth manager specifically is a structural moat.

### Moat 3: Bilingual EN + FR Capability

Geneva wealth management operates in both English and French. Most competitors serve one language market. The ability to operate in both — with documentation, communication, and evidence in both languages — is a structural moat in the Geneva market.

### Moat 4: Renewal Window Expertise

The renewal window is a specific, time-bounded trigger with distinct ICP psychology. The ICP at renewal is more informed, more anxious, and more exposed than at initial attestation. Expertise in renewal-specific documentation and misrepresentation risk is a distinct competitive capability.

## Competitive Vulnerabilities of Incumbents

### Framework Provider Vulnerability
Frameworks don't produce evidence. The ICP who receives an institutional DDQ cannot use a framework to answer it. The framework provider cannot help the ICP document specific human authorization for specific AI-assisted decisions.

### Technology Vendor Vulnerability
Vendor language ("we're compliant") doesn't match carrier form language ("evidence of human authorization"). The vendor has no incentive to produce documentation that might reveal the firm's actual AI use. The vendor's interest is in "compliance" language, not evidence.

### Legal Advisor Vulnerability
Legal analysis is retrospective and billable by the hour. The ICP who needs ongoing evidence maintenance cannot afford to call their lawyer every time an associate uses an AI tool on a client matter. Legal advice is not evidence production.

## Key Competitive Insight

The competitive landscape is organized around **frameworks, tools, and analysis**. The white space is **evidence production for specific AI-assisted decisions at the boutique wealth management scale**.

The ICP doesn't need a framework. They need evidence. They don't need a tool. They need documentation. They don't need analysis. They need a system that produces evidence in carrier-attestation-ready format, institutional DDQ-ready format, and FINMA-examination-ready format — simultaneously.

The category is not "AI governance." The category is "evidence of human authorization for specific AI-assisted decisions at boutique wealth management scale."
