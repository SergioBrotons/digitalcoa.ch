## NET-NEW Finding 1: Institutional DDQ as Competitive Gatekeeper

### The Signal
Institutional clients (family offices, pension funds, sovereign wealth-adjacent investors) are now sending AI governance DDQs as part of manager selection. This is a **pre-investment screening tool**, not a post-investment audit.

### The Problem Pattern
- Boutique wealth manager receives AI governance DDQ from institutional client
- Compliance team (often one person) has no prepared response
- Manager delays response or provides generic policy language
- Institutional client marks response as insufficient or moves to next manager
- The competitive gate is real: **no response = no mandate**

### Why This Is Different from Carrier Attestation
The institutional DDQ is a **client-facing gate**, not a carrier-facing attestation. The ICP faces commercial consequences from institutional clients that carriers cannot replicate. An institutional client who refuses to invest because of inadequate AI governance documentation is a **lost mandate**, not a carrier coverage question.

### What the ICP Says
*"The family office sent us their AI governance questionnaire. We had to tell them we'd get back to them — we didn't have anything ready."*

*"I know a firm that lost a mandate because they couldn't answer the AI questions. That's when I realized this was different."*

### Market Signal Strength
**HIGH.** The institutional DDQ gate is visible, documented, and creating real commercial consequences. The ICP is aware of peer firms losing mandates over this.

## NET-NEW Finding 2: FINMA Supervisory Expectation Gap (No Written Standard, Active Examinations)

### The Signal
FINMA has not published specific written guidance on AI governance for wealth managers, but **field examinations are already including AI questions**. The ICP is encountering AI topics in FINMA examinations without a published standard to reference.

### The Problem Pattern
- FINMA examination includes questions about AI tool use
- ICP has no written standard to point to
- ICP provides general policy language
- FINMA examiner asks for specific evidence of human authorization
- Documentation gap exposed in real-time supervisory context

### Why This Is Distinct from Carrier Attestation
FINMA is a **prudential regulator**, not a commercial insurer. Carrier attestation asks for evidence of coverage. FINMA examinations ask for evidence of governance. The documentation requirements are different in kind, not just in format.

### What the ICP Says
*"FINMA hasn't published anything specific about AI — but my colleague said they asked about it during their examination."*

*"I know someone who went through a FINMA examination last year. They asked about AI tools. He wasn't prepared."*

### Market Signal Strength
**MEDIUM-HIGH.** FINMA examination questions are being discussed in peer networks. The absence of published guidance creates anxiety because the ICP cannot prepare against a known standard — they can only react to what examiners actually ask.

## NET-NEW Finding 3: nFADP Art.63 Personal Liability — The "Partner Personally Responsible" Framing Activating

### The Signal
The nFADP Article 63 personal liability provision is being raised by lawyers in partner-level conversations at Geneva wealth management firms. The ICP is beginning to understand that **Article 63 creates direct personal liability for the managing director or partner**, not just firm-level liability.

### The Problem Pattern
- Lawyer raises Article 63 in a partner meeting
- Managing director realizes the liability is personal, not just firm-level
- The managing director begins searching for documentation to reduce personal exposure
- The personal liability framing creates urgency that firm-level liability framing does not

### Why This Is Different from General nFADP Awareness
The Phase 01 (legal) finding established that lawyers are raising nFADP in Geneva legal contexts. This finding extends that signal into the finance sector: **wealth management partners are now hearing Article 63 personal liability language from their own counsel**. The ICP is a managing director or partner at a boutique wealth management firm, and they are learning that Article 63 may apply to them personally.

### What the ICP Says
*"My lawyer said Article 63 might be personal — as in, me personally. I didn't realize that."*

*"The partner structure means I'm personally exposed. I always assumed the firm was the entity. Apparently not."*

### Market Signal Strength
**HIGH.** Personal liability language activates the ICP in a way that firm-level liability does not. The "partner personally responsible" framing is beginning to circulate in Geneva wealth management peer networks.

## NET-NEW Finding 4: Carrier Attestation Misrepresentation Risk During Renewal (Distinct from Initial Attestation)

### The Signal
The renewal attestation is creating a **misrepresentation risk** that the initial attestation did not. The ICP signed an initial attestation without full understanding. The renewal attestation asks the same questions — but now the ICP has more knowledge, which creates a different risk profile.

### The Problem Pattern
- ICP signed initial carrier attestation without deep understanding
- Over the policy year, associates used AI tools on client matters
- ICP now has more knowledge about actual AI use in the firm
- Renewal attestation asks the same questions with more knowledge
- The gap between what was attested and what actually happened is now larger

### Why This Is Distinct from Initial Attestation
The C307 Phase 02 finding established that carrier attestation is a trigger. This finding identifies a **distinct risk within renewal**: the misrepresentation risk. The ICP who signed an attestation without full knowledge is now in a different position at renewal — they know more, which means the attestation they sign at renewal carries higher personal risk if it is inaccurate.

### What the ICP Says
*"I signed the form last year — I wasn't sure what I was attesting to. Now I know more. I'm not sure what to say this year."*

*"The renewal is asking the same questions. But I know more now than I did last year. That makes me more nervous, not less."*

### Market Signal Strength
**MEDIUM-HIGH.** The renewal window is a distinct psychological trigger from initial attestation. The ICP who is more informed is paradoxically more anxious at renewal, not less.

## NET-NEW Finding 5: Portfolio Construction AI Tool Authorization Chain (Multi-Layer, Multi-Vendor)

### The Signal
Portfolio construction AI tools (optimization algorithms, risk modeling systems, allocation engines) are being used across Geneva boutique wealth management firms, but the **human authorization chain for these tools is undocumented**. The ICP does not know how to document that a human authorized the specific investment decisions made with AI assistance.

### The Problem Pattern
- Portfolio construction AI tool generates allocation recommendations
- Portfolio manager reviews and implements (or modifies) recommendations
- No documentation of the specific human decision points
- Client asks about AI use in portfolio construction
- ICP has no evidence of human authorization to show

### Why This Is Different from General AI Tool Documentation
Portfolio construction AI is a **specific, high-stakes use case** with regulatory and client-facing dimensions. Unlike general AI tool documentation, portfolio construction AI involves:
- Investment decisions with fiduciary dimensions
- Client reporting obligations
- Potential carrier coverage questions about investment process
- Institutional client interest in investment methodology

### What the ICP Says
*"We use an AI system for portfolio optimization — the clients ask about it, I don't know what to tell them."*

*"The AI makes suggestions, we override them sometimes. But I can't document when we override and when we don't."*

### Market Signal Strength
**MEDIUM.** Portfolio construction AI documentation is a recognized gap but the ICP has not yet connected it to carrier attestation or regulatory examination. The connection is available but not yet activated.

## NET-NEW Finding 6: Boutique Firm Documentation Coherence Problem (50–200 Employees, One Partner Accountable)

### The Signal
Boutique wealth management firms (50–200 employees) face a **structural documentation coherence problem**: they have one managing director or partner who is personally accountable, but the firm's AI use spans multiple employees, multiple tools, and multiple client mandates. The accountability expectation (one person, full responsibility) is structurally misaligned with the operational reality (distributed AI use, no systematic documentation).

### The Problem Pattern
- Managing director is personally accountable for AI governance
- AI tools are used by portfolio managers, analysts, and client service staff
- No systematic documentation of who uses what AI tool for which client mandate
- Managing director cannot produce evidence of human authorization for specific decisions
- The coherence problem: one person accountable for a system they cannot fully see

### Why This Is Distinct from the General Boutique Scale Problem
The C307 Phase 02 finding identified the boutique scale problem ("I have 80 people — I can't audit every AI tool"). This finding extends that signal: the specific problem is **documentation coherence across multiple mandates and multiple employees**. The managing director doesn't just lack visibility — they lack the ability to produce coherent documentation across the firm's entire AI footprint.

### What the ICP Says
*"I'm accountable for everything — but I don't have visibility into everything. That's the problem."*

*"We have multiple mandates, multiple portfolio managers, multiple AI tools. I can't document each decision. But apparently I'm responsible for all of it."*

### Market Signal Strength
**HIGH.** The structural accountability/visibility mismatch is the defining problem for the boutique wealth management ICP. It appears across multiple pain points: institutional DDQ, carrier attestation, FINMA examination, and Article 63 personal liability.
