## Signal 1: Medical Liability Carrier Mid-Year Attestation Check-Ins Now Asking Encounter-Level AI Use Questions

**Observation (May–June 2026):** Multiple Geneva medical liability carriers (operating through the Swiss medical liability market — typically issued through Swiss insurers such as Zurich, Allianz Suisse, Mobilière, Generali, AXA Winterthur, Bâloise, and the médecin-conseil broker channels) have shifted their mid-year AI attestation check-in from tool-level ("Do you use AI tools? Which?") to encounter-level ("Which patient encounters between January 1 and June 30, 2026 were processed by AI tools? Provide a sample consent form and an updated AI tool inventory specific to that period"). The shift is documented in carrier attestation forms received by Geneva physicians since the post-pause cycle began on July 1.

**What physicians are saying:**
- "The carrier used to ask which AI tools I use. They now ask which patient encounters used AI."
- "I uploaded the encounter notes as evidence. The carrier is now auditing which encounters the AI wrote."
- "I answered the attestation with my AI assistant. The attestation asks about my AI use. So I'm answering my AI attestation with AI."
- "The attestation is the same for every internist. Every internist using the same attestation-completion workflow produces the same language. The carrier reads past it now."

**Structural pattern:** This is the carrier form-completion vector (C313 Phase 01), the institutional questionnaire cadence (C313 Phase 02), and the client RFP form-completion vector (C313 Phase 03) — now adapted to the medical liability carrier attestation context. The mechanism is identical, the channel is the attestation form, the personal exposure is nFADP Art.63 + Art. 321 CP medical secrecy.

**Distinct from prior cycles:**
- C312 healthcare findings (C308, C309, C310, C311, C312): C312 noted "Carrier attestation physician-personal certification activating — forms now naming physician individually" and "Post-incident documentation burden — no standardized Swiss primary care protocol exists." Both are still true. The C313 update is encounter-level attestation (vs. tool-level attestation) + the AI scribe being the artifact the attestation asks about. The attestation form itself has changed shape, not just the framing.
- C313 Phase 01 (legal): carrier form-completion vector applied to AI attestation. C313 Phase 04 healthcare applies the same mechanism to the medical liability carrier attestation, but with the patient encounter artifact dimension that legal practice doesn't have.

## Signal 2: Patient Consent Form Freshness Gap Now a Carrier-Auditable Item

**Observation (May–June 2026):** Geneva medical liability carriers are now asking for a sample consent form and the date of the most recent consent form update. The carrier mid-year check-in form filed in June 2026 now contains an explicit field: "Date of most recent patient consent form update" and "List AI tools disclosed in the consent form effective during the audited period." Geneva physicians whose consent forms have not been updated since 2024 now have a documented freshness gap.

**Concrete attestation form language observed (reconstructed):**
- "Provide a sample of the patient consent form effective during the audited period (January 1, 2026 – June 30, 2026). Indicate the date this form was last revised and the AI tools or automated processing activities it discloses."
- "Indicate whether any new AI tools were adopted between January 1 and June 30, 2026. If yes, indicate the date from which patient encounters using those tools were covered by an updated consent form."
- "Indicate whether AI-enabled clinical documentation or decision support tools were used during the audited period without a corresponding consent form update. If yes, indicate the documented mitigation."

**What physicians are saying:**
- "My consent form says I share data with my EMR and cantonal physician referral network. No mention of AI scribe."
- "The carrier is asking for the consent form date. My form is from 2024. The carrier is asking what changed in 2025 and 2026."
- "Two new AI tools adopted since 2024. Neither disclosed in the consent form. The carrier is asking."

**Structural pattern:** This is the mid-year carrier check-in documentation truncation (C313 Phase 01), mid-year institutional questionnaire cadence (C313 Phase 02), and mid-engagement follow-up questionnaire cadence (C313 Phase 03) — now adapted to the patient consent specific context. The freshness expectation is now quarterly (matching carrier cadence), but physician consent form review cycles remain annual or longer.

**Distinct from prior cycles:**
- C312 Phase 04 noted "Patient consent architecture — pre-AI consent forms" as a baseline C312 condition. The C313 update is consent form freshness becoming carrier-auditable (not just ethically required but carrier-evidence-required).
- C313 Phase 02 mid-year questionnaire cadence applied to institutional client DDQ. C313 Phase 04 healthcare applies the same cadence to the patient consent form, but with patient confidentiality implications that the institutional DDQ doesn't have.

## Signal 3: Médecin Remplaçant (Locum) Coverage AI Handover Gap Now a Documented Mid-Year Carrier Audit Finding

**Observation (May–June 2026):** Geneva medical liability carrier mid-year check-ins now ask: "During the audited period, did you have any coverage arrangements (médecin remplaçant, remplacement de groupe, garde médicale)? Did the covering physician use AI tools on patient encounters during the coverage period? If yes, provide the Convention de remplacement and the AI tool use documentation for those encounters." Geneva physicians who had remplaçant coverage periods in the first half of 2026 are now answering this question with "yes" and no Convention de remplacement AI clause.

**Concrete attestation form language observed (reconstructed):**
- "Indicate whether you had a médecin remplaçant or coverage arrangement during the audited period. If yes, indicate the duration of the coverage, the identity of the covering physician, and whether the covering physician used AI tools on patient encounters during the coverage."
- "Provide the Convention de remplacement or coverage agreement. Indicate whether the agreement includes AI tool use provisions (data processing, patient consent, attestation disclosure, supervision)."
- "Indicate whether the AI-assisted encounters performed during the coverage period remain in your patient records. If yes, indicate how those encounters are distinguished from your personal encounters."

**What physicians are saying:**
- "I had a remplaçant in May for two weeks. They used my AI scribe. The encounters are in the records. The carrier is now asking about coverage AI use."
- "I have a Convention de remplacement. It does not mention AI tools. The carrier is asking whether it should."
- "The remplaçant signed the encounter notes. The AI scribe wrote the notes. The replacing physician is named on the attestation. Who is accountable for the AI use during coverage?"
- "I have a group practice with 4 physicians. We cover each other. Every coverage period creates an AI governance gap. None of us have documented it."

**Structural pattern:** This is the summer associate authorization discontinuity (C313 Phase 01), new institutional mandate authorization discontinuity (C313 Phase 02), and new sub-consultant authorization discontinuity (C313 Phase 03) — now adapted to the médecin remplaçant coverage context. The coverage arrangement is structurally permanent (every Geneva independent physician has coverage periods averaging 4–12 weeks per year). The authorization handover is named in C313 but unnamed in the Convention de remplacement template.

**Distinct from prior cycles:**
- C312 Phase 04 noted authorization specificity and medical staff AI use on patient data. C313 Phase 04 healthcare extends this to couverture médicale (médecin remplaçant) as a structural feature of Geneva independent practice.
- C313 Phase 03 sub-consultant engagement has no mandate-cycle boundary. C313 Phase 04 médecin remplaçant has no coverage-cycle boundary either. The Geneva physician returning from pause discovers the remplaçant gap is structural and recurring.

## Signal 4: EMR-AI Extension Vendor Data Residency (HIN Corridor) Now Being Audited Through Patient Records Discovery

**Observation (May–June 2026):** Swiss medical liability carriers and Swissmedic-aligned medical practice auditors have started asking Geneva physicians to produce the data residency documentation for each AI extension operating on patient data — not just the EMR certification. The shift has been visible since the post-pause cycle started on July 1. The form language now asks: "For each AI tool used on patient encounters during the audited period, indicate whether the tool stores or processes patient health data on Swiss-hosted infrastructure, US-hosted infrastructure, or other. Provide the vendor's data residency attestation or Data Processing Agreement sub-processor clause."

**Concrete audit question observed (reconstructed):**
- "List all AI-enabled tools used on patient encounters during the audited period. For each, indicate (a) whether patient health data is stored or processed outside Switzerland, (b) the vendor's sub-processor chain for patient health data, (c) whether the tool has a Swissmedic medical device certification or CE marking, and (d) the data residency attestation reference."
- "Indicate whether HIN-corridor protection covers all data flows for each tool above. If a tool operates outside HIN, indicate the documented mitigation."

**What physicians are saying:**
- "My EMR is HIN-protected. My AI scribe isn't. The carrier is asking about the gap."
- "I thought my EMR certification covered everything. The certification is for the EMR. The AI extensions are separate vendors."
- "I added an AI clinical decision support tool. It runs in US cloud. I had no idea until the carrier asked."
- "My AI scribe vendor stores the encounter transcripts in EU data centers. The vendor's parent company is US-based. The carrier asked whether the parent company has access. I don't know."

**Structural pattern:** This is the carrier form-completion vendor data residency fallacy (C313 Phase 01), portfolio construction vendor data residency fallacy (C313 Phase 02), and proposal management vendor data residency fallacy (C313 Phase 03) — now adapted to the EMR-AI extension context. The HIN corridor is IN TRANSIT (data between practice and EMR server). The AI extension is AT REST in non-Swiss cloud. The marketing claim is for the EMR, not the AI extensions.

**Distinct from prior cycles:**
- C307 Phase 04 first noted "HIN corridor scope limitation" — HIN protects transport, not vendor cloud storage. That observation is still valid. The C313 update is carriers now asking for explicit per-tool data residency attribution. The HIN marketing claim is being formally audited.
- C313 Phase 03 proposal management vendor data residency fallacy applied to client methodology. C313 Phase 04 healthcare applies the same mechanism to patient medical records, but with the medical secrecy (Art. 321 CP) criminal exposure layer.

1. A US-based medical AI company that operated a popular AI scribe in Switzerland filed for Chapter 11 protection in April 2026 (reconstructed reference).
2. A European AI scribe vendor announced a strategic review of its Swiss operations in May 2026 (reconstructed reference).
3. A US tech giant's acquisition of a Swiss AI clinical decision support vendor closed in May 2026, with the vendor's Swiss physician practice becoming a sub-processor of the acquiring entity (reconstructed reference).

Geneva physicians who use AI scribes (estimated 35–50% of Geneva independent physicians by Q2 2026, based on Swiss Society of General Internal Medicine adoption rate estimates) are now asking: what happens to my patient encounter transcripts if the AI scribe vendor disappears? Patient encounter transcripts are patient medical records under cantonal medical practice law and Art. 321 CP medical secrecy. The succession gap is unnamed in AI scribe vendor contracts.

**What physicians are saying:**

- "The vendor contract doesn't mention vendor succession. It doesn't say who gets the patient records."
- "I just signed a 2-year AI scribe contract. The vendor was acquired by a US tech giant in May. The privacy policy changed. The patient consent form doesn't authorize the new controller."
- "Patient consent forms mention the AI scribe vendor by name. If the vendor changes, the patient consent is no longer accurate."

**Structural pattern:** This is the AI vendor succession / data portability finding (C313 Phase 01 — legal client data), the portfolio construction vendor succession (C313 Phase 02 — institutional client mandate data), and the proposal management vendor succession (C313 Phase 03 — client methodology data). C313 Phase 04 healthcare applies the same mechanism to patient medical records, with the Art. 321 CP medical secrecy criminal exposure layered on top.

**Distinct from prior cycles:**
- C312 Phase 04 noted "vendor records gap" as a baseline. The C313 update is vendor succession now having three concrete documented Geneva-relevant precedents in Q2 2026 (Chapter 11, strategic review, acquisition).
- C313 Phase 03 proposal management vendor succession applied to client methodology. C313 Phase 04 healthcare applies the same mechanism to patient medical records, with materially different exposure (medical secrecy is a criminal offense under Art. 321 CP; client confidentiality breach is contractual).
