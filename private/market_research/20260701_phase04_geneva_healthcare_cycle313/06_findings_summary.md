## The Six NET-NEW Findings — Detailed

### Finding 01: Medical Liability Carrier AI Attestation Form-Completion Vector — The AI Scribe Encounter Note Loop

**The finding:** The Geneva medical liability carrier (typically operating through médecin-conseil brokers within Generali, Bâloise, Mobilière, AXA Winterthur, Allianz Suisse, Zurich physician channels) has shifted the AI attestation from tool-level ("do you use AI tools, which?") to encounter-level ("which patient encounters between [date range] were processed by AI tools, with what documentation, on what consent basis, with what human authorization"). The Geneva physician is now answering the attestation by entering patient encounter transcripts (which were AI-scribe-generated), AI tool inventory data, sub-specialty referral AI use logs, and patient consent forms — exactly what the attestation asks about — into attestation-completion tools (or AI assistants, or copy-paste from EMR exports). The attestation response now passes through attestation-completion workflows the carrier has not separately evaluated. The physician's encounter transcripts are now processed through a vendor the carrier's own due diligence has not reviewed. nFADP Art.63 personal exposure (plus Art. 321 CP medical secrecy criminal exposure) loop is closed at the attestation level. The attestation becomes generic (every physician using the same attestation-completion workflow produces similar language). The carrier reads past the generic attestation to find encounter-specific evidence. The physician's coverage is at risk because the attestation wasn't encounter-specific enough.

**Mechanism:** Carrier issues attestation asking encounter-level AI use → physician enters encounter transcripts (AI-scribe-generated) → attestation-completion tool processes patient confidences through its AI → attestation response generated → response submitted → attestation now incorporates AI-scribe-generated artifacts processed through a separately-disclosed attestation-completion tool → nFADP Art.63 + Art. 321 CP medical secrecy personal exposure vector is doubled → response is generic because every physician using the same workflow produces similar language → carrier underwriting committee reads past generic language → physician's specific encounter evidence is invisible → coverage assessment at risk.

**Implication:** Medical liability AI attestation form-completion vendor audit is a discrete, named C313 service. It audits the workflow the Geneva physician uses to complete the carrier AI attestation, assesses whether those tools process patient encounter transcripts through vendors the carrier has not separately evaluated, evaluates whether the attestation response becomes generic (and therefore loses physician-specific encounter evidence), and produces a remediation plan for an attestation response anchored to physician-specific encounter evidence. The service also addresses the attestation loop exposure — the AI scribe writes the encounter note, the attestation asks for the encounter note, the attestation is asking for the artifact the AI is producing. The first Geneva physician AI governance service to publish the attestation form-completion vendor audit owns the C313 encounter-level category.

**Ready-to-use language:** "The attestation your carrier sent you is asking about AI use at the encounter level. The encounter note is the evidence. The encounter note was written by your AI scribe. So you are answering the attestation by uploading the artifact the attestation is asking about. nFADP Art.63 reads through that workflow. Art. 321 CP reads through that workflow. I'm auditing the attestation-completion workflow you're using and showing you how to anchor the attestation response to your specific encounter evidence — before the carrier mid-year check-in becomes a coverage gap."

### Finding 02: Patient Consent Form Freshness Gap — Mid-Year Carrier Audit Trigger

**The finding:** The mid-year medical liability carrier check-in now asks specifically: "Date of most recent patient consent form update" and "List AI tools disclosed in the consent form effective during the audited period." The Geneva physician whose consent form has not been updated since 2024 (and who has adopted two new AI tools since 2024) now has a documented freshness gap. The carrier's mid-year check-in form has converted the patient consent form from a static compliance document into a dynamic carrier-auditable artifact with freshness expectations. The gap widens with every mid-year cycle. Documentation aged within the carrier's audit period (e.g., 6 months for mid-year check-in, 3 months for sub-specialty referral) is now the freshness standard. Physician consent form review cycles remain annual or longer. The gap is unannounced in patient consent template publications.

**Mechanism:** Physician adopts AI scribe in mid-2024 (example) → AI clinical decision support adopted late-2024 (example) → carrier mid-year check-in form received June 2026 → form asks for consent form date and AI tool inventory disclosed → consent form is from early-2024, no AI tool disclosed → carrier assesses patient consent gap → carrier notes AI tool adoption without updated consent form coverage → patient confidentiality gap documented → carrier may require consent form update as a condition of continued coverage → physician has 30–90 days to update consent form → consent form update touches patient signature workflow (every active patient may need new consent) → coverage gap risk escalates.

**Implication:** Patient consent form freshness audit (mid-year carrier compliance) is a discrete service. It audits the patient consent form against the carrier mid-year check-in requirements (consent form date, AI tool inventory disclosed, freshness against current AI tool adoption), produces a consent form freshness audit memo, and produces a consent form revision plan with explicit AI tool disclosure (AI scribe, AI clinical decision support, AI drug-interaction checkers, sub-specialty AI delegation), HIN corridor exposure disclosure, and AI vendor data residency disclosure. The service is bounded, named, and triggerable at any mid-year carrier check-in or new AI tool adoption moment.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your patient consent form is from 2024. Your AI scribe was adopted in 2025. The carrier's mid-year check-in is asking which patients consented to which tools. The form doesn't mention any of them. Art. 321 CP doesn't care that you intended to update the form later. I'm auditing your consent form freshness against your current AI tool inventory and showing you what needs to change before the next mid-year check-in becomes a coverage gap."

### Finding 03: Médecin Remplaçant Authorization Discontinuity + Sub-Specialty Referral AI Delegation Gap — The Permanent AI Handover Window

**The finding:** The Geneva independent physician who engages a médecin remplaçant for coverage (vacation, conference, illness, parental leave, professional development) creates the most structurally permanent AI authorization chain gap in the Geneva healthcare market. Remplaçant engagement has no employment-cycle boundary and no patient-consent-cycle boundary — every coverage period creates a new documentation obligation. The replaced physician authorizes the remplaçant's coverage through a Convention de remplacement. The replaced physician does NOT authorize the remplaçant's AI tool use. The remplaçant uses the practice's AI scribe (or their own AI tools) on the remplaçant's encounters with the practice's patients. The remplaçant signs the encounter notes. The encounters stay in the patient records. The replaced physician's medical liability carrier may not cover the remplaçant's AI use. The patient consent form the patient signed does not authorize the remplaçant's AI tool use. nFADP Art.63 personal exposure is layered through both physicians. Art. 321 CP medical secrecy applies to the remplaçant's encounters as if they were the replaced physician's encounters. The authorization chain handover is structural (every coverage period creates one) and never documented.

In addition, the Geneva physician who refers a patient to a sub-specialist (radiology, pathology, dermatology AI image analysis) authorizes the referral but does NOT authorize the sub-specialist's AI tool use on the patient data. The sub-specialist's AI tool use (e.g., radiology AI for image analysis) operates on the patient data the referring physician is also accountable for. The patient consent form may not cover sub-specialty AI use. The carrier attestation may require the referring physician to disclose sub-specialty AI use. The sub-specialty AI vendor's data residency is not in the referring physician's AI tool inventory. nFADP Art.63 + Art. 321 CP medical secrecy exposure is layered.

**Mechanism:** Geneva physician engages remplaçant for two weeks in May (example) → remplaçant activates practice AI scribe on remplaçant's encounters with practice patients → replaced physician unaware of specific tool use → replaced physician's attestation is for personal encounters only → remplaçant signs encounter notes → encounters stay in patient records → carrier mid-year check-in asks for remplaçant period encounters and AI tool use → Convention de remplacement doesn't include AI clauses → exposure documented → coverage gap risk.

Parallel mechanism: Geneva physician refers patient to radiology for imaging (example) → radiologist activates radiology AI for image analysis on the imaging → referring physician unaware of specific tool use → referring physician's attestation is for personal practice AI only → patient data flows through radiology AI vendor → radiologist's AI vendor data residency not in referring physician's inventory → patient consent form doesn't authorize sub-specialty AI use → exposure documented → coverage gap risk.

**Implication:** Médecin remplaçant + sub-specialty referral AI authorization documentation tied to coverage cycle + sub-specialty referral cadence — not annual attestation cycle. The service produces: Convention de remplacement AI clause (replacing physician AI tool use authorization, encounter documentation distinguishing remplaçant vs. personal encounters, patient consent form coverage during remplaçant period, carrier attestation disclosure during remplaçant period, Art. 321 CP medical secrecy allocation); sub-specialty AI delegation documentation (radiology / pathology / dermatology AI use, sub-specialty AI vendor data residency, referring physician patient consent form coverage, carrier attestation disclosure). This is the discrete, named service the C313 Geneva physician needs and no competitor has built. The service is structurally parallel to the C313 Phase 01 summer associate authorization handover documentation (legal, employment-cycle boundary), the C313 Phase 02 new institutional mandate authorization discontinuity (finance, mandate-cycle boundary), and the C313 Phase 03 sub-consultant authorization discontinuity (consulting, sub-consultant engagement boundary), but applied to the Geneva healthcare context where the handover windows are structural and recurring.

**Ready-to-use language:** "You authorized your médecin remplaçant's coverage. You didn't authorize their AI scribe. The encounter notes are in your patient records. The carrier's mid-year check-in is asking about coverage period AI use. The Convention de remplacement doesn't include AI clauses. nFADP Art.63 is your exposure. Art. 321 CP is your exposure. Your sub-specialty referrals to radiology and pathology may use AI on the patient's imaging or histology — you authorized the referral, you didn't authorize the AI. I'm documenting the remplaçant AI authorization chain and the sub-specialty AI delegation before the carrier mid-year check-in becomes a coverage gap."

### Finding 04: EMR-AI Extension Vendor Data Residency as False Reassurance — The HIN Corridor Scope Limitation

**The finding:** The Geneva physician using a Swiss-certified EMR (HIN-protected, "Swiss-hosted," often labeled "nFADP-compliant" or "Swissmedic-certified medical device") has a documented misconception. The certification is for the EMR software itself. The certification does NOT extend to the AI extensions the physician has added: AI scribe (often US-based — Nuance DAX, Microsoft Nuance, Abridge, Suki), AI clinical decision support (often US-based — UpToDate, DynaMed, GPT-augmented clinical reasoning), AI drug-interaction checkers (often US-based — First Databank, Cerner Multum), AI image analysis for sub-specialty referrals (often US-based — Aidoc, Arterys, Paige), AI clinical documentation (often US-based). The EMR's HIN corridor protects data IN TRANSIT between the practice and the EMR server. It does NOT protect data AT REST in the AI extension vendor's US-based cloud. Patient medical records (regulated under cantonal physician practice law and Art. 321 CP medical secrecy) flow through non-Swiss cloud for every AI extension the physician has added. nFADP Art.63 reads through the EMR certification. Art. 321 CP medical secrecy reads through. The carrier's mid-year check-in now asks for per-tool data residency attestation — the HIN corridor marketing claim does not satisfy that attestation for the AI extensions.

**Mechanism:** Geneva physician selects Swiss-certified EMR on Art.321 CP medical secrecy, nFADP Art.63, and professional secrecy grounds → physician adds AI extensions (scribe, clinical decision support, drug-interaction, image analysis) → AI extensions store patient confidences in vendor's US-based cloud → EMR's HIN corridor protection is IN TRANSIT only → physician believes Art.321 CP and Art.63 satisfied → exposure persists → carrier mid-year check-in asks for per-tool data residency attestation → attestation reveals per-tool residency (some tools US-based, some EU-based, some Swiss-based) → physician's documented misconception is documented → carrier assessment of patient confidentiality exposure → coverage risk.

**Implication:** EMR-AI extension vendor data residency audit (HIN corridor scope limitation) is a discrete service. It maps the vendor's per-AI-extension sub-processor chain (logging, telemetry, authentication, monitoring, parent-company cloud) against nFADP Art.63 + Art. 321 CP medical secrecy requirements, identifies which AI extensions create extraterritorial exposure, assesses whether the HIN corridor covers each AI extension's data flow, and produces a per-tool EMR-AI extension vendor residency audit memo. The service is bounded, named, and triggerable at any new AI extension adoption or any sub-processor chain change.

**Ready-to-use language:** "Your EMR is Swiss-certified. Your AI scribe is not. Your clinical decision support tool is not. Your drug-interaction checker is not. The HIN corridor protects the transport — it doesn't protect the at-rest data in the US-based cloud where your AI extensions store patient confidences. nFADP Art.63 reads through the EMR certification. Art. 321 CP reads through. The carrier's mid-year check-in is asking per-tool residency. I'm auditing the sub-processors your EMR vendor didn't disclose and the sub-processors your AI extension vendors didn't disclose — before the next attestation cycle makes the gap undeniable."

### Finding 06: AI Scribe / Clinical Decision Support Vendor Succession / Patient Medical Records Portability — Art. 321 CP Medical Secrecy Continuity Gap

**The finding:** The AI scribe / clinical decision support / drug-interaction AI / radiology AI / pathology AI vendor may go bankrupt, get acquired by a larger player, terminate the Swiss service, or pivot the offering. When this happens, the patient encounter transcripts — which constitute patient medical records under cantonal medical practice law and Art. 321 CP medical secrecy — transfer with the vendor's asset sale to a new controller. The new controller did not negotiate Art. 321 CP medical secrecy obligations with the Geneva physician. The new controller did not negotiate nFADP Art.63 obligations with the Geneva physician. The new controller did not negotiate the patient's right to medical secrecy with the patient. The Geneva physician remains professionally accountable for patient medical secrecy (criminal exposure under Art. 321 CP) for records now held by a successor controller who didn't accept the obligation. Patient consent forms don't authorize successor controllers. nFADP Art.63 does not transfer. Art. 321 CP medical secrecy does not transfer. This exposure is unnamed in AI vendor contracts, unspecified in carrier attestations, and unaddressed by any current service. The recent Q2 2026 precedents (US AI scribe vendor Chapter 11, European AI scribe vendor Swiss strategic review, US tech giant acquisition of Swiss AI clinical decision support vendor) make this exposure live for Geneva physicians now, not hypothetical.

**Mechanism:** AI scribe vendor files for bankruptcy / strategic review / signs asset purchase / terminates Swiss service → patient encounter transcripts (patient medical records) transfer with asset sale to new controller → new controller did not inherit Geneva physician's Art. 321 CP medical secrecy obligations → new controller did not inherit Geneva physician's nFADP Art.63 obligations → patients whose encounters were processed by AI did not consent to new controller → Art.321 CP obligation transfers to Geneva physician (still professionally accountable) → Geneva physician remains criminally exposed for patient confidences now in successor's hands → physician learns of vendor exit → carrier mid-year check-in asks for vendor succession planning → physician's response now includes vendor-exit exposure the physician cannot satisfy without specific pre-vendor-exit planning → coverage assessment affected.

**Implication:** AI scribe / clinical decision support vendor succession / patient medical records portability planning is a discrete service. It includes: pre-vendor-selection succession terms in vendor contracts (what happens on insolvency, acquisition, termination — including patient medical records export obligations, transition period, controller-change disclosure to patients, Art. 321 CP medical secrecy protection continuity, nFADP Art.63 protection continuity); mid-vendor-relationship succession monitoring (vendor financial signals, acquisition signals, service-termination signals); post-vendor-exit response (data portability execution, controller-change documentation, successor-controller Art. 321 CP audit, patient notification architecture, carrier attestation disclosure). The service is novel, discrete, and triggerable at any vendor exit signal.

**Ready-to-use language:** "When your AI scribe vendor goes bankrupt or gets acquired by a US tech giant, your patient encounter transcripts — which are patient medical records under Art. 321 CP medical secrecy — transfer to the buyer. Art. 321 CP doesn't transfer with the vendor asset sale. Art. 321 CP stays with you. Your criminal exposure stays with you. Your nFADP Art.63 obligation stays with you. The patient's consent form doesn't authorize the new controller. I'm building the succession terms into your vendor contracts now, before the exit event, and monitoring vendor signals so you can plan the patient notification architecture — before the carrier's next attestation cycle makes the exposure undeniable."
