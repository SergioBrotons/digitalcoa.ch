### What Changed During the Pause (Geneva Healthcare Lens)

Three things happened during what was not a pause at all:

*First:* **the medical liability carrier AI attestation is now encounter-level, not tool-level.** Same mechanism as the C313 Phase 01 carrier form-completion finding, the C313 Phase 02 DDQ form-completion finding, and the C313 Phase 03 RFP form-completion finding — but applied to the medical liability attestation context. The carrier used to ask "do you use AI tools, and if so, which?" The carrier now asks "which patient encounters between [date range] were processed by AI tools, with what documentation, on what consent basis, with what human authorization." The Geneva physician is answering the attestation by entering patient encounter transcripts (which were AI-scribe-generated), AI tool inventory data, sub-specialty referral AI use, and médecin remplaçant coverage AI logs — exactly what the attestation asks about — into attestation-completion tools (or AI assistants, or copy-paste from EMR exports). The attestation response now passes through a vendor (the attestation-completion tool) the carrier has not separately evaluated. nFADP Art.63 personal exposure loop is closed at the attestation level. The attestation is generic. Every physician using the same attestation-completion workflow produces indistinguishable language. The carrier reads past the generic attestation to find encounter-specific evidence. The coverage is at risk.

*Second:* **the carrier is now asking mid-year check-ins about patient consent architecture.** Same mechanism as the C313 Phase 01 mid-year carrier check-in and the C313 Phase 02 mid-year institutional questionnaire — but adapted to the patient consent specific context. The carrier that received the January 2026 attestation is now sending a mid-year check-in asking: "Which patient consent forms were updated between January 1 and June 30, 2026? Which new AI tools were added to the consent form in that period? Which patient encounters used AI tools the consent form does not mention? Please provide a sample consent form and an updated list of AI tools disclosed to patients." The Geneva physician's patient consent form was last updated in 2024. The physician adopted two new AI tools in 2025 (AI scribe, AI clinical decision support). The consent form mentions neither. The mid-year check-in asks about freshness the physician's consent architecture isn't built for. The patient consent problem is now a documented mid-year carrier question.

*Third:* **June 2026 Geneva medical conferences produced public named-physician AI attestation benchmarks.** Same mechanism as the C313 Phase 01 peer signature visibility at bar conference, the C313 Phase 02 peer DDQ visibility at Geneva WM conferences, and the C313 Phase 03 peer RFP visibility at Geneva consulting conferences — but adapted to the Geneva medical conference circuit. The Journée de la SSMIG (Swiss Society of General Internal Medicine), the FMH spring Continuing Medical Education conferences, the Association des Médecins de Genève (AMG) specialty section meetings, the Hôpitaux Universitaires de Genève (HUG) Grand Rounds and the colloques médico-chirurgicaux — all produced public case studies (with physician consent) of how named Geneva physicians handle the carrier AI attestation, the patient consent architecture, the médecin remplaçant handover, and the EMR-AI data flow routing. The Geneva physician now has direct visibility into what specific named peers declared — not inference from peer-group averages, but direct reading of named-physician attestation language and named-group-practice consent form templates. Several named peers' attestation responses and consent forms are visibly more comprehensive than the average Geneva physician's. Several named peers' consent forms specifically mention AI scribe disclosure, AI clinical decision support disclosure, sub-specialty AI delegation terms, and médecin remplaçant AI supervision terms. The Geneva physician who thought their 2024 consent form was still adequate now has direct peer benchmarks showing them it isn't.

And the médecin remplaçant who covered the practice for two weeks in May? The remplaçant used the practice's AI scribe on the remplaçant's own patients. The replaced physician's medical liability carrier doesn't cover the remplaçant's use of AI. The remplaçant signed the encounter notes (generating the attestation evidence). The remplaçant's encounters are now in the patient records being reviewed at the mid-year carrier check-in. nFADP Art.63 personal exposure through the physician's signature on the original attestation, but the encounters are the remplaçant's encounters with the remplaçant's AI scribe use. Authorization chain handover gap is structural — every coverage period creates one.

The carrier's mid-year check-in has converted the patient consent form from a static compliance document into a dynamic carrier-auditable artifact with freshness expectations.

**"My remplaçant covered the practice for two weeks. They used my AI scribe. The encounters are in my patient records. The carrier is auditing the records."**
Médecin remplaçant authorization discontinuity. Geneva private practices use médecin remplaçant coverage arrangements as a structural feature of independent practice — vacation, conference attendance, illness, parental leave, professional development, sabbatical. The replaced physician authorizes the coverage arrangement through a Convention de remplacement with the remplaçant. The replaced physician does NOT authorize the remplaçant's AI tool use. The remplaçant uses the practice's AI scribe (or their own AI tools) on the remplaçant's encounters with the practice's patients. The replaced physician's medical liability carrier does not cover the remplaçant. The remplaçant signs the encounter notes. The encounters stay in the patient records. The carrier mid-year check-in asks for the encounters. The encounters include AI tool use the replacing physician authorized in their own attestation. The authorization chain handover gap is structural (every coverage period creates one) and never documented.

**"I use a Swiss-certified EMR. The certification says Swiss. The certification doesn't cover my AI scribe. The AI scribe isn't Swiss."**
EMR-AI extension HIN corridor residency fallacy. The Geneva physician uses a Swiss-certified EMR (often labeled "HIN-protected," "Swiss-hosted," "nFADP-compliant," or "Swissmedic-certified medical device"). The certification is for the EMR software itself. The certification does not extend to the AI extensions the physician has added: AI scribe (often US-based — Nuance DAX, Microsoft Nuance, Abridge, Suki), AI clinical decision support (often US-based — UpToDate, DynaMed, GPT-augmented clinical reasoning), AI drug-interaction checkers (often US-based — First Databank, Cerner Multum), AI image analysis for sub-specialty referrals (often US-based — Aidoc, Arterys, Paige). The EMR's HIN corridor protects data IN TRANSIT between the practice and the EMR server. It does NOT protect data AT REST in the AI extension vendor's US-based cloud. Patient medical records (regulated under cantonal medical practice law and Art. 321 CP medical secrecy) flow through non-Swiss cloud for every AI extension the physician has added. nFADP Art.63 reads through the EMR certification. Medical secrecy (penalty: criminal prosecution) reads through.

**"I saw what three other Geneva internists declared at the AMG meeting last week. My consent form doesn't mention AI scribe. Theirs does."**
Public named-physician AI governance visibility. June 2026 Geneva medical conferences — SSMIG Journée, FMH Continuing Medical Education, AMG Geneva specialty section meetings (médecine interne générale, cardiologie, psychiatrie, pédiatrie, gynécologie, etc.), HUG Grand Rounds and colloques médico-chirurgicaux — produced public case studies (with physician consent) of how named Geneva physicians handle the carrier AI attestation, the patient consent architecture, the médecin remplaçant handover, and the EMR-AI data flow routing. The named-physician benchmarks are visible in AMG Geneva section meeting minutes, FMH CME session recordings, and SSMIG case study publications. The Geneva physician now has direct visibility into what specific peers declared. They are discovering their 2024 consent form is less comprehensive than named peers' 2026 consent forms. The comparison trap has moved from peer inference to direct comparison.

**"What happens to my patient records if my AI scribe vendor goes bankrupt or gets acquired?"**
AI scribe vendor succession exposure for patient medical records. The AI scribe vendor (Nuance DAX, Microsoft, Abridge, Suki, etc.) may go bankrupt, get acquired by a larger player, terminate the Swiss service, or pivot the offering. The patient encounter transcripts — which constitute patient medical records under cantonal medical practice law and Art. 321 CP medical secrecy — transfer with the vendor's asset sale to a successor controller. The successor controller did not negotiate Art. 321 CP medical secrecy obligations with the Geneva physician. The successor controller did not negotiate nFADP Art.63 obligations with the Geneva physician. The successor controller did not negotiate the patient's right to medical secrecy with the patient. The Geneva physician remains professionally accountable for patient medical secrecy (criminal exposure under Art. 321 CP) for records now held by a controller who didn't accept the obligation. Patient consent forms do not authorize successor controllers. nFADP Art.63 does not transfer. Medical secrecy does not transfer. This exposure is unnamed in AI scribe vendor contracts.

## The AI Scribe Attestation Loop (C313 Specific)

The most distinctive C313 Phase 04 ICP language pattern is the discovery that the patient encounter note — the artifact the medical liability carrier now wants as evidence of AI governance — is the artifact the carrier's attestation is asking about.

**"I dictated the encounter. My AI scribe wrote the note. The carrier is asking for the note. The note is the evidence of AI governance. The note was written by the AI the carrier is asking about."**

The Geneva physician uses an AI scribe (Nuance DAX, Abridge, Suki, Microsoft DAX Copilot, or Swiss-developed alternatives) on the majority of patient encounters. The AI scribe generates the encounter documentation the carrier now requires as attestation evidence. The attestation response is now being produced by uploading the AI-scribe-generated artifacts the attestation asks about. The physician is answering the attestation by handing the carrier the AI output. nFADP Art.63 personal exposure loop is closed at the attestation level. The attestation becomes generic (every physician using the same AI scribe generates similar documentation). The carrier reads past the generic attestation language to find physician-specific encounter evidence. The physician's coverage is at risk because the encounter documentation wasn't physician-specific enough.

**"I thought the encounter was confidential. It's now the attestation evidence. The attestation is what the carrier wants."**

The Geneva physician believed the patient encounter note was a confidential clinical artifact — and it still is, but it is now also being used as carrier attestation evidence. The physician is now disclosing encounter-level AI tool use at the carrier's request. The carrier's mid-year check-in asks: which encounters between [date range] were processed by AI tools, on what consent basis, with what supervision? The physician is now disclosing patient encounter AI use to a third party (the carrier's underwriting team) on an encounter-by-encounter basis. Patient confidentiality (Art. 321 CP medical secrecy) intersects with carrier attestation disclosure obligation. The intersection is unnamed in physician practice.

This is the C313 Phase 04 ICP language pattern: "The encounter was between me and the patient. Now the carrier wants to read it. The carrier wants it because the carrier is asking about AI use. The encounter was processed by the AI the carrier is asking about."
