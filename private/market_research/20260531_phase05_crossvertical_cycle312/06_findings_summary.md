## 6 NET-NEW Cross-Vertical Findings — C312 Phase 05

### Finding 1: Carrier Form Architecture Has Shifted From Organizational to Individual

**The finding:**
Carrier forms are no longer organizational compliance documents. They are individual professional accountability instruments. The managing partner, managing director, or physician is named personally on the form. The signature creates a documented personal standard. The professional did not design the specification.

**Evidence across verticals:**

- **Legal:** Carrier form requires attestation to matter-level AI use practices by the managing partner personally. No delegation framework exists. Associate's unauthenticated AI use is the managing partner's personal liability.
- **Finance:** Carrier form requires attestation to human oversight adequacy by the managing director personally. "Adequate" is undefined. The managing director certified to a standard they cannot operationalize.
- **Consulting:** Carrier form requires attestation to sub-consultant AI delegation practices by the managing director personally. The sub-consultant is an independent professional. The delegation was never documented.
- **Healthcare:** Carrier form requires attestation to specialty-specific AI diagnostic authorization by the physician personally. The medical assistant's AI use is the physician's personal liability. The patient's question about AI involvement creates a record.

**The structural shift:**
This is not an emerging trend. It is a confirmed architectural shift. Carrier forms across all four verticals have implemented individual professional accountability architecture. The organizational policy is no longer sufficient. The individual's signature is the trigger.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "The form has your name on it. You didn't design the specification. You're accountable for meeting it."

### Finding 2: Authorization Chain Is the Universal Liability Structure

**The finding:**
In every vertical, the professional is personally accountable for AI use they did not directly authorize. The authorization chain runs through associates, vendors, sub-consultants, and assistants — and has never been documented. This is not a gap the professional is aware of. The carrier audits are revealing it for the first time.

**Evidence across verticals:**

- **Legal:** Managing partner authorized the associate. The associate activated AI tools. The managing partner never authorized the specific tools. The authorization is invisible.
- **Finance:** Managing director authorized the engagement. The associate implemented AI tools within the portfolio workflow. The vendor created infrastructure. The managing director never authorized the specific implementation. The chain is undocumented.
- **Consulting:** Managing director authorized the sub-consultant's engagement. The sub-consultant used AI tools. The sub-consultant is an independent professional who cannot be directed. The delegation to AI tools is invisible.
- **Healthcare:** Physician authorized the medical assistant's employment. The assistant used AI on patient data. The physician never authorized AI tool use. The patient data routes through servers outside the HIN corridor. The physician is unaware.

**The compound structure:**
The authorization chain is compound: vendor implementation + associate activation + principal oversight gap. Each layer adds undocumented liability. The professional is at the top of a chain they never designed.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "Your associate's AI use is your liability. Your vendor's data processing is your accountability. The chain was never documented."

### Finding 3: Documentation Debt Is Non-Linear and Compounding

**The finding:**
The documentation debt is not cumulative — it is compounding. Every month of undocumented AI use adds more than linear time to the reconstruction problem. The tools change. The staff changes. The scope changes. The reconstruction becomes progressively more difficult.

**Evidence across verticals:**

- **Legal:** 12–24 months of associate AI use across 40 concurrent matters. Different tools, different associates, different clients. Each matter has a different evidence requirement. Retroactive reconstruction requires matter-by-matter forensic review.
- **Finance:** 12–24 months of AI-assisted portfolio construction. Different models, different associates, different mandates. Each institutional client asks different questions. Retroactive reconstruction requires mandate-by-mandate evidence recovery.
- **Consulting:** 12–24 months of AI-assisted proposals. Different methodologies, different sub-consultants, different clients. Each client asks different questions. Retroactive reconstruction requires deliverable-by-deliverable methodology recovery.
- **Healthcare:** 12–24 months of AI-assisted diagnostic support. Different tools, different physicians, different patient populations. Each patient interaction is a separate record. Retroactive reconstruction requires encounter-by-encounter clinical reasoning documentation.

**The compounding mechanism:**
Three vectors compound simultaneously: time × tool count × staff use. Each vector accelerates the documentation debt. Linear forward-only solutions are structurally insufficient. Retroactive reconstruction requires a distinct methodology.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "Linear solutions for a non-linear problem. Going forward doesn't answer what you've already done."

### Finding 4: "Adequate" Is the Universal Floating Signifier — And the Primary Action Blocker

**The finding:**
"Adequate" appears in every carrier form, every regulatory guidance, and every professional standard across all four verticals — and is never defined operationally. The professional cannot document to an undefined standard. This is the primary action blocker across all four verticals.

**Evidence across verticals:**

- **Legal:** "Adequate supervision of AI-assisted work" — carrier form. No operational definition. No evidence specification. No calibration standard. The managing partner cannot document adequacy without knowing what it means.
- **Finance:** "Adequate human oversight of AI-influenced investment decisions" — FINMA guidance and carrier form. No operational definition. The managing director cannot document adequacy without knowing what it means.
- **Consulting:** "Adequate disclosure of AI methodology in client deliverables" — client questionnaire. No operational definition. The managing director cannot document adequacy without knowing what it means.
- **Healthcare:** "Adequate human oversight of AI-assisted diagnostic decisions" — FMH guidance and carrier form. No operational definition. The physician cannot document adequacy without knowing what it means.

**The paralysis mechanism:**
"Adequate" is universally understood to mean something — but no one can say what. The professional waits for clarity. The carrier already has a definition. The gap between the professional's undefined understanding and the carrier's implicit definition is the documentation exposure.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "'Adequate' appears on every form. No one defines it. That's the primary action blocker."

### Finding 5: Commercial Enforcement Activates Before Regulatory Deadline

**The finding:**
In every vertical, the commercial consequence of undocumented AI use arrives before the regulatory consequence. The professional loses the mandate, the coverage, or the client before the regulator arrives. The structural window is between commercial enforcement and regulatory enforcement.

**Evidence across verticals:**

- **Legal:** Client loses confidence in firm that cannot explain its AI methodology. Engagement letter has no AI clause. Carrier renewal asks specific questions. Regulatory inquiry follows mandate loss.
- **Finance:** Institutional client declines next mandate due to inadequate AI governance evidence. DDQ response was insufficient. Competitor had coherent documentation. Regulatory inquiry follows competitive failure.
- **Consulting:** Boutique loses RFP because proposal methodology cannot be defended. Client AI questionnaire revealed documentation gaps. Regulatory inquiry follows commercial failure.
- **Healthcare:** Patient files complaint after learning AI was used without documented consent. Carrier asks for AI diagnostic authorization evidence. Swissmedic inquiry follows patient complaint.

**The structural window:**
The commercial enforcement pathway and the regulatory enforcement pathway are separate. Commercial enforcement activates first — through mandate loss, coverage gap, client loss, and professional complaints. Regulatory enforcement follows. The professional who waits for regulatory clarity will face commercial enforcement before regulatory enforcement.

**Ready-to-use language:**
> "The commercial consequence arrives first. You lose the client before the regulator arrives."

### Finding 6: One Evidence Architecture Serves All Four Verticals

**The finding:**
The same evidence architecture applies across legal, finance, consulting, and healthcare. The vertical overlay is terminology and scope — the architecture is identical. The professional who serves all four verticals has a structural efficiency advantage no competitor can match without rebuilding from scratch.

**The universal evidence architecture:**

1. **Pre-signing carrier form review** — extract the evidence specification before signing
2. **Authorization chain documentation** — all people, all tools, all scopes, all oversight
3. **Retroactive reconstruction protocol** — distinct methodology for existing undocumented use
4. **Vendor records gap analysis** — what vendor holds, what vendor cannot produce
5. **"Adequate" operational definition** — specific to vertical, firm size, practice type
6. **Commercial document AI clause** — engagement letter, fee agreement, mandate letter
7. **Ongoing monitoring system** — evidence maintenance, not one-time documentation
8. **Cross-mandate coherence protocol** — consistent calibration across all clients/matters/patients
