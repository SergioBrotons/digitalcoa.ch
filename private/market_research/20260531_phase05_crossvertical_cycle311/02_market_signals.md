# 02 — Market Signals: Cross-Vertical Problems That Appear in All Four Verticals

**Phase:** C311 Phase 05 | **Cycle:** 311 | **Date:** 2026-05-31
**Verticals:** Legal · Finance · Consulting · Healthcare

## Cross-Vertical Market Signals: What the Four Verticals Share

Market signals are the observable indicators of demand — the triggers, the forcing functions, the timing patterns that appear identically across all four verticals. These signals define when the ICP moves from passive concern to active seeking. The cross-vertical market signals are the patterns that define the demand side of the market, independent of supply.

---

## Signal 1: The Form Arrival — Universal Trigger

**What it looks like in each vertical:**

**Legal:** Carrier renewal form arrives with AI-specific attestation questions. Managing partner has 30 days to sign. The form requires documentation infrastructure that doesn't exist in a 3-attorney boutique.

**Finance:** Institutional client sends AI governance DDQ as part of mandate renewal. Managing director has 2–4 weeks to respond. The questions ask for evidence the firm doesn't have. The mandate is at risk.

**Consulting:** Client sends AI governance questionnaire with contract renewal or procurement process. Managing director has two weeks to complete it. The questions probe methodology, sub-consultant AI use, and personal liability exposure.

**Healthcare:** Carrier sends physician-personal attestation form requiring name, FMH license number, and personal certification of human oversight over AI tools used in practice. Physician signs without supporting documentation.

**The pattern:** In every vertical, the form arrives as a triggering event — not as a proactive governance exercise. The professional did not seek help before the form arrived. The form created the urgency. The timeline is always short. The documentation gap is always already there.

**Market signal language:**
> "The form arrived. That's when the conversation starts. Not before."

---

## Signal 2: The Authorization Chain Becomes Visible to Carriers — Not Just to the Professional

**What it looks like in each vertical:**

**Legal:** Carrier asks specifically about which attorneys used which AI tools on which matters. Managing partner cannot answer because the authorization chain was never documented. Junior associate's personal device use becomes firm-level exposure.

**Finance:** Carrier asks for vendor contract documentation and associate activation records. Managing director discovers the vendor implemented an AI module he didn't authorize and the associate activated it without his review. Both steps are now carrier audit questions.

**Consulting:** Carrier asks about sub-consultant AI use. Managing director realizes he authorized the sub-consultant's engagement but never authorized the specific AI tools they used on the client's work. The delegation chain is visible to the carrier, not just to the professional.

**Healthcare:** Carrier asks specifically about medical assistant AI tool use on patient data. Physician realizes the medical assistant has been using AI tools for months without a documented authorization framework. The delegation chain is now a carrier audit question.

**The pattern:** The authorization chain — invisible to the professional, visible to the carrier — is the structural exposure in every vertical. The professional signed the attestation. The carrier is now asking about the chain of authorization behind that signature. The gap between signature and authorization is the exposure.

## Signal 3: Pre-Signing Review Gap Creates Post-Signing Exposure — Identified Retrospectively

**What it looks like in each vertical:**

**Legal:** Managing partner signed the carrier form at renewal, under time pressure, without reading the questions carefully. Three months later, a peer mentions a specific AI question their carrier asked. The managing partner realizes he cannot answer it. The exposure was created at signing, not at the carrier's question.

**Finance:** Managing director signed the carrier attestation and the institutional DDQ response within the same renewal cycle, without recognizing they were different documents requiring different evidence. A peer mentions their institutional client asked a specific question about model drift monitoring. The managing director realizes he has no monitoring documentation for 18 months.

**Consulting:** Managing director signed the carrier form at renewal. Six months later, a client sends an AI governance questionnaire. The managing director realizes the carrier form questions and the client questionnaire questions are structurally similar — and he answered the carrier form without having documentation for either. The client questionnaire becomes an exposure revelation moment.

**Healthcare:** Physician signed the carrier form requiring personal certification. Three months later, at an AMGe event, a colleague mentions their carrier asked specific questions about medical assistant AI tool use. The physician realizes he certified personal oversight of staff AI use without having a delegation framework. The exposure was created at signing.

**The pattern:** Pre-signing review gap creates exposure that is only identified retrospectively — when a peer mentions a carrier question, when a client sends a questionnaire, when a colleague describes their experience. The professional did not know the question was coming. The signing moment was the exposure creation moment.

## Signal 4: The "Adequate" Word as Market Signal

**What it looks like in each vertical:**

**Legal:** "Adequate" appears on the carrier attestation. The managing partner reads it and has no reference point for what it means. The word creates paralysis — they know they need to do something, but without a definition of "adequate," they cannot determine what is enough.

**Finance:** FINMA uses "adequate" in its AI guidance. The managing director reads FINMA's principles and has no operational definition of "adequate" for a boutique WM firm. The institutional client's DDQ is more specific than FINMA's guidance — but still doesn't define "adequate" for the specific context.

**Consulting:** Carrier form asks if the firm has "adequate" AI governance documentation. The managing director has no reference point. No carrier has published a definition of "adequate" for a Geneva consulting boutique of any size.

**Healthcare:** Carrier asks if the practice has "adequate" AI governance documentation for physician oversight of AI tools. No definition exists for a small practice with 4 physicians and 2 medical assistants.

**The pattern:** "Adequate" is a market signal. It is the anxiety trigger in every vertical. It appears in every carrier form. It has never been defined. The professional who can name "adequate" precisely — and provide a proportionate, Geneva-specific, sector-specific definition — occupies the highest-value positioning in the market.

**Market signal language:**
> "The word 'adequate' has never been defined for a Geneva firm of your size in your sector. That's the word on your form. That's the word that matters. And no one has told you what it means."

## Signal 5: 12–24 Month Documentation Debt — Universal, Retrospective, Non-Linear

**What it looks like in each vertical:**

**Legal:** Firm has used AI tools (ChatGPT, contract review, research assistance) for approximately two years. Documentation begins from today, not from the beginning. The 12–24 months prior is undocumented. Linear forward-only documentation solutions address only the future — the past exposure remains.

**Finance:** AI model has been running in the portfolio system for 18 months. Performance monitoring records do not exist. Model drift has occurred. The institutional client is now asking for evidence of monitoring — evidence that does not exist for the period of operation.

**Consulting:** Firm has produced AI-assisted deliverables for 12–18 months. Methodology documentation does not exist. The managing director cannot describe the AI's role in the work produced. The client may have asked or will ask how the work was produced. The documentation debt is retrospective.

**Healthcare:** Medical assistant has been using AI tools on patient data for over a year. The physician did not document the delegation from day one. The OaF survey asks about AI tool deployment dates. The documentation only starts from today — the prior period is undocumented.

**The pattern:** The documentation debt is retrospective and universal across all four verticals. It is 12–24 months of undocumented AI use. Linear forward-only documentation solutions address only the future — the past exposure remains. The market needs retroactive reconstruction, not just forward documentation. The solution architecture must include both.

## Signal 6: Client Questions Activating the Commercial Document Gap

**What it looks like in each vertical:**

**Legal:** Clients are beginning to ask their attorneys about AI use in their matters — how is AI being used, who reviews the AI output, what is the attorney's human oversight process? The fee agreement has no AI clause. The attorney has no calibrated response prepared.

**Finance:** Institutional clients are sending AI governance questionnaires as standard procurement documents. The WM firm's engagement letter has no AI clause. The managing director is responding to client questions without a documented AI governance position.

**Consulting:** Client AI questionnaires arrive as procurement triggers. The engagement letter predates AI and has no methodology disclosure clause. The managing director responds to client questions without a documented AI methodology position.

**Healthcare:** Patient communication about AI is becoming a trust question. Some patients ask directly about AI use in their care. No standard protocol exists for how physicians should communicate about AI involvement in diagnostics.

**The pattern:** Client questions are activating the commercial document gap in every vertical. The governing document — fee agreement, engagement letter, client questionnaire response, patient communication protocol — predates AI and now creates accountability the professional didn't know existed. The commercial document gap is a market signal, not just a documentation gap.

---

## Signal 7: The Enforcement Sequence — Commercial Before Regulatory

**What it looks like in each vertical:**

**Legal:** OaF guidance will come — but the carrier renewal form is here now. The managing partner will face carrier questions before the bar issues guidance. The commercial sequence (carrier renewal, client questions) activates before the regulatory sequence (OaF guidance, FINMA examination).

**Finance:** The institutional client asks before FINMA asks. The mandate is at risk before the regulator becomes a direct problem. The competitive gate (DDQ) activates before the regulatory floor (FINMA) becomes an enforcement issue.

**Consulting:** The client questionnaire is a procurement deadline. The regulatory deadline (EU AI Act awareness, nFADP enforcement) is further away. The managing director responds to the commercial pressure (client questionnaire) before the regulatory pressure becomes active.

**Healthcare:** Swissmedic operator classification is a real question now — but the carrier form with the physician's personal certification is also here now. The physician faces carrier questions before Swissmedic formalizes guidance. The commercial consequence (coverage implications) activates before the regulatory consequence.

**The pattern:** In every vertical, commercial enforcement activates before regulatory enforcement. The professional loses the mandate, the coverage, the client, the procurement opportunity — before the regulator or the carrier becomes a direct enforcement problem. The market signal is: the timeline is shorter than the professional thinks.

---

## Signal 8: The Peer Network as Primary Information Channel

**What it looks like in each vertical:**

**Legal:** Managing partner learns about carrier form questions through peer conversations — at OaF events, in the Geneva legal community, through broker networks. "My colleague mentioned his carrier asked about AI tool use on specific matters" becomes the trigger for the managing partner to check his own situation.

**Finance:** Managing director learns about institutional DDQ questions through broker intelligence — the broker has seen the questionnaire before the MD has. "Your broker flagged that clients are asking AI questions now" is the primary trigger for the finance ICP to seek help.

**Consulting:** Managing director learns about client questionnaire content through peer networks — what questions were asked, what was the deadline, what did peers answer. The AMGe / professional society network surfaces the information before the managing director receives the form.

**Healthcare:** Physician learns about carrier form changes through AMGe peer network — "my colleague mentioned the carrier form now requires personal certification, name and license number." The peer-to-physician channel is the primary information channel for what the carrier forms are actually asking.

**The pattern:** Peer networks are the primary information channel for market signals — what the carrier is asking, what the client is requiring, what the regulatory body is signaling. The broker or the peer colleague surfaces the information before the professional encounters it directly. The service that works through broker and peer referral networks reaches the ICP at the right moment.
