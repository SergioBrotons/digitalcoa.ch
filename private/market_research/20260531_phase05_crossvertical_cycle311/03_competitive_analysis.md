# 03 — Competitive Analysis: Cross-Vertical White Space

## Cross-Vertical Competitive White Space: What No One Has Built

The competitive landscape for AI accountability services in Geneva is defined by absence as much as presence. Across all four verticals, the same structural white spaces appear — positions that no competitor has occupied, service models no competitor has built, and channel strategies no competitor has executed. The cross-vertical synthesis reveals that the white spaces are universal, not vertical-specific. One evidence architecture serves all four verticals. The competitive analysis is therefore the same in structure across legal, finance, consulting, and healthcare — only the specific terminology changes.

---

## White Space 1: Pre-Signing Form Review — The Highest-Value Intervention, Zero Competitors

**What it is:** Reading the carrier form or client questionnaire before the professional signs it — identifying exactly what the questions require, exactly what documentation exists, exactly what gaps need to be filled before the signature creates the exposure.

**Why no one has built it:** The service requires Swiss-specific regulatory knowledge, carrier-specific form language, and documentation architecture expertise — a combination that does not exist in any single competitor's offering. It also requires reading the form as a market intelligence document, not as a compliance checklist.

**What it would require:** 
- Detailed knowledge of carrier form language across all four verticals (Helsana, Swica, Sympany in healthcare; standard professional liability carriers in legal/finance/consulting)
- Knowledge of institutional client DDQ language and how it differs from carrier attestation language
- Documentation architecture design capability — not just reviewing forms, but building the documentation to answer them
- Retroactive reconstruction expertise — for professionals who have already signed and need to build the supporting record

**Cross-vertical applicability:** Pre-signing carrier form review is the highest-value intervention in all four verticals. In legal: carrier renewal form. In finance: carrier attestation + institutional DDQ. In consulting: carrier form + client AI questionnaire. In healthcare: physician-personal carrier attestation form.

**Competitive status:** Unoccupied. No competitor has built pre-signing form review as a service. No competitor reads carrier forms as market intelligence documents.

## White Space 2: The Authorization Chain Documentation — Universal, Unnamed, Unaddressed

**What it is:** Documenting the chain of authorization for AI use across staff, vendors, and sub-consultants — the chain that runs from the signing professional through the people and tools they didn't directly control.

**Why no one has built it:** The authorization chain is a cross-vertical structural problem — it appears identically in legal (junior associate personal device), finance (vendor implementation + associate activation), consulting (sub-consultant delegation), and healthcare (medical assistant AI use on patient data). No competitor has named it as a distinct documentation category. No competitor offers a proportionate, Geneva-specific authorization chain documentation framework for small professional firms.

**What it would require:**
- A documentation framework that is specific to the vertical's staff structure (associates, medical assistants, sub-consultants)
- Evidence format that demonstrates the principal authorized specific tools for specific purposes
- Monitoring and review documentation that demonstrates ongoing oversight
- Retroactive reconstruction for chains already in operation without documentation

**Cross-vertical applicability:** Authorization chain documentation is the universal liability structure — the principal is accountable for AI use they didn't authorize, through people and tools they didn't directly control. The documentation framework is one architecture with four vertical overlays.

**Competitive status:** Unnamed in the market. No competitor has a service called "authorization chain documentation" or a product specifically designed to address this structural gap across verticals.

## White Space 3: Retroactive Reconstruction Methodology — The Primary Market Problem

**What it is:** Building a credible documentation record for AI use that occurred before any documentation practice existed — reconstructing the evidence architecture for 12–24 months of undocumented operation.

**Why no one has built it:** Retroactive reconstruction is technically complex (reconstructed records have evidentiary limitations), legally sensitive (must not create false evidence), and requires a specific methodology distinct from forward documentation. The market has not developed a retroactive reconstruction service because no one has acknowledged that the primary market problem is retrospective, not prospective. The dominant consulting approach is forward-only: "here's a policy for going forward." This is structurally insufficient for a market where the majority of exposure is retrospective.

**What it would require:**
- A methodology for establishing what existed at specific points in time — not fabricating documentation, but demonstrating the reasonable basis for what was known and done
- A legal framework for retroactive documentation that is credible and defensible — distinguishing between reconstructed records (lower evidentiary weight) and documented contemporaneous evidence (higher weight)
- A service architecture that addresses the already-exposed professional differently from the professional preparing for a future form
- A specific retroactive reconstruction methodology that is distinct from forward documentation architecture

**Cross-vertical applicability:** Retroactive reconstruction is the primary market problem in all four verticals. Every vertical has 12–24 months of undocumented AI use. Linear solutions address only the future. The market needs retroactive reconstruction for the period that has already passed.

**Competitive status:** No competitor has published a retroactive reconstruction methodology. No competitor offers retroactive reconstruction as a distinct service. The primary market problem is unaddressed by any competitor.

## White Space 4: Broker Referral Channel — Primary Acquisition Path, Unaddressed by All Competitors

**What it is:** A structured broker referral program for AI accountability services — where insurance brokers, financial intermediaries, and professional association networks actively refer clients to the service as a risk management intervention.

**Why no one has built it:** The broker channel requires a specific product architecture (bounded, proportionate, Geneva-specific), a specific pricing structure (entry-level, clearly valued), and a specific positioning that fits the broker's client conversation (risk management, not compliance). No competitor has built this for AI accountability services. The broker referral channel is structurally unaddressed.

**What it would require:**
- A broker-facing product that is bounded and proportionate for small Geneva firms (CHF 1,100–2,200 entry)
- A referral protocol that the broker can explain to clients in one sentence: "This is about the form your carrier sent you"
- Evidence that the service is Swiss-specific (carrier form language, Geneva regulatory context, bilingual EN/FR)
- A positioning statement that the broker can use: "I produce evidence, not frameworks"
- A lead magnet that the broker can use in client conversations

**Cross-vertical applicability:** The broker referral channel is primary in finance (broker as primary intelligence channel for institutional DDQ arrival) and significant in legal, consulting, and healthcare (broker as early warning for carrier form changes). The channel works across all four verticals with the same referral language.

**Competitive status:** No competitor has built a broker referral program for AI accountability services. The primary acquisition path is structurally unaddressed.

## White Space 5: Bilingual EN/FR as Standard Product — Regulatory Design Requirement, Not Translation

**What it is:** Offering AI accountability services in both English and French as standard — not as a localization exercise, but as a design requirement for the Geneva market where English carrier forms and French regulatory proceedings create two documentation environments.

**Why no one has built it:** The bilingual service requirement is not a translation task — it is a documentation design requirement. The evidence architecture must produce documentation that works in both linguistic environments simultaneously. No competitor offers bilingual as standard. Most competitors are English-only.

**What it would require:**
- Documentation products that are designed for both linguistic environments from the beginning
- Carrier form review in the language the carrier uses (English)
- Regulatory response in the language the regulatory body uses (French for Swiss regulators)
- Lead magnet and client communication materials in both languages
- Evidence format that works in both linguistic contexts

**Cross-vertical applicability:** Bilingual EN/FR is required in all four verticals — Geneva legal (French OaF, English carrier forms), Geneva finance (English institutional clients, French regulatory proceedings), Geneva consulting (English client questionnaires, French regulatory context), Geneva healthcare (French AMGe network, English international physician context).

**Competitive status:** No competitor offers bilingual EN/FR as standard product. The regulatory design requirement is unaddressed.

## White Space 6: One Evidence Architecture / Four Vertical Overlays — Structural Product Advantage

**What it is:** A single evidence architecture system that produces documentation calibrated to the specific carrier form, the specific client questionnaire, the specific regulatory standard, and the specific professional liability exposure in each vertical — without requiring four separate products or four separate engagements.

**Why no one has built it:** The market has not recognized that the documentation debt structure is identical across all four verticals — carrier form triggering, authorization chain undocumented, 12–24 months of undocumented AI use, "adequate" undefined, commercial enforcement preceding regulatory, personal liability personal. The structural identity means one evidence architecture serves all four verticals. Competitors are building vertical-specific frameworks, not one architecture with vertical overlays.

**What it would require:**
- A universal evidence architecture that addresses the common structure (pre-signing review, authorization chain documentation, retroactive reconstruction, "adequate" definition, commercial document AI clause)
- Vertical-specific overlays that calibrate the universal architecture to the specific vertical's carrier forms, client questionnaires, regulatory standards, and professional liability exposures
- A service model that can serve a legal firm and a healthcare practice with the same core system — without requiring two separate product lines

**Cross-vertical applicability:** One evidence architecture is the structural product advantage. The architecture is the same in all four verticals — only the specific overlay (carrier form language, client questionnaire format, regulatory standard, professional liability framing) changes.

**Competitive status:** No competitor offers one evidence architecture with four vertical overlays. The structural product advantage is unoccupied.
