## Cross-Vertical Regulatory Patterns: What Holds Across All Four Sectors

The regulatory landscape for AI accountability in Geneva is characterized by principles without operational definitions, requirements without documentation standards, and personal liability without clear prevention pathways. The cross-vertical regulatory patterns are the structural features that define what is required — and what is missing — across all four verticals. The compliance context is not about understanding the regulations abstractly. It is about understanding what the regulations actually require the professional to document, and identifying where that requirement creates a gap the professional cannot currently fill.

---

## Pattern 1: "Adequate" — The Universal Regulatory Non-Definition

**The pattern:** Every relevant regulatory instrument uses the word "adequate" without defining it for a Geneva small professional firm of any vertical. The word appears in:
- FINMA AI guidance for financial institutions (principles-based, "adequate" used repeatedly, no operational definition)
- nFADP Articles 52 and 63 (personal liability for "responsible persons," no documentation standard defined)
- Carrier attestation forms across all four verticals ("adequate AI governance documentation" required)
- FMH guidance on human oversight (principle stated, "adequate" used, no procedure defined)
- OaF survey language (asks about AI governance practices, "adequate" undefined)

**The structural problem:** "Adequate" is a floating signifier across all regulatory instruments. Every regulator uses it. No regulator defines it. The professional cannot determine what "adequate" means for their firm in their sector, in Geneva, at their size. The regulatory requirement exists; the documentation standard does not.

**Cross-vertical applicability:** This is the same pattern in all four verticals. The word "adequate" is on every carrier form, in every regulatory guidance document, in every professional standard — and has never been defined for a Geneva boutique firm of any size or sector.

**The compliance gap this creates:**
- Professional signs "adequate" attestation without knowing what "adequate" requires
- Carrier asks for evidence of "adequate" AI governance — professional has no reference point for what to produce
- Regulatory body issues guidance with "adequate" — professional has no way to assess whether their documentation meets the standard
- No published benchmark exists for "adequate" in any vertical for a Geneva small firm

**Resolution pathway:** Define "adequate" for each vertical as a specific, proportionate, Geneva-sized documentation standard — not as an abstract principle, but as a concrete list of what must exist in documentation to meet the carrier form's requirement.

## Pattern 2: Personal Liability Without Prevention Pathway

**The pattern:** nFADP Article 63 (financial sector), Article 52 (consulting/professional services), and analogous medical liability provisions create personal liability for the "responsible person" — the managing director, the partner, the physician — without providing a documented pathway to prevent that liability. The regulation creates obligation without solution.

**In finance (nFADP Art. 63):** Managing director personally accountable for AI-influenced investment decisions. The article creates the liability. No published guidance tells the managing director what specific documentation prevents the liability.

**In consulting (nFADP Art. 52):** Managing director personally accountable for data processing decisions in client work. The article creates the liability. No published guidance tells the managing director what specific documentation prevents the liability.

**In legal:** Attorney personal liability for AI-influenced advice. Swiss civil liability standard applies. No published guidance tells the attorney what specific documentation prevents the liability.

**In healthcare:** Physician personal liability for AI-influenced diagnostic decisions. FMH standard of care applies. No published guidance tells the physician what specific documentation prevents the liability.

**The structural problem:** The regulatory architecture creates personal liability without a documented prevention pathway. The professional is told they are personally accountable — and then given no specific documentation requirement to meet to demonstrate they met the standard. The liability is real; the prevention mechanism is absent.

**Cross-vertical applicability:** Personal liability without a prevention pathway is the same structural pattern in all four verticals. The professional is named personally, the signature creates documented exposure, and no regulatory instrument provides a specific documentation standard that would satisfy the liability question.

## Pattern 3: Human Oversight Required, Not Defined

**The pattern:** Every relevant regulatory instrument requires "human oversight" of AI systems — FINMA for financial institutions, FMH for medical practice, carrier attestation forms across all verticals, nFADP accountability requirements. Human oversight is required. Human oversight is not defined.

**FINMA:** "Adequate human oversight" of AI systems required. No operational definition of what "oversight" means in practice, what documentation demonstrates it, or what "adequate" looks like for a boutique WM firm.

**FMH:** Human oversight of AI tools required. Principle stated without procedure. No published protocol for what "oversight" means in a small medical practice with 2–10 physicians.

**Carrier forms (all verticals):** Attestation that human oversight was maintained over AI tools. No definition of what documentation demonstrates that oversight occurred.

**The structural problem:** Human oversight is the required standard across all four verticals. No regulatory instrument defines what human oversight looks like in practice for a small Geneva professional firm. The requirement exists without the operational definition. The professional attests to something they cannot fully describe.

**Cross-vertical applicability:** Human oversight is the universal regulatory requirement. It is required in finance (FINMA), in healthcare (FMH), in legal (carrier attestation, OaF guidance), and in consulting (carrier attestation, nFADP). The requirement is universal. The definition is absent in all four verticals.

## Pattern 4: Documentation Debt is Retrospective, Regulatory Instruments Assume Prospective

**The pattern:** Every regulatory instrument — carrier forms, FINMA guidance, nFADP articles, FMH guidance — assumes documentation begins at the point of the requirement. No regulatory instrument addresses the retrospective documentation gap: 12–24 months of undocumented AI use that already occurred before any documentation practice existed.

**Carrier attestation forms:** Ask about current AI governance practices. Assume the firm has been documenting. Do not address the period before documentation began.

**FINMA AI guidance:** Principles-based, forward-looking. Does not address retroactive documentation requirements for AI systems already in operation without documentation.

**nFADP articles:** Establish personal liability for data processing decisions. Do not provide a retroactive documentation pathway for decisions that were made without contemporaneous documentation.

**FMH guidance:** Requires human oversight going forward. Does not address how to document oversight for AI tools already in use without documented oversight framework.

**The structural problem:** The documentation debt is retrospective. The regulatory instruments are prospective. The gap between retrospective debt and prospective requirement is unaddressed by any regulatory instrument or guidance document.

**Cross-vertical applicability:** This is the same pattern in all four verticals. Every vertical has 12–24 months of undocumented AI use. Every regulatory instrument assumes documentation begins from today forward. The retrospective gap is unaddressed in all four verticals.

---

## Pattern 5: Multi-Authority Documentation Non-Alignment

**The pattern:** In each vertical, the professional faces documentation requirements from multiple authorities simultaneously — carrier, regulator, client, professional bar — and these authorities do not align on what "adequate" documentation looks like. The professional produces documentation for one authority and discovers it does not satisfy another's requirements.

**Legal:** SBA (Swiss Bar Association) standards, OaF guidance, carrier attestation requirements, and potential future court evidentiary standards all require documentation — but none defines what "adequate" means for a Geneva boutique, and none specifies the format required.

**Finance:** FINMA principles, institutional client DDQ requirements, carrier attestation requirements, and nFADP Article 63 personal liability documentation — four different authorities, four different documentation frameworks, no alignment on what "adequate" looks like.

**Consulting:** Carrier attestation, client AI questionnaire requirements, nFADP Article 52 documentation, and engagement letter methodology disclosure — four different documentation frameworks, no alignment.

**Healthcare:** FMH human oversight requirement, Swissmedic operator classification documentation, carrier attestation requirements, and Swissdamed incident reporting format — four different documentation frameworks, no alignment.

**The structural problem:** The multi-authority environment creates documentation complexity that the professional cannot navigate without a unified framework. Producing documentation for one authority does not satisfy another's requirements. The professional needs one evidence architecture that produces documentation calibrated to all authorities simultaneously.

**Cross-vertical applicability:** Multi-authority documentation non-alignment is the same structural pattern in all four verticals. The professional needs one evidence architecture that addresses all authorities in each vertical — not four separate documentation exercises.

---

## Pattern 6: Enforcement Sequence — Commercial Before Regulatory, Active Before Formal

**The pattern:** In each vertical, the commercial consequence (mandate loss, client loss, coverage gap, procurement gate) activates before the regulatory consequence (FINMA inquiry, OaF guidance, Swissmedic classification, carrier audit). The professional loses the mandate before they hear from the regulator.

**Legal:** Client questions about AI methodology activate before OaF issues formal guidance. The attorney faces client scrutiny before bar guidance arrives.

**Finance:** Institutional DDQ activates before FINMA examination. The mandate is at risk before the regulator becomes a direct problem.

**Consulting:** Client AI questionnaire activates before nFADP enforcement. The procurement gate closes before the regulatory deadline arrives.

**Healthcare:** Carrier form with physician-personal certification activates before Swissmedic formalizes operator classification guidance. The physician signs the attestation before the regulatory classification is clear.

**The structural problem:** The enforcement sequence means the professional faces commercial consequences before regulatory ones — and the regulatory instruments are not yet sufficiently developed to provide the documentation standard the professional needs. The professional is caught between an immediate commercial problem and an incomplete regulatory framework.

**Cross-vertical applicability:** The enforcement sequence is the same in all four verticals. Commercial enforcement activates first, regulatory enforcement follows. The timeline is shorter than the professional thinks because the commercial consequence comes first.
