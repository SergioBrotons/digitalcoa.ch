That sense is correct.

**The accountability gridlock is this:** The SAV guidelines describe principles. They do not describe what documentation those principles require you to build, in what format, to what standard, before the SAV asks the question. Most Geneva attorneys are managing AI accountability in a structure that was not designed for it — and the gap between what the structure requires and what the documentation shows is growing every week.

The vendor DPA you signed when you activated Copilot does something specific to your client data. Most Geneva attorneys who deployed Copilot did not read the DPA. The briefing addresses what the deployment actually does to your client matter data, and what the SAV expects you to be able to document about it.

3. **The documentation window.** The decisions your firm is making about AI right now — which tools, for which purposes, with what human authorization — are accumulating without documentation. The documentation you build during the open window is defensible evidence of what human authorization preceded AI decisions. Documentation built retroactively, after a question arrives, is visibly retroactive. The window is not a metaphor. It is a present-tense positioning event.

This is not a technicality. It is a professional secrecy exposure. The briefing addresses what the DPA gap means for your specific vendor stack.

5. **Shadow AI in your firm.** Your associates are using consumer AI tools on client matters — probably without authorization you documented, probably without a compliant vendor DPA, probably without a framework that defines what they can and cannot do. The accountability falls on you regardless. The briefing addresses the shadow AI situation as a discrete governance problem with a specific remediation sequence.

6. **nFADP Article 63 — the personal exposure.** Article 63 creates individual criminal liability for responsible bodies at firms that fail to implement required AI governance documentation. This is not a firm liability. It is your personal liability as the managing attorney. Your general liability insurance does not cover it. The briefing addresses what Art.63 means in specific terms for a Geneva boutique practice.

**What this briefing addresses:**

1. **The vendor data extraction clause exposure.** When you signed the vendor terms for your AI tool, you agreed to specific terms about how the vendor can extract, use, and improve on patient data. The Art.321 CP exposure — criminal liability for unauthorized disclosure of patient data — attaches to you as the data controller. The vendor's extraction clause may create exposure you didn't know you were accepting. The briefing reviews the specific exposure and what a proportionate response looks like.

2. **The Art.22 reversal right / medical record integrity structural paradox.** The paradox is real and unresolved at the outpatient practice level. The briefing addresses what the conflict actually means for your specific AI tool, what you can do in the absence of published guidance, and how to document your reasoning in a way that is defensible under FMH audit.

3. **AI triage and referral accountability.** If you use an AI triage tool — an algorithm that triages patient symptoms and recommends referral — the accountability for the triage decision sits with you regardless of who built the algorithm.
