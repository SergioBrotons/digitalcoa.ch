## Regulatory Architecture — The Stack

The ICP's accountability exposure comes from a stack of overlapping regulatory obligations — not a single regulation. The stack creates a documentation requirement that no single regulation specifies precisely, because the interaction between regulations is not defined by any single authority.

The stack (from C294 Phase 05 synthesis):
1. **nFADP** — Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (revised, in force)
2. **CoE AI Convention** — Council of Europe AI Convention (in force for Switzerland as signatory)
3. **SAV/FINMA/FMH/SAI Guidelines** — Professional association AI guidance (vertical-specific)
4. **Carrier/Insurance Underwriting Standards** — Third-generation attestation requirements
5. **Institutional Counterparty DDQs** — AI methodology requests from institutional clients
6. **Federal Council AI Consultation Draft** — Anticipated Swiss AI-specific legislation (pending)

The documentation gap is not a nFADP compliance gap. It is a gap across all six of these venues simultaneously.

## nFADP — The Universal Anchor

### Article 63 — The Most Consequential Provision for DigitalCoa.ch's Practice

**What it does:** Creates individual criminal liability for responsible bodies at firms that fail to implement required AI governance documentation. This is the first time in Swiss regulatory history that a data protection provision has created direct personal criminal exposure for professional practice principals.

**Why it is the universal urgency trigger:** The Art.63 exposure is:
- Universal across all four verticals (attorney, WM principal, consulting partner, physician)
- Personal (the fine attaches to the individual, not just the firm)
- Not covered by general firm liability insurance in most cases
- Present-tense (not dependent on Federal Council AI legislation passing)

**DigitalCoa.ch's positioning relative to Art.63:** The briefing must communicate that Art.63 creates a personal liability exposure that existing general liability coverage does not address. The nFADP compliance engagement is not a firm decision — it is a decision about personal criminal exposure.

### Article 8 — Profiling Notification Obligations

**What it does:** Requires notification to data subjects when automated decision-making profiling produces legal effects or significant impacts.

**Why it matters for DigitalCoa.ch's practice:** Geneva WM firms using AI for client portfolio management, Geneva healthcare practices using AI for administrative profiling, and Geneva consulting firms using AI to profile client needs — all trigger Art.8 profiling notification obligations that most practices are not currently meeting.

**The naming opportunity:** "Profiling notification" is not a term Geneva professional practice principals use. They don't know they have this obligation. The Phase 02 (finance) finding: Art.8 profiling notification is an unnamed present-tense obligation for Geneva WM practices. DigitalCoa.ch naming it is both a trust signal and an urgency trigger.

### Article 13 — Explanation Obligation

**What it does:** Requires that data subjects receive meaningful explanation when automated decisions are made about them.

**The paradox:** The explanation obligation is present-tense (it applies now). But the response architecture — what the explanation must contain, in what format, to what standard — is not defined by any published guidance. Practices are required to provide explanations they cannot currently produce.

**DigitalCoa.ch's positioning:** The explanation obligation creates a documentation need that practices cannot discharge with generic templates. The methodology must be built to produce explanations that are specific to the practice's AI deployment and defensible under examination.

## Council of Europe AI Convention

**What it does:** Creates present-tense private law obligations for AI system operators across Council of Europe member states including Switzerland. The obligations include human oversight requirements, transparency obligations, and accountability documentation requirements.

**Why it matters for DigitalCoa.ch's practice:** The CoE AI Convention obligations are structurally different from nFADP obligations:
- They operate through private law (not administrative law), meaning they create liability exposure in client relationships, not just regulatory exposure
- They apply to AI system operators regardless of size — the Geneva boutique practice is a covered operator
- The accountability documentation requirements are specific: human oversight must be documented, not just designed

**The carrier attestation connection (C294 Phase 02):** Carrier attestation requirements and CoE AI Convention obligations are beginning to reference similar concepts but in different registers. The firm building documentation to satisfy the carrier renewal may simultaneously be building documentation that addresses CoE AI Convention requirements — but without knowing it. DigitalCoa.ch can position for this convergence: the documentation architecture serves multiple venues simultaneously.

## Professional Association Guidelines

### SAV — Swiss Lawyers Association (Legal ICP)

**What it is:** SAV published AI usage guidelines for law firms in June 2024. The guidelines address attorney AI use, client data handling, and documentation obligations.

**The gap:** The SAV guidelines describe principles but do not provide a methodology for implementation. Geneva attorneys have a framework without a decision tree — they know what the principles say but not what they require on a Tuesday afternoon when an associate wants to use Copilot on a client matter.

**DigitalCoa.ch's positioning:** The SAV guideline implementation gap is a specific service opportunity — not SAV compliance certification (DigitalCoa.ch is not a bar-certified body), but SAV-aligned documentation architecture that demonstrates the attorney has addressed the guideline principles in their practice operations.

### FINMA — Financial Market Supervisory Authority (Finance/WM ICP)

**What it is:** FINMA Guidance 08/2024 addresses AI governance for supervised institutions. The guidance is calibrated for FINMA-supervised institutions (banks, insurers) — not boutique WM firms directly.

**The gap:** Boutique WM firms are not FINMA-supervised but face carrier third-generation attestation requirements that reference similar concepts. The FINMA guidance vocabulary has migrated into carrier underwriting without a SMB-calibrated interpretation pathway.

**DigitalCoa.ch's positioning:** The WM firm needs documentation that satisfies carrier attestation requirements while being proportionate to their practice scale. The FINMA vocabulary is the reference language, but the implementation must be practice-calibrated.

### FMH — Swiss Medical Association (Healthcare ICP)

**What it is:** FMH has published principle-level AI usage guidance for physicians. The guidance is at the principle level and does not address implementation for outpatient practice.

**The gap:** The FMH guidance does not address:
- Art.22 reversal right vs. medical record integrity reconciliation
- Vendor data extraction clause exposure under Art.321 CP
- AI triage tool accountability and malpractice coverage implications
- Audit-readiness for FMH or cantonal medical society review

**DigitalCoa.ch's positioning:** FMH-aligned documentation architecture that addresses the six unnamed healthcare ICP problems (C294 Phase 04) — specifically the Art.22/record integrity paradox and the vendor data extraction exposure.

### SAI — Swiss Association of Consultants (Consulting ICP)

**What it is:** SAI has not published AI-specific guidance as of C294 research. The consulting ICP faces AI governance exposure through insurance broker questions, institutional client DDQs, and nFADP Art.63 personal liability — without association guidance to reference.

**The gap:** No SMB-calibrated AI governance methodology for Geneva consulting practices exists from any source. The consulting ICP's accountability exposure is entirely unguided by professional association standards.

**DigitalCoa.ch's positioning:** The practice that develops consulting AI governance methodology for Geneva boutiques is creating the standard, not adopting an existing one. First-mover in methodology creation is a durable competitive advantage.

## Carrier Third-Generation Attestation Requirements

**What they are:** Carrier and insurance underwriting AI governance attestation requirements have evolved through three generations:
- First generation: process documentation (does the firm have an AI policy?)
- Second generation: vendor due diligence (are your AI vendors compliant?)
- Third generation: liability absorption capacity (can the signatory demonstrate that specific human authorization preceded specific AI decisions?)

**Why third generation matters:** The third-generation question requires contemporaneous documentation of human authorization — not a policy document, not a vendor compliance certificate. The question is: what decision did the human make, when, based on what information, before what AI output was used for what purpose.

Most Geneva boutique practices cannot answer this question from existing documentation because no methodology has existed to build documentation architecture that would produce the answer.

**DigitalCoa.ch's positioning:** Carrier pre-renewal review as a discrete service — specifically designed to close the gap between what the practice has documented and what the third-generation attestation requires the signatory to be able to demonstrate.

## Institutional Counterparty AI DDQ

**What they are:** Institutional clients (banks, corporate clients, institutional investors) are sending AI methodology due diligence questionnaires to their service providers — including Geneva professional practices.

**Why they matter:** The institutional DDQ is a commercial gate: firms that cannot respond are losing engagements. The consulting ICP lost a client mandate specifically because they could not respond to the client's AI DDQ (C294 Phase 03 finding).

**The documentation connection:** The institutional DDQ probes the same accountability architecture that carrier attestation probes — human authorization documentation, sub-consultant delegation protocols, vendor data handling. The documentation built for carrier attestation simultaneously positions the firm to respond to institutional DDQs.

**DigitalCoa.ch's positioning:** Build documentation once that serves multiple venues: carrier renewal + institutional client response + regulatory compliance. The documentation architecture is venue-neutral in its design properties but specifically calibrated to survive examination at each venue.

## Federal Council AI Legislation — Anticipatory Positioning

**What it is:** The Federal Council consultation draft on AI-specific legislation is anticipated. The consultation response window is an anticipatory positioning event — firms that build documentation during the open window are building the evidence base that will demonstrate compliance readiness when the legislation arrives.

**Why it matters:** Anticipatory compliance positioning is different from post-enactment compliance building. The firm that has built documented AI governance architecture before the legislation arrives has evidence of how their governance evolved — which is more credible than documentation built after the legal requirement is defined.

**DigitalCoa.ch's positioning:** The briefing addresses what the Federal Council draft will require before it arrives, and what the firm can position in the anticipatory window. The documentation built now is the evidence base for future regulatory examination.

## Regulatory Convergence — The Strategic Picture

The six regulatory venues are converging on the same documentation requirement — a named accountability architecture with:
1. Specific human authorization for specific AI decisions
2. Contemporaneous documentation of that authorization
3. Evidence trail that survives examination across multiple venues with different evidentiary standards
4. Personal liability exposure documentation for the responsible principal

No single regulation defines this requirement precisely. The convergence is structural, not doctrinal — the requirements are pointing in the same direction across different regulatory systems. DigitalCoa.ch's methodology is designed for this convergence: a documentation architecture that serves multiple venues simultaneously, calibrated to practice scale, with Geneva circuit intelligence about how standards are forming.
