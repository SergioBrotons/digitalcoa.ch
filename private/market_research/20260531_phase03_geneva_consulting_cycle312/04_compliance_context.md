# Compliance Context — Geneva Consulting Boutique AI Accountability
## C312 Phase 03 — Cycle 312
## ICP: Small consulting boutiques, 2–50 employees, Geneva-based

---

## Geneva / Switzerland Regulatory Landscape

### Primary Accountability Framework

**nFADP (Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection)**
Article 52 and Article 63 create personal liability for managing directors:
- Article 52: obligations for data controllers regarding automated processing
- Article 63: personal liability of responsible persons for compliance failures

Application to consulting boutiques:
- AI-assisted analysis of client data triggers Article 52 obligations
- Managing director personally accountable for AI decisions affecting data subjects
- No safe harbor for using AI tools provided by third parties — the accountability stays with the responsible person

Key implication: The consulting boutique MD cannot delegate AI accountability to a tool vendor or sub-consultant. The personal liability follows the responsible person regardless of who provided the AI capability.

### Swiss Contract Law Framework

**Swiss Code of Obligations (OR)**
- Contract law principles apply to consulting engagements where AI-assisted deliverables are part of the mandate
- Misrepresentation risk when AI-assisted work product is delivered as if human-produced
- Scope of mandate obligations may not clearly cover AI methodology disclosure

Key implication: The engagement letter predates AI. The mandate was likely defined without AI methodology obligations. AI-assisted deliverables may create ambiguity about scope compliance.

### Insurance and Carrier Context

**Professional Liability Insurance**
- Carrier attestation questions now cover AI methodology, sub-consultant delegation, and proposal defensibility
- Misrepresentation on carrier forms can void coverage
- Personal liability exposure extends to professional license, not just financial damages

**Carrier Form Architecture**
- Carrier forms for consulting firms have escalated in specificity (parallel to legal and WM carrier forms)
- Questions now cover: sub-consultant AI monitoring, proposal methodology, AI tool inventory, human review protocols
- Pre-signing review gap: no one is reviewing carrier forms before MDs sign them as a standard practice
- Post-signing evidence reconstruction: MDs are trying to reconstruct evidence after signing, which inverts the correct sequence

### EU AI Act Context (for Geneva consulting with EU clients)

**EU AI Act — Deployer Obligations**
- Consulting firms working with EU-based clients may be classified as AI deployers
- Deployer obligations include: maintaining instructions for AI systems, monitoring operation, ensuring human oversight
- GPAI (General Purpose AI) model providers have specific obligations; deployers have compliance obligations

Key implication: Geneva consulting boutiques with EU client engagements face a two-jurisdiction documentation requirement. English carrier forms + French regulatory proceedings = bilingual documentation environment. EU AI Act deployer obligations add a third documentation layer.

## The Bilingual Documentation Requirement

Geneva consulting firms operate in two documentation environments simultaneously:

**English-language carrier forms** — Professional liability insurance carriers use English-language attestation forms (consistent with international insurance markets). The MD must respond in English.

**French regulatory proceedings** — Swiss regulatory and legal proceedings, as well as French-speaking client relationships, require French documentation. FINMA, Swiss courts, and French-speaking clients all operate in French.

The documentation requirement is not translation. It is two parallel evidence systems:
- English evidence calibrated to carrier form questions
- French evidence calibrated to Swiss regulatory and client expectations

No competitor offers this as a standard product. This is a structural service design requirement.

---

## The Proportionate Service Gap

The Geneva consulting boutique faces a documentation requirement that no one has designed a proportionate response to. The requirement is real. The solution options are:
- Enterprise compliance platforms (wrong scale, wrong price, wrong timeline)
- Generic AI governance frameworks (wrong specificity)
- Policy templates (wrong document type)
- Training (addresses knowledge, not evidence)

The gap is not knowledge. The gap is evidence production calibrated to carrier forms and client questionnaires, in a bilingual format, at a proportionate scale for a 2–50 person firm, delivered in days, not months.
