## NET-NEW Findings for C312 Phase 03

### Finding 1: Client AI Governance Questionnaire Has Become the Primary Commercial Gate (Distinct from Carrier Form)

**The finding:** The client AI governance questionnaire is now a scored procurement document, not a compliance exercise. For consulting boutiques, mandate outcomes are being determined by AI governance questionnaire responses during vendor qualification — before any engagement begins.

This is distinct from the carrier form pressure identified in Phase 01. The carrier form creates personal exposure through attestation. The client questionnaire creates commercial exposure through mandate loss.

**Phase 01 context (carrier):** Carrier form creates documented standard; post-attestation evidence reconstruction is primary need; unauthorized delegation is active and named.

**Phase 03 addition:** The client questionnaire is competitive intelligence — it reveals what the institutional buyer actually requires before the mandate is at risk. No competitor has built pre-questionnaire review as a discrete service. The questionnaire pre-review is the highest-value intervention point for the acquisition phase.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "Your client is scoring you on AI governance before they've scored your methodology."
- "The RFP has a 12-point AI governance section. Your response determines whether you get to the methodology presentation."
- "The client questionnaire is competitive intelligence. No one reads it as such."

### Finding 2: Proposal Methodology Indefensibility Creates Three-Directional Liability Exposure

**The finding:** The consulting proposal is simultaneously: (1) a capability commitment — you are describing what you will deliver; (2) a methodology exposure — you are describing how you produce the work; and (3) an audit standard — the proposal becomes the benchmark against which your delivery is measured.

AI-assisted proposals that cannot describe the AI methodology create simultaneous exposure in all three directions. You committed to something you cannot describe. The client holds you to the commitment. The carrier asks about methodology defensibility.

This is distinct from the carrier form question (Phase 01) and the institutional DDQ (Phase 02). The proposal is the commercial document that precedes both — and it is now being scored during procurement.

**Phase 02 context (DDQ):** Institutional DDQ is portfolio-specific competitive gate; portfolio construction authorization chain undocumented; model drift monitoring activated.

**Phase 03 addition:** The proposal is the pre-DDQ commercial gate. The methodology you describe in the proposal becomes the methodology the client expects you to follow. If you used AI to build the proposal and cannot describe the methodology, the proposal is a liability in three directions simultaneously.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "Your proposal is the product. If you can't describe how it was made, the proposal is a liability."
- "Three things happen when you submit an AI-assisted proposal you can't defend: the client holds you to a methodology you didn't describe, the carrier asks about methodology defensibility, and a competitor uses your methodology gap against you in the next pitch."
- "The proposal commits you to a methodology. If the methodology is indefensible, the proposal is indefensible."

### Finding 3: Sub-Consultant AI Delegation Is Structurally Different from Associate AI Use

**The finding:** The sub-consultant AI delegation problem for consulting boutiques is structurally distinct from the associate AI use problem identified in legal and WM verticals. The managing director authorized the sub-consultant's engagement — not the AI tools they use. The sub-consultant operates under their own professional license, their own carrier coverage, and their own methodology — none of which the MD controls.

In law firms and WM firms, the accountability chain runs through employees who can be directed. In consulting boutiques, the accountability chain runs through independent professionals who cannot be directed — only contracted.

This creates a specific documentation gap: the MD has evidence of engagement authorization, not evidence of AI tool authorization. The carrier form asks about AI methodology for delivered work product. The work product was partially produced by a sub-consultant using AI tools the MD never authorized.

**Phase 01 context (unauthorized delegation):** Unauthorized delegation active; OaF two-tier visibility; vendor compliance assumption creates hidden exposure.

**Phase 03 addition:** Sub-consultant delegation is not associate supervision. The sub-consultant is not an employee. The MD can contract requirements but cannot direct methodology. The delegation framework must account for this structural difference — authorization of engagement versus authorization of AI tools.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "You authorized the engagement. You didn't authorize the AI tools."
- "The sub-consultant has their own carrier. Does that cover AI use on your client's project?"
- "Your engagement letter authorized deliverables. It didn't authorize AI-assisted deliverables."

### Finding 4: Engagement Letter Pre-AI Architecture Creating Active Client Relationship Friction

**The finding:** Engagement letters and mandate agreements for Geneva consulting boutiques predate AI as a standard delivery tool. The engagement scope, deliverable definitions, and review protocols were all established before AI existed in the delivery context.

Clients are now asking about AI use. The MD has no AI clause, no standard disclosure response, and no calibrated approach — and is improvising responses in real-time client conversations. This creates inconsistency, potential misrepresentation, and a growing client relationship risk.

This is distinct from the fee agreement gap identified for legal (Phase 01) — the consulting engagement letter has different commercial logic, different scope definitions, and different client expectation structures.

**Phase 01 context (fee agreement):** Client questions activating fee agreement gap; unresolved commercial question creating client relationship friction.

**Phase 03 addition:** The consulting engagement letter has a specific AI clause architecture need: scope of AI use in deliverables, disclosure obligations to client, sub-consultant AI provisions, client rights to request methodology documentation, and human review confirmation. This architecture does not exist as a proportionate, boutique-specific template.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "Your engagement letter predates AI. Your clients do not."
- "A client asked how we use AI. I improvised. That's not a disclosure strategy."
- "The engagement letter defines what you deliver. It doesn't define how AI contributes to the delivery."

### Finding 5: Retroactive Methodology Reconstruction Is a Distinct Service Need (Not Forward Documentation)

**The finding:** Geneva consulting boutiques have 12–24 months of AI-assisted deliverables — proposals, analyses, strategic recommendations — completed without methodology documentation. Clients are now asking about methodology. Carriers are asking about methodology. The documentation does not exist.

The response most firms attempt is to implement forward documentation going forward. This is the wrong solution to the right problem. Forward documentation does not address the retroactive gap. The retroactive gap requires a specific reconstruction methodology: identifying what was AI-assisted, what the human review process was, and producing documentation that satisfies carrier and client questions about past work.

This is distinct from the retroactive reconstruction need identified for legal (Phase 01) and WM (Phase 02). The consulting retroactive gap involves proposal methodology, deliverable methodology, and client expectation calibration — not just tool use documentation.

**Phase 02 context (model drift):** Model drift monitoring obligation activated; 18 months undocumented; retroactive reconstruction required.

**Phase 03 addition:** Consulting retroactive reconstruction involves methodology calibration for proposals and deliverables that were delivered to clients, who may now ask about the methodology used. This is distinct from internal documentation — the client is a third-party witness to the delivery and may have expectations about methodology documentation.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "You have 18 months of AI-assisted proposals without methodology documentation. Forward-only solutions don't close that gap."
- "Retroactive reconstruction is a specific methodology, not a policy update."
- "The client received the deliverable. They may now ask how it was produced. Do you have an answer?"

### Finding 6: Personal nFADP Article 52 Exposure Activating Through Client Conversations, Not Regulatory Channels

**The finding:** Personal exposure under nFADP Article 52 for the consulting boutique managing director is being activated through client conversations, not through regulatory communications. Clients are beginning to ask about AI governance as part of vendor qualification. The MD's response — improvised, inconsistent, uninformed — creates documented evidence of awareness without documentation of compliance.

This is distinct from Phase 01 (legal) where carrier form activation was primary, and Phase 02 (finance) where institutional DDQ was primary. For consulting, the client conversation is the primary activation vector — and it is happening before any carrier or regulatory pressure.

**Phase 02 context (Art.63):** nFADP Art.63 personal liability for investment decisions; managing director personally exposed through carrier and institutional channels.

**Phase 03 addition:** For consulting, Article 52 (data controller obligations for automated processing) activates through client data use in AI-assisted analysis — a distinct trigger from the financial exposure pathway. The consulting MD analyzing client data with AI tools is the Article 52 trigger. The client conversation about AI governance is the documentation trigger.

**Ready-to-use language:**
- "Article 52 activates when you process client data with AI. The question isn't whether you're exposed — it's whether you can prove you're compliant."
- "Your client's AI questionnaire is also an Article 52 trigger. Your response is documented evidence."
- "The broker mentioned AI governance in a client conversation. That conversation is the activation point."

## Phase 03 Core Insight

**The market has moved from "can I keep the mandate?" (C311 Phase 03) to "can I win the next one AND contain the exposure from the last twenty?"**

The Phase 03 finding is that two forcing functions are operating simultaneously:
1. **Acquisition pressure** — client AI questionnaire as procurement gate (can I win the next mandate?)
2. **Risk management pressure** — carrier attestation, retroactive methodology gap, and client questions about past work (can I contain the exposure from AI-assisted deliverables I already produced?)

The consulting boutique ICP needs an evidence architecture that serves both: pre-questionnaire review for the next mandate, and retroactive reconstruction for the last twenty.

**The Phase 03 synthesis:** The client questionnaire is the procurement forcing function. The carrier form is the personal exposure forcing function. The proposal methodology is the commercial and legal liability. The sub-consultant delegation is the structural gap. The engagement letter is the commercial document that needs an AI clause. The retroactive gap is the accumulated weight of every undocumented AI-assisted deliverable.

## Phase-to-Phase Bridge: C312 Phase 02 → Phase 03

**Phase 02 finding:** Dual-entry evidence architecture (carrier form + institutional DDQ) established for boutique WM. Institutional DDQ as portfolio-specific competitive gate; authorization chain undocumented; model drift monitoring activated.

**Phase 03 extension:** The consulting boutique faces a parallel dual-entry structure:
1. **Carrier form** (personal exposure document) — the same form architecture, now with consulting-specific questions about sub-consultant delegation, proposal methodology, and client questionnaire responses
2. **Client AI questionnaire** (commercial exposure document) — the procurement gate, functionally equivalent to the institutional DDQ for WM, but with different timing, different audience, and different evidence requirements

The consulting boutique needs the same dual-entry evidence architecture as the WM firm — but the two entry points are carrier form and client questionnaire, not carrier form and institutional DDQ.

**The Phase 03 service model addition:** Pre-client-questionnaire review is the parallel service to pre-carrier-form review. Both are pre-signing intervention points. Both are unoccupied by competitors. Both produce the highest-value evidence at the lowest competitive exposure.
