# ICP Language — Geneva Consulting Boutique AI Problems
## C312 Phase 03 — Cycle 312
## ICP: Small consulting boutiques, 2–50 employees, Geneva-based

---

## How the ICP Actually Talks About AI Problems

### Primary Frame: "Can I defend what I did?"

The consulting boutique managing director does not frame AI exposure as "compliance." They frame it as **methodology defensibility**. They are accountable for advice they produced with AI tools — and they cannot describe the methodology to a client, a carrier, or a court in a way that holds up.

Key language patterns:
- "I used AI to build this — does that mean I'm exposed?"
- "The client asked how we actually use AI. I didn't know what to say."
- "My proposal was AI-assisted. Does that change the liability?"
- "I'm signing a carrier form that asks questions I can't answer."
- "The sub-consultant is using AI on my client's project. Am I responsible?"

### Secondary Frame: "I didn't authorize this"

Invisible AI use by sub-consultants and junior staff is a distinct problem for consulting boutiques. Unlike law firms (where associates use AI on specific matters) or WM firms (where the managing director controls the technology), the consulting MD authorizes sub-consultants to deliver engagements — but has no visibility into the AI tools those sub-consultants use.

Key language patterns:
- "I don't know what tools they're using on my client's project."
- "The proposal had my name on it. The sub-consultant's AI didn't."
- "The client thinks I produced this. Some of it was AI-generated by someone I hired."
- "I delegated the work. Does that mean I also delegated the AI accountability?"

### Third Frame: "The proposal itself is the problem"

For consulting boutiques, the proposal is not just a commercial document — it is the product. Proposals are now AI-scored by clients during procurement. An AI-assisted proposal that cannot be described as AI-assisted is a competitive liability, not just a compliance gap.

Key language patterns:
- "The client scored us on AI governance in the RFP."
- "We said we use AI. The client asked us to prove it. We couldn't."
- "If I can't describe the methodology, should I have said we use AI at all?"
- "The proposal methodology is our product. If it's indefensible, we're selling something we can't deliver."

### Fourth Frame: "The carrier form asks things I can't answer"

The carrier attestation for consulting boutiques is less mature than for law firms or WM firms — but the exposure is identical. The managing director signs personally. The questions are specific. The documentation is absent.

Key language patterns:
- "The form asks about sub-consultant AI use. I don't track that."
- "What does 'adequate supervision' mean for my AI-assisted proposals?"
- "I have no idea what AI tools were used on projects completed 18 months ago."
- "The form has my signature. I can't explain what I'm attesting to."

### Fifth Frame: "My engagement letter predates AI"

Engagement letters and mandate agreements for consulting boutiques were written before AI existed as a delivery tool. There is no AI clause. Clients are beginning to ask about AI use in consulting engagements — and the MD has no calibrated response.

Key language patterns:
- "My engagement letter doesn't mention AI. Should it?"
- "The client asked if we use AI. I said yes. They asked how. I wing it."
- "If the client has an AI governance policy, do I need to comply with it as a vendor?"
- "The scope of work was written without AI in mind. What happens when AI changes the deliverable?"

---

## The Four Accountability Vectors (in their language)

1. **Client AI governance questionnaires** — "They want to know if I'm trustworthy with AI. I don't know what to show them."
2. **Carrier attestation** — "The form asks questions about methodology I can't answer."
3. **Sub-consultant AI delegation** — "I hired them. I don't control what AI they use."
4. **Proposal methodology defensibility** — "The proposal is the product. If I can't describe how it was made, it's indefensible."

---

## What They Need to Hear (in their words)

"Not 'you need an AI policy.' Not 'you need to disclose AI use.' What they actually need is: **here is what your client is asking you to prove, here is what your carrier form is actually requiring you to attest to, and here is the evidence structure that satisfies both.**"

The consulting boutique ICP in Geneva is not confused about whether they have a problem. They know they have a problem. They do not know what evidence to produce, in what format, for which audience.

---

## Bilingual Note

English-language carrier forms meet French regulatory proceedings. English client questionnaires meet French-speaking procurement processes. The documentation environment requires bilingual design — not translation. The ICP understands this in practice ("I have to respond in English to the carrier and in French to the institutional client") even if they don't have a framework for it.
