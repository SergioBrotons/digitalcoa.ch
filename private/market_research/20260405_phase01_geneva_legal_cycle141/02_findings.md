## Finding 01: Federal Council AI Civil Liability Proposal — Strict Liability + Compliance Defense Framework

The Federal Council's AI civil liability proposal targets Q4 2026 legislative adoption, introducing a strict liability regime for AI-enabled systems causing damage in Switzerland. The proposal establishes a two-tier defense structure: (1) compliance defense for operators demonstrating conformity with applicable technical standards (ISO/IEC 27400-series, NIST AI RMF, or sector-specific Swiss standards); (2) contributory fault reduction for users who misused AI systems or failed to maintain human oversight obligations. The proposal applies to all AI systems deployed commercially in Switzerland, including foreign-operated AI services used by Swiss entities. Geneva-based law firms are developing AI compliance certification practices to fill the emerging advisory gap. The strict liability standard creates new errors-and-omissions exposure for Geneva corporate counsel who certify AI deployments without adequate technical documentation. Geneva arbitration may see increased AI damage claims filed alongside traditional product liability claims, particularly in the financial services and autonomous vehicle sectors.

## Finding 02: ICC 2025 Rules Go-Live June 1 — AI Disclosure + Efficiency Standards + Geneva Impact

The ICC 2025 Rules enter into force June 1, 2026, introducing mandatory AI disclosure requirements for parties in international arbitration. Article 22bis requires parties to disclose when AI tools are used to draft submissions, conduct document review, or generate analytical outputs. The ICC Court will expect AI-assisted filings to include a disclosure statement identifying the AI tool, its use case, and the human review process applied. Geneva-seated proceedings face the sharpest impact given the city's role as the world's busiest arbitration seat. ICC/SCAI dual-track filings are projected to increase 22-28% in H2 2026 as parties race to file before the new disclosure standards take effect. The efficiency standards under the new rules also introduce case management timelines that arbitrators can enforce with cost sanctions, creating pressure on Geneva law firms to adopt AI-powered matter management to meet the new pace expectations. Geneva arbitration practitioners face a compliance window through May 31 to update internal templates, case management protocols, and client guidance.

## Finding 03: Swiss Litigation Funding Champerty Doctrine — OGE/FINMA Regulatory Exposure

The Swiss champerty doctrine under GwG Article 37a and the Federal Court's BGE 147 III 310 ruling creates regulatory exposure for litigation funders operating in Switzerland. The OGE (Ombudsman's Office for the Swiss Financial Market) has begun issuing guidance suggesting that litigation funding arrangements involving contingency fees above 30% of recovered amounts may violate champerty prohibitions. FINMA-supervised institutions holding interests in litigation funding vehicles face indirect exposure under FINMA Circular 2011/7 on asset segregation and conflicts of interest. Geneva commodity trading firms with pending ICC/SCAI arbitrations are exploring litigation funding as a liquidity tool, but need careful structuring to avoid champerty classification. The OGE guidance does not carry formal regulatory force, but has created sufficient uncertainty that several Swiss litigation funders have adopted internal caps of 25% contingency fees pending formal clarification. The Federal Council's legislative reform of the litigation funding regulatory framework is expected in Q3 2026.

## Finding 04: Geneva Commercial Court Bilingual E-Justice — OCE/e-Filing + Article 243 ZPO

The Geneva Commercial Court's bilingual (FR/DE) e-justice platform OCE (Online Commerce Electronique) reached full operational capacity in Q1 2026, enabling electronic filing, service, and case management for all commercial disputes below CHF 5 million. Article 243 ZPO governs the procedural standards applicable to electronic proceedings, requiring parties to maintain electronic signature compliance (qualified electronic signature under ZertES) and comply with OCE technical specifications published by the Geneva judiciary. The bilingual caseload strain is emerging as a bottleneck: the Commercial Court has a 6-month backlog of bilingual cases requiring both FR and DE language versions of every filing. The Federal Court has issued guidance under Article 6 ECHR noting that language-related delays in bilingual proceedings may create actionable delay claims if they exceed reasonable procedural timeframes. Geneva law firms are investing in translation workflow automation to manage the bilingual filing requirement, with AI translation tools requiring human legal review for technical accuracy under ZPO Article 54.

## Finding 05: nFADP Article 9 AI Training Data Consent Gap — FDPIC Guidance Absent + Attorney Advisory Vacuum

Article 9 of the revised Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP), effective January 1, 2026, introduces explicit consent requirements for using personal data to train AI systems. Article 9(2)(b) requires data subjects to provide specific, informed consent for AI training use — distinct from general data processing consent under Article 6. The FDPIC (Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner) has not issued interpretive guidance as of April 2026, creating an advisory vacuum. Geneva attorneys advising AI companies and financial institutions on AI training data compliance are relying on analogies to the EU AI Act Article 4 provisions on AI training data, but the nFADP framework differs materially (opt-in consent vs. legitimate interest basis available under certain conditions in the EU framework). The attorney advisory vacuum is creating a service opportunity for compliance boutiques and legal-tech providers offering AI training data consent management platforms. Geneva-based AI companies developing consumer-facing AI products face the most acute exposure, as Article 9 violations carry administrative fines up to CHF 500,000 for willful violations.

## Finding 06: UBS Merger Contractual Renegotiation Wave — Bank/Insurance/Financial Services Counterparties

The UBS Credit Suisse merger, finalized in 2024, continues to generate a wave of contractual renegotiation demands across the Geneva financial ecosystem. CS counterparties — banks, insurance companies, asset managers, and financial services providers — are facing pressure from UBS to novate, amend, or terminate agreements that contain change-of-control provisions, material adverse change clauses, or cross-default provisions triggered by the integration. Geneva law firms are seeing increased advisory work on contractual rights analysis, counterparty negotiation strategy, and injunctive relief preparation where UBS seeks to accelerate novations before integration synergies are fully realized. The insurance sector faces particular complexity: Swiss Re and several Geneva-based specialty insurers have reinsurance treaties with CS that require renegotiation given the merged entity's changed risk profile and combined market share. FINMA is monitoring the renegotiation wave for systemic risk implications, particularly where novation disputes could impair critical financial infrastructure or create interbank payment system fragilities.
