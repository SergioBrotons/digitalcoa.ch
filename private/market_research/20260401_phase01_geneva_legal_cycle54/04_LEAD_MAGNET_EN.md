## Headline
**Your Lawyers Are Using AI. Does Your Firm Have a Policy? A Record? A Defense?**

Swiss law firms have crossed a threshold: 70% of legal professionals are now using AI tools in their practice. Most of them are doing so without a formal policy, without documented oversight, and without a clear answer to the question every Swiss law firm needs to answer:

*If the Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner asked how your firm uses AI to process client data, what would you say?*

The Federal Act on Data Protection has been in force since September 1, 2023. The FDPIC has intensified enforcement across three sectors in the past twelve months: X/Twitter (March 2025), a Swiss retail cooperative (January 2026), and a Swiss transport company (February 2026). The legal sector has not yet been targeted. That is not a sign of safety. It is a sign of timing.

## The Three Timelines Are Converging

**Timeline 1 — Now:**
The FADP applies to your AI use today. There is no grace period. There is no exemption for law firms. If you are processing client data through an AI tool — even incidentally — you have obligations under Articles 5(c), 7, 21, 22, 26, and 27. The FDPIC's 2024/2025 Annual Report confirms enforcement is intensifying.

**Timeline 2 — May 2026:**
Digitalswitzerland will publish the First Draft of its AI Action Plan for Switzerland. This is not a compliance deadline. But it is a market signal: the direction of Swiss AI governance will become clear, clients will start asking questions, and competitors will position accordingly.

**Timeline 3 — End of 2026:**
The Federal Council is expected to publish its consultation draft for formal AI legislation, incorporating the Council of Europe AI Convention (which Switzerland signed on March 27, 2025). Whatever framework emerges will govern AI use in Swiss law firms from 2027 onward.

## What FADP Compliance Requires (For a Small Law Firm)

The FADP obligations that apply to your AI use are not abstract:

**Article 5(c):** You must have a lawful basis for processing any client personal data through an AI tool. Incidental processing — pasting a client name into ChatGPT to summarize a document — is still processing.

**Article 7:** Data protection must be built into your AI systems from the outset, not added afterward. This is the Privacy by Design obligation.

**Article 21:** Clients have the right to request human review of any automated decision that produces legal effects. If your AI is being used to analyze contracts, assess claims, or support legal opinions, that right applies.

**Article 22:** If your AI use creates high risk to personality or fundamental rights — which most client-facing AI processing does — you must conduct a Data Protection Impact Assessment before deployment.

**Article 26:** You must disclose AI use to clients, and clients have the right to know if their data has been used to train AI models.

**Article 27:** Any security incident involving personal data processed by AI must be reported to the FDPIC without undue delay.

## The Shadow AI Audit: Start Here

Before you build a compliance framework, you need to know what already exists. Answer these six questions honestly:

**Decision 0 — Shadow AI Audit:**
1. Which AI tools are your lawyers using today? (ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, CASUS, others?)
2. Are AI outputs being incorporated into client deliverables — memos, contracts, opinions, research summaries?
3. Is client confidential data being entered into AI tools, even for internal analysis?
4. Are AI tools being used for due diligence, contract review, or litigation research on client matters?
5. Do your engagement letters or terms of business address AI use?
6. Do your professional liability or cyber insurance policies cover AI-related errors or data breaches?

If you cannot answer all six questions with documented certainty, your firm has a governance gap. The question is not whether the gap exists. It is whether you close it before a client asks, a regulator investigates, or a data breach occurs.

## What a Practical AI Governance Framework Looks Like for a Small Law Firm

You do not need a 50-page policy. You need clarity on six decisions:

1. **AI Inventory:** What AI tools are in use, by whom, for what purposes?
2. **Data Classification:** What client data, if any, is being processed through AI tools?
3. **Lawful Basis:** For each AI use case involving client data, what is the legal basis under FADP Article 5(c)?
4. **Client Disclosure:** Are your engagement letters and terms of service clear about AI use?
5. **Human Review Protocols:** For which automated decisions do clients have a right to human review under Article 21?
6. **Incident Response:** If an AI tool you use suffers a data breach involving client data, what is your notification procedure?
