# 03 — Competitive Analysis: Cross-Vertical Competitive Landscape

*Phase 05 · Cycle 289 · 2026-05-28*
*Synthesis across Geneva legal, finance, consulting, and healthcare ICPs*

## Purpose

This file maps what exists in the Geneva professional AI accountability market across all four verticals, identifies what is missing uniformly across all sectors, and isolates the structural market positioning opportunity that emerges from cross-vertical analysis.

---

## The Cross-Vertical Market Structure

The competitive landscape across all four verticals has a consistent three-layer structure:

**Layer 1 — Overserved:** Institutional-grade, expensive, wrong scale for small firms and independent practices. Big 4 consultancies, generic AI governance frameworks, SRO/compliance frameworks designed for larger institutions.

**Layer 2 — Absent:** The specific service the Geneva small-firm MD and independent physician actually needs — proportionate to their scale, calibrated to their sector, addressing their specific personal liability position. This layer is uniformly missing across all four verticals.

**Layer 3 — Accidental:** AI vendors, practice management system updates, and carrier forms — which are creating the accountability problem without providing any governance infrastructure to address it. This layer is actively making the situation worse, not better.

## What's Missing Across All Four Verticals

### Missing Service 1: Carrier Attestation Pre-Signing Review (Sector-Universal)

**The gap:** No Geneva service in any sector offers independent pre-signing review of carrier AI attestation forms from the signatory's personal liability perspective.

**What it would do:** Analyze what the professional commits to personally when signing. Map commitments to Geneva-sector-specific adequacy standards. Identify gaps between current practice and what the attestation requires. Produce documented basis for signing with confidence or negotiating amendments.

**Why it doesn't exist:** Carrier attestation is designed as a one-directional instrument with no mechanism for signatory-side review. No Geneva service has positioned itself to represent the signatory's interest against the carrier's document.

**Why it's acute now:** Third-generation attestations are creating personal liability exposure for signatories who signed without understanding what they committed to. The exposure is present-tense across all four sectors simultaneously.

**Cross-vertical finding:** This is the highest-conversion entry point in the Geneva professional AI accountability market — present in all four sectors, acute in all four sectors, with no market participant owning the service.

---

### Missing Service 2: Minimum Viable Proportionate AI Governance Protocol (Sector-Universal)

**The gap:** No Geneva service in any sector provides an AI governance protocol specifically calibrated to the structural configuration of a small professional practice — where one person holds user, compliance, and accountability roles simultaneously.

**What it would do:** Translate sector-specific professional obligations (bar ethics, fiduciary duty, consulting confidentiality, FMH physician ethics) into operational documentation requirements, workflow standards, and evidence standards implementable without dedicated compliance infrastructure.

**Why it doesn't exist:** Professional body guidance is calibrated to institutional scale. No Geneva service has specifically addressed the structural inversion created by small-practice scale: the person who most needs operational guidance is the person for whom institutional guidance is least useful.

**Why it's acute now:** Carrier attestation forms and client questionnaires are asking about AI governance frameworks. Professionals have no framework to reference. The documentation they produce is reactive and inconsistent.

**Cross-vertical finding:** The minimum viable proportionate governance protocol is the foundational service product — required for carrier attestation support, client questionnaire responses, and internal AI governance documentation. It is uniformly absent across all four sectors.

---

### Missing Service 3: AI Tool Review Against Professional Obligations (Sector-Universal)

**The gap:** No Geneva-calibrated methodology exists in any sector for reviewing AI tools against sector-specific professional obligations — attorney-client privilege, fiduciary duty, consulting confidentiality, FMH physician ethics.

**What it would do:** Evaluate AI tools against sector-specific criteria: what data goes to the vendor, under what terms, with what retention, and how that interacts with the professional's specific legal obligations. Produce a proportionate review that tells the professional what the tool means for their specific accountability position.

**Why it doesn't exist:** AI tool review in the Geneva market is either US-centric and generic (tech review) or GDPR-adjacent and data-protection-focused (compliance review). Neither addresses the professional obligation dimension that makes AI tool selection a personal liability decision for Geneva professionals.

**Why it's acute now:** AI tool adoption has occurred through staff self-service and platform updates, not through governance review. The tools are in use. The professional cannot enumerate them. No methodology exists to evaluate what is already deployed.

**Cross-vertical finding:** The AI tool review is the diagnostic service — it tells the professional what AI surface they have, what vendor terms are attached, and what their accountability exposure is from already-deployed tools.

---

### Missing Service 4: Client/Institutional AI Questionnaire Response Framework (Sector-Universal)

**The gap:** No Geneva service in any sector provides structured response methodology for institutional AI governance questionnaires — the primary accountability formation mechanism operating in the Geneva professional market.

**What it would do:** Build a response framework that produces consistent, defensible, portfolio-coherent answers to institutional AI questionnaires. Track accumulated commitments across client relationships. Identify inconsistencies before they become accountability problems.

**Why it doesn't exist:** Client questionnaires are experienced as one-off events in all four sectors, not as portfolio-forming commitments. The market has not framed questionnaire responses as a systematic accountability management function requiring professional support.

**Why it's acute now:** Institutional clients in all four sectors are using AI governance questionnaires as manager/vendor selection criteria. Strong responses win mandates. Weak responses lose them. The competitive dimension elevates questionnaire response quality from compliance exercise to business development issue.

**Cross-vertical finding:** The questionnaire response framework is the operational deliverable — the document the professional can show to clients, carriers, and when asked by regulators.

---

### Missing Service 5: nFADP Article 63 / Article 9 Professional Translation (Sector-Universal)

**The gap:** General nFADP guidance exists in all four sectors but no Geneva service provides sector-specific translation of Article 63 (personal liability) and Article 9 (processor compliance) for each sector's specific data handling patterns and professional consequences.

**What it would do:**
- **Legal:** Map client matter data flows to AI vendors; assess Art.9 processor compliance; connect failure to attorney-client privilege breach consequences
- **Finance:** Assess Art.63 personal liability exposure for WM MD deploying AI tools; map client financial data flows to vendor infrastructure
- **Consulting:** Map sub-consultant delegation chains for Art.9 processor flows; assess Art.63 exposure for MD who authorized AI deployment
- **Healthcare:** Map patient health data flows to practice management AI vendors; assess Art.9 + Art.63 exposure for AI-assisted clinical decisions

**Why it doesn't exist:** nFADP guidance in the Geneva market is generic (GDPR-adjacent) and does not account for the professional obligation dimension that creates categorically different consequences for the same data protection failure across sectors.

**Why it's acute now:** nFADP enforcement is active. Article 63 creates personal liability up to CHF 1M. AI tools are processing personal data on vendor infrastructure under vendor terms not reviewed against professional obligations. The exposure is present-tense and unaddressed.

**Cross-vertical finding:** The nFADP professional translation is the risk identification layer — it tells the professional exactly what their specific data flows mean for their specific professional liability position.

---

### Missing Service 6: Documentation Architecture Positioning Service (Sector-Universal)

**The gap:** No Geneva service in any sector frames documentation as burden-of-proof positioning — specifically helping professionals act during open documentation windows to position themselves against arriving standards.

**What it would do:** Identify which documentation obligations are in active window periods. Build proportionate documentation architecture evaluated against forming standards (not arrived standards). Position the professional to demonstrate appropriate governance during window, not reactive compliance after close.

**Why it doesn't exist:** Documentation is framed as compliance obligation in the market. The positioning function of documentation during open windows is not in market discourse. The distinction between compliance deadline and burden-of-proof exercise is not made.

**Why it's acute now:** Some documentation windows have already closed for some obligations. Professionals who built documentation during open windows are in a different evidentiary position than professionals who are now explaining the absence of documentation for decisions made during those periods.

**Cross-vertical finding:** The documentation positioning service is the forward-looking risk management layer — it addresses the fact that accountability formation is faster than information circulates, and that the documentation window is a finite positioning opportunity.

## The Cross-Vertical Positioning Opportunity

**The market positioning finding:**

> The Geneva professional AI accountability market is not four separate markets. It is one market with four sector contexts, experiencing the same structural problem at the same moment, with no service provider positioned to address the cross-vertical pattern.

**The service architecture that serves all four verticals:**

1. **Carrier Attestation Pre-Signing Review** — the highest-conversion entry point; the professional is already facing the form, the signature is imminent, the personal liability is explicit
2. **AI Governance Policy Document** — the deliverable that survives client questionnaires, carrier attestations, and regulatory inquiry; the written record of the firm's position
3. **AI Tool Review** — the diagnostic; what AI is in use, on what data, under what terms, and what it means for the professional's accountability position
4. **nFADP Professional Translation** — the risk identification; sector-specific mapping of Article 63/9 exposure to the professional's specific data flows
5. **Documentation Positioning Service** — the forward-looking risk management; acting during open windows before standards crystallize

**The pricing signal:** CHF 1,800–2,500 per engagement for the proportionate briefing package calibrated to the professional's sector context. The price performs qualification: it says "this is a serious, proportionate service for a Geneva professional facing a specific accountability moment." It attracts the right ICP in all four sectors.

**The cross-vertical language that works:** The same four linguistic structures identified in the cross-vertical ICP language file apply to all four sectors. Content that works in one sector will contain language patterns that resonate across all four.

L'opportunité de positionnement n'est contestée par aucun acteur crédible existant.

**L'opportunité de positionnement:** Le marché genevois de la responsabilité IA professionnelle n'est pas quatre marchés séparés. C'est un seul marché avec quatre contextes sectoriels, connaissant le même problème structurel au même moment, sans aucun fournisseur de services positionné pour répondre au modèle transversal.
