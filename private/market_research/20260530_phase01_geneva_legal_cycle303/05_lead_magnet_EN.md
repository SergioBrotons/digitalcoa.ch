# The Geneva AI Accountability Gap: What Every Small Law Firm Managing Partner Needs to Know Before June 19

*Why you might be personally exposed — and what you can actually do about it*

---

I run a three-attorney firm in Geneva. I've been using AI tools for about two years. I reviewed every output before it went anywhere. I thought that was enough.

Then the carrier renewal came.

They asked me to describe how I supervise AI output. I answered. But when they asked me to show them the documentation — I didn't have any.

That's when I realized: I had an accountability gap. Not a policy gap. Not a process gap. A documentation gap. And it was mine to own.

---

## What Actually Happened

The AI tools went in fast. That was 2023. We were all trying to keep up. The efficiency gain was real — research got faster, brief drafts got easier. No one told us there was a documentation obligation that came with it.

The carrier asks questions now that assume documentation exists. The FSA says attorneys are responsible — but doesn't define what responsible looks like. The clients are starting to ask about AI. And in three weeks, I'll be at the Day of Lawyers hearing what everyone else is doing.

I can't show anyone our AI process. I can't reconstruct a single research path. I signed the attestation. The renewal went through. But I know — if someone asks the right questions — I have a problem I can't explain.

---

## What I've Learned Since Then

**The "internal tool" assumption is wrong.**  
I thought if I wasn't sending files to a third party, I was safe. But cloud-based AI tools process data on external servers. That's not my definition — that's how the technology works. I didn't know that. I should have.

**The attestation I signed is a record, not a defense.**  
The carrier asked about AI tools. I answered honestly. But what I answered and what I can evidence are two different things. The form created a record. It didn't create protection.

**The FSA guidance confirmed the problem without solving it.**  
They said attorneys remain responsible. They didn't say what responsible looks like. That's not guidance — that's confirmation of an obligation with no operational definition.

**The Geneva standard doesn't exist yet.**  
No one has defined what "adequate AI governance documentation" means for a two-to-five attorney Geneva firm. That's not a gap in the market. That's a gap in the infrastructure. Someone has to define it.

---

## What I'm Doing About It

I stopped waiting for the bar to tell me what to do. They're not going to. The carrier will keep asking. The clients will keep asking. The June 19 event will keep approaching.

I started with the evidence question: "Can I show what my AI did, on which client's matter, and prove I reviewed it?"  
The answer was no. I couldn't.

Then I asked: "What would the evidence look like if it existed?"  
That's the harder question. But it's the one that leads somewhere.

---

## The Four Things I Now Know I Need

**1. An AI Governance Documentation System**  
Not a framework. Not a policy template. An actual system that produces evidence I can show — before the carrier asks, before the client asks, before June 19 arrives.

**2. A Privilege Protocol for AI Research**  
I use AI for case research. I assumed that was protected. I can't prove it. I need a protocol that documents the research path in a way that maintains privilege protection — and shows it was maintained.

**3. A Vendor Contract Review**  
I clicked through vendor terms like everyone does. I don't know what I accepted. I don't know if it conflicts with my obligations under the bar rules. That's a gap. Someone needs to close it.

**4. A Personal Liability Protection Plan**  
I'm the managing partner. It's on me. The carrier will come after me personally if something goes wrong. I need to be able to show I did what a reasonable attorney would do. That's the standard. That's the protection.
