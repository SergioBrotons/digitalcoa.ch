## Opening

Thirteen weeks is not a lot of time. It's enough — but only if you stop treating Swissdamed as something to think about later.

Swissmedic has confirmed there is no grace period for high-risk medical devices with serious incident reporting obligations. The July 1, 2026 date is not a target. It's a line.

If your practice uses AI-assisted diagnostic imaging, AI-based symptom triage, or any AI tool that processes patient data, you fall under Swissdamed's scope. The question is not whether you have obligations. You do.

The question is whether you have a plan.

---

## Section 1: What Swissdamed Actually Requires

Swissdamed is Switzerland's implementation of the EU Medical Device Regulation. It classifies medical software — including AI-based tools — by risk level.

**High-risk AI medical devices** (Class IIb and above) require:
- Conformity assessment before market placement
- Post-market surveillance
- Serious incident reporting to Swissmedic
- Technical documentation maintained and available

**Lower-risk AI tools** still fall under:
- nFADP obligations if they process personal health data
- General safety and performance requirements
- Documentation obligations

The practical problem for small practices is not the classification itself — it's knowing which tools in your practice fall into which category. Most AI tools used in primary care are not obviously medical devices. Some are.

---

## Section 2: The Tools You May Already Be Using

Common AI tool categories in small medical practices that may trigger Swissdamed obligations:

- AI-assisted diagnostic imaging (radiology, pathology)
- AI-based symptom checkers or triage tools used in clinical decision-making
- Voice-to-text tools used for clinical documentation that access patient data
- Scheduling or administrative AI that processes identifiable patient information
- AI-based clinical decision support tools

The critical test: does the tool have a medical purpose — diagnosis, treatment, monitoring? If yes, Swissdamed applies. If the tool is purely administrative (scheduling, billing), Swissdamed may not apply, but nFADP still does.

## Section 3: The 30/60/90-Day Sequence

**By Jul 1 (30 days):** Inventory and classify
- List every AI tool used in your practice, including personal devices
- Classify each by Swissdamed risk category
- Identify which tools process patient data (triggering nFADP Art. 5(c))

**By Aug 1 (60 days):** Assess and document
- Conduct a lightweight DPIA for tools processing patient data
- Document the conformity assessment status of each high-risk tool
- Establish a serious incident reporting process

**By Sep 1 (90 days):** Implement and maintain
- Put an AI governance policy in place covering tool use, review protocols, and reporting
- Assign a named point of responsibility for AI tool oversight
- Set an annual review cycle for your AI inventory

## Section 4: Why This Is Different From General AI Governance Advice

Most AI governance frameworks are built for large enterprises or tech companies. They assume you have a legal team, an IT department, and months of implementation time.

Small medical practices have none of these. What you have is a small team, real time pressure, and a regulatory obligation that doesn't care about your staffing constraints.

The framework for small practices needs to be proportional. It needs to address the actual tools in use, the actual risk categories, and the actual deadlines — without requiring infrastructure you don't have.

## Section 5: The Window Is Closing

Swissmedic has designated healthcare as a priority sector. FDPIC's enforcement posture in healthcare reflects the same priority. The combination means that practices without documented AI governance are increasingly exposed — both to regulatory action and to liability if an AI-assisted clinical decision goes wrong.

Thirteen weeks is enough time to get to a defensible position. It's not enough time to start from scratch when Swissmedic sends its first compliance enquiry.
