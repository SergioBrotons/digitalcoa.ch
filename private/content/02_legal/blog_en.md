## Opening

There is a 46-point gap between how many legal professionals use generative AI and how many their firms have policies for.

69% use it individually. 23% of firms with fewer than 10 employees have formal AI data rules.

That gap is not a statistic. It is an exposure.

---

## Section 1: Where the Gap Lives

The 46-point gap is not evenly distributed. It concentrates in specific practices:

- Research: attorneys using ChatGPT or Gemini for case law research on matters without disclosing it
- Drafting: AI-assisted document preparation where the attorney hasn't reviewed the output at all
- Review: paralegals running documents through AI tools for summarization or analysis
- Communication: AI drafting client correspondence that goes out with minimal human review

Each of these is a potential nFADP exposure point. Each is happening in most small Geneva law firms right now, documented or not.

---

## Section 2: Why FDPIC's Current Absence Doesn't Help

FDPIC has opened multiple AI enforcement actions in 2025 and 2026. Transport companies. Retailers. Social media platforms. None targeting law firms.

This is not a exemption. This is a sequencing decision.

FDPIC is building its AI enforcement portfolio. It is starting with sectors where the AI use cases are most visible and the compliance gaps most obvious. When it turns to professional services — and it will — law firms with active Shadow AI use and no documented governance framework will be the most exposed.

The firms that prepare now will have a defensible position when the enquiry comes. The firms that wait will not.

---

## Section 3: The Geneva Regulatory Calendar Is Not Waiting

Three overlapping pressure events for Geneva law firms in 2026:

**Now — nFADP operative since September 1, 2023**
Your nFADP obligations for AI processing of client data are already active. Art. 5(c) data controller obligations. Art. 22 DPIA requirements for high-risk processing. These are not future obligations.

**August 2, 2026 — EU AI Act high-risk obligations activate**
For Geneva law firms with EU-based clients, EU-based data processing, or outputs intended for the EU market, the EU AI Act high-risk obligations become operative. Contract analysis AI, due diligence AI, and other tools used in EU-related matters may qualify as high-risk AI systems.

**May 26, 2026 — Digital Gipfel Schweiz Andermatt**
The Federal Council AI Action Plan reaches its First Draft milestone. This will be covered by legal media and professional associations. It will raise awareness across the Geneva bar. The firms that already have frameworks in place will be the reference points.

## Section 4: What Small Firms Can Actually Do

The mistake most small firms make is treating AI governance as an IT project or a compliance project. It is neither.

It is a practice management issue with legal, ethical, and regulatory dimensions.

The practical starting point is simple: document what AI is being used, by whom, and on what data. This is your AI inventory. It is the foundation of everything else.

From there, the framework breaks into three decisions:

1. Are you using AI? (If yes, nFADP obligations are active)
2. Are you processing client data with AI? (If yes, DPIA may be required)
3. Do you have EU AI Act exposure? (If yes, August 2, 2026 is your deadline)

Each decision has a clear answer path. The output is a prioritized action sequence specific to your firm's practice areas and client profile.

First-mover advantage in professional services is not about marketing. It is about being the firm that had a framework when the question became unavoidable.
