## Opening

CARF has been operative since January 1, 2026.

That is not a future event. It is not a consultation document. It is live regulatory infrastructure generating reporting obligations for crypto-asset transactions — whether your firm is ready or not.

The FATF mutual evaluation of Switzerland is also active, with evaluators in-market and asking questions about AML/KYC AI systems. This is the international compliance context that Geneva wealth managers cannot ignore.

And underneath both of these: nFADP obligations for any AI processing client personal data that have been operative since September 2023.

Three overlapping compliance layers. One firm. One window to get organized.

---

## Section 1: Why CARF Changes the AI Governance Conversation

CARF is the Crypto-Asset Reporting Framework. It is not primarily an AI regulation. But it lands at the same moment that most Geneva wealth managers are actively deploying AI tools — often without understanding that the combination creates a multi-layer compliance exposure.

The connection: if your firm uses AI tools to process, analyze, or report on crypto assets, CARF obligations are active and auditable. If those same AI tools process client personal data, nFADP obligations layer on top. If the firm has EU-nexus clients or infrastructure, add the EU AI Act.

Most boutique firms don't see this stacking. They see CARF as an IT problem and nFADP as a legal problem. The reality: it is one governance problem with multiple regulatory faces.

---

## Section 2: The FATF Evaluation Is Not Background Noise

FATF evaluations are not compliance audits. They are international credibility assessments. Switzerland's evaluation determines its standing in global finance — which affects every Geneva wealth manager's operating environment.

The FATF is paying particular attention to AI use in AML/KYC processes. If your firm uses AI for client screening, transaction monitoring, or suitability assessment, the evaluators will ask about the governance framework behind it.

That framework should exist before the question comes. Not after.

---

## Section 3: The 3-Layer Compliance Stack

For boutique Geneva wealth managers, the active compliance layers are:

**Layer 1: nFADP (domestic Swiss)**
Any AI tool processing client personal data. Operative since September 2023. DPIA required for high-risk AI processing. Data controller obligations apply.

**Layer 2: FINMA + FATF + CARF (Swiss international)**
AI systems used in AML/KYC, client suitability, or investment advice must be documented and auditable. CARF has been generating reporting obligations since January 1, 2026. FATF evaluation is active now.

**Layer 3: EU AI Act (extraterritorial, if applicable)**
If your firm has EU-based clients, processes data through EU-based infrastructure, or operates AI systems with EU-intended outputs, the EU AI Act applies. GPAI obligations have been active since August 2, 2025. High-risk obligations activate August 2, 2026.

The practical starting point is not building a compliance framework from scratch. It is documenting what AI tools are currently in use — including on personal devices, including unofficial tools.

This is your AI inventory. Without it, you cannot assess your obligations. With it, the path to compliance becomes navigable.

From the inventory, the 3-layer framework provides a structured assessment of which obligations apply, which deadlines are active, and which gaps require immediate attention.
